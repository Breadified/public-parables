# John 20:1-31

**Title:** Out of the Tomb

**Date:** 14 April 2019

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Experiencing Jesus: From Death to Resurrection

The story of Jesus doesn't end with the crucifixion and darkness of Chapter 19. Just as the fire at Notre-Dame initially seemed to signal total destruction, the narrative of Christ's passion moves toward something unexpected—resurrection. While creation conspired to kill the Creator and it appeared that all was finished, the story continues. And it is the resurrection that transforms everything.

The resurrection isn't merely a neat feature of the gospel—it is *the* feature. We are saved not just from sin, but to resurrection. We are saved to be raised from the dead. As Paul reminds us:

> If you declare with your mouth, "Jesus is Lord," and believe in your heart that God raised him from the dead, you will be saved. (Romans 10:9)

Yet the resurrection is the hardest thing to believe. Who rises from the dead? In this chapter, John shows us how people moved from unbelief to belief through four distinct ways of encountering the risen Christ.

## 1. Sight: The Empty Tomb and the Evidence

> Early on the first day of the week, while it was still dark, Mary Magdalene went to the tomb and saw that the stone had been removed from the entrance. (John 20:1)

Mary's shock reveals something crucial: there was no conspiracy to steal the body. She wasn't expecting a resurrection. As we see in the story of Lazarus, the disciples understood resurrection only as a future event—not something that would happen to Jesus immediately. Mary's grief-stricken confusion demonstrates the absence of any predetermined plan.

When Peter arrives, he sees the strips of linen lying flat—as if the body had simply passed through them. The burial had been elaborate: Nicodemus brought approximately one hundred pounds of myrrh and aloes, and Jesus' body was carefully wrapped and placed in a tomb. Now the grave clothes lay empty, undisturbed in their form.

Some suggest the "swoon theory"—that Jesus merely revived in the cool tomb after 36 hours without food, water, or medical care, then somehow removed the heavy spices, rolled away a massive stone, and neatly folded his burial cloth as he departed. This strains credulity entirely. Jesus didn't merely wake up. He was resurrected from the dead.

When we speak of death, we mean actual death. Within 24 hours without a heartbeat, the body begins to pool and decompose. Rigour mortis sets in. The body decays. That is death.

But Jesus rose alive and whole. His wounds were healed. The pain was gone. He was in full health and strength. When John enters the tomb and sees the linens lying there, he believed.

> He saw and believed. (John 20:8)

The disciples saw with their own eyes what the prophets had spoken. Jesus himself had declared it plainly:

> Destroy this temple, and I will raise it again in three days. (John 2:19)

Yet somehow no one believed—not until they saw the evidence with their own eyes. Like the fire at Notre-Dame, which seemed merely rumor until people witnessed the actual flames, the disciples could not believe the resurrection until they encountered it directly.

**Why did they believe? They saw.**

## 2. Sound: The Voice of the Shepherd

Now it is Mary's turn to encounter the risen Jesus. Standing outside the tomb weeping, she sees two angels sitting where Jesus' body had been—one at the head and one at the foot, like the cherubim over the Ark of the Covenant. Yet in her anguish, she speaks only of loss:

> They have taken my Lord away, and I don't know where they have put him. (John 20:13)

She turns and sees someone standing there, but does not recognize him. In her desperation, thinking him the gardener, she offers an impossible bargain:

> Sir, if you have carried him away, tell me where you have put him, and I will get him. (John 20:15)

A woman overwhelmed by grief, offering what she could never deliver.

Then comes the moment:

> Jesus said to her, "Mary." (John 20:16)

One word. Her name, spoken in that familiar voice. And she knew. The shepherd calls his sheep by name, and they know his voice and follow him (John 10:27). A baby who cannot yet see, in those early months, knows its mother's voice in the darkness. Mary heard the Lord, and in that intimate utterance of her name, she knew.

> She turned toward him and cried out in Aramaic, "Rabboni!"—Teacher. (John 20:16)

**Why did Mary believe? She heard.**

## 3. Smell: The Breath of New Life

That evening, with the doors locked for fear, Jesus appears among the disciples. John makes a specific point: though the doors were locked, Jesus stood in their midst. This speaks to the nature of his resurrection body—transformed, spiritual, no longer bound by physical limitation.

> Jesus came and stood among them and said, "Peace be with you!" (John 20:19)

He showed them his hands and his side—the wounds remaining, yet healed. Then came something profound:

> He breathed on them and said, "Receive the Holy Spirit." (John 20:22)

The word for spirit is literally breath. This recalls the creation account: God breathed into Adam the breath of life. It echoes Ezekiel's vision of dry bones coming alive when the breath of God enters them. When Jesus breathes the Holy Spirit into his disciples, he is breathing life-giving Spirit—the very thing that animates creation itself.

This is not gross but glorious. It is the new creation. When someone performs CPR, breath enters the body. Life is restored. Just as God once breathed life into Adam from the dust, Jesus now breathes the Holy Spirit into all who follow him. The disciples didn't smell decay. They smelled life.

And that life gives them new authority:

> If you forgive anyone's sins, their sins are forgiven; if you do not forgive them, they are not forgiven. (John 20:23)

This is not the power of individual priests to absolve, but the corporate witness of the church proclaiming the gospel of forgiveness.

We have expressions that reveal this truth: "I smell a rat." "It smells right." Our sense of smell—connected to thousands of sensitive cells—perceives what our conscious mind cannot yet articulate. We can sense truth. We can smell deception.

**Why did the disciples believe? They experienced him—the very breath and presence of life.**

## 4. Touch: The Scars of Victory

Thomas was not with the disciples when Jesus appeared. When they told him, "We have seen the Lord," he refused to believe:

> Unless I see the nail marks in his hands and put my finger where the nails were, and put my hand into his side, I will not believe. (John 20:25)

A week later, Jesus appears again. This time he addresses Thomas directly:

> Put your finger here; see my hands. Reach out your hand and put it into my side. Stop doubting and believe. (John 20:27)

Thomas needed to touch the wounds. And in that moment, he cries out the highest confession in John's Gospel:

> My Lord and my God! (John 20:28)

The resurrected body is healed, yet the scars remain. This is profoundly meaningful. Scars are not marks of shame but of survival. When a wound heals, the body's immune system works to seal and strengthen that place. The scarred tissue becomes tougher than before. These are symbols of victory, not defeat. They are marks we can be proud of.

Consider a scar on your knee from childhood, a reminder of bullying you survived. Stretch marks from carrying a child—evidence of pain endured and overcome. The scar from a heart transplant marking a life given a second chance.

We don't need tattoos because we already bear marks of our sufferings. And in Christ, we bear the marks of *his* suffering—the One who bore suffering for us. One day we will touch those scars not with revulsion but with love, with gratefulness, with overwhelming thanks for the price he paid.

**Why did Thomas believe? He touched him.**

## For Us: Believing Without Seeing

Yet there remains a problem for us who live nearly 2,000 years later. We don't have the physical body of Jesus with us. The ascended Jesus has, by definition, departed. While he promises to return, when he does, faith will no longer be possible—only sight.

So how do we believe?

> Blessed are those who have not seen and yet have believed. (John 20:29)

This beatitude is spoken directly to us. We are the blessed ones. And what do we have instead of physical presence?

> These are written that you may believe that Jesus is the Messiah, the Son of God, and that by believing you may have life in his name. (John 20:31)

We have eyewitness testimony. We have the combined evidence of sight, sound, smell, and touch—all recorded for us in Scripture. We have history. We have written evidence of a reality that happened in time and space, testified to by those who encountered it directly.

If we believe that truth exists and that truth can be transmitted through testimony, we have everything we need. We have the words of those who saw, heard, smelled, and touched the risen Lord. Through their testimony, we encounter him.

## Hope is Not Lost

As with Notre-Dame, where hope emerged from destruction—the stained glass windows survived, the great organ was saved, the crown of thorns was preserved—so too does the resurrection emerge from the death of Christ. The terrible death finds its meaning in the resurrection of the same Christ.

Our sufferings, our setbacks, our guilt—these do not have the final word. Hope is not lost. In Christ, we find hope and forgiveness once more. Just as we will bear the scars of our own suffering, we are invited to touch the scars of Christ and know that we too are raised with him, transformed, alive, and whole.
