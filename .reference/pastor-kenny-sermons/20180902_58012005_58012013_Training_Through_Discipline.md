# Hebrews 12:5-13

**Title:** Training Through Discipline  
**Date:** September 2, 2018  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction: Understanding Pain

Pain is far more complex than simple tissue damage. Even amputees experience phantom limb pain, and many with chronic pain continue suffering long after tissues have healed. Conversely, historical accounts show soldiers with severe injuries feeling no pain at all. Pain is deeply connected to how our minds process experience.

This passage speaks to Christians struggling with their faith during difficult times. They faced persecution, Jesus hadn't returned as expected, and being a follower of Christ was painful. The author of Hebrews writes to remind them of God's comforting word.

## 1. Earthly Suffering (verses 5-8)

> My son, do not make light of the Lord's discipline, and do not lose heart when he rebukes you, because the Lord disciplines the one he loves, and he chastens everyone he accepts as his son. (Proverbs 3:11-12)

The word "discipline" carries multiple meanings—correction, rebuke, training, and sometimes punishment. It feels painful, and sometimes it *is* punishing, yet we're called to accept it positively.

God's discipline is compared to how earthly fathers train their children. A loving father disciplines because he must. The Proverbs are clear:

> He who spares the rod hates his son, but he who loves him is careful to discipline him. (Proverbs 13:24)
>
> Train up a child in the way he should go; even when he is old, he will not depart from it. (Proverbs 22:6)

Discipline happens throughout childhood and into the early teen years—crucial investment periods. This is rooted in a profound theological truth: contrary to modern philosophy, we are not born naturally good. Popular culture suggests children are perfect beings corrupted by society and rules. Scripture teaches otherwise: we're born with a bent toward selfishness, and without discipline, children grow into criminals and tyrants.

> If you are not disciplined—and everyone undergoes discipline—then you are not legitimate, not true sons and daughters at all. (verse 8)

Children without discipline are like illegitimate children—fatherless, neglected. When this becomes widespread, we face social crisis. The crisis of childhood poverty in wealthy nations isn't primarily about inequality or capitalism; it's about fatherlessness. President Obama noted the statistics: children raised without fathers are five times more likely to live in poverty, nine times more likely to drop out of school, and twenty times more likely to end up in prison.

Fathers, with their testosterone and directness, typically serve as the primary source of discipline. While mothers certainly can provide it, the broader pattern shows the necessity of paternal discipline. This doesn't diminish exceptions—there are many—but the exception isn't living without discipline; the exception is thriving despite it.

**Short-term pain brings long-term benefit.** Without discipline now, we face greater problems when they become adults—broken teeth, poor math skills, inability to calculate consequences. With discipline, they learn to flourish.

## 2. Heavenly Comfort (verses 9-11)

> Moreover, we have all had human fathers who disciplined us and we respected them for it. How much more should we submit to the Father of spirits and live! Our fathers disciplined us for a little while as they thought best; but God disciplines us for our good, in order that we may share in his holiness. (verses 9-10)

Here's the contrast: earthly fathers discipline us "as they see fit"—according to their limited understanding. God, with infinite knowledge, disciplines us for our benefit—to make us share in His holiness.

There's a profound difference between what *appears* to be true and what *actually is* true:

- **Appearance:** Temporary pain, for a moment, fleeting
- **Reality:** Eternal purpose, sharing in holiness, righteousness and peace

> No discipline seems pleasant at the time, but painful. Later, however, it produces a harvest of righteousness and peace for those who have been trained by it. (verse 11)

The word "trained" carries the sense of athletic discipline. Pain doesn't equal bad. Ease doesn't equal good. Many of life's greatest goods involve pain and struggle.

**Application:** Consider John Piper's article "Don't Waste Your Cancer." When diagnosed with cancer, Piper reframed his suffering not as a curse but as an opportunity—a training ground for deeper faith, for seeking God's comfort rather than escape, for wrestling with mortality and sin, for cleansing his heart of pride and greed. He saw cancer not as disability but as *training*.

Similarly, God uses the suffering in our lives—the deaths in our church community, the chronic pain some endure, the devastating memories and failures we carry—as instruments of His purpose. Without denying the pain, we can see God's greater hand at work, a hand larger than death and sin, larger than suffering itself. Just as God used the Cross to bring redemption to the world, He uses our suffering to bring good.

## 3. Enduring Pain (verses 12-13)

> Strengthen your feeble arms and weak knees. Make level paths for your feet, so that the lame may not be disabled, but rather healed. (verses 12-13)

These verses speak metaphorically of spiritual strength. The author calls us to stand firm, to raise our hands in worship, to walk the straight path of truth. "Making level paths" means not turning aside to false teaching—don't be "turned aside" (the Greek word appears in warnings against those who "turned toward Satan" or "turned toward myths").

**Enduring Pain as Training:** Life is a long race. In a corrupt world, more consequences bring more pain. In a fallen world, more service means more suffering. In a sorrowful world, more grief is coming. Our present endurance equips us for what's ahead.

The science confirms this: elite athletes train their brains to manage pain. Through repeated exposure, their brains send out pain-inhibiting signals. One researcher explains that *pain kills pain*—the brain learns to suppress excessive pain signals. Athletes tolerate pain better than non-athletes because they've trained their minds differently. They have a different *relationship* with pain.

**Application:** Daily and weekly, exercise yourself with God's Word. Build habits of Bible reading, prayer, discussion about God's things, and church attendance. These spiritual disciplines might feel awkward at first, but over time they strengthen your core. Your spiritual muscles grow. The path stays straight.

As elite athletes are simply ordinary people who've learned to think about pain differently, so we become spiritually mature through training. Discipline has a disciple in it—there's no separation between the two.
