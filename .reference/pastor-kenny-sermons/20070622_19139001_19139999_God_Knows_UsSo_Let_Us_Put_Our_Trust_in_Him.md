# Psalm 139

**Title:** God Knows Us—So Let Us Put Our Trust in Him  
**Date:** June 22, 2007  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## God Knows All

The psalmist David opens with a profound truth: God knows everything about us. This knowledge encompasses both our minds and our presence—who we are and where we are.

> O LORD, you have searched me and you know me. (139:1)

### God Knows Our Thoughts and Actions

David illustrates God's complete knowledge across every dimension of life:

> You know when I sit and when I rise; you perceive my thoughts from afar. You discern my going out and my lying down; you are familiar with all my ways. (139:2-3)

God knows what we do—our work, our rest, our movements. He knows what we think, even before we speak.

> Before a word is on my tongue you know it completely, O LORD. (139:4)

This knowledge might feel overwhelming, yet David expresses it with awe rather than dread:

> You hem me in—behind and before; you have laid your hand upon me. Such knowledge is too wonderful for me, too lofty for me to attain. (139:5-6)

### God Is Present Everywhere

David poses a profound question: Can we escape God's presence?

> Where can I go from your Spirit? Where can I flee from your presence? (139:7)

The answer, throughout these verses, is unequivocal: nowhere.

> If I go up to the heavens, you are there; if I make my bed in the depths, you are there. If I rise on the wings of the dawn, if I settle on the far side of the sea, even there your hand will guide me, your right hand will hold me fast. (139:8-10)

Even darkness offers no refuge:

> If I say, "Surely the darkness will hide me and the light become night around me," even the darkness will not be dark to you; the night will shine like the day, for darkness is as light to you. (139:11-12)

There is no place on earth where we are separated from God's presence or beyond His sight.

### A Parent's Intuition

I have a one-year-old daughter who has recently learned to walk. In our small lounge room, she explores everything—the toy box, the sofa, the piano. But when she becomes quiet, a parent's sixth sense activates. I can sense when she's about to head toward the electrical wires. I can see her thinking before she throws her food on the floor. I know where she is at all times because I'm present with her in that small space.

In the same way, God knows us intimately. He knows our thoughts, our location, our intentions. There is no way to escape His knowledge. Our sin cannot be hidden from Him; He sees all.

## Why Does God Know Us So Completely?

God's intimate knowledge of us flows from the fact that He is our Creator. This is not mere surveillance—it is the knowledge of a maker who has formed us.

> For you created my inmost being; you knit me together in my mother's womb. I praise you because I am fearfully and wonderfully made; your works are wonderful, I know that full well. (139:13-14)

God saw us when we were being formed in secret:

> My frame was not hidden from you when I was made in the secret place. When I was woven together in the depths of the earth, your eyes saw my unformed body. All the days ordained for me were written in your book before one of them came to be. (139:15-16)

We were created, made, and woven together by God in secret darkness—yet nothing was hidden from His eyes. He alone could see what humans cannot.

### The Creator Knows His Creation

When I was young, I crafted a clay statue of a dog from memory. I know every detail because I made it. I know its history, the care I invested, things that would be invisible to anyone simply looking at it. Similarly, God knows us completely because He is our Creator. His knowledge runs deeper than mere observation.

As David recognizes this truth, his response is gratitude:

> How precious to me are your thoughts, O God! How vast is the sum of them! Were I to count them, they would outnumber the grains of sand. When I awake, I am still with you. (139:17-18)

God's thoughts toward us are infinite. His presence is constant. We are like a pebble held in the palm of His hand, known in every thought, every place, every act—past and future alike.

## David's Request for Judgment

What follows is surprising. After celebrating God's omniscience and omnipresence, David makes a strange request:

> If only you would slay the wicked, O God! Away from me, you bloodthirsty men! They speak of you with evil intent; your adversaries misuse your name. Do I not hate those who hate you, O LORD, and abhor those who rise up against you? I have nothing but hatred for them; I count them my enemies. (139:19-22)

David is calling on God to judge the wicked. But then he adds something crucial—a justification for his right to call for judgment:

> Search me, O God, and know my heart; test me and know my anxious thoughts. See if there is any offensive way in me, and lead me in the way everlasting. (139:23-24)

### The Logic of Judgment

David's logic is profound: *If you demand justice, you must first ensure you are clean.* It's like telling a teacher to mark someone's paper down for a mistake when you yourself made that same error. You cannot call for equity unless you are innocent of the same offense. Before removing the speck from your neighbor's eye, remove the log from your own.

This creates a fundamental problem. David himself is not that clean. At some point in his life, he committed adultery with Bathsheba and orchestrated murder. He is someone who should not throw stones when his own house is made of glass.

## The Fulfillment in Jesus

The ultimate fulfillment of this psalm does not rest in David, the sinful king. It rests in another person: Jesus, the Davidic Messiah.

Jesus alone can authentically call for judgment because He alone can say:

> Search me, O God, and know my heart; test me and know my anxious thoughts. See if there is any offensive way in me.

There is no offensive way in Him. He is the Son who pleases the Father and obeys His will even unto death. Because He is perfectly righteous, He has been granted kingship and judgment. He alone can judge the world justly.

### What This Means for Us

Jesus is able to judge you. When He returns, He will judge the wicked—those who prioritize themselves above others, those who speak of God with evil intent, those who cry out "Father, Father" in times of trouble while ignoring Him otherwise. These are His enemies, and they will face His judgment.

So the question becomes: Where do you stand with Jesus, the Judge who truly judges?

## The Call to Repentance and Faith

God sees us all intimately, knowing every detail and deed of our lives. Some are ashamed of what He sees; others are proud. But to all of us, He calls for repentance.

If you repent—if you turn from your sin and say sorry to God—He truly forgives you. He sees you as though you had never sinned before. He grants you His status as a Son or Daughter of God, one who can stand rightly before God Almighty and call Him Abba Father.

And from that place of repentance and restored relationship, we are able to exercise judgment—not arrogantly, but humbly. Like one beggar showing another beggar where to find bread. We judge through the gospel of the Son, with the love and mercy we ourselves have received.
