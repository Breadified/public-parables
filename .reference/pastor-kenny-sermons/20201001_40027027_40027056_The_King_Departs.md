# Matthew 27:27-56

**Title:** The King Departs
**Date:** 25 October 2020
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Introduction: A Great Swap

Before we dive into this passage, consider the story of Kyle MacDonald. In 2005, he posted online that he wanted to swap a red paperclip for something more impressive. What followed was remarkable: two women traded him a wooden fish pen, which a Seattle sculptor exchanged for a ceramic doorknob. That doorknob became a camping stove, then a 1,000-watt generator, then a snowmobile, a ski vacation, a van, a recording contract, a year's rent-free apartment in Phoenix, an afternoon with rock star Alice Cooper, and finally—a KISS snow globe.

When Kyle traded the Alice Cooper afternoon for that snow globe, people thought he'd lost his mind. The comments poured in calling it "the dumbest decision ever." But then something unexpected happened: Corbin Bernsen, a famous actor and collector of snow globes, offered Kyle a role in his next film for that globe. That film role was then traded to the town of Kipling, Saskatchewan for a renovated 1920s house on Main Street.

Fourteen trades. A red paperclip became a house.

Today, we're going to hear about two even greater swaps—swaps that Jesus made for you.

## 1. Kingship Swapped for Servanthood (27:27-38)

> 27 Then the governors soldiers took Jesus into the Praetorium and gathered the whole company of soldiers around him. 28 They stripped him and put a scarlet robe on him, 29 and then twisted together a crown of thorns and set it on his head. They put a staff in his right hand. Then they knelt in front of him and mocked him. Hail, king of the Jews! they said.

At first glance, Jesus looks like a king. He has all the royal trappings:
- A robe (v. 28)
- A crown (v. 29)
- A staff in his right hand (v. 29)
- Subjects kneeling before him, greeting him (v. 29)
- Even a servant to carry his cross (v. 32)

But these are mock trappings—cruel jokes. Each symbol of kingship is perverted:

| Royal Symbol | What It Becomes |
|---|---|
| Robe | Stripped away |
| Crown of glory | Crown of thorns |
| Staff to rule | Staff used to strike him |
| Kneeling subjects | Mock insults and spitting |
| Wine (a king's drink) | Bitter vinegar |
| His servant | Forced to carry the cross—an instrument of death |

> 35 When they had crucified him, they divided up his clothes by casting lots. 36 And sitting down, they kept watch over him there. 37 Above his head they placed the written charge against him: this is jesus, the king of the jews.

And then:

> 38 Two rebels were crucified with him, one on his right and one on his left.

The irony is devastating. Jesus is indeed the King of the Jews—but he's identified as a criminal. He's crucified between two robbers, not honored officials. His royalty is swapped for criminality. His lordship is swapped for the status of a loser.

### The Downward Swap

When we think of swapping, we think of moving upward. We upgrade from iPhone 11 to iPhone 12. We move from high school to university. We trade an old car for a new one, an apartment for a house. A paperclip becomes a house.

But Jesus does the unimaginable. He swaps downward.

Imagine trading a modern, air-conditioned car for an old one with no air conditioning. Trading a three-bedroom house for a granny flat. Trading new clothes for something from the thrift store. Trading a house for a paperclip.

When that happens, you look like a loser. And by the world's standards, Jesus looks like the biggest loser of all.

### What It Means to Be a Disciple

But this is what it means to be a Christian. If you follow Jesus, you too have made this swap. You are like Simon of Cyrene, conscripted to carry a cross.

> Matthew 16:24 Then Jesus said to his disciples, "Whoever wants to be my disciple must deny themselves and take up their cross and follow me."

We are called to:
- **Swap to be servants** rather than masters
- **Swap to be humble** rather than proud
- **Swap to serve those weaker than us** at their level, not from above
- **Bear the cross** as we've been recruited into God's kingdom
- Become **sons defined by service, not privilege**

The world's trajectory is clear: as we gain power, wealth, and success, we tend toward arrogance and lose the common touch. We begin to see our time as precious, our skills as something others should be grateful for. We charge $300 an hour and can't spare 30 minutes. We fit others into our schedule; they don't fit us into theirs.

But the solution isn't to reject success. I want you to be successful. I want you to move upward in life. The solution is to **serve with the blessings God has given you**. The definition of being God's son has been redefined—not through privilege, but through service.

## 2. Glory Swapped for Suffering (27:45-48, 55-56)

> 45 From noon until three in the afternoon darkness came over all the land.
> 46 About three in the afternoon Jesus cried out in a loud voice, Eli, Eli, lema sabachthani? (which means My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?)

Here we encounter something profoundly tragic. To understand what's happening, we need to step back to Matthew 17—the Transfiguration.

### The Transfiguration (Matthew 17)

In that glorious moment, Jesus took Peter, James, and John up a high mountain. There, his face shone like the sun. His clothes became white as light. Moses and Elijah appeared with him—figures from the distant past recognizing Jesus as the Messiah. And a voice from heaven declared:

> "This is my Son, whom I love; with him I am well pleased."

It was the pinnacle of Jesus's exaltation. His divine sonship was openly affirmed. He was honored and glorified.

### The Crucifixion: A Mirror Image Reversed

Now look at what happens at the crucifixion. The parallels are unmistakable—but inverted:

| Transfiguration | Crucifixion |
|---|---|
| Jesus took his disciples (v. 17:1) | Jesus was taken by soldiers (v. 27) |
| Jesus enveloped in great light (v. 17:2) | Darkness covered the whole land (v. 45) |
| His garments shining (v. 17:2) | He was stripped naked (v. 28) |
| Elijah appears (v. 17:3) | Elijah fails to appear (v. 27) |
| Two famous prophets with him (v. 17:4) | Two nameless criminals with him (v. 38) |
| God confesses: "This is my beloved Son" (v. 17:5) | God abandons him: "My God, why have you forsaken me?" (v. 46) |

The glory is swapped for suffering. Exaltation becomes humiliation. Affirmation becomes abandonment.

### What Forsakenness Means

When Jesus cries out, "My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?"—this is not a question Jesus needed answered. He knew why. This is a question for us, those who hear him.

God forsook Jesus because our sin is so grievous that when Jesus took the penalty for it, God had to turn away. The Father had to judge it. The wrath of God came upon the Son. Jesus experienced what hell is—separation from the God with whom he had been in perfect communion from all eternity.

We sometimes focus on the physical suffering of crucifixion—the blood, the gore, the prolonged agony. Mel Gibson's *Passion of Christ* took 127 minutes to depict this horror. But Matthew tells the story in less than a chapter. His death takes about one sentence.

The heart of the crucifixion is not the physical torture. It is that Jesus got forsaken by God. He was separated from the Father, severed from the one with whom he had existed in perfect relationship forever.

### Witnessing the Cost

> 55 Many women were there, watching from a distance. They had followed Jesus from Galilee to care for his needs. 56 Among them were Mary Magdalene, Mary the mother of James and Joseph, and the mother of Zebedees sons.

Three women watched. Just as three male disciples witnessed his glory on the mountain, three female disciples witnessed his shame at the cross. They saw everything. They bore witness.

### Appreciating What Jesus Has Done

We talk a lot about Jesus's death in the church. Like Paul, I hope to preach nothing but the cross of Christ. But because we're so familiar with it, we can take it for granted. We've heard the story so many times we don't know what to say anymore.

But consider this: Jesus swapped his salvation for yours. His glory for your redemption.

## 3. Salvation Swapped (27:39-44)

> 39 Those who passed by hurled insults at him, shaking their heads 40 and saying, You who are going to destroy the temple and build it in three days, save yourself! Come down from the cross, if you are the Son of God! 41 In the same way the chief priests, the teachers of the law and the elders mocked him. 42 He saved others, they said, but he cant save himself! Hes the king of Israel! Let him come down now from the cross, and we will believe in him.

The mockery is relentless. "Save yourself! Come down from the cross!"

And here's the thing: Jesus could have. He is the King. He is God. He has twelve legions of angels at his command. I'm sure the thought crossed his mind—avoid this forsakenness, avoid this pain, avoid this separation from the Father.

But he did not come down.

And in that choice, something true emerges from the mockery: "He saved others, but he can't save himself." Yes. That's exactly right. Because to save you, Jesus had to become lowly. He couldn't remain king. He couldn't call twelve legions. He couldn't save himself.

This was the only way. The only path to redemption. He had to die so that you might live. He had to be forsaken so that you wouldn't have to be.

## 4. Jesus Dies (27:50-54)

> 50 And when Jesus had cried out again in a loud voice, he gave up his spirit.
> 51 At that moment the curtain of the temple was torn in two from top to bottom. The earth shook, the rocks split 52 and the tombs broke open. The bodies of many holy people who had died were raised to life. 53 They came out of the tombs after Jesus resurrection and went into the holy city and appeared to many people.

When Jesus died, cosmic signs accompanied his death. The veil that separated the Holy of Holies from the rest of the temple was torn from top to bottom—symbolizing that access to God was now open to all. The earth quaked. The dead were raised.

### Two Responses

Notice the two different responses to what happened:

**The mockers said (v. 42):** "Let him come down now from the cross, and we will believe in him."

This is the modern version too: "Show me a miracle and I will believe." But think about it. He just died for you. And we're adding conditions? The cross is the final evidence. If you can't believe that, you won't believe anything. He couldn't come down from the cross to make you believe him. He had to go on the cross to save you.

**The centurion said (v. 54):**

> Surely this was the Son of God!

The centurion saw the earthquake and all that had happened, and he was terrified. But his terror led to recognition. He confessed what was true.

If you realize that Jesus is the Son of God—if you understand the swap that took place—then you are no longer condemned. You enter into eternal life. His forsakenness became your acceptance. His death became your life.

## The Greatest Swap of Your Life

Remember Kyle MacDonald's "worst" swap? When everyone thought he'd made a fool of himself trading an afternoon with Alice Cooper for a snow globe, something miraculous happened. What looked like the dumbest decision became the gateway to the greatest trade of all.

Jesus on the cross looks like a loser. Like the dumbest decision God could have made. But if you trust in him—if you understand that his death is your redemption—it becomes the greatest swap you've ever made.

### Who Do You Think Jesus Is?

No historian disputes that Jesus died. They dispute why he died.

Some say he died as a rebel. A criminal. A failed messianic pretender.

But Christians say something else: He died for you.

Will you now live for him?
