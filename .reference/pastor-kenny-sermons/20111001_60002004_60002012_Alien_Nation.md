# 1 Peter 2:4-12

**Title:** Alien Nation
**Date:** 23 October 2011
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## An Alien Race

When my family migrated to Sydney from Malaysia over 23 years ago, I was a young boy encountering a strange new world. I noticed peculiar things about Australian culture that seemed backwards to what I knew: football where you pass the ball backward while moving forward, barefoot outside but shoes inside the house, eating rice with knife and fork instead of hands.

This illustrates an important spiritual reality. Imagine aliens landing on earth with no prior knowledge of human civilization. What would they observe? They'd notice we're enslaved to LCD screens—laptops, iPads, monitors dominating our attention. They'd observe that dogs are actually the master race, with humans as their servants cleaning up after them. Most strangely, they'd see that humans stop and start their movement based on colored lights.

The truth is, Christians are described in Scripture as aliens—foreigners dumped into another world. So what do we observe when we look at Christians? The passage tells us something remarkable.

## 1. Christians Have Come to the Living Stone (v. 4)

> As you come to him, the living Stone—rejected by men but chosen by God and precious to him. You also, like living stones, are being built into a spiritual house to be a holy priesthood, offering spiritual sacrifices acceptable to God through Jesus Christ. (1 Peter 2:4-5)

Christians have come to Jesus. Peter describes him as the living Stone—precisely as Old Testament prophecy foretold—first chosen by God, then rejected by men.

### The Living Stone Is a Chosen, Precious Stone (vv. 6-7)

> See, I lay a stone in Zion, a chosen and precious cornerstone. (Isaiah 28:16)

In ancient construction, the cornerstone was the first stone laid. All other stones were positioned in reference to it, determining the entire structure's alignment. A skewed cornerstone meant a skewed building. Getting a large, straight stone was difficult work in those days—hand-chiseling rocks into perfect square blocks. When builders found such a stone, God's expert craftsmanship was evident. He chose a precious, flawless stone as the foundation.

> The one who trusts in him will never be put to shame. (1 Peter 2:7)

Those who believe recognize this stone's preciousness. This stone won't lead you astray. This stone won't cause your spiritual building to fail. But not everyone recognizes its value.

### The Living Stone Was Rejected by Men (vv. 7b-8)

> The stone the builders rejected has become the capstone. (1 Peter 2:7b)

The foolish builders—the leaders of Israel—rejected the precious stone that would have made their building. Perhaps they wanted something prettier. Perhaps their eyes deceived them during measurement. They rejected what would truly complete their work.

It's like the rumor about cricketer Michael Clarke's fiancée throwing her 4.7-carat diamond ring down the toilet—a $200,000 stone foolishly rejected. Jesus wasn't accepted by everyone. The leaders of Israel rejected and killed Him.

> A stone that causes men to stumble and a rock that makes them fall. They stumble because they disobey the message—which is also what they were destined for. (1 Peter 2:8)

The rejected stone, discarded at the building site, now trips the foolish builder. It's like stepping on a rake while wearing slippers—they stumble and fall, injuring themselves. People who reject Jesus will stumble and fall.

Why? From a human perspective, verse 8 tells us they disobeyed the message (the word of God). They did not believe. But from God's perspective, something profound occurs: they were destined for this fall. The word "destined" in verse 8 uses the same language as "laid" in verse 6. God laid down the cornerstone of Christ for people to follow. God also laid the stone that causes unbelievers to stumble.

Here's the critical point: this is a living stone. It's not passive. It does things to people. For some, this stone in the middle of nowhere saves them—living stones are added to it daily. For others, this stone in the middle of nowhere causes them to stumble and fall. There is no neutral ground. God's word always calls for a response. Either you accept or you reject.

Where do you stand?

## 2. Christians Are Living Stones (v. 5)

As a result of coming to the living stone, we ourselves become living stones in God's new temple.

### We Are the New Temple (v. 5)

> You also, like living stones, are being built into a spiritual house to be a holy priesthood, offering spiritual sacrifices acceptable to God through Jesus Christ. (1 Peter 2:5)

The spiritual house is the description of God's ancient temple in Jerusalem—the place where God's visible presence dwelt. Throughout Israel's history, God's presence appeared in different forms:

**The History of God's Visible Presence:**

- In Eden, God walked with Adam and Eve until sin separated humanity from His presence.
- For 1,500 years, God's glory led the people of Egypt as a pillar of cloud by day and fire by night.
- This glory filled the tent of meeting where God met with Moses.
- Around 1000 BC, this same glory filled Solomon's temple—the house God promised to His servant David.
- But in 586 BC, Ezekiel saw the glory depart from the temple because of the people's sins. The Babylonians destroyed the temple, and the Jews faced exile.
- Before, during, and after exile, God's prophets promised the temple would be rebuilt. God declared, "The latter glory of this house shall be greater than the former" (Haggai 2:9).

Yet the glory never descended on the second temple as it had filled Solomon's temple.

The faithful waited over 400 years for Malachi's prophecy: "The Lord whom you seek will suddenly come to his temple" (Malachi 3:1). Then Jesus appeared, startling the crowds in the temple. He revealed Himself as the true temple of God—His body is the one in whom the fullness of God dwells (Colossians 1:19). Jesus is the new and better temple, God's dwelling place among men.

After Pentecost, Jesus promised something even more radical. His presence would dwell not just in a building, but in His people. By His word and Spirit, He promised to be in their midst, to be with them always, and to dwell within them (John 14:17, 23). The people of God themselves became the true temple—the place where God dwells (1 Corinthians 6:19).

As Jesus promised through the prophet: "Out of these stones God can raise up children for Abraham" (Matthew 13:9). And so it came to be. Peter encourages us to think of ourselves as the living stones of God's new temple.

**The Temple Is No Longer a Building**

If we are living stones, then the new temple—God's dwelling place—is *not* a church building. The building is practical, but it's not sacred. Whether a window is colorful or plain, whether the pulpit is on the left or center, whether a cross dominates the middle or an organ stands there—these are secondary matters. The temple of God is not a building of bricks or glass, but a building of people.

Think of the church as a giant human pyramid—or simply this room of people gathered together. The people are what matter. We could meet in a fancy building, a tent, a school hall, or a renovated house. My church meets in a school hall. The extra setup is worth it because it clarifies what church really is: not a building, but people.

This understanding changes our language. We no longer say "see you at church" if we mean the building. We say "see you at our next gathering" at the school or parish center. The church is the people—the living stones being built together into God's dwelling place.

**Treat Each Other as Sacred**

When we enter a temple, we treat it with sacred reverence. In the Old Testament, careful preparation was required before entering God's temple. You were ritually cleansed, brought a sacrifice, dressed appropriately, removed your shoes. The temple was *sacred*.

Now, when we gather as the church, I don't think we need ritual showers beforehand (though some of you could use a shave!). And it's not about wearing sandals or shoes, t-shirts or ties. Those externals aren't sacred.

What is sacred is the real temple—the people in our gathering. We must be careful how we treat each other. We must hold each other sacred, honoring each other because in each Christian dwells the presence of God through His word and Spirit. As Peter wrote earlier:

> Now that you have purified yourselves by obeying the truth so that you have sincere love for your brothers, love one another deeply, from the heart. (1 Peter 1:22)

**Application: Treating Relationships as Sacred**

This has profound implications for how we treat the opposite sex. Treat someone as someone's future husband or wife—someone else's sacred treasure. When you're thoughtless in a relationship, the consequences ripple forward. I've counseled couples where the girl or guy carried deep sadness because they weren't the first one, even though it happened long ago and before they met. Instead, treat each other as brothers and sisters. Would you hold your sister the way you hold your girlfriend? Would you dress that way around your brother?

**Ridding Ourselves of Sin**

> Therefore, rid yourselves of all malice and all deceit, hypocrisy, envy, and slander of every kind. (1 Peter 2:1)

- **Malice**: Hating another, wishing harm for them
- **Deceit**: Hiding our sins; exposure is actually healing when Christians confess to one another
- **Hypocrisy**: Hiding our sins while pretending to be sinless
- **Envy**: Not rejoicing in others' blessings—jealous of their good marks, their courses, their looks
- **Slander**: Gossip and character assassination

These sins are particularly dangerous in church community. Politics around money and building can emerge when people care too much—gossip breaks boundaries and destroys sacred community.

But perhaps our congregation's challenge is different. We're young, flexible, and harmonious in our leadership. We may struggle not with politics but with *apathy*. We treat each other as disposable and transactional. We show up when others are useful to us, and we disappear during exams, holidays, and breaks. We should treat the people—the living stones—with utmost sacredness.

Consider what activities you treat as truly sacred in your life. Many of us have pastimes we never miss—favorite TV shows, golf, computer games, Facebook. We check Facebook many times daily without exception. According to one report, a 19-year-old burglar was arrested because he checked Facebook before robbing a house and forgot to log out. It's a sacred activity for us.

But do we treat each other as sacred? Do we treat our gatherings of living stones as sacred?

You wouldn't treat your employer this way. You wouldn't text your boss minutes before an appointment, "I slept in, sorry, can't come today." You wouldn't arrive 20 minutes late as if it's no big deal. Yet we do this to church. An implicit appointment exists between us to gather, and when we skip it, we dump our faithful friends.

You might say, "It's just once." But that's rarely true. One absence for an assignment, one for a piano concert, one for oversleeping, one for exams, one for university camps, one for a baptism, two months of holidays—suddenly you're at church only 8 months out of 12. What other enterprise do you treat that way?

The word "holiday" comes from "holy-days"—days set apart for what's sacred. In medieval times, workers labored seven days a week with no rest. When the world became Christianized, holy-days were invented so people could do what was sacred. What do we do with our holidays? We get away from each other.

For some, extended time away is unavoidable—family lives overseas. But ask yourself: is this sacred? Do we really need to be away for months every year?

You cannot love casually. You can't flirt with this group, then another, then another. Like a gym membership—if you stop, all benefits disappear; it's pointless. You can't do church casually. You can't love each other casually.

You are to each other not a convenience store where you grab what you want and leave. You are to each other the living stones of God—brick by brick laid down by God, cemented together by the blood of Jesus. We are the temple of God. Treat each other sacredly. Christ died for us.

**Prioritizing the Living Stones**

Consider what you turn up to most regularly. When are you most punctual? Most consistent? When do you do your highest quality work? If your commitment to the living stones of God isn't at the top, I urge you to reorganize your priorities. Be committed. Don't give up meeting together.

When Jesus returns and visits us, what will He think is important to you? What is sacred in your life? I pray He would notice your love for one another—deep, sincere love from the heart. That you genuinely care about each other and aren't apathetic. That you're truly committed to your church community as your fellow aliens in this world. That commitment should be as sacred as the temple was to ancient Jews.

Exams are coming. Holidays are coming. Challenges lie ahead. Encourage each other with love and gentleness. Let people know in advance if you won't be there.

## 2b. We Are the New Priesthood (vv. 9-10)

We are not merely passive stones in God's living temple. We actively care for the temple. We are the priests.

### We Declare the Praises of God (vv. 9b-10)

> But you are a chosen people, a royal priesthood, a holy nation, a people belonging to God, that you may declare the praises of him who called you out of darkness into his wonderful light. Once you were not a people, but now you are the people of God; once you had not received mercy, but now you have received mercy. (1 Peter 2:9-10)

We don't typically think of priests as those who declare praises. We associate them with animal sacrifice in the temple. Yet one crucial duty of Israel's priests was to proclaim God's word.

**The Priests' Role in Israel**

Moses wrote the Law and entrusted it to the Levites and elders for safekeeping and teaching (Deuteronomy 31:9). In his final words about the priestly tribe of Levi:

> Your Thummim and Urim belong to the man you favored. He said of his father and mother, "I have no regard for them." He did not recognize his brothers or acknowledge his own children, but he watched over your word and guarded your covenant. He teaches your precepts to Jacob and your law to Israel. He offers incense before you and whole burnt offerings on your altar. (Deuteronomy 33:8-10)

The prophet Malachi recalled these priestly tasks:

> For the lips of a priest ought to preserve knowledge, and from his mouth men should seek instruction—because he is the messenger of the Lord Almighty. (Malachi 2:7)

But this priestly duty wasn't meant for Levites alone. God intended what Levi modeled in small scale to become the practice of the entire nation. In Exodus 19:6, God declared to the whole nation:

> You will be for me a kingdom of priests and a holy nation. (Exodus 19:5-6)

Israel was to emulate the Levites, passing God's word forward collectively. The entire nation, led by the priesthood of Levi, was meant to be a priesthood to the Gentiles—set apart by their morality and righteous behavior. God's vision for Jerusalem was remarkable:

> In the last days the mountain of the Lord's temple will be established as chief among the mountains; it will be raised above the hills, and all nations will stream to it. Many peoples will come and say, "Come, let us go up to the mountain of the Lord, to the house of the God of Jacob. He will teach us his ways, so that we may walk in his paths." The law will go out from Zion, the word of the Lord from Jerusalem. (Isaiah 2:2-3)

The nations would be drawn to Jerusalem by the word proclaimed there. As in the time of King David and Solomon, it would be a world center where people traveled from all nations to hear God's praises declared (1 Kings 4:34).

Tragically, Israel failed in this priestly duty. The nation became sinful, entangled in foreign wars and intrigue. Solomon was distracted by his many wives and pursued other gods. The Levite priests themselves fell short. When they should have called Israel to repentance, they hesitated. When they should have proclaimed the law entrusted to them, they remained silent. Instead, they became self-indulgent and corrupt, turning the temple into a haven for idols and a den of robbers.

This priestly calling found its true fulfillment in Jesus. He is the Israel of God. He is the true priest of God. He proclaimed God's praises, declaring that the kingdom of God had come. He offered the ultimate sacrifice—His own body to appease God's wrath. He called to Gentiles, ate with sinners, and offered them God's blessings. As Hebrews declares:

> If perfection could have been attained through the Levitical priesthood, why was there still need for another priest to come—one in the order of Melchizedek, not in the order of Aaron? Because of this oath, Jesus has become the guarantee of a better covenant. (Hebrews 7:11, 22)

**What Does "Praise" Actually Mean?**

If we are in Christ, we're now part of this task to declare God's praises. But what does "praise" really mean? We often think it means saying "Hallelujah"—which is simply the Hebrew for "Praise the Lord." That's circular and unhelpful. Or we think praise is singing worship songs. Christian bands label their CDs "praise and worship songs."

But the word "praise" describes something far more ordinary and mundane: it simply means *to advertise*.

Suppose you observe Matt doing something excellent or notice a wonderful quality in his character. You then tell others about this quality. That's praise—advertising his noble character or extraordinary achievements. Praise isn't necessarily a religious exercise set to music in church. I can say "Matt is wearing a nice shirt today," or I can sing it. It doesn't matter—one's just more enjoyable!

When we declare praise, we advertise about God. We tell forth as loudly and widely as possible the excellencies of the God of Israel so everyone might honor Him. This is why Psalm 96 combines command with content:

> Sing to the Lord a new song; sing to the Lord, all the earth. Declare his glory among the nations, his marvelous deeds among all peoples. For great is the Lord and most worthy of praise. (Psalm 96:1, 3-4)

Too often, modern praise songs call for praise without providing any content—just repetitive invitations with no substance about God or what Jesus has done.

**What Is the Content of Our Praise?**

Verse 10 gives us the answer:

> Once you were not a people, but now you are the people of God; once you had not received mercy, but now you have received mercy.

Our praise is through the cross. God's mercy extended to us through Christ's death.

Praise is a public declaration. While it's possible to praise someone directly, I don't think that's the primary meaning. We don't praise God by saying, "We praise you God, thank you Jesus, Hallelujah." We praise God when we tell others of His wonderful works in history. When the Psalms cry out "Praise God," they're calling on others to join in declaring God's wonderful qualities.

**Declare Praise to Each Other**

First, declare God's praises to each other. This is encouragement—proclaiming God's word to each other daily (Hebrews 3:13, 10:25).

- Tell of God's work in your lives. How are you changing? What anger have you suppressed? What malice have you overcome? What events have helped you? What talks challenged you? What witness of a friend impacted you? Share your conversion story and reflection on it. This is advertising God's mighty saving deeds to each other.
- When we do this, we are built up. We're doubtful creatures who need to keep hearing again and again that the gospel is true and works.

Though we don't sing together due to time and logistics, I hope we praise each other through our words and testimonies.

**Declare Praise to the World**

But we don't just advertise to our Christian friends. We advertise to the whole world. This is where Israel failed—they became introverted, running their own little God-club with idiosyncratic rules. We must do better.

If you care about praise, you must care about advertising to your non-Christian friends what Jesus has done for you.

As we near the end of the year, I know you're busy. But how many friends have you told about Jesus this year? If that's too hard, how many have you invited to church? If even that's too hard, how many have you prayed for?

Do you care to advertise our Lord? You are the chosen race, the royal priesthood, a holy nation, a people belonging to God.

**The Danger of Pre-Evangelism**

We spend too much time in pre-evangelism—setting things up perfectly before mentioning Jesus. We tell ourselves, "I'll spend a year building a relationship, and then I'll tell them about Jesus." But after a year of silence, suddenly bringing up Jesus would be *weird*. It would be like saying after a year, "By the way, I'm married." No one does this.

Instead, build a natural relationship where your Christian faith naturally emerges. You don't blurt out "I'm married" awkwardly, but it comes up much more quickly in conversation. When someone asks you to tell them about yourself, being a committed Christian should be somewhere up there—not hidden as a secret until year two.

Every day, you have the opportunity to help someone from darkness into light.

Some of you will go on beach missions. Others hang around Eastwood having conversations about life's important things. In December, there are talks especially for non-Christians. Plan now to invite at least one person. Give them notice. Call those friends you've wanted to reach all year but were busy, or who said they were busy. December people aren't busy.

**Spiritual Sacrifices**

Hebrews echoes this theme:

> Through Jesus, therefore, let us continually offer to God a sacrifice of praise—the fruit of lips that confess his name. And do not forget to do good and to share with others, for with such sacrifices God is pleased. (Hebrews 13:15-16)

But what if you've tried to declare God's praises and your loved ones have ignored you? What if friends haven't given you the opportunity? This was Peter's reality. The government wasn't friendly. Many Christians were servants to Roman masters. Others were married to non-Christian husbands. What do you do when you're stuck for words?

### We Offer Spiritual Sacrifices (v. 5)

> You also, like living stones, are being built into a spiritual house to be a holy priesthood, offering spiritual sacrifices acceptable to God through Jesus Christ. (1 Peter 2:5)

In the Old Testament, priests were set apart. The tribe of Levi had no land inheritance—they were different. They wore distinctive clothes to enforce their separation from common people. They had a singular calling: "Be holy because I am holy."

Yet separation wasn't meant to isolate them. Priests were separated *for a task*. They didn't sit around idly. They had work to do.

The offering of spiritual sacrifices means declaring the praises of Him who called you out of darkness (v. 9). Every one of us has this task—to proclaim the praises of the One who called us out of darkness, who saved us, who gave us mercy we didn't deserve.

This happens in various ways. Some declare from the pulpit. Some from the pews. Praise isn't just music. Praise is declaring, "Darren is a wonderful man," whether you sing it or say it. The means doesn't matter; the content does.

### We Belong to God (v. 9)

> You are a chosen people, a royal priesthood, a holy nation, a people belonging to God.

Belonging to God means not belonging to the world. These are mutually exclusive, like:

- Male and female
- Water and oil
- Britney Spears and good music

**The Cost of Belonging**

We don't like that we have to choose. We want to be Christians *and* attend the party. We want church *and* that baptism. But some things are mutually exclusive.

Belonging to God means sin no more. It means abstain from sin. Yet we want to be Christians while still sinning—to have both. That's impossible.

### We Are Aliens and Strangers (v. 11)

> Dear friends, I urge you, as aliens and strangers in the world, to abstain from sinful desires, which war against your soul. (1 Peter 2:11)

Christians are aliens—foreigners in this world. Our true citizenship is elsewhere. We belong to God, not to the world's system. Therefore we abstain from sinful desires that war against our souls—our very selves, our salvation, our spiritual lives.

This is who we are: aliens in a foreign land, living stones in God's temple, priests declaring His praises, belonging to the One who called us from darkness into His wonderful light.
