# Acts 2:1-13

**Title:** The End of Confusion

**Date:** November 10, 2013

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Speaking in Tongues (2:1-4)

Finally, something happens. The disciples are sitting in a house, waiting as Jesus instructed them—not to stare into the sky, but to do God's work on earth while waiting for His return, empowered by the Holy Spirit.

> Suddenly a sound like the blowing of a violent wind came from heaven and filled the whole house where they were sitting. They saw what seemed to be tongues of fire that separated and came to rest on each of them. All of them were filled with the Holy Spirit and began to speak in other tongues as the Spirit enabled them. (Acts 2:2-4)

Luke employs deliberate wordplay throughout this passage. The day is "fulfilled" (v.1), and then the wind "fills" the whole house (v.2). The disciples were "sitting" and then fire "sat" on them (v.3). The room was filled by a "violent wind," and the disciples were filled with the Holy Spirit—the Holy "wind" (v.4). Most importantly, "tongues" carries a double meaning: the flames resembled the shape of tongues (the organ of speech), while simultaneously referring to languages.

## The Miracle of Mutual Understanding (2:5-11)

Now there were staying in Jerusalem God-fearing Jews from every nation under heaven. When they heard this sound, a crowd came together in bewilderment, because each one heard them speaking in his own language. Utterly amazed, they asked: "Are not all these men who are speaking Galileans? Then how is it that each of us hears them in his own native language?" (Acts 2:5-8)

The crowd represented the Jewish diaspora—Jews scattered across the known world due to exile and persecution, yet still faithful enough to keep the festival of Pentecost. Despite being spread across continents and speaking different languages, they maintained their religious identity and traditions. Like Chinese people celebrating Chinese New Year wherever they live, these Jews gathered in Jerusalem for this sacred festival held 50 days after Passover.

The remarkable phenomenon is clear: the disciples spoke in actual, distinct languages—not ecstatic babble. Each person in the crowd heard the gospel proclaimed in their own native tongue, understanding perfectly.

### Against the Pentecostal Interpretation

Some churches today claim that "speaking in tongues" refers to a special spiritual language that sounds like unintelligible babble. They argue this is the evidence of the Holy Spirit's coming, pointing to certain passages as support.

However, this interpretation fails exegetically:

**1 Corinthians 13:1** says, "If I speak in the tongues of men and of angels..." Why assume the language of angels equals babble? Angels are messengers—whether divine or human messengers—and could speak any intelligible language. The text gives no indication of unintelligibility.

**1 Corinthians 14:2** states: "For anyone who speaks in a tongue does not speak to men but to God. Indeed, no one understands him; he utters mysteries with his spirit." This doesn't mean he's speaking rubbish—he may have something important to say, but only God understands because he's speaking in a foreign language. A "mystery" is a secret simply because no one knows what's being said, not a heavenly language.

Yet Acts 2:11 is unambiguous: "We hear them declaring the wonders of God in our own tongues!" The people understood perfectly. Clearly, these are languages.

The pattern throughout Acts consistently describes recognizable languages. In Corinth—a cosmopolitan city like Sydney—the church faced a real problem: when everyone speaks in their own language, no one benefits from mutual understanding. This is why Paul corrects them, emphasizing that prophecy (intelligible speech) builds up the church far more than speaking in languages others cannot understand.

The theological truth is this: God already judged humanity with different languages at Babel. At Pentecost, He shows grace—not judgment. He brings people together again, each understanding the gospel in their own language. To promote unintelligible language would be to reverse Pentecost.

## The Reversal of Babel (2:6, 12-13)

The event begins with confusion:

> When they heard this sound, a crowd came together in bewilderment, because each one heard them speaking in his own language... Amazed and perplexed, they asked one another, "What does this mean?" Some, however, made fun of them and said, "They have had too much wine." (Acts 2:6, 12-13)

Yet by the end, something extraordinary has shifted:

> When the people heard this, they were cut to the heart and said to Peter and the other apostles, "Brothers, what shall we do?" (Acts 2:37)

The bewilderment resolves into understanding. This is theologically significant, pointing back to an ancient catastrophe.

### Genesis 11: The Tower of Babel

After the flood, as humanity filled the earth again, they gathered on the plains of Shinar with one language and common speech. They proposed building a city with a tower reaching to heaven, declaring: "Let us make a name for ourselves and not be scattered over the face of the whole earth."

God's response was severe:

> "If as one people speaking the same language they have begun to do this, then nothing they plan to do will be impossible for them. Come, let us go down and confuse their language so they will not understand each other." So the Lord scattered them from there over all the earth, and they stopped building the city. That is why it was called Babel—because there the Lord confused the language of the whole world. From there the Lord scattered them over the face of the whole earth. (Genesis 11:6-9)

God's judgment achieved two things: no name (humility), and scattering (dispersion). Foreign languages became the instrument of division and confusion.

### Foreign Languages as Symbols of Division

A foreign language is a barrier. When you cannot understand what's being said, everything becomes babble to you. It separates relationships. When Kenny's grandmother passed away, he could never truly know her—not because of distance or intellect or lack of interest, but because she spoke Fukien and Mandarin, languages he didn't speak. All her wisdom remained inaccessible. That barrier was profound.

The world contains roughly 6,900 languages. Each one symbolizes the curse of Babel—fragmentation, misunderstanding, incomprehension. Yet imagine if that barrier could vanish. What if everyone suddenly understood each other?

This is exactly what happens at Pentecost.

### The Promise Fulfilled

The solution to Babel is found in Genesis 12:1-2:

> The Lord said to Abram, "Leave your country, your people and your father's household and go to the land I will show you. I will make you into a great nation and I will bless you; I will make your name great, and you will be a blessing."

After a thousand years, the promises to Abraham come true through Jesus. His name becomes great—not through human pride but through salvation. The scattering ends as people are gathered together, and most remarkably, there is no more Babel-like confusion. After the initial bewilderment, everyone understands in their own language.

As Peter speaks, they hear the gospel of Jesus in languages they comprehend. Acts 2:9-11 lists the very nations scattered at Babel—Parthians, Medes, Elamites, Mesopotamians, and many others—now gathered and understanding together. It's a reversal of the Table of Nations, a healing of humanity's deepest division.

## Making the Gospel Intelligible (2:14-36)

Peter stands up with the Eleven and addresses the crowd with clarity:

> "Fellow Jews and all of you who live in Jerusalem, let me explain this to you; listen carefully to what I say. These men are not drunk, as you suppose. It's only nine in the morning!" (Acts 2:14-15)

In a language everyone can hear and understand, Peter explains that this is no drunken episode—it's the fulfillment of Joel's prophecy:

> "In the last days, God says, I will pour out my Spirit on all people. Your sons and daughters will prophesy, your young men will see visions, your old men will dream dreams... And everyone who calls on the name of the Lord will be saved." (Acts 2:17-21)

### The Principle of Intelligibility

If we promote unintelligible language in our proclamation, we reverse Pentecost and keep people under the judgment of Babel. But when the gospel is intelligible—when people hear it in a language they truly understand—salvation becomes possible.

This is why the Bible was translated into English and countless other languages. Missionary organizations like SIL have worked tirelessly to translate Scripture into over 2,500 languages. We cannot create one universal language, but we can make the gospel available in all languages.

Language is more than grammar and syntax. It includes slang, cultural references, humor—the things that connect a speaker to their listeners. Each cultural context requires its own translation of the gospel message. There are as many ways to welcome people into God's kingdom as there are people to welcome.

> "If the whole church comes together and everyone speaks in tongues, and some who do not understand or some unbelievers come in, will they not say that you are out of your mind? But if an unbeliever or someone who does not understand comes in while everybody is prophesying, he will be convinced by all that he is a sinner and will be judged by all, and the secrets of his heart will be laid bare. So he will fall down and worship God, exclaiming, 'God is really among you!'" (1 Corinthians 14:23-25)

When an unbeliever hears the gospel in a language they can understand, salvation happens.

## The Results (2:37-47)

> When the people heard this, they were cut to the heart and said to Peter and the other apostles, "Brothers, what shall we do?" Peter replied, "Repent and be baptized, every one of you, in the name of Jesus Christ for the forgiveness of your sins. And you will receive the gift of the Holy Spirit. The promise is for you and your children and for all who are far off—for all whom the Lord our God will call." (Acts 2:37-39)

Three thousand people were baptized that day. From 120 disciples to 3,000 believers—the church exploded through intelligible proclamation.

### The Early Church's Life Together

> They devoted themselves to the apostles' teaching and to the fellowship, to the breaking of bread and to prayer. All the believers were together and had everything in common. They sold possessions and goods to give to anyone who had need. Every day they continued to meet together in the temple courts. They broke bread in their homes and ate together with glad and sincere hearts, praising God and enjoying the favor of all the people. And the Lord added to their number daily those who were being saved. (Acts 2:42-47)

This wasn't socialism—Scripture consistently affirms private property (Exodus 20:15-17, Deuteronomy 21:16, Proverbs 10:4). Rather, it was voluntary generosity. Those with means gave freely when others faced need, motivated by love and unity in Christ.

The church grew steadily, not through massive rallies alone, but through:

- **Teaching** (intelligible instruction)
- **Fellowship** (genuine community and communion)
- **Breaking bread** (shared meals)
- **Prayer** (dependence on God)

The results were both dramatic and gentle—daily additions, steady growth, and the ongoing miraculous demonstration that God was truly with His people.

## Application: Our Task Today

We speak with imperfect language, with accents, sometimes as second-language speakers. Yet God uses us to make the gospel known. Whether translating Scripture across language barriers, speaking to cultural contexts different from our own, or simply explaining Christ's message clearly, we participate in reversing Babel.

Our church gatherings must remain intelligible. If not, they must be translated. Some of you have skills to speak Mandarin, Korean, Arabic—you're part of the task of translation, making the unknown known.

When this happens consistently and faithfully, the promise of Acts 2:21 comes true: "Everyone who calls on the name of the Lord will be saved."

Give the message of Christ crucified. God will use us.
