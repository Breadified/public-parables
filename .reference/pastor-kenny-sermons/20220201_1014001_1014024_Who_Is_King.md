# Genesis 14:1-24

**Title:** Who Is King?  
**Date:** 13 February 2022  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Geopolitical Crisis: A Battle for Dominion

In the opening of this passage, we find ourselves in the ancient world where questions of power and sovereignty shape the lives of nations. The circumstances Kenny mentions—rising petrol prices due to tensions between Russia and Ukraine—mirror an ancient conflict described in Genesis 14. Just as modern nations vie for control and influence, so too did the kings of the ancient Near East struggle for supremacy.

The passage presents us with a fundamental question: Who will rule? This is not merely a question about earthly thrones, but about ultimate authority and submission to God's kingdom.

---

## The Eastern Alliance and Western Vassals

### The Aggressors: Eastern Kings (vv. 1-4)

Four kings from Mesopotamia form a powerful alliance:

> **Amraphel** king of Shinar,  
> **Arioch** king of Ellasar,  
> **Kedorlaomer** king of Elam,  
> and **Tidal** king of Goyim

These names represent ancient powers from the east of Israel. Shinar is the ancient name for Babylon, the cradle of civilization in modern-day Iraq. The other kingdoms—Ellasar, Elam, and the territories of Goyim—trace back to descendants of Shem, Noah's son, showing how humanity spread after the flood.

The word "king" appears twenty-eight times throughout this chapter, underscoring the theme of kingship and authority.

### The Western Alliance: Five Kings in Rebellion (vv. 2-4)

The five southern kings—rulers of Sodom, Gomorrah, Admah, Zeboim, and Zoar—dwell in the Valley of Siddim, the Dead Sea region. These cities we recognize from earlier narratives. Sodom and Gomorrah are already known to us as places full of wickedness. The king of Sodom bears a name meaning "in evil," suggesting the moral character of his rule.

For twelve years, these western kings had been subject to Kedorlaomer in a suzerain-vassal relationship. In the ancient world, a suzerain was a powerful overlord who controlled vassal states through treaties. The vassal states paid taxes, provided military support, and acknowledged the suzerain's authority in exchange for protection and stability. This relationship is similar to how Ukraine traditionally existed under Russian influence—nominally independent but functionally subordinate.

### The Rebellion and Conflict (vv. 4-5)

In the thirteenth year, the western alliance rebelled. Whether they refused further tribute, rejected military service, or simply sought independence, they determined they would no longer submit to the eastern powers.

Kedorlaomer responded by organizing a military campaign. The Eastern coalition first defeated various peoples along the King's Highway—a crucial trade route stretching from Mesopotamia through Canaan. They conquered the Rephaites, Zuzites, Emites, and Horites, then moved south toward the territories of the southern kings. This strategic route explains why the land of Israel has repeatedly become a battleground between great powers—it serves as the highway connecting East and West, making it what Kenny calls "the Belgium of the Middle East."

### The Battle's Outcome (vv. 8-12)

The five southern kings assembled in the Valley of Siddim to make their stand:

> They marched out and drew up their battle lines in the Valley of Siddim against Kedorlaomer and his allies.

But the battle was brief and devastating. The Valley of Siddim, with its tar pits and slime deposits, became a trap for the defeated southern forces:

> The kings of Sodom and Gomorrah fled, and some of the men fell into the tar pits while the rest fled to the hills.

To the victor went the spoils. The Eastern kings seized all goods and food, and crucially, they took captive Lot—Abraham's nephew who had chosen to dwell in Sodom—along with his possessions.

## Abraham the Unexpected King (vv. 13-24)

### The Call to Action (vv. 13-14)

A refugee came to Abraham with news of Lot's capture. Abraham was living by the great trees of Mamre with his Amorite allies Eshkol and Aner—men who had made covenant with him.

Here we discover something remarkable about Abraham. He maintained a household of 318 trained men—warriors he had discipled and educated. This was not a standing army in the modern sense, but rather young men Abraham had mentored, preparing them for conflict. The text says he "armed those he had trained"—his disciples in the art of warfare.

When Abraham heard of Lot's capture, he immediately mobilized these trained fighters and pursued the Eastern coalition all the way to Dan, tracking them northward along the King's Highway.

### The Victory (vv. 15-16)

Under cover of night—executing a guerrilla-style attack—Abraham divided his forces and attacked the Eastern alliance. His small force routed the massive coalition, pursuing them as far as Hobah, north of Damascus. This is a distance of hundreds of kilometers—comparable to traveling from Sydney to Newcastle.

Abraham recovered all the goods and successfully rescued Lot, his possessions, the women, and the other people taken captive. Against all military logic, an aged patriarch with a small trained force defeated the very kings who had conquered the organized armies of the southern coalition.

The only explanation given is what Melchizedek later affirms: God Most High delivered Abraham's enemies into his hand.

### The Encounter with Melchizedek (vv. 17-20)

As Abraham returned victorious, a remarkable figure emerged: Melchizedek, king of Salem.

> Then Melchizedek king of Salem brought out bread and wine. He was priest of God Most High, and he blessed Abram, saying, "Blessed be Abram by God Most High, Creator of heaven and earth. And praise be to God Most High, who delivered your enemies into your hand."

Melchizedek's name carries profound meaning. Melek means "king" and Zedek means "righteousness"—so his name speaks of kingship rooted in righteousness. Salem is Jerusalem, the city of peace. This is the first appearance of a priest-king in Scripture, and his blessing reveals the source of Abraham's victory.

The New Testament connects Melchizedek to Christ himself. In Psalm 110:4, the Messiah is declared to be "a priest forever, in the order of Melchizedek." And in Hebrews, we learn that:

> Jesus, [has] become a high priest forever, in the order of Melchizedek... Without father or mother, without genealogy, without beginning of days or end of life, resembling the Son of God, he remains a priest forever.

### The Refusal of Worldly Reward (vv. 21-24)

The king of Sodom approached Abraham with an offer:

> "Give me the people and keep the goods for yourself."

Abraham had every right to take the spoils. By the laws of war, everything seized belonged to the conqueror. But Abraham raised his hand and swore an oath:

> "With raised hand I have sworn an oath to the Lord, God Most High, Creator of heaven and earth, that I will accept nothing belonging to you, not even a thread or the strap of a sandal, so that you will never be able to say, 'I made Abram rich.'

Abraham refused to be enriched by Sodom. He would not become the suzerain over these wicked cities. He would not align himself with evil, even when offered great wealth. This was not merely pride or self-righteousness—it was a matter of loyalty and covenant fidelity.

Abraham accepted only provisions for his men's sustenance and permitted his allies Aner, Eshkol, and Mamre to take their rightful share. The rest he returned or refused.

## The Hierarchy of Kings

The passage reveals a crucial spiritual reality: there exists a hierarchy of authority and power.

The Eastern kings ruled over the Western kings as suzerains. Abraham, through God's intervention, defeated the Eastern kings and thus possessed authority over the territorial kings. Yet Abraham himself was vassal to another Suzerain—God Most High.

This is the structure of the cosmos. Earthly kings, no matter how powerful, remain under submission to the King of righteousness and peace. Abraham's victory came not from military superiority but from standing under God's authority. His refusal of the spoils of Sodom demonstrated his ultimate allegiance.

---

## The Covenant Foundation

This victory and its aftermath must be understood within the context of God's covenant with Abraham. In Genesis 12:1-3, God had promised:

> "I will make you into a great nation, and I will bless you; I will make your name great, and you will be a blessing. I will bless those who bless you, and whoever curses you I will curse; and all peoples on earth will be blessed through you."

Those associated with Abraham share in blessing; those who dissociate themselves from him—like Lot, who had chosen to dwell in Sodom—experience the consequences of that separation. Lot was captured precisely because he had aligned himself with a wicked city rather than remaining with Abraham under God's covenant.

## Living Under the True King

This ancient narrative speaks directly to our contemporary struggle: Who will be king in our lives?

In modern secular culture, God's kingship has been progressively diminished. He is no longer acknowledged as king over our nations, our businesses, or our personal decisions. The more we shrink God's domain, the less of life falls under His righteous rule.

But Abraham's example calls us to expand God's kingship. He submitted himself to the sovereignty of God Most High and refused the temptations and riches that would have placed him under obligation to wickedness.

As you disciple young people—in youth groups, in your families, in your spheres of influence—consider Abraham's model. He trained warriors in righteousness, young men prepared to stand for what is right even when facing overwhelming opposition. He taught them that victory comes not from worldly power but from alignment with God's purposes.

The time is short. Schools are starting, universities are opening, seasons change rapidly. Use this season well. Train the young in the fear of the Lord and submission to His kingship. Expand His rule in your own heart and in the hearts of those you influence.

For ultimately, Jesus Christ is our King—the King of Righteousness and Peace, the eternal High Priest in the order of Melchizedek, to whom every knee will bow and every tongue confess His lordship.
