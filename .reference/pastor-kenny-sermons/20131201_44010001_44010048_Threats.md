# Acts 10:1-48

**Title:** Threats
**Date:** 1 December 2013
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Introduction: Living in Uncertain Times

We live in a world of constant turmoil. As threats arise—whether pandemics, economic uncertainty, or social upheaval—the question becomes: how do we think biblically about these challenges? The gospel calls us to break down barriers, not to build walls of fear around ourselves.

Today we examine a pivotal moment in Acts where God transforms Peter's understanding of who belongs in God's kingdom. This is not merely a story about food laws; it's about the radical inclusion of all people in God's plan of salvation.

---

## Peter's Vision: The Unclean Made Clean

### The Setting

Peter finds himself in Joppa (modern-day Tel Aviv), staying with Simon the Tanner. Around noon, while praying on the roof and waiting for lunch, he falls into a trance and receives a vision:

> About noon the following day as they were on their journey and approaching the city, Peter went up on the roof to pray. He became hungry and wanted something to eat, and while the meal was being prepared, he fell into a trance. (Acts 10:9-10)

### The Vision Itself

Peter sees heaven opened, and a massive sheet descends containing every kind of animal—four-footed creatures, reptiles, and birds. Then comes a voice:

> Then a voice told him, "Get up, Peter. Kill and eat." (Acts 10:13)

Peter's response is immediate and emphatic. He refuses three times, saying:

> "Surely not, Lord! I have never eaten anything impure or unclean." (Acts 10:14)

### Understanding Jewish Dietary Laws

Peter's refusal stems from the Mosaic laws of cleanness outlined in Leviticus 11. These laws specify which animals are permissible for food:

> You may eat any animal that has a divided hoof and that chews the cud... You must not eat their meat or touch their carcasses; they are unclean for you. (Leviticus 11:3, 8)

Pork and shellfish were forbidden; beef and fish were clean. The rabbinical term "kosher" (from the Hebrew *kasher*, meaning "lawful") came to describe these requirements.

Some argue these laws were partly practical—pigs are scavengers, and improperly cooked pork could harbor *Trichinella spiralis*, a parasitic worm deadly to humans. Others suggest God designed unclean animals to dispose of earth's waste. Whether arbitrary or practical, the laws were absolute for faithful Jews. Breaking them meant ritual defilement.

Yet the voice speaks again:

> "The voice spoke to him a second time, 'Do not call anything impure that God has made clean.'" (Acts 10:15)

This happens three times before the sheet is withdrawn into heaven.

## Simon the Tanner: Despised and Yet Loved

### The Unclean Among Us

Before we judge Peter's initial reluctance, we must understand the context of his companionship. Peter is staying with Simon the Tanner—a man who represents the lowest social stratum of Jewish society.

Tanners worked with animal hides, using acid, enzymes, lime, and animal dung to process leather. It was bloody, toxic, and stinking work. For this reason, tanneries were located by the sea, where water was plentiful and the odor could dissipate. The occupation was so despised that the Babylonian Talmud declared: "The world cannot do without perfume makers and tanners, happy is he who prepares perfumes, woe to him whose craft is tanning."

The social stigma was devastating. No respectable person would befriend a tanner, invite him to gatherings, or marry into his family. Religious authorities even granted women grounds for divorce if they discovered their husbands were tanners.

Furthermore, his work made him ritually unclean:

> If an animal that you are allowed to eat dies, anyone who touches its carcass will be unclean till evening. (Leviticus 11:39)

A tanner touched dead animals constantly. By strict interpretation, he was perpetually unclean—a spiritual outcast.

### Peter's Radical Hospitality

Yet Peter does something astonishing:

> Peter stayed in Joppa for some time with a tanner named Simon. (Acts 9:43)

He doesn't merely visit; he *lives* with this man. He shares his home, his meals, his life. In the context of Jewish purity concerns, this is nothing short of revolutionary. Both men bear the same name—Simon—suggesting deep fellowship and equality before God.

This is the gospel: the breaking down of barriers. Peter, who commanded a lame beggar to "rise up and walk" into the temple, extends that same transformative love to the despised. Simon the Tanner receives the dignity of an apostle's friendship.

### A Question for Us

In our own time, who do we despise? Media and culture demean abattoir workers, miners, and farmers—those whose labor sustains us. Yet we marginalize them. Who are the "unclean" in our society whom we avoid? The homeless? The addict? The prisoner?

If pestilence comes and the elderly are dying, will we abandon the vulnerable? Will we hoard resources while others perish? Or will we, like Peter, extend ourselves to the despised and the dying?

Martin Luther, during the plague of 1527, wrote that those in positions of leadership—pastors, physicians, officials—must remain. Parents cannot abandon their children. Neighbors cannot flee if their departure means others suffer. *This is our time.* We must be ready to love the unlovable.

## Peter's Transformation Deepens: The Gentiles

### Breaking the Boundary

As Peter ponders the vision's meaning, men arrive at Simon's house—sent by Cornelius, a Roman centurion in Caesarea. They request Peter's presence. After the Spirit confirms their coming, Peter agrees and travels with them.

Caesarea was an artificial harbor built by Herod the Great, a predominantly Gentile city where Jews were a minority. When Peter arrives, Cornelius falls at his feet in reverence, but Peter stops him:

> "Stand up, I am only a man myself." (Acts 10:26)

Peter then speaks the revolutionary truth he has learned:

> "You are well aware that it is against our law for a Jew to associate with or visit a Gentile. But God has shown me that I should not call anyone impure or unclean." (Acts 10:28)

### The Old Separation

The Law commanded Israel's separation from the nations. God had set them apart as holy:

> "You will possess their land; I will give it to you as an inheritance... I am the Lord your God, who has set you apart from the nations." (Leviticus 20:24-26)

The purpose of this separation was never exclusion for its own sake. Rather, Israel was meant to become a light to the nations, eventually bringing all peoples into God's covenant. Yet over time, the command to be separate became a rule of absolute non-association. The vision commands Peter to reverse this misinterpretation.

It's analogous to how pandemic precautions—washing hands, limiting contact—can harden into complete isolation and hoarding. The principle of health becomes an excuse for selfishness.

### Cornelius: The God-Fearer

Cornelius is no ordinary Gentile. He is:

- A Roman centurion (military commander)
- Righteous and God-fearing
- A man of prayer at traditional prayer times
- A giver of alms to the Jewish people
- Respected by all the Jews

Yet because he had not undergone circumcision—the final step of full proselytization—he remained technically unclean, an outsider to the covenant community.

### The Gospel Proclaimed

Peter addresses the assembled household with the heart of the gospel:

> "You know what has happened throughout the province of Judea, beginning in Galilee after the baptism that John preached—how God anointed Jesus of Nazareth with the Holy Spirit and power, and how he went around doing good and healing all who were under the power of the devil, because God was with him." (Acts 10:37-38)

And the climax:

> "All the prophets testify about him that everyone who believes in him receives forgiveness of sins through his name." (Acts 10:43)

*Everyone.* Not Jews alone, but all who believe.

## The Eschatological Confirmation

### The Spirit's Outpouring

As Peter speaks, something remarkable occurs:

> While Peter was still speaking these words, the Holy Spirit came on all who heard the message. The circumcised believers who had come with Peter were astonished that the gift of the Holy Spirit had been poured out even on Gentiles. For they heard them speaking in tongues and praising God. (Acts 10:44-46)

This is not the quiet inner indwelling of the Holy Spirit at conversion. This is the eschatological gift—the end-times outpouring that began at Pentecost, now extended beyond Jews and Samaritans to the full nations. It is God's own validation that the Gentiles belong.

### Baptism and Welcome

Peter declares:

> "Surely no one can stand in the way of their being baptized with water. They have received the Holy Spirit just as we have." (Acts 10:47)

He orders their baptism and stays with them several days. The circle of God's kingdom widens. What was once unclean is now made clean. What was once excluded is now embraced.

---

## The Challenge to the Church

The promise to Abraham was that his descendants would become a blessing to all nations. This has been fulfilled. The gospel has reached beyond Judea to Samaria and now to the ends of the earth. Peter was sent to these Gentiles as surely as Jonah was sent to Nineveh—though Peter goes willingly where Jonah fled.

The question for us remains: Are we a church preoccupied with rearranging deck chairs and perfecting our internal programs? Or are we a community compelled by the gospel to go out, to reach the despised, to welcome the excluded, and to testify to the radical inclusion Christ has purchased?

We don't socially engineer what a church should look like. We simply tell others about Jesus—whoever will listen, whoever we can reach—and we welcome all who believe. This is the ever-widening circle of God's grace. This is why we exist.
