# Romans 7:1-25

**Title:** The Struggle with God
**Date:** 17 December 2012
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## The Law's Binding Power (7:1-6)

### Death Releases Us From Legal Obligation

Paul begins with a principle from everyday life: the law binds a person only as long as he lives. Death severs legal relationships.

He illustrates this through marriage. A woman is bound by law to her husband while he lives, but when he dies, she is released from the law of marriage. If she marries another man while her husband is alive, she becomes an adulteress. But if her husband dies, she is free to remarry without violating the law.

> Or do you not know, brothers—for I am speaking to those who know the law—that the law is binding on a person only as long as he lives? For a married woman is bound by law to her husband while he lives, but if her husband dies she is released from the law of marriage. Accordingly, she will be called an adulteress if she lives with another man while her husband is alive. But if her husband dies, she is free from that law, and if she marries another man she is not an adulteress. (7:1-3)

Marriage reflects God's design for covenant partnership. The law protects the family unit by making marriage a binding, lifelong commitment. In Scripture's view, marriage exists for the stable raising of children. This is not a romantic fantasy but a serious legal and spiritual obligation.

Notice that even the strongest earthly obligations—ones so serious that adultery once carried the death penalty—cease at death. Nothing in this life continues into heaven, where there are no more births or the raising of children. We become one family with God as our Father and Jesus as our brother.

### Applied to Our Relationship With the Law

The metaphor shifts in verse 4. Before, the husband died. Now, we die—but the principle is the same:

> Likewise, my brothers, you also have died to the law through the body of Christ, so that you may belong to another, to him who has been raised from the dead, in order that we may bear fruit for God. (7:4)

We have been released from our old legal relationship—not because the law is bad, but because we now belong to another: Christ himself. Like a widow freed from her first marriage to enter a new one, we are dead to the old covenant so we can bear fruit in a new relationship.

## The Law Is Good, Not Evil (7:7-12)

### The Critical Question

If we are dead to the law, does that mean the law itself is sinful? Paul emphatically rejects this:

> What then shall we say? That the law is sin? By no means! (7:7)

The law and sin are often mentioned together in Scripture—they seem inseparable. Sin uses the law. The law exposes sin. Yet they are not the same thing. Paul's conclusion is unmistakable:

> So the law is holy, and the commandment is holy and righteous and good. (7:12)

The law is God's revealed morality in history. It is the privilege and treasure of Israel. To say the law is evil is to say God's Word is evil. We cannot divide the Old Testament from the New Testament as though God changed his mind about goodness.

### The Law Reveals Sin

The law's first great purpose is to show us what sin is:

> Yet if it had not been for the law, I would not have known sin. For I would not have known what it is to covet if the law had not said, "You shall not covet." (7:7)

The tenth commandment addresses the heart: "Do not covet your neighbor's house, wife, servants, animals, or anything that belongs to him." But the moment the law speaks, sin springs to life within us. We become acutely aware of our desires.

This is like telling someone not to think of pink elephants. The prohibition itself creates the thought. The more we're told "don't think of it," the more impossible it becomes. So too with coveting: apart from the law, sin lies dormant and powerless. But the law awakens it.

> But sin, seizing an opportunity through the commandment, produced in me all kinds of covetousness. (7:8)

### The Law Promises Life But Sin Brings Death

There is a deeper dimension to this struggle. The law doesn't just reveal sin—it also promised life:

> I was once alive apart from the law, but when the commandment came, sin came alive and I died. The very commandment that promised life proved to be death to me. (7:9-10)

This echoes Adam's experience in the garden. God gave Adam a commandment that promised life: keep the command, and you remain in God's protective care, immortal in creaturely dependence. The command itself was good—it preserved Adam's position as creature before God.

But sin twisted that command. The serpent deceived Adam, making him desire what was forbidden—the knowledge of good and evil, the desire to be like God himself. The commandment that should have brought life brought death instead:

> For sin, seizing an opportunity through the commandment, deceived me and through it killed me. (7:11)

### An Illustration: The Infected Scalpel

Think of the law as a surgical scalpel, clean and good in itself. Sin is a tumor on our flesh that needs to be removed. The scalpel is meant to excise the tumor.

But imagine the scalpel is infected with bacteria. When the doctor uses it to cut away the tumor, the infection enters the wound. The good instrument, instead of healing us, makes things worse. The bacteria, which were powerless before the scalpel touched our bodies, now have an opportunity to spread. The law's very attempt to address sin gives sin a foothold.

This is why the law is holy and good, yet simultaneously exposes the problem: sin is the real culprit, not the law.

## The Real Problem: Sin, Not the Law (7:13-20)

### Sin Uses the Law Against Us

> Did that which is good, then, bring death to me? By no means! Rather it was sin, producing death in me through what is good, in order that sin might be shown to be sin, and through the commandment might become sinful beyond measure. (7:13)

The law's purpose is precisely to unmask sin for what it is. And what we discover is a profound internal conflict:

> For we know that the law is spiritual, but I am of the flesh, sold under sin. For I do not understand my own actions. For I do not do what I want, but I do the very thing I hate. (7:14-15)

The law is spiritual—it reflects God's character. But I am of the flesh—my fallen nature is enslaved to sin. There is a fundamental contradiction in my being: the things I want to do, I don't do. The things I hate, I do anyway. I don't understand myself.

### The Divided Self

Paul presents the divided state twice, with subtle progression:

**First reflection (verses 15-17):**
- I don't do what I want; I do what I hate
- If I'm doing what I don't want, I agree the law is good
- But it's not really "I" doing it—it's sin dwelling in me

**Second reflection (verses 18-20):**
- Nothing good dwells in my flesh
- I have the desire to do what is right, but not the ability
- I don't do the good I want; I do the evil I don't want
- Again: it's not "I" who do it, but sin dwelling in me

The repetition shows the persistence of this struggle. And notice the subtle shift: Paul moves from "I am sold under sin" to recognizing that "nothing good dwells in me." This is not an excuse-making move. Sin is a usurper, an indwelling reality that cannot be ignored while we remain in these bodies.

### Is This a Christian or Non-Christian?

There has been much debate in church history about whose experience Paul describes. Is this a believer or an unbeliever? Pre-conversion or post-conversion?

Some say it must be a non-Christian because:
- "I am of the flesh, sold under sin" (7:14)
- "With my flesh I serve the law of sin" (7:25)
- "Sin dwells in me" (7:17, 20)

How could a Christian say such things?

Others insist it must be a Christian because:
- "I delight in the law of God" (7:22)
- "Wretched man that I am! Who will deliver me?" (7:24)
- "Thanks be to God through Jesus Christ our Lord!" (7:25)

How could an unbeliever say such things?

The early church read this as describing the non-Christian. Augustine and the Reformers read it as the Christian's experience. I lean toward understanding this as Paul's present experience as a Christian.

Only a Christian can truly say the law is good. Only a Christian experiences the tense of verses 15 onward as present struggle, not past failure. And critically: only the Christian struggles in this way.

Martin Luther once saw a painting showing two groups of people before God. On one side stood the saints—calm, peaceful, serene. On the other side, the wicked—worried, anxious, tormented at judgment. Luther said, "Those are the Christians." The ones who struggled daily with sin, who trembled before God's holiness, who knew their need—those were the true believers. The so-called saints who were comfortable, proud of their heritage, oblivious to their sin—they were the deceived.

## The War Within (7:21-25)

### Two Laws at War

> So I find it to be a law that when I want to do right, evil lies close at hand. For I delight in the law of God, in my inner being, but I see in my members another law waging war against the law of my mind and making me captive to the law of sin that dwells in my members. (7:21-23)

Paul uses striking language to describe this internal conflict:
- Evil versus the law of right
- Inner being versus outward members
- Mind versus flesh
- The law of God versus the law of sin

He's not making precise distinctions between soul and body in the Greek sense. Rather, he's describing the daily reality of the Christian: there is war within us. We are pulled in two directions.

### The Cry of the Redeemed

> Wretched man that I am! Who will deliver me from this body of death? Thanks be to God through Jesus Christ our Lord! So then, I myself serve the law of God with my mind, but with my flesh I serve the law of sin. (7:24-25)

This is the cry of someone who has tasted redemption and knows what he ought to be—yet finds himself still captive to sin while in this body. It's the paradox of the Christian life: regenerate heart living in unredeemed flesh.

We are caught between Adam and Christ. We have a new nature that delights in God's law, yet we inhabit bodies still subject to death and sin. This is the "already but not yet" of Christian existence. God has raised us to spiritual life, given us a new birth, a new heart. But our bodies await redemption. We live "uneasily between the two," as the tension between verses 15 and 25 makes clear.

Three times Paul repeats: "Sin dwells in me" (7:17, 20, 13). This isn't excuse-making. It's an honest acknowledgment of the indwelling reality we cannot escape while in these mortal bodies. The Christian is the one bound by sin, waiting and sighing for the redemption of the body.

## Application: The Christian Struggle (8:1-2)

### Freedom From Condemnation

What does it mean to be dead to the law and dead to sin? The answer comes immediately:

> There is therefore now no condemnation for those who are in Christ Jesus. For the law of the Spirit of life has set you free in Christ Jesus from the law of sin and death. (8:1-2)

We are dead to the *penalty* of the law. We are dead to the *condemnation* of sin. There is no judgment hanging over us for our failures, no eternal sentence for our ongoing struggle.

This doesn't mean sin no longer dwells in us or that we no longer struggle. Rather, we have been given a remedy that the law alone could never provide. Returning to our illustration: the problem is not the scalpel (the law); the problem is the infection (sin). In this world of death, the scalpel alone cannot save us. What we need is antibiotics—treatment that works against the infection itself. Christ is our healing.

### From Legalism to New Life

We are freed from the law not to abandon it, but to serve in a new way:

> But now we are released from the law, having died to that which held us captive, so that we serve in the new way of the Spirit and not in the old way of the written code. (7:6)

We have died to solutions that cannot justify us before God—to religion as mere external ritual. We have died to legalism—that slavish attempt to calculate the minimum we must do to stay right with God, playing angles to avoid judgment.

Consider how easily we fall into legalism:
- **Dating:** "How far is too far?" We make rules and measure ourselves by them.
- **Giving:** "How much must I give to church?" We calculate obligation.
- **Holidays:** At Christmas, we shop to impress, buying expensive junk beautifully wrapped, all at once in a frantic glut, then wonder why our gifts feel empty.
- **Studying:** We focus on grades and marks rather than learning.

All of this is the "old way of the written code"—external obedience born from fear or duty, not love.

### Bearing Fruit Through the Spirit

Instead, we are to bear fruit in a new way:

> For while we were living in the flesh, our sinful passions, aroused by the law, were at work in our members to bear fruit for death. But now we are released from the law, having died to that which held us captive, so that we serve in the new way of the Spirit. (7:5-6)

Fruit born of the Spirit looks different:
- **Giving:** Give throughout the year. Give what is needed, not what impresses. Give your time. Buy someone lunch. Show small kindnesses.
- **Studying:** Learn for the love of knowledge itself, not for guilt or obligation.
- **Living:** Move not from contractual duty to your old self, but from grateful love for Christ, your new husband.

Good deeds flow from a renewed relationship, not from fear of judgment. This is bearing fruit.

## The Core Truth: Sin Is the Problem, Not the Law

Paul must guard against a subtle misunderstanding. All this talk of being "dead to the law" and free from legalism makes it sound as though the law itself is the enemy. It is not.

> The law can only show us the way; it cannot bring us there.

The law is a good instrument. It reveals God's will. It shows us what is right. But as a master—as the means of our salvation and transformation—it is powerless. It cannot produce obedience. It cannot transform our hearts. It exposes the problem without solving it.

Think of a study guide for an exam. The guide lists the topics and says, "You need to study ten hours on this material." The guide is good—it shows the way. But if you become enslaved to the number ten, you've missed the point. You might think, "If I read faster, can I do it in five hours?" Or you might rebel: "Five hours is ridiculous!" Or you might become scrupulously legalistic: "Exactly ten hours, not a minute more, not a minute less." Meanwhile, you've lost sight of the actual goal: to understand the material.

The number haunts you. The guide, meant to help, has become a burden.

So too with the law and Christmas gift-buying, dating boundaries, giving calculations—every area where we slip into legalism. The law itself is not the problem. The problem is our fleshly nature, our tendency to take what is good and turn it into a tool for pride, fear, or self-justification.

---

## Living in the Paradox

The Christian life is lived in paradox. We have two natures: a regenerate heart that delights in God's law, and an unredeemed body that groans under the weight of sin. We are linked to both Adam and Christ. We experience both condemnation and freedom simultaneously.

The Christian is the one who continues to struggle against sin—not because he is failing, but because he has been awakened to what sin is. He knows God's holiness. He wants to obey. And in that wanting, he discovers his own weakness.

This struggle is not a sign of spiritual immaturity. It is the mark of a genuine believer. It is the sign that you are spiritually alive, not that you are dying. The person who does not struggle, who feels no tension between his desires and God's law, has not yet truly seen himself or truly seen God.

But here is the paradox within the paradox: even as you struggle and fail, even as you find new sins to address just when you thought you'd conquered the old ones, you are forgiven. "There is therefore now no condemnation." Your progress is measured not by your victories but by how much less you slip backward. Like cleaning a mansion: you finish one room only to discover a whole wing awaits. This humility—this utter dependence on God's mercy—this is the mark of a true man or woman of God.

Keep struggling. Look forward to heaven, where you will receive new bodies and the war will end. Until then, know that your struggle itself is evidence of your salvation.
