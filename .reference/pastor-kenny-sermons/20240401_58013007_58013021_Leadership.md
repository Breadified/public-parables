## Introduction: Who Do You Remember?

I didn't grow up in a church, but my parents sent me to Sunday school for about six years. They would drop me off while they shopped, and later the church provided buses to ferry my sister and me back home. I never made close friends during those years—dropped off late, picked up on time—but there's one person I remember distinctly: **Miss Kwok**, the head Sunday school teacher.

She was a kindly, middle-aged unmarried woman who led in singing and prayer every single Sunday for those six years. Though I've since forgotten what she looks like and even her first name, and we lost touch when I migrated to Australia, she planted seeds that bore fruit decades later.

Years after leaving that Sunday school, a friend invited me to church and I read the Bible as an adult. Suddenly, all those Sunday school stories fit together. The stories of Noah's flood revealed God's judgment, the Easter egg hunts and songs about Jesus finally showed me why He died. It all made sense, and I committed my life to Christ.

This is what I want to explore today: **Do you remember your Sunday school leader? Your youth leader? An influential Christian person in your life?** Let me draw from Hebrews 13 some principles of Christian leadership.

> Remember your leaders, who spoke the word of God to you. Consider the outcome of their way of life and imitate their faith. (Hebrews 13:7)

This verse gives us our outline for today.

## 1. Leaders and the Word

The leaders we remember are those who **spoke the word of God**. What's critical here is not the personality of the leader, but the content they communicated. The Bible was taught, explained, and used to disciple others.

### The Message Never Changes

This matters profoundly because **Jesus Christ is the same yesterday and today and forever** (Hebrews 13:8). The word of God doesn't shift with circumstances. It's not one message yesterday, a different word today, and something else tomorrow.

> Your word, LORD, is eternal; it stands firm in the heavens. (Psalm 119:89)

> All people are like grass, and all their glory is like the flowers of the field; the grass withers and the flowers fall, but the word of the Lord endures forever. And this is the word that was preached to you. (1 Peter 1:24-25)

The unchanging nature of God's word is crucial precisely because **leaders come and go**. When Hebrews was written, we suspect the original leaders had been persecuted—perhaps even martyred for their faith. It was a time of great doubt. Some leaders may have recanted and fallen away. In such darkness, the church needed to remember: the word is the same. Persecution doesn't change it. The moral failings of leaders don't change it.

This echoes the Donatist controversy of church history—the question of whether a sacrament's validity depends on the priest's character. The answer is no. We don't judge teaching by the leader; we judge good leaders by what they teach.

### The Word Speaks Louder Than the Leader

> Do not be carried away by all kinds of strange teachings. (Hebrews 13:9)

Perhaps these were Judaistic teachings, calling the Hebrew Christians back to the Jewish religion rather than to Christ. Charismatic leaders—impressive leaders—might advocate for teachings that contradict God's word. Such people are false teachers, regardless of their appeal.

> It is good for our hearts to be strengthened by grace, not by eating ceremonial foods, which is of no benefit to those who do so. (Hebrews 13:9)

The false teachers may have been Judaisers insisting on temple ceremonial foods, attempting to keep people bound to the old system. But grace, not works, strengthens the heart.

In God's wisdom, He conveys His word through people. Others speak to us, shape us through the words of God. Yet we must remain vigilant:

> Then we will no longer be infants, tossed back and forth by the waves, and blown here and there by every wind of teaching and by the cunning and craftiness of people in their deceitful scheming. Instead, speaking the truth in love, we will grow to become in every respect the mature body of him who is the head, that is, Christ. (Ephesians 4:14-15)

### Everyone Leads, Everyone Follows

We are all leaders and being led simultaneously—formally or informally. Older to younger. Parent to child. Husband to wife. Employer to employee.

**When we are leading: speak the word of God.**

**When we are following: remember the word of God.**

This means engaging the word seriously. Read the Bible yourself and develop your own understanding. Read secondary sources—the great scholars and teachers who have stood the test of time, from the Reformation figures like Luther and Calvin to the early Church Fathers like Augustine. Read the primary sources in Greek and Hebrew to go deeper.

What we remember about our leaders is not how charismatic or good-looking they were, or what games they organized. **We remember that they spoke the word of God.**

## 2. Leaders and Character: Consider the Outcome

> Consider the outcome of their way of life and imitate their faith. (Hebrews 13:7)

"Outcome" carries the sense of trajectory—where did their life land? While secondary to the word of God itself, outcomes matter. A nutritionist who is morbidly obese, a financial advisor who is poor—there's a disconnect between message and life.

> If what a prophet proclaims in the name of the Lord does not take place or come true, that is a message the Lord has not spoken. That prophet has spoken presumptuously, so do not be alarmed. (Deuteronomy 18:21-22)

Similarly, there must be correlation between what one says and what one does. **Consider the outcome of their way of life.**

### Leaders Are Sacrificial

This is where the teaching becomes sharp. Look at what the text says:

> We have an altar from which those who minister at the tabernacle have no right to eat. The high priest carries the blood of animals into the Most Holy Place as a sin offering, but the bodies are burned outside the camp. (Hebrews 13:10-11)

There were two types of sacrifices in the temple system. For a general sin offering (sins against one another), the priests could eat. The Levites had no land of their own, so they relied on temple offerings for sustenance.

> The priest who offers it shall eat it; it is to be eaten in the sanctuary area, in the courtyard of the tent of meeting. (Leviticus 6:26)

But once a year, on the Day of Atonement, there was a special sin offering for sins against God itself. For this sacrifice:

> Any sin offering whose blood is brought into the tent of meeting to make atonement in the Holy Place must not be eaten; it must be burned up. (Leviticus 6:30)

The priest couldn't eat from this one. The practice illustrates what Jesus did:

> Jesus also suffered outside the city gate to make the people holy through his own blood. (Hebrews 13:12)

In Jesus' day, Jerusalem was pictured as the camp of Israel. Jesus was taken outside the gate, away from the camp, tortured and killed on a cross like a common criminal between two thieves at Golgotha. The inspired writer then turns to us:

> Let us, then, go to him outside the camp, bearing the disgrace he bore. For here we do not have an enduring city, but we are looking for the city that is to come. (Hebrews 13:13-14)

Jesus said something similar: **If anyone would come after me, let him deny himself and take up his cross daily and follow me. For whoever would save his life will lose it and whoever loses his life for my sake will save it.** (Luke 9:23) Being a Christian means following Jesus, and following Jesus means going with Him to Calvary—outside the camp.

### What "Outside the Camp" Means

The camp for Israel was a place of safety, sanctity, and comfort. Outside the camp lay potential danger—wild animals, enemies, and the burial places of unclean animals and refuse. Outside was unclean and risky. Inside was secure.

In this calling for every follower of Jesus: **we follow Jesus to death**. But leaders have one distinctive quality—they go first. They leave safety first. They take the risk first. They sacrifice themselves for others first. They eat last.

In contemporary terms, Simon Sinek describes how leaders create a safe environment by eating last. When a leader sacrifices for those around them, they trigger oxytocin—that neurochemical of safety and trust. But witnessing such generosity causes oxytocin release in others too. This creates a virtuous cycle: the more we witness sacrifice, the more we're inspired to sacrifice; the more we give, the safer and more cohesive our community becomes.

Leadership is not a rank or position. It's a choice. You can identify a true leader easily: when there's a problem no one wants to address, the leader says, "I will sacrifice to fix it." Not "someone should really fix that"—that's a consultant. A true leader acts.

Consider a dirty toilet at church. Some think it's embarrassing if a visitor sees it but do nothing. Some think someone should fix it. But the leader grabs toilet paper, cleans it, and solves the problem. The leader is a servant.

> Jesus called them together and said, "You know that those who are regarded as rulers of the Gentiles lord it over them, and their high officials exercise authority over them. Not so with you. Instead, whoever wants to become great among you must be your servant, and whoever wants to be first must be slave of all. For even the Son of Man did not come to be served, but to serve, and to give his life as a ransom for many." (Mark 10:42-45)

**And they eat last.**

What we see in verse 15 illustrates this:

> Through Jesus, therefore, let us continually offer to God a sacrifice of praise—the fruit of lips that openly profess his name. And do not forget to do good and to share with others, for with such sacrifices God is pleased. (Hebrews 13:15-16)

The "sacrifice of praise" isn't merely singing. It's the fruit of the lips—the fruit of the message leaders proclaim. But it must be matched with action: "do good and share with others." Leaders don't just talk the talk; they walk the walk.

This is the pattern Jesus Himself established: He saw a problem (humanity's sin), came to earth, underwent suffering and persecution, and ate last—He gave His life. We follow Him, and as leaders follow Jesus, others will imitate us as we follow Him.

## 3. Leaders Lead: Authority and Accountability

> Have confidence in your leaders and submit to their authority, because they keep watch over you as those who must give an account. Do this so that their work will be a joy, not a burden, for that would be of no benefit to you. (Hebrews 13:17)

"Have confidence" in some translations reads "obey." Authority must be earned—it's relational, not merely structural. It's built through:

- **The Word**: Speaking God's truth
- **Example**: Living out what is taught
- **Character**: The outcome of their way of life

Leadership authority flows from being under the Chief Shepherd:

> Now may the God of peace, who through the blood of the eternal covenant brought back from the dead our Lord Jesus, that great Shepherd of the sheep... (Hebrews 13:20)

Our flocks are servants, but servants of God—not servants of us. Leaders are accountable to the Chief Shepherd, not autonomous.

## Conclusion: The Seeds Bear Fruit

I've forgotten what Miss Kwok looks like. I've forgotten her first name. We lost touch decades ago when I moved to Australia. But she planted seeds. Six years of Sunday school stories, songs about Noah's judgment and Jesus' sacrifice—they were sown in my heart.

Years later, when my friend invited me to church and I finally read the Bible as an adult, those seeds sprouted. All those pieces fitted together. The stories made sense. The purpose of Jesus became clear. And I committed my life to Christ.

> Equip you with everything good for doing his will, and may he work in us what is pleasing to him, through Jesus Christ, to whom be glory for ever and ever. Amen. (Hebrews 13:21)

You have everything you need. Remember your leaders who spoke the word of God. Consider the outcome of their lives and imitate their faith. And as you lead, in whatever sphere, speak the word, live it out, and be willing to eat last.
