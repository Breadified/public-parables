# Hebrews 3:1–4:13

**Title:** Jesus is a Better Prophet
**Date:** December 5, 2008
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction: The Promise of Rest

We live in a culture of exhaustion. Whether you're cramming for exams, working multiple jobs, or juggling responsibilities at home, we all know that bone-deep weariness that makes you long to simply collapse on your favorite sofa. We crave rest—real rest, not just an evening off before the next wave of obligations.

Today we're exploring the rest that Jesus offers us. This isn't merely physical rest, though God certainly cares about our bodies. It's something far deeper and more permanent: the eternal rest that comes through faith in Jesus Christ.

## Keep Your Eyes on Jesus

The writer of Hebrews opens this passage with a simple command: "Keep your eyes on Jesus" (3:1). To understand why this matters, we need to grasp who Jesus is:

- **Apostle** — the one sent from God
- **High Priest** — our human mediator, our brother who represents us before God

The writer then gives us three compelling reasons to keep our eyes fixed on Jesus:

1. Jesus is greater than Moses
2. Israel missed out on the rest because of unbelief
3. The rest is still to come

## Reason 1: Jesus is Greater Than Moses

### The Builder and the House

> For Jesus has been found worthy of greater honor than Moses, just as the builder of a house has greater honor than the house itself. For every house is built by someone, but God is the builder of everything. (3:3–4)

This comparison uses two striking images. First, consider the relationship between a builder and what he builds. God is the ultimate builder—He created all things. Moses, magnificent as he is, is part of the created order. As Psalm 95 reminds us, God made the seas and formed the dry land. Moses and the people he led are part of God's building project, not its architects. Jesus, by contrast, is the builder Himself (as declared in 1:10). Therefore, Jesus has greater honor than Moses.

### The Servant and the Son

The second image cuts even deeper:

> Moses was faithful as a servant in all God's house, testifying to what would be said in the future. But Christ is faithful as a son over God's house. (3:5–6)

Here's the crucial distinction: both Moses and Jesus were faithful, but their roles were radically different.

A servant has access to every part of a house—including the parents' bedroom and bathroom—but only to clean and maintain. A son has access not to serve, but to live. He can jump on the bed, use the facilities, crawl in on Saturday mornings to cuddle with his parents. The difference between workplace and home. The difference between duty and belonging.

There's also a difference in responsibility. A servant completes his tasks and leaves. A son inherits the management of the entire household—a permanent, 24/7 responsibility. Jesus is faithful over more than Moses because the Son carries the weight of the house itself.

This comparison is especially striking because Moses is no small figure in Jewish tradition. He's the third most-quoted name in the Old and New Testament (after Jesus and David). Through Moses, God delivered Israel from Egypt, constituted them as a nation, and gave them the Law. In Jewish estimation, Moses stands almost without peer. Numbers 12:6–8 records God saying of Moses:

> "When a prophet of the Lord is among you, I reveal myself to him in visions, I speak to him in dreams. But this is not true of my servant Moses; he is faithful in all my house. With him I speak face to face, clearly and not in riddles; he sees the form of the Lord."

To say that Jesus has even greater honor than Moses is to make an extraordinary claim.

### The Application of Belonging

Here's where the passage becomes personal:

> And we are his house, if we hold on to our courage and the hope of which we boast. (3:6b)

Notice the conditional: *if*. This is not automatic. You don't inherit the privileges of sonship by accident. You must hold on to your confidence and hope. You must remain in the house.

If you belong to this house as a son or daughter of God, you have access to everything Jesus has—the same intimacy, the same security, the same sense of home. But only if you're truly in the house. Only if you hold fast to your faith.

## Reason 2: Israel Missed Out on the Rest

### The Warning from History

To illustrate what happens when people fail to hold on to their faith, the writer points us to Israel's wilderness wanderings—a story his Hebrew audience knew well. Psalm 95 captures the warning:

> So, as the Holy Spirit says: "Today, if you hear his voice, do not harden your hearts as you did in the rebellion, during the time of testing in the desert, where your fathers tested and tried me and for forty years saw what I did. That is why I was angry with that generation, and I said, 'Their hearts are always going astray, and they have not known my ways.' So I declared on oath in my anger, 'They shall never enter my rest.'" (3:7–11)

### What Happened in the Desert

The story is tragic. After God delivered Israel from Egypt and established His covenant at Mount Sinai, the people began their journey toward the Promised Land. They reached its very edge, a place where they could finally rest from slavery and hardship.

But something went terribly wrong.

When scouts returned from the land, reporting the presence of other tribes and fortified cities, fear gripped the people. Despite God's explicit promise that He—the one who defeated mighty Egypt—would also defeat the Canaanites, the Israelites panicked. They couldn't trust what God had promised.

This moment of unbelief was actually the climax of an entire journey filled with grumbling and discontentment. Reading through Numbers is like reading a catalog of complaints:

- Not enough bread
- Not enough water
- Not enough meat
- "It was better in Egypt"
- Too many warriors
- Too many obstacles

At every step, the people questioned God's care. They hardened their hearts. They tested God repeatedly. They refused to believe His promises.

And so God made a decision:

> "That is why I was angry with that generation, and I said, 'Their hearts are always going astray, and they have not known my ways.' So I declared on oath in my anger, 'They shall never enter my rest.'" (3:10–11)

The entire generation that complained—except for Joshua and Caleb, who alone believed—wandered in the wilderness for forty years. They never entered the Promised Land. They never found rest from their wandering, their work, their homelessness.

### The Application: Don't Harden Your Heart

> See to it, brothers, that none of you has a sinful, unbelieving heart that turns away from the living God. (3:12)

The warning is clear: faithlessness is dangerous. When we grumble against God's work in our lives, when we complain about the cost of following Jesus, when we murmur about persecution or the difficulty of obedience, we're walking the same path as Israel. We're hardening our hearts.

But we're also called to active encouragement:

> But encourage one another daily, as long as it is called Today, so that none of you may be hardened by sin's deceitfulness. We have come to share in Christ if we hold firmly till the end the confidence we had at first. (3:13–14)

Notice what authentic encouragement looks like: it's not mere positive affirmation or surface-level cheerleading. It's pointing one another to Jesus. It's reminding each other of His faithfulness when circumstances tempt us to doubt.

**Encouragement means giving courage**—the courage to keep following Jesus even when it's hard, even when the path is unclear, even when others have given up.

This happens everywhere: in informal conversations with Christian friends, in church gatherings, in small groups, at home around the table, even in a simple prayer before a meal. These aren't complicated spiritual practices; they're simply moments where we remind one another that God is faithful and the race continues.

The opposite of grumbling isn't silence; it's encouragement. The antidote to deception isn't isolation; it's community speaking truth. Every day—today—we have the opportunity to strengthen each other's confidence in Jesus.

## Reason 3: The Rest Is Still to Come

### A Promise Still Outstanding

> Therefore, since the promise of entering his rest still stands, let us be careful that none of you be found to have fallen short of it. For we also have had the gospel preached to us, just as they did; but the message they heard was of no value to them, because those who heard did not combine it with faith. (4:1–2)

Here's something puzzling: the writer says the promise of entering God's rest still stands. But didn't Israel eventually enter the Promised Land? Under Joshua, they conquered Canaan. Under David, they established a kingdom. So in what sense is the rest still outstanding?

The answer is that the Promised Land was never really the final rest God intended.

### Understanding God's Rest

Consider this carefully:

> Now we who have believed enter that rest, just as God has said, "So I declared on oath in my anger, 'They shall never enter my rest.'" And yet his work has been finished since the creation of the world. For somewhere he has spoken about the seventh day in these words: "And on the seventh day God rested from all his work." And again in the passage above he says, "They shall never enter my rest." (4:3–5)

The writer is reframing what "rest" actually means. When God created the world, He rested on the seventh day—not because He was exhausted, but because His creative work was complete. This is the pattern: genuine rest comes when the work is finished and you can simply be, simply exist in the goodness of what has been accomplished.

The Promised Land was a shadow, a picture of something greater. The land itself wasn't the ultimate rest. The ultimate rest is eschatological—it belongs to the future, to heaven, to the new creation.

### Why the Land Wasn't the Final Rest

Notice how the writer demonstrates this:

> It still remains that some will enter that rest, and those who formerly had the gospel preached to them did not go in because of their disobedience. Therefore God again set a certain day, calling it "Today," when a long time later he spoke through David, as was said before: "Today, if you hear his voice, do not harden your hearts." For if Joshua had given them rest, God would not have spoken later about another day. (4:6–8)

If the Promised Land were the final rest, why would God still be speaking about "another day" through David? The fact that centuries after Joshua conquered the land, God was still calling people to enter His rest proves that the land wasn't the ultimate fulfillment. There's a greater rest still to come.

### A Brief Note on the Sabbath

The Jewish Sabbath—the seventh day of rest—was connected to God's pattern of creation and the promise of the Promised Land. But with the coming of Jesus, something fundamental has changed.

Jesus is greater than Moses. Jesus is greater than Joshua. Jesus is greater than the ritual Sabbath itself. The ceremonial laws that governed Israel—including the observance of specific days and rituals—have been fulfilled and transcended in Christ.

This doesn't mean rest is abolished; rather, it's been reoriented. We don't need to observe Saturday as a special holy day, because Jesus Himself is our rest. The Christian life isn't bound to ritual days; instead, every day is "Today"—an opportunity to draw near to God through faith in Christ.

We establish our own rhythms of rest, worship, and community not because they're commanded, but because they help us draw closer to Jesus and encourage one another toward the finish line.

### The Rest We Enter Now and the Rest Still to Come

Here's the beautiful tension: in one sense, we who believe have already entered God's rest. We've ceased from our own works of self-righteousness and self-salvation, trusting instead in what Jesus has done. But in another sense, we haven't yet experienced the fullness of that rest. We don't yet sit in heaven; we're still on the pilgrimage.

> There remains, then, a Sabbath-rest for the people of God; for anyone who enters God's rest also rests from his own work, just as God did from his. (4:9–10)

God finished His work of creation. Jesus finished His work of redemption at the cross. One day, we'll finish our work and rest in His presence forever. Until then, we must press on.

## Make Every Effort to Enter That Rest

> Let us, therefore, make every effort to enter that rest, so that no one will fall by following their example of disobedience. (4:11)

This is the crucial application. "Make every effort" doesn't mean earning salvation through striving. Rather, it means ordering your life around the pursuit of Jesus and the hope of His rest.

This has profound practical implications:

**In relationships:** If a relationship—romantic or otherwise—isn't drawing you closer to Jesus but pulling you away, you must have the courage to step back. Why would you invest daily life energy in something that undermines your faith? Marriage is the everyday; it will either build you up daily or gradually drag you away. Choose wisely.

**In work and study:** Different careers and educational paths demand different energies. If a pursuit is consuming so much time and emotional energy that you can't maintain your faith, can't be part of a faith community, can't be encouraged by Christian friends, then it's not worth it. Make every effort to pursue what builds your faith.

**In spiritual disciplines:** If attending church, Bible study, prayer groups, or informal Christian fellowship helps you stay faithful, make every effort to be there. If smaller, intimate gatherings with Christian friends strengthen your walk, prioritize them. Set a schedule, establish priorities, and protect them.

**In specific sins:** If you know something is wrong—piracy, deception, substance abuse, sexual compromise—stop it today. Don't risk it. Don't rationalize it. The temptation to give up is powerful, but the finishing line is near.

## Keep Your Eyes Fixed on Jesus

The Christian life is a difficult race. It's like swimming backstroke without lane markers—you must trust the direction you're pointed. It's like running without knowing when the finish line will appear, sustained only by voices from the sidelines saying, "Keep going. The end is near."

Consider Steven Bradbury, who won gold in short-track speed skating at the 2002 Winter Olympics. Throughout the competition, he was not the fastest skater. In the quarterfinals, he advanced only because another skater was disqualified. In the semifinals, he was nowhere near the lead but advanced because others crashed. In the finals, he was coming from the back of the pack when everyone in front of him collided, leaving him to cross first.

After the race, Bradbury said something profound: "Obviously I wasn't the fastest skater. I don't think I'll take the medal as the minute-and-a-half of the race I actually won. I'll take it as the last decade of the hard slog I put in."

He'd trained for a decade through injuries—skaters' blades had cut through his leg, costing him four liters of blood; he'd broken his neck. But he kept showing up. He kept training. He didn't give up when victory seemed impossible.

That's the Christian life. You might not be the fastest or the flashiest. You might feel like you're coming from behind. But if you keep your eyes fixed on Jesus—the rock that doesn't move—and refuse to waver, you will finish. What matters is who's there at the finish line, not how fast you're going during the race.

## The Power of God's Word

As we encourage one another, we must be rooted in Scripture itself. Don't settle for mere human wisdom or positive thinking. Ground your encouragement in God's Word.

> For the word of God is living and active. Sharper than any double-edged sword, it penetrates even to dividing soul and spirit, joints and marrow; it judges the thoughts and attitudes of the heart. Nothing in all creation is hidden from God's sight. Everything is uncovered and laid bare before the eyes of him to whom we must give account. (4:12–13)

God's Word doesn't work through spectacular healings or amazing feats. It works by changing your mind, by persuading you that God's way is better than our own. It exposes the hidden places of our hearts, just as God did in the Garden of Eden. Words are powerful things. The Word of God is the most powerful of all.

## Conclusion: Your Choice Today

You may be tired. You may be weary from the struggles of life. If so, take a break. Rest your body. God cares about your physical well-being.

But don't confuse temporal rest with the eternal rest Jesus offers.

The rest that matters most—the rest that will satisfy your deepest longings—can only come through Jesus. It cannot be bought. It cannot be earned. It can only be entered through faith, held onto through perseverance, and anticipated with hope.

Jesus is the apostle and high priest. He alone is sufficient.

So keep your eyes on Jesus. Not on circumstances. Not on other people's pace. Not on how far you've come or how far you have to go. Fix your gaze on the one who has already finished the work—both of creation and of redemption.

Repent of unbelief. Take up the race again. And do it today.
