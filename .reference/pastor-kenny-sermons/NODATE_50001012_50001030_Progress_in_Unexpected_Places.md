# Philippians 1:12-30

**Title:** Progress in Unexpected Places
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## When Progress Doesn't Look Like Progress

Have you ever felt stuck? You thought you'd be further along by now—in the top maths class, or your family would have moved to the area you wanted, or you'd have gotten that new computer. We all measure progress by certain markers, and when we don't hit them, it feels like we're standing still.

Consider Russia in 1812. Napoleon, at the height of his power with virtually all of continental Europe under his control, invaded with an army of 400,000—the largest ever assembled in European history at that time. Russia, by contrast, was disorganized and weak, with only 175,000 soldiers. As Napoleon won every battle, the Russian army retreated again and again toward Moscow. By every measure of military success, Russia was losing.

Paul found himself in a similar situation. He was stuck in Rome, imprisoned and unable to advance in the ways the world measures success. No freedom, no career progression, no social standing—just a prison cell and a few letters smuggled out.

Yet in this very context, Paul invites us to consider a different measure of progress.

## The Gospel's Progress

> Now I want you to know, brothers, that what has happened to me has really served to advance the gospel. As a result, it has become clear throughout the whole palace guard and to everyone else that I am in chains for Christ. Because of my chains, most of the brothers in the Lord have been encouraged to speak the word of God more courageously and fearlessly. (Philippians 1:12-14)

What looked like a setback was actually progress—progress of the gospel, the good news about Christ's love for us. How did this happen?

- Through Paul speaking boldly to the palace guards (v. 13)
- Through others being encouraged to follow his example of boldness (v. 14)

Paul's life priority was radically different from ours. He wasn't interested in career success, material wealth, a prestigious home, or even getting into a selective school. He believed the gospel—that Jesus was raised from the dead and will one day return to judge—was so important that people everywhere needed to hear it. He traveled wherever he could, by whatever means, and preached Christ, even when it landed him in jail.

He was so serious about gospel progress that he could say:

> For to me, to live is Christ and to die is gain. (Philippians 1:21)

This wasn't suicidal despair. Paul clarifies later that he knows he will remain and continue with the Philippians (v. 25). Rather, he's saying that his entire reason for living is the proclamation of Christ. To suffer for that cause is acceptable to him; his life has found its ultimate purpose.

## What Progress Really Means

Think about how the world measures progress: learn your ABCs and numbers, get into an advanced class, make it to a selective school, then university, then a career, then a family, a nice car, a nice house. These are the markers we've been taught to pursue.

But God's values are different. He places you where you are for a reason—and that reason is the progress of His gospel.

Speaking to different groups in the school:

**For those progressing as you hoped:** You're here for gospel progress. This doesn't mean neglecting your studies—work hard. But make time for Christian activities. Join Soul Purpose. Pray. Invite friends. Tell them about Jesus. Don't let other activities crowd out time for Christ. When special talks come (especially those geared toward people who don't know Jesus), commit to attending and bring friends.

**For those feeling imprisoned:** If school feels like a prison—your second choice, or somewhere you're sick of being—Christ gives you another reason to live. You can rejoice like Paul, even in difficult circumstances. You have something to live for: the progress of the gospel. That's a purpose that transcends your situation.

**For those who've labored faithfully with little fruit:** I'm thinking especially of those who've been faithful to Soul Purpose for years, who help lead each week, who pray and talk to friends about Jesus, yet see little result. You're rejected. You're overlooked. You're discouraged. But keep going.

## Victory in Apparent Defeat

Here's what happened with Russia: they lost the battles but won the war.

Every time the French won, they advanced deeper into Russia's vast territory, stretching their supply lines impossibly thin. The endless winter, starvation, disease, and desertion devastated the French army more than any battlefield ever could. Without grass, there were no horses. Without horses, there was no cavalry and no way to transport cannons or supplies. The French army collapsed from within.

When the French finally reached Moscow, they found it evacuated and its supplies burned. They broke into buildings for shelter, but their small fires grew into massive ones—and with Moscow built largely of wood and no efficient fire department, four-fifths of the city burned to the ground. The French had nowhere to shelter and faced a Russian counterattack.

Half of Napoleon's original 400,000 were lost before even reaching Moscow. The other half were lost on the retreat through the Russian winter. Only about 10% of the original army returned. The Russians won their victory through apparent defeat.

## Living for Gospel Progress

> Whatever happens, conduct yourselves in a manner worthy of the gospel of Christ. Then, whether I come and see you or only hear about you in my absence, I will know that you stand firm in one spirit, contending as one man for the faith of the gospel without being frightened in any way by those who oppose you. This is a sign to them that they will be destroyed, but that you will be saved—and that by God. For it has been granted to you on behalf of Christ not only to believe on him, but also to suffer for him, since you are going through the same struggle you saw I had, and now hear that I still have. (Philippians 1:27-30)

Paul's circumstances don't look like progress at all. He's in a Roman prison. Yet the gospel advanced. Suffering came, but so did an opportunity to witness. Opposition came, but so did courage in unlikely places.

In a world that judges success by external measures—wealth, status, comfort, achievement—the Christian is called to a different standard. We measure progress by how many hear about Christ, how many are encouraged to boldness, how many find their purpose in His kingdom rather than in the world's markers.

Sometimes what looks like loss is actually victory. Keep advancing.
