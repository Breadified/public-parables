# Luke 17:7-19

**Title:** Adulting: Persevering  
**Date:** 26 September 2020  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## God Does Not Owe Us

Jesus tells a parable about two characters: a master and his servant. The servant has worked a grueling day in the field—plowing the soil, turning over its upper layer to bring fresh nutrients to the surface while burying weeds, or tending sheep across miles of terrain. Under the Middle Eastern sun, exhausted with sore muscles, blisters, and bloodied hands, the servant naturally expects rest.

> Suppose one of you has a servant plowing or looking after the sheep. Will he say to the servant when he comes in from the field, 'Come along now and sit down to eat'? (Luke 17:7)

But the master's response reveals a hard truth:

> Won't he rather say, 'Prepare my supper, get yourself ready and wait on me while I eat and drink; after that you may eat and drink'? (Luke 17:8)

The work is not finished. The master has not yet eaten. The servant must prepare the meal and continue serving—only after the master is satisfied can the servant finally rest and eat. This is not extra work deserving commendation or gratitude.

> Will he thank the servant because he did what he was told to do? (Luke 17:9)

The answer is no. The servant has merely done his duty—nothing more.

> So you also, when you have done everything you were told to do, should say, 'We are unworthy servants; we have only done our duty.' (Luke 17:10)

### The Entitlement Trap

Our generation struggles with entitlement. Our parents arrived overseas with a single suitcase and worked for everything they built. We, by contrast, want everything without effort. We speak of "entitlements"—welfare, education, housing, comfort—as if they were owed to us. But entitlement is a right to something we have not earned, something that is not legitimately ours.

This manifests as "first world problems"—complaints that reveal how much we take for granted:
- Forgotten earphones at university
- Too many seeds in a watermelon
- Forced to watch Netflix in standard definition
- A slurpee that is too cold
- A gardener mowing the lawn during sleep time
- A house so large it requires two routers

These problems exist only because 99% of the world lacks the luxuries we treat as obligations.

As Jon Krakauer writes in *Into the Wild*: "It is easy, when you are young, to believe that what you desire is no less than what you deserve, to assume that if you want something badly enough, it is your God-given right to have it."

We extend this entitlement to God. We believe He owes us a beautiful spouse, health, wealth, a particular dream or ambition. When we don't receive what we demand, we rebel—skipping church after poor exam results, punishing God for circumstances beyond our control. We bite off our nose to spite our face, hurting ourselves most of all.

The parable invites us to compare ourselves to the servant and the master to God. We owe God our being. He created us for a purpose: to serve one another and to serve Him.

> You are worthy, our Lord and God, to receive glory and honor and power, for you created all things, and by your will they were created and have their being. (Revelation 4:11)

The question is not what God has done for us, but what have we done for God? Christ has died to redeem us. Will we respond in service to Him? And when we do, we have only done our duty.

### Understanding Duty

We hate the word duty. It sounds forced, coerced, joyless—the opposite of genuine love. But this understanding is false.

When a mother presses forward despite exhaustion to care for her child rather than neglect him, she does her duty. There is profound love in this sacrifice. When a spouse offers charity in darkest moments, they do their duty through love. When a soldier marches into battle despite fear, driven by love for country and family, he does his duty.

Duty is not something forced upon us from without. It is self-discipline—the act of overruling our weakness and temptation to do what is right. Duty is when we are tempted to run away but we stay and love anyway. It is the replacement of fear and sadness with joy, trust, and anticipation.

Jesus Himself did His duty. He walked toward the cross, not because He desired it emotionally, but because love compelled Him to do what was right.

Duty is what we practice when we are thankful.

## Being Thankful

Jesus travels toward Jerusalem along the border between Samaria and Galilee. Ten men with leprosy meet Him, standing at a distance—a disease that numbed nerve endings so completely that victims could injure themselves without feeling pain. The ancient world believed leprosy highly contagious, so these men lived in desert exile, separated from civilization.

> As he was going into a village, ten men who had leprosy met him. They stood at a distance and called out in a loud voice, 'Jesus, Master, have pity on us!' (Luke 17:12-13)

Their existence is cruel and isolating. They cry out: have pity on us.

Jesus shows compassion:

> When he saw them, he said, 'Go, show yourselves to the priests.' (Luke 17:14)

This seems like dismissal, but it is profound mercy. According to the law in Leviticus, the priest examines the healed person and performs a cleansing ceremony to confirm the healing and restore them to the community. Jesus sends them to receive this confirmation and acceptance.

> One of them, when he saw he was healed, came back, praising God in a loud voice. He threw himself at Jesus' feet and thanked him—and he was a Samaritan. (Luke 17:15-16)

But only one returned. Only one gave thanks. Only one recognized what had happened to him. Jesus questions this silence:

> Were not all ten cleansed? Where are the other nine? Has no one returned to give praise to God except this foreigner? (Luke 17:17-18)

There is a profound wordplay here. "Judah" means "praise the Lord." Jesus asks where those of Judah are—the ones who should be praising. The answer is striking: it is the Samaritan, the foreigner, the outsider who gives praise. As Paul writes:

> A person is not a Jew who is one only outwardly, nor is circumcision merely outward and physical. No, a person is a Jew who is one inwardly; and circumcision is circumcision of the heart, by the Spirit, not by the written code. Such a person's praise is not from other people, but from God. (Romans 2:28-29)

The outsider is the true insider. The foreigner is the true believer. He alone recognizes his healing as gift rather than entitlement.

> Then he said to him, 'Rise and go; your faith has made you well.' (Luke 17:19)

### Thankfulness: The Antidote to Entitlement

Thankfulness cures entitlement. We must count our blessings and recognize how fortunate we are. Migrants understand this better than most. They have seen firsthand what hard work and perseverance can accomplish. Compare your opportunities to those still in the home country, facing limited prospects. How can we feel entitled when we have been given so much?

This generation witnessed their parents build everything from nothing. Some choose to denigrate this country's history, forgetting that it welcomed migrants, opened its schools and suburbs to those willing to work. Be proud of what has been given to you. Do not let others weaponize your gratitude against you.

Thankfulness is the motivation to do our duty. It drives the work ethic we see in our students. Why do they study so hard? Not primarily from external coercion, but from gratitude. My parents have given me this opportunity. I will not waste it. They have invested in my tutoring. I will honor them.

The Queen, despite personal challenges, did not abdicate. She was never meant to be Queen. Her uncle chose scandal and romance over duty, disqualifying himself from the throne. He was an entitled, spoiled man who dropped the baton when it came to him. Her father, King George, despite his own fears, picked it up. That baton passed to the Queen.

Unlike most European monarchies, she was anointed during her coronation—much like a wedding before the Lord. She made a covenant with God to serve Him and her country for all her days.

She will do her duty.

## Living Out Our Calling

We are a spoiled generation. We get tired, lose motivation, lose steam. During difficult seasons, we rest, forgetting that the baton has been passed to us. It is not a garment to be discarded when joy fades. Instead, we do our duty.

Well done, good and faithful servant. We will say: "We have but done our duty."

## Prayer

Heavenly Father, we thank you for all you have given us, especially your mercy shown in Jesus' death for us.
