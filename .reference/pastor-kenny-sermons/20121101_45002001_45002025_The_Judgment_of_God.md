# Romans 2:1-25

**Title:** The Judgment of God
**Date:** 11 November 2012
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## God's Universal Principle: Performance and Judgment

> Therefore you have no excuse, O man, every one of you who judges. For in passing judgment on another you condemn yourself, because you, the judge, practice the very same things. (2:1)

Paul opens with a striking indictment: we cannot escape God's judgment by condemning others when we ourselves commit the same sins. This is not primarily about hypocrisy, but about a fundamental misunderstanding of how God's judgment operates.

> We know that the judgment of God rightly falls on those who practice such things. Do you suppose, O man—you who judge those who practice such things and yet do them yourself—that you will escape the judgment of God? (2:2-3)

The rhetorical force is devastating. Those who judge others while doing the same things cannot escape judgment. There is an expectation of incongruency in the minds of Paul's audience—especially the Jews, who believed their covenant status exempted them from the principle of judgment based on obedience.

> Or do you presume on the riches of his kindness and forbearance and patience, not knowing that God's kindness is meant to lead you to repentance? But because of your hard and impenitent heart you are storing up wrath for yourself on the day of wrath when God's righteous judgment will be revealed. (2:4-5)

God's patience and mercy are not permission to sin—they are invitations to repentance. Those who harden their hearts against God's kindness are actually storing up judgment for themselves.

## The Principle Stated: Judgment According to Works

> He will render to each one according to his works: to those who by patience in well-doing seek for glory and honor and immortality, he will give eternal life; but for those who are self-seeking and do not obey the truth, but obey unrighteousness, there will be wrath and fury. (2:6-8)

God's judgment is congruent—aligned perfectly with performance. Those who persist in doing good receive eternal life; those who pursue unrighteousness receive wrath.

> There will be tribulation and distress for every human being who does evil, the Jew first and also the Greek, but glory and honor and peace for everyone who does good, the Jew first and also the Greek. For God shows no partiality. (2:9-11)

The principle applies universally without exception. God's impartiality means that ethnicity, covenant status, or religious identity offers no exemption from this standard.

> For all who have sinned without the law will also perish without the law, and all who have sinned under the law will be judged by the law. For it is not the hearers of the law who are righteous before God, but the doers of the law who will be justified. (2:12-13)

The measure by which you are judged is the revelation you have received. Knowing the law without obeying it provides no advantage. Only doing the law brings justification.

## Application: The Gentiles Who Know God's Principle

> For when Gentiles, who do not have the law, by nature do what the law requires, they are a law to themselves, even though they do not have the law. They show that the work of the law is written on their hearts, while their conscience also bears witness, and their conflicting thoughts accuse or even excuse them on that day when, according to my gospel, God judges the secrets of men by Christ Jesus. (2:14-16)

Even those without the written law demonstrate that God's moral principle is inscribed on human conscience. Gentile believers and God-fearers who obey the law's demands—though they lack the law itself—prove that the Spirit's work in their hearts fulfills what the law requires. Their conscience bears witness; their internal moral struggle shows the law written on their hearts.

## Application: The Jews Who Took God's Law for Granted

> But if you call yourself a Jew and rely on the law and boast in God and know his will and approve what is excellent, because you are instructed from the law; and if you are sure that you yourself are a guide to the blind, a light to those who are in darkness, an instructor of the foolish, a teacher of children, having in the law the embodiment of knowledge and truth—you then who teach others, do you not teach yourself? (2:17-21)

Paul now addresses the Jews directly. They have received tremendous privilege: instruction in God's law, understanding of His will, and the responsibility to teach others. Yet with biting irony, he asks: if you teach others, do you teach yourself?

> While you preach against stealing, do you steal? You who say that one must not commit adultery, do you commit adultery? You who abhor idols, do you rob temples? You who boast in the law dishonor God by breaking the law. (2:21-23)

The contradiction is glaring. To break the very law you claim to represent is to dishonor God Himself. Your actions contradict your words.

> For, as it is written, "The name of God is blasphemed among the Gentiles because of you." (2:24)

This hits at the heart of the matter. When God's people break God's law while claiming to represent Him, the Gentiles mock God Himself. Your unfaithfulness damages God's reputation among those who don't know Him.

## The Heart of the Matter: True Identity in God

> For circumcision indeed is of value if you obey the law, but if you break the law, your circumcision becomes uncircumcision. (2:25)

The outward sign means nothing without obedience. Circumcision—the mark of the covenant people—loses its meaning if the law is broken.

> So, if a man who is uncircumcised keeps the precepts of the law, will not his uncircumcision be regarded as circumcision? Then he who is physically uncircumcised but keeps the law will condemn you who have the written code and circumcision but break the law. (2:26-27)

An astounding reversal: the uncircumcised person who keeps the law is more truly part of God's covenant people than the circumcised person who breaks it. Obedience, not ethnicity, determines true membership in God's family.

> For no one is a Jew who is merely one outwardly, nor is circumcision outward and physical. But a Jew is one inwardly, and circumcision is a matter of the heart, by the Spirit, not by the letter. His praise is not from man but from God. (2:28-29)

True identity as God's people is inward, spiritual, and internal. It is not conferred by external markers or human recognition, but by God's own assessment. God alone determines who truly belongs to His covenant family—and that determination is based on the circumcision of the heart, wrought by the Spirit, not by the letter of the law.
