# Numbers 1:1-2 | Luke 15:3-4 | Acts 2:40-41

**Title:** Numbers Matter: Growth, Godliness, and Kingdom Accountability
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Census: God Counts What Matters

God commanded Moses to take a census of Israel—to count every person by name. This wasn't mere bureaucracy. The God of heaven deemed it important to know exactly how many had been redeemed from Egypt. Later, in Numbers 26, He would do it again. And in Acts, when three thousand responded to Peter's call, Luke recorded the exact number. The pattern is clear: **God counts.**

In the parable of the lost sheep, Jesus teaches that even one matters. A shepherd leaves ninety-nine to search for the single lost one. To Jesus, numbers represent real people—each infinitely valuable.

## We've Been Trained to Play the Numbers Game

Our entire culture trains us to measure what matters by counting:
- **Education:** Thirteen years of schooling reduced to HSC scores and university rankings
- **Career:** Resume metrics—job titles, salary brackets, years of experience
- **Money:** How we keep score, measure progress, demonstrate success
- **Lifestyle:** Collecting experiences (countries visited before age twenty-five)

If we apply quantitative measures to trivial pursuits, how much more should we measure progress in what truly matters?

## Where Numbers Reveal Our Priorities

Consider what numerical accountability looks like in various domains:

**In worldly pursuits:** We obsessively track wealth, status, and achievement.

**In the Kingdom of God:** We often dismiss the importance of numbers entirely.

This contradiction suggests our priorities may be misaligned. If we measure progress in holidays and net worth but not in Kingdom growth—in disciples made, lives transformed, churches planted, communities impacted—perhaps we need to examine what we actually believe matters.

## The Uncomfortable Question: Are We Being Faithful?

When we claim "God is sovereign" in response to decline, we're often making an excuse. Yes, God is sovereign. But we also have agency. The consequences of our choices and inactions are real.

**Some hard truths:**
- If all our children fall away, we cannot blame God alone. What was our example? Our investment?
- When we control everything and lose to a small minority, something has gone wrong.
- When we silence ourselves out of fear—fear of government, fear of disagreement, fear of persecution—we've abandoned faithfulness.
- When the church becomes indistinguishable from "slow progressivism," is that faithfulness?

We are reaping the fruit of boomer complacency. We can either continue down that path or change course.

## Godliness Should Produce Growth

This isn't to say godliness doesn't matter—it absolutely does. And circumstances vary; sometimes people face real barriers and setbacks through no fault of their own.

But all things being equal, genuine godliness should translate into growth: growth in maturity, in discipleship, in Kingdom impact. Numbers become a symbol of spiritual health, not an end in themselves.

## Theology: Living Out Scripture Beyond the Text

The Bible contains sixty-six books. It doesn't address electricity, holidays, or even explicitly define the Trinity. Yet its impact cannot be limited to what we can proof-text directly.

In the nineteenth century, German scholars attempted to go "beyond the Bible," resulting in destructive theologies that led to two world wars and fractured Christendom. The pendulum swung the other direction: "Only what the Bible explicitly says."

But this creates impoverishment. **Theology is Scripture applied to life through logic, observation, and tradition.**

Consider the Trinity: The Father is called God. The Son is spoken of as God and acts with divine authority. The Holy Spirit is identified as God. Yet we're one God in three persons. Scripture alone doesn't explain the mechanism—theology does, by faithful reasoning.

Similarly, while the Bible doesn't prescribe specific holiday practices, it speaks extensively about living for pleasure versus being good stewards. We look at Scripture, apply logic, observe the world's approach, and witness the effects on the church. From this integrated wisdom, we develop sound theology.

**The same applies to marriage.** Scripture points toward the purpose of bearing and raising children. Our theology of marriage isn't isolated proof-texts but Scripture integrated with the reality of human flourishing.

## Speaking to Life as It Unfolds

When a sermon is preached, what should be spoken? **What people are actually facing.**

Throughout a church's history, the pressing issues shift:

- A season focused on welcome and hospitality
- A season emphasizing evangelism and Bible reading
- A season confronting the idolatry of academic achievement (HSC worship)
- A season breaking the habit of virtue signaling in church (leading to real serving teams)
- A season addressing prolonged singleness and the refusal to grow up (pointing toward marriage)
- A season now addressing married believers who haven't matured into parenthood and family responsibility

As we mature, our spiritual focus must scale from the personal (Bible reading, personal godliness, one-on-one evangelism) to the corporate (church health and growth) to the communal (Kingdom impact in our neighborhoods and cities).

Currently, the cultural climate touches on politics—not because politics is inherently our focus, but because fundamental issues like marriage, freedom, and sexuality are now contested in the public square and entering the church.

**This isn't about politics. It's about relevance.**

## Growing Up Together as a Church Body

Think of a multi-child family. The firstborn requires enormous investment and sets the cultural tone for siblings that follow. As that child enters mid-life, you'll address new challenges. When teenagers rebel, you adapt again. When retirement approaches, the conversation shifts once more.

A church grows similarly. Not every sermon speaks to everyone equally—and that's okay. Some weeks address one season; different topics rotate. The real question isn't whether a sermon is personally relevant to you, but whether it challenges presuppositions you've held dearly. Often our offense stems not from irrelevance but from conviction.

**Objection:** "This doesn't apply to me." If you're a statistical outlier, perhaps not. But generally, we track similar life trends. And a healthy church family addresses the majority season while acknowledging exceptions.

## Beyond the Bubble

Many imagine Christianity as a safe cultural enclave—a quaint, private affair. The aim: become a full-time paid minister and lead the bubble safely from within.

But historically, religious leaders were political figures—health ministers, defense ministers. There were more religious ministers than cabinet members because faith informed all of life.

We've retreated into a bubble, insulated from real problems. Yet the Gospel is meant to transform society. The atheist goal is to confine us to an irrelevant cultural relic. Don't accept that limitation.

## The Numbers That Matter

I see numbers emerging in our church—numbers that could turn things around, numbers that challenge and even frighten a parish accustomed to decline. Numbers that could overnight revitalize youth groups, Sunday schools, mothers' ministries, community outreach.

These aren't vanity metrics. They represent lives touched, families strengthened, a community transformed by the Kingdom of God.

**So the real question remains: What are we counting? And more importantly—are we counting the things that matter most?**
