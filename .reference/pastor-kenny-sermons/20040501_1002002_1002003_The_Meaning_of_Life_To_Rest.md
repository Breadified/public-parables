# Genesis 2:2-3

**Title:** The Meaning of Life: To Rest  
**Date:** 23 May 2004  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Seventh Day Rest

> Thus the heavens and the earth were finished, and all the host of them. And on the seventh day God finished his work that he had done, and he rested on the seventh day from all his work that he had done. So God blessed the seventh day and made it holy, because on it God rested from all his work that he had done in creation.
> — Genesis 2:1-3

Life is ultimately about the Seventh Day. After God completed creation, He dedicated an entire day to mark this completion and blessed it as holy. This is not an afterthought—it's the climax of the creation account.

Consider a building project. The basement we sit in, along with the rest of this building, was constructed starting in 1977. The land cost $65,000 and construction took years of work. But the completion day—the dedication ceremony where the building's purpose was proclaimed—was the real culmination. The plaque read: "A place where people matter, where families matter and where people can be equipped to serve Christ in the world. A place that will serve our community in the name of Christ."

The work matters, but the dedication day matters more. The Seventh Day is that dedication day for all creation. Notice too that unlike the other six days, the Seventh Day never ends—it has no evening and morning. It points to the goal toward which all of creation is heading.

## The Rest Ruined

Yet we are not currently in this Seventh Day Rest. The rebellion of humanity changed everything.

> Because you have listened to the voice of your wife and have eaten of the tree of which I commanded you, "You shall not eat of it," cursed is the ground because of you; in pain you shall eat of it all the days of your life; thorns and thistles it shall bring forth for you; and you shall eat the plants of the field. By the sweat of your face you shall eat bread, till you return to the ground, for out of it you were taken; for you are dust, and to dust you shall return.
> — Genesis 3:17-19

Now everything is hard, corrupted, and tiring. We wear ourselves out over eighty years and then we die. We continue to long for Rest—a place where we can simply sit and enjoy creation without toil. For many, that longing points toward heaven.

## Finding Rest: The Promise Land

For the Israelites, this longing found expression in the promise of the land.

> You shall therefore keep the whole commandment that I command you today, that you may be strong, and go in and take possession of the land that you are going over to possess, and that you may live long in the land that the Lord swore to your fathers to give to them and to their offspring, a land flowing with milk and honey. For the land that you are entering to take possession of it is not like the land of Egypt, from which you have come, where you sowed your seed and irrigated it, like a garden of vegetables. But the land that you are going over to possess is a land of hills and valleys, which drinks water by the rain from heaven, a land that the Lord your God cares for. The eyes of the Lord your God are always upon it, from the beginning of the year to the end of the year.
> — Deuteronomy 11:8-12

After being nomads, then slaves, then forty years wandering in the wilderness, the Israelites were promised a land flowing with milk and honey where they would find Rest. Unlike Egypt where they labored to irrigate gardens, this land had abundant rain and God's provision. They received cities they didn't build, houses full of goods they didn't fill, and vineyards and olive trees they didn't plant. Imagine being given everything you could want with no effort required.

But this fulfillment was always intended to be temporary.

### The Sign: The Sabbath Commandment

> Remember the Sabbath day, to keep it holy. Six days you shall labor, and do all your work, but the seventh day is a Sabbath to the Lord your God. On it you shall not do any work, you, or your son, or your daughter, your male servant, or your female servant, or your livestock, or the sojourner who is within your gates. For in six days the Lord made heaven and earth, the sea, and all that is in them, and rested the seventh day. Therefore the Lord blessed the Sabbath day and made it holy.
> — Exodus 20:8-11

The Israelites were commanded to remember the Sabbath. Six days of work, but the seventh day belonged to the Lord. The reason? It commemorates the Seventh Day of creation. To remember the Sabbath is to remember the ultimate Seventh Day Rest.

Nothing was permitted to distract from this remembrance—not your children, not your servants, not even your livestock. This was so serious that breaking the Sabbath carried the death penalty. Even during harvest, when wind threatened to ruin crops, the Israelites were to lay down their tools.

Here's the key insight: if they needed a commandment reminding them to remember the Seventh Day, then they weren't actually in the Rest. The sign pointing to the bathrooms means you're not at the bathrooms yet—you still have further to go. The Sabbath was Israel's sign that the ultimate Rest was still to come.

The Sabbath is not primarily about getting a break—though rest naturally follows remembrance. The Pharisees made this mistake, thinking the Sabbath was about ceasing from work. But it's about remembrance. It's the activity of remembering that there is life beyond work, that we are heading toward something greater.

How do we forget the Sabbath today? By using it to merely rest without remembering, or by packing our six days so full of work and pursuit that by the seventh day we're too exhausted to remember anything. Alternatively, we pack the Sabbath with so many activities—tennis, meals, entertainment—that we never pause to remember at all.

Instead, we should use the Sabbath wisely:
- Enjoy coffee with friends while praying for Jesus's return
- Serve the church and others as we wait for the ultimate Rest
- Reflect on where our lives are truly heading—toward work or toward eternity

This is why we gather together. This is our primary way of remembering that there is more to life than work and productivity. If we work seven days a week, what are we truly living for? The Sabbath calls us to remember that we have valued something eternal.

### Another Sign: God's Continued Promise

> Since therefore it remains for some to enter it, and those who formerly received the good news failed to enter because of disobedience, again he appoints a certain day, "Today," saying through David so long afterward, in the words already quoted, "Today, if you hear his voice, do not harden your hearts." For if Joshua had given them rest, God would not have spoken of another day later on. So then, there remains a Sabbath rest for the people of God.
> — Hebrews 4:6-9

Joshua brought the Israelites into the promised land. Yet God said there remained another Rest to come. This was spoken during Israel's finest hour—at peace with enemies, living in a land of milk and honey, prosperous and wealthy, envied by other nations. Even at this pinnacle, God declared that the ultimate Sabbath Rest was still ahead.

This challenges our modern hunger for more. We long for heaven in the form of real estate, career success, money, a certain lifestyle, romantic love. We think if we could only be like the lottery winner, if we could just have enough, life wouldn't be boring or tiresome. But God says the Rest is still to come. We cannot find security in things that rot and fade away. The curse of Genesis 3 still applies in this lifetime. No earthly possession or achievement can truly satisfy because sin and rebellion remain unresolved at the deepest level.

## Jesus Truly Provides Rest

> For whoever has entered God's rest has also rested from his works as God did from his. Let us therefore strive to enter that rest, so that no one may fall by the same sort of disobedience. For the word of God is living and active, sharper than any two-edged sword, piercing to the division of soul and of spirit, of joints and of marrow, and discerning the thoughts and intentions of the heart. And no creature is hidden from his sight, but all are naked and exposed to the eyes of him to whom we must give account. Since then we have a great high priest who has passed through the heavens, Jesus, the Son of God, let us hold fast our confession. For we do not have a high priest who is unable to sympathize with our weaknesses, but one who in every respect has been tempted as we are, yet without sin. Let us then with confidence draw near to the throne of grace, that we may receive mercy and find grace to help in time of need.
> — Hebrews 4:10-16

Jesus is the first to enter God's Rest. He is the great high priest who passed through the heavens and now sits at the right hand of God. What do you do on a throne? You sit. He sits because He has finished the work—finished providing purification for sins. He has completed His labor.

> All things have been handed over to me by my Father, and no one knows the Son except the Father, and no one knows the Father except the Son and anyone to whom the Son chooses to reveal him. Come to me, all who labor and are heavy laden, and I will give you rest. Take my yoke upon you, and learn from me, for I am gentle and lowly in heart, and you will find rest for your souls. For my yoke is easy, and my burden is light.
> — Matthew 11:27-30

Jesus invites us into His Rest. You may be sitting here worried—about assignments due tomorrow, work looming in the coming year, mortgages and marriage prospects for the decade ahead. But Jesus says: Come to Me. I will give you Rest.

This is the meaning of life: to long for and enter the Rest that Jesus offers. Being with Jesus in heaven is what ultimately matters. If you don't yet have confidence that you possess this Rest through the forgiveness found in Him, wouldn't it be good to have that comfort tonight?

## Living Between the Promise and the Fulfillment

We cannot find Rest in taking breaks. No amount of long weekends or exotic holidays will satisfy the deep longing in our souls.

We cannot find Rest in comfort and materialism. These fade away. Even our bodies, right now as we sit here, are decaying. They cry out for true Rest.

Life is the way it is because of sin's effects. The ambitions and achievements of this world are ultimately meaningless. There's no point blaming your parents for a suburban life, or the absence of a boyfriend or girlfriend for your miseries, or your boss for a lack of satisfaction at work. This is the reality we live in because we live in a fallen world.

Rather, Rest on earth is an activity that looks forward to heaven. Taking a day off is a sign of our commitment to eternity, not to work and this world. It's our weekly reminder that we are living for something greater.

That's why we gather. Tonight is our primary way of remembering that there is more to life than work. Why pack assignments and chores into every moment? Why not stay late tonight? Why not have dinner together? Why not commit to a life beyond work and the world?

If we work seven days a week, what are we really living for? Yes, deadlines are pressing. But what is life about? Rely on God. Trust that what you have done, even if it seems not enough, is enough because you have valued eternity.

We need not get caught up in technicalities about the exact duration required for "remembering." But genuine remembrance takes time—time to relate to one another, to encourage each other, to remember together what Jesus has done for us on the cross. He did the work so completely that He now sits in heaven. As the author of Hebrews says, let us not neglect gathering together, especially as the day draws near.

Spend the rest of your life? That will be in heaven. But meanwhile, let us long earnestly for it.
