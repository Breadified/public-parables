# Zechariah 11:4-13

**Title:** The Rejected King  
**Date:** 13 December 2020  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## A Nation in Need of Leadership

The backdrop for this passage is the post-exilic period. Israel has returned from exile and faces a critical moment. The leaders who should have cared for the people—their shepherds—have abandoned their responsibilities. Throughout the book of Kings, we see a pattern of rulers who governed for themselves rather than for their people's welfare.

This problem isn't unique to ancient Israel. Our modern systems face the same challenge: leadership driven by short-term thinking. Elections every few years incentivize decisions that look good immediately, just as companies that cut workers to boost quarterly profits discover too late that the machinery breaks down and problems multiply. True leadership requires vision beyond the next election cycle.

## The Good Shepherd Appointed (11:4-8)

> This is what the Lord my God says: "Shepherd the flock marked for slaughter." (v. 4)

The prophet Zechariah is called to lead. In Scripture, to shepherd means to be a leader—a role held by Moses, Joshua, David, and God himself. But this flock faces dire circumstances: they are "marked for slaughter."

The situation is bleak. The sheep's owners see only two options: sell the meat for profit (ending the flock entirely), or take wool and milk (a slower but more sustainable approach). The buyers choose the most lucrative path—immediate gain at the cost of the flock's future. Zechariah inherits this dying enterprise.

> So I shepherded the flock marked for slaughter, particularly the oppressed of the flock. (v. 7)

This echoes what God declared in chapter 10:

> The people wander like sheep oppressed for lack of a shepherd. My anger burns against the shepherds, and I will punish the leaders; for the Lord Almighty will care for his flock, the people of Judah, and make them like a proud horse in battle. (10:2-3)

The shepherd takes two staffs, naming them **Favor** and **Union**—symbols drawn from Ezekiel's vision of restored unity:

> I will take the stick of Joseph and of the Israelite tribes associated with him, and join it to Judah's stick. I will make them into a single stick of wood, and they will become one in my hand. (Ezekiel 37:18-19)

**Favor** represents the covenant of peace promised by God:

> I will make a covenant of peace with them; it will be an everlasting covenant. I will establish them and increase their numbers, and I will put my sanctuary among them forever. (Ezekiel 37:26)

### What True Leadership Looks Like

God presents a picture of servant leadership—a shepherd who:
- **Unites** the fractured people
- **Cares** through mercy and justice

This stands in stark contrast to self-serving rulers. Jesus embodies this ideal perfectly:

> I am the good shepherd. The good shepherd lays down his life for the sheep. The hired hand is not the shepherd and does not own the sheep. So when he sees the wolf coming, he abandons the sheep and runs away. (John 10:11-12)

Jesus came to find and care for the oppressed and marginalized:

> While Jesus was having dinner at Matthew's house, many tax collectors and sinners came and ate with him and his disciples. When the Pharisees saw this, they asked his disciples, "Why does your teacher eat with tax collectors and sinners?" Jesus said, "It is not the healthy who need a doctor, but the sick. I desire mercy, not sacrifice. For I have not come to call the righteous, but sinners." (Matthew 9:10-13)

### Leadership in Your Life

As you progress in your career, you will likely hold positions of leadership—as a manager, employer, school leader, or class officer. True Christian leadership isn't merely about hiring, firing, and policies. It's about treating people as human beings, not numbers.

This sometimes requires courage: standing for Christian convictions in a secular workplace, celebrating Christmas when others discourage it, making decisions that benefit people long-term rather than looking good short-term. Some Christian schools prioritize short-term enrollment numbers over the spiritual health of their communities. Real leaders think generationally.

## The Good Shepherd Rejected (11:8-17)

Yet this shepherd meets unexpected rejection.

> The flock detested me, and I grew weary of them. (v. 8)

When a leader cares for people and they reject him, the shepherd's burden becomes intolerable. What follows is the reversal of his mission:

> I will not be your shepherd. Let the dying die, and the perishing perish. Let those who are left eat one another's flesh. (v. 9)

The problem with much of our world isn't that leaders don't lead well—it's that no one bothers to lead at all. No one stands up. No one cares. In a democracy, we can't simply blame leaders; we must examine ourselves. We don't get involved. We reward the short-term. We don't care enough.

The shepherd breaks his first staff:

> Then I took my staff called Favor and broke it, revoking the covenant I had made with all the nations. It was revoked on that day, and so the oppressed of the flock who were watching me knew it was the word of the Lord. (vv. 10-11)

And his second staff:

> Then I broke my second staff called Union, breaking the family bond between Judah and Israel. (v. 14)

The covenant of unity and God's favor—both shattered. If you will not accept my leadership, then my obligations to you cease.

### The Price of Rejection

> I told them, "If you think it best, give me my pay; but if not, keep it." So they paid me thirty pieces of silver. (v. 12)

This payment appears generous, but it is actually a symbol of rejection—redundancy pay. When companies make you redundant, they offer a severance package. You might initially celebrate the lump sum, until you realize: they're paying you to go away. It's rejection money, hush money. They think you're so deficient that they'd rather pay you *not* to work for them.

The thirty pieces of silver carry deep meaning. This is the price of a slave gored by a bull:

> If the bull gores a male or female slave, the owner must pay thirty shekels of silver to the master of the slave, and the bull is to be stoned to death. (Exodus 21:32)

A hint of violence. A shepherd valued as a damaged slave. God's response is telling:

> And the Lord said to me, "Throw it to the potter"—the handsome price at which they valued me! So I took the thirty pieces of silver and threw them to the potter at the house of the Lord. (v. 13)

The prophet rejects their payment. He casts it away as worthless.

### The Fulfillment in Jesus

This passage haunts the New Testament because it was fulfilled so precisely. Jesus was betrayed for—

> "What are you willing to give me if I deliver him over to you?" So they counted out for him thirty pieces of silver. (Matthew 26:14-15)

The echo is unmistakable. When Judas later realized Jesus was innocent, he attempted to return the blood money:

> Judas threw the money into the temple and left. Then he went away and hanged himself. (Matthew 27:5)

And the priests, unable to use blood money in the temple treasury, used it to fulfill Zechariah's prophecy:

> So they decided to use the money to buy the potter's field as a burial place for foreigners. That is why it has been called the Field of Blood to this day. Then what was spoken by Jeremiah the prophet was fulfilled: "They took the thirty pieces of silver, the price set on him by the people of Israel, and they used them to buy the potter's field, as the Lord commanded me." (Matthew 27:7-10)

The rejection of God's shepherd has consequences woven through history itself.

## The Worthless Shepherd (11:15-17)

> Take again the equipment of a foolish shepherd. For I am going to raise up a shepherd over the land who will not care for the lost, or seek the young, or heal the injured, or feed the healthy, but will eat the meat of the choice sheep, tearing off their hooves. (vv. 15-16)

A new shepherd emerges—one who represents the opposite of the good shepherd. Tearing off hooves suggests total exploitation; using every scrap of the sheep, leaving nothing. It's crass commercialization without mercy.

This shepherd's characteristics mirror exactly what Ezekiel condemned:

> I am against the shepherds who only take care of themselves. Should not shepherds take care of the flock? (Ezekiel 34:2)

The identity of this worthless shepherd remains debated—some point to Herod the Great, others to figures after the Roman siege in AD 70. But the theological point is clear: rejection of the good shepherd leaves the people to worse rulers.

## The Shepherd Will Be Struck (12:7-10)

The narrative continues into chapter 12, where God reveals an astonishing reversal:

> "Awake, sword, against my shepherd, against the man who is close to me!" declares the Lord Almighty. "Strike the shepherd, and the sheep will be scattered, and I will turn my hand against the little ones. (12:7)

Here's the mystery: the good shepherd—the one close to God—will be struck. When Jesus quoted this passage, he clarified what it meant:

> Jesus told them, "This very night you will all fall away on account of me, for it is written: 'I will strike the shepherd, and the sheep of the flock will be scattered.' But after I have risen, I will go ahead of you into Galilee." (Matthew 26:31-32)

The shepherd bears the blow meant for the flock. In a disaster—imagine an earthquake at a school—who survives? Those protected by a teacher or parent who shields them with their own body. The sheep who scatter and run are lost.

Jesus offers his protection:

> In the whole land, two-thirds will be struck down and perish; yet one-third will be left in it. This third I will put into the fire; I will refine them like silver and test them like gold. They will call on my name and I will answer them; I will say, 'They are my people,' and they will say, 'The Lord is our God.' (12:8-9)

The shepherd is struck, but a remnant survives—those who remain under his protection. By the cross, Jesus offers protection to all his sheep. But we must not run away. We must trust in him.

### The Ultimate Leadership

Jesus is the ultimate leader:

> I am the good shepherd; I know my sheep and my sheep know me—just as the Father knows me and I know the Father—and I lay down my life for the sheep. (John 10:14-15)

He was appointed to shepherd the flock marked for slaughter. He was rejected. He was paid the price of a slave. He was struck. And yet in that striking, the remnant finds salvation.

---

## Application

**For leaders in any sphere:** You are called to shepherd with mercy and justice. Think long-term. Care for the oppressed. Unite rather than divide. Your reward may be rejection, but your faithfulness echoes into eternity.

**For all believers:** The good shepherd has been struck on your behalf. Don't scatter when persecution or pressure comes. Trust in him. By his sacrifice, you are protected. His love for you is not theoretical—it cost him everything.
