# James 3:13 – 4:12

**Title:** Fighting: Against Each Other, Against God, and How God Fights For Us

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction: The Problem of Fighting

We live in a world filled with conflict at every level—from wars between nations to personal disputes over trivial things like who gets to shower first or claim the best parking spot. But why do we fight so much?

James tells us the root cause: we fight because of our desires. Our endless wants collide with finite resources, creating an endless cycle of competition and conflict.

> What causes fights and quarrels among you? Don't they come from your desires that battle within you? You want something but don't get it. You kill and covet, but you cannot have what you want. You quarrel and fight. (James 4:1-2)

## 1. Fighting Against Each Other

When we look at what others possess—their clothes, their phones, their appearance—envy consumes us. We become willing to fight for what we want.

Consider the chaos at clearance sales: people pushing, shoving, snatching items from each other's hands. Or the brutality of finding parking at shopping centers during peak times—the honking, yelling, and occasional physical altercations over a single space. Even with food, we convince ourselves that someone else's meal is better than our own.

In a world of infinite desires but finite resources, not everyone gets what they want. When we can't obtain what others have, frustration builds and we resort to "other ways" to get it. If we can't have what you have, why not just take it from you?

### The Link Between Envy and Murder

James goes further, connecting envy directly to killing. This isn't merely theoretical—Scripture and history show us the devastating trajectory of unchecked envy.

Throughout the Bible, envy leads to murder:

- **Cain and Abel**: Cain's envy of Abel's accepted offering drove him to kill his own brother.
- **Saul and David**: Saul's jealousy of David's popularity consumed him with murderous rage.
- **Joseph and his brothers**: Joseph's brothers were so envious they plotted to kill him, only sparing him because one brother intervened.
- **Jesus**: The chief priests handed Jesus over to be crucified out of envy (Mark 15:10).

> It was out of envy that the chief priests had handed Jesus over. (Mark 15:10)

To envy is to set yourself up for murder. Envy makes us willing to kill our brothers, our children, and even God himself. Though we may not resort to literal murder, envy can manifest as relational destruction—turning against friends when they won't return our affection, sabotaging relationships out of frustration when someone won't reciprocate our feelings.

## 2. Fighting Against God

When we can't get what we want through fighting each other, we become desperate and turn to God—but with the wrong attitude.

> You do not have, because you do not ask God. When you ask, you do not receive, because you ask with wrong motives, that you may spend what you get on your pleasures. (James 4:2-3)

We approach God like a vending machine: "I tried everything else, now You owe me this." But God doesn't answer prayers rooted in selfish desires. When He doesn't give us what we want, we shake our fists at Him, questioning His goodness, His love, or His power. We stop trusting Him and turn our backs on Him entirely.

But James tells us the problem isn't with God—it's with us. Our desires are self-indulging, self-gratifying, self-centered. We want the comfort, the status, the appearance for our own benefit. When God withholds these things, we become enemies of God.

> You adulterous people, don't you know that friendship with the world is hatred toward God? Anyone who chooses to be a friend of the world becomes an enemy of God. (James 4:4)

To pursue friendship with the world—to prioritize our selfish desires over God's will—is to commit adultery against Him. We are saying that what we want is more important than our relationship with the God who made us. We are choosing to be God's enemy.

### The Call to Humility

Yet God offers us grace, not judgment.

> Or do you think Scripture says without reason that the spirit he caused to live in us envies intensely? But he gives us more grace. (James 4:5-6)

God did not give us the Holy Spirit so we would be envious of others. Instead, He gives us grace—and that grace is proportional to our humility.

> God opposes the proud but gives grace to the humble. (James 4:6, quoting Proverbs 3:34)

Envy is linked to pride. When we're proud, we have a sense of entitlement—we deserve what others have, or we believe we're better than they are. We twist this into thinking we must keep up with others, own what they own, wear what they wear. Worse, we approach God with pride, thinking that obedience obligates Him to answer our prayers. But God is not a magical genie. He opposes the proud.

Instead, we must humble ourselves before God:

> Submit yourselves, then, to God. Resist the devil, and he will flee from you. Come near to God and he will come near to you. Wash your hands, you sinners, and purify your hearts, you double-minded. Grieve, mourn and wail. Change your laughter to mourning and your joy to gloom. Humble yourselves before the Lord, and he will lift you up. (James 4:7-10)

Humility means submitting to God and His desires rather than our own. It means purifying our hearts and fixing our minds on Him alone. It means genuinely repenting of our self-centered desires.

This might mean giving up that parking spot to someone else, waiting a few more minutes to find another. It might mean letting someone else have the bargains at the sale, saving yourself the hassle of waking up early while helping someone less fortunate. It might mean humbling yourself before others and letting them have their way, even when you're right, because earthly things simply aren't worth fighting over.

## 3. God Fights For Us

### Through Jesus Christ

We have the perfect model of humility in Jesus Christ:

> Your attitude should be the same as that of Christ Jesus: Who, being in very nature God, did not consider equality with God something to be grasped, but made himself nothing, taking the very nature of a servant, being made in human likeness. And being found in appearance as a man, he humbled himself and became obedient to death—even death on a cross! (Philippians 2:5-8)

Jesus—who was God in nature—did not cling to His position. He made Himself nothing, taking on the role of a servant. His entire life demonstrated radical humility. When people insulted Him, He didn't retaliate. When they tried to kill Him, He didn't fight back. He lived a life completely surrendered to God.

Even in His darkest hour, facing crucifixion, Jesus prayed:

> Father, if you are willing, take this cup from me; yet not my will, but yours be done. (Luke 22:42)

Though Jesus wanted to avoid death, He submitted His will to God's. He laid aside the pride of being the Son of God and humbled Himself on the cross, bearing everyone's sins and everyone's judgment. He died for people who fought each other, for those who fought against Him, even for those who crucified Him.

We are called to follow His example. When we face persecution for being Christians, we must not fight back. When people quarrel with us, we must not retaliate. Though it's difficult to live as Jesus did, we can follow in His footsteps, finding comfort in the fact that He is our Savior.

### Jesus Will Save Us

> There is only one Lawgiver and Judge, the one who is able to save and destroy. But you—who are you to judge your neighbor? (James 4:12)

Jesus alone has the power to save us. He will save us from those who fight against us. Though we might lose our possessions or even our lives, we will not lose God—He will save us.

Through the sacrifice of His Son, God has saved us from His judgment. Jesus paid the price for our sins and rebellion. He has satisfied what we truly desire: forgiveness. He saved us then, and He will save us again when He returns.

### He Will Judge His Enemies

Moreover, Jesus will judge His enemies.

> There is only one Lawgiver and Judge, the one who is able to save and destroy. (James 4:12)

When Jesus returns, He will bring justice. This is why we don't need to fight others—God will destroy those who are friends with the world. This is why we can back down from conflict without fear.

> Resist the devil, and he will flee from you. Come near to God and he will come near to you. (James 4:7-8)

God will protect us. He will shield us from evil, from the devil himself. There is nothing we need to fear.

### Through His Wisdom

How do we live humbly in light of what Jesus has done? How do we discern right desires from wrong ones? Through God's wisdom.

> Who is wise and understanding among you? Let him show it by his good life, by deeds done in the humility that comes from wisdom. (James 3:13)

> But the wisdom that comes from heaven is first of all pure; then peace-loving, considerate, submissive, full of mercy and good fruit, impartial and sincere. Peacemakers who sow in peace raise a harvest of righteousness. (James 3:17-18)

When we have God's wisdom, it results in a good life of humble deeds. We move away from impure desires and fighting, toward lives centered on others. We must humble ourselves before God to receive His wisdom.

## 4. Fighting For Each Other

As one wise teacher said: "The best fights are the ones we avoid."

To address fighting in our world, we must fight—to not fight. We shift our energy from fighting *against* each other to fighting *for* each other.

### Be Content with What You Have

Start by being content with what you have. When the latest iPhone comes out, don't envy those who buy it. Your current phone works fine. Most of what we want beyond necessities is luxury. We can learn to live without it. Don't envy others for what they possess—whether material things or relationships—because all we truly need is Jesus.

### Don't Tempt Others

One way to fight for each other is to not tempt them with our possessions. If you have something others don't:

- Dress modestly, not to show off expensive clothes from overseas
- Don't display your latest gadgets boasting how they're better than your friends'
- If you're in a relationship, keep public displays of affection minimal—they tempt singles around you

### Be Self-Sacrificial

Learn to be self-sacrificial toward each other, just as Jesus sacrificed Himself for us. Put others' needs before your own.

- Give up your seat on the bus or train so someone else can travel comfortably
- Offer to buy your friends lunch or dinner when they don't have enough money
- Offer rides to church, even if they live far away—it's better they come than stay home

### Speak With Love

Be careful with your words. James tells us:

> Brothers, do not slander one another. Anyone who speaks against his brother or judges him speaks against the law and judges it. When you judge the law, you are not keeping it, but sitting in judgment on it. (James 4:11)

Don't slander each other. But also, rebuke each other through Scripture in a loving, sincere way—correcting sins so people become more godly. This is different from speaking *against* someone; this is speaking *for their good*.

### Follow the Example of Humility

When the Anglican Archbishop Peter Jensen appeared on a public panel and faced hostile questions about God's existence, he humbled himself. Though many lashed out at him with rude comments, he didn't retaliate. Instead, he spoke in love and shared the truth of Jesus Christ.

Let us follow his example and love the people around us with the same humble, sacrificial love.
