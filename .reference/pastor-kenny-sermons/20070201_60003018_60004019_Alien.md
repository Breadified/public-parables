# 1 Peter 3:18–4:19

**Title:** Alien
**Date:** 11 February 2007
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Our Identity Has Changed

We naturally resist being different from others. We chase conformity in fashion, phones, and gadgets—everyone wants the same model, the same look. Yet sometimes we delight in standing out through particular skills or rare circumstances (like being born on February 29th).

In 1 Peter, we've been exploring how Christians are fundamentally different. We have an alien status in the world. This identity shapes everything about how we live.

## 1. Alien Status (3:18)

> For Christ died for sins once for all, the righteous for the unrighteous, to bring you to God.

The Christian identity is rooted in this transformative reality: we have been brought to God through Christ's death. This is who we are now.

Christ died for sin—bearing its penalty—and He did this once for all. The concept of "the righteous for the unrighteous" echoes the prophet Isaiah, who centuries before Christ's birth prophesied about the suffering servant:

> But he was pierced for our transgressions, he was crushed for our iniquities; the punishment that brought us peace was upon him, and by his wounds we are healed. We all, like sheep, have gone astray, each of us has turned to his own way; and the LORD has laid on him the iniquity of us all. (Isaiah 53:5–6)

### Effective for the Present

Christ's death brings us to God now. We are seated in the heavenly realms. Our identity is different—we belong to Him.

### Effective for the Past (3:19–20a)

> He was put to death in the body but made alive by the Spirit, through whom also he went and preached to the spirits in prison who disobeyed long ago when God waited patiently in the days of Noah.

This is one of Scripture's most puzzling passages. What does it mean that Christ preached to the spirits in prison?

Various interpretations have been proposed: some suggest it refers to fallen angels, others to judgment preached metaphorically during Noah's time, and still others invoke purgatory. But as Protestant Christians, we reject purgatory—it contradicts the sufficiency of Christ's death. If purgatory existed, Christ's work would be incomplete, and we wouldn't truly be brought to God.

A more compelling interpretation recognizes the biblical pattern of liberation and freedom. Isaiah 61 speaks of the anointed one coming to "proclaim freedom for the captives and release from darkness for the prisoners." When John the Baptist's disciples asked if Jesus was the Messiah, Jesus pointed back to Isaiah 61, affirming that these prophecies were being fulfilled:

> The blind receive sight, the lame walk, those who have leprosy are cured, the deaf hear, the dead are raised, and the good news is preached to the poor. (Matthew 11:4–5)

The question that haunted believers then was: What about the Old Testament saints who died before Christ came? They experienced exile and death before witnessing God's promised restoration and salvation through Christ. Did they miss out on this hope?

Peter answers: No. These Old Testament exiles didn't miss out on God's promises. Christ goes to them by His Spirit and proclaims the gospel. The time has finally come. They are not left behind. God remains faithful to His ancient promises.

This same faithfulness appears in the mention of Noah:

> In the days of Noah while the ark was being built. In it only a few people, eight in all, were saved through water. (3:20b)

Noah represents God's pattern: judgment comes, yet God works salvation, protects His people, and promises future blessing. Isaiah explicitly connected this to the exiles:

> For a brief moment I abandoned you, but with deep compassion I will bring you back. In a surge of anger I hid my face from you for a moment, but with everlasting kindness I will have compassion on you. To me this is like the days of Noah, when I swore that the waters of Noah would never again cover the earth. So now I have sworn not to be angry with you, never to rebuke you again. (Isaiah 54:7–9)

The flood brought death to the world, yet it brought salvation to Noah's family. The waters of death became waters of salvation for them. There is no need to fear. God saves His people even in judgment. God keeps His promises. You have been brought to God through the death of Christ.

**Application:** A common question at youth groups is, "What happened to people who died in the Old Testament before Jesus came? They never heard the gospel or even His name." The answer: Jesus doesn't forget about them. They had faith too—faith in the future promises of God. We have faith in the One who fulfilled those promises. Being born in a different era doesn't disqualify you from the faith. What matters is faith in Christ, whether you lived before or after His incarnation.

### Effective for the Future (3:20b–4:1)

> And this water symbolizes baptism that now saves you also—not the removal of dirt from the body but the pledge of a good conscience toward God. It saves you by the resurrection of Jesus Christ, who has gone into heaven and is at God's right hand—with angels, authorities and powers in submission to him. (3:21–22)

Peter reframes baptism. It's not about the ritual washing itself or removing physical dirt. Rather, baptism symbolizes identifying yourself with Christ's death and resurrection. It's a pledge of good conscience—a commitment to turn from sin and follow Christ. This identification with Christ's death and resurrection saves you.

Christ's death and resurrection is the game-changing event. It's effective not just for those who lived before Christ or after Him, but for all eternity. Ten thousand years from now, people will still sing the same redemptive tune because Christ's work is eternally sufficient.

**Application: Assurance About the Future**

Many of us struggle with doubt about the future. We second-guess our decisions and rob ourselves of joy through worry about what might go wrong. Yet we make significant commitments despite uncertainty: we buy homes, ask people out, marry, choose schools, sign job contracts. We don't let possible future scenarios paralyze us. We commit, stay optimistic, and find our way through obstacles.

In your Christian faith, there's no need for doubt about the future. If problems come, you'll find a way through. New questions will have new answers. New doubts will meet new assurance. This is the reality of following Christ—His sufficiency covers all of it.

We live in a post-modern age of endless choices, and this surplus actually makes us unhappier. Too many options leave us perpetually wondering if we chose wrong. Instead, choose to commit deeply to Christ. You have been brought to God. With this secure identity, live differently.

## 2. Live Not for Human Desires (4:1–5)

> Therefore, since Christ suffered in his body, arm yourselves also with the same attitude, because he who has suffered in his body is done with sin. (4:1)

Christ suffered in His body through death. If we identify with Christ, we must follow His path. Those who suffer in the body have broken with sin—they've said goodbye to the flesh and its desires. We must arm ourselves with this same attitude.

> As a result, he does not live the rest of his earthly life for evil human desires, but rather for the will of God. For you have spent enough time in the past doing what pagans choose to do—living in debauchery, lust, drunkenness, orgies, carousing and detestable idolatry. (4:2–3)

Peter names the "flood of dissipation"—the lifestyle of the world characterized by unchecked pleasure and indulgence. In Noah's day, people partied and lived as though choices had no consequences. Today, this appears in the party scene, in excessive drinking, in unmarried sexuality, in materialism, in idolatry.

The culture around us has normalized alcohol consumption at alarming levels. Australians alone spend billions annually on alcohol, consuming vast quantities that damage health. Alcohol is second only to tobacco as a preventable cause of death. It's linked to thousands of hospitalizations each year, to a third of road deaths, to violence and assault, and to irreparable brain damage affecting memory, mood, and cognition. Many don't realize alcohol substantially increases cancer risk.

But Peter isn't calling us to suffer through self-damage. He means a body that suffers biblically—a body that withdraws from worldly desires, that says "no" to the trash and chooses something better. If you're offered cocktails, choose a mocktail. If beer, choose ginger ale. If wine, choose grape juice. If we're aliens, we should desire alien food and drink.

> They think it strange that you do not plunge with them into the same flood of dissipation, and they heap abuse on you. But they will have to give account to him who is ready to judge the living and the dead. (4:4–5)

When you choose differently, others will judge you as strange. They may even abuse you for it. But remember: they answer to the One who judges all people. Your citizenship is elsewhere.

## 3. Live For: Prayer, Love, and Service (4:7–11)

> The end of all things is near. Therefore be clear minded and self-controlled so that you can pray. (4:7)

Judgment is near, and this should produce clarity and self-control in us—qualities that alcohol won't provide. We should be clear-minded so we can pray. Prayer becomes our response to temptation; it interrupts our desires and redirects them to God.

> Above all, love each other deeply, because love covers over a multitude of sins. Offer hospitality to one another without grumbling. (4:8–9)

Love is the centerpiece of Christian community. It covers over a multitude of sins. Hospitality—welcoming others, feeding them, making them comfortable—is a basic Christian skill we've largely lost. When you host a party or gather, provide food, seating, and care. These practices teach us Christ.

> Each one should use whatever gift he has received to serve others, faithfully administering God's grace in its various forms. If anyone speaks, he should do it as one speaking the very words of God. If anyone serves, he should do it with the strength God provides, so that in all things God may be praised through Jesus Christ. To him be the glory and the power for ever and ever. Amen. (4:10–11)

Serve with the gifts God has given you. If you speak, speak as one delivering God's words. If you serve, serve with God's strength. In everything, let God be praised. This year, commit to prayer, love, and service. Volunteer. And if you lead, be willing to receive feedback. Be self-critical. Improve.

## 4. Rejoice in Your Identity (4:12–19)

> Dear friends, do not be surprised at the painful trial you are suffering, as though something strange were happening to you. But rejoice that you participate in the sufferings of Christ, so that you may be overjoyed when his glory is revealed. If you are insulted because of the name of Christ, you are blessed, for the Spirit of glory and of God rests on you. (4:12–14)

Suffering as a Christian is not strange or accidental. It's part of belonging to Christ's family. When you're insulted for His name's sake, you're blessed. This is a sign of assurance—evidence that you belong to Him.

> If you suffer, it should not be as a murderer or thief or any other kind of criminal, or even as a meddler. However, if you suffer as a Christian, do not be ashamed, but praise God that you bear that name. (4:15–16)

There's a crucial distinction: make sure your suffering comes from following Christ, not from foolishness or wrongdoing. If you suffer for bearing Christ's name, rejoice in it. This is the mark of belonging to Him.

> For it is time for judgment to begin with the family of God; and if it begins with us, what will the outcome be for those who do not obey the gospel of God? (4:17)

> So then, those who suffer according to God's will should commit themselves to their faithful Creator and continue to do good. (4:19)

In all of this—in being different, in standing against the world's dissipation, in suffering for Christ's name—commit yourself to your faithful Creator. He is worthy of trust. Continue doing good, knowing that your identity is secure and your joy is assured.

You are an alien in this world, and that is your glory.
