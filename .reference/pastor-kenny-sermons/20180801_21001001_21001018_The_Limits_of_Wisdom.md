# Ecclesiastes 1:1-18; 12:8-14

**Title:** The Limits of Wisdom  
**Date:** 16 August 2018  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Search for Knowledge

We gather here united by a common pursuit—the search for knowledge. Whether you're studying engineering, psychology, or business like I did, we're all here because we believe knowledge will make us better. We're willing to invest years and accumulate significant debt because we trust that understanding how the world works will lead us to meaning and satisfaction.

Sydney University's original charter captured this vision beautifully: the university exists "for the advancement of religion and morality and the promotion of useful knowledge." Education promises to help us find how the world fits together, to discover meaning, and to know how to live well.

But the Book of Ecclesiastes has something challenging to say about this pursuit.

## The Limits of Knowledge (Ecclesiastes 1:1-18)

### Solomon's Search

The speaker in Ecclesiastes is the Preacher—a Hebrew word meaning "assembler" (from *qahal*, the same root as the Greek *ekklesia*, church). This is King Solomon, the wisest man on earth, the repository of knowledge and the ultimate teacher. If anyone could find meaning through wisdom and understanding, it would be him.

> The words of the Preacher, the son of David, king in Jerusalem. Vanity of vanities, says the Preacher, vanity of vanities! All is vanity. (Ecclesiastes 1:1-2)

Solomon applied his heart to seek out by wisdom all that is done under heaven. He devoted himself to understanding the right way to live. Yet after exhausting his intellectual and material resources, his conclusion was stark:

> I have seen everything that is done under the sun, and behold, all is vanity and a striving after wind. (1:14)

The Hebrew word *hevel*—"vanity"—means something ephemeral and temporary, like breath or mist. It appears thirty-eight times throughout Ecclesiastes, emphasizing the fundamental meaninglessness of knowledge pursued apart from God.

### The Vanity of Accumulated Knowledge

Consider your own experience as students. You spend weeks preparing for exams, filling your mind with facts and formulas. The night before the test, you know everything. But what happens afterward? Can you still solve the problems you spent six years learning? Where did all that knowledge go? It vanishes like breath on a mirror.

Solomon discovered this same pattern:

> I said in my heart, "I have acquired great wisdom, surpassing all who were over Jerusalem before me, and my heart has had great experience of wisdom and knowledge." And I applied my heart to know wisdom and to know madness and folly. I perceived that this also is but a striving after wind. (1:16-17)

### Knowledge Cannot Solve the Fundamental Problems

We live in an age that loves to say, "Studies show..." One year we're told butter is bad for you; the next year, studies reveal it's actually good. The food pyramid was the standard for decades, yet obesity has risen even as we've promoted that scientific knowledge. Why? Because knowledge by itself cannot fix what is fundamentally broken in us.

> For in much wisdom is much vexation, and he who increases knowledge increases sorrow. (1:18)

The more we understand about the world, the more we see what we cannot control or fix. Knowledge becomes a burden of anxiety and helplessness.

### The Modern Age and Its Discontents

Our modern era broke free from the Middle Ages and enthroned empirical knowledge in place of God. "God is dead," the philosophers declared. "Knowledge is our new god. We can think our way to heaven."

Yet the culmination of modernity gave us the worst violence in human history. The Great War killed twenty million. Just when we thought we'd learned our lesson, World War II arrived with eighty million dead. We learned so much about physics and chemistry—and used it to invent more efficient ways of killing each other.

This is the legacy of modernity: brilliant knowledge wielded by broken people.

### The Postmodern Reaction

Seeing where reason led us, postmodernity rejected the entire project. "Get rid of reason," it says. "Operate on instinct—anger, outrage, passion." We no longer sit down to persuade one another; we shout slogans and substitute force for dialogue. Postmodernity calls us to unlearn, to redefine everything from scratch.

But postmodernity offers no solution. What gives it confidence that it can handle knowledge better than modernity did?

### Why Knowledge Fails

The problem is not knowledge itself. It's the knower.

> What is crooked cannot be made straight, and what is lacking cannot be counted. (1:15)

We are crooked—bent, folded, damaged like a dented car or a Mona Lisa with a fold through the canvas. We can still function, but not perfectly. Knowledge can extend life expectancy to eighty years, but we still die. We can fix a broken leg and improve life a little, but the fundamental brokenness remains.

Moreover, we don't know what we don't know. The gap in our knowledge is too vast to measure. We're hunting in the dark, unable to see the whole picture.

Knowledge is like the different types of fats—some good, some bad. The reader must discern. Some academics deserve listening to; others don't. Some books should be followed; others should be read and rejected. Some ideas we adopt; some we criticize.

What we desperately need is **direction**—a framework for knowledge, an ethic to guide what we do with it.

## The Beginning of Wisdom (Ecclesiastes 12:8-14)

### Solomon's Conclusion—Confirmed and Corrected

The book ends with an editor's perspective—a later author reflecting on Solomon's journey with wisdom:

> Vanity of vanities, says the Preacher; all is vanity. Besides being wise, the Preacher also taught the people knowledge, weighing and studying and arranging many proverbs with great care. The Preacher sought to find words of delight, and uprightly he wrote words of truth. (12:8-10)

The editor confirms that Solomon was right: knowledge pursued without God is indeed vain. But the editor offers what Solomon missed—hope. Yes, Solomon's analysis of vanity is correct. But that's only true if knowledge exists apart from God.

> The words of the wise are like goads, and like nails firmly fixed are the collected sayings; they are given by one Shepherd. (12:11)

There is a Shepherd. There is an author of true wisdom. And the editor directs us to it:

> The end of the matter; all has been heard. Fear God and keep his commandments, for this is the whole of man. For God will bring every deed into judgment, with every secret thing, whether good or evil. (12:13-14)

### Fear as the Foundation and Conclusion of Wisdom

"Fear God" is not merely the *beginning* of wisdom—it is also the *end* of wisdom, the final conclusion of all inquiry. As one commentator writes: "Fear God is a call that puts us in our place, and all other fears, hopes, and admirations in their place."

When we come to the limits of our own knowledge and understanding, we must fear the Lord. This fear is not terror but reverence, awe, and submission.

### Obedience as the Whole of Humanity

"Keep his commandments, for this is the whole of man." The King James Version unfortunately added the word "duty," but the Hebrew simply says: "this is the whole of the man"—this applies to everyone.

Our inquiry into knowledge, no matter how brilliant, is necessarily limited. We cannot find perfect answers to all our questions. But in His mercy, God has not left us wandering in darkness wondering what to do.

### God's Wisdom Revealed in Christ

In these last days, God has been merciful to reveal true wisdom to us:

> For the word of the cross is folly to those who are perishing, but to us who are being saved it is the power of God. For it is written, "I will destroy the wisdom of the wise, and the discernment of the discerning I will thwart." (1 Corinthians 1:18-19)

By human wisdom, the cross appears foolish. How can someone dying save anyone? Yet in God's wisdom—not man's—this is His plan: the righteous died for the unrighteous once for all, to bring us to God.

Jesus is perhaps the one out of a thousand whom Ecclesiastes glimpses—the truly wise one who feared God and obeyed His commands wholeheartedly. He is the lens through which we read the world. He is the lamp by which we see rightly.

### The Integration of Knowledge and Faith

Theology must go hand in hand with science. This great institution, Sydney University, was founded explicitly "for the advancement of religion and morality and the promotion of useful knowledge." These are not opposing forces but complementary pursuits.

Knowledge is not the enemy. But knowledge without the fear of God, without submission to His commandments, without Christ as our interpretive lens—that knowledge becomes vanity, a chasing of wind.

The call to you, as you pursue knowledge in these halls, is to do so with reverence for God, obedience to His word, and faith in the gospel of Jesus Christ. This is wisdom. This is the whole of what it means to be human.
