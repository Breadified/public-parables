# Luke 23:1-25

**Title:** The Greatest Swap in the World  
**Date:** April 12, 2003  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Question

If you could swap your life with anyone in the world, who would it be? What would be your greatest swap?

For many of us, it's someone we admire—an athlete, a successful person, someone whose life seems enviable. Growing up, I wanted to be like Michael Jordan. The athleticism, the skill, the confidence—everything about him seemed worth trading for.

But today we're looking at something far greater than a pretty good swap. We're examining **the Greatest Swap in the World**, centered on Jesus and three other characters: Pilate, Herod, and Barabbas.

## Pilate's Verdict (Luke 23:1-16)

The scene: Jesus has been brought before Pilate, the Roman governor of Jerusalem. The religious authorities accuse Jesus of subverting the nation—claiming he opposes taxes to Caesar and claims to be a king.

> "We have found this man subverting our nation. He opposes payment of taxes to Caesar and claims to be Christ, a king."

The word *subverting* means insurrection—rebellion against established authority. They're essentially saying Jesus is trying to overthrow Roman rule.

But Pilate examines the evidence and finds nothing. He declares Jesus innocent—**not guilty**. From Rome's perspective, Jesus is no threat.

When the religious leaders insist that Jesus stirs up unrest throughout Judea, Pilate sees an opportunity to pass the problem along. Learning that Jesus is from Galilee (Herod's territory), he sends him to Herod instead.

## Herod's Entertainment (Luke 23:8-11)

Now we meet Herod, the local ruler of Galilee—more tyrant than leader. When he sees Jesus, he's delighted. He's heard the stories and hopes to see a spectacular miracle.

> "When Herod saw Jesus, he was greatly pleased, because for a long time he had been wanting to see him. From what he had heard about him, he hoped to see him perform some miracle."

Herod bombards Jesus with questions, but Jesus remains silent. There's no miracle, no show, only mockery. Herod dresses Jesus in an elegant robe and sends him back to Pilate, finding him not guilty as well.

**A warning for us:** Sometimes we approach Jesus the same way Herod does—wanting him for his power, hoping for a spectacular display to convince us. We say, "If only Jesus would show me a miracle, then I'd believe." But if we're seeking Jesus primarily for his power rather than his truth, we're not much better than Herod. We're just mocking him.

## Pilate's Weakness (Luke 23:13-25)

Pilate calls together the crowds and makes his position clear for a third time:

> "You brought me this man as one who was inciting the people to rebellion. I have examined him in your presence and have found no basis for your charges against him. Neither has Herod, for he sent him back to us; as you can see, he has done nothing to deserve death."

Three times, Pilate declares Jesus innocent. But then the crowd demands something different: "Release Barabbas to us!"

## Barabbas: The Criminal (Luke 23:18-19)

Barabbas is a man guilty of the very crimes Jesus was accused of—insurrection and murder. He had actually stirred up the city to rebel against Roman rule. If he lived today, he'd be orchestrating terrorist attacks. He's dangerous. He's guilty. He deserves death.

Yet when the crowd shouts for his release, Pilate capitulates. His desire to appease the masses overrides his sense of justice. He releases the guilty man and surrenders the innocent Jesus to crucifixion.

## The Greatest Swap

And here is the heart of it all, captured in a single verse:

> "He released the man who had been thrown into prison for insurrection and murder, the one they asked for, and surrendered Jesus to their will."

**Barabbas was guilty of insurrection. Jesus was not.**

Barabbas walks free. Jesus is sentenced to death.

For Barabbas, this must have been unimaginable—the greatest swap of his life. He was counting down his final hours, awaiting execution, when suddenly everything changed. He did nothing to deserve it, yet he was released.

## Our Greatest Swap

But here's the profound truth: **we are like Barabbas.**

We are guilty of insurrection too—not against a political governor, but against the King of the Universe. Every time we say, "No, God, I want to live my way instead of yours," we're rebelling against his rule of our lives. Every time we disregard what God thinks and choose to do wrong anyway, we're in insurrection against him.

And the penalty for insurrection is death. The wages of rebellion against God is the death penalty.

Yet Jesus was willing to make the swap.

Instead of facing the death penalty we deserve, we can be free and released. Jesus takes our place. He bears the penalty we earned, even though he is completely innocent. He carries our guilt, our shame, our rebellion—and pays for it all through his blood on the cross.

This is the exchange: **Jesus surrenders his innocence so that we might receive his freedom.**

Just as if I somehow swapped lives with Michael Jordan, he would gain all my attributes (and lose his athletic greatness), so Jesus takes on all our guilt and condemnation. He does this not because he deserves it, but because he loves us.

## Will This Be Your Greatest Swap?

This is the heart of the gospel. The question isn't whether Jesus made this swap—he did, two thousand years ago. The question is: **Will you accept it?**

Will you stop rebelling against God's rule in your life and instead listen to him? Will you receive the freedom that Jesus purchased for you?

## Prayer

Father, thank you for Jesus, who was willing to swap his innocence for our guilt. We ask that we would be truly grateful to you and love you because of what he's done. Help us to stop rebelling against your rule in our lives and instead listen to you and follow your way.

All this we pray in Jesus' name. Amen.
