# Isaiah 2:1-22

**Title:** City of Righteousness

**Date:** 20 November 2022

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Part 1: Nations Will Come to the Mountain (2:1-5)

### The Vision of God's Exalted House

Isaiah sees a profound future for Jerusalem. In the last days, the mountain of the Lord's temple will be established as the highest of all mountains, exalted above every hill. This isn't primarily about physical height—it's about spiritual prominence. The temple will become a lighthouse, visible to all nations, drawing people from every tribe and language.

> In the last days the mountain of the Lord's temple will be established as the highest of the mountains; it will be exalted above the hills, and all nations will stream to it.

The word "stream" is significant. Just as water flows downward in a valley after heavy rain, so too will multitudes flow toward God's house—so numerous they'll appear like a river in motion.

### Why Nations Come: Teaching and Walking in God's Ways

Many peoples will come to Jerusalem saying, "Come, let us go up to the mountain of the Lord, to the temple of the God of Jacob." Their purpose is clear: learning God's ways so they might walk in His paths. This reveals the pattern of the gospel's appeal—God teaches His truth, and through that knowledge, people order their lives according to His standards.

> He will teach us his ways, so that we may walk in his paths.

How do we know which way to walk? Through God's law. The law originates from Zion; the word of the Lord proceeds from Jerusalem. Today, we find this revealed completely in Scripture—God's collected wisdom for human life.

### God's Judgment and Peace

God doesn't merely teach; He also judges. He will settle disputes between nations, resolving conflicts through His righteousness rather than human violence. The result is radical peace:

> They will beat their swords into plowshares and their spears into pruning hooks. Nation will not take up sword against nation, nor will they train for war anymore.

This is the world remade under God's authority—not through human progress, but through submission to the Lord's governance.

### The Invitation and the Bridge

The invitation extends to all: "Come, descendants of Jacob, let us walk in the light of the Lord." This addresses a profound gulf between Jew and Gentile, insider and outsider. God's original intention for Israel was that they would become so righteous, so faithful, and so blessed that their very existence would attract the world. Like the Queen of Sheba coming to witness Solomon's wisdom and faith, nations would be drawn by the visible reality of God's blessing—the abundance, the justice, the wholeness.

Historical examples confirm this pattern: Abraham was promised that through his offspring, all nations would be blessed. The Queen of Sheba traveled to see Solomon's wisdom, his wealth, his temple worship, and she recognized God's hand upon His people. The prophets envisioned Israel as a light to the Gentiles, a beacon drawing all peoples to discover the living God.

When pagan cultures encounter genuine Christian civilization—true wisdom, genuine compassion, authentic righteousness—they're attracted to something they recognize as superior. History shows this: pagans flocked to Christianized nations, and through Christian influence, entire civilizations were transformed. Schools taught Scripture, values shifted, and spiritual darkness gave way to light.

## Part 2: Jerusalem Becomes Like the Nations (2:6-22)

### The Tragic Reversal

Rather than being an example, Israel followed the nations into idolatry and corruption. God's abandonment of His people marks a devastating reversal:

> You, Lord, have abandoned your people, the descendants of Jacob. They are full of superstitions from the East; they practice divination like the Philistines and embrace pagan customs.

The "East" represents international paganism—the religious systems of Assyria and Babylon. The Philistines represent local, corrupted worship—the fertility cults of Canaan centered on Baal. Israel adopted both. And with these religions came their moral horrors: child sacrifice, sexual exploitation and prostitution normalized, brutal violence, slavery, warfare.

Yet superficially, Israel maintained wealth. Their land overflowed with silver and gold, horses and chariots. They had horses imported from Egypt and Arabia—tokens of political alliances with pagan powers. But beneath the prosperity lay spiritual bankruptcy:

> Their land is full of idols; they bow down to the work of their hands, to what their fingers have made.

Man-made gods. Human invention masquerading as divine truth.

### The Corruption of Leadership and Justice

The opening of Isaiah 1 describes the faithful city become a prostitute—once full of justice and righteousness, now inhabited by murderers. The silver has become dross, the wine diluted with water. Most tellingly:

> Your rulers are rebels, partners with thieves; they all love bribes and chase after gifts. They do not defend the cause of the fatherless; the widow's case does not come before them.

When leadership abandons God's law, justice dies. The vulnerable are left defenseless.

### The Day of Humbling and God's Exaltation

Because Israel has abandoned God's ways, judgment comes. The pattern of history repeats: as God did with the pagan nations of Canaan and Babylon, so He will do to His own people when they turn from Him. People will be brought low, and everyone will be humbled. They'll hide in caves and holes in the ground, fleeing from the fearful presence of the Lord and the splendor of His majesty when He rises to shake the earth.

> The eyes of the arrogant will be humbled and human pride brought low; the Lord alone will be exalted in that day.

This refrain echoes throughout the judgment section. God's standards alone matter. His laws alone govern what is righteous. There is no cultural relativism in God's eyes—not all religions are equal. There is God, and then there is everything else.

The proud thought themselves mountains; they discover they're merely caves. In the end:

> In that day people will throw away to the moles and bats their idols of silver and idols of gold, which they made to worship.

All that wealth, all that craftsmanship, all that pride—discarded to creatures that live in darkness.

### The Call to Stop Trusting in Humanity

The passage concludes with an urgent exhortation:

> Stop trusting in mere humans, who have but a breath in their nostrils. Why hold them in esteem?

Human rule, human wisdom, human standards—all are temporary and ultimately worthless. Only God endures; only His judgment is true.

## The Broader Application: God's Covenant to Restore

Yet judgment is not God's final word. Even as He threatens, there remains hope through repentance:

> Therefore the Lord, the Lord Almighty, the Mighty One of Israel, declares: "Ah! I will vent my wrath on my foes and avenge myself on my enemies. I will turn my hand against you; but I will thoroughly purge away your dross and remove all your impurities. I will restore your leaders as in days of old, your rulers as at the beginning. Afterward you will be called the City of Righteousness, the Faithful City."

God's judgment is not arbitrary destruction—it's refining fire. Like a smelter burning away impurities to reveal pure metal, God's judgment purges His people so they can be restored to their original calling: a righteous, faithful people, attractive to the world, a light to the Gentiles.

---

## Personal and Corporate Challenge

The ancient pattern demands contemporary reflection. Just as Israel faced a choice—remain the city of righteousness or become like the nations—so do we.

Are we different from the world? Or are we lazy adopters of pagan culture, wanting the same things as unbelievers? Do our pagan friends see genuine godliness in us? Do our children see something in us that distinguishes us from any other parent they know?

The fundamental questions cut deep:

- Do we want our children to be godly above all else, with everything else secondary?
- Or do we want them rich, educated, and successful—like every other pagan parent—with a little godliness added on the side?
- Is there anything in our lives, our families, our churches, our nations that is genuinely attractive to the watching world?

The warning is real. When nations abandon God's law, they don't advance—they regress. History shows the tragic reversal: Western civilization built on Christian foundations has begun returning to barbarism. Sexual exploitation is normalized. Violence escalates. Governments suppress dissent. We bow to creation rather than Creator—valuing trees and animals above human life, especially the vulnerable. Schools teach pagan ceremonies. Step by step, we're being converted back to paganism.

Yet the invitation remains: If we haven't been godly, we must turn back. We must listen to the law of God, the word of God. We must follow Jesus.

Then—and only then—will we be restored as the City of Righteousness, the Faithful City. Then will our lives, our families, our churches, our nations truly be light to the Gentiles.
