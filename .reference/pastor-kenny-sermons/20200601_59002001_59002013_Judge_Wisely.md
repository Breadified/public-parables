## Introduction: Discrimination in Culture and Church

Discrimination is indeed a "dirty word" in our culture. Recent social upheaval has raised important questions about how we treat one another—particularly regarding racial injustice. Australia, like many nations, grapples with historical discrimination against Indigenous peoples. The rise of movements like Black Lives Matter reflects genuine grievances, yet it also presents an opportunity for the church to examine what Scripture says about discrimination and how we should think about it in light of God's Word.

James speaks directly into this cultural moment. His letter is the "word implanted"—sometimes uncomfortable, sometimes confronting—but always designed to cut away our prejudices and transform our behavior and thinking.

## 1. Discriminating Badly: Judging by Appearances

### The Scenario

James presents a vivid scene that we can easily imagine:

> Suppose a man comes into your meeting wearing a gold ring and fine clothes, and a poor man in filthy old clothes also comes in. (James 2:2)

The contrast is stark. The rich man arrives adorned with visible symbols of wealth—gold rings, fine garments, the kind of gleaming, polished appearance that commands attention. By contrast, the poor man wears filthy, unwashed clothes—hand-me-downs, nothing to distinguish him, nothing to catch the eye.

The natural (but sinful) response follows:

> If you show special attention to the man wearing fine clothes and say, "Here's a good seat for you," but say to the poor man, "You stand there" or "Sit on the floor by my feet." (James 2:3)

One receives honor; the other receives dishonor. All based purely on appearance.

### The Judgment

James cuts to the heart of the matter:

> Have you not discriminated among yourselves and become judges with evil thoughts? (James 2:4)

This is the core issue: we are judging based on appearances alone—what James calls "favoritism" (literally, "respecter of faces").

### God's Standard

Scripture consistently warns against this shallow judgment. Jesus taught:

> Stop judging by mere appearances, but instead judge correctly. (John 7:24)

The prophet Samuel learned this lesson when selecting Israel's next king. He was drawn to Eliab's outward appearance, but God corrected him:

> The Lord does not look at the things people look at. People look at the outward appearance, but the Lord looks at the heart. (1 Samuel 16:7)

Peter discovered the same truth:

> I now realize how true it is that God does not show favoritism. (Acts 10:34)

God's choices are not based on appearances but on His will and knowledge of the heart. He chose us while we were still sinners—not because we looked impressive or had our lives together, but because of His grace alone.

### Why We Fall into This Trap

Yet human nature—corrupted by sin—is drawn to appearances. We love the shine, the polish, the visible markers of success. Consider how we make decisions:

**Buying a car:** We might choose a beautiful lemon with a faulty engine over a reliable but ugly vehicle.

**Buying a house:** We might reject a structurally sound home in a good location because it's unattractive, yet embrace an overpriced, poorly-located property because it has trendy fixtures and fresh paint.

**Relationships:** We often fall in love with physical appearance, overlooking incompatibility in faith, values, and personality. Lust focuses on the external; true love looks deeper.

The pattern is clear: we default to judging by what we see, not by what we know.

### In the Church

Thankfully, our church is generally welcoming and inclusive. Yet it's worth examining our hearts. Do we sometimes avoid people because they seem:

- Boring or quiet?
- From a different cultural background?
- Speakers of a different language?
- Lacking our interests or hobbies?

How do we know they're boring until we invest time in knowing them? How do we know they won't become dear friends? We should approach everyone with openness, treating them as a friend first and allowing God to bless genuine relationships.

Of course, we must never judge someone based on the color of their skin or cultural background. While we may sometimes struggle with differences, that is never grounds for withholding welcome.

### The Modern Problem: Prejudice Masquerading as Justice

Today's culture is confused about what discrimination truly is. The powerful—celebrities, media personalities, politicians—dressed in their finest and speaking from positions of influence, often feed us simplistic narratives:

- "All whites are privileged and racist"
- "All blacks are victims and innocent"
- "All police are brutal"

These sweeping generalizations play to our inner prejudices. We judge according to what we've experienced or what we've been told to believe, not according to truth.

**Example:** We easily believe "all police are evil" because our encounters with police tend to be negative—a speeding ticket, a warning, a confrontation. We rarely remember that the peace and safety we enjoy comes from law and order. We judge based on superficial, limited experience.

**Another example:** As minorities in predominantly white countries, many of us have experienced racism. But we tend to focus on the majority experience while forgetting or overlooking racism from other minorities. We remember the 9 out of 10 negative encounters and forget the 1 positive one—or ignore kindness from white friends and neighbors entirely.

**Another example:** We judge "all rich people are evil" partly because of envy.

But this is still judging by appearance—precisely what James forbids.

### The Reality Behind the Stereotypes

The truth is far more complex than the narratives we're fed:

- Some of the poorest people in America are white—in Appalachia, the mountains of Kentucky—stereotyped but largely ignored by media.
- There are wealthy black communities and wealthy white communities.
- There are poor black communities and poor white communities.
- Not all black people are poor; not all white people are rich.
- Just because one police officer commits a crime doesn't mean all officers are corrupt.

**The biblical principle:** There are good people and bad people in every category—rich and poor, black and white, police and civilians. The world is more complicated than the black-and-white narrative the powerful want us to accept.

### A Warning to Christians

As Christians, we must be careful. We bear the brunt of bigotry when we take moral stands. We speak of sin, uphold moral integrity, and rebuke evil—and people hate us for it. Even when we offer forgiveness and grace, not condemnation, the offended rarely see the good we do; they focus on what they perceive as judgment.

The powerful who claim to champion justice today may come after the church tomorrow. Don't be fooled into thinking secular movements are your friends:

> Is it not the rich who are exploiting you? Are they not the ones who are dragging you into court? Are they not the ones who are blaspheming the noble name of him to whom you belong? (James 2:6-7)

We cannot afford to pander to the powerful simply because they appear strong or promise to help us. They will use us and then turn against us.

## 2. Good Discrimination: Judging Correctly

Here's what we often miss: **Jesus doesn't forbid all discrimination; He forbids bad discrimination.** He says:

> Stop judging by mere appearances, but instead judge correctly. (John 7:24)

The expectation throughout Scripture is that we exercise judgment—but *good* judgment, based on truth and wisdom.

> Why don't you judge for yourselves what is right? (Luke 12:57)

> Watch out for false prophets. They come to you in sheep's clothing, but inwardly they are ferocious wolves. (Matthew 7:15)

Even the Apostle Paul affirms our responsibility to judge:

> Do you not know that the saints will judge the world? And if you are to judge the world, are you not competent to judge trivial cases? Do you not know that we will judge angels? How much more the things of this life! (1 Corinthians 6:2-3)

### Honoring the Faithful

James points us toward a different kind of discrimination:

> Has not God chosen those who are poor in the eyes of the world to be rich in faith and to inherit the kingdom he promised those who love him? But you have dishonored the poor. (James 2:5)

Last week we learned that those facing trials are not weak—they are strong and resilient. So why would we disregard their faith? Why dishonor them?

Consider how society honors those who serve and endure:
- We reserve seats on public transport for those with disabilities
- We provide special accommodations for pregnant women and parents with infants
- At church, shouldn't we honor those who have persevered through hardship and trials?

Those "rich in faith"—who have endured like Job—deserve our respect and honor:

> We count as blessed those who have persevered. You have heard of Job's perseverance and have seen what the Lord finally brought about. The Lord is full of compassion and mercy. (James 5:11)

### Good Discrimination in Practice

The principle is this: **Judge with information. Judge with understanding. Consider the facts.**

Don't discriminate indiscriminately; discriminate *discriminatingly*.

**A caution about postmodernism:** Our culture increasingly refuses to make any distinctions. We're told we cannot judge gender, sexual behavior, lifestyle choices, or appearance without being accused of discrimination. Yet discrimination is unavoidable in life:

- Why do beautiful people become models?
- Why do intelligent people get high-income positions?
- Why do tall people play basketball and short people become jockeys?

These aren't moral failures; they're natural distinctions based on relevant criteria.

### Descriptions Are Not Racism

One final point: describing someone's appearance is not racist. The Bible uses descriptive terms naturally:

> Now in the church at Antioch there were prophets and teachers: Barnabas, Simeon called Niger... (Acts 13:1)

"Niger" is Latin for "black"—a simple descriptor, not a judgment of character.

When Jeremiah asks, "Can the Cushite change his skin?" he's using "Cushite" (a term for black-skinned peoples) as a simple descriptor, just as Shakespeare used it when his Othello was translated to Hebrew as "Ithiel the Cushite."

**The distinction:** Racism means asserting that another ethnic group is inferior or showing antagonism based solely on skin color. Mere description of appearance is not racism. There is no judgment of character in saying someone is black or white—it's simply what they look like.

## 3. Love Your Neighbor

### The Royal Law

> If you really keep the royal law found in Scripture, "Love your neighbor as yourself," you are doing right. But if you show favoritism, you sin and are convicted by the law as lawbreakers. (James 2:8-9)

This is the fulfillment of all the law:

> For whoever keeps the whole law and yet stumbles at just one point is guilty of breaking all of it. For he who said, "You shall not commit adultery," also said, "You shall not murder." If you do not commit adultery but do commit murder, you have become a lawbreaker. (James 2:10-11)

The principle is simple: two wrongs don't make a right. If someone's ancestors committed theft a century ago, that doesn't justify theft today. In fact, it makes it doubly wrong because the original perpetrators are long gone—you're hurting innocent people to settle an old score.

### The Individual Matters Most

Notice that the law says "Love your neighbor"—singular. Your *neighbor* is an individual, not a collective group.

In the parable of the Good Samaritan, the question wasn't "Are all Samaritans good or all Jews evil?" The answer was specific: the individual Samaritan showed love to the individual Jew in need.

**This is the opposite of identity politics.** Identity politics groups people by tribe, by face, by appearance, and makes sweeping judgments. But God doesn't see us that way.

God looked at us—not as a race (though He could have, as the chosen people were the Jews) but as individuals. He sees the elect one by one. The greatest minority is that of the individual person.

We must do the same. **Judge wisely means dealing with the person in front of you.**

### Mercy Triumphs Over Judgment

> Speak and act as those who are going to be judged by the law that gives freedom, because judgment without mercy will be shown to anyone who has not been merciful. Mercy triumphs over judgment. (James 2:12-13)

This is the heart of the gospel. The Law of Liberty—the law of Christ—is fundamentally about love and mercy. If you have not shown mercy to others, don't expect mercy in your own judgment. But mercy transcends judgment; grace overcomes condemnation.

### The Law of Love

The Old Testament captures this beautifully:

> Do not pervert justice; do not show partiality to the poor or favoritism to the great, but judge your neighbor fairly. Do not go about spreading slander among your people. Do not do anything that endangers your neighbor's life. Do not hate your brother in your heart. Rebuke your neighbor frankly so you will not share in his guilt. Do not seek revenge or bear a grudge against one of your people, but love your neighbor as yourself. (Leviticus 19:15-18)

This is the fulfillment of the gospel: overcome judgment with love. Instead of working toward sin and division, we work toward the good through faith in Christ.

## Application for Our Church

We have been blessed with a new meeting location at 88 Cecil Avenue, Castle Hill—the site of Hills Adventist College. This is a miracle. We are now in the heart of a growing town center with:

- Close proximity to transport hubs and dense population
- An opportunity to share the gospel with many more people
- A partnership with an Indonesian language church, welcoming their second-generation believers

With this opportunity comes responsibility. We must not waste it.

### How James Applies

**First:** Don't judge on superficial things. We welcome and accept anyone as they are. We don't discriminate based on appearance, culture, language, or background.

**Second:** Do judge on what matters. We grow together toward discernment and maturity in Christ. We distinguish between what's essential and what's peripheral.

**Third:** Give honor discriminatingly. Just as the body of Christ honors different members according to their role:

> The parts that seem to be weaker are indispensable. The parts that we think are less honorable we treat with special honor. (1 Corinthians 12:22-23)

We honor the older and wiser, those in authority, and those who lead us well:

> Do not rebuke an older man harshly, but exhort him as if he were your father. (1 Timothy 5:1)

> The elders who direct the affairs of the church well are worthy of double honor, especially those whose work is preaching and teaching. (1 Timothy 5:17)

Most importantly, we distinguish between those who persecute the church and those who receive the gospel with openness:

> The Berean Jews were of more noble character than those in Thessalonica, for they received the message with great eagerness and examined the Scriptures every day. (Acts 17:11)

## Conclusion: True Racism

When we fail to make these distinctions—when we judge by mere appearances instead of by truth—we become exactly what Jesus and James warn against.

**True racism** is not merely using descriptive words or making distinctions based on relevant criteria. True racism is systems and attitudes that assume inferiority based on ethnicity. Forms that ask "Are you Torres Strait Islander/Aboriginal?" without regard to actual need or merit are examples of this. They assume, based on appearance alone, that you are poor, that you are less capable, that you need special help. *That* is racism—judging by appearance and assuming inferiority.

The antidote is the gospel: mercy triumphs over judgment. Judge wisely. Love your neighbor as yourself.
