# Luke 3:1-20

**Title:** Preparing for the New Year
**Date:** January 2003
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction

As we enter a new year, we naturally think about preparation. Some of us plan vacations, organize our homes, or get a new diary. Others archive emails to keep our systems running smoothly. But John the Baptist teaches us something far more important: how to prepare spiritually for what God is doing in our lives.

> A voice of one calling in the desert,
> "Prepare the way for the Lord,
> make straight paths for him." (Isaiah 40:3)

Today, Luke shows us what it means to truly prepare for the coming of Christ.

## Two Kingdoms: Roman Rule vs. God's Reign

Luke begins with remarkable historical precision, naming the rulers of his time:

> In the fifteenth year of the reign of Tiberius Caesar—when Pontius Pilate was governor of Judea, Herod tetrarch of Galilee, his brother Philip tetrarch of Iturea and Traconitis, and Lysanias tetrarch of Abilene—during the high priesthood of Annas and Caiaphas, the word of God came to John son of Zechariah in the desert. (Luke 3:1-2)

This is no accident. Luke, the careful historian, is drawing a contrast between two kinds of rule: human authority and God's kingdom.

### Roman Governance

The Roman Empire was the most organized government of the ancient world—a well-oiled machine with Caesar at the top, governors and prefects carrying out his orders, local tetrarchs enforcing his will, and high priests managing religious affairs. Through this chain of command, Caesar's word became law across the empire. The people paid taxes, submitted to authority, and lived under Caesar's rule.

### God's Rule

But Luke points us to another ruler. When John the Baptist emerges in the desert, he announces something revolutionary:

> The Lord isn't Caesar. The Lord is God. The Lord reigns. The Lord is coming.

He quotes Isaiah:

> Every valley shall be filled in, every mountain and hill made low. The crooked roads shall become straight, the rough ways smooth. And all mankind will see God's salvation. (Luke 3:5-6)

God doesn't merely assign names to territories—He remakes the very land into His dominion. He reshapes geography itself.

### Time Belongs to God

The Romans numbered their years by Caesar's reign—the fifteenth year of Tiberius. They gave us our calendar, with months named after emperors: July after Julius Caesar, August after Augustus. But something extraordinary happened in history. Around the year 525, a Christian named Dionysius Exiguus recalibrated the calendar to *Anno Domini*—the Year of Our Lord. Every time we write a year now, we're acknowledging that it's not Caesar's year, but Christ's year.

When we say 2013 (or whatever year we're living in), we're declaring: "This is the 2013th year of the reign of the Lord Jesus Christ." Not the fifteenth year of Tiberius. Not the thirtieth year of Augustus. The year of our Lord.

### Application: Life Under Christ's Rule

As Christians, we live under Christ's sovereignty. We must grasp what this means:

- **Time is God's.** This new year isn't yours to do with as you please—it belongs to the Lord. Your holidays, your work hours, your days of rest—all His.
- **Property is God's.** Your bank account, your car, your clothes, your land—you're a steward, not an owner.
- **Your obedience is God's.** Your allegiance, your morality, your choices—they belong to Him.

This isn't restriction. It's freedom. When you stop living for yourself and start living for Christ's kingdom, everything changes.

## Fruit in Keeping with Repentance

John's message was direct and challenging. To the crowds coming for baptism, he said:

> You brood of vipers! Who warned you to flee from the coming wrath? Produce fruit in keeping with repentance. And do not begin to say to yourselves, "We have Abraham as our father." For I tell you that out of these stones God can raise up children for Abraham. The ax is already at the root of the trees, and every tree that does not produce good fruit will be cut down and thrown into the fire. (Luke 3:7-9)

John was warning against spiritual complacency. Some in the crowd relied on their heritage—they thought being descendants of Abraham was enough. But spiritual inheritance must become personal faith. If you grew up in a Christian home, don't assume your parents' faith is yours. Make it your own.

### Practical Fruit for Different People

When the crowds asked, "What should we do?" John gave specific, practical counsel:

**To the crowds (generosity):**
> The man with two tunics should share with him who has none, and the one who has food should do the same. (Luke 3:11)

**To tax collectors (honesty):**
> Don't collect any more than you are required to. (Luke 3:13)

Tax collectors had a reputation for extortion. John calls them to integrity in their work.

**To soldiers (contentment):**
> Don't extort money and don't accuse people falsely—be content with your pay. (Luke 3:14)

Both tax collectors and soldiers served the Roman Empire, yet John calls them to follow God's standards within their work. Their profession doesn't excuse them from righteousness.

### Fruit for Different Seasons of Life

**High school students:** Don't rely solely on your parents' faith. Train yourself in Christian knowledge through church, Bible study, and spiritual mentorship.

**University students:** Time is your greatest asset right now. You're building habits that will define your life. Don't waste it on endless scrolling, gaming, or part-time work that doesn't matter. Remember: you have an "extra subject"—your faith. Train in Christian knowledge alongside your degree. Serve at church and on campus. Allocate your time wisely. Build God's kingdom first.

**Young graduates:** The first years of work are challenging. Don't drop your Christian fellowship and family relationships to climb the ladder. When your paycheck increases tenfold, don't blow it away. Build good habits: wake early, put in honest work, learn teamwork, submit to authority. Be generous. Consider supporting the church if you believe in the work God is doing here.

**Parents:** As you enter this new year, examine your life. What fruit are you bearing? What are you modeling for your children?

## Proclaim the Year of the Lord's Favor

As John's ministry grew, people began to wonder if he was the Messiah. But John redirected their attention:

> I baptize you with water. But one more powerful than I will come, the thongs of whose sandals I am not worthy to untie. He will baptize you with the Holy Spirit and with fire. His winnowing fork is in his hand to clear his threshing floor and to gather the wheat into his barn, but he will burn up the chaff with unquenchable fire. (Luke 3:16-17)

John's role was clear: prepare the way, proclaim Christ, warn of judgment.

His faithfulness came at a cost. When John rebuked Herod the tetrarch for his sin with Herodias, Herod threw him in prison. Speaking truth about Christ sometimes carries a price.

### Our Call in This New Year

> The people were waiting expectantly... John answered them all... and with many other words John exhorted the people and preached the good news to them. (Luke 3:15-18)

John's example calls us to the same task. The Lord is coming. There's one key reason why Jesus hasn't returned yet: the task remains incomplete. God is still calling people to repentance and faith.

In this new year, let's prepare ourselves mentally and spiritually. Let's equip ourselves to go beyond our comfort zones and proclaim the year of the Lord's favor.

**A practical step:** Think of a friend who needs to encounter Christ. Invite them to church. Help them with transport if needed. Arrive a bit early to help reset the room. Be ready to welcome them warmly. Most importantly, be ready to introduce them to Jesus.

The Lord is coming. Let's get ready.
