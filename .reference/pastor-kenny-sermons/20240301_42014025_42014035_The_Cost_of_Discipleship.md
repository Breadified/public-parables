# Luke 14:25-35

**Title:** The Cost of Discipleship
**Date:** 29 March 2024 (ARISE) | 19 February 2017 (SMAC)
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Question We All Ask

When we're considering any major decision, there's one question we invariably ask: How much does it cost? Whether we're looking at a meal, shopping for a vacuum cleaner, or buying a phone, the price tag matters. When it comes to following Jesus, we can't help but wonder the same thing. What's the cost? What's in the fine print? Where will it hurt?

Jesus addresses this directly. He speaks with large crowds travelling toward Jerusalem and doesn't hide from them what discipleship demands. He lays out the cost upfront.

## The Three Demands of Discipleship

### 1. Loyalty That Transcends All Relationships

> 25 Large crowds were traveling with Jesus, and turning to them he said: 26 "If anyone comes to me and does not hate father and mother, wife and children, brothers and sisters—yes, even their own life—such a person cannot be my disciple.

The word "hate" here is jarring, but we must understand it carefully. Jesus isn't calling us to animosity toward our families. Scripture commands us to honor our parents and love our neighbors. Matthew's parallel account clarifies the meaning:

> Anyone who loves their father or mother more than me is not worthy of me; anyone who loves their son or daughter more than me is not worthy of me. (Matthew 10:37)

The Greek idiom uses "hate" as a comparative term. When we say we "hate shopping," we don't mean actual hatred—we simply prefer something else. Here, Jesus means that our love for Him must surpass all other attachments. Like finding one thing so preferable that everything else pales by comparison.

**The core issue:** When Christ's calling contradicts other voices—our family, our culture, our social media, our schools—who wins? Following Jesus means He takes absolute priority. We listen to Him more than we listen to anyone else, even ourselves.

### 2. Willingness to Die to Self

> 27 And whoever does not carry their cross and follow me cannot be my disciple.

In the Roman world, carrying a cross meant one thing: you were walking to your own execution. This wasn't a metaphor—it was a real symbol of death. Jesus is calling His disciples to die the way He died: through self-sacrifice for the sake of others.

Verse 26 adds another layer: "even their own life." Many people listen to others, or to God, but refuse to listen to themselves. They follow their own desires above all. Jesus demands something radical—that we listen to Him even more than we listen to our own inner voice. Allegiance to Christ surpasses self-interest.

### 3. Surrender of Everything

> 33 In the same way, those of you who do not give up everything you have cannot be my disciples.

This doesn't mean taking a vow of poverty or abandoning your family responsibilities. It means acknowledging that we cannot know what God will ask of us in the future.

Following Jesus means we commit without knowing the cost in advance:

- There may be opportunities to serve that demand everything we have—even our lives
- There may be persecution
- The path may cost our comfort, our ambitions, our relationships, our security

**When you become a Christian, you repent.** You turn away from where you were heading and turn to follow Christ instead. You sign a blank cheque, not knowing how—or how much—God will cash it in. Perhaps He withdraws daily, little by little, as you die to selfishness, sin, and pride. Perhaps He takes out a large lump sum. Either way, it will cost everything.

### The Example of Jim Elliot

Consider Jim Elliot, a young man who gave up a comfortable life to pursue missionary work among unreached tribes in Ecuador. He studied linguistics, met and married Elisabeth, and together they ventured into the jungle. At twenty-eight, in the prime of his life, Jim and four companions were killed by the Huaorani people they were trying to reach on January 8, 1956. He left behind a young widow and a one-year-old daughter.

Jim signed a blank cheque. God chose to cash it that way.

He lived by a remarkable principle: "When the time comes to die, make sure that all you have to do is die." His life was entirely dedicated to God. He had no regrets. This is what it means to follow Jesus.

Many of us live with regrets and worry. But before we turn away, we must honestly weigh the cost against the benefit.

## The Cost-Benefit Analysis of Discipleship

Jesus illustrates this principle through two parables:

### The Tower Builder

> 28 "Suppose one of you wants to build a tower. Won't you first sit down and estimate the cost to see if you have enough money to complete it? 29 For if you lay the foundation and are not able to finish it, everyone who sees it will ridicule you, 30 saying, 'This person began to build and wasn't able to finish.'"

A wise builder calculates whether his resources match his ambitions. To start without finishing brings embarrassment, financial loss, bankruptcy. It's crucial to enter any major commitment with your eyes open.

### The King at War

> 31 "Or suppose a king is about to go to war against another king. Won't he first sit down and consider whether he is able with ten thousand men to oppose the one coming against him with twenty thousand? 32 If he is not able, he will send a delegation while the other is still a long way off and will ask for terms of peace."

This parable introduces something deeper. The foreign king is already marching toward you with superior forces. You face a grim decision: negotiate, surrender land, or be destroyed.

Every option is costly. But here's the key insight: **when something seems too expensive, we must always ask—compared to what?**

The weaker king won't surrender his crown. It's too expensive! But what's the alternative? Destruction or slavery. Suddenly, negotiating doesn't seem quite so costly after all. When you weigh all the options, some choices become rational—even necessary.

### The Hidden Cost of Not Following

There's always a cost to action. But there's also a cost to inaction.

Consider house chores. The cost of action: effort, missing your favorite show. The cost of inaction: cockroaches, rats, spoiled food, sleepless nights startled by shadows. Suddenly, the chore doesn't seem so expensive.

**Similarly, the cost of discipleship is high—undeniably.** But we must weigh it against the cost of not following Jesus:

- The cost of judgment
- The cost of facing a King coming with His army of angels
- The cost of separation from God forever

Which is worse—costly obedience now, or destruction later? Either way, you pay.

### Pascal's Wager Applied

A mathematician named Pascal once worked through this calculation on the question of God's existence. When you actually perform a cost-benefit analysis, following God becomes the most rational choice.

Jesus is not a cruel master. When He calls us to follow Him, He does it for our good. The path may lead through dangerous rivers, rocky terrain, or thorny hedges—but it leads to green pastures. He carries the majority of the cost Himself:

- He hated His own life
- He carried the cross
- He gave up everything

And when you weigh the true costs of not following Jesus against the benefits of following Him, you recognize that **He is worth it.**

## Reordering Your Priorities

The key is not to be paralyzed by cost. The question isn't "How much does it cost?" but rather "Is it worth it?"

A meal costs $50. That seems expensive—until you discover it's an all-you-can-eat seafood buffet with free drinks. Suddenly the price makes sense. Big cost. Big benefits.

I learned very young what my life priorities had to be. Before I became a Christian, I ranked them: education first, family second, everything else third. When I committed to follow Jesus, everything changed:

1. God
2. Everything else

I was anxious about signing that blank cheque. But then I remembered what Christ had done for me. It wasn't blank. It was filled with His life, written in blood. And He cashed it in for me.

Don't worry about the cost. Ask instead: Is this worth it?
