# John 12:1-50

**Title:** The Hour Has Come  
**Date:** 20 February 2015  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## The Wrong Order: Three Episodes Out of Sequence

Life requires doing things in the right order. Marie Kondo teaches us to declutter clothes before books, books before documents, and documents before sentimental items—because the sentimental choices are hardest and take longest. We apply this principle everywhere: floors before ceiling paint, cleaned kitchen before cooking, shampoo before conditioner, saving before spending.

John 12 presents us with the opposite problem. Here we find a variety of episodes, but in the wrong order. Jesus' hour has come, yet the sequence of events seems inverted—as if the ending is being shown before its time. Three crucial moments unfold out of order, each revealing something profound about God's kingdom and how it operates.

---

## A Burial Before Death (1-8)

Six days before Passover, Jesus returns to Bethany where Lazarus—whom he had raised from the dead three months earlier—now lives. A dinner is held in Jesus' honor, with Martha serving and Lazarus reclining at table. Then Mary enters with an expensive perfume, pure nard worth a year's wages (300 denarii, equivalent to roughly $75,000 today). She pours it on Jesus' feet and wipes them with her hair. The fragrance fills the entire house.

We don't know Mary's exact intention—she remains silent. Perhaps she honors Jesus at this meal given in his name. Perhaps she expresses gratitude for her brother's life, using the costly perfume (made partly of myrrh, traditionally used in burial) to anoint his dusty, travel-worn feet.

Judas objects, questioning why the perfume wasn't sold for the poor. John notes pointedly that Judas was a thief who helped himself from the money bag—his concern for the poor masked his true motivations. Jesus doesn't accuse him directly but addresses his objection:

> "Leave her alone," Jesus replied. "It was intended that she should save this perfume for the day of my burial."

Here lies the problem: **embalming happens *after* death, not before**. The order is fundamentally wrong.

In Jewish tradition, burial held deep significance. Abraham purchased a cave to bury Sarah properly (Genesis 23:4-19). Jacob's body was taken from Egypt to Canaan for burial (Genesis 50:4-14). Joseph's bones, though buried in Egypt, were eventually exhumed and brought to Canaan (Joshua 24:32). To lack proper burial was a curse—to be left unburied meant exposure to animals, bones scattered, no dignity in death. This is why Jeremiah's warnings employed such disturbing imagery: bodies left as "dung on the surface of the ground" (Jeremiah 7:33; 8:2).

Yet Jesus seems to affirm Mary's action as preparation for his burial. There's a deeper truth here: Jesus knows what's coming. He acknowledges that this costly act—done while he still lives—will serve as his burial anointing because he will not receive a proper burial after death. As John 20 reveals, after the crucifixion, Joseph of Arimathea and Nicodemus will bring myrrh and aloes to wrap his body. But that's a hasty burial, not the loving, leisurely preparation Mary offers now.

The order is wrong, yet it is right. Mary loves Jesus more than the cost demands. She prioritizes Jesus himself over the poor, understanding instinctively that his presence is temporary and infinitely precious. She does what she can, while he is here.

## A Procession Without an Army (12-16)

The next day, the great festival crowd hears Jesus is coming to Jerusalem. They take palm branches and go out to meet him, shouting:

> "Hosanna! Blessed is he who comes in the name of the Lord! Blessed is the king of Israel!"

Jesus finds a young donkey and sits on it, fulfilling the prophecy of Zechariah 9:9—the king comes righteous and victorious, lowly and riding on a donkey's colt. The crowd sings from Psalm 118, the great processional psalm celebrating God's victory and the righteous entering through the gates.

In ancient times, royal processions were elaborate affairs. After conquest, a king would return with captured gifts, tributes, slaves, soldiers, weapons—a display of power and victory. Think of ticker-tape parades celebrating returning heroes.

But here's the problem: **where is the army?**

The prophecy promises a king and kingship, yet behind Jesus there is no entourage of soldiers, no display of military might, no trailing army. The disciples themselves don't understand. They wouldn't grasp the significance of these events until after Jesus was glorified (verse 16).

It appears premature—all promise, all fanfare, but the substance seems missing. A great trailer with no film to follow. The order seems wrong again.

## People Drawn In Before Being Lifted Up (17-22, 32-33)

The drama intensifies as Jesus reaches the festival. The crowd that witnessed Lazarus' resurrection continues spreading the word. Many come specifically because they heard of this sign. The Pharisees despair, saying to one another:

> "See, this is getting us nowhere. Look how the whole world has gone after him!"

Among those at the festival are Greeks who approach Philip: "Sir, we would like to see Jesus." Philip tells Andrew, and Andrew brings word to Jesus.

Jesus' response is crucial: "The hour has come for the Son of Man to be glorified."

---

### The Hour Refrain

Throughout John's Gospel, Jesus repeatedly says "my hour has not yet come"—in the wedding at Cana (2:4), to the Samaritan woman (4:21), when nearly seized by authorities (7:30; 8:20). The hour represents his glorification, his public revelation, his confrontation with the world's powers. But each time, it wasn't yet his moment to act.

Now, everything converges. The hour has come.

What triggers this announcement? The Greeks seeking him. The whole world drawing near. The chief priests' hyperbolic despair that "everyone has gone after him" contains an ironic truth: Jesus came to save the whole world without distinction (John 3:16). All humankind—Jew or Greek, male or female, slave or free—are the object of his mission. At this Passover, the whole world will witness his hour.

Yet here again the order seems inverted. People are drawn toward Jesus—the crowds, the Greeks—but then he says he must be "lifted up from the earth" to draw all people to himself. After this announcement, Jesus withdraws into private discourse with his disciples, eventually retiring to the upper room. The drawing of multitudes will only happen *after* his death and resurrection, not before.

The incomplete procession. The people gathering before they understand what they're gathering toward. Everything seems out of sequence.

---

## The Right Order (24-26)

Jesus himself reveals why the timing appears wrong. He speaks in terms of a seed:

> "Unless a kernel of wheat falls to the ground and dies, it remains only a single seed. But if it dies, it produces many seeds."

This is the natural order of life: death first, then fruit. A seed placed in soil must break apart, its insides consumed in growth, before any harvest appears. The seed must be obliterated for the plant to live. The order is irreversible and irreplaceable.

Applying this principle:

- **The burial** makes sense only *after* death, not before. Yet Mary's preparation precedes it, because Jesus will not receive a normal burial.
- **The procession** remains incomplete until after his death, when the "army" of all humanity—redeemed through his sacrifice—follows him in truth.
- **The drawing of all people** happens through the cross. His "lifting up" (crucifixion and exaltation) becomes the magnetic center that draws all people toward salvation.

Death must come first. Only through his death can the seed produce many seeds. Only through his death can burial have meaning. Only through his death can the procession be completed and the whole world truly be drawn to him.

## Death First, Then Glory (25-26)

For those who follow Jesus, this same order applies:

> "Anyone who loves their life will lose it, while anyone who hates their life in this world will keep it for eternal life. Whoever serves me must follow me; and where I am, my servant also will be. My Father will honor the one who serves me."

The Christian path mirrors Christ's path: death before glory. Where the master goes, the servant must follow. The temptation is to seek glory now—success in school, advancement in career, prominence in ministry, recognition that validates our efforts. We look to big things, impressive numbers, flashy events for encouragement. We see Christian conventions with beautiful people, Southern Cross magazines filled with success stories, Christianity becoming culturally cool and popular.

But the reality is different. The Christian life today looks like:

- The faithful person quietly inviting friends to church week after week, with little visible fruit
- The unheralded servant preparing a meal for the Bible study group without acknowledgment
- The background helper compiling songsheets, work noticed by almost no one
- The believer staying after church on a cold evening, washing dishes as everyone leaves
- The mother waking through the night to feed her child, praying for them in those quiet hours

These are not glamorous pictures. They are deaths—small, continuous, unglamorous deaths. They are the slow dying to self, the surrender of recognition, the surrender of ease.

Yet this is where true glory lies—not in present accolades, but in future vindication. The Christian's glory comes later, after suffering. As Peter writes:

> "The God of all grace, who called you to his eternal glory in Christ, after you have suffered a little while, will himself restore you and make you strong, firm and steadfast." (1 Peter 5:10)

## Three Responses to the Hour (37-50)

### Those Who See But Do Not Believe (37)

Despite all of Jesus' miraculous signs, many "still would not believe in him." This pattern repeats throughout John: crowds believe when they witness miracles, but when Jesus speaks—when his words demand commitment—they resist.

In John 2, many believed when they saw signs, but Jesus didn't trust them. In John 6, the crowd believed because of the feeding miracle, yet when Jesus speaks difficult truths, they say, "This is a hard saying; who can listen to it?" and walk away. In John 8, those who believed in him attempted to stone him when his teaching conflicted with their assumptions.

The pattern is clear: signs draw crowds, but words divide them. This is crucial: **the problem is never the signs; it's always the words.** The Pharisees never questioned whether the miracles were real. They questioned Jesus' authority to perform them, his interpretation of them, and the radical claims he made about himself.

Jesus himself explains this through an illustration: a road sign pointing to Carlingford Village serves its purpose when you follow it to the destination. You don't stand at the roadside admiring the glazing on the sign or the craftsmanship of the pole. The sign exists to direct you, not to be the destination itself.

In the same way, Jesus' signs point beyond themselves to his identity. They testify that he comes with authority from the Father, that he is the promised Messiah. But signs are not the foundation of genuine belief. The words are.

Today, we face the same temptation: "If only God would do a miracle, then I'll believe." But miracles won't work if we won't listen to the words. We must learn to believe through hearing, not through seeing. The signs have been given and documented; now we must trust the words themselves.

### Those Who Believe But Fear (42-43)

A second group genuinely believes yet remains silent. Even among the authorities, many believed in Jesus. Yet they wouldn't openly acknowledge their faith. Why? Fear. Fear of the Pharisees, fear of being cast out of the synagogue—the center of community and belonging in Jewish culture.

To be expelled from the synagogue meant ostracism, shame, and loss of community identity—a profound consequence in a culture where community was everything. Moreover, these were people of status, with privileges that came from being in good standing with the ruling class.

John cuts to the heart of the matter: "For they loved the glory that comes from man more than the glory that comes from God."

The issue isn't rational—it's spiritual. They chose human approval over God's approval. They chose comfort and status over truth.

**This strikes home in our own context.** Peer pressure doesn't end with adolescence; it merely changes form. We fear being labeled the "intense" person who wants to pray before dinner, the "weird" one who brings up faith in casual conversation, the party pooper who suggests something's not right with a friend's choices. We worry people will think us judgmental or superior.

But what about rebuking and exhorting one another in relationships where we have earned the right to speak? What about calling friends toward consistency between their church life and their outside life? It's hard because we'll be labeled puritans, and our comfort with them will diminish.

The pressure to seek human glory is real. But Jesus offers an alternative. The procession continues, incomplete though it seems. The King comes—not on a warhorse, but on a donkey, the animal of peace. And he comes with forgiveness, not judgment.

### Those Who Hear and Believe (44-50)

The third response—those who truly believe—is presented as a call, a standard to live into:

> "Whoever believes in me believes not in me but in him who sent me. Whoever sees me sees him who sent me. I have come into the world as light, so that whoever believes in me may not remain in darkness."

True belief means trusting Jesus' words and keeping them. These aren't mere intellectual assent but a commitment to live according to what he teaches. The words carry God's authority. They contain eternal life.

Jesus emphasizes: "Blessed are those who have not seen and yet believe" (John 20:29). This is a higher state than signs ever offered. You don't need to see Carlingford Village once you know where it is; the directions themselves suffice.

Throughout John's Gospel, the emphasis is consistent. In John 20:31, the Gospel's summary statement: "These are written so that you may believe that Jesus is the Christ, the Son of God, and that by believing you may have life in his name." We who read these words without having witnessed the signs directly are actually in the paradigm Jesus commends: belief based on hearing, not sight.

Signs point; words sustain. Signs attract; words transform. At the end of the book of signs, John's verdict is clear: the emphasis falls not on what the eyes see, but on what the ears hear and the heart receives.

## The Final Passover

We approach not merely another Passover, but the final Passover. What took two years of ministry (chapters 1-12) will unfold in just two weeks (chapters 13-21). Time slows. Jesus steps back from public ministry into intimate time with his disciples. The hour demands his undivided attention to them.

The hour has come. Death and suffering await. Yet this is the book of glory!

Through this final Passover, through this death and suffering, salvation opens for all—Jew or Greek or Asian, with no distinction. We need not change our identity or culture to approach God. We need only hear and believe the words.

Don't be like the crowds of old who saw but didn't believe, and were condemned. Hear the words of God the Father. Believe them. Trust them. Let them be the foundation of your faith.

The order seemed wrong—burial before death, procession before victory, drawing before lifting up. But God's order is right. Death must come before resurrection. The seed must fall and die before it produces many seeds. You must die to yourself before you can live eternally.

The hour has come. And through this hour, all people can be drawn to salvation.
