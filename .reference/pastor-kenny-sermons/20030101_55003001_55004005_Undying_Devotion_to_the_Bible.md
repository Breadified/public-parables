# 2 Timothy 3:1–4:5

**Title:** Undying Devotion to the Bible
**Date:** January 2003
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Terrible Times

> But mark this: There will be terrible times in the last days. People will be lovers of themselves, lovers of money, boastful, proud, abusive, disobedient to their parents, ungrateful, unholy, without love, unforgiving, slanderous, without self-control, brutal, not lovers of the good, treacherous, rash, conceited, lovers of pleasure rather than lovers of God—having a form of godliness but denying its power. Have nothing to do with them. They are the kind who worm their way into homes and gain control over weak-willed women, who are loaded down with sins and are swayed by all kinds of evil desires. (2 Timothy 3:1–6)

We are living in terrible times. The world around us exhibits the very characteristics Paul warned Timothy about: self-love, greed, pride, disrespect for authority, ingratitude, and an obsession with pleasure rather than God. 

What's striking is that these things haven't changed—we've simply become better at hiding them. The brothels no longer advertise openly. Drunkenness stays confined to its venues. We maintain respectable facades in our selective schools and communities. We present ourselves as nice, civilized people, yet the underlying depravity remains. Don't be fooled by external respectability; the human heart, when separated from God, reveals the same darkness Paul described.

The root cause is clear: the world rejects the acknowledgment of Jesus Christ as Lord and truth.

## Opposition to Truth

> Always learning but never able to acknowledge the truth. Just as Jannes and Jambres opposed Moses, so also these men oppose the truth—men of depraved minds, who, as far as the faith is concerned, are rejected. But they will not get very far because, as in the case of those men, their folly will be clear to everyone. (2 Timothy 3:7–9)

Paul references Jannes and Jambres, traditionally understood to be the magicians in Pharaoh's court who opposed Moses. Just as they resisted the truth God revealed through Moses, so too in Paul's time—and in ours—people set themselves against the truth of the gospel. Their opposition may persist for a time, but ultimately their folly becomes evident to all.

## Persecution of the Godly

> You, however, know all about my teaching, my way of life, my purpose, faith, patience, love, endurance, persecutions, sufferings—what kinds of things happened to me in Antioch, Iconium and Lystra, the persecutions I endured. Yet the Lord rescued me from all of them. In fact, everyone who wants to live a godly life in Christ Jesus will be persecuted, while evil men and impostors will go from bad to worse, deceiving and being deceived. (2 Timothy 3:10–13)

Paul points to his own life as evidence. He has endured persecution for proclaiming truth, yet the Lord rescued him from every trial. This pattern is not unique to Paul—it applies to all who follow Christ. If you commit yourself to living godly in Christ Jesus, you will face opposition. This is not a promise of ease but a promise of God's faithfulness in the midst of hardship.

## The Foundation: Scripture

> But as for you, continue in what you have learned and have become convinced of, because you know those from whom you learned it, and how from infancy you have known the holy Scriptures, which are able to make you wise for salvation through faith in Christ Jesus. All Scripture is God-breathed and is useful for teaching, rebuking, correcting and training in righteousness, so that the man of God may be thoroughly equipped for every good work. (2 Timothy 3:14–17)

Here lies the basis of everything. The Scriptures are our anchor—there is no other means of becoming wise unto salvation. They are not merely a helpful resource or interesting historical document; they are God-breathed, God-expired through the Spirit itself.

Scripture serves multiple purposes: teaching us truth, rebuking our errors, correcting our course, and training us in righteousness. Through these functions, the person of God becomes thoroughly equipped for every good work. This is why devotion to the Bible isn't optional—it's fundamental to spiritual maturity and effective service.

## The Call to Preach the Word

> In the presence of God and of Christ Jesus, who will judge the living and the dead, and in view of his appearing and his kingdom, I give you this charge: Preach the Word; be prepared in season and out of season; correct, rebuke and encourage—with great patience and careful instruction. For the time will come when men will not put up with sound doctrine. Instead, to suit their own desires, they will gather around them a great number of teachers to say what their itching ears want to hear. They will turn their ears away from the truth and turn aside to myths. But you, keep your head in all situations, endure hardship, do the work of an evangelist, discharge all the duties of your ministry. (2 Timothy 4:1–5)

Paul issues a solemn charge before God: preach the Word. This is not limited to the pulpit or formal ministry settings. It means speaking about Christ in conversations, in your home, with your family—in season and out of season, whether circumstances are favorable or difficult.

The warning is equally vital: a time will come when people reject sound doctrine. Instead, they will seek out teachers who affirm their desires and scratch their ears with what feels good. They will abandon truth for myths. In the face of this cultural drift, Timothy—and we—must keep our heads, endure hardship, do the work of an evangelist, and faithfully discharge all the duties of our ministry.

The foundation for all of this is an undying devotion to Scripture itself.
