## What Makes the World Go Round?

We often hear that love makes the world go round. Physically, Newton would point to gravitational forces and inertia. Metaphorically, there's an inner drive in all of us to love and be loved—it's the source of our daily motivation. Interestingly, medieval theologians even combined these ideas, suggesting love physically moved the heavens and earth.

But Jesus offers a radically different answer to this question, one that challenges our most basic assumptions about how we relate to others.

## 1. The Command to Love Enemies

At the conclusion of the Sermon on the Mount, Jesus delivers a shocking statement: "Love your enemies and pray for those who persecute you."

This directly contradicts the prevailing attitude of His time. The common teaching was simple and intuitive: "Love your neighbor and hate your enemy." Look after your friends and protect yourself from your enemies. This principle governed everything—from business dealings to politics to school rivalries. It's the logic of reciprocity: if you help me, I help you. If you don't benefit me, I owe you nothing.

Consider how this worked in practical terms. In the marketplace, if you showed me kindness, you became my friend and I showed you kindness in return. If you didn't, you remained my enemy. This tit-for-tat mentality is deeply ingrained in human nature.

But Jesus says something radically different. Under His new system, reciprocity is abolished. It's no longer eye for an eye or tooth for a tooth, but turn the other cheek. If someone forces you to go one mile, go two. If someone sues you for your tunic, give them your cloak as well. It's no longer "I help you only if you help me," but rather unconditional love toward those who oppose you.

This isn't merely good advice. Jesus presents it as **the mark of following Him**—"that you may be sons of your Father in heaven." Being a child of God means loving enemies. It's part of gaining a reward from the Father.

## 2. God's Providential Love in Creation

Why would Jesus command something so difficult and counterintuitive?

We might guess: "Perhaps it's for evangelism—to lure enemies into lowering their guard so churches grow." Or: "Maybe it's for peace—we want to live without suffering, so we pretend to love them." Or: "Maybe we're just too weak to fight them now, so we'll wait until we're stronger."

But Jesus gives none of these reasons. Instead, He points to something far more fundamental:

> "He causes his sun to rise on the evil and the good, and sends rain on the righteous and the unrighteous."

The reason is **God's providential love**. God loves His creation without discrimination. He sends the sun and rain to everyone—evil and good, righteous and unrighteous alike—without preference or condition.

### Sun and Rain as Life's Foundation

Sun and rain are more than weather. They are the source of all life. Without them, there is no nutrition, no energy, no beauty—no life at all. We often forget this fundamental dependence because we're insulated by refrigerators, supermarkets, technology, and umbrellas. But the reality remains: all life depends on God's provision.

In Psalm 33, we're reminded that God loves the world and all within it. He watches over the mightiest king and the tiniest sparrow. He created this world in Genesis 1, and despite humanity's rebellion against Him, He continues to sustain, nourish, and control it. Indiscriminately, God makes the world literally go round—His hand causes the sun to rise and the rain to fall. Everything is under His providence.

### Providence and Suffering

Here's the profound truth: God's control is so complete that even evil and suffering fall under His sovereignty.

Many people use suffering as an argument against God's existence or control. But Scripture reveals something deeper: God is so sovereign that He can take even Satan's rebellion and humanity's evil, and work them toward His good purposes.

We see this most clearly in Jesus. God orchestrated the entire history of creation to bring about salvation through Christ. Through seismic events—creation, flood, earthquakes. Through individuals—Abraham, Isaac, Jacob, David, Pharaoh, Roman generals, and humble peasants. Through nations—Egypt, Babylon, Assyria, Rome. All these events converged on one point: Jesus.

And at the cross—the climax of evil and suffering—when Jesus hung bleeding and all seemed lost, God's wisdom prevailed. On the third day, He brought good out of evil through the resurrection. Christ defeated death itself.

The same is true with suffering today. It doesn't indicate God's absence; rather, it deepens our resolve to look forward to that final day when Jesus returns and we will see how it was all providential—all because of love.

On that day, we'll finally understand:
- Why I suffered cancer
- Why my baby passed away
- Why I live with diabetes, eczema, or multiple sclerosis
- Why my parents separated
- Why things didn't work out the way I planned

It will all make sense because God was providentially working good for us—drawing us into relationship with Him.

## 3. Therefore, Love Your Enemies

The application flows naturally: Since God loves the world without discrimination—evil and good, righteous and unrighteous—we must do the same.

Love your enemies. Not because you can gain something from them or manipulate them. Not for personal benefit. But because they are loved by God. They are loved by the God who sent His Son to die for them.

This manifests differently depending on our circumstances. In our peaceful city, we may not face enemies in the traditional sense. But for some, non-Christians represent the world's opposed values. For others, it's the bully at school. For others, a relationship that has soured. For still others, a rivalry with a sibling or competitor.

Regardless of the form, the message is clear: love them. This means looking out for their interests. It means being committed to their good, even if they oppose you. It means treating them with the same dignity and care God lavishes on all His creation.

## 4. Who Really Makes the World Go Round

In the end, love does make the world go round—but not the sentimental, feel-good Hallmark version.

It is **God's providential love** that makes the world go round. He loves His creation and everything in it. He sustains it, runs it, controls it—even in this era of sin and rebellion. He causes the sun to rise on everyone.

But there is more. It is also **God's sacrificial love** that makes the world go round. He desperately wants us to be His friends. He was willing to sacrifice Himself on the cross to make that friendship possible. This time, He causes not just the sun, but the Son—the Son of God—to rise for both the righteous and the unrighteous.

If you've never followed Jesus, perhaps today is the day. Recognize that every event in your life—your struggles, your blessings, your friendships, being at this school, a Christian friend inviting you here—all of it has been providential. God has been drawing you toward repentance and belief.

If that's you, respond to His love. Believe in Him. And let your friend know.
