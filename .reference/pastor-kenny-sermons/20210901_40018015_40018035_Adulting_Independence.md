## Setting the Stage: Forgiveness in Crisis

On September 11, 2001, terrorists coordinated a devastating attack on America. Planes struck the World Trade Center towers, the Pentagon, and one crashed in Pennsylvania. Nearly 3,000 people died, with over 25,000 injured. For an hour and a half, people trapped above the impact zones faced an unimaginable choice: die in flames or jump. Over 400 firefighters and police rushed into the buildings, only to be buried in the collapse.

This tragedy raises the hardest question about forgiveness: *How can we forgive those who sin so gravely against us? When there is so much raw emotion and anger? When they are not even sorry? Can we forgive the unrepentant? Can you forgive those dead terrorists, forever beyond our reach?*

At the trials of those responsible, families spoke their pain:
- "Forgiveness for them? I'm not that good."
- "When I sit in court with these guys, can I forgive them? I have a hard time. They don't want my forgiveness. I think justice is the word."
- "Her whole life was taken away from her. There is no way I could ever forgive them. I want justice, not forgiveness. I'm still very bitter. Rage."

These are not rhetorical questions. They echo in the hearts of millions.

## Understanding Adulthood Through Scripture

This series explores the nature of adulthood. Last week we examined personal responsibility. This week, through Matthew 18, we look at how mature people respond to perceived wrongs.

A quick note on our approach: We're using a parable as a launching point to explore related biblical themes. The Bible is rich literature that operates on multiple levels—historical, metaphorical, philosophical, poetic, and allegorical. Even those outside the faith can appreciate its literary depth and wisdom.

## The Process of Reconciliation (Matthew 18:15-17)

The Bible gives us a clear reconciliation process.

### Step One: Direct Conversation

> If your brother or sister sins, go and point out their fault, just between the two of you. If they listen to you, you have won them over. (Matthew 18:15)

**"Go and point out their fault"** means naming the perceived wrong directly and privately. This is the mature way. As adults, we cannot read minds. A frown could mean anything—genuine offense or just a bad breakfast. Adults take responsibility for communicating their thoughts clearly and for not assuming what others feel.

As one psychologist puts it: "When we act like adults, we tell others how we feel and ask them what they feel. We do not make statements about what they should feel, do or say."

**"Just between the two of you"** prevents triangulation—that dysfunctional pattern of venting to a third party instead of addressing the issue directly. If they listen and repent, you've won them over. You've brought back a lost sheep, and there is rejoicing in that reconciliation.

### Step Two: Escalation with Witnesses

If they refuse to listen, the process escalates:

> Take one or two others along, so that 'every matter may be established by the testimony of two or three witnesses.' (Matthew 18:16)

The witnesses serve multiple purposes. They protect you from false accusation—perhaps you're in the wrong. They provide another voice that might convince the other party. And they establish whether genuine repentance has occurred. If the person now listens and repents, there is again reason to rejoice.

**This reconciliation process is unlimited.** You can repeat it as many times as needed. When Peter asks, "Lord, how many times shall I forgive my brother or sister who sins against me? Up to seven times?" Jesus answers, "Not seven times, but seventy-seven times" (Matthew 18:21-22).

"Seven" carries Hebrew symbolic meaning—completeness. Seventy-seven times is not a literal voucher system where forgiveness runs out. It means forgive as much as it takes. Yes, we must be discerning about genuine versus manipulative repentance. But when sincere, there is no limit.

We live in community. We step on each other's toes. We say wrong things, break promises, gossip, project our insecurities, harbor jealousy. In families especially, we annoy, swear, hit, lie, steal, hoard, ignore, and abandon. This is the reality of human relationship.

### Step Three: Community Accountability

If they still refuse to listen:

> Tell it to the church; and if they refuse to listen even to the church, treat them as you would a pagan or a tax collector. (Matthew 18:17)

The matter moves beyond your hands to a wider authority. The church—a community of others not initially involved—now hears the case. Their role is to test whether genuine repentance exists or whether your accusation was false.

What "treat them as a pagan or tax collector" means is debated. Jesus himself associated with both groups, so it's not simply rejection. Rather, it may mean recognizing them as spiritually immature and starting over in witness. Or it may mean limiting fellowship, as Paul writes:

> You must not associate with anyone who claims to be a brother or sister but is sexually immoral or greedy, an idolater or slanderer, a drunkard or swindler. Do not even eat with such people. (1 Corinthians 5:11-12)

What's certain is this: **the responsibility is no longer yours.** You've done your part. Now it rests with another authority. This is a crucial reminder: **We cannot control the actions of others.** We can only control how we relate.

## The Heart of Maturity: Emotional Independence

This truth—that we control only ourselves—cuts to the heart of adulthood.

We live in an age where immaturity has become normalized. When someone disagrees with us, we don't discuss—we scream, walk off, stage protests, delete friends. We throw tantrums. Some defend this as reasonable, claiming: "If I cannot control my feelings, it is your fault." 

But consider a child in a shopping center. The mother says no to a toy. The child drops to the floor, kicking and screaming. It's embarrassing—and it's childish.

Adults have learned something crucial: **The only person we can change and control is ourselves.**

This is what psychology calls "differentiation." Mature adults can:
- Differentiate their thoughts from others' thoughts
- Differentiate their thoughts from their own feelings
- Form informed opinions they hold without needing constant external validation
- Develop a "solid self"

They express emotions appropriately—grief when there's loss, joy at celebration. They don't mimic others' feelings (sympathy) but rather understand what others experience because they've walked similar paths (empathy).

Undifferentiated people cannot separate feelings from thoughts. They're flooded by emotion, react rather than respond, and mimic views they've heard rather than developing their own. This is childish, regardless of age.

Paul writes to the Ephesians about this spiritual maturity:

> Then we will no longer be infants, tossed back and forth by the waves, and blown here and there by every wind of teaching and by the cunning and craftiness of people in their deceitful scheming. Instead, speaking the truth in love, we will grow to become in every respect the mature body of Christ. (Ephesians 4:14-15)

A child's identity is fluid. This month Pokemon, next month Transformers. This best friend is tomorrow's enemy. That's normal childhood. But adults have formed settled convictions. They've searched for truth, reached tentative conclusions, and are willing to test them further. They don't simply follow authority or the majority. They commit. They have ownership over their views.

And here's the beautiful part: When you respond as a mature person, your response generates mature responses from others. You can even break multi-generational patterns. In another's chaos, you remain in control. **You can forgive.**

## Why We Forgive: Because We Have Been Forgiven (Matthew 18:23-35)

The reason we forgive is simple: we have been forgiven.

Jesus tells a parable:

> The kingdom of heaven is like a king who wanted to settle accounts with his servants. As he began, a man who owed him ten thousand bags of gold was brought to him. Since he was not able to pay, the master ordered that he and his wife and his children and all that he had be sold to repay the debt. (Matthew 18:23-25)

In those days, there were no bankruptcy laws. The debtor would be sent away, his family sold into servitude—a debt impossible to repay.

> At this the servant fell on his knees before him. "Be patient with me," he begged, "and I will pay back everything." (Matthew 18:26)

He asks for patience, for time to repay. But the master goes far beyond what was asked:

> The servant's master took pity on him, canceled the debt and let him go. (Matthew 18:27)

This is the story of Christ's love for us. To forgive means to take on the burden. It means canceling the debt, releasing pride, letting go of bitterness. It means valuing the other person, seeking relationship, working for reconciliation.

But then:

> When that servant went out, he found one of his fellow servants who owed him a hundred silver coins. He grabbed him and began to choke him. "Pay back what you owe me!" he demanded. His fellow servant fell to his knees and begged him, "Be patient with me, and I will pay it back." But he refused. Instead, he went off and had the man thrown into prison until he could pay the debt. (Matthew 18:28-30)

The contrast is brutal. The servant who was forgiven an enormous debt showed no mercy to someone who owed far less.

> Then the master called the servant in. "You wicked servant," he said, "I canceled all that debt of yours because you begged me to. Shouldn't you have had mercy on your fellow servant just as I had on you?" (Matthew 18:32-33)

**We forgive because we have been forgiven.**

Note that adult forgiveness is not casual—"whatever," "no worries," "it's nothing." **Forgiveness means counting the cost, absorbing the hurt, choosing no longer to hold it against them.** Jesus took our burden. He took the penalty. He paid it.

Forgiveness is the act of taking on a cost that should be borne by another. Those who receive forgiveness receive it freely—but it's not free. Someone else has paid.

This may answer some hard questions about forgiveness. Can you forgive someone beyond your reach, like the September 11 terrorists? If forgiveness is paying a debt, you might. It doesn't require the other person's repentance. It's your choice to no longer carry the weight.

Luke 17:3 says, "If your brother or sister sins against you, rebuke them; and if they repent, forgive them." This seems to require repentance. But perhaps the nature of forgiveness—bearing the cost yourself—means you can forgive unilaterally. There may be no reconciliation, but you release yourself from the burden of the debt.

Even on the cross, Jesus prayed, "Father, forgive them, for they do not know what they are doing" (Luke 23:34). His forgiveness wasn't contingent on their repentance.

When we realize we're simply passing on what was given to us—that our phone, our life, our everything was free to begin with—maybe we can cut others slack. If we've received forgiveness for every selfish thing we've done, how can we not forgive others' petty grievances?

## You Are the Adult (Matthew 18:34-35, 6:6-9)

The parable's conclusion carries weight:

> In anger his master handed him over to the jailers to be tortured, until he should pay back all he owed. "This is how my heavenly Father will treat each of you unless you forgive your brother or sister from your heart." (Matthew 18:34-35)

More is expected from adults. There is greater responsibility.

> If anyone causes one of these little ones—those who believe in me—to stumble, it would be better for them to have a large millstone hung around their neck and to be drowned in the depths of the sea. (Matthew 18:6)

"Little ones" in Scripture refers not just to children but to vulnerable believers—those new, weak, immature, or unlearned. The "little ones" of verse 6 are disciples vulnerable to stumbling.

**"Cause to stumble"** means putting an obstacle in their path so they trip and fall. There's bitter irony: put a stone in the way, and the punishment is a stone around the neck, drowning in the sea.

The point is clear: **More responsibility is expected from the adult.** Adults look after the vulnerable.

Think about a family fight. Are you caught up in the emotion, adding fuel to fire? Or are you the adult? The adult is not necessarily the oldest—it's the one who takes responsibility for their own emotions and behavior. Adults regulate themselves and look after the vulnerable.

If you think the other person is wrong, you should know better, yes? So you won't scream in frustration. You'll stay calm and speak clearly. You'll let them know your thoughts directly before it reaches the church. You'll be that responsible adult.

## The Call to Mature Independence

Immaturity is fracturing us. We can no longer talk about disagreements. We cannot find solutions that don't demand the loss of liberty. We've lost forgiveness and tolerance. We've lost control of our emotions. We think only in black and white.

But you are not like that.

Christians are those who remember they've been forgiven. And because of that, they can differentiate. They can stay calm. They can speak truth. They can maintain their integrity while respecting another's right to disagree.

Anger can ruin lives. Some families who lost loved ones on September 11 found strength through faith. One mother who lost her son at the Pentagon said: "I know he is with me. I believe very firmly, and I always have." Another said: "The Lord will help you through anything in life. You just have to ask."

These are adults. They've chosen maturity in the midst of unimaginable loss.

Adulthood is independence—not from relationship, but in it. It's the courage to be yourself, to hold your convictions, to control your emotions, and to forgive lavishly because you've been forgiven.

**You are the adult.**
