# Matthew 16:13-28

**Title:** Discipleship: Follow the Son!  
**Date:** 8 February 2010  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Misaligned Expectations

Jesus' teaching catches us off guard, much like the surprise that swept through history. In 1281, Japan faced invasion by Kublai Khan's Mongol forces—massive fleets carrying tens of thousands of troops. But just as victory seemed certain, a great typhoon destroyed the entire army. The Japanese called this salvation the "Divine Wind," or *kamikaze*.

Centuries later, the term took on new meaning. In 1945, when Japan faced defeat in the Pacific, military leaders recognized that direct assault wasn't working. One accidental crash, they calculated, caused more damage than ten planes firing weapons. So they revived the name *kamikaze*—pilots would crash planes loaded with explosives into enemy ships. The strategy worked because it was completely unexpected. Americans couldn't comprehend the mentality behind such sacrifice.

We are caught off guard by Jesus' statement today in much the same way.

## Who Do People Say I Am?

### Jesus as Prophet

When Jesus asked his disciples, "Who do people say the Son of Man is?" (v. 13), they reported: "Some say John the Baptist; others say Elijah; and still others, Jeremiah or one of the prophets" (v. 14).

These weren't random guesses. They represented the greatest heroes of Israel:

- **John the Baptist** commanded such power that King Herod feared him
- **Elijah** stood alone against 400 prophets of Baal—the greatest of the Old Testament prophets  
- **Jeremiah** spoke not just to Israel but to the nations themselves

Jesus was expected to be another great prophet—a messenger from God who would speak God's truth and foretell the future. In this, the people were correct. Jesus even spoke of himself this way: "No prophet is accepted in his own native place" (Luke 4:24).

### Peter's Confession: Jesus as King

But when Jesus pressed deeper—"Who do *you* say I am?"—Peter answered with something far greater: "You are the Messiah, the Son of the living God" (v. 16).

The term "Messiah" (Greek: *Christ*) literally means "the Anointed One." In Israel, kings were anointed with oil, not crowned. Peter wasn't just calling Jesus a prophet; he was declaring him King.

This carried enormous weight. Peter was saying:

- You are the King the prophets spoke about
- You will rule the whole world and bring justice
- You will save the oppressed and humble the proud
- You will defeat the Roman invaders and free Israel

And here's the striking thing: **Jesus received this confession with joy and blessing.** He affirmed Peter's insight came not from human reasoning but from God the Father himself. Then he declared: "On this rock I will build my church, and the gates of Hades will not overcome it" (vv. 17-18).

The church—God's gathering—would begin with Peter's confession of Jesus as King and Savior. This echoed Israel's history: first gathered at Mount Sinai, then at Jerusalem in the temple, then scattered in exile with the promise of future regathering. Now Jesus announces the ultimate gathering would be built on faith in him.

No wonder expectations were high. Jesus was both Prophet and King—the culmination of Israel's hopes.

## The Shocking Turn: A Dying King

And then, into this charged, excited atmosphere, Jesus announces something that shatters everything: **he must go to Jerusalem, suffer, be killed, and be raised on the third day** (v. 21).

This makes no sense. Kings don't announce their death. Leaders don't step down. The powerful in this world cling to power—think of CEOs being grilled in Congress, or autocrats refusing to release authority. Once you gain power, you keep it.

It's like scoring 99.95 in your final exams and then announcing you're going to work at a supermarket. Your parents would be appalled.

Peter's reaction was visceral. He pulled Jesus aside and **rebuked him**: "Never, Lord! This shall never happen to you!" (v. 22). Peter spoke to Jesus like a child being corrected, as if to say: *Jesus, you don't understand how the world works. Let me teach you.*

But Peter didn't understand what it means to be a radical disciple. Jesus' response was swift and severe: "Get behind me, Satan! You are a stumbling block to me; you do not have in mind the concerns of God, but merely human concerns" (v. 23).

The irony is sharp: Peter, the rock on which the church would be built, had become a *stumbling block*. Jesus called him Satan—not demon-possessed, but an *adversary*, working against God's purposes.

**The core problem:** Peter wanted Jesus to avoid death because he had "in mind the things of men, not the things of God." Yes, Peter likely spoke from love—he didn't want his master to die. But there was also self-interest at stake. If your leader dies, who comes next? For disciples freshly gathered around Jesus, this looked like a death sentence for all of them. It was like joining the Imperial Navy just as they were recruiting *kamikaze* pilots.

Self-interest is the very thing Jesus wants us to abandon.

## Disciples Must Die Too

Jesus then turned to all his disciples with the radical call:

> **"Whoever wants to be my disciple must deny themselves and take up their cross and follow me."** (v. 24)

When Jesus says "Get behind me," he doesn't mean "Leave me." He means "Come, follow me." The call to discipleship demands:

- **Deny yourself**—surrender your autonomy, your plans, your desires
- **Take up your cross**—embrace death as your reality
- **Follow me**—keep your eyes on Jesus, not on comfort or security

In the first century, taking up one's cross was no metaphor. Condemned prisoners would carry the heavy crossbeam to their execution, where they'd be attached to the vertical pole and crucified. "Taking up one's cross" meant one thing: **death**.

This is precisely what happened to Jesus. We are sinners condemned by God's judgment to eternal death. But Jesus took that sentence upon himself—he denied himself and carried his cross so that we wouldn't have to. A great exchange has taken place.

And now, his followers are called to the same pattern. Not to literal crucifixion, but to a fundamental reorientation of life: **the slow, deliberate putting to death of our own interests for the interests of Christ.**

Notice: this is not the quick, impulsive sacrifice of a *kamikaze* pilot. This is not self-centered terrorism seeking personal reward. This is the daily, intentional sacrifice motivated by love for others. It's the constant choosing of others' welfare—especially their spiritual welfare—over our own comfort.

### The Comfortable Life We Plan

The problem is that we want to construct our lives on our own terms. We think:

- *I'll serve, but only after I'm rich and successful, so people will respect me*
- *I'll do the unpopular thing once I've become popular*
- *I'll sacrifice when it doesn't hurt to sacrifice*
- *I'll serve, but only under these conditions...*

We create acronyms like WWJD (What Would Jesus Do?) as if we don't already know the answer. We hide behind phrases about "glorifying God in everything"—true enough, but not everything we do glorifies God.

Jesus demands something different. He wants **100%, not 10%**. He asks: What are your deepest ambitions? Are they your own comfort—a nice house in a nice suburb, nice furniture, nice car, regular holidays, church attendance when convenient? Or is your ambition to **slowly die for others**?

Consider what radical discipleship actually looks like:

- Giving up social media to witness to your family
- Organizing your life so your poorer friends aren't burdened by expense
- Viewing university primarily as training for gospel ministry, with your degree as secondary
- Taking opportunities to speak about Jesus to strangers rather than attending another party
- Living simply so you can give generously
- Leaving home and comfort to serve as a pastor or missionary
- Doing what is *needed* for others rather than what you're comfortable with

During times like a pandemic, we're tested. The government tells us to do nothing, and we gladly accept the excuse. But we should ask: *Is what we're currently doing good for God's kingdom? Are we living for ourselves or for others? Does gospel work stop because of the possibility of sickness?*

## The Promise: Life Through Loss

Yet Jesus offers profound encouragement to those who embrace this costly calling:

> **"For whoever wants to save their life will lose it, but whoever loses their life for me will find it. What good will it be for someone to gain the whole world, yet forfeit their soul? Or what can anyone give in exchange for their soul?"** (vv. 25-26)

Here's the paradox: **losing your life for Jesus is the way to find it.** Clutching at comfort, security, and pleasure leads to spiritual death. But radical self-surrender leads to life—real, abundant life.

Jesus also offers perspective on what should and shouldn't frighten us:

- Death should not be our fear
- Poverty and suffering should not be our fear  
- Lacking status or popularity should not be our fear
- The only thing worth fearing is losing our soul

But if Jesus has secured your eternal life, there is nothing left to fear.

> **"For the Son of Man is going to come in his Father's glory with his angels, and then he will reward each person according to what they have done."** (v. 27)

Jesus will return in judgment and glory. He will reward those who have lived for him and his kingdom. This is the Christian's hope: not comfort in this age, but vindication and reward when Christ returns.

The martyred missionary Jim Elliott captured this truth perfectly: **"He is no fool to give up what he cannot keep to gain what he cannot lose."**

## Are You a Radical Follower?

When Japan recruited *kamikaze* pilots, their criteria were simple: youth, alertness, and zeal. Flight experience was secondary; expertise in landing was irrelevant. They didn't need pilots who knew how to survive.

The Christian calling has similar criteria:

- **Deny yourself and follow Jesus**
- **Youth, alertness, and zeal**
- **Life is not necessary; the world is a luxury**

We are called to be radical followers of Christ—not primarily concerned with how we "land" or what comfort we end with. We are called to live lives that are **other-centered and Christ-centered.**

The world watches to see if Christians truly believe what we claim. Julian, a Roman emperor in the fourth century, grudgingly admitted: "Nothing has contributed to the progress of Christianity as their charity to strangers... the Galileans provide not only for their own poor, but for ours as well."

Our faith is revealed not in our words but in our willingness to lose everything for the gospel's sake.

So the question Jesus poses to each of us is unavoidable: **Are you committed to being my disciple? Are you willing to deny yourself, take up your cross, and follow me?**
