## Understanding Slavery in Context

Before examining this passage, we must understand what slavery meant in biblical times versus the modern slave trade. The word "slavery" encompasses vastly different systems, and conflating them obscures the text's meaning.

### The Evil of Slave Trading

The slave trade—the commercial trafficking of human beings—is explicitly condemned throughout Scripture. Paul lists "slave traders" alongside murderers and perverts as deserving of death (1 Timothy 1:9-10). Under Old Testament law, kidnapping a person was punishable by death (Exodus 21:16; Deuteronomy 24:7).

Scripture demonstrates slavery's evil through Joseph's story. His brothers sold him into Egypt, an act explicitly described as evil (Genesis 45:4). Yet God redeemed this injustice: "You intended to harm me, but God intended it for good" (Genesis 50:20).

The 18th and 19th centuries witnessed terrible injustice—Africans kidnapped from families, traded across the Atlantic, and commercialized as commodities. This represents the slave trade condemned by Scripture.

### Christianity's Response to Slavery

The early Christian church actively opposed slavery and worked to eliminate it:

- Around 90 AD, church leaders like Clement of Rome freed slaves with their own resources
- By 395 AD, Augustine noted that Christians regularly purchased and freed kidnapped victims, including 120 slaves the Galatians were boarding onto their ships
- In 284-305 AD, Chromatius emancipated 1,400 slaves after their baptism
- In 1102, the London Church Council forbade slavery and the slave trade, freeing 10% of England's population

The Quakers, John Newton, and William Wilberforce led the abolition movement in England. America fought a civil war—costing 600,000 lives—to end slavery. No other nation sacrificed so much for this cause.

Yet with Christianity's decline, slavery has returned. Today it manifests through people smuggling and refugee trafficking—the same business model as historical slavery.

### Slavery as a System vs. Abuse of That System

Every civilization has practiced some form of servitude. Vikings enslaved Europeans. Africans enslaved other Africans. Moors enslaved Europeans for 400 years. Chinese were enslaved by Japanese. This was not peculiar to any single race or culture.

The key distinction: **slavery as an institution is different from slave trading**. One is a way of organizing society (like democracy or monarchy). The other is the abuse of that system.

## Good Servitude: Different Forms

The Bible recognizes several legitimate forms of servitude, each with different rules and protections.

### Debtors

Those who could not pay debts could work them off. Exodus 21:2 provides: "If you buy a Hebrew servant, he is to serve you for six years. But in the seventh year, he shall go free, without paying anything."

This resembles modern indentured servitude or voluntary work arrangements—a person exchanges labor for economic assistance.

### Criminals and Thieves

Those who committed crimes could be sentenced to servitude as restitution. Exodus 22:3 states: "Anyone who steals must certainly make restitution, but if they have nothing, they must be sold to pay for their theft."

This is more just than modern incarceration. A criminal working to repay what they stole serves the community they injured and contributes productively rather than consuming resources in prison.

Australia's first convicts were transported as slaves for crimes like theft and forgery. Many—like James Ruse and Francis Howard Greenway—became productive members of society, transforming the land into an agricultural paradise.

### Prisoners of War

What do you do with enemy combatants? The alternatives are limited:

- You cannot release them back to their own country
- You cannot let them roam freely in your territory
- Without prisons, exile is the only viable option
- Lifelong bondage is arguably more merciful than death

Deuteronomy 20:10-11 permits taking captives as forced laborers. This represents a humane alternative to execution in warfare.

### Voluntary Servants

Some people voluntarily sold themselves into servitude to escape poverty. Leviticus 25:39 protects such servants: "If any of your fellow Israelites become poor and sell themselves to you, do not make them work as slaves. They are to be treated as hired workers or temporary residents among you; they are to work for you until the Year of Jubilee."

Many migrants today work multiple jobs, sweatshops, and night shifts—modern slavery by choice for economic survival. Filipino maids in Hong Kong and Indonesian maids in Malaysia work with restricted passports and movement, separated from families for years, to build houses and educate children back home. They sacrifice for future generations.

## Biblical Protections for Servants

The Bible radically reformed slavery by granting servants extensive rights—so much so that some scholars argue it was not slavery in the ordinary sense, but a total revamping of the institution.

### For Hebrew Servants

> Exodus 21:2-11
>
> "If you buy a Hebrew servant, he is to serve you for six years. But in the seventh year, he shall go free, without paying anything... If he does not provide her with these three things [food, clothing, marital rights], she is to go free, without any payment of money."

**Remuneration:** Servants received generous provision upon release (Deuteronomy 15:13-14).

**Protection:** Masters who beat servants faced punishment if they died (Exodus 21:20-21).

**Compensation:** If a master destroyed a servant's eye or tooth, the servant went free (Exodus 21:26-27).

**Rest:** Servants received the Sabbath rest like free persons (Exodus 23:12).

**Rights:** Foreign servants could participate in religious life (Exodus 12:44).

**Limited Duration:** Hebrew servants served a maximum of six years, with release in the Year of Jubilee (Exodus 21:2; Leviticus 25:40).

**Voluntary Extension:** If a servant loved his master and chose to stay, he was marked with an earring at the doorpost, serving for life (Exodus 21:5-6).

### For Foreign Servants

Foreign servants could be held permanently (Leviticus 25:44-46), yet they too participated in religious life and received protections. The Gibeonites, realizing the benefits of servitude under God's people, voluntarily sought to become Israel's servants (Joshua 9).

This raises a profound question: Would those foreigners have been better off as servants in Israel—participating in true worship—than free in spiritual darkness?

## Application: Servitude Continues Today

Every civilization practices some form of servitude. Today, most of us are employees—modern servants with better conditions than past generations:

- **Scholarships with service requirements:** Students work for sponsors after graduation
- **Restraint of trade:** Employees cannot use their expertise locally if they leave
- **Military service:** Volunteers serve with restricted freedom
- **Internships:** Young people work without pay for experience
- **Mortgages and HECS debt:** Homeowners and graduates work to repay obligations

We accept these arrangements because the trade-off seems fair: work now for future stability.

If you feel like a slave at work, you probably are—but that's not inherently wrong. The issue is whether the system uses authority responsibly or abuses it.

## Submission to Harsh Masters

> 1 Peter 2:18 Slaves, submit yourselves to your masters with all respect, not only to those who are good and considerate, but also to those who are harsh.

Peter calls servants to submit even to harsh masters. This challenges our modern assumption that submission depends on the master's character.

### Submission is to the Office, Not the Person

We submit because of the position held, not the merit of the person occupying it. You submit to a principal because they hold the office of principal, not necessarily because they are competent or kind. Similarly, a servant submits to a harsh master because he holds the office of master.

This protects us from a dangerous alternative: submission based on personality. Such conditional submission allows people to choose which authorities they obey based on feelings, creating chaos.

### Why Harsh Masters Matter

> 1 Peter 2:19 For it is commendable if a man bears up under the pain of unjust suffering because he is conscious of God.

The real test of submission comes when it costs something. Bearing up under unjust suffering while conscious of God—submitting when the authority doesn't deserve it—demonstrates genuine faith.

If submission only applied to good masters, anyone could do it. Peter writes precisely because not all masters are good. Some hurl insults. Some are harsh. Some overlook your work or demand impossible hours.

## The Example of Sarah

> 1 Peter 3:5-6 For this is the way the holy women of the past who put their hope in God used to make themselves beautiful. They were submissive to their own husbands, like Sarah, who obeyed Abraham and called him her master.

Sarah was no pushover. She laughed at an angel (Genesis 18:10-12). She expelled Hagar when necessary (Genesis 21:10). Yet she called Abraham "master"—using the same word Peter uses for Christ's lordship.

Her submission was unqualified. Not restricted to big decisions, spiritual matters, or godly directives. Not dependent on whether they got along or whether she felt in love. She submitted because he held the position of husband.

Sarah is presented in Galatians as the symbol of Christian freedom. Her submission did not diminish her; it represented her freedom as a daughter of Abraham and heir of the promise.

## Addressing Objections

### What About Domestic Abuse?

Abuse is terrible and inexcusable. But the solution is not to dilute the husband's authority—it is to call for its correct use.

Police abuse exists, yet we don't replace police with fire departments or bind their hands. We call for better policing.

Similarly, husbands need authority to fulfill their responsibility: presenting their wives as spotless brides without stain or wrinkle (Ephesians 5:27), leading their children in the Lord, and loving their wives as Christ loves the church. These are unqualified responsibilities requiring unqualified authority.

In extreme cases, legal authority limits an abusive husband's power. Seek the police. Withdraw temporarily. Get counseling. But don't reject the principle of authority because it is abused.

### What About Mutual Submission?

Some cite Galatians 3:28 ("there is neither male nor female") and Ephesians 5:21 ("submit to one another") as evidence for symmetrical mutual submission.

But Galatians addresses salvation equality, not the elimination of gender or social roles. And Ephesians 5:21's call to mutual submission introduces different relationships—each asymmetrical. Only wives are told to submit to husbands, not vice versa.

Mutual submission language can be asymmetrical. "Bear one another's burdens" doesn't mean everyone exchanges burdens equally; the strong help the weak. "So that men slay one another" doesn't require simultaneous death.

Reality is asymmetrical. Someone must lead. Democracy works for philosophy, but not for families making daily decisions. If you take a vote on dinner, the children choose candy. Families require clear authority lines.

Submission is not beautiful only in ideal marriages. It makes difficult marriages work. A godly woman submitting to a difficult husband may do less ministry but preserve her marriage. Peter writes to exactly these situations.

### What If My Husband Is Not a Christian?

Even a godless husband deserves submission. Peter explicitly addresses wives of non-Christian husbands, suggesting submission may win them over (1 Peter 3:1-2).

Submit to the office, not the personality. Just as you submit to a non-Christian employer or government official, you submit to a non-Christian husband. The standard doesn't depend on his faith.

This is costly. It may mean less church involvement, unequal labor, or foregone adventures. But submission makes the marriage endure. And you are "her daughters if you do what is right and do not give way to fear" (1 Peter 3:6).

## True Beauty

> 1 Peter 3:3-4 Your beauty should not come from outward adornment, such as braided hair and the wearing of gold jewelry and fine clothes. Instead, it should be that of your inner self, the unfading beauty of a gentle and quiet spirit, which is of great worth in God's sight.

We have lost sight of true beauty. Extended families once exposed us to aging, illness, and death—natural reminders that physical beauty is fleeting. Grandparents and aunties showed us that what remains is gentle spirit.

Now we surround ourselves with same-age friends, all in the same season of life, reinforced by media showing only the youngest and skinniest. Sisters compare themselves to each other. This is distorted.

God cares far more about your character than your appearance. A plain jane with a pure, reverent heart is truly beautiful. Gentle and quiet spirit—not nagging, manipulating, or argumentative—is of great worth in God's sight.

**For young women:** Don't be pressured by the world. Modest, plain dress is acceptable. Build a gentle spirit.

**For young men:** Stop seeking the physically hottest girls. Pay attention to the godly and modest ones. Your attention shapes what young women aspire to become.

## Submission and the Greater Pattern

Peter calls servants to submit to harsh masters. He calls wives to submit to husbands. He calls all citizens to submit to governments. This is not cultural accommodation—it is a pattern for transformed living.

> 1 Peter 2:21-23 To this you were called, because Christ suffered for you, leaving you an example, that you should follow in his steps. He committed no sin, and no deceit was found in his mouth. When they hurled their insults at him, he did not retaliate; when he suffered, he made no threats. Instead, he entrusted himself to him who judges justly.

Christ is our example of submission. He submitted to unjust suffering without retaliation or threat. He entrusted himself to God who judges justly.

We follow this pattern:

- **We entrust ourselves to God as judge.** Every injustice will be accounted for. We need not be calculative or vengeful. God's justice is perfect.

- **We die to sin and live for righteousness.** We commit no sin ourselves. We don't retaliate, swear, steal, or manipulate to compensate for unfair treatment.

> 1 Peter 2:24 He himself bore our sins in his body on the tree, so that we might die to sins and live for righteousness; by his wounds you have been healed.

By his wounds, we are healed. Through submission to unjust authority, we participate in Christ's redemptive suffering and become witnesses to his grace.

This is the calling. Not comfortable, not always fair, but redemptive—and ultimately vindicated by the one who judges justly.
