# Genesis 12-13

**Title:** A New Hope
**Date:** 15 January 2011
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## The Context: A Kingdom in Ruins

From Genesis 4-11, we've witnessed the progressive collapse of God's kingdom—His people in His place under His rule. What began as one man's sin with Cain has spiraled into a pattern of humanity's rebellion and God's judgment.

- **Genesis 4:** Cain's murder, his wandering, yet God's protection
- **Genesis 5-9:** Noah's generation's wickedness met with the flood, but God preserves one family
- **Genesis 11:** The Tower of Babel—humanity's attempt to build a name for themselves through independence, scattered into nations

Each time, God mitigates the judgment, but the cycle worsens. It would seem reasonable for God to abandon this failed experiment. Instead, our gaze is drawn to a man named Abram in chapter 12—a new hope in the midst of despair.

---

## A New Promise (12:1-3)

God breaks the cycle with a radical pronouncement to Abram:

> The LORD had said to Abram, "Leave your country, your people and your father's household and go to the land I will show you. I will make you into a great nation and I will bless you; I will make your name great, and you will be a blessing. I will bless those who bless you, and whoever curses you I will curse; and all peoples on earth will be blessed through you."

This promise contains two symmetrical elements, each forming a direct response to humanity's rebellion at Babel.

### The Promise of a Great Name

At Babel, the people declared, "Come, let us build ourselves a city, with a tower that reaches to the heavens, so that we may make a name for ourselves." Their unity—one language, one purpose—was weaponized for self-exaltation and the rejection of God's command to fill the earth.

God's judgment shattered that unity. He scattered them by confusing their language, breaking their false ambitions into fragments.

But here is God's mercy: He takes what they sought—a great name—and gives it not to them, but to His chosen man. "I will make your name great." The nations' desire for greatness is redirected through God's covenant.

This promise finds its ultimate fulfillment centuries later at Pentecost. At Babel, unity was broken; at Pentecost, it is restored—not through human language, but through divine comprehension. People from every nation hear God's wonders in their own tongues, united under one gospel and one name: Jesus.

The apostle Paul captures this in Philippians 2:9-11:

> Therefore God exalted him to the highest place and gave him the name that is above every name, that at the name of Jesus every knee should bow, in heaven and on earth and under the earth, and every tongue confess that Jesus Christ is Lord, to the glory of God the Father.

The question for us becomes: Whose name are we building? When we chase titles, credentials, and reputation—"waste consultant," "water engineer," "domestic engineer"—we're echoing Babel's ambition. But a disciple of Jesus asks a different question: Does what I do point to Christ?

### The Promise of a Great Nation

God also promises to make Abram "a great nation"—in other words, a kingdom. God's kingdom consists of three inseparable elements:

**Place:** When Abram arrives in Canaan, he travels through the land, building altars at Shechem, Bethel, and Mamre. He plants himself like a flag on the moon—a symbolic deposit on a land he does not yet possess. The Canaanites still inhabit it.

**People:** God appears to Abram and says, "To your offspring I will give this land." Yet Abram is seventy-five years old and childless. The promise seems impossible.

**Rule:** God Himself is the guarantor. "I will bless you. I will bless those who bless you. I will curse those who curse you." God is the rule-maker, the promise-keeper, the one whose presence ensures fulfillment.

These are audacious promises. The land is occupied by stronger peoples. Abram has no offspring. Yet God commits Himself: one day, Abram's descendants will inherit this land and become a blessing to all the peoples of the earth.

## The Story Unfolds: God's Faithfulness Through History

The Bible is not a collection of timeless wisdom or a cyclical mythology like Eastern tales. It is a linear history—a record of how God fulfills His covenant promises. Genesis 1-11 may present cycles, but Genesis 12 onwards is a story with direction and an end.

As you read Scripture, understand where you stand in this unfolding narrative:

- **Pentateuch (Genesis-Deuteronomy):** The promise of offspring is being fulfilled. A people is being born.
- **Historical Books (Joshua, Judges, Samuel, Kings):** God's people are securing the promised land.
- **The Divided Kingdom (1-2 Kings, 2 Chronicles):** The question becomes, who is the king? Where is David's heir?
- **The Prophets:** The exile shatters everything. No land. No people. No king. Yet the prophets announce that God will re-promise.
- **The New Testament:** The final fulfillment arrives. All of God's promises find their "Yes" in Christ.

Paul writes in 2 Corinthians 1:20:

> For no matter how many promises God has made, they are "Yes" in Christ. And so through him the "Amen" is spoken by us to the glory of God.

What does this fulfillment look like?

- **The People:** The promises to a people are fulfilled in the Person of Christ—the one who perfectly obeys God's commands, whose lips do not lie, whose heart follows His Father's will even unto death.
- **The Place:** God's communion with His people is established not in a physical temple, but in the presence of Christ Himself.
- **The Rule:** The king who sits on David's throne is Jesus, the righteous heir, the royal descendant, the Lord of all.

God is faithful. His promises are sure.

## The Empire Strikes Back (12:10-20)

But Abram is not yet the embodiment of faith. When famine strikes the land—not the modern inconvenience of empty cupboards, but a severe crisis threatening survival—Abram falters.

Instead of trusting God's promise and remaining in the land, he flees to Egypt. Worse, he schemes. Fearing that the Egyptians will kill him for his beautiful wife, he tells Sarai to claim she is his sister. In doing so, Abram essentially abandons his wife to save himself—an act of profound unfaithfulness to both God and his covenant partner.

His plan succeeds materially. Pharaoh takes Sarai and treats Abram well, blessing him with livestock, servants, and wealth. But the same pattern repeats: humanity chooses self-preservation over trust in God.

Yet something different happens this time.

In the past, swift judgment came—the flood, Babel's scattering. Entire peoples were destroyed. But now, rather than annihilate Abram for his failure, God acts differently. He inflicts serious diseases on Pharaoh and his household because of Sarai. Pharaoh, confronted by God's judgment, demands answers from Abram, then sends him away—with his wife restored, his wealth intact, and ironically, allowed to keep the goods obtained through deception.

This is a hint of God's new method: He will not destroy His people and start over. He will rescue them. He will bring them back to the promised land. He will multiply their blessings despite their faithlessness.

---

## The Return (13:1-18)

Abram exits Egypt wealthy and returns to the place where he first built an altar—Bethel. There, having learned nothing, he calls on God's name again.

But now a new problem arises. Abram and his nephew Lot possess so many flocks and herds that the land cannot support them both. Their possessions have become so great that quarreling erupts between their herdsmen.

Rather than assert his right as the elder and the covenant bearer, Abram offers generously: "Let's not quarrel. Is not the whole land before you? Let's part company. If you go left, I'll go right."

Lot, looking at the well-watered plains of Jordan—beautiful as the garden of the LORD, lush as Egypt—chooses for himself and journeys eastward toward Sodom.

And then God speaks to Abram again:

> Lift up your eyes from where you are and look north and south, east and west. All the land that you see I will give to you and your offspring forever. I will make your offspring like the dust of the earth, so that if anyone could count the dust, then your offspring could be counted. Go, walk through the length and breadth of the land, for I am giving it to you.

The promise is repeated, now in greater clarity and with expanded vision. Abram can see further. His offspring will be countless as dust. The land will be his—all of it, as far as the eye can see, as far as his feet can walk.

Abram responds by moving his tents to Hebron, near the great trees of Mamre, and builds another altar to the LORD.

## A New Beginning in Christ

God wants to do something radically different this time. He wants to bless.

After the Fall of Adam, the murder of Cain, the wickedness of Noah's generation, the prideful rebellion at Babel—after centuries of failure—it would seem reasonable for God to declare the experiment finished and destroy humanity entirely.

But God's commitment is stronger than our unfaithfulness. He sets His hope in Abram. This is the last and complete attempt to restore His kingdom: a new people in a new land who will bring blessing to all the earth.

Yet Abram himself never fully realizes the promise. He dies owning nothing but a burial plot at Mamre, where he and Sarah are laid to rest. He sees his offspring multiply, but never like the dust of the earth. He walks through the land but never possesses it.

But we know how the story ends. The promises made to Abram find their ultimate fulfillment in Christ. Jesus is the offspring who inherits all things. He is the King whose name is above every name. He is the blessing that flows to all peoples on earth.

Like Abram, who stepped out in faith toward a land he had never seen, trusting God's promise with nothing but God's word, let us step out in faith toward Jesus. But we have something Abram never had: we know what lies before us. We have seen the resurrection. We have tasted His mercy. We have the Spirit as our guarantee.

He wants you to be in the kingdom of God—His people, in His place, under His rule. He wants a new beginning for you in Christ.

## Application: The Faithfulness of God Calls for Our Faithfulness

God's commitment is unwavering, but we are unfaithful people. We say yes when we mean no. We change our minds. We abandon our word.

James writes:

> Above all, my brothers, do not swear—not by heaven or by earth or by anything else. Let your "Yes" be yes, and your "No," no, or you will be condemned.

As you step into a new year, commit to being a person of your word. Here's how:

**Step One: Identify Your Priorities**

What truly matters? What deserves your consistent time? Your timetable reveals what you actually value. Examine it honestly. What are your real priorities—your ministry, your family, your studies?

**Step Two: Protect Your Commitments**

Once you identify what matters, guard those times fiercely:

- Keep Sundays for corporate worship—not because Sunday is inherently sacred, but because it's when your church family gathers.
- Protect your regular Bible study times, your regular ministry commitments, your family time.
- If these fall on Sunday, negotiate or find alternatives—but don't let them erode.
- Don't procrastinate on your studies. The work you delay becomes a crisis you create.

One-time events—birthday parties, recitals, reunions—feel important and can seem to enhance your reputation. But they threaten the stability of your faithful commitments. Create a specific time slot for these occasional events. Don't let them seep into your protected times.

Think of it this way: You protect your work or school time fiercely. You would never skip school because the church scheduled a Monday morning seminar. Apply the same principle to your church commitments and family time. Protect them.

**Step Three: Treat Emergencies as Emergencies**

Yes, a timetable is only a guide. Real emergencies do arise. But when they do, recognize them for what they are: rare disruptions. When you must break your commitments due to a true emergency, apologize. Don't pretend it was planned. Don't blur the lines between genuine crisis and convenient excuse.

Be honest: Don't say "family emergency" when you really mean "I changed my mind." Own what happened. The integrity of your word depends on your honesty about when you've broken it.

God's faithfulness to His promises is absolute. Our calling is to mirror that faithfulness—to let our yes be yes and our no be no. In doing so, we reflect the character of Christ, who was utterly faithful to His Father's will, even unto death.
