# Malachi 1:1-2:9
**Title:** Preparing Priests
**Date:** June 3, 2012
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction

Over these four weeks, we're exploring the book of Malachi to understand God's purpose for his people—particularly to motivate us to tell our friends about the good news of Jesus Christ. If you're not yet a Christian, I hope this will give you insight into what Christians believe and why your Christian friends keep inviting you to church and wanting to talk about Jesus.

## Malachi: The Messenger

> An oracle: The word of the LORD to Israel through Malachi. (Malachi 1:1)

Malachi means "messenger," and this choice works both as a proper name and descriptive title. The prophet was God's messenger to Israel during the post-exile period (around 433-445 BC), when Ezra and Nehemiah were also active. Israel had been displaced by the Babylonians, but within seventy years—a single generation—their rule ended. The Persian empire sent the Israelites home.

In this context, Israel begins wrestling with God through a series of questions and answers that forms the basis of this prophecy. We'll explore these exchanges together, and you're welcome to ask questions as we go.

## 1. God Loves Israel

God makes a stunning assertion:

> "I have loved you," says the LORD. (Malachi 1:2)

But Israel responds with doubt: "How have you loved us?"

God's answer is equally striking:

> "Was not Esau Jacob's brother? Yet I have loved Jacob, but Esau I have hated." (Malachi 1:2-3)

### Understanding "Hate"

God's language here—loving Jacob but hating Esau—sounds harsh to us. But in the Old Testament, "hate" doesn't always mean violent hatred. Sometimes it means avoiding something (as in Proverbs 11:15, avoiding debt), or being unloved and rejected (as in Proverbs 19:7, the poor man hated by relatives). Here, "hate" means rejection or not choosing.

When Jesus says, "Hate your father and mother" (Luke 14:26), he doesn't mean violent hatred either. He means don't choose them over God.

### God Chooses Jacob

The history of Israel is the history of Jacob—Abraham, Isaac, then Jacob, whose name God changed to Israel. Jacob had a twin brother, Esau. These brothers went on to found two nations: Jacob became Israel, Esau became Edom. Esau was the older, entitled to inherit God's blessings, yet before either was born, God chose the younger. The twins were probably fraternal (one very hairy, one not), emphasizing they were equally ordinary. Jacob was as much a disaster as his brother in the womb—yet God chose him.

Over the centuries between this promise and Malachi, Edom proved itself deserving of God's judgment:
- When Israel fled Egypt, the Edomites blocked their path
- During the monarchy, Edomites joined Israel's enemies and raided them (2 Chronicles 20)
- When Babylon conquered Judah, Edom rejoiced, preyed on fleeing Israelites, and helped plunder Jerusalem (Psalm 137:7)
- Edom never cared for God or their brother. Even their founder Esau traded his birthright for lentil soup

> Though Edom says, "We have been crushed, but we will rebuild," the LORD declares: "They may build, but I will demolish. They will be called the Wicked Land, a people always under the wrath of the LORD." (Malachi 1:4)

Edom is gone. Who remembers the Edomites? We know of King Herod, and that's about it.

### How Can Israel Doubt?

How can Israel ask, "How have you loved us?" when God has clearly chosen them above all nations? God demonstrated his love by selecting Israel—not the largest nation, not the greatest, not even the best—and restoring them to their homeland. God went beyond Israel's borders and brought them back. These are miracles, fulfillment of promises made long ago.

> You will see it with your own eyes and say, "Great is the LORD—even beyond the borders of Israel!" (Malachi 1:5)

### For Christians Today

We are chosen too. Peter writes:

> To God's elect... who have been chosen according to the foreknowledge of God the Father, through the sanctifying work of the Spirit, for obedience to Jesus Christ and sprinkling by his blood. (1 Peter 1:2)

Not because we were good or deserving, but out of God's love, open to all by faith.

As God has chosen us, let us choose God in return. Jesus called his disciples to this radical commitment:

> "If anyone comes to me and does not hate his father and mother, his wife and children, his brothers and sisters—yes, even his own life—he cannot be my disciple." (Luke 14:26)

For those of us who've grown up and left home, this means choosing God's interests for our families over our parents' interests. Our parents want us to be successful, powerful, rich—that's their job. But God wants us to be godly, to serve him, to build his kingdom, not our family dynasty.

We honor our parents (Deuteronomy 5:16)—ensuring they're never homeless or hungry, treating them with respect. But we don't let their priorities override God's calling. That would be role reversal; you wouldn't follow everything your child says.

Choose God over romantic relationships that spiritually compromise you. If you're single, choose God as your bridegroom. Whatever pulls you from following him—choose him first.

## 2. Israel Failed to Love God

God makes another assertion:

> "A son honors his father, and a servant his master. If I am a father, where is the honor due me? If I am a master, where is the respect due me? It is you, O priests, who show contempt for my name." (Malachi 1:6)

Israel has failed to honor their Father and Master. They have shown contempt for God's name.

### The Importance of Names

Names carry weight, don't they? We correct spelling (Emily with an ie, not y) and mispronunciations. There's something tied to a name—honor. In Arthur Miller's *The Crucible*, John Proctor sacrifices wealth and life itself to protect his name and honor.

Yet Israel has shamed God's name through their worship practices.

### Defiled Offerings

God continues:

> "You place defiled food on my altar... You bring blind animals for sacrifice. Is that not wrong? When you sacrifice crippled or diseased animals, is that not wrong?" (Malachi 1:7-8)

The priests weren't bringing their best. They were offering animals that cost nothing—crippled, diseased creatures that would die anyway, animals no one wanted to eat or sell. They thought God would be grateful.

Why was the altar so important? The altar in the temple was where God dwelt. The most crucial act in the temple was animal sacrifice to atone for sin. These weren't arbitrary rituals; they taught a profound truth: sin is costly. Breaking God's law results in violent, costly consequences. To show how serious sin is, people had to bring their finest animals—the fattest cow, the sheep with the best wool, their best livestock.

But the priests brought garbage.

God's response is pointed:

> "Try offering them to your governor! Would he be pleased with you? Would he accept you? Now implore God to be gracious to you. With such offerings from your hands, will he accept you?" (Malachi 1:8-9)

Common sense says it's not acceptable. And God concludes:

> "Oh, that one of you would shut the temple doors, so you would not light useless fires on my altar! I am not pleased with you, and I will accept no offering from your hands." (Malachi 1:10)

### A Modern Parallel

We do this same thing with the Salvation Army. We give them our leftovers—things we've used, things that are obsolete, things we don't want anymore. Things that cost us nothing. VCR tapes, shirts with holes, outdated textbooks. We think they should be grateful for our junk. We give them things we'd be embarrassed to give friends.

The situation has become so absurd that 25% of donated items are thrown away. It costs the Salvos $5 million a year in dumping fees.

But what if we gave our best? Imagine eyeing that new blouse or piece of technology you've saved for, bought, and are about to open—then giving it to the Salvos instead. That's costly giving. That's offering an iPad, not an old phone.

God wants that kind of giving. Sin is costly. Payment is not trivial.

### Half-Hearted Commitment in Our Faith

The priests' problem wasn't just ancient. We offer God things we'd never offer anyone else.

If we stay up late Saturday partying or cramming, we crash Sunday morning. We're late, half-dead, and we decide not to bother. We cancel on God. But we'd never treat our employer that way. We'd never do that to good friends or to someone we're trying to impress romantically.

This extends beyond Sunday worship. If we keep pushing down serving each other until there's a better time, we're lying to ourselves. There is no better time. When we compartmentalize our Christian life—setting God aside when we're busy and putting him back on the agenda when we're free, then taking a break again during holidays—we're giving him scraps.

Give God your best time. The best time of the day. The best time of the week. The best time of the month. The best time of the year.

During the semester, if that's when you can serve best, don't let studies consume you so completely that you neglect God. During holidays, if that's your best opportunity, don't waste it on trips you don't care about, places you only wanted to visit because everyone else was going, curiosities to tick off a box. Face it: we travel to escape the reality of serving God.

Now is the time—especially when you're single, unencumbered by family obligations, able to do weekend aways and long beach missions, equipped with social skills and energy your parents no longer have.

**Now is the time.**

Save the freshest time of your day for Scripture. Pray when you're alert, not exhausted. Serve while you're young and strong, not when retired. Give God your healthiest years.

## 3. God's Name Must Be Great Among the Nations

What's at stake here isn't merely our relationship with God or Israel's attitude toward temple worship. God's entire project for the world hangs in the balance.

> "My name will be great among the nations, from the rising to the setting of the sun. In every place incense and pure offerings will be brought to my name, because my name will be great among the nations." (Malachi 1:11)

> "I am a great king, and my name is to be feared among the nations." (Malachi 1:14)

God is predicting that one day, Gentiles will follow the God of Israel. Yet the slackness of the Israelite priests was threatening this entire vision.

In honor cultures, people are never merely individuals. They reflect their families, their culture, their fathers—like wearing a school uniform. You're not just yourself; you represent the institution. In Samoan culture, as our chaplain Joe describes, people see you not as Joe but as Stan's son. You reflect credit or shame on your father.

When the nations looked at Israel, they saw God. And what did they see? A God whose followers brought crippled animals. A God with no honor, no respect, no fear among his people. Who would want to follow a God like that? God's redemptive project was under threat.

## 4. The Priests' Failure

The priests weren't doing their job:

> "And now this admonition is for you, O priests. If you do not listen, and if you do not set your heart to honor my name, I will send a curse upon you, and I will curse your blessings. Yes, I have already cursed them, because you have not set your heart to honor me." (Malachi 2:1-2)

> "Because of you I will rebuke your descendants; I will spread on your faces the offal from your festival sacrifices, and you will be carried off with it." (Malachi 2:3)

> "You have turned from the way and by your teaching have caused many to stumble; you have violated the covenant with Levi. So I have caused you to be despised and humiliated before all the people, because you have not followed my ways but have shown partiality in matters of the law." (Malachi 2:8-9)

The priesthood itself carried an ancient curse. Jacob blessed Levi's sons, but not without warning:

> "Simeon and Levi are brothers—their swords are weapons of violence. Cursed be their anger, so fierce, and their fury, so cruel! I will scatter them in Jacob and disperse them in Israel." (Genesis 49:5-7)

Yet God had also made a covenant with Levi through Phinehas:

> "Phinehas son of Eleazar, the son of Aaron, the priest, has turned my anger away from the Israelites; for he was as zealous as I am for my honor among them. Therefore I am making a covenant of peace with him. He and his descendants will have a covenant of a lasting priesthood, because he was zealous for the honor of his God and made atonement for the Israelites." (Numbers 25:11-13)

God was hoping that covenant with Levi might continue. He wanted to remove the bad elements and start fresh.

> "My covenant was with him, a covenant of life and peace, and I gave them to him; this called for reverence and he revered me and stood in awe of my name. True instruction was in his mouth and nothing false was found on his lips. He walked with me in peace and uprightness, and turned many from sin. For the lips of a priest ought to preserve knowledge, and from his mouth men should seek instruction—because he is the messenger of the LORD Almighty." (Malachi 2:5-7)

The ideal priest was one who chose God before family (Deuteronomy 33:9), who guarded God's word and covenant, who taught God's law faithfully, who offered sacrifices with integrity, and whose lips preserved only truth.

## 5. Jesus: Our True Priest

While Malachi speaks of the coming messenger (John the Baptist, as Jesus confirms in Matthew 11:10), the ultimate fulfillment is Jesus himself. He is the priest who made God's name great:

> Being found in appearance as a man, he humbled himself and became obedient to death—even death on a cross! Therefore God exalted him to the highest place and gave him the name that is above every name, that at the name of Jesus every knee should bow, in heaven and on earth and under the earth, and every tongue confess that Jesus Christ is Lord, to the glory of God the Father. (Philippians 2:8-11)

Jesus didn't bring crippled offerings. He offered himself—a perfect, spotless sacrifice. He gave everything.

## 6. Messaging the World Through Priests

But here's what transforms this passage for us: we are now the priests.

> You are a chosen people, a royal priesthood, a holy nation, a people belonging to God, that you may declare the praises of him who called you out of darkness into his wonderful light. (1 Peter 2:9)

Jesus is the great priest. We follow him. We listen to his message. And we point others to him—not preaching our own message, but his.

Just as God's plan was that his name become great among the nations, that Gentiles would come to follow him, so our calling is to help make Jesus known. We are the priests of the new covenant, and our role is to offer ourselves—our best time, our best energy, our best gifts—to expanding his kingdom.

The nations are watching. Your friends are watching. What kind of God do they see in you? One who receives your leftovers, your half-hearted efforts, your scraps of time? Or one who captures your best, your passions, your priorities?

Spend your healthiest and most sociable years making his name great. Offer your bodies—your time, your talents, your presence—sacrificially to this task. Even during exam season when life is stressed and scrappy, push forward. Forgive each other, support one another, and make your time on earth count.

So that the nations will come to follow Jesus. So that his name will be great.
