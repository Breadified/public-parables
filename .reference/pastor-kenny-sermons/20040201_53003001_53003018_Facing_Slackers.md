# 2 Thessalonians 3:1-18

**Title:** Facing Slackers  
**Date:** 29 February 2004  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Problem: Idleness and Disregard for Tradition

Last week Paul addressed the Thessalonians' confusion about the day of the Lord. This week he tackles a related issue—some believers have become idle, living in contradiction to the apostolic tradition they received.

> Now we command you, brothers, in the name of our Lord Jesus Christ, that you keep away from any brother who is walking in idleness and not in accord with the tradition that you received from us. (2 Thessalonians 3:6)

Their idleness likely stemmed from the same false belief: if Jesus is coming tomorrow, why work at all? Why bother with employment, family responsibilities, or daily obligations? This thinking led them to abandon their duties and live off others' generosity.

## Why Work? Four Biblical Motivations

### 1. To Not Be a Burden

Paul sets himself as an example:

> For you yourselves know how you ought to imitate us, because we were not idle when we were with you, nor did we eat anyone's bread without paying for it, but with toil and labor we worked night and day, that we might not be a burden to any of you. (2 Thessalonians 3:7-8)

Paul traveled throughout the Mediterranean proclaiming the gospel—work that involved substantial expenses for food, accommodation, and travel. Yet rather than burden the churches he served, he supported himself through labor (likely as a tentmaker, as mentioned in Acts 18:3). He worked day and night, alternating between tent-making and gospel proclamation.

**The principle:** We naturally recoil from burdensome people—those who leech off others without bearing their own weight. As Christians, our calling is to carry our own load and not take others for granted.

**For us today, this means:**
- Don't bludge off your parents. If you're gaining independence and working, offer to contribute financially. If they cook for you regularly, take your turn or treat them to dinner.
- Don't procrastinate your studies then burden your friends for notes. Put in your fair share of work.
- When you're part of a community or small group, ask how you can help rather than merely receive.

This doesn't mean life should be transactional or that we keep meticulous scorecards of who owes whom. As Christians living by grace, there's room for genuinely helping one another and carrying each other's burdens. What Paul condemns is the *attitude* of purposefully exploiting others through laziness.

### 2. To Eat and Sustain Life

> For even when we were with you, we would give you this command: If anyone is not willing to work, let him not eat. For we hear that some among you walk in idleness, not busy at work, but busybodies. Now such persons we command and encourage in the Lord Jesus Christ to do their work quietly and to earn their own living. (2 Thessalonians 3:10-12)

In the ancient economy, this command made real sense. Most people lived on a subsistence economy—they didn't receive weekly or monthly paychecks. Instead, they went to the marketplace daily, found employment for the day, received their pay in the evening, and bought food on the way home. There were no pantries or freezers. No work literally meant no food.

The link between work and survival was immediate and unbreakable.

**The application:** We work to survive. For a student, your job is to be a student—treat it responsibly, put in the due effort, and earn the food you eat. For an employee, work provides the means of sustenance.

The danger many of us face isn't idleness but its opposite: we work for far more than mere survival. We work for material accumulation, status, toys, and greed. Paul's point is simpler and more profound—work to eat, not to excess.

### 3. To Support Gospel Work

> It was not because we do not have that right, but to give you in ourselves an example to imitate. (2 Thessalonians 3:9)

Paul had every right as a gospel worker to receive wages for his labor. Just as a tentmaker expects payment for tents, a minister deserves support for ministry. Yet Paul forewent this right, not for his own benefit, but to teach the Thessalonians an essential lesson: *someone has to pay for gospel work.*

Living in Sydney, we might forget this reality. We see free street performances, free maps, free services everywhere. But someone is always paying—usually the taxpayer. The world runs on economics: finite resources, unlimited wants. There is no unlimited pot of gold.

When you benefit from gospel ministry—whether it's free public talks, training classes, or community events—remember: *if you're not paying for it, someone else is.* This applies whether it's university ministry or a local church. If finances dry up, ministry stops. Workers get cut. Opportunities diminish.

**The call:** As you're able, support gospel work financially. Be generous. Don't merely consume; contribute. Bear your appropriate burden in sustaining the work you benefit from.

### 4. For the Good of the Gospel

> As for you, brothers, do not grow weary in doing good. (2 Thessalonians 3:13)

Paul frames this in his opening plea:

> Finally, brothers, pray for us, that the word of the Lord may speed ahead and be honored, as happened among you, and that we may be delivered from wicked and evil men. For not all have faith. But the Lord is faithful. He will establish you and guard you against the evil one. And we have confidence in the Lord about you, that you are doing and will do the things that we command. May the Lord direct your hearts to the love of God and to the steadfastness of Christ. (2 Thessalonians 3:1-5)

Paul's ultimate concern isn't self-sufficiency or even sustainability—it's that *the word of the Lord speeds ahead and is honored.* He wants the gospel to advance among the Thessalonians as it has, and to reach all people. He desires their deliverance from evil and their hearts directed toward God's love and Christ's steadfastness.

This is the supreme motivation: we work ultimately for the good of the gospel and the world.

How different this is from worldly motivations—climbing the career ladder, gaining job satisfaction, earning status and wealth, feeding pride and ego. Following such motives leads only to disappointment and disillusionment as we shift from job to job, never satisfied.

But when we work for the gospel's advancement, even the frustrations and boredom of daily labor become bearable. We endure the mundane because we're serving something infinitely greater than ourselves.

## Conclusion: Work With Purpose

> If anyone does not obey what we say in this letter, take note of that person, and have nothing to do with him, that he may be ashamed. Do not regard him as an enemy, but warn him as a brother. (2 Thessalonians 3:14-15)

Paul's instruction is firm but pastoral—withdraw support from the persistently idle, not as an enemy, but as a correction that might lead to repentance.

The congregation at that time faced genuine slackers. Most of us don't. If anything, we tend toward the opposite—we're too busy. Yet in our busyness, we must ask: are we working for the right reasons? Are we laboring for things that matter?

We work:
- To not be a burden on others
- To eat and sustain ourselves and our families
- To support gospel work
- For the advancement of God's kingdom and the good of the world

When these are our motivations, work becomes more than drudgery—it becomes participation in God's purposes. That changes everything.
