# Acts 16:1-40

**Title:** The Household of God
**Date:** 6 June 2015
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Individual Flexibility

Paul and Barnabas set out on a missionary journey to revisit the churches they had planted. Their plan was simple—encourage the believers and strengthen the growing movement of Christ followers. Yet right from the start, disagreement arose among the apostles themselves.

> 36 Some time later Paul said to Barnabas, "Let us go back and visit the believers in all the towns where we preached the word of the Lord and see how they are doing."

### The Sharp Disagreement Over John Mark

The first conflict involved John Mark. Barnabas wanted to bring him along, but Paul refused.

> 37 Barnabas wanted to take John, also called Mark, with them, 38 but Paul did not think it wise to take him, because he had deserted them in Pamphylia and had not continued with them in the work.

Mark had been recruited as a helper early in their ministry. He came from a well-to-do family—his cousin Barnabas was a man of means—and his mother's house in Jerusalem served as a gathering place for the early church. Yet during their first missionary journey, Mark abandoned them when the work became difficult. Whether he couldn't handle the hardship or was troubled by Paul's emerging leadership, Mark left them to return to Jerusalem.

Paul saw this desertion as disqualifying. You cannot trust someone in ministry who abandons the work when circumstances grow tough. Barnabas, however, was moved by mercy and wanted to give Mark another chance.

The result was a sharp parting of ways:

> 39 They had such a sharp disagreement that they parted company. Barnabas took Mark and sailed for Cyprus, 40 but Paul chose Silas and left, commended by the believers to the grace of the Lord.

Remarkably, this story does not end in permanent estrangement. Years later, Paul would write to Timothy: "Get Mark and bring him with you, because he is helpful to me in my ministry." Mark eventually matured into faithful service. Barnabas's grace and second chances bore fruit.

### The Principle of Adiaphora

What can we learn from this disagreement between apostles? Sometimes it is right to agree to disagree and part ways. Not every disagreement requires resolution or compromise.

The key is distinguishing between matters of ultimate importance and matters of indifference. In Greek theology, these are called *adiaphora*—things neither commanded nor forbidden by Scripture. On such matters, Christians are free to choose different paths without demanding uniformity.

The Gospel and the person of Christ are never adiaphora. But many other things are. Individuals should give and take on peripheral matters, trust God's guidance, and maintain flexibility when circumstances change. This is not weakness; it is wisdom.

### Circumcision: Flexibility for the Sake of the Gospel

As Paul traveled, he came to Lystra and met Timothy, a young believer whose mother was Jewish and devout, but whose father was a Greek pagan.

> 1 Paul came to Derbe and then to Lystra, where a disciple named Timothy lived, whose mother was Jewish and a believer but whose father was a Greek. 2 The believers at Lystra and Iconium spoke well of him. 3 Paul wanted to take him along on the journey, so he circumcised him because of the Jews who lived in that area, for they all knew that his father was a Greek.

This is striking. Remember, the Jerusalem Council had just ruled that Gentiles need not be circumcised to be saved. Paul had been a fierce defender of that decision. Yet here he chooses to circumcise Timothy.

Why? The answer lies in pastoral wisdom. Timothy was already a believer before circumcision. His faith did not depend on it. Yet the Jews in that region knew his background—half-Jew, half-Greek. For ministry effectiveness, it mattered. To preach Jesus to the Jews, Paul needed credibility. Circumcision was not salvation for Timothy; it was a cultural bridge that removed barriers to the gospel message.

This shows another aspect of flexibility: adapting to your audience without compromising truth. Paul could say, "I became as a Jew to win the Jews." For Timothy, circumcision honored his Jewish heritage while his faith rested entirely in Christ.

### Direction: Led by the Spirit

> 6 Paul and his companions traveled throughout the region of Phrygia and Galatia, having been kept by the Holy Spirit from preaching the word in the province of Asia. 7 When they came to the border of Mysia, they tried to enter Bithynia, but the Spirit of Jesus would not allow them. 8 So they passed by Mysia and went down to Troas. 9 During the night Paul had a vision of a man of Macedonia standing and begging him, "Come over to Macedonia and help us." 10 After Paul had seen the vision, we got ready at once to leave for Macedonia, concluding that God had called us to preach the gospel to them.

Paul's plans kept hitting barriers. He wanted to preach in Asia—but the Holy Spirit prevented him. He attempted to enter Bithynia—but the Spirit of Jesus would not allow it. Instead, a vision came calling him west to Macedonia. This was the moment the gospel would first reach Europe.

The key principle here is flexibility aligned with God's direction. Paul had a vision—take the gospel to the Gentiles. The route mattered far less than the destination. When doors closed, he changed course. When God showed a new path through vision, he obeyed immediately.

For us, this means holding our plans lightly. Major commitments—serving Christ, loving others, pursuing righteousness—are non-negotiable. But the specific paths, methods, and timing should remain flexible, responsive to God's leading and changing circumstances.

## Households as the Foundation

Paul and his team finally reached Philippi, the leading city of Macedonia and a Roman colony. Here the gospel took root in a remarkable way—through households.

> 11 From Troas we put out to sea and sailed straight for Samothrace, and the next day we went on to Neapolis. 12 From there we traveled to Philippi, a Roman colony and the leading city of that district of Macedonia. And we stayed there several days.

On the Sabbath, they looked for a synagogue but found only a place of prayer by the river, where women gathered. There they met Lydia, a dealer in fine purple cloth from Thyatira.

> 13 On the Sabbath we went outside the city gate to the river, where we expected to find a place of prayer. We sat down and began to speak to the women who had gathered there. 14 One of those listening was a woman from the city of Thyatira named Lydia, a dealer in purple cloth. She was a worshiper of God. The Lord opened her heart to respond to Paul's message. 15 When she and the members of her household were baptized, she invited us to her home. "If you consider me a believer in the Lord," she said, "come and stay at my house." And she persuaded us.

Lydia believed and was baptized—along with her entire household. Her home became the first church gathering place in Europe.

### The Demon-Possessed Slave Girl

Yet trouble followed. As they returned to the place of prayer, they encountered a slave girl possessed by a spirit of divination. She made a living for her owners by fortune-telling.

> 16 Once when we were going to the place of prayer, we were met by a female slave who had a spirit of python by which she predicted the future. She earned a great deal of money for her owners by fortune-telling. 17 She followed Paul and the rest of us, shouting, "These men are servants of the Most High God, who are telling you the way to be saved." 18 She kept this up for many days. Finally Paul became so annoyed that he turned around and said to the spirit, "In the name of Jesus Christ I command you to come out of her!" At that moment the spirit left her.

What seemed like free advertising troubled Paul deeply. The girl's words were technically true—but they came from a demonic source, and associating the gospel with fortune-telling would corrupt its message. Paul cast out the demon, freeing the girl from bondage.

Her owners lost their income source immediately:

> 19 When her owners realized that their hope of making money was gone, they seized Paul and Silas and dragged them into the marketplace to face the authorities. 20 They brought them before the magistrates and said, "These men are Jews, and are throwing our city into an uproar 21 by advocating customs unlawful for us Romans to accept or practice."

### The Prison and the Jailer's Household

The magistrates, swayed by an angry crowd, ordered Paul and Silas beaten and imprisoned. Yet their response was remarkable:

> 22 The crowd joined in the attack against Paul and Silas, and the magistrates ordered them to be stripped and beaten with rods. 23 After they had been severely flogged, they were thrown into prison, and the jailer was commanded to guard them carefully. 24 When he received these orders, he put them in the inner cell and fastened their feet in the stocks.
>
> 25 About midnight Paul and Silas were praying and singing hymns to God, and the other prisoners were listening to them. 26 Suddenly there was such a violent earthquake that the foundations of the prison were shaken. At once all the prison doors flew open, and everyone's chains came loose. 27 The jailer woke up, and when he saw the prison doors open, he drew his sword and was about to kill himself because he thought the prisoners had escaped. 28 But Paul shouted, "Don't harm yourself! We are all here!"

Instead of fleeing, Paul and Silas remained. The jailer, expecting to face execution for a prison break, found them still there. His terror turned to desperate faith:

> 29 The jailer called for lights, rushed in and fell trembling before Paul and Silas. 30 He then brought them out and asked, "Sirs, what must I do to be saved?" 31 They replied, "Believe in the Lord Jesus, and you will be saved—you and your household." 32 Then they spoke the word of the Lord to him and to all the others in his house. 33 At that hour of the night the jailer took them and washed their wounds; then immediately he and all his household were baptized. 34 The jailer brought them into his house and set a meal before them; he was filled with joy because he had come to believe in God—he and his whole household.

Notice the repeated phrase: "you and your household." This is not a promise of automatic salvation for family members through the head of household alone. Rather, it reflects how the gospel spreads most naturally—through the relational networks of families. The message was preached to the jailer and his whole household. They all heard it. They all believed it. They were all baptized.

### Why Households Matter

Christianity is fundamentally relational. We encounter God as Father. Jesus calls us brother and sister. The church is the family of God. Marriage represents Christ's covenant love for the church. We learn about love, sacrifice, commitment, and faithfulness first in our families.

The gospel takes root most readily where these relational bonds already exist. When a parent comes to faith, it becomes natural for children to hear and believe. When spouses share faith, they reinforce each other's convictions. When extended families embrace Christ, entire networks of relationships are transformed.

Conversely, broken families make the gospel harder to understand. A child abused by an earthly father struggles to trust a heavenly Father. A child who witnesses parental infidelity finds it difficult to believe in unconditional love. A marriage ending in divorce teaches a child that covenant commitment is negotiable. The decline of family correlates directly with the decline of faith.

If you want to reverse irreligion, you must start with the family. The churches that will flourish in coming generations are those built on strong Christian families—people learning to love as Christ loved, to sacrifice as Christ sacrificed, to remain faithful as Christ remains faithful.

For many in our congregation, you are the first Christian in your family or generation. You carry the responsibility and privilege of starting a new lineage of faith. As you raise children, you are establishing Christian values, Christian ways of thinking, Christian approaches to suffering and joy. You are pioneering a new generation in the ways of the Lord.

## Justice and the Rule of Law

The final scene shows Paul standing for something he had flexibility on before—his rights as a Roman citizen.

> 35 When it was daylight, the magistrates sent their officers to the jailer with the order: "Release those men." 36 The jailer told Paul, "The magistrates have ordered that you and Silas be released. Now you can leave. Go in peace."

Paul could have accepted this quietly, grateful for his freedom. Instead:

> 37 But Paul said to the officers: "They beat us publicly without a trial, even though we are Roman citizens, and threw us into prison. And now do they want to get rid of us quietly? No! Let them come themselves and escort us out."

Why did Paul, who showed flexibility on Mark, on circumcision, and on his travel plans, refuse to bend here? The answer reveals a crucial biblical principle: individual flexibility is appropriate in matters of personal preference, but justice in governance must never be compromised.

> 38 The officers reported this to the magistrates, and when they heard that Paul and Silas were Roman citizens, they were alarmed. 39 They came to appease them and escorted them from the prison, requesting them to leave the city.

Roman citizens possessed specific legal protections. They could not be imprisoned without trial. They could not be subjected to degrading punishments like scourging. The magistrates had committed a flagrant violation of law. When Paul insisted on acknowledgment of this injustice, he was not being difficult—he was defending the rule of law itself.

### A Question of Justice, Not Personal Rights

Here is the crucial insight: Paul's concern was not primarily personal vindication. Rather, he understood that when laws are broken and nothing happens, injustice spreads. If magistrates can illegally arrest Roman citizens without consequence, what stops them from doing it again? What about the next person? The next town? The next generation?

When we ignore laws being broken, we dilute justice for everyone. We teach officials that laws are optional. We breed corruption. We create a society where rights become conditional and fragile.

### The Christian Response to Injustice

Many Christians today hesitate to stand for their rights or to advocate for justice in the public square. We fear we are "imposing our values" on a secular society. But this reflects a misunderstanding. When we advocate for justice, we are not imposing Christian doctrine. We are insisting on the rule of law—a principle that benefits everyone, believers and non-believers alike.

Consider the Education Department's ban of Christian religious education books. Christians protested, the ban was lifted—and then many Christians quieted down. But this was premature. An illegal act had occurred. Laws in New South Wales grant churches, not the Department, authority over religious education curriculum. To simply accept the Department's reversal without demanding accountability is to accept injustice. We essentially arm those who violated the law to violate it again in the future.

This is like thanking a bully for stopping to punch you. We don't congratulate bullies. We demand apology and justice.

Advocating for traditional marriage is not imposing Christian values. It is defending the rule of law and protecting the rights of children to be raised by their biological parents when possible. This is justice, not religious imposition.

Christians must learn this: we should be flexible on peripheral matters—our preferences, our convenience, our comfort. But on matters of justice and the rule of law, we must stand firm. Not for ourselves alone, but for everyone who comes after.

### Fighting for a Godly Society

The tragedy is that many Christians have opted out of the public arena entirely. We could give money to defend truth. We don't. We could speak. We stay silent. We could vote. We don't bother. We could write to our representatives. We make excuses.

Consider what happened in Ireland. A nation with a deep Catholic heritage voted to redefine marriage. But look at the numbers: only 60 percent of those who voted supported it. Only 60 percent of voting-age citizens voted at all. That means only about one-third of the entire adult population actually voted yes.

Yet the pro-marriage-redefinition campaign mobilized extensively. They used every legal means available. They funded campaigns. They organized voters. They got people to the polls. Meanwhile, Christians largely abstained from the process—not because we were outnumbered, but because we chose not to participate.

There is nothing inevitable about cultural decline. It only becomes inevitable when we fail to use the tools available to us. We have the right to vote, to speak, to petition, to organize, to engage the law for justice. Paul did. So must we.

### The Gospel Transforms Civilizations

Europe today stands as a monument to what the gospel accomplishes when it transforms a society. When Christianity arrived, Europe was pagan and barbaric. Rome had crumbled. The continent was home to superstition, slavery, child sacrifice, and violence. While China developed sophisticated civilizations, Europe was backward.

Yet the gospel transformed Europe completely. Monasteries became centers of learning and productivity. Savage warriors became chivalric knights. Cities rose where wastelands had been. Education flourished. Laws developed. Science emerged. Art, architecture, and literature reached unprecedented heights.

All of this flowed from Christian conviction: that Jesus is Lord, that all people bear God's image, that justice matters, that education is sacred, that human dignity must be honored. As these beliefs took root, civilization flourished.

But today, Europe has abandoned its Christian heritage. It kept the buildings but lost the faith. And as Christianity retreats, so do all the blessings it brought. Crime increases. Debauchery spreads. Where Sharia law takes hold, women face violence and oppression. The virtues that took centuries to build are collapsing in decades.

Australia follows Europe's trajectory, though somewhat delayed. We are retreating from our Christian heritage. Unless we fight for it, we will follow Europe's path of decline.

### The Call to Action

The fight is not yet lost. But it requires courage. It requires Christians willing to:

- Stand for good laws and resist bad ones
- Vote and encourage others to vote
- Speak truth civilly but clearly
- Petition our representatives
- Use the legal and democratic tools available to us
- Do it all without fear, remembering we are Roman citizens in a free society

We will make mistakes. Distilling centuries of wisdom about marriage, law, and justice into social media posts is challenging. We will stumble and need correction. But the fight must be fought.

Paul learned this. He was flexible when flexibility served the gospel. But when justice was at stake, he stood firm. So must we.

The gospel alone can heal the brokenness of our age—spiritual brokenness, moral brokenness, relational brokenness. But the gospel works best when it takes root in healthy families, when it shapes just institutions, and when it guides wise laws.

Fight for it. Before it's too late.
