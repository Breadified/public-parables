# Mark 2:1-12

**Title:** Jesus Brings Forgiveness

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Biggest Problem We Face

Jesus enters Capernaum and the crowds gather so densely that there's no room, not even outside the door. In this desperate context, four men bring a paralyzed friend to Jesus, determined to get him healed. When they can't reach Jesus through the crowd, they dig through the roof—breaking through the mud and branches of the house—and lower their friend down on a mat.

> When Jesus saw their faith, he said to the paralytic, "Son, your sins are forgiven." (Mark 2:5)

This response seems strange at first. The paralyzed man is lying helpless on the floor, unable to work, unable to provide for himself. Surely healing would be the most practical and welcome gift. Forgiveness for sins? What good is that if you're confined to a mat? You can't eat forgiveness. You can't pay rent with it. You can't wear it.

Yet Jesus is doing the most important thing. By addressing the man's spiritual condition rather than only his physical one, Jesus reveals what our greatest problem truly is: **not disability, not climate change, not exams, but sin itself**. Sin carries judgment and penalty—it is humanity's deepest crisis.

## Which Is Easier?

The teachers of the law sitting nearby are scandalized. "Who can forgive sins but God alone?" they ask themselves. They're right in one sense—but they're wrong about who Jesus is.

> Which is easier: to say to the paralytic, "Your sins are forgiven," or to say, "Get up, take your mat and walk"? (Mark 2:9)

A physical healing is visible and verifiable. A miracle confirms itself immediately. But forgiveness? Forgiveness is far harder because it demands payment. True forgiveness is not pretending a wrong never happened or simply forgetting about it. God cannot ignore sin. When a friend betrays you, genuine forgiveness means you absorb the cost yourself—you pay for their mistake.

This is what God does. He forgives by paying the cost himself. Instead of the paralytic bearing the consequences of his sin, Jesus bears them. Instead of the paralytic's arms and legs being free while his spirit is bound to judgment, Jesus's arms and legs are bound to the cross. Jesus is nailed there. He pays for you. Whatever sins you carry, forgiveness means Jesus paid the price.

## The Proof and the Call

> "But that you may know that the Son of Man has authority on earth to forgive sins . . . ." He said to the paralytic, "Get up, take your mat and go home." (Mark 2:10-11)

Jesus demonstrates his authority by accomplishing the harder thing—forgiveness. The physical healing follows as proof. The man gets up, takes his mat, and walks out in full view of everyone. They're amazed and praise God, saying, "We have never seen anything like this!"

The teaching is clear: Jesus brings forgiveness of sins, not as a distant theological concept, but as the central work of his mission. And this forgiveness comes through payment—his payment on the cross.
