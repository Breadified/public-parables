# Revelation 5

**Title:** The Lion, the Switch, and the Throne Room
**Date:** 12 January 2002
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Main Point

Real power is Lamb-like.

---

## The Throne Room

### God's Perfect Plans

When we look for power, the best place to look must be the throne room of God. In chapter 5, John receives a grand vision of heaven itself—a cinematic sweep around the room filled with twenty-four elders, four living creatures, and the appearance of the almighty God, glorious as jasper and precious gemstones, seated at the centre of all things. All honour, glory, and power converge on that throne.

As John's eyes sweep across this magnificent scene, they fall upon a scroll held in the right hand of the Enthroned One.

> I saw in the right hand of him who sat on the throne a scroll with writing on both sides and sealed with seven seals. (Revelation 5:1)

This scroll contains the complete plans and purposes of God for the world—comprehensive, nothing left out, written inside and outside, both sides covered. To open it is to implement God's will for all creation. But the scroll is sealed, not with one seal but seven. These seals will be opened later, each bringing divine judgment. For now, the scroll remains perfectly sealed, unreachable.

### The Challenge

Then comes a mighty angel, proclaiming in a voice that cuts through all the heavenly singing:

> Who is worthy to break the seals and open the scroll? But no one in heaven or on earth or under the earth could open the scroll or even look inside it. (Revelation 5:2-3)

Who can do it? The answer echoes through all creation: no one. Not in heaven, not on earth, not under the earth. There is no one in this entire universe good enough, worthy enough, to fulfil God's plans. Humanity stands unable—not even able to peek through the side gaps of the scroll.

John hears this silence and weeps:

> I wept and wept because no one was found who was worthy to open the scroll or look inside. (Revelation 5:4)

The agony is real. The hopelessness is palpable. The promise of good things to come remains unfulfilled. In that moment, there seems to be no one.

---

## The Lion Is Powerful

Then comes comfort. One of the elders, clothed in white and representing the victorious elements of heaven, speaks:

> Do not weep! See, the Lion of the tribe of Judah, the Root of David, has triumphed. He is able to open the scroll and its seven seals. (Revelation 5:5)

Hope returns. The Lion is worthy.

When we think of a lion, we think of power itself. Power is strength—and the Lion is strong. Power is authority—the Lion rules with wisdom, king of beasts. Power is security and trust—you would deposit your money in a bank bearing the lion as its emblem. Even in nations where lions have vanished from the earth, they still appear on coats of arms as symbols of kingship and monarchy. The Lion represents strength, authority, security, wealth, and wisdom.

This Lion is Jesus, the chosen King. Though Revelation does not quote a specific Old Testament reference to "the Lion of the tribe of Judah" or "the Root of David," the picture is unmistakable. Throughout Revelation, John weaves Old Testament imagery like a tapestry to create new collages of meaning. Here, the picture is clear: God's chosen King is the Lion. Since King David's reign, Israel has longed for a king as great as he. Finally, here He is—Jesus, the descendant of great King David, the conqueror of foreign invaders, the one who brings a new era of peace by crushing and devouring His enemies. The great, majestic, powerful Lion to rule the world.

This is what we see presented: As God's chosen representative, the Lion is able to open the scroll and its seven seals. The Lion is powerful.

---

## The Switch: From Lion to Lamb

### Switch One: The Powerful Lion Becomes the Powerless Lamb

John turns to see the Lion triumph. But what does he see?

> Then I saw a Lamb, looking as if it had been slain, standing in the centre of the throne, encircled by the four living creatures and the elders. (Revelation 5:6)

John is expecting to see a powerful Lion. Instead, he sees a sheep—not even a full-grown one, but a lamb. And not just any lamb: one that looks as if it had been slain.

The powerful Lion switches to be the powerless Lamb. How absurd is this?

It would be like America, with all its mighty power—its missiles, tanks, battleships, stealth bombers, smart bombs, satellite reconnaissance, artillery, and nuclear arsenal—sending a baby with a toy into battle when war is declared. The contrast is staggering. How is a weak and powerless baby supposed to conquer?

Jesus could have amassed His own army. He could have called on a legion of angels to fight for Him. At a stroke, all the kingdoms and nations of the world would have been His. He could have come victoriously. But instead, He chose to be slain. And when you are dead, you cannot do anything—let alone conquer or be victorious.

Why would a Lion choose to be a powerless, slain sheep? Why come like a weak baby with a toy when you have the might of heaven behind you? Why settle for baby power when you can have uncle Sam?

The answer cuts to the heart of everything:

**Real power is gained by trusting God.**

Jesus, in His obedience and trust, follows God all the way to the cross. He disarms Himself of anything remotely powerful and enters the battlefield like a baby. This is where real power actually lives. Real power is Lamb-like.

### Side Application: Power Is Trust in the Powerful God

The world teaches us to see power through a very different lens. We look at the pathetic Jesus—a slain lamb—and we do not see power. Instead, we trust the standards of this world and its perceptions of wealth, wisdom, and strength to define what real power is.

Consider how we navigate the world. I drive my Corolla down a busy road every day. When someone on their learner's permit or driving an older car or a Volvo pulls ahead of me, I have no problem revving up and zooming past them. But when I find myself behind a luxury car—a BMW convertible—I hesitate to overtake. Here is a powerful person, and I feel intimidated to pass them.

But the other day, I drove a big Verada—fairly luxurious, spacious, champagne coloured (not plain yellow but sparkling wine), with power windows, leather seats, a CD player, and those antennas that go up and down automatically. Suddenly, I was no longer worried about the opinions of other drivers. I cruised along with confidence. That is what our props do—they give us the impression that we are a success. We all use props to make ourselves feel better.

It is the school we attended, the university course we are studying, the graduate job we will have. It is the latest clothing brand that defines us, or the latest toy that shows we are in. It is the 7:15 social buzz where we think we attend a happening church. It is the boyfriend or girlfriend we have in tow that helps us meet our social standing. It is our search for significance in exam results and status, our wanting to be labelled a success so that others will be impressed.

Instead of making ourselves a weak, pathetic lamb, we would rather make a name for ourselves.

But the powerless lamb cuts across all those perceptions and shows that power actually comes from trusting in the powerful God.

It is hard for us to trust God because we live in a nice part of the world and are young—most of us have not yet been tested. Only when we are terribly sick, fail exams, lose our popularity, or someone dies do we discover whether we really trust God or not. When we lose everything that makes us feel powerful in this world, when our earthly props give way—that is when we see where our trust really is.

Jesus is the Lion without the props. Shave the mane, mute the roar, file the nails and teeth, shrink the size, and kill it—what you have is the slain Lamb. Jesus relying not on Himself but on God.

Can we trust God? Can we rely on the powerful God? Yes, absolutely. And here is the proof that it is worth it.

## The Switch: From Slain to Powerful

### Switch Two: The Slain Lamb Becomes Powerful

In a split second, the slain Lamb switches to become powerful:

> Then I saw a Lamb, looking as if it had been slain, standing in the centre of the throne, encircled by the four living creatures and the elders. He had seven horns and seven eyes, which are the seven spirits of God sent out into all the earth. (Revelation 5:6)

This mortally wounded Lamb is standing. He is alive. He stands in a place of great prominence, in the centre of the throne, encircled by the four living creatures and the elders. The Lamb has seven horns, signifying complete power, and seven eyes, pointing to complete knowledge. He possesses the complete Spirit of God. The Lamb is promoted to the highest status.

The Lamb is at once crucified and powerful. It is in this very absurdity—weakness and power together—that we see the complete picture.

It is this Lamb who, in verse 7, comes and takes the scroll from the right hand of him who sat on the throne:

> He came and took the scroll from the right hand of him who sat on the throne. (Revelation 5:7)

The Lion who triumphed was said to be worthy to take the scroll. Yet the Lion is never seen nor heard of again in Revelation—only the Lamb that looked as if it had been slain. This slain Lamb is worthy to take the scroll and reveal the purposes of God. The Messianic Son of David is the divinely appointed conqueror, but only in respect to His death. It is not the Lion as Lion who takes the scroll. It is the Lion as the Lamb, looking as if it had been slain, who takes the scroll.

The weak baby rescues the world.

### Heaven's Response

And when He had taken the scroll, all creation responds:

> The four living creatures and the twenty-four elders fell down before the Lamb. Each one had a harp and they were holding golden bowls full of incense, which are the prayers of the saints. (Revelation 5:8)

All creation and all authority bow down in worship before the Lamb. Their harps and golden bowls of incense—the prayers of God's people—rise in adoration. And there is more:

> Then I looked and heard the voice of many angels, numbering thousands upon thousands, and ten thousand times ten thousand. They encircled the throne and the living creatures and the elders. In a loud voice they sang: "Worthy is the Lamb, who was slain, to receive power and wealth and wisdom and strength and honour and glory and praise!" (Revelation 5:11-12)

If the throne room was noisy before, listen now—in Super Dolby Surround Sound. Thousands upon thousands and ten thousand times ten thousand. (The mathematicians can calculate exactly how many—for the rest of us, it is enough to know it is a lot.) All in a loud voice singing, "Worthy is the Lamb, who was slain." Worthy to receive sevenfold adoration—complete adoration: power, wealth, wisdom, strength, honour, glory, and praise.

Where once there was a dumbfounded silence across heaven, earth, and under heaven at no one being worthy to open the scroll, now something extraordinary happens:

> Every creature in heaven and on earth and under the earth and on the sea, and all that is in them, sang: "To him who sits on the throne and to the Lamb be praise and honour and glory and power, for ever and ever!" The four living creatures said, "Amen," and the elders fell down and worshipped. (Revelation 5:13-14)

Previously, in chapter 4, all glory and honour was given to the One who sits on the throne. Now, all glory and honour is given also to the Lamb. The highest honour and respect reserved for God alone is also given to the Lamb—because He is God and He is worthy.

The Lamb has real power. In fact, the Lamb is so powerful that even heaven's theme song has changed.

### Heaven's New Song

> They sang a new song: "You are worthy to take the scroll and to open its seals, because you were slain, and with your blood you purchased for God persons from every tribe and language, people and nation. You have made them to be a kingdom and priests to serve our God, and they will reign on the earth." (Revelation 5:9-10)

Imagine my mother, who gets super mad when our dog jumps on the couch. Here, not only is a farm animal sitting on God's favourite armchair, not only is all heavenly attention now turned to the Lamb, but the pre-programmed CD player has been changed as well.

It is a new song. Where the song in chapter 4 was dedicated to the Creator God, this song is dedicated to the Lamb because He was slain. Heaven's song of all eternity has been changed. How good being the Lamb must be that even the song in heaven has changed.

## Side Application: The Power Worth Having

### Is It Worth It?

May it be absurd, but is being the Lamb worth it for Jesus? Is trusting God all the way to the cross worth it? Well, judging by verses 6 through 14, yes. The Lamb is exalted and powerful.

For us, is following the way of the Lamb worth it? Is giving up my time, my money, my CDs, my boyfriend or girlfriend for the sake of the kingdom worth it? Is being a loser in this world worth it? Is trusting God all the way worth it for me?

When you could be president of a superpower, is merely being a powerless baby worth it?

Yes. There is real hope and confidence in our demotion. Jesus lived on the premise that it is better by far to trust God, be a loser, die, and go to heaven. This is how real power actually works—by trusting in God. That fact has to affect how we live in this world.

Why be a slain lamb when you can be a lion? Why would you want to give up two weeks of summer to go on a beach mission? Why give up Tuesday nights for Bible Fellowship when you could catch a cheap movie? Why give up half your pay packet for gospel work? Why give up power? Don't you want to have fun? Don't you want the power to enjoy yourself while you have the chance?

Of course we do. Just later.

A friend once commented to me that greed is not so much wanting more and more as wanting more and more now. As Christians and as citizens of heaven, we have everything—we are heirs to all that is in heaven and earth. We just have to wait for it.

Christians are not suffering for nothing. This is not party-pooper theology. There is a real promise of real good things—of real power—in the future, which sustains us in the present. If we see heaven like this, if we can see the Lamb glorified, it gives us reason to give up the things of this world. Power comes in giving up the props of this world and trusting God. Power comes from being Lamb-like—from giving up power.

It is scary, but it is a step of faith. We are given a vision from heaven today about what it will be like. The exalted Lamb means that trusting and relying in God makes sense. It is worth it. Baby Sam rather than Uncle Sam is worth it.

### Power We Can Have

Not only is this true for Jesus, but we can follow the Lamb ourselves.

He has done this for humanity. As John tells us:

> You were slain, and with your blood you purchased for God persons from every tribe and language, people and nation. You have made them to be a kingdom and priests to serve our God, and they will reign on the earth. (Revelation 5:9-10)

The Lamb was slain to purchase men and women for God—people in slavery to sin and to Satan. God gave a ransom to buy back His people, and nothing would satisfy except the price of the blood of His Son. No longer do we belong to Satan.

Now we are the people of God. We are given not the power to sin but the power to serve. We have been purchased not to do what we like but to be priests. And pardon the pun—we are to serve sacrificially. Through Jesus, we see that the way to having lion power is through becoming a lamb. And if you want to be a lion, be a lamb. Real power is sacrificial.

Why bother with serving each other in those unattractive and boring areas—like cleaning up the dishes, washing the tea towels, wiping down the toilet, taking the garbage out, talking to that socially difficult person? It does not get us anywhere. There is no popularity in doing that, no credit. No one sees it.

But the Lamb on the throne shows that not only is the way of the Lamb worth it, but that the way of the Lamb is followable. We have been purchased for that very reason.

It is in this way—in Lamb-like, sacrificial servitude—that we are to be a kingdom. This is how we are to reign over the earth: by sacrificially serving.

---

## Conclusion: Living as Subjects After a Glimpse into Heaven

So, how can we have power? How can we have wealth, wisdom, strength, and all the things we desire?

The world's way is self-promotion and self-preservation. God's way is Lamb-like—the way of demotion and sacrificial servitude.

From the vision of the slain Lamb on the throne, we know that the way to real power works. It is worth it. The Lamb is exalted. All of it was done for us so that we can follow Him—by trusting and by faith in God, which means powerlessness for us now.

It is like Frodo in Lord of the Rings. Everyone wants power. Humans, elves, dwarves, and orcs all want that power. But the way to power, in the end, is to get rid of that power. However sincere the motivation, Frodo realizes that this power ultimately corrupts and brings ruin. The ring must be destroyed.

Similarly, all power on earth ultimately corrupts. Whatever our motivations, if we keep seeking worldly power, it leads us away from humble obedience to God. The Lamb shows us that giving up power results in real power. Real power is the Lion trusting God, even if it meant being a Lamb all the way to the cross.

If we want real power, it means following the Lamb.

In the end, real power is Lamb-like.
