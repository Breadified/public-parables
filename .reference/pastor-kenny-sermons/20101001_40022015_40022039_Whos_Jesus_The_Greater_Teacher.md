# Matthew 22:15-39

**Title:** Who's Jesus? The Greater Teacher
**Date:** 31 October 2010
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Great Teachers

When we think of great teachers, we think of those whose influence extends far beyond their classroom. The fruit of truly great teaching can only be measured over years and even decades. But inevitably, when a new teacher arrives on the scene, there comes a testing—a trial to see if this person is truly worthy of being followed.

In Matthew 22, Jesus enters as the new teacher on the block. And three key Jewish groups come to test him, each approaching with their own agenda and their own trap.

## 1. The Herodians (22:15-17)

The first group are the Herodians—a political party aligned with King Herod and, by extension, with Rome. Though Jewish, they pragmatically accepted Roman rule for the sake of political efficiency.

Their approach to Jesus is deceptively flattering:

> "Teacher," they said, "we know you are a man of integrity and that you teach the way of God in accordance with the truth. You aren't swayed by men, because you pay no attention to who they are." (Matthew 22:16)

Notice how they address him as "Teacher." Throughout this passage, teaching and teachers appear repeatedly—each group testing him on this very point. And yes, their words about Jesus are true. But they speak with an edge—this is flattery, wrapped around a trap.

> "Tell us then, what is your opinion? Is it right to pay taxes to Caesar or not?" (Matthew 22:17)

This is the snare. If Jesus says to pay Caesar, he appears to betray God. If he says to obey God instead, it's treason—an executable offense. There seems to be no good answer.

But Jesus, knowing their evil intent, responds with sharp clarity:

> "Show me the coin used for paying the tax." They brought him a denarius, and he asked them, "Whose portrait is this? And whose inscription?" "Caesar's," they replied. Then he said to them, "Give to Caesar what is Caesar's, and to God what is God's." (Matthew 22:19-21)

The answer cuts through the false dilemma. The question is not "obey God or authorities"—the answer is: both. The coin bears Caesar's image, so give it back to Caesar. But here's the deeper point: humans bear God's image. We belong ultimately to God.

Jesus then establishes the principle that undergirds Christian citizenship:

> Everyone must submit himself to the governing authorities, for there is no authority except that which God has established. (Romans 13:1)

Our allegiance flows differently than the world assumes. Because our kingdom is not of this world but of the next, we can accept any government's authority. We don't require our government to be Christian to serve it faithfully. We need Christian citizens, not a Christian state. We want politicians who are Christians, not governments that are Christian. We want Christian judges, but the judicial system need not be Christian.

This is why the Church needs more Christian evangelists than Christian lawyers or politicians. Our mission is to convert people to Christ, not to Christianize the machinery of state.

As Christians, we follow the law of the land—not only when it's convenient, but genuinely. We can maintain both faith and citizenship without compromising our ultimate allegiance to Jesus.

### The Application: How We Dishonor Both

The problem is that we often disrespect both God and earthly authority at once—like the Herodians, we pit them against each other, following neither with genuine respect.

Consider taxes. We minimize our tax obligations to have more for God's work, then minimize our church giving to ensure we can cover taxes. In the end, the money goes to neither—it goes to ourselves. We've dishonored both while serving only ourselves.

Or consider speed limits. We speed when heading somewhere "spiritual," but arrive late to ordinary meetings. We use God and the state to justify our own rules. We end up honoring neither.

For young people, a similar tension appears with parental authority. We pit our parents against church, or church against family, when in reality the conflict is with our own laziness and lack of organization. We disrespect both, then blame the other. Don't try to trick God with false either-or choices.

## 2. The Sadducees (22:23-33)

The second group are the Sadducees—the skeptics who denied the resurrection of the dead. They believed only in the Pentateuch (the first five books), so they rejected the resurrection passages found in Isaiah, Daniel, Job, and the Psalms. Perhaps the joke was that they were always "sad, you see"—sad because they believed this life was all there was.

They approach Jesus with a convoluted scenario designed to expose the absurdity of resurrection faith:

> "Teacher," they said, "Moses told us that if a man dies without having children, his brother must marry the widow and have children for him." (Matthew 22:24)

This reflects the Old Testament law in Deuteronomy—the levirate marriage—designed to preserve a family's line. The Sadducees then construct a situation: imagine seven brothers, each marrying the same widow in succession as each dies childless. Finally the woman dies. "Now then, at the resurrection, whose wife will she be of the seven, since all of them were married to her?" (Matthew 22:28)

If resurrection is real, they argue, you'll have chaos in heaven. Jesus must be trapped.

But he replies:

> "You are in error because you do not know the Scriptures or the power of God. At the resurrection people will neither marry nor be given in marriage; they will be like the angels in heaven." (Matthew 22:29-30)

Different rules apply in heaven. The material reality of the future is not immaterial—it's simply different. No marriage means no procreation. (This makes evangelism eternally important; we cannot reproduce souls in heaven.)

But there's a deeper point. Jesus appeals to the Pentateuch itself—the very text the Sadducees claimed to honor:

> "But about the resurrection of the dead—have you not read what God said to you, 'I am the God of Abraham, the God of Isaac, and the God of Jacob'? He is not the God of the dead but of the living." (Matthew 22:31-32)

When God spoke to Moses at the burning bush, Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob had been dead for centuries. Yet God says "I am"—not "I was." If they had ceased to exist, God would say, "I was the God of Abraham." But resurrection means they live. God is the God of the living. When the crowds heard this, they were astonished at his teaching.

### The Application: Resurrection Changes Everything

We can fall into the Sadducees' error today when we live as though heaven operates by earth's rules. We chase degrees and promotions and titles—Rabbi, Doctor, Reverend—as if these will matter in eternity. We pour our lives into careers, studies, hobbies, possessions—accumulating things that will eventually become junk, as I've seen at countless garage sales.

But there is a resurrection to live for. This life is not all there is.

I know it's hard when you see friends living a certain lifestyle, indulging in sins that look appealing. You sacrifice your time, your money, your social networks, your comfort. You deny yourself things others enjoy. And I feel the weight of asking this of you. I love you, and I don't want you to feel like you're missing out.

But you're not missing out. Because there is a resurrection, your hard work goes toward something eternal. It will be remembered by the God of the living, and you will see it remembered in the presence of God forever.

## 3. The Pharisees (22:34-40)

The third group are the Pharisees—and frankly, they're the group most like us. They're obsessed with law and interpretation. They have commentaries upon commentaries, exploring what each law means. The Talmud, Mishnah, and Gemara represent layers of rabbinic opinion. Today we collect podcasts from our favorite Bible teachers the way they collected written commentaries.

But even among laws, the Pharisees recognized a hierarchy. Which commandment is greatest? If laws seem to contradict, which takes priority? It's the kind of question that sounds reasonable but has no satisfying answer—like asking a parent which child is their favorite.

So Jesus is tested again:

> "Teacher, which is the greatest commandment in the Law?" (Matthew 22:36)

How can he answer without creating a hierarchy that diminishes other commandments? Yet Jesus does:

> "Love the Lord your God with all your heart and with all your soul and with all your mind.' This is the first and greatest commandment. And the second is like it: 'Love your neighbor as yourself.' All the Law and the Prophets hang on these two commandments." (Matthew 22:37-40)

The answer is brilliant. There is no real hierarchy because all commandments flow from the same nature. They are all consistent with each other. They don't contradict—they all express the same fundamental principle. You cannot truly love God without loving your neighbor (Matthew 25:35-37).

If you needed one principle for simplicity, Jesus says it's love. Love God. Love each other. And they're not two separate things.

### The Critical Qualification

But here's where we must be careful: love means God's definition of love, not ours.

We're constantly tempted to redefine love in our own terms. We want to call premarital sex "love." We want to call adultery "love." We want to call homosexuality "love." How can something that feels so good be wrong?

Just this week, a young man in my Scripture class at Cherrybrook Tech decided that heaven couldn't really be heaven if his non-Christian friends weren't there. He couldn't accept that Christians teach non-Christians will face judgment. So he decided he didn't want to be Christian anymore—because that heaven wasn't the one he signed up for. When I asked him whose definition of heaven that was, he admitted: his own.

We constantly try to define both heaven and love in terms that suit us. But God has already revealed his definitions in Scripture.

There's also something subtle happening among the Pharisees: they've forgotten the big principle and gotten lost in the nitty-gritty details. What exactly constitutes "work" on the Sabbath? They've obsessed over minutiae so much that they've become blind to the forest—and they end up conspiring to kill the very one who embodied love itself.

## 4. Jesus: The Greater Teacher (22:41-46, 23:1-12)

Now it's Jesus' turn to question:

> "What do you think about the Christ? Whose son is he?" "The son of David," they replied. He said to them, "How is it then that David, speaking by the Spirit, calls him 'Lord'? For he says, 'The Lord said to my Lord: Sit at my right hand until I put your enemies under your feet.' If then David calls him 'Lord,' how can he be his son?" No one could say a word in reply, and from that day on no one dared to ask him any more questions. (Matthew 22:41-46)

The Greater Teacher has silenced them all. And what makes him greater than all the other teachers?

> "The teachers of the law and the Pharisees sit in Moses' seat. So you must obey them and do everything they tell you. But do not do what they do, for they do not practice what they preach. They tie up heavy loads and put them on men's shoulders, but they themselves are not willing to lift a finger to move them." (Matthew 23:2-4)

The false teachers preach without practicing. They burden people without bearing the burden themselves. But Jesus:

- Practices what he preaches
- Frees people from their burdens
- Is willing to lift a finger—in fact, willing to be lifted on a cross—to help you

> "But you are not to be called 'Rabbi,' for you have only one Master and you are all brothers... Nor are you to be called 'teacher,' for you have one Teacher, the Christ. The greatest among you will be your servant. For whoever exalts himself will be humbled, and whoever humbles himself will be exalted." (Matthew 23:8-12)

## The Call

So listen to the Greater Teacher. Here's what he asks of us:

**To the Herodians:** Stop pitting obedience to God against obedience to law. They're not opposed. Serve both faithfully.

**To the Sadducees:** Stop living as if there is no resurrection. You can make an impact on eternity. Your work matters forever.

**To the Pharisees:** Stop getting lost in the details and forgetting the principle. Love God. Love your neighbor. Everything flows from that.

In the movie *Mr. Holland's Opus*, an old student reflects on her retired teacher:

> "Mr. Holland had a profound influence on my life... But I have a feeling that he considers a great part of his own life misspent. Rumor had it he was always working on this symphony of his... But Mr. Holland isn't rich and he isn't famous, at least not outside of our little town. So it might be easy for him to think himself a failure. But he would be wrong... There is not a life in this room that you have not touched, and each of us is a better person because of you. We are your symphony, Mr. Holland. We are the melodies and the notes of your opus. We are the music of your life."

You are the symphony of Jesus. You are the melody of his teaching, the notes of his opus. Let that shape how you live—not for temporary gain, but for eternal significance.
