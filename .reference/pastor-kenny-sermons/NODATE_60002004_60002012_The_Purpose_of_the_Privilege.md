## The Living Stone

### Jesus as the Foundation

When aliens from space observed human behavior without context, they might conclude that red lights mean stop, green means go, and yellow means go faster. They'd notice we're glued to screens and that dogs are actually the master race on Earth. But what would they observe about Christians?

Peter begins by establishing a fundamental truth: **Christians have come to the living Stone** (verse 4).

> As you come to him, the living Stone—rejected by humans but chosen by God and precious to him. (2:4)

This living Stone is Jesus, whom the Old Testament prophesied would be both chosen and rejected. Isaiah 28:16 describes God's deliberate selection:

> See, I lay a stone in Zion, a chosen and precious cornerstone. (2:6)

### Understanding the Cornerstone

In ancient construction, the cornerstone was absolutely critical—it was the reference stone by which all other stones were positioned. If the cornerstone was crooked or poorly placed, the entire building would be skewed. Finding and preparing such a stone required tremendous skill and effort. The stone had to be perfectly straight and at right angles, carved from rough rock with rudimentary tools.

In biblical metaphor, the cornerstone was placed with deliberation. It determined the structural integrity of the entire building. Once positioned correctly, all other stones were set in relation to it, and it bore the weight of the entire structure.

God the expert builder carefully chose a precious, valuable stone—Jesus—as the basis for everything to come. This stone is so perfect that **those who trust in him will never be put to shame** (2:6b).

### The Response: Belief and Rejection

To believers, this stone is precious (timē in Greek—valuable and honored). Verse 7 reveals this precious stone is also:

> The stone the builders rejected has become the cornerstone. (2:7)

Foolish builders—like the leaders of Israel who rejected and killed Jesus—overlooked what would have made the entire building whole. What they carelessly rejected became the very stone that now causes them to stumble:

> A stone that causes people to stumble and a rock that makes them fall. (2:8)

The rejected stone, discarded at the building site, now trips those who refuse it. They stumble and fall, injuring themselves.

**Why do they stumble?** From a human perspective, they disobeyed the message (verse 8)—they refused to believe (verse 7). From God's perspective, they were destined for this fall. The word "destined" (tithēmi) in verse 8 is the same word used for "laid" in verse 6: God laid down the cornerstone of Christ for some to follow, and the same stone for others to stumble over.

Here lies a profound mystery: **This is not a passive stone. It is a living stone—it does things to people.** It has agency and action. To some, it is the foundation to build upon. To others, it becomes an obstacle they cannot escape. There is no neutral ground. The word of God always calls for a response: acceptance or rejection.

## Living Stones: The New Temple

### God's Presence Throughout History

As a result of coming to the living Stone, Christians become something entirely new:

> You also, like living stones, are being built into a spiritual house to be a holy priesthood, offering spiritual sacrifices acceptable to God through Jesus Christ. (2:5)

The temple is no longer a building.

Throughout the Old Testament, God's visible presence followed a remarkable pattern. In the Garden of Eden, God walked with Adam and Eve, but sin separated humanity from his visible presence. Centuries later, when God led Israel out of Egypt, his glory appeared as a pillar of cloud by day and fire by night—the visible evidence of his presence among his people.

This same glory filled the tent of meeting where God met with Moses. Later, it filled Solomon's temple in Jerusalem—the house God had promised to David. But in Ezekiel's time, around 586 BC, that glory departed from the temple because of the people's sin, and the temple was destroyed by Babylonian invaders.

The prophets promised restoration. Haggai declared that the glory of the new temple would be greater than the former (Haggai 2:9). Yet when the second temple was rebuilt after exile, God's glory never descended as it had under Solomon. For over 400 years, faithful Israel waited for the fulfillment of Malachi's prophecy: "The Lord whom you seek will suddenly come to his temple" (Malachi 3:1).

### Jesus: The True Temple

Then Jesus appeared. In the temple courts, he startled everyone by claiming that **he himself is the true temple of God**. His body is the dwelling place of God:

> God was pleased to have all his fullness dwell in him. (Colossians 1:19)

In the New Testament, Jesus is the new and better temple—God dwelling among his people. But the promise doesn't stop there. After Pentecost, Jesus promised his presence would also indwell his people:

> If anyone loves me, he will obey my teaching. My Father will love him, and we will come to him and make our home with him. (John 14:23)

> Do you not know that your bodies are temples of the Holy Spirit, who is in you? (1 Corinthians 6:19)

**The people of God are now the true temple—the place where God dwells.**

### We Are the Sacred Building

The church began with a cornerstone laid in Zion, metaphorically replacing the original temple. Living stones—the apostles, early followers, and now **you**—are continually being added. The temple is now us, not a building.

This doesn't mean church buildings are unimportant for practical reasons, but we must never confuse a sacred building with sacred people. A building with beautiful architecture, ornate furniture, and stained glass windows is not what makes a place holy. The people do.

The church could meet in an elaborate cathedral or a tent, a school hall, or a renovated house. **What matters is that God's people gather together.** Imagine a giant human pyramid—that's the temple. You're looking at it now in this room.

This reality has profound implications: **God's people can meet anywhere, and we must treat each other as sacred.**

### Treating Each Other as Sacred

In the Old Testament, approaching God's temple required careful preparation. One had to be ritually cleansed, bring a sacrifice, dress appropriately, and show reverence. The temple was sacred space demanding sacred behavior.

We don't need ritual washings now, nor must we wear special clothes or remove our shoes. But something is sacred—**the real temple: the people gathered before you.** In every Christian present is God through his Word and Spirit. We must honor each other accordingly.

Peter reminds us of what was taught earlier:

> Now that you have purified yourselves by obeying the truth so that you have sincere love for each other, love one another deeply, from the heart. (1 Peter 1:22)

### Commitment to One Another

Many people maintain sacred commitments to less important things. Some have favorite TV shows, hobbies, or pastimes they never skip—checking Facebook, playing piano, attending sports events. We treat these with a reverence that should shame us.

Yet we often treat our commitment to the church casually. We SMS an apology minutes before a gathering, saying we're busy with other things. We'd never do this to our employer or school. We couldn't text our piano teacher: "Sorry, I slept in, wasn't paying attention." Yet we do this to church constantly.

Some argue, "It's just a once-off." But once-offs accumulate. A couple of group assignments, a few piano concerts, oversleeping a few times, exams, university camps, baptisms, extended holidays—and suddenly you're only present eight months out of twelve. What other enterprise do you treat as optional two-thirds of the time?

**You cannot love casually.** Genuine love requires commitment. You can't flit between groups and expect deep connection. Church is like fitness: interrupting the practice loses most of the benefit. You're not a convenience store where people come to grab what they want and leave.

**You are to each other living stones of God—brick by brick added by God, cemented together by the blood of Jesus.** Treat each other as the sacred temple you are.

### Rid Yourselves of Evil

Peter explicitly calls for removal of all that poisons the temple:

> Therefore, rid yourselves of all malice and all deceit, hypocrisy, envy, and slander of every kind. (2:1)

**Malice** is hatred—wishing harm on another.

**Deceit** is falsehood, hiding our sins. Ironically, exposure through confession is the healthiest path forward.

**Hypocrisy** is insincerity—concealing our failures while pretending to be sinless.

**Envy** is dissatisfaction with others' blessings, jealousy of their achievements, marks, or appearance. It's the failure to rejoice in the gifts God has given others. We discount what we have because we want what they have.

**Slander** is destructive speech—gossip and character assassination.

These toxins corrode the temple. Our job is to serve this temple of people, prioritizing living stones above all else.

## The New Priesthood

### Sacrificial Service

Just as Old Testament priests offered sacrifices to God, **we are now the holy priesthood, offering spiritual sacrifices acceptable to God** (2:5). These aren't bulls and goats. Spiritual sacrifice means loving each other sacrificially—serving one another.

In particular, we serve by speaking about God.

### Declaring the Praises of God

> But you are a chosen people, a royal priesthood, a holy nation, God's special possession, that you may declare the praises of him who called you out of darkness into his wonderful light. (2:9)

When Peter speaks of praise, he's not referring to the religious exercises or songs we perform at church. The word means something simpler and more profound: **to advertise.**

Suppose you observe a friend doing something excellent or displaying admirable character. You then tell others about this quality. That's praise—advertising someone's nobility or achievements. Praise can be spoken naturally or sung, but the essence is the same: you're promoting the excellences of someone worthy of honor.

When we declare praise, we advertise God. We tell forth—as loudly and widely as possible—the excellences of God, that everyone might honor him. The Psalms constantly follow the command to praise God with the actual declaration of his mighty character and deeds.

Yet often the call for praise isn't followed by actual praise. **What is the content of our praise?**

> Once you were not a people, but now you are the people of God; once you had not received mercy, but now you have received mercy. (2:10)

We declare what God has done. We didn't deserve his mercy, yet he gave it. We were outsiders; now we belong to his people.

### Declaring Praise to One Another

Praise isn't primarily about what we say directly to God. It's a public declaration—we praise God when we tell others about his wonderful works and character. When we declare God's praises to each other, we encourage the body:

- Share how God is changing you. What anger did you suppress? What malice have you put away? What events helped you?
- Tell of how specific talks challenged you, how a friend's witness affected you.
- Reflect on your own conversion and how the gospel is working in your life.
- Recognize that we are doubtful creatures who need to hear the gospel again and again, needing continual assurance that it works.

**When we declare God's praises to each other, we are built up.** Our connection deepens as we become mutually aware of God's saving work.

### Declaring Praise to the World

But our declaration isn't limited to fellow believers. We must advertise to the whole world what Jesus has done:

> If you are into praise—you better be into advertising to your non-Christian friends about what Jesus has done for you.

As the year ends, reflect: How many friends have you told about Jesus? If that's too difficult, how many have you invited to church? If that seems impossible, how many have you prayed for?

**Do you care to advertise your Lord?**

You are the chosen race, the royal priesthood, a holy nation, a people belonging to God. This calling is not optional—it's central to your identity.

## What Will He See?

### When Jesus Returns

When next week arrives—after the excitement of this service fades, when being Christian feels less fun, and you're tired and tempted—**if Jesus were to visit and observe you, what would he see?** What would he identify as sacred in your life?

More practically, when you go about your week, what will observers conclude is important to you? Would they see a difference between your life and those who don't follow Christ?

The aliens observing human civilization would assess us by our priorities and commitments. Similarly, Jesus will assess what we truly value.

### Two Marks of Authentic Christianity

As Christians come to the living Stone, Jesus will look for two things:

**First: Deep love for one another.** 

> I pray that you would demonstrate sincere love for each other, loved deeply from the heart. That you show you are genuinely committed to your church family—these fellow aliens in this world. That your commitment to each other is as sacred as the ancient temple was sacred to the Jews.

**Second: Declaration of God's praises.**

> He will find a people declaring the praises of his Son to each other and to the world. He'll come and find us talking—testifying to what God has done, encouraging each other, and boldly proclaiming Jesus to those who need to hear.

These are the marks of a people who have truly come to the living Stone and become living stones themselves.
