# Exodus 1:1-3:7

**Title:** The People of God  
**Date:** 18 August 2024  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction: A World in Decline

Our world is moving backward. We see it in the Olympics with nepotism and corruption masquerading as competition. We witness a demographic winter descending—the Malthusian Population Bomb never arrived, but instead an aging world that will lose its dynamism, creativity, scientists, workers, and innovators. Fewer taxpayers. A shrinking economy. Stagnant living standards. Urban decay like Detroit, abandoned Japanese villages, and collapsing European towns.

This is the backdrop against which the story of Exodus unfolds. The people of God find themselves enslaved by a powerful king in a foreign land, trapped by oppression and their own idolatry. The question Exodus asks is urgent and ancient: **Is there a way out?**

## Part 1: The People (1:1-7)

### Israel Multiplies

> These are the names of the sons of Israel who went to Egypt with Jacob, each with his family: Reuben, Simeon, Levi and Judah; Issachar, Zebulun and Benjamin; Dan and Naphtali; Gad and Asher. The descendants of Jacob numbered seventy in all; Joseph was already in Egypt. (Exodus 1:1-5)

This opening is a continuation of Genesis. Israel is Jacob—he had twelve sons, each with their own families, totaling seventy people in Egypt. Joseph had already risen to become Prime Minister under Pharaoh and, through God's provision, saved Egypt from famine. When the famine came, Jacob's entire family migrated to Egypt under Joseph's care and protection.

Then comes a generational shift:

> Now Joseph and all his brothers and all that generation died, but the Israelites were exceedingly fruitful; they multiplied greatly, increased in numbers and became so numerous that the land was filled with them. (Exodus 1:6-7)

Notice the cumulative language of blessing: *exceedingly fruitful* (Hebrew: *phar*), *multiplied greatly* (Hebrew: *shara*—to swarm like locusts), *increased in numbers* (*rav*—mighty), *so numerous* (*tsum*—exceedingly), *filled the land* (*mala*—swarmed).

This repeated vocabulary appears throughout the passage (verses 9, 10, 12, 20), emphasizing God's fulfillment of His ancient promise. The historical numbers suggest perhaps 600,000 men, possibly 2-3 million Israelites total—a staggering increase from seventy people over roughly four hundred years.

### Why Did They Have So Many Children?

The Israelites trusted in God's promises. They were living out the creation mandate given to Adam and Eve:

> God blessed them and said to them, "Be fruitful and increase in number; fill the earth and subdue it." (Genesis 1:28)

This command was renewed to Noah and to Abraham. When Abraham and Sarah were barren and old, God made an audacious promise:

> I will make you into a great nation, and I will bless you; I will make your name great, and you will be a blessing. All peoples on earth will be blessed through you. (Genesis 12:1-3)

And when Abraham questioned how this could be:

> He took him outside and said, "Look up at the sky and count the stars—if indeed you can count them." Then he said to him, "So shall your offspring be." Abram believed the Lord, and he credited it to him as righteousness. (Genesis 15:4-6)

The Israelites, enslaved and powerless, had only one freedom left: to obey God's command to multiply. They trusted His promises with their fertility.

### Children as Blessing

Throughout Scripture, children are defined as blessing, not burden. This is anchored in creation, not circumstance:

> Like arrows in the hands of a warrior are children born in one's youth. Blessed is the man whose quiver is full of them. They will not be put to shame when they contend with their opponents in the gate. (Psalm 127:4-5)

> Your wife will be like a fruitful vine within your house; your sons will be like olive shoots around your table. Thus is the man blessed who fears the Lord. (Psalm 128:3-4)

> Children are a heritage from the Lord, offspring a reward from him. (Psalm 127:3)

The word "heritage" means inheritance—but not *my* inheritance. We often misunderstand. In agrarian societies, children were assets: extra hands for farm work. In modern cities, an extra child feels like another mouth to feed. So we tell ourselves: the fewer, the better. In a wealthy society with superannuation, we don't need children to care for us.

But this thinking misses the point. Children are not primarily for me. They are an inheritance *for the future*—a gift from God to coming generations. They are the next generation who will serve the world, proclaim the gospel, teach righteousness. When I am gone, the work continues through them. Children teach us to be unselfish 24/7. This is how we grow spiritually.

### A Wartime Perspective

Consider World War II. When soldiers were dying and the war's end was uncertain, the value of children heightened for a nation's future. Mothers were bearing 5-7 children. Suddenly, measures considered too expensive or politically unacceptable in peacetime became urgent:

- The National Milk Scheme provided subsidized or free milk to pregnant women and nursing mothers
- The Vitamin Welfare Scheme gave expectant mothers free orange juice, cod liver oil, and vitamins
- Hospital maternity beds increased by fifty percent, institutionalizing childbirth

Desperate times called for desperate measures. The problem today is that we think it's peacetime. We're comfortable, complacent, blissfully unaware of our community's needs, ghettoed in our churches or distracted by consumption.

### God's Promises Continue

Some ask: does the creation mandate still apply after Christ? Haven't things changed?

Much has changed. The offspring Israel hoped for—Jesus—has come and risen. The promised land is now heavenly, not earthly. The gospel's proclamation is our primary work.

Yet the creation mandate continues because marriage continues. It hasn't been abolished. And to populate the kingdom:

> After this I looked, and there before me was a great multitude that no one could count, from every nation, tribe, people and language, standing before the throne and before the Lamb. (Revelation 7:9)

This multitude is made of people—the same language used for the stars in the sky: *no one could count them*. To build this multitude, we need children. Children made ideally within marriages, the same institution unchanged since creation.

> "She is your partner, the wife of your marriage covenant. And what does the one God seek? Godly offspring." (Malachi 2:14-15)

God seeks godly offspring. They are His gift to the world.

### Practical Counsel for Young Families

To young people pressured by culture: don't waste your youth. Have a modest lifestyle. Skip the overseas holiday for a season. Live in a house suitable for renting to gain extra income. Perhaps the husband earns more to free the wife to mother. Find creative ways to make money without the pressure of both spouses' full-time work.

I tell you this not to enforce legalism or judge your circumstances, but out of fatherly love for your future. Other pastors counsel you to earn for the church. I counsel you to earn for your children. Other pastors are unmoved if you're poor and must move far away. I help you so we can stay together as a community.

There is no need to be suspicious of my intent. I gain nothing except the wish that our great-grandchildren might live together in a better world while waiting for Jesus.

### Overcoming Cultural Pressure

Yes, there's immense pressure against children. Our culture medicalizes pregnancy to eliminate children. Take a pill; the problem disappears. We do everything possible to avoid them, at detriment to our spiritual growth. We cannot mature without investing in the next generation.

Yet the culture is not impossible to change. We start with ourselves. With greater numbers of committed people, we can shift the point. The best day to start was yesterday. The second best is today.

And practically: in Australia, the government subsidizes child-raising. There's free healthcare, free education. FTBA, FTBAS, FTBB, FTBBS, youth allowance, Austudy. They've budgeted for 1-2 children per couple but set no limit. Stack it up. They're paying you to have children.

But the hardest hurdle is internal—our mind, our fears, our willingness. Do we subscribe to culture, or kingdom culture? Do we hold our dreams, or God's promises? Do we keep our ambitions, or bow to His glory?

The Israelites could have kept a small target: quality over quantity. But they trusted God's promises. As slaves, their only freedom was to obey and trust Him with the results. And that trust was rewarded.

## Part 2: Persecution (1:8-2:10)

### Pharaoh's Threat

> Then a new king, to whom Joseph meant nothing, came to power in Egypt. "Look," he said to his people, "the Israelites have become far too numerous for us. Come, we must deal shrewdly with them or they will become even more numerous and, if war breaks out, will join our enemies, fight against us and leave the country." (Exodus 1:8-10)

A new generation of Egyptians forgot Joseph and his salvation. But they couldn't ignore the Israelites' explosive growth. When a minority becomes visible, it becomes a threat. The more numerous a population, the more it can change things. Pharaoh panicked.

### First Strategy: Enslavement

> So they put slave masters over them to oppress them with forced labor, and they built Pithom and Rameses as store cities for Pharaoh. The Egyptians worked them ruthlessly. They made their lives bitter with harsh labor in brick and mortar and with all kinds of work in the fields; in all their harsh labor the Egyptians worked them ruthlessly. (Exodus 1:11, 13-14)

But oppression backfired:

> But the more they were oppressed, the more they multiplied and spread; so the Egyptians came to dread the Israelites. (Exodus 1:12)

God's hand was upon them. The more pressure applied, the more they grew—like spray-resistant pests multiplying despite pesticides.

### Second Strategy: Infanticide (Male Genocide)

Enslavement failed. So Pharaoh escalated to abortion:

> The king of Egypt said to the Hebrew midwives, whose names were Shiphrah and Puah: "When you are helping the Hebrew women during childbirth on the delivery stool, if you see that the baby is a boy, kill him; but if it is a girl, let her live." (Exodus 1:15-16)

The logic was twisted: kill the boys (future warriors, rebels) but keep the girls (who would marry Egyptians or become domestic help). But the midwives feared God:

> The midwives, however, feared God and did not do what the king of Egypt had told them to do; they let the boys live. (Exodus 1:17)

God's law transcends man's law. You cannot hide behind "I was following orders." You are responsible for your actions. The midwives thought for themselves and feared God more than Pharaoh.

When questioned, they answered cleverly: "Hebrew women are not like Egyptian women; they are vigorous and give birth before the midwives arrive." (Exodus 1:19). Whether literal truth or strategic delay, they preserved life.

And once again, despite oppression, the people multiplied:

> So God was kind to the midwives and the people increased and became even more numerous. And because the midwives feared God, he gave them families of their own. (Exodus 1:20-21)

The midwives, likely women without children of their own (having pursued work instead), were blessed with families. God rewards fear of Him over fear of authorities.

### Third Strategy: Mass Infanticide

When subtlety failed, Pharaoh became explicit:

> Then Pharaoh gave this order to all his people: "Every Hebrew boy that is born you must throw into the Nile, but let every girl live." (Exodus 1:22)

The Nile—the god of Egypt. A dual purpose: kill the boys and feed their deity. But one family would escape this fate.

### Moses' Rescue: God's Providence

> Now a man of the tribe of Levi married a Levite woman, and she became pregnant and gave birth to a son. When she saw that he was a fine child, she hid him for three months. (Exodus 2:1-2)

Babies are beautiful so that we won't give them back. The mother hid her son, but after three months, his cries became impossible to conceal:

> But when she could hide him no longer, she got a papyrus basket for him and coated it with tar and pitch. Then she placed the child in it and put it among the reeds along the bank of the Nile. His sister stood at a distance to see what would happen to him. (Exodus 2:3-4)

In a darkly ironic way, the mother was following Pharaoh's law—she put the baby in the Nile! But not to kill him; most likely, she hid him in the reeds during the day, retrieving him at night, with her daughter standing guard.

Then the unexpected:

> Then Pharaoh's daughter went down to the Nile to bathe, and her attendants were walking along the riverbank. She saw the basket among the reeds and sent her female slave to get it. She opened it and saw the baby. He was crying, and she felt sorry for him. "This is one of the Hebrew babies," she said. (Exodus 2:5-6)

An Egyptian princess heard a Hebrew baby's cry and felt compassion. And who appeared at just that moment? A Hebrew girl.

> Then his sister asked Pharaoh's daughter, "Shall I go and get one of the Hebrew women to nurse the baby for you?" "Yes, go," she answered. So the girl went and got the baby's mother. Pharaoh's daughter said to her, "Take this baby and nurse him for me, and I will pay you." So the woman took the baby and nursed him. When the child grew older, she took him to Pharaoh's daughter and he became her son. She named him Moses, saying, "I drew him out of the water." (Exodus 2:7-10)

Pharaoh failed to kill this boy. Instead, God arranged for Pharaoh's own daughter to raise him, educate him in Pharaoh's court, and pay for his care! The baby pulled from the water became the man who would lead Israel *out* of Egypt.

### The Pattern: God Against Opposition

Throughout this passage, we see a pattern: Pharaoh opposes; God's people multiply anyway. It's like fighting against an enemy you cannot defeat—because you're not really fighting against the people. You're fighting against God.

Pharaoh is playing chess against an invisible opponent who knows every move before it's made.

## Part 3: Progress Stalled (1:10, 22; 2:22)

### The Problem: Foreignness

The Israelites multiplied. They grew numerous, a blessing fulfilled. But there was a problem: they were aliens in a foreign land.

> When the child grew older, she took him to Pharaoh's daughter and he became her son. She named him Moses, saying, "I drew him out of the water." ... Moses agreed to stay with the man, who gave his daughter Zipporah to Moses in marriage. Zipporah gave birth to a son, and Moses named him Gershom, saying, "I have become an alien in a foreign land." (Exodus 2:10, 21-22)

*Alien*—a temporary resident who doesn't belong. *Foreign land*—not home. Consider today's asylum seekers in Nauru or East Timor. In the ancient Near East, land meant everything: food, security, identity, survival. This explains why people in Palestine and Afghanistan fight so fiercely for home.

More importantly: God's promise to Abraham was always about *land*.

> The LORD appeared to Abram and said, "To your offspring I will give this land." (Genesis 12:7)

> "Lift up your eyes from where you are and look north and south, east and west. All the land that you see I will give to you and your offspring forever." (Genesis 13:14-15)

> "I am the LORD, who brought you out of Ur of the Chaldeans to give you this land to take possession of it." (Genesis 15:7)

> "On that day the LORD made a covenant with Abram and said, 'To your descendants I give this land, from the river of Egypt to the great river, the Euphrates.'" (Genesis 15:17-18)

> "The whole land of Canaan, where you are now an alien, I will give as an everlasting possession to you and your descendants after you." (Genesis 17:8)

The report card reads: **People—CHECK.** Numerous as stars. But **Land—NOT YET.** They are slaves in a foreign land. And as long as they remain in Egypt, they remain enslaved.

The Israelites' true hope doesn't lie in Midian or Egypt. It lies elsewhere—in God's promise and His timing.

## Part 4: The God Who Hears and Saves (2:23-3:7)

### The Crisis and the Call

> During that long period, the king of Egypt died. The Israelites groaned in their slavery and cried out, and their cry for help because of their slavery went up to God. God heard their groaning and he remembered his covenant with Abraham, with Isaac and with Jacob. So God looked on the Israelites and was concerned about them. (Exodus 2:23-25)

Forty years pass. A new Pharaoh dies. The Israelites cry out under renewed oppression. But their cry reaches God's ears.

*Remembered*—not because God had forgotten. Rather, He did not forget. From our human perspective, He remembers His covenant.

Then Moses encounters God:

> Now Moses was tending the flock of Jethro his father-in-law, the priest of Midian, and he led the flock to the far side of the desert and came to Horeb, the mountain of God. There the angel of the LORD appeared to him in flames of fire from within a bush. Moses saw that though the bush was on fire it did not burn up. So Moses thought, "I will go over and see this strange sight—why the bush does not burn up." When the LORD saw that he had gone over to look, God called to him from within the bush, "Moses! Moses!" And Moses said, "Here I am." (Exodus 3:1-4)

> "Do not come any closer," God said. "Take off your sandals, for the place where you are standing is holy ground. Then he said, "I am the God of your father, the God of Abraham, the God of Isaac and the God of Jacob." At this, Moses hid his face, because he was afraid to look at God. (Exodus 3:5-6)

### God Sees, Hears, and Acts

> The LORD said, "I have indeed seen the misery of my people in Egypt. I have heard them crying out because of their slave drivers, and I am concerned about their suffering. So I have come down to rescue them from the hand of the Egyptians and to bring them up out of that land into a good and spacious land, a land flowing with milk and honey—the home of the Canaanites, Hittites, Amorites, Perizzites, Hivites and Jebusites. And now the cry of the Israelites has reached me, and I have seen the way the Egyptians are oppressing them." (Exodus 3:7-9)

Notice the progression: **I have seen... I have heard... I am concerned... I have come down to rescue.**

If an Egyptian princess could hear the cry of a Hebrew baby and be moved with compassion, surely God hears His people. If Moses could hear the cries of Jethro's daughters and rescue them, surely God hears and will rescue.

He hears their cry. He is concerned for them. And He will draw them from the water—out of slavery into a land flowing with milk and honey, the first time this phrase appears in Scripture. The promise is about to be fulfilled.

### The Name of God

Moses asks a crucial question:

> "Suppose I go to the Israelites and say to them, 'The God of your fathers has sent me to you,' and they ask me, 'What is his name?' Then what shall I tell them?" (Exodus 3:13)

God's response is revolutionary:

> God said to Moses, "I AM WHO I AM. This is what you are to say to the Israelites: 'I AM has sent me to you.'" (Exodus 3:14)

The name carries a play on Hebrew letters. *Yahweh*, the Tetragrammaton, comes from the same root as "I AM" or "I will be what I will be."

What kind of name is this? It's not a label. It's a declaration of *being* and *action*. "I am what I do." A name in the ancient Near East revealed character. Moses—drawn out of water. Gershom—alien in a foreign land. God's name reveals who He is through His deeds.

> God also said to Moses, "Say to the Israelites, 'The LORD, the God of your fathers—the God of Abraham, the God of Isaac and the God of Jacob—has sent me to you.' This is my name forever, the name by which I am to be remembered from generation to generation." (Exodus 3:15)

Today names might feel arbitrary—Apple, Happy, Dollar. But in the ancient world, names mattered. They revealed essence. God's name—Yahweh, *I AM*—means: look at My deeds. You will see who I am. God is known by His actions.

As we read through Exodus, we'll see exactly what kind of God He is:

> "The elders of Israel will listen to you. Then you and the elders are to go to the king of Egypt and say to him, 'The LORD, the God of the Hebrews, has met with us. Let us take a three-day journey into the desert to offer sacrifices to the LORD our God.' But I know that the king of Egypt will not let you go unless a mighty hand compels him. So I will stretch out my hand and strike the Egyptians with all the wonders that I will perform among them. After that, he will let you go. And I will make the Egyptians favorably disposed toward this people, so that when you leave you will not go empty-handed." (Exodus 3:18-21)

Through the Passover, through the sea, through the desert—through His mighty actions—we see that *Yahweh is a God who saves*. "I am what I do" means "I am the Savior."

### Jesus: The Fulfillment

A thousand years later, this theme continues:

> About eight days after Jesus said this, he took Peter, John and James with him and went up onto a mountain to pray. As he was praying, the appearance of his face changed, and his clothes became as bright as a flash of lightning. Two men, Moses and Elijah, appeared in glorious splendor, talking with Jesus. They spoke about his exodus departure, which he was about to bring to fulfillment at Jerusalem. (Luke 9:28-31)

Jesus—Joshua—*He saves*. That is who God is. That is the action that defines Him. On the cross, God shows us His character through His deeds. He lives up to His name. He is Savior.

## Conclusion: Who Are You?

The question comes back to us: **What is your name?** Who are you becoming? Are you a Christian—one who lives under grace and whose calling is to evangelize? Or are you something else?

The God of Exodus is still the God of today. He hears the cries of His people. He sees their misery. He is concerned about their suffering. And He comes down to rescue.

The work of building God's kingdom continues. It requires people—children raised to know and love Jesus, to serve their world, to carry forward His gospel. It requires us to trust His promises, even when the culture opposes. It requires faith that when we obey and multiply, God will provide.

The best day to start was yesterday. The second best day is today.
