# John 12:1-33

**Title:** The Hour Has Come  
**Date:** 20 February 2015  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## The Problem of Wrong Order

John invites us to explore John 12:1-33 through a lens of *timing*—discovering that several key events in this passage seem to occur in the wrong sequence. The episode with Mary's sacrifice (12:1-8), the triumphal entry (12:12-16), and the drawing of all nations (12:17-22) all point forward to Jesus's death and glorification. Yet they all happen *before* that death occurs.

This isn't accidental. It's John showing us something profound: the events of Passover week unfold in an order that only makes sense when we understand what must happen first.

---

## 1. A Burial Before Death (12:1-8)

Six days before Passover, Jesus arrives in Bethany where his friend Lazarus—whom he had raised from the dead—now lives. A dinner is held in his honor, with Martha serving and Lazarus reclining at table.

Then Mary does something remarkable: she takes about a pint of pure nard, an expensive perfume (myrrh), and anoints Jesus's feet, wiping them with her hair. The fragrance fills the entire house.

### Why Myrrh?

Mary's choice is deliberate. Myrrh was used in Jewish burial practices—the same spices used to prepare bodies for entombment. Yet here she uses it while Jesus is still very much alive. This is the embalming ritual performed *before the death*.

Jesus himself confirms this interpretation: "Leave her alone. It was intended that she should save this perfume for the day of my burial" (12:7). Mary somehow understands what's coming. In her gratitude for her brother's resurrection, she prepares Jesus for his own death.

### Judas's Objection

Not everyone appreciates Mary's act. Judas Iscariot objects: "Why wasn't this perfume sold and the money given to the poor? It was worth a year's wages" (12:5-6). The Gospel is blunt about Judas's motives—he didn't care about the poor but was a thief who helped himself from the money bag.

The greedy are always shocked by generosity. Judas sees only wasted money; he cannot comprehend love expressed through costly sacrifice.

Jesus's response reframes the entire issue:

> "You will always have the poor among you, but you will not always have me" (12:8)

This isn't a callous statement about poverty. Rather, Jesus is saying: *this is a funeral*. And funerals are expensive. No one questions the cost of a coffin or flowers at a graveside—we understand that honor for the dead demands resources. Mary's act is an honor to Jesus before his death.

### The Timing Problem

But there's the problem: embalming happens *after* death, not before. This anointing is premature—offensive, even, like asking to read someone's will before they die. Yet Mary performs it while Jesus lives.

Why? Because **Jesus is going to die**.

## 2. A Procession Without an Army (12:12-16)

The next day brings another scene of wrong timing. The crowd hears that Jesus approaches Jerusalem and rushes out to meet him. They take palm branches and shout:

> "Hosanna!"  
> "Blessed is he who comes in the name of the Lord!"  
> "Blessed is the king of Israel!" (12:13)

Jesus finds a young donkey and sits on it. In Judea's mountainous terrain, donkeys were the transport of kings—a grand choice. The crowds quote Zechariah's prophecy:

> "Do not be afraid, Daughter Zion; see, your king is coming, seated on a donkey's colt" (12:15; cf. Zechariah 9:9)

### What a Royal Procession Should Look Like

Historically, when kings returned from victory, their processions were elaborate: tributes, gifts, conquered peoples, slaves, soldiers, weapons. Think of Alexander the Great or a modern ticker-tape parade. The picture is of power, conquest, vindication.

The crowds arrive expecting exactly this—they've seen the signs (especially Lazarus's resurrection), and the anticipation is enormous. "The whole world has gone after him!" the Pharisees cry in frustration (12:19).

### Where Is the Army?

Yet something is conspicuously absent: **there is no army. There is no victory**. This procession has all the fanfare but none of the substance—all the trailer, no feature film. Great front page, hype and nothing more.

The disciples sense the confusion. "At first his disciples did not understand all this" (12:16). Only after Jesus was glorified did they realize these things had been written about him and done to him. The procession was real, but its meaning wasn't yet clear.

Why is the order wrong? Because a victorious procession only makes sense *after* the victory. And victory comes through death.

## 3. People Drawn In Before Being Lifted Up (12:17-22, 32-33)

The word spreads. The crowd that witnessed Lazarus's resurrection continues to testify about it. Many come to the festival specifically because of this sign. Even Greeks—foreigners, symbol of the world—are present. They approach Philip and Andrew (whose Greek names may have made them accessible): "Sir, we would like to see Jesus" (12:21).

### The Hour Arrives

Jesus's response is extraordinary:

> "The hour has come for the Son of Man to be glorified" (12:23)

Up until now, throughout John's Gospel, the refrain has been consistent: "My hour has not yet come" (John 2:4, 7:30, 8:20). But now it has. What triggers this announcement? **The Greeks coming to seek him.**

For the first time, people from outside Israel—representatives of "the whole world"—desire to see Jesus. This signals that the time has come. Not for coronation or conquest, but for glorification through death.

Jesus explains:

> "Unless a kernel of wheat falls to the ground and dies, it remains only a single seed. But if it dies, it produces many seeds" (12:24)

The seed must die before it can bear fruit. Only through death does multiplication occur.

And later:

> "And I, when I am lifted up from the earth, will draw all people to myself" (12:32)

**Here again is the wrong order**: the Greeks are already coming, people are already being drawn—yet Jesus hasn't been "lifted up" (crucified) yet. The drawing happens before the lifting, yet it only makes sense because the lifting is coming.

---

## The Right Order (12:24-26)

The seed parable reveals what must happen:

**Death first, then fruit.**

Before the hour of glory can come, the hour of death must precede it. Jesus explains his followers must walk this same path:

> "Whoever loves their life will lose it, while anyone who hates their life in this world will keep it for eternal life. Whoever serves me must follow me; and where I am, my servant also will be" (12:25-26)

Where Jesus goes, his servants follow. And Jesus goes to death. Therefore his followers must be willing to die too—to lose their lives in this world in order to gain eternal life.

### Applied to Believers

This means the Christian life also follows the right order: **death first, glory later**. We are tempted to seek glory now—in our studies, careers, ministries, achievements. But if we grasp for present glory, we miss the pattern Jesus sets.

Lazarus, raised from the dead, becomes a target for persecution. Those who follow the resurrected One also face death and opposition. Yet this is the way of life—the only way that produces fruit.

## Responses to the Hour (12:37-50)

As Jesus announces the hour and calls for commitment, John records three responses from the crowds:

### a. Seeing But Not Believing (12:37)

Despite performing so many signs, many still refuse to believe. This is a persistent pattern in John's Gospel: crowds see miracles and initially believe, but when Jesus speaks, they turn away.

In chapter 6, people believe after the feeding miracle, but when Jesus teaches about eating his flesh and drinking his blood, they abandon him. In chapter 8, would-be believers become so hostile they attempt to stone him.

Here too, crowds come because of Lazarus. But when Jesus begins speaking about death and being "lifted up," many cannot accept it. They've seen the sign; they cannot accept the message.

### b. Believing But Fearing (12:42-43)

A second group does believe—even some among the authorities. Yet they refuse to acknowledge their faith openly:

> "Nevertheless, many even of the authorities believed in him, but for fear of the Pharisees they did not confess it, lest they should be put out of the synagogue; for they loved the glory that comes from man more than the glory that comes from God" (12:42-43)

Their fear is real: expulsion from the synagogue meant social death, loss of community, shame in a culture where belonging is everything. And with it would come loss of position and privilege.

But John diagnoses the real issue: **they love human praise more than God's praise**. Following Jesus means accepting that others will judge you negatively. It means being willing to be excluded, called a party-pooper, seen as different. Yet many cannot bear this cost.

### c. Hearing and Believing (12:44-50)

The third response—the one Jesus calls us to embody—isn't actually witnessed in the crowds. Instead, Jesus describes it hypothetically:

> "Whoever believes in me, believes not in me but in him who sent me. And whoever sees me sees him who sent me" (12:44-45)

True believers are those who hear Jesus's words and keep them. They understand that his words carry the authority of God the Father. These words give eternal life. They don't need signs once they have the words.

Indeed, Jesus later tells Thomas: "Blessed are those who have not seen and yet believe" (John 20:29). A better state than requiring signs. Once you have the directions to your destination, you don't need to keep examining the sign itself—you follow where it points.

This is the paradigm for John's entire Gospel: belief rests on *hearing*, not *sight*. The words of God the Father, transmitted through the Son to the disciples, and written down in this Gospel for all who will read it—these are what call forth faith. The signs point to the words; the words are the true foundation.

---

## Application: Which Response Are You?

Are you looking for signs? Jesus performed many, and many still didn't believe. The Pharisees acknowledged the miracles but questioned his authority. Signs alone cannot compel faith.

Instead, hear his words. Read them regularly. Listen to what Jesus says about himself, about death, about the cost of following him. The words have been written for you. They will also judge you. On the last day, you cannot claim you weren't warned.

Are you fearful of what others will think? Of being excluded or mocked for your faith? Jesus offers comfort: "Do not be afraid. Your King is coming" (Zechariah 9:9). He offers forgiveness and acceptance to those who follow him—a glory that comes from God, not from men.

Are you willing to hear and believe? To trust his words above signs, above comfort, above the praise of others? This is the response Jesus seeks.

---

## The Final Passover

We approach no ordinary Passover, but the final Passover. The prophecies of the saving King will come true. What takes two years in chapters 1-12 will unfold in less than two weeks in chapters 13-21. Time slows down. Jesus withdraws from public places and spends intense, private time with his disciples. He has much to say and show them before the end.

The timing seems wrong: a burial before death, a victory procession without an army, the drawing of all nations before the cross. Yet all of it makes sense through one central event: **the death of Jesus**.

Through this final Passover, through this death and suffering, salvation opens for all—Jew or Greek, Asian or any nation. There is no distinction. You need not change who you are to believe in God. You need only hear his words and believe.

Don't be like the crowds of old who saw but didn't believe—and were condemned. Hear the words of God the Father. Believe. And follow Jesus through death to life.

The hour has come.
