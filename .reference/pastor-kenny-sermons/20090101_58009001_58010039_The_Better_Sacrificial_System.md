# Hebrews 9:1–10:39

**Title:** The Better Sacrificial System
**Date:** 25 January 2009
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction: Is the Church Obsolete?

We live in a world of obsolete systems. Think of the Commodore 64, the XT processor, the 286, 386, 486—all replaced by newer technology. Some today argue that the church itself is obsolete, nothing more than a vestige of tradition and outdated practice.

But before we accept that conclusion, we need to ask: What exactly are we doing when we gather as a church? Are we simply maintaining religious traditions? Or has Jesus transformed what worship should be?

To answer these questions, we must return to the book of Hebrews and understand what Jesus came to accomplish. Our practices today should be evaluated against His intentions for us. How did He want His people to meet together?

The book of Hebrews walks us through three essential movements:

1. **The Jewish practices** – the Levitical system, which was never designed to work on its own
2. **The Jesus system** – His complete fulfillment of the Levitical order
3. **Our practices today** – how we should respond to what Jesus has done

## Part 1: The Levitical System Never Worked

> By calling this covenant "new," he has made the first one obsolete; and what is obsolete and aging will soon disappear. (Hebrews 8:13)

### The Structure of the Tabernacle

The first covenant came with elaborate regulations for worship centered on an earthly sanctuary:

> Now the first covenant had regulations for worship and also an earthly sanctuary. A tabernacle was set up. In its first room were the lampstand, the table and the consecrated bread; this was called the Holy Place. Behind the second curtain was a room called the Most Holy Place, which had the golden altar of incense and the gold-covered ark of the covenant. This ark contained the gold jar of manna, Aaron's staff that had budded, and the stone tablets of the covenant. Above the ark were the cherubim of the Glory, overshadowing the atonement cover. (Hebrews 9:1–5)

### The Role of the Priest

A carefully regulated priesthood maintained this system:

> When everything had been arranged like this, the priests entered regularly into the outer room to carry on their ministry. But only the high priest entered the inner room, and that only once a year, and never without blood, which he offered for himself and for the sins the people had committed in ignorance. (Hebrews 9:6–7)

A complete sacrificial system developed—special places, special people, special offerings. The idea was clear: if you wanted to please God, you needed the temple, the priest, and the blood sacrifice.

### God's Purpose in the Old Testament

The Old Testament wasn't a random collection of strange rituals. Rather, God accommodated Himself to His people over two thousand years, gradually teaching us about Himself, about ourselves, and ultimately about Jesus. The entire sacrificial system pointed forward to something greater.

The Holy Spirit revealed that the way into the Most Holy Place had not yet been truly disclosed while the first tabernacle still stood. The system was an illustration for that time:

> This is an illustration for the present time, indicating that the gifts and sacrifices being offered were not able to clear the conscience of the worshiper. They are only a matter of food and drink and various ceremonial washings—external regulations applying until the time of the new order. (Hebrews 9:8–10)

### Why the System Never Worked

The Levitical system was always meant to be a prototype, a temporary measure—never designed to actually take away sin. Consider a toy car: it looks like the real thing, but you can never drive it on a road or get it registered. It serves only to give a child an idea of what a real car is. To give such a toy to an adult would be an insult—we all know it could never work.

The repeated nature of the sacrifices proved their ineffectiveness:

> The law is only a shadow of the good things that are coming—not the realities themselves. For this reason it can never, by the same sacrifices repeated endlessly year after year, make perfect those who draw near to worship. If it could, would they not have stopped being offered? For the worshipers would have been cleansed once for all, and would no longer have felt guilty for their sins. But those sacrifices are an annual reminder of sins, because it is impossible for the blood of bulls and goats to take away sins. (Hebrews 10:1–4)

Day after day, priests stood performing the same duties, offering the same sacrifices—sacrifices that could never take away sins. The very repetition demonstrated that the system couldn't accomplish what people hoped it would. It was only ever meant to point beyond itself to a better sacrifice, a better temple, a better Most Holy Place.

## Part 2: The Jesus System Works

When Christ came as high priest, everything changed:

> When Christ came as high priest of the good things that are already here, he went through the greater and more perfect tabernacle that is not man-made, that is to say, not a part of this creation. He did not enter by means of the blood of goats and calves; but he entered the Most Holy Place once for all by his own blood, having obtained eternal redemption. (Hebrews 9:11–12)

### Jesus Fulfills the Three Elements

The Levitical system required three things: a temple, a priest, and blood. Jesus embodies all three:

- **Temple:** Jesus's body became the true sanctuary. He is the Most Holy Place to which all the shadows pointed.
- **Priest:** He is our great high priest, not of the Levitical order but of the order of Melchizedek—eternal and unchanging.
- **Offering:** His own blood, offered once for all, perfectly and completely.

The contrast is staggering:

> How much more, then, will the blood of Christ, who through the eternal Spirit offered himself unblemished to God, cleanse our consciences from acts that lead to death, so that we may serve the living God! (Hebrews 9:14)

Where the old system could only provide external cleansing, Jesus cleanses our consciences—the deepest part of who we are. His work is so complete that we can serve God with confidence and joy.

> For this reason Christ is the mediator of a new covenant, that those who are called may receive the promised eternal inheritance—now that he has died as a ransom to set them free from the sins committed under the first covenant. (Hebrews 9:15)

### The Rending of the Curtain

When Jesus died, something remarkable happened:

> With a loud cry, Jesus breathed his last. The curtain of the temple was torn in two from top to bottom. And when the centurion, who stood there in front of Jesus, heard his cry and saw how he died, he said, "Surely this man was the Son of God!" (Mark 15:37–39)

The curtain that separated the Holy Place from the Most Holy Place—the barrier that only the high priest could pass once a year—was torn from top to bottom. Jesus's body, torn for us, opened the way. Access to God was no longer restricted. The old system was finished.

## Part 3: We Do Not Need a System—We Need Jesus

Here's where many modern churches have gone tragically wrong. We live in the age after Jesus, yet we often reconstruct the very system He completed. This dishonors His work and confuses our people.

### Early Christian Practice

Notice how the early Christians gathered:

> They devoted themselves to the apostles' teaching and to the fellowship, to the breaking of bread and to prayer. (Acts 2:42)

No temple. No priests offering sacrifices. No elaborate rituals. Just believers learning together, sharing life, breaking bread, and praying. Simple. Ordinary. Beautiful.

When the gospel first spread through the Roman Empire, pagans called Christians "atheists"—not because they rejected God, but because they had no temple, no idols, no priesthood, no offerings. What they saw instead was something revolutionary: people loving one another.

### How We've Lost Our Way

During the Medieval period, the church reimposed the Levitical system in disguised form. After Christians culturally dominated the Jews, the victors took on the practices of the vanquished. Partly from superstition, partly from forgetting what Jesus had accomplished, the church reintroduced temple worship:

- Churches became "temples"
- Tables became "altars"
- The Lord's Supper became a "sacrifice"
- Teachers became "priests"
- Donations became "offerings"
- Singing became "chants"

The Reformation addressed some of the theology, but unfortunately, many of these practices remain. Walk into many churches today, and they look more like temples than fellowship meetings.

### Our Modern Problem

Do we still carry temple-thinking into our gatherings? Consider our actual practices:

**Priests:** We often honor a clergy class as spiritual mediators, forgetting that Jesus made us all priests before God.

**Temples:** We treat church buildings as special religious spaces—places to dress formally, speak reverently, avoid eating. We "enter God's house" as if He dwells there specially.

**Sacrifices:** We speak of "offering" our worship, our gifts, our praise as if God needs them or is pleased by the gesture itself.

Even our worship songs sometimes perpetuate this confusion:

- "We enter your courts, Lord / With grace and thanksgiving / With hands lifted up"
- "I come to You in humble sacrifice / Hear my heart I worship"
- "Here I am to worship / Here I am to bow down / Here I am to say that You're my God"

While beautiful, this language can mislead. It sounds like we're meant to present ourselves as sacrifices, to bow before a distant God, to offer something that pleases Him. But that's the old system—and it's been made obsolete.

### What God Actually Wants

Listen to what Scripture says God desires:

> First he said, "Sacrifices and offerings, burnt offerings and sin offerings you did not desire, nor were you pleased with them" (although the law required them to be made). Then he said, "Here I am, I have come to do your will." He sets aside the first to establish the second. And by that will, we have been made holy through the sacrifice of the body of Jesus Christ once for all. (Hebrews 10:8–10)

God doesn't want our sacrifices. He never did. What He wants is obedience to His word, godliness, genuine fellowship with one another, and love.

This is exactly what Hebrews calls us to:

> Therefore, brothers, since we have confidence to enter the Most Holy Place by the blood of Jesus, by a new and living way opened for us through the curtain, that is, his body, and since we have a great priest over the house of God, let us draw near to God with a sincere heart in full assurance of faith, having our hearts sprinkled to cleanse us from a guilty conscience and having our bodies washed with pure water. Let us hold unswervingly to the hope we profess, for he who promised is faithful. And let us consider how we may spur one another on toward love and good deeds. Let us not give up meeting together, as some are in the habit of doing, but let us encourage one another—and all the more as you see the Day approaching. (Hebrews 10:19–25)

Four "let us" statements follow, each one precious:

**First—Faith in God (vv. 19–23):** Draw near to God with a sincere heart and full assurance. Never believe your sin is too great for His forgiveness. Never think it's too late to repent. Jesus's work is final and complete. You can approach God directly, confidently, knowing that His anger toward sin was satisfied at the cross.

**Second—Hold Fast to Hope (v. 23):** The one who promised is faithful. Cling to this hope as you wait for Christ's return.

**Third—Spur One Another On (v. 24):** Consider how to encourage each other toward love and good deeds. This is what matters—not religious performance, but genuine love in action.

**Fourth—Encourage One Another (v. 25):** Don't abandon gathering together. Meet to strengthen each other, to remind each other of the gospel, to help each other endure until the Day of Christ.

### The Bottom Line

Here's what counts: love for one another. Not offerings presented to God. Not tokens of devotion. Not religious acts meant to impress or appease God. But genuine love expressed toward each other.

When the New Testament uses temple language, it's often redirecting us toward this truth. If you want to offer something to God, offer to the poor. If you want to sacrifice, sacrifice for others. These are the sacrifices that please Him.

When we have an offering time in our gathering, give—but give for the right reasons. Give generously to support ministry, to free teachers for full-time work, to advance God's kingdom. But don't give thinking it will earn God's favor or make Him closer to you. Don't give out of fear that He'll be angry if you don't. 

God is not angry with those who trust in Jesus. His wrath was satisfied. We have confidence to draw near because of Christ's blood.

## Conclusion

The old ways are obsolete. The Levitical system is finished. We don't need temples, priests, or offerings anymore. We need Jesus—and He is sufficient.

Friendship with God doesn't come through religion or rituals. It comes through believing in Jesus, trusting His work, and following Him in obedience and love. When you gather with other believers, you're not entering a sacred space to perform religious duties. You're gathering with family to learn God's Word together, to encourage one another, and to love as Jesus loved.

As you begin this new year and consider joining various groups and ministries, remember: we gather to learn, to mature, and to encourage one another toward love and good deeds. That's all. That's everything. And it's beautifully, wonderfully sufficient.
