# Hebrews 4:1-11

**Title:** Jesus: The Better Rest

**Date:** 25 February 2018 (SMAC)

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Rest Ruined: How Work Became a Curse

> Therefore, since the promise of entering his rest still stands, let us be careful that none of you be found to have fallen short of it. For we also have had the good news proclaimed to us, just as they did; but the message they heard was of no value to them, because they did not share the faith of those who obeyed. Now we who have believed enter that rest, just as God has said, "So I declared on oath in my anger, 'They shall never enter my rest.'" (Hebrews 4:1-3)

God created us for rest. On the seventh day, He rested from all His works—not because He was tired, but because His creation was complete and good. We were meant to enjoy that rest with Him.

But Adam and Eve's disobedience interrupted everything. When they ate from the tree of the knowledge of good and evil, they didn't just break relationship with God—they fundamentally changed the nature of work itself. In Genesis 3, we see the consequences:

> "Cursed is the ground because of you; through painful toil you will eat food from it all the days of your life. It will produce thorns and thistles for you, and you will eat the plants of the field. By the sweat of your brow you will eat your food until you return to the ground." (Genesis 3:17-19)

Work existed before the Fall—Adam was called to tend the garden. But now work became *toil*. It became mental exhaustion, frustration, difficulty, pain. Work without completion. Work without reaching purpose. Work without rest.

This curse compounded across generations. By the time of the exodus, Israel had become physical slaves in Egypt—working 24/7/365 with no freedom, only slave drivers and endless labor. But God rescued them, giving them a chance to restart.

Yet even after their rescue, they disobeyed again. God promised them the land of final rest—the promised land flowing with milk and honey. But they were terrified by false reports and refused to enter. God's response echoed Eden: "They shall never enter my rest."

After forty years in the desert, the old generation died and their children finally entered the promised land. But even that was not true rest:

> For if Joshua had given them rest, God would not have spoken later about another day. (Hebrews 4:8)

Joshua led the conquest, but the work remained incomplete. They didn't fully drive out the inhabitants. Pagan idols remained scattered throughout the land, becoming a snare to future generations. Hundreds of years later, King David brought military rest from the Philistines, yet even David pointed beyond himself:

> God again set a certain day, calling it "Today." This he did when a long time later he spoke through David, as in the passage already quoted: "Today, if you hear his voice, do not harden your hearts." (Hebrews 4:7)

Despite his victories—conquering Jerusalem, defeating Goliath, extending Israel's borders—David still said "today." The rest was still to come. *There is no rest in this world.* No true cessation from toil, anxiety, or incompleteness.

## Entering the Rest: God's Finished Work

> And yet his works have been finished since the creation of the world. For somewhere he has spoken about the seventh day in these words: "On the seventh day God rested from all his works." (Hebrews 4:3b-4)

Here's what strikes us: God's work is already finished. We're living in the seventh day right now—the day that has no ending recorded in Genesis like the other days. The first day: "and it was good." Second day: little said. But the seventh day? It continues. *We are in the rest.*

This is why the Sabbath was so sacred to Israel:

> Remember the Sabbath day by keeping it holy. Six days you shall labor and do all your work, but the seventh day is a sabbath to the Lord your God. On it you shall not do any work, neither you, nor your son or daughter, nor your male or female servant, nor your animals, nor any foreigner residing in your towns. For in six days the Lord made the heavens and the earth, the sea, and all that is in them, but he rested on the seventh day. Therefore the Lord blessed the Sabbath day and made it holy. (Exodus 20:8-11)

The Sabbath wasn't just about ceasing from work—it was about remembering their rescue from slavery and looking forward to the final rest to come. One day in seven, everyone stopped: family members, servants, even animals. Everything ceased because everyone ceased. The tools of work were put down. The machines stopped. For one day, they were reminded they were not beasts of burden, not slaves, but people created for something higher.

And in multiples of seven, Israel rested more deeply. Every fiftieth year—seven times seven—came the Jubilee, when the entire national system reset. Debts were forgiven, loans released, contracts voided. Everything returned to its original purpose. This was a foretaste of the true rest to come.

All of this pointed to Jesus:

> "Come to me, all you who are weary and burdened, and I will give you rest. Take my yoke upon you and learn from me, for I am gentle and humble in heart, and you will find rest for your souls. For my yoke is easy and my burden is light." (Matthew 11:28-30)

Jesus is Lord of the Sabbath. When He sat down at the right hand of God after offering Himself as the final sacrifice, He announced what the psalmist declared centuries before: *It is finished.*

> When he had received the drink, Jesus said, "It is finished." With that, he bowed his head and gave up his spirit. (John 19:30)

The work of salvation is complete. We rest not because we've earned it, but because Christ has done all the work. We've entered the rest through faith in Him.

## What About Sunday? Entering the Rest Today

> There remains, then, a Sabbath-rest for the people of God; for anyone who enters God's rest also rests from their works, just as God did from his. Let us, therefore, make every effort to enter that rest, so that no one will perish by following their example of disobedience. (Hebrews 4:9-11)

The question naturally arises: *Should we still observe the Sabbath?* There are thoughtful believers on different sides of this question. Some believe we must keep Saturday as the literal seventh day, pointing to Scripture's continuous affirmation of the Sabbath throughout history. Others believe that in Christ, every day has become a Sabbath—the distinction between days is no longer binding.

The early Church gradually shifted to Sunday, the Lord's Day. As the early Christians saw it:

- **Ignatius of Antioch** (110 A.D.): "Those who were brought up in the ancient order of things have come to the possession of a new hope, no longer observing the Sabbath, but living in the observance of the Lord's day, on which also our life has sprung up again by him."

- **Justin Martyr** (155 A.D.): "Sunday is the day on which we all hold our common assembly, because it is the first day on which God made the world; and Jesus Christ our Savior on the same day rose from the dead."

- **Athanasius** (345 A.D.): "The Sabbath was the end of the first creation, the Lord's day was the beginning of the second. The Sabbath was a memorial of the first things; the Lord's day is a memorial of the new creation."

I don't have a definitive answer, only perhaps a compromise. There is a special day—and that day is Sunday, our corporate gathering. But in spirit, every day is a Sabbath for the believer who rests in Christ's finished work.

The Sabbath is not primarily an individual activity; it's communal. You cannot truly keep the Sabbath alone on a beach. The seventh-day rest will not be *me* alone, but *us* together. This is why the commandment included not just the individual but family, servants, animals, and foreigners—everyone stops together.

Consider Christmas: it's one of the rare days when society collectively stops. Not because the government forces it, but because we want everyone to rest together. Families gather. Work ceases. This reflects what the Sabbath was always meant to be.

Thirty years ago in Australia, shops closed on Sundays. Universities had common lunch hours (1-2 pm) so people could gather. But economic pressure changed that. We couldn't waste an hour of collective non-productivity. We lost our shared time together.

COVID accelerated this loss further. We're left only with God's law—designed to remind us we're not beasts of burden or slaves, but people made for something greater. Rest to come.

### Practically, Here's What This Means

**We gather on Sunday.** It's not arbitrary—it's rooted in apostolic practice and the significance of Christ's resurrection. Whether we also honor Saturday is a matter of conscience, but Sunday is our corporate day of rest.

On that day:
- We look at our watches and phones less often
- We gather with brothers and sisters—not because we're paid to, not because we're productive, not to extract value—but simply because we belong to each other
- We remind each other that the 1/7 rest we taste now points to the 7/7 rest to come
- We do work that lasts not just a business cycle, but eternity
- We encourage one another not to follow the path of disobedience

**But it takes effort.** "Make every effort to enter that rest" means being diligent about maintaining this habit. Work gets hard. Kids become resistant. Life gets disorganized. We get tempted to skip for vacations or busyness. But "remember the Sabbath."

### The Danger: Working for Work's Sake

The reason this matters is not legalism—it's the soul's health. There's a tragedy happening in our world: people working for the sake of working, as though that's all there is. In Japan, this reaches the level of *karoshi*—death by overwork. People stay at work with no goal except that everyone else is staying, so they stay too.

But Japan needs what we need: the fourth commandment. A reminder that *there is something else we work for.* Rest.

You say: "But I've already entered that rest through Christ. Why do I still feel so exhausted?"

Perhaps because you don't know how to rest well. You plan an expensive holiday overseas and push yourself to see everything, experience everything. You come back more exhausted than when you left. Then you spend months paying off the credit card debt, working harder than before.

The fisherman story captures this perfectly: A skilled fisherman is asked by a venture capitalist why he doesn't expand his business. "Double your staff," the capitalist urges. "Build a factory. Process and can the fish. Export globally. Work night and day. At retirement, float the company, sell shares, make a million dollars."

"Why?" asks the fisherman.

"So you'll have the freedom to do what you want," says the capitalist. "Work mornings, then go back to enjoy your family and support your church."

The fisherman already has that. He's working toward something the capitalist is trying to sell him fifty years from now.

There's nothing wrong with working hard. But don't lose sight of your true goal. Working for work's sake is like alcoholism—drinking not for fellowship or celebration, but for drinking itself. Similarly, we work not for the sake of work or money or status, but for the sake of providing, creating, serving, and ultimately for the rest that gives work meaning.

## The Paradox of Rest

Here's the tension we live in: You've entered the rest through faith in Christ. Yet the world around you—and the old nature within you—resists this rest at every turn. Rest interrupted by thunderstorms. Rest interrupted by earthquakes. Rest interrupted by life.

Even the Sabbath in Israel was interrupted by the curse that continued to press down. Work is still toil. Thorns still grow. Sweat still flows.

But we have something they didn't fully have: we know the rest is secured. Jesus finished it. Our role now is to *believe* it, to *enter into it daily*, and to taste it each week when we gather—when we stop working, stop striving, stop climbing, and simply remember: *It is finished.*

---

**Summary of Key Points:**

- Adam's disobedience turned work into toil and rest into a distant promise
- Throughout history—Eden, Egypt, the promised land, even David's kingdom—true rest remained elusive
- God's work was finished at creation; we live in the eternal seventh day
- The Sabbath pointed forward to Christ, who completed the final work of salvation
- Sunday is our weekly gathering to taste the rest to come and remind each other of our true identity
- The challenge: maintaining this rhythm against cultural pressures and our own resistance
- The promise: we've already entered the rest. Now we must live into it, believe it, and gather weekly to reinforce it
