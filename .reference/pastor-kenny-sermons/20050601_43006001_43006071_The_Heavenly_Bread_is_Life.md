# John 6:1-71

**Title:** The Heavenly Bread is Life  
**Date:** 12 June 2005  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction: Why Bread Matters

I'll be honest—I'm not naturally a bread person. Give me a taro bun or chocolate danish, and sure, I'll eat it, but really I'm eating the filling, not the bread. Back in high school, I packed bread sandwiches day after day until I was thoroughly sick of them. Either too soggy or too crusty. A friend who arrived fresh from overseas would bring a thermos of rice and vegetables instead. Now that made sense to me.

But here's the thing: in biblical times, bread wasn't optional. In Palestine, bread was *the* principal food. The word "bread" and the word "food" were essentially the same thing. Breaking bread meant eating a meal. For Jewish people, rice matters to us Asians the way bread mattered to them—there's even a Chinese proverb suggesting rice is more valuable than jade. When times get hard, rice sustains you. Bread did the same for Jews.

This context matters enormously for understanding John 6, because we're reading about the Passover—the most significant annual feast in the Jewish calendar, commemorating God's deliverance from Egypt. The Passover was held around sundown over a meal, and bread featured prominently because the Israelites, escaping Egypt in haste, had carried unleavened dough on their journey. For a Jewish person, the Passover was what Christmas is to us—a major annual observance centered on food and remembrance.

## Part 1: Jesus Has Bread—So What Do the Crowds Want?

### The Problem: Where Are We to Buy Bread? (vv. 5-24)

Jesus has gathered a massive crowd. His reputation as a healer and miracle-worker has spread, and thousands have come seeking him. It's the Passover season—perfect timing for a grand Jewish feast. But there's a problem.

> Where are we to buy bread? (v. 5)

It's a question that reveals multiple crises:

**An administrative crisis:** There were no bakeries or fast-food outlets in the first century. Even if there had been, nothing would be open during the festival—try finding a shop open on Christmas Eve.

**A logistical crisis:** Five thousand people need feeding. Modern shopping centers would struggle with that volume.

**A financial crisis:** Philip calculates it would cost 200 denarii—roughly 200 days' wages, or about $30,000 to feed everyone at $5 per person.

**A religious crisis:** This wasn't just hunger. The Passover meal was commanded by God himself—a core obligation of Jewish identity. Failing to provide it was a failure to fulfill the law.

Yet Jesus, knowing exactly what he would do, provides a solution. From five barley loaves and two fish, he miraculously feeds the crowd. Everyone eats as much as they want. There are even leftovers—twelve baskets full. It's staggering. Food appears from nowhere to satisfy five thousand people completely, with abundance remaining.

### The Crowds' First Response: Make Him King (vv. 25-27)

Immediately after this miracle, the crowds want to make Jesus king by force. He escapes during the night, but they're undeterred. By the next morning, they've found him again, still determined to crown him.

> "Truly, truly, I say to you, you are seeking me, not because you saw signs, but because you ate your fill of the loaves. Do not labor for the food that perishes, but for the food that endures to eternal life, which the Son of Man will give to you." (vv. 26-27)

Their reasoning makes sense on the surface. Jesus is clearly resourceful—he fed five thousand. He's generous—an all-you-can-eat abundance. He's a provider. Why wouldn't they want him as king?

But Jesus cuts to the heart of their motivation: they're following him for bread, not for belief. They want a political king who will solve their material problems. In a time without social welfare, Jesus could provide food. In an age of scarcity, he offers abundance. In a time without medical insurance, he heals the sick. From a purely practical standpoint, their desire to make him king is understandable.

Yet Jesus says no. He's not here to be an earthly political king. He's not here with a social agenda. He's not here to eliminate hunger and sickness. He's not an earthly provider at all. He's here to establish an eternal kingdom and offer eternal food—sustenance that lasts forever.

### The Crowds' Second Response: Tell Us What Works to Accomplish (vv. 28-29)

The crowds are still confused. They conclude Jesus must want them to do something to earn this bread.

> "What must we do, to be doing the works of God?" (v. 28)

Perhaps they should pray more? Visit the temple more frequently? Offer more sacrifices? Keep following him everywhere in Palestine? Fight the Romans on his behalf?

But again, Jesus redirects:

> "This is the work of God, that you believe in him whom he has sent." (v. 29)

Jesus isn't talking about physical labor. You can't work your way to eternal life—the effort would be infinite. He's speaking about a different kind of response entirely: belief. The real work is trust. This echoes what Jesus told Nicodemus in chapter 3—you must be born of the Spirit, not the flesh. You must be spiritually born.

The path to eternal life isn't through works or material effort. It's through believing in the one whom God has sent.

### The Crowds' Third Response: Show Us a Sign (vv. 30-33)

The crowds still don't understand. They want Jesus to prove himself through signs.

> "Our fathers ate the manna in the wilderness; as it is written, 'He gave them bread from heaven to eat.'" (v. 31)

They're invoking Moses, the greatest prophet in their tradition. Moses brought them out of Egypt and miraculously provided bread from heaven in the desert. If Jesus is claiming to be greater, shouldn't he match Moses' credentials? What sign will he perform?

Jesus responds:

> "Truly, truly, I say to you, it was not Moses who gave you the bread from heaven, but my Father gives you the true bread from heaven. For the bread of God is he who comes down from heaven and gives life to the world." (vv. 32-33)

They've already seen a sign—they have full stomachs. But they want more confirmation, more concrete evidence, more spectacular miracles. Jesus, though, isn't an entertainer pandering to crowd demands. He won't continuously perform signs to win belief. The material realm isn't where confirmation should come from—it's his words that should be enough. Jesus himself should be enough.

The bread they ate was real but temporary. The true bread from heaven—Jesus himself—is eternal. Moses gave manna that fed people for a day. The Father offers something immeasurably better. Moses parted the sea; Jesus walks on it. Forget signs. If you want real bread, real life, he's here to give it.

### The Crowds' Fourth Response: Give Us This Bread Always (vv. 34-40)

Intrigued by his promise, they ask:

> "Sir, give us this bread always." (v. 34)

Here's the problem: they're still thinking with their stomachs, not their minds. They want daily bread the way manna was provided—perpetual security, never going hungry. They long for the comfort of guaranteed food, just as their ancestors had it.

But Jesus makes his declaration:

> "I am the bread of life; whoever comes to me shall not hunger, and whoever believes in me shall never thirst." (v. 35)

The crowds are stuck in material thinking when Jesus speaks of heavenly realities. He's not offering an earthly kingdom or earthly bread or earthly work. He's offering himself—the one from heaven. To use an Asian frame: "I am the Rice of Life."

If you believe in him, if you trust him completely, you will never be spiritually hungry or spiritually thirsty. This bread—Jesus himself—never decays like manna did. Unlike Moses, who gave daily bread only to see his people eventually die, Jesus offers a solution to hunger, thirst, and death itself. He offers eternal life.

## The Turning Point: They Don't Understand Because Jesus Is from Heaven (v. 38)

Here's the fundamental issue: the crowds don't understand because they haven't grasped that Jesus is from heaven. He's not speaking about earthly matters. He's speaking about eternal consequences, heavenly realities, spiritual life.

### The Crowds' Good Intentions Gone Wrong

Their intentions seem admirable on the surface:
1. They want Jesus to be king
2. They want to do the works of God
3. They want to believe Jesus
4. They want security forever

But these good intentions have all gone sideways because their motivations are earthly. They come to Jesus with fixed expectations of what a king should be. They want him to solve their daily problems, meet their physical needs, secure their immediate comfort. Their question is always: "What about today? It's lunchtime. How can I get more food?"

They've found the wrong guy for what they're looking for.

Jesus challenges their perceptions entirely. When they want a political king, he talks about a heavenly kingdom. When they ask for signs and works, he talks about belief. When they worry about daily bread, he talks about eternal life. Jesus and the crowds are operating on completely different wavelengths.

**To put it simply:** The crowds are reading a telephone directory when Jesus is teaching them a road map. They're asking about numbers; he's talking about directions.

## Part 2: What It Means That Jesus Is from Heaven

### His Heavenly Access (vv. 41-51)

The Jews now understand Jesus' claim clearly: he says he came down from heaven. But they reject it outright.

> "Is not this Jesus, the son of Joseph, whose father and mother we know? How does he now say, 'I have come down from heaven'?" (v. 42)

They've watched him grow up in his father's carpentry shop. He's a local boy. How can he possibly be from heaven?

But Jesus insists:

> "I am the living bread that came down from heaven. If anyone eats of this bread, he will live forever. And the bread that I will give for the life of the world is my flesh." (v. 51)

Jesus claims he's the only one with direct heavenly access. He's not mediated through priests or prophets. He's not like Moses, who never actually saw God's face. Jesus came directly from the Father's presence. He has always been there, from before time began.

When Jesus quotes Isaiah 54:13—"They will all be taught by God"—he's claiming to be the fulfillment of that prophecy. No longer do you need intermediaries. No longer must you go through various channels. Here is God himself, on earth, the one who intimately knows the Father completely, who does only what the Father commands.

**An illustration:** Imagine being on a train, starving for a taro bun. You get off at Eastwood and run to the bakery. But who's really responsible for satisfying your hunger? Not the shop owner, not the baker, not the sales assistant. It's the bun itself. Jesus claims to be the food. He is not merely a provider of life—he is life itself. He is the direct access to life. Life only comes through relationship with him.

### He Abides in a Heavenly Way (vv. 52-59)

The language shifts from bread to flesh:

> "Unless you eat the flesh of the Son of Man and drink his blood, you have no life in you." (v. 53)

The Jews are horrified. Cannibalism? What is he saying?

But Jesus is speaking about heavenly realities, not physical consumption. The language transitions from bread (material, temporary) to flesh (a hint of his coming suffering and death). His flesh will be offered, torn, killed to pay for another's sin. Through his flesh he makes his abiding possible within us.

Notice the absoluteness: "Unless you eat Jesus' flesh and drink his blood, you have no life." Unless you have him within you, constituting your very being, you have no life. The phrase goes, "You are what you eat." Believing in Jesus, belonging to Jesus, means consuming him—that flesh from the cross, bloodied, scarred, torn, left in the sun, sweating, seeming utterly repulsive. Yet we eat it.

But this isn't literal cannibalism. Look at verse 63:

> "It is the Spirit who gives life; the flesh is of no avail. The words that I have spoken to you are spirit and life." (v. 63)

It's not physical eating—you can't ingest grace like a meal. Eating is metaphorical language for relationship. We use this language all the time: "You're so cute I just want to gobble you up." Or "You look good enough to eat" (grandmother language). Love songs speak of starving for someone's love, hungering for their touch. 

Eating is the language of abiding together, of intimacy and relationship. This is what Jesus means: accept him. Accept the flesh he has sacrificed for your sake. Eating his flesh means loving and trusting him as he loves us. It means him remaining intimately within us. Eating his flesh means believing him. Believe, and you have eaten.

### He Speaks Heavenly Words (vv. 60-65)

His disciples are shocked and offended. This is a hard saying. Who can listen to it?

> "This is a hard saying; who can listen to it?" (v. 60)

They're not offended by thoughts of cannibalism. Jesus responds by asking: "What if you were to see the Son of Man ascending to where he was before?" He's saying: Would you believe if you saw me go to heaven?

Their real offense is that **he speaks from heaven**. He claims to be from heaven. He claims direct access to the Father. He claims to be the *only* way to relationship with the Father. He speaks of heavenly things, not earthly matters.

But the Jewish rejection of heavenly words runs deep historically. As John 1:11 says, "He came to his own, and his own did not receive him." Ultimately, they cannot accept that Jesus is life itself. It's his words that offend—his claim that he alone is the source of eternal life.

This is why, in verse 66, many of his disciples turn back. They can no longer walk with him. It's too hard to believe in something you can't see. No signs, no daily food. Believing in something you can't physically experience like food. The disciples who followed him because of his miracles now find he offers something very different. Life with him will cost them everything they thought they wanted.

## Application: How Do We Eat His Flesh Today?

Jesus is not physically present on earth anymore. As he always said, he is from heaven and has returned there. But what remains are his words.

**So eat his words.** Devour the Bible to understand who he is. Digest his words. Ruminate on his wisdom. Drink up his truth. Understand it until it becomes woven into the very fabric of who you are. If Jesus were here today, he'd be saying: Be genetically modified. Have me in your DNA.

### Practical Ways to Consume His Word This Week

- Print a copy of Scripture and leave it where you'll see it—on the toilet, by your bed
- Use church and Bible study as accountability; prepare by pre-reading passages
- Attend Bible talks in the city, at university
- Call Christian friends and discuss Scripture together
- Find whatever rhythms work for you, but the core idea remains: get into Jesus through his words

## Conclusion: Where Are We to Buy Bread? (vv. 66-71)

Many disciples turn back and no longer follow Jesus. The Twelve remain.

> "Do you want to go away as well?" Jesus asks them.
>
> Simon Peter answered, "Lord, to whom shall we go? You have the words of eternal life, and we have believed and have come to know that you are the Holy One of God." (vv. 67-69)

Where are we to buy bread? Where do we go to find life?

Jesus has his own answer—one he's been giving all along in verse 6 and throughout this chapter. His agenda is utterly different from the crowds'. It's different from our petty earthly concerns. And he presses this not because he's hard-hearted, but because of his profound love for us. While we think about the next meal, the next house, the next fifty years, he is thinking of the eternal good. He is thinking of eternal life.

You don't have to buy bread. Jesus is himself the bread of life. Or the rice of life. Or the taro bun of life—whatever sustenance you cannot live without. He is life itself.

So eat him by believing in his words. Consume his truth. Make him part of your very being. That is where real bread is found.
