# John 21:1-19

**Title:** Regressing and Progressing
**Date:** 28 April 2019
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction

Throughout John's Gospel, we've witnessed a story of progress—from one man gathering followers, to increasingly greater miracles, culminating in the greatest miracle of all: the resurrection of Jesus. But Chapter 21 presents something unexpected: what seems at first like a step backward, an anticlimax. Some scholars dismiss it as an epilogue, an unnecessary appendix. Yet this chapter holds profound lessons about the human struggle between regression and progression in our faith.

Regression happens to all of us. Like children who regress during stress, like pets who forget their training, like athletes trying to recapture their youth—we too can slip backward. Peter and the disciples experience this vividly in this chapter.

## 1. Recollection (verses 1-2)

The chapter opens by gathering the key disciples back together by the Sea of Galilee (also called the Sea of Tiberias). We see Simon Peter, Thomas, Nathanael from Cana, the sons of Zebedee, and two other unnamed disciples. The recall of these names carries us back to Chapter 1, where these disciples first encountered Jesus. This deliberate recollection frames what follows as a moment of remembering—a return to the beginning.

## 2. Regression (verses 3-5)

### The Forgotten Calling

Peter announces, "I'm going out to fish," and the others immediately join him. This is deeply significant. These men were called to be fishers of men—Jesus specifically told them in Matthew 4:19, "Come, follow me, and I will send you out to fish for people." Yet here they are, returning to fishing for literal fish.

That night, they catch nothing.

### Signs of Spiritual Decline

Multiple indicators show how far they've regressed:

- **They don't recognize Jesus.** When He stands on the shore in the early morning, they fail to understand who He is.

- **Jesus addresses them as "children,"** not as brothers or disciples. They've reverted to spiritual immaturity—gone back to being infants in their faith.

- **The fishing failure reflects their emptiness.** Without Jesus, their efforts yield nothing.

### Connection to Earlier Betrayal

There's a poignant echo here of Chapter 18. Both scenes occur early in the morning. Both feature a charcoal fire. In Chapter 18, Peter stood warming himself by such a fire while denying Jesus three times. Now, in Chapter 21, Jesus prepares another charcoal fire—a chance at restoration.

Peter's regression isn't mere laziness or forgetfulness. It's rooted in the trauma of his betrayal. The shame of denying Jesus may have driven him back to what felt safe: his old trade, his old identity.

### Why Do We Regress?

This is a crucial question for all believers. Perhaps the call felt too hard. Perhaps service in Jesus's name brought hurt rather than joy. Perhaps after the resurrection's shock and the confusion of the forty days, Peter simply wanted to return to a simpler life he understood.

We recognize this temptation in ourselves. We leave bad relationships and find Jesus, swearing we'll never repeat those mistakes—then we drift back into similar patterns, telling ourselves "this time is different." We escape meaningless work to find purpose in Jesus, only to feel the old itch pulling us back into the very chaos that once burned us out. We find true fellowship in a good church, then gradually disappear, forgetting why we were ever grateful.

**Jesus asks us, as He asked Peter: "Do you love me more than these?"**

## 3. Remembering Jesus (verses 6-14)

### The Divine Intervention

But the disciples listen when Jesus instructs them: "Throw your net on the right side of the boat and you will find some." A difference of mere meters on the water—yet it makes all the difference. They haul in 153 large fish, unable even to drag the net for its weight.

This detail connects powerfully to John 6, the feeding of the five thousand. There Jesus multiplied five loaves and two fish. The disciples watched Him take what was insufficient and make it abundant. Here, the abundant catch reminds them: **Jesus is the one who provides.** Not their effort, but His grace.

### Remember Who Feeds You

When they reach shore, they find Jesus has already prepared a breakfast of fish and bread. He doesn't need their catch—He's already provided. The invitation is simple: "Come and have breakfast."

This parallels Chapter 6, where Peter confessed, "Lord, to whom shall we go? You have the words of eternal life." Yet somehow Peter had forgotten this confession. He'd forgotten that Jesus alone is the bread of life, the sustenance for both body and soul.

### The Restoration Meal

The scene echoes the Last Supper of Chapter 13, where Jesus washed the disciples' feet in an act of profound humility and service. There, He removed His outer garment and wrapped a towel around His waist. Here, Peter wraps his outer garment around himself—but not with a towel. There's a stark contrast: Jesus strips Himself to serve; Peter clothes Himself perhaps out of shame.

Yet the charcoal fire, the fish, the bread—all these elements are designed to awaken Peter's memory. This isn't just breakfast. It's an invitation to remember who Jesus is and what He offers.

## 4. Recommitment (verses 15-18)

### Three-Fold Restoration

After they've eaten, Jesus turns to Peter with a penetrating question. Notably, He calls him "Simon son of John"—not "Peter the rock." He's addressing Peter's original identity, perhaps inviting him to consider who he was meant to become.

**"Do you love me more than these?"**

The question is devastatingly simple. And it's asked three times.

Three times Peter denied Jesus. Three times Jesus asks about love. This isn't cruelty; it's grace. Each denial finds its answer in a fresh affirmation:

- **First exchange:** "Feed my lambs"
- **Second exchange:** "Take care of my sheep"  
- **Third exchange:** "Feed my sheep"

The repetition and variation matter. Lambs speak of the young and vulnerable. Sheep encompass the whole flock. Peter isn't restored to greatness or authority—he's restored to service. His calling hasn't changed; his heart is being recalibrated to remember it.

### The Cost of Following

Then Jesus tells Peter something that would have pierced his heart:

> "When you were younger you dressed yourself and went where you wanted; but when you are old you will stretch out your hands, and someone else will dress you and lead you where you do not want to go."

Jesus is foretelling Peter's martyrdom. Yet even with this knowledge, He ends with the same call He gave at the beginning: **"Follow me."**

And Peter does. History tells us he eventually dies for his faith, even requesting crucifixion upside down so as not to be compared to Christ. But the Gospel leaves this moment open—unanswered. We don't see Peter's response because the real question is meant for us.

### Don't Pass the Buck

One final temptation appears: Peter sees John and asks, "Lord, what about him?" Someone else can carry the burden. Someone else is more suited. But Jesus's response is sharp: "What is that to you? You must follow me."

Each of us must answer for ourselves. We can't outsource our faithfulness.

## Application: Progress Through Remembrance

We backslide. We grow lazy. We forget. We get distracted. We lose motivation.

But regression need not be permanent. The path forward runs backward—back to first principles, back to our initial confession, back to the cross and resurrection, back to the Gospel itself.

When doubt creeps in, return to Christ. When you've wandered from fellowship, come back—on His terms, not yours. When service has become burdensome, remember that Jesus provides, that He invites you to His table, that He prepares what you need.

Most importantly: **Remember your first love.**

The solution isn't striving harder in your own strength. It's remembering who feeds you. It's allowing Jesus to restore your heart through His grace. It's answering His question honestly: Do you love me?

And then, in response to that love, to feed His sheep—however that calling looks in your life. Through Sunday school, through volunteer service, through reading Scripture to your children, through recognizing a world filled with spiritual hunger and asking how you might serve.

Jesus extends the same call to each of us that He gave to Peter at the beginning and again at the end:

**"Follow me."**

Will you?
