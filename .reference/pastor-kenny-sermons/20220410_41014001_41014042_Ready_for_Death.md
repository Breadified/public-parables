# Mark 14:1-42

**Title:** Ready for Death

**Date:** April 10, 2022 (ARISE)

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Opening Reflection

As we approach Good Friday, we prepare ourselves for Jesus' death by examining what preparation means. I'm attending my second funeral in recent weeks, and I make it my practice to always say yes to such invitations. Funerals represent a unique pastoral opportunity—people are at their saddest and most open to spiritual conversation. Unlike weddings or celebrations, funerals require effort, often falling on weekdays. Yet they open doors for deeper discussions about death and what lies beyond. Are you prepared for death? That's the question guiding us through Mark 14.

---

## 1. Preparing Jesus' Body (14:1-9)

### Setting the Scene

We're in Bethany, at the home of Simon the Leper (presumably healed, though he retains the name). During a dinner, a woman enters with an alabaster jar—a sealed stone vessel—containing pure nard, extremely expensive perfume.

The word "alabaster" refers to a stone vessel sealed with wax, designed to preserve precious contents. The perfume itself is nard, mentioned in Song of Songs 1:12-14. Scholars debate its exact nature: some identify it as oils from the Himalayas, others as lavender from Naarda in Syria. Either way, it was exceedingly valuable.

### The Breaking and the Cost

In a dramatic gesture, the woman breaks the jar—much like breaking the neck of a champagne bottle. Once broken, it cannot be resealed. All the perfume flows out onto Jesus' head.

Those present react with indignation: "Why this waste? This perfume could have been sold for more than a year's wages—300 denarii—and the money given to the poor!" Their objection sounds reasonable. Perfume seems like mere aesthetics compared to helping the poor. By their logic, spending money on luxuries rather than necessities is wasteful extravagance.

### Jesus' Defense

But Jesus rebukes them sharply. "Leave her alone. She has done a beautiful thing to me. The poor you will always have with you, and you can help them any time you want. But you will not always have me. She did what she could. She poured perfume on my body beforehand to prepare for my burial."

This is no waste. The woman has intuitively grasped what the disciples have missed: Jesus is about to die. Her act is one of profound love and prophetic insight—she anoints him for burial.

### Jewish Burial Customs

Understanding this moment requires knowing how Jews approached death. They did not practice Egyptian-style mummification. Instead, bodies were placed in tombs where they decomposed. After time had passed, families would return, collect the bones, and place them in ossuaries—small jars stored in niches beneath the benches, making room for additional burials.

The Jews weren't concerned with preserving bodies through embalming because they believed in resurrection. As Ezekiel taught, God would bring life from dry bones (Ezekiel 37:5). The body's physical state mattered far less than faith in God's power to restore life.

Yet perfume served a practical purpose: it masked the smell of decomposition, allowing mourners to spend extended time with their dead. In Middle Eastern heat, living bodies are malodorous enough; decomposing bodies are unbearable. Perfume made prolonged grieving possible.

When Mary Magdalene and other women visited Jesus' tomb on Easter morning, what did they bring? Spices—for the same purpose. They came to spend hours mourning, and they needed the perfume to manage the odor. But their intention was cut short by resurrection.

### The Deeper Meaning

Jesus recognizes what this woman has done: she has prepared his body for burial. The expensive nard—myrrh—carries symbolic weight. Myrrh is associated with death, with funeral rites. She is telling him, wordlessly but unmistakably: "I know you're going to die. I'm mourning you now, while you're still here."

Jesus promises that her act will be remembered forever: "Truly I tell you, wherever the gospel is preached throughout the world, what she has done will also be told, in memory of her." She has given all she could, like the widow who gave her last coins to the temple treasury. This is sacrificial love made visible.

## 2. The Passover Preparation (14:12-26)

### The Commemoration Begins

Meanwhile, another preparation unfolds. On the first day of the Festival of Unleavened Bread—when the Passover lamb was customarily sacrificed—the disciples ask Jesus where he wants them to prepare the Passover meal.

This isn't casual dining; it's remembrance of the exodus. In Exodus 12, God commanded Moses to instruct each household to select a lamb on the tenth day of the month. For four days they would care for it, then on the fourteenth day, at twilight, they would slaughter it ceremonially. The blood would be smeared on doorframes, and the roasted lamb would be eaten with bitter herbs and unleavened bread. God would pass through Egypt that night, striking down the firstborn of every family—unless the blood of the lamb marked their door. Then God would "pass over" them, sparing them from judgment.

For nearly fifteen hundred years, Jews had commemorated this act of deliverance every spring.

### The Mysterious Preparation

Jesus sends two disciples ahead, giving them cryptic instructions: "Go into the city, and a man carrying a jar of water will meet you. Follow him. Say to the owner of the house he enters, 'The Teacher asks: Where is my guest room, where I may eat the Passover with my disciples?' He will show you a large room upstairs, furnished and ready."

Everything happens exactly as Jesus predicted. The disciples find the man, locate the room, and prepare the meal.

### What's Missing?

But here lies something strange: where is the lamb? The Passover requires a sacrificial lamb, yet we see no mention of one being slaughtered or prepared. What happened to the central symbol of the feast?

The answer is profound: Jesus himself is the Passover lamb. He no longer needs to be procured. As the text unfolds, the traditional elements are transformed.

### The New Covenant

> "While they were eating, Jesus took bread, and when he had given thanks, he broke it and gave it to his disciples, saying, 'Take it; this is my body.' Then he took a cup, and when he had given thanks, he gave it to them, and they all drank from it. 'This is my blood of the covenant, which is poured out for many,' he said to them. 'Truly I tell you, I will not drink again from the fruit of the vine until that day when I drink it new in the kingdom of God.'"

The lamb is replaced by the cup—by blood. This is the blood of the covenant, spoken of in Zechariah 9:11. Jesus is reestablishing the covenant between God and his people, not through animal sacrifice but through his own shed blood.

When he says, "I will not drink again from the fruit of the vine until that day when I drink it new in the kingdom of God," there's an ominous edge. The meal ends, they sing a hymn, and they depart to the Mount of Olives—toward arrest, trial, and execution.

We no longer celebrate Passover as the Jews did. Instead, we commemorate Christ through communion—holy communion, the Lord's Supper—remembering his sacrifice. Though we haven't held formal communion for a while due to COVID, we can treat every shared meal as a memorial, especially our church lunches. Every time we eat together, we're participating in the Lord's Supper.

## 3. Preparing the Disciples (14:17-31)

### The Betrayal Foretold

During the meal, Jesus drops a bombshell: "Truly I tell you, one of you will betray me—one who is eating with me."

The disciples are devastated. "Surely you don't mean me?" they ask, one by one. In Greek, their protests are emphatic: "Not I! (Neti ego!)" They're wounded, indignant, insisting on their loyalty. Yet Jesus is direct: the betrayer is one of the Twelve, one who dips bread with him in the bowl. He quotes Psalm 41:9: "Even my close friend, someone I trusted, one who shared my bread, has turned against me."

### Peter's Boast

Jesus delivers a hard truth: "You will all fall away... I will strike the shepherd, and the sheep will be scattered" (quoting Zechariah 13:7). But then: "After I have risen, I will go ahead of you into Galilee."

Peter, confident and defensive, insists: "Even if all fall away, I will not." Jesus responds with brutal honesty: "Truly I tell you, today—yes, tonight—before the rooster crows twice, you yourself will disown me three times."

Peter protests emphatically: "Even if I have to die with you, I will never disown you." And all the others echo the same sentiment.

### The Uncomfortable Truth

Why does Jesus subject them to this harsh prophecy? He's not critiquing them out of hurt feelings. He's preparing them for reality. None of us can stand up to our own sin. We all fall away—not because we're uniquely weak, but because that's the human condition.

Consider a volleyball game where every missed note equals a sin. Or imagine trying to play a piano exam flawlessly for forty-five minutes straight. If gaining access to God requires perfect performance, no one can do it. We underestimate our capacity for sin and overestimate our capacity for goodness.

The perverseness of sin runs deep: we congratulate ourselves for abstaining from evil, calling that righteousness. But abstaining from evil isn't the same as doing good. What about all the good we've failed to do? What about our indifference, our self-centeredness, our apathy?

There's no perfect life where we obey God completely while achieving our careers, our dream homes, well-behaved children, and happy endings. Such a life doesn't exist.

### Jesus' Compassionate Response

Before we flagellate ourselves, realize this: Jesus has already anticipated our failure. Nothing we do can disappoint him anymore. He knows our self-centeredness already. He doesn't offer judgment; he offers another path: "Let's figure out another way. Let's try again."

Jesus understands that only one person can achieve the perfection required to stand before God. Only he can do it. So he must now prepare himself for death—to accomplish what we cannot.

## 4. Jesus Preparing Himself (14:32-42)

### Gethsemane: Deep Distress

They arrive at Gethsemane. Jesus tells the disciples to wait while he prays, taking Peter, James, and John deeper into the garden.

Then his composure cracks: "My soul is overwhelmed with sorrow to the point of death. Stay here and keep watch."

These are shocking words from someone of infinite power. Yet power doesn't negate the reality and awfulness of death. You might know that a dental appointment is necessary and beneficial, yet you still dread it. For Jesus, the dread is incomparably deeper.

He will face excruciating physical pain. He will face uncertainty about the hours ahead. Most importantly, he will face the judgment of God—bearing the sins of the world. The weight of that knowledge crushes him.

### The Cup

Going further, Jesus falls to the ground and prays: "Abba, Father, everything is possible for you. Take this cup from me."

The cup is not a literal vessel but the cup of the new covenant—filled with his blood, shed when he drinks the cup of God's wrath. This blood won't come from a transfusion or a monthly donation. It will be spilled through agony.

Yet he completes his prayer: "Yet not what I will, but what you will."

These eight words are dangerous. They represent the most dangerous prayer in the world. Imagine saying to God: "Not what I want, but what you want." Not what your bosses will, but God's will. Not what your wallet wants, not what your parents want, not what your children want—but God's will.

When Jesus prays this, he's submitting to an agenda he knows includes death. For us, submitting to God's will lifts a burden. Our lives come under God's loving control rather than remaining under our own anxious management.

### The Sleeping Disciples

When Jesus returns, he finds the disciples asleep. "Simon, are you asleep? Couldn't you keep watch for one hour? Watch and pray so that you will not fall into temptation. The spirit is willing, but the flesh is weak."

He prays again with the same words. He comes back to find them asleep again, their eyes heavy, speechless with exhaustion. A third time, he prays and returns to find them still sleeping.

The disciples don't grasp the gravity of what's about to happen. They're asleep—literally unable to keep vigil while their teacher prepares to die. They don't understand the cost. They're tired, they can't help it, and they don't realize what Jesus faces alone.

This is the lonely reality: no one is prepared to die with Jesus. No one understands what's at stake. Jesus alone comprehends the weight of what must happen. And Jesus alone must bear it.

### The Hour Arrives

Finally, Jesus speaks with finality: "Enough! The hour has come. Look, the Son of Man is delivered into the hands of sinners. Rise! Let us go! Here comes my betrayer!"

With these words, he surrenders to the chain of events leading to the cross and ultimately to Easter. He has prepared himself through prayer, through surrender to God's will, through honest acknowledgment of his fear and sorrow. He is ready.

## The Question for Us

While Jesus prays in the garden, Judas is actively watching, scheming with the chief priests, seeking an opportunity to betray him. The chief priests themselves are plotting to arrest Jesus and kill him—but carefully, so the people don't riot.

Jesus is ready for death. His disciples are asleep. His enemies are awake and working. The Passover lamb has been replaced by the Lamb of God.

Are we ready? Will we, like Jesus, submit to God's agenda rather than our own? Will we carry our cross and follow him, as he taught in Mark 8?

The burden of self-control, of self-direction, of self-perfection is impossible. But that burden is meant for Jesus alone. When we pray, "Not what my will, but what you will," we transfer that impossible burden to God's capable hands. Life becomes less hard not when we have fewer problems, but when more people join us on this journey together, trusting in God's loving control.
