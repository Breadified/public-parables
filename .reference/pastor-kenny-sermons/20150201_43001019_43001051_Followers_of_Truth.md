# John 1:19-51

**Title:** Followers of Truth
**Date:** 8 February 2015
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Crisis of Leadership

The Jewish leaders faced a profound crisis. Who should they follow? Who is their king? This same tension appears throughout Scripture—the people of God wrestling with leadership, authority, and allegiance.

## Part 1: Who Is the Messiah? (1:19-23)

### John's Clear Denial

When priests and Levites from Jerusalem interrogated John the Baptist, asking "Who are you?", his response was unambiguous:

> "He did not fail to confess, but confessed freely, 'I am not the Messiah.'" (1:20)

The Greek text emphasizes this forcefully: John is *really* not the Messiah. It's a decisive rejection of the title.

### Understanding "Messiah"

*Messiah* (Hebrew) means "the anointed one"—the Greek equivalent is *Christ*. In the Old Testament, people chosen for special tasks were anointed: priests (Exodus 29:7) and especially kings. King David was anointed (1 Samuel 16:13), as was even King Cyrus of Persia (Isaiah 45:1).

The Jewish hope, crystallized in Psalm 2, was for God to finally install a king who would rescue them from oppression—specifically from Roman occupation. They longed for an anointed leader who would restore their nation.

### What John Was Not

The interrogators pressed further:

> "Then who are you? Are you Elijah?" (1:21)

**Elijah** represented messianic preparation. This great prophet (850 years before Jesus) never died but was taken to heaven in a chariot of fire. Malachi prophesied that Elijah would return before the Messiah came (Malachi 4:5-6).

Then: "Are you the Prophet?" This likely refers to Deuteronomy 18:15-18, where Moses predicts that God will raise up another prophet like himself—another Moses to lead God's people.

To each question, John answered: "No."

### John's Role

The desperate priests needed *some* answer to report. John gave them this:

> "I am the voice of one calling in the wilderness, 'Make straight the way for the Lord.'" (1:23, quoting Isaiah)

John's task was simple: call people to repentance and prepare them for the Lord's arrival. He was the forerunner, not the main event.

## Part 2: Jesus Is the Messiah (1:24-36)

### The Purpose of John's Baptism

The Pharisees questioned why John was baptizing if he wasn't the Messiah, Elijah, or the Prophet (1:24-25). Baptism—a washing symbolizing cleansing from sin—seemed like a messianic act.

John's answer reveals his hidden knowledge:

> "I myself did not know him, but the reason I came baptizing with water was that he might be revealed to Israel." (1:31)

> "And I myself did not know him, but the one who sent me to baptize with water told me, 'The man on whom you see the Spirit come down and remain is the one who will baptize with the Holy Spirit.'" (1:33)

John was given a divine mechanism to identify the Messiah: the one upon whom the Holy Spirit descended. So John went through the population, calling them to repentance, watching for this sign. He was looking for heaven's confirmation.

### John's Testimony: The Spirit Descends

Then came the moment John had been waiting for:

> "Then John gave this testimony: 'I saw the Spirit come down from heaven as a dove and remain on him.'" (1:32)

The Spirit descending "as a dove" echoes Genesis 1:2, where God's Spirit hovers over the waters—the same hovering motion of a bird. This connects Jesus's identification to the very beginning of creation.

There's also a subtle connection to Elijah. Though John denied being Elijah, he operated in the spirit and power of Elijah (Luke 1:16-17). Just as Elijah passed his ministry to Elisha by transferring the Spirit (2 Kings 2:9), so John passes his role to Jesus. The Spirit—God's empowerment—now rests on Jesus.

### John's Declaration

With absolute certainty, John proclaimed:

> "I have seen and I testify that this is God's Chosen One." (1:34)

John had *seen* God. This is remarkable given that no one can see God and live (Exodus 33:20). Yet John saw—and testified. His witness moved from uncertainty ("I myself did not know") to absolute conviction ("I have seen and I testify").

## Part 3: Following Jesus (1:37-51)

### The Invitation to "Come and See"

When John's disciples heard his testimony, they immediately followed Jesus (1:37). Jesus turned and asked them directly:

> "What do you want?" (1:38)

They responded: "Rabbi (which means Teacher), where are you staying?"

Jesus's reply was an invitation: "Come, and you will see" (1:39).

Notice the theme: *seeing* is central to faith. John saw the Spirit descend. The disciples saw where Jesus stayed. The invitation is always "come and see for yourself." This is not blind belief but open-eyed investigation.

### Telling Others: Andrew's Witness

Andrew, moved by what he'd witnessed, found his brother Simon and declared:

> "We have found the Messiah (that is, the Christ)." (1:41)

Andrew became a witness to witnesses. He brought Simon to Jesus, and Jesus renamed him Peter (Cephas), meaning "rock." The chain of testimony was spreading.

### Doubt and Conviction: Nathanael's Journey

Philip encountered Nathanael with good news:

> "We have found the one Moses wrote about in the Law, and about whom the prophets also wrote—Jesus of Nazareth, the son of Joseph." (1:45)

Nathanael's response was skeptical:

> "Nazareth! Can anything good come from there?" (1:46)

This was a legitimate objection. Scripture clearly prophesied that the Messiah would come from Bethlehem (Micah 5:2), the royal city. Nazareth was insignificant. Yet Jesus was born in Bethlehem but grew up in Nazareth. Nathanael's doubts were understandable.

### Jesus Knows the Heart

Philip simply replied: "Come and see" (1:46).

When Jesus saw Nathanael approaching, He said something astonishing:

> "Here truly is an Israelite in whom there is no deceit." (1:47)

This phrase carries deep weight. Israel comes from Jacob, a man known for deception (Genesis 27:35). The descendants of Jacob had inherited their ancestor's tendency toward dishonesty—God rebuked the Israelites for this very sin in Micah 2:11 and 6:11-12.

Yet Jesus saw in Nathanael an honest heart—a man in whom "there is no deceit." This likely describes someone repentant and sincere, perhaps baptized by John.

Nathanael pressed: "How do you know me?" (1:48)

Jesus answered: "I saw you while you were still under the fig tree before Philip called you." (1:48)

The fig tree represents meditation on Scripture. Nathanael was likely studying the very prophecies about the Messiah, perhaps Micah 4, which speaks of peace when all will sit "under their own vine and under their own fig tree, and no one will make them afraid" (Micah 4:4).

This knowledge—that Jesus saw him in his private devotion—shattered Nathanael's doubt. He immediately confessed:

> "Rabbi, you are the Son of God; you are the king of Israel." (1:49)

### The Greater Vision

Jesus promised him more:

> "You will see greater things than that... You will see heaven open, and the angels of God ascending and descending on the Son of Man." (1:50-51)

This allusion to Jacob's ladder (Genesis 28:12) suggests that in Jesus, heaven and earth meet. He is the bridge between the divine and human realms.

## The Heart of Following Jesus

More important than our knowing God is that **God knows and sees us**. Jesus's knowledge of Nathanael gave the doubter confidence to trust. This is the pattern throughout Scripture: God takes the initiative. He reveals Himself. He knows us completely—our ignorance, our need, our hidden hearts.

This is why we follow Jesus. Not because of grand titles like "Messiah" or "Prophet." Not because He was born in the royal city of Bethlehem. But because:

**He loves us. He knows us. He sees us.**

He is the Lamb of God who takes away the sin of the world (1:29). He came from heaven itself. Earthly leaders fail and disappoint. They are weak and fickle. But Jesus—the one who came from heaven, who knows our hearts, who laid down His life for us—He is worthy of our complete allegiance.

*Follow the one who came from heaven.*
