## The Difficulty of This Teaching

This is a difficult subject, and I acknowledge that many of you bring personal pain to this conversation. Some of you come from divorced families. Some of you are divorced yourselves. All of us know someone close to us whose family has been touched by divorce.

I want to be clear: this is a general teaching, not a personal one. I cannot speak to every circumstance from the pulpit—sometimes people are pressured into decisions, sometimes circumstances are beyond their control, sometimes we make errors of judgment, and sometimes we sin. Rather than attempting to qualify every possible scenario, I'll offer a general framework with important caveats. Please don't be defensive if something resonates with your situation; that doesn't make this message personal. I'm addressing how divorce affects society broadly, not making judgments about individual cases.

This topic generates genuine debate among faithful, godly Christians. Even scholars like D.A. Carson and John Piper, both rigorous biblical thinkers, arrive at different conclusions.

## The Biblical Passages

> "It has been said, 'Anyone who divorces his wife must give her a certificate of divorce.' But I tell you that anyone who divorces his wife, except for sexual immorality, makes her the victim of adultery, and anyone who marries a divorced woman commits adultery." (Matthew 5:31-32)

The core New Testament passages are found in Mark 10:1-16, Luke 16:18, and 1 Corinthians 7:10-11. The Old Testament foundation appears in Deuteronomy 24:1-4 and Jeremiah 3:1-4:2.

Jeremiah depicts God's grief over Israel's unfaithfulness using the metaphor of divorce:

> "If a man divorces his wife and she leaves him and marries another man, should he return to her again? Would not the land be completely defiled? But you have lived as a prostitute with many lovers—would you now return to me?" declares the Lord. (Jeremiah 3:1-5)

This imagery shows that divorce represents a breaking of covenant so serious that God uses it to describe spiritual adultery.

## The Purpose of Marriage: Children

The foundational principle underlying biblical marriage law is the protection and raising of children. This purpose appears throughout Scripture:

- Malachi 2:16 emphasizes children as central to God's design for marriage
- Historical marriage laws and annulments recognize only specific grounds—incest, impotence, non-consummation—precisely because they prevent the procreation that justifies the covenant
- The Anglican marriage liturgy speaks of children as one of marriage's primary purposes
- Children provide the raison d'être for the laws of marriage itself

This principle generates significant implications: **marriage is meant to be for life.** The debate among believers concerns whether there are exceptions to this permanence.

## The Debate: No Exceptions vs. Qualified Exceptions

**The Minority View: No Exceptions**

Some Christians—including scholars like Gordon Wenham, Andrew Cornes, and (historically) William Heth—hold that divorce with remarriage is simply not permitted. This view sees marriage as genuinely indissoluble. It represents the Catholic position and the traditional Anglican understanding (though modern Anglicanism has shifted).

**The Majority View: Limited Exceptions (AAA)**

Most evangelical Christians recognize three possible exceptions:

### 1. Adultery (Porneia)

Matthew 5:32 and 19:9 include an "exception clause" permitting divorce for sexual immorality (*porneia*). However, significant interpretive difficulties arise:

- **Terminology issue**: The word *porneia* (sexual immorality, fornication, incest) differs from the usual *moicheia* (adultery). This semantic distinction raises questions about what exactly constitutes grounds for divorce.

- **The Joseph problem**: How do we harmonize Jesus's teaching with righteous Joseph's decision to divorce Mary quietly when he discovered her pregnancy before their marriage was consummated? (Matthew 1:18-25). Joseph appears as a model of righteousness, yet he contemplated divorce. Some scholars (Julian Freeman, John Piper) suggest the exception clause may refer specifically to *betrothal*—the period before actual marriage when a couple had legal status as husband and wife but had not yet been intimate. In Jewish custom, betrothal could last up to a year before the wedding ceremony, and a woman discovered to have conceived during betrothal would constitute grounds for dissolution. This interpretation allows Joseph's righteousness to remain intact while respecting the exception clause.

### 2. Abandonment

1 Corinthians 7:12-16 addresses the case of an unbelieving spouse who leaves a Christian partner:

> "If the unbelieving partner separates, let it be so; in such cases the brother or sister is not enslaved. God has called you to peace." (1 Corinthians 7:15)

**However, a critical distinction exists**: separation does not automatically permit remarriage. The Greek words—"send away" (Deuteronomy 24), "release" (Matthew, Mark, Luke), "leave" or "separate" (1 Corinthians 7:11, 12)—often describe separation rather than remarriage.

Verse 11 explicitly states: "if she does separate, let her remain unmarried or else be reconciled to her husband" (1 Corinthians 7:11). Separation becomes possible when facing abandonment, but remarriage remains prohibited.

### 3. Abuse

Ephesians 5 describes how husbands should love their wives, mirroring Christ's self-sacrifice. Some argue that persistent abuse violates this standard so fundamentally that separation becomes necessary.

**Caveat**: The primary biblical solution for abuse is separation—not remarriage. When abuse makes a marriage unlivable, separation may be justified as protection, but it does not automatically dissolve the marriage covenant.

## God's Own Example

Remarkably, God Himself divorced Israel. Isaiah 50:1 and Jeremiah 3:8 depict God sending Israel away into exile. Yet God never remarried; instead, He pursued reconciliation:

> "I will betroth you to me forever; I will betroth you in righteousness and justice, in love and compassion." (Hosea 2:19)

Even when God severed the relationship temporarily, His heart remained oriented toward restoration. This pattern challenges any easy claim that divorce permits remarriage.

## The Covenant View

A crucial theological distinction exists between a **sacramental** view of marriage and a **covenantal** view:

- **Sacramental**: Marriage is ontologically indissoluble—it's literally impossible to break (like the unbreakable nature of the Eucharist or "one flesh"). This view was held uniformly before the Reformation.

- **Covenantal**: Marriage creates a moral obligation that is binding but theoretically dissolvable. The covenant itself remains unconditional—what matters is our fidelity to the promise, not whether the other party keeps theirs.

The marriage covenant echoes God's covenants with His people. In Genesis 15:12-17, God makes a covenant with Abram by passing through animal sacrifices alone—indicating that God binds Himself unconditionally, regardless of whether the human partner remains faithful.

The traditional marriage vow captures this: "for better for worse, till death do us part." Either the marriage covenant is unconditional, or it isn't.

This principle becomes visible in Ephesians 5, where husbands are called to love their wives as Christ loved the church—even unto death. The covenant points us to the Gospel: Christ remained faithful despite our unfaithfulness. His love did not depend on our performance.

Without this unconditional commitment, divorce becomes self-fulfilling. If we enter marriage with an escape clause, we undermine the very vulnerability and trust that makes marriage transformative.

Historically, the Anglican Church recognized that divorce could occur but always resisted easy remarriage—bishops were required to approve remarriage, understanding that the covenant, while dissolvable, carried profound spiritual weight.

## Pastoral Reflections

Some situations create genuine lose-lose dilemmas. There is little that actually works, and making exceptions based on hard cases can undermine the integrity of law itself. Laws exist to set ideals even when they are not perfectly met in every circumstance. We shouldn't craft legislation around exceptional situations.

If we hold that adultery disqualifies someone from marriage and sexual ethics matter, then the same principle applies to leadership and covenant-keeping. If divorcees cannot lead, that flows from the same logic that sets the marriage standard high.

Law functions as a teacher, establishing ideals. Start with your own life. If you are married, commit yourself to the covenant unreservedly. Model what covenant marriage looks like. The world doesn't need exemptions—it needs to see believers who take their promises seriously.

## The Undefinition of Marriage

What is really happening in contemporary culture is the systematic **undefinition** of marriage itself.

In the 1960s, widely available contraception severed the link between sex and children for the first time in human history. Marriage shifted from being about procreation and family to being about personal enjoyment and companionship—a "friends with benefits" arrangement.

Once children were removed from the equation, the rationale for permanence vanished. In the 1970s, "no-fault" divorce arrived, requiring no reason whatsoever. Remarriage became automatic. Wedlock became "wedlease"—a temporary arrangement rather than a covenant.

This undefinition continues today with the question of one man and one woman. Tomorrow, it will move to exclusivity—monogamy will become "monogamish" or open to polygamy. And once marriage loses all definition, we lose the life script that creates healthy adults and stable societies. That loss has profound intergenerational consequences.

**Yet the fight is not necessarily lost**—at least not in our circles.

Believe the Word of God. Start with the family of believers. Model what covenant marriage means. And the world will follow.

We see this happening already. Divorce rates among the wealthy have leveled off because they are "smart enough to work it out." The poor remain trapped in cycles of family breakdown caused by laws that devalued covenant. The difference is visible and measurable.

If the church believes Scripture, if we practice what we teach about marriage, if we stand firm on covenant even when culture ridicules permanence, we become a counterculture witness to the Gospel itself. Marriage points to Christ's unbreakable love. That testimony is too important to abandon.
