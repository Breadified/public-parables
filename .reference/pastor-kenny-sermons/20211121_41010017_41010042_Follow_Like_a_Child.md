# Mark 10:17-42

**Title:** Follow Like a Child  
**Date:** November 21, 2021  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Our Conflicted Relationship with Wealth

Christians have a love-hate relationship with wealth. As children, we understand intuitively that wealth can be dangerous—we think of Scrooge McDuck or industrial monopolists hoarding their riches. But as we mature, we discover that money is actually necessary to accomplish good in the world. This creates an uncomfortable tension: Is wealth a necessary evil? Something to be reluctant about? Something to condemn?

The Bible presents a complex picture of wealth that requires honest wrestling. Jesus's teaching on this subject is provocative and demands our careful attention.

---

## 1. The Problem with Wealth (Mark 10:17-25)

### A Confronting Command

> 21 Jesus looked at him and loved him. "One thing you lack," he said. "Go, sell everything you have and give to the poor, and you will have treasure in heaven. Then come, follow me." 22 At this the man's face fell. He went away sad, because he had great wealth.

Here is a man who has done everything right. He ran to Jesus, fell on his knees, and asked sincerely about inheriting eternal life. Yet Jesus gave him a command that broke him. The man went away grieving—not because he was rejected, but because he could not part with his wealth.

### A Heartbreaking Scene

This is poignant. Jesus loved this man, yet the man walked away. It's like watching someone pour his heart out, asking another to follow him, only to have that invitation rejected. The deal breaker? His great wealth. He was willing to do anything for Jesus—except this one thing.

**Wealth became a barrier to following God wholeheartedly.**

### What Scripture Reveals About Misused Wealth

The Bible shows us several ways wealth becomes destructive:

**Hedonism and materialism.** Using wealth purely for self-indulgence and luxury rather than for good.

> Luke 12:19 "And I'll say to myself, 'You have plenty of grain laid up for many years. Take life easy; eat, drink and be merry.'"

**Hoarding without mercy.** Accumulating wealth while refusing to help the poor.

> James 5:1-3 Your wealth has rotted, and moths have eaten your clothes. Your gold and silver are corroded. Their corrosion will testify against you. You have hoarded wealth in the last days.

**Exploitation.** Using power and resources to oppress and cheat workers.

> James 5:4 The wages you failed to pay the workers who mowed your fields are crying out against you. The cries of the harvesters have reached the ears of the Lord Almighty.

**Pride and self-trust.** Depending on riches instead of depending on God.

> 1 Timothy 6:17 Charge the rich not to be haughty, nor to set their hopes on the uncertainty of riches, but on God, who richly provides us with everything to enjoy.

> Matthew 6:24 You cannot serve both God and money.

### The Rich Exercise Outsized Influence

The wealthy today are not necessarily factory owners or industrialists—they're the elites we see everywhere: media moguls, politicians, celebrities, and cultural influencers. They occupy glamorous spaces and attend exclusive events. They attend the world's best universities. They are the voices we hear shaping culture.

**The real danger is that we are captivated by them.** We love their aesthetic. We emulate their lifestyle. We crave their approval. We try to copy their impractical, aspirational beliefs—the "luxury beliefs" that only the rich can afford to hold without suffering consequences.

Consider: The wealthy can advocate for extreme positions—divorce, transgenderism, open borders, abandoning coal—because they can afford the consequences. They can buy the best therapists, surgeons, and private schools for their children. They can insulate themselves from the fallout of ideas they promote. But ordinary people cannot. These beliefs are only "luxuries" the rich can maintain.

Yet we look to our Instagram feeds and see who our idols are. We emulate the rich and famous, adopting their views and their lifestyle aspirations, not realizing we're mimicking people with vastly different resource bases than our own.

> Jesus's warning: "Beware of the wealthy."

## 2. The Real Issue: Love of Money, Not Money Itself (Mark 10:17-20, 32-34)

### The Actual Problem

The issue isn't simply that the man was wealthy—it's that he wasn't willing to follow Jesus wholeheartedly. He wanted to follow conditionally, picking and choosing what he would obey. He wanted Jesus to rubber-stamp his life, confirming he was already on the right path, rather than genuinely asking how to gain eternal life.

Jesus called his bluff. The man had kept the commandments (the second half of the law—those about not murdering, committing adultery, stealing, etc.), but the first half—loving God fully—remained incomplete. When Jesus invited him to "come, follow me," the man refused.

> 1 Timothy 6:10 The love of money is a root of all kinds of evils.

> Hebrews 13:5 Keep your life free from the love of money.

**The love of money represents the love of wealth for itself—love of luxury, love of exploitation, love of self-protection, love of depending on ourselves rather than God.** Money itself is neutral. The problem is the orientation of the heart.

### Many Wealthy People Did Great Good

Scripture records numerous wealthy believers who used their resources faithfully:

- Joseph (Barnabas) gave his land to the church
- Dorcas used her wealth to serve the poor with generosity
- Cornelius gave alms generously to those in need
- Lydia opened her home and her heart to Paul
- Aquila and Priscilla used their wealth to disciple Apollos
- Philemon was wealthy yet treated his slave Onesimus as a beloved brother

In the Old Testament, Abraham was a pastoral magnate, and David ruled a kingdom. The Proverbs explicitly commend saving and wisdom in financial matters:

> Proverbs 21:20 The wise store up choice food and olive oil, but fools gulp theirs down.

**The problem is not being wealthy itself. The problem is using wealth wrongly to do evil, or failing to use wealth for good. The problem is not following Jesus.**

### What We're Actually Afraid to Give Up

Here's the honest truth: Most of us aren't actually wrestling with wealth. We don't have vast fortunes. But we are wrestling with something else—comfort and safety.

We want to serve, but in comfort. We want to help the world, but with a stable salary and benefits. We'll serve at church, but at our convenience, around our timetable. We want the middle-class lifestyle: the four-bedroom house with ducted air conditioning, good schools nearby, ski trips and overseas holidays every year. We don't want to work—we chase passive income, not so we can be freed to serve, but so we can retire to Bali and live an easy life.

**We want safety. We want weekends to ourselves. We want Netflix and familiar comforts. We want zero risk.**

COVID exposed how little risk we're actually willing to tolerate. We shut down entire economies to reduce microscopic percentages of danger because "MY LIFE is important." We couldn't conceive of sacrifice for the greater good.

And we stand before God saying, "I've done all this. I have a good, safe, uncomplicated life. Tick the box."

### A Sobering Reality

By the World Bank's 2012 standards, if you earn more than $50,000 annually, you are in the global 1 percent. **We are the wealthy.** We are hoarding and fattening ourselves.

**But God does not call us to be comfortable and safe.**

The world changes. New problems emerge. Different needs arise at different times. The harvest remains plentiful. To adapt to a changing world means getting uncomfortable. It means assuming some risk. We are called to carry our cross daily—not once and then enjoy comfort forever, but continually letting go of our preoccupations and refusing to trust in the things that cannot deliver what they promise.

**Follow Jesus like a child**—with trust, flexibility, and willingness to be led into the unknown.

## 3. The Wealthy Can Be Saved (Mark 10:26-31)

### Hope for the Seemingly Impossible

> 26 The disciples were even more amazed, and said to each other, "Who then can be saved?" 27 Jesus looked at them and said, "With man this is impossible, but not with God; all things are possible with God."

The disciples understood the weight of Jesus's words. If wealth is such a barrier to the kingdom, how can anyone be saved? But Jesus offered hope: **All things are possible with God.**

The camel and the needle's eye may seem impossible—a camel squeezed through an needle's eye. But perhaps the camel must be lean, stripped of its fat, reduced to essentials. Perhaps the image suggests burning away accumulated excess.

**For some of us, this is possible.** Those with the best hearts. The most honorable. The most honest. Those most able to withstand temptation. Perhaps you can be that camel.

### A Third Way

We are often given two false choices:

1. **Be rich for myself.** Indulge. Hoard. Live for pleasure.
2. **Be poor so I'm less self-centered.** Avoid the temptation of wealth.

Both options are fundamentally selfish—they're still about myself.

**But there is a third way: Gain wealth in order to use it sacrificially for others.**

This is not for everyone, but for some called to it:

- Run a business that provides employment and solves real problems
- Invest in property to provide affordable housing  
- Stand for political office to secure religious liberty and fair laws
- Build institutions (schools, hospitals, halls) that serve communities long-term
- Create wealth that you will never personally enjoy, but that future generations will inherit

This requires far more effort and sacrifice than getting a degree and settling into a comfortable career. It demands great wealth, real risk, genuine discomfort, and forgone leisure.

### Following Jesus Radically

Consider someone like Elon Musk—not as a moral exemplar in all things, but as an illustration. He was not willing to simply get a job and be comfortable. He risks everything. He owns almost nothing personally; all his wealth is in company stock. When Tesla had factory failures and SpaceX rockets exploded, he suffered directly. He continues to risk everything on his vision. As a result, thousands are employed, new technologies exist, and industries have been revived.

**Now imagine channeling that same radical sacrifice toward God's kingdom.**

> 28-31 "Truly I tell you," Jesus replied, "no one who has left home or brothers or sisters or mother or father or children or fields for me and the gospel will fail to receive a hundred times as much in this present age: homes, brothers, sisters, mothers, children and fields—along with persecutions—and in the age to come eternal life. But many who are first will be last, and the last first."

The principle is simple: **Put others first, and you can gain wealth to put others first.**

### Practical Sacrifice

Imagine working hard for years, saving carefully, and finally receiving a bank loan for a house—your one chance at security. Instead of buying for yourself, you invest it in rental property for others. You continue living minimally, moving every six months to find the cheapest rent, while profits go toward helping the next person find housing.

Or you own a four-bedroom house. Instead of creating a media room for your comfort, you rent rooms to an international student, a grandmother downsizing, or foster children. You give up walking around freely in your underwear. You share your kitchen with strangers. You relinquish your precious comfort.

"But my comfort! My holidays! My lifestyle!"

**That is exactly the nature of discipleship.** The first will be last so that the last will be first.

## Conclusion: The Calling

Friends, I love you all. I cherish life with you, especially these last two years. But one thing you are lacking:

**Come, follow Jesus like a child.**

Not with calculation. Not with conditions. Not with a backup plan. Not clinging to comfort and safety. But with the trust and flexibility of a child, willing to go where He leads.

Some of you are called to build wealth sacrificially. Some are called to simplicity. But all of us are called to relinquish our preoccupation with ourselves and follow Him completely.

The task before us is greater than our individual dreams. Gospel proclamation requires schools and hospitals and strong institutions. It requires resources. It requires risk. It requires sacrifice.

Will you follow Him with your whole heart?
