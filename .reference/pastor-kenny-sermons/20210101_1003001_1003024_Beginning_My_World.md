# Genesis 3:1-24

**Title:** Beginning: My World  
**Date:** 17 January 2021  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Ideal World vs. Our Experience

Genesis 1-2 presents an ideal world—one where wild animals coexist peacefully with humanity, where man and woman live in harmony, and where God walks contemplatively with His creation in the cool of the evening. Yet the world we experience is starkly different: we know suffering, death, vulnerability, war, and hurt. We experience sin.

The reason? Our world, as we actually experience it, has another beginning: **Genesis 3**.

## A Dissection of Sin: The Four Steps of Rebellion

Sin is not merely the breaking of a rule—it is the determination of a new rule under a new ruler. It is humanity saying to God, "No, I don't want you to rule my world. I will run it myself." The serpent's temptation reveals the anatomy of this rebellion in four distinct steps.

### Step One: God's Word Is Doubted

> "Did God really say, 'You must not eat from any tree in the garden'?" (3:1)

The serpent begins innocuously, planting doubt. Did God really mean this? Maybe He meant something else? This smallest wedge of doubt is where sin begins.

### Step Two: God's Word Is Exaggerated and Misrepresented

The serpent distorts what God said. The actual command was:

> "You must not eat from the tree of the knowledge of good and evil" (2:16-17)

One tree. But the serpent's question frames it as "any tree." Once the lie is posed this way, the wedge deepens. If God didn't say "any tree," perhaps He didn't forbid "the tree" either.

The woman herself then exaggerates further, adding words that God never spoke:

> "You must not eat fruit from the tree that is in the middle of the garden, and **you must not touch it**, or **you will die**." (3:2-3)

God never forbade touching it. She added "touch" to make God sound unreasonable and restrictive—a common tactic: impute bad motives to the lawgiver so you don't have to obey the law.

### Step Three: God's Word Is Denied Outright

> "You will not certainly die," the serpent said to the woman. (3:4)

Now comes the outright lie. What God said will happen, the serpent says will not happen.

### Step Four: God's Goodness Is Denied

> "For God knows that when you eat from it your eyes will be opened, and you will be like God, knowing good and evil." (3:5)

The serpent suggests God has a hidden agenda—He's keeping something good from you out of jealousy or malice. God is not only wrong; He is deceptive and selfish.

## The Nature of "Knowing Good and Evil"

Some interpret this as gaining wisdom or understanding morality. Others think it relates to losing innocence. But the key insight is this: **"knowing good and evil" means determinative knowledge—the power to decide what is good and what is evil for yourself.**

I know I will eat KFC for lunch because I have determined it. I know the lawn will be mowed because I have determined it. In moral terms, to have determinative knowledge of good and evil is to decide for yourself what should be good and what should be evil.

When Eve saw the fruit:

> The fruit of the tree was good for food and pleasing to the eye, and also desirable for gaining wisdom. She took some and ate it. (3:6)

Eve determined it was good. Adam joined her with no resistance. The act of eating was the determining of good and evil—not the fruit itself, but their choice. She decided: *This is good, despite what God said.*

## The Essence of Sin

Many people think they don't sin because they haven't broken obvious rules. But **sin is not the mere breaking of rules; sin is the making of new rules under a new ruler.** Law-breaking is merely the symptom. The disease is rebellion—the attitude that says, "I will determine what is right and wrong for myself."

This rebellion plays out the same way across generations. Consider how contemporary culture redefines moral boundaries—it follows the same four steps:

1. **Doubt God's word**: "Surely culture has evolved. God must want to be relevant."
2. **Exaggerate God's word**: "God is against love itself!"
3. **Deny God's word**: "This is good and should be legal and taught everywhere."
4. **Deny God's goodness**: "God must be hateful and bigoted to oppose this."

In doing so, we crown ourselves as the determiners of good and evil. We become like God—or rather, we usurp His throne.

## The Consequence: Nakedness and Fear

> Then the eyes of both of them were opened, and they realized they were naked; so they sewed fig leaves together and made coverings for themselves. (3:7)

"Naked" does not mean unclothed. It means **vulnerable, unprotected, at-risk.**

When you decide your own morality and I decide mine, without reference to God, you are naked. You have no protection. If I can determine what is right and wrong, and my neighbor can determine what is right and wrong, then we are vulnerable to each other—subject to each other's arbitrary will.

When Jack Dorsey, one of the world's richest men, banned a sitting president from Twitter while allowing others to preach hatred, billions of people suddenly realized: *We are naked.* We exist at the mercy of those with determinative power. Our livelihoods, our voices, our platforms—all subject to the arbitrary changing minds of billionaires. If he can do this to a president with no consequences, what can he do to you?

This is the condition of a world without God: **everyone naked against each other, everyone vulnerable to everyone else's determination of right and wrong.**

And we are naked against God Himself.

> Then the man and his wife heard the sound of the Lord God as he was walking in the garden in the cool of the day, and they hid from the Lord God among the trees of the garden. (3:8-9)
>
> But the Lord God called to the man, "Where are you?"
>
> He answered, "I heard you in the garden, and I was afraid because I was naked; so I hid." (3:10)

Fear. When you are vulnerable to someone who has power over you—especially God, who is the true determiner of good and evil and who said, "You will surely die"—fear is the natural response.

## The Blame Game and Reversal of Order

When God asked what had happened, the blame began:

> The man said, "The woman you put here with me—she gave me some fruit from the tree, and I ate it."
>
> The woman said, "The serpent deceived me, and I ate." (3:12-13)

In God's original order: Man, Woman, Creation. But sin reverses this order. The Serpent tells the Woman, who tells the Man. Then in judgment, the blame flows backward: Man blames Woman who blames Serpent. The curses follow in this reversed order.

## The Curses: Disorder in Three Relationships

### The Serpent

> "Cursed are you above all livestock and all wild animals! You will crawl on your belly and you will eat dust all the days of your life." (3:14)

The animals, once peaceful companions under humanity's care, become hostile. We see this enmity everywhere—in Australia's deadly wildlife, in the predators that hunt us. We have spent centuries trying to domesticate animals through selective breeding, making them safe and palatable. Without this long work of domestication, animals remain dangerous to us. We are at war with creation.

> "And I will put enmity between you and the woman, and between your offspring and hers; he will crush your head, and you will strike his heel." (3:15)

### The Woman

> "I will make your pains in childbearing very severe; with painful labor you will give birth to children.
>
> Your desire will be for your husband, and he will rule over you." (3:16)

The role given to her—the bearing of life—is now marked by pain. The relationship with her husband, once one of mutuality and intimacy, is now characterized by tension and hierarchy.

### The Man

> "Because you listened to your wife and ate fruit from the tree about which I commanded you, 'You must not eat from it,'
>
> Cursed is the ground because of you; through painful toil you will eat food from it all the days of your life. It will produce thorns and thistles for you, and you will eat the plants of the field. By the sweat of your brow you will eat your food." (3:17-19)

The work of civilizing the world, which was meant to be his calling and fulfillment, becomes grueling toil. The ground resists him. Thorns and thistles mock his efforts.

> "Until you return to the ground, since from it you were taken; for dust you are and to dust you will return." (3:19)

**Death.** This is the wage of sin (Romans 6:23).

## The World As We Experience It

This is our world: hostility between creation and humanity, tension and struggle between man and woman, and perpetual conflict with God that ends in death. While we live, we face endless fighting and struggles. And then we die.

This is not how God created the world to be. It is profoundly, devastatingly wrong—wrong beyond words, beyond description. It can only be expressed in tears.

Yet God does not abandon us to this fate.

## God's Mercy in Judgment

Before banishing them from Eden, God shows mercy:

> Adam named his wife Eve, because she would become the mother of all the living. (3:20)

Life continues. There is still future, still hope.

> Adam made love to his wife Eve, and she became pregnant and gave birth to Cain. She said, "With the help of the LORD I have brought forth a man." (4:1)

God is the God of life, even in the shadow of death.

> The Lord God made garments of skin for Adam and his wife and clothed them. (3:21)

Not fig leaves that offer no real protection, but proper garments of skin—leather that will shelter them from the elements. Even in judgment, God shows compassion. He mitigates His punishment: He must punish because He is just, but He ensures they will survive and have opportunity to continue their calling.

## The Promise of Redemption

A hint of the ultimate solution appears in Genesis 3:15:

> "He will crush your head, and you will strike his heel."

**The man Jesus is the offspring of the woman.** The serpent strikes His heel at the cross, appearing to achieve victory through death. But in that very contact, He crushes the serpent's head. Through His death and resurrection, Jesus defeats death itself. The vicious cycle is broken.

> For the wages of sin is death, but the free gift of God is eternal life in Christ Jesus our Lord. (Romans 6:23)

And so in the midst of our toil and struggle, we have hope: death is not the end. Resurrection is coming. We are not abandoned to determine our own fate in an arbitrary world. We belong to a family of believers who support and help one another. We have been given the grace of community, of shared meals, of gladness in the midst of struggle.

This is Kenny's pastoral encouragement: Come together, eat, enjoy what has been domesticated and made good, take gladness to heart. Remember that God has not left us alone. Before Monday comes and the toil resumes, thank God for one another.
