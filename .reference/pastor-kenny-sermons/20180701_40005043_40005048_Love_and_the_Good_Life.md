# Matthew 5:43-48

**Title:** Love and the Good Life
**Date:** 1 July 2018
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Love Your Neighbour

Jesus quotes the Pharisees, who were the social justice warriors of their time: "Love your neighbour." This command has backing from the Old Testament. In Leviticus 19:17-18, God speaks to the Israelites about their social obligations to one another:

> "'Do not hate a fellow Israelite in your heart. Rebuke your neighbor frankly so you will not share in their guilt. Do not seek revenge or bear a grudge against anyone among your people, but love your neighbor as yourself. I am the Lord."

The whole of Leviticus 19 is about social behaviour between Israelites—do not steal, do not lie, do not deceive one another, do not hold back wages from workers, do not curse the deaf or put stumbling blocks in front of the blind, do not show partiality in justice, do not spread slander, do not endanger your neighbour's life. The summary of all these commandments is this: love God and love each other.

The law forbids hating one's brother and taking revenge; it commands rebuking and loving. We are to carry each other's burdens, as Paul writes: "Carry each other's burdens, and in this way you will fulfill the law of Christ" (Galatians 6:2).

### The Historical Context of the Law

The law was given to the Israelites when they had just been freed from Egyptian slavery and were entering a new land to form a new nation. In ancient Near East culture, life beyond your immediate family was a dog-eat-dog world. God's call through the law was to think beyond your family—to think of the tribe, the community, the nation. This was radically countercultural.

Consider the things we take for granted in Australia that embody this principle: how we drive, giving way in traffic and saying thank you; volunteering at schools; not taking free shopping trolleys to our homes; the honour system at self-checkout. These small acts reflect a love of neighbour.

Compare this to other cultures. In China, driving is chaotic—cars hit pedestrians and other drivers pass by without stopping. In India, the roads are lawless. We've developed a society where people care for one another and strangers are treated with dignity. This is the fruit of Christian values.

Yet we're fast becoming like those other cultures. We've largely abandoned the idea of loving our neighbour. We don't know who our neighbours are, let alone talk to them. We move every seven years—right around when our mortgage repayments start biting into the principal. We don't volunteer, don't join political parties or community groups, don't attend church, scouts, P&C meetings, or even strata meetings that affect us directly. We come together as a community not to serve, but to watch Vivid. Shopping trolleys litter the streets. Trash accumulates in kerbs. The problem of individualism leaves us alone.

This is not to say we shouldn't value individuality. God made each of us uniquely, and we rightly despise conformity for its own sake. But God made us relational as well. Your church community does look out for each other well—coming together in tragedy, when someone faces a crisis, for projects and shared meals. This is the good life: a life of giving, harmony, and care for each other. It's a community where strangers are formed into family by following God's command to love your neighbour.

## Love Your Enemies

However, the Pharisees quoted the law differently. Look at verse 43:

> "You have heard that it was said, 'Love your neighbor and hate your enemy.'"

There is actually no such quote in the Bible. In fact, the Old Testament says the opposite:

> "If you come across your enemy's ox or donkey wandering off, be sure to return it. If you see the donkey of someone who hates you fallen down under its load, do not leave it there; be sure you help them with it." (Exodus 23:4-5)
>
> "Do not gloat when your enemy falls; when they stumble, do not let your heart rejoice." (Proverbs 24:17)
>
> "If your enemy is hungry, give him food to eat; if he is thirsty, give him water to drink." (Proverbs 25:21)

### The Pharisaic Distortion

How did the Pharisees arrive at "hate your enemy"? They likely reasoned from silence in Leviticus 19:17, which speaks of "a fellow Israelite." The Pharisees interpreted this narrowly: "Love your people" became "love *only* your people," and therefore "hate your enemy."

The Pharisees faced many real enemies at the time. They lived under Roman occupation—Pompeii and the Romans invaded Judea in 60 BC and remained a Roman province, desecrating the temple and introducing pagan practices. The Romans would destroy the temple in 70 AD. The Pharisees also chafed under the rule of King Herod, who was only half-Jewish, installed as a puppet king by the Romans. The Idumeans were foreigners, not fellow Israelites. There were many reasons to "hate your enemy."

But in adopting this ethic, the Pharisees weren't quoting Scripture—they were quoting Greek philosophy. Plato's character Meno defined virtue this way: "A man's virtue consists of taking part in political life, being good to his friends and doing evil to his enemies."

The Pharisees manipulated the law. When it says "love your neighbour," they concluded it must mean "hate your enemies." They engaged in a kind of spiritual rationalization—what Jesus elsewhere calls "Korban"—where they pitted laws against each other to justify ignoring both. In Matthew 15:3-6, Jesus rebukes this exact pattern: they say that if wealth is devoted to God, one need not honour and care for aging parents. They set two good things against each other and end up following neither.

### Jesus's Reinterpretation

Instead, Jesus gives us the proper interpretation:

> "But I tell you, love your enemies and pray for those who persecute you, that you may be children of your Father in heaven." (Matthew 5:44-45)

To "love your enemies" means to still want what is best for them. This doesn't make you a pushover or mean overlooking injustice. It means you want justice for them, you want to warn them, you want to bless them. You don't seek revenge.

Your enemies want what is bad for you. We want what is good for them. To "pray for those who persecute you" means praying not just for them to stop persecuting you, but for their salvation.

Why should we do this? Because of who God is:

> "He causes his sun to rise on the evil and the good, and sends rain on the righteous and the unrighteous." (Matthew 5:45)

The sun rises on all people regardless of their character. Rain falls on the just and unjust alike. There is one sky above us all. We are all connected through God's providence. When you hurt your enemies, you ultimately hurt yourself. If conflict goes unresolved, people bite back, and the cycle continues. God is above the nations, above good and evil, and treats all the same.

This is fundamentally different from other ethical systems. Some religions teach one ethic for believers and another for outsiders—you can tell the truth to your co-religionists but lie to others; you can murder someone outside your faith. But Christians don't distinguish people that way. Both the foreigner and the native are under the same law.

### The Challenge of Equality

Verses 46-47 pose a challenge:

> "If you love those who love you, what reward will you get? Are not even the tax collectors doing that? And if you greet only your own people, what are you doing more than others? Do not even pagans do that?"

When you love only those who love you and greet only your own people, you're not living differently from anyone else. Tax collectors loved their own. Families of other religions—Muslims, Buddhists, pagans—are also close-knit. Even Hitler loved his wife and mother; he loved his family and his tribe. But that's not distinctive. Bad people love too. They have friends and family. The Mafia loves their children, but there's nothing Christian about that.

But if God is God of the universe and Christ died for all, then the Christian also loves all—including enemies. This is what it means to be children of God: to reflect His character of universal love, not tribal preference.

### The Refugee Question

Given this teaching, it seems logical that Christians should support open border policies and take in refugees from war-torn countries. But this reasoning falls into the Pharisaic trap of Korban—doing good to one group while neglecting another.

Consider the facts: Australia took in over 21,000 refugees from Syria and Iraq in two years, with 30 percent settling in Fairfield. This created real pressure on local schools, where resources shifted to teaching English rather than maths and science, creating discipline issues. Welfare services were strained. Rent and property prices increased, affecting those already struggling.

Those who advocate for open borders often live in areas insulated from the consequences. If 7,000 refugees were resettled in Cherrybrook, it would overwhelm schools already at capacity, depress property values (your life savings), and undermine the Christian values that built this nation—values that depend on a shared understanding, which becomes harder to maintain with rapid demographic change.

But the people of Cherrybrook matter. The people of Fairfield matter. If you think "the people of Syria matter, so the people of my own community don't," you are thinking like a Pharisee—doing good in one area to justify harm in another.

The answer is not to neglect either group but to serve both wisely. We can set up refugee camps in Turkey, closer to the conflict zone and cheaper, allowing us to help more people without destabilizing our own communities. We do what we can without jeopardising the long-term viability of our own society.

Love your neighbour applies to the unpopular ones too—those often neglected by media, politicians, and even Christian organizations. The media loves distant neighbours, loves minorities, but neglects the homeless white males who lost mining and agricultural jobs to green policies, neglects the suicidal, neglects the jobless. They advocate for the 50 domestic violence deaths annually but ignore the 100,000 babies aborted each year.

### The Good Life

Who is your neighbour? All of them. This is what it means to follow Jesus. This is justice.

> "Be perfect, therefore, as your heavenly Father is perfect." (Matthew 5:48)

Perfection here doesn't mean sinlessness but wholeness—a complete, undivided commitment to loving all people equally, regardless of tribe, race, religion, age, or status. The greatest expression of this love to anyone is the gospel itself, poured out on all.
