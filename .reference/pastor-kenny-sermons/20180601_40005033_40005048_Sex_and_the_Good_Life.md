# Matthew 5:33-48

**Title:** Sex and the Good Life
**Date:** 9 June 2018
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Seventh Commandment: Adultery

> "You have heard that it was said, 'You shall not commit adultery.'" (Matthew 5:27)

The prohibition against adultery stands among the Ten Commandments because it strikes at the very foundation of society—the family unit. God gave Israel not a government of arbitrary kings (who can be corrupt) nor anarchy (where people are corrupt), but a nation of laws given by God, who is inherently good. Both rulers and people were to live under these same laws.

Adultery is remarkably simple in the Hebrew—two words meaning "adultery" and "no." Yet its consequences are profound. Why does God place this command in the top ten? Because adultery destroys the family, which in turn collapses entire societies.

### The Devastation of Adultery

Adultery violates everything a couple has built together in the most intimate way possible. The betrayal runs deep—it feels like death: the death of trust, the death of affection, the death of all the work invested in marriage. It introduces deception and disease into what should be a sacred bond.

When couples exchange vows—"for better or worse"—adultery represents the worst. It is worse than bankruptcy, worse than disability. Infidelity betrays the foundation of commitment itself.

### Impact on Children

The ripple effects extend to every child involved. When an affair shatters a family, children lose the stable, secure foundation they need. When they discover the infidelity, they experience something more than personal loss—their entire understanding of their parents' character crumbles. The parents who taught them to be good and tell the truth are revealed to have violated their most sacred promises.

Across generations, instability and mental illness spread, robbing children of the security necessary for healthy development.

### Impact on the Church

Adultery's damage extends into God's community, especially when it involves church leadership. It shakes the faith of those who witness it and undermines the witness of Christ in the world.

Worse than physical consequences, adultery damages our relationship with God. Faithfulness is an attribute of God we are called to emulate. When we commit adultery, we fail to reflect His character.

> "I have seen a horrible thing in Israel: There Ephraim is given to prostitution, Israel is defiled." (Hosea 6:10)

## The Heart of the Matter: Lustful Desire

> "But I tell you that anyone who looks at a woman lustfully has already committed adultery with her in his heart." (Matthew 5:28)

Jesus moves beyond outward action to the condition of the heart. He understood what we now see clearly in our digital age—you don't need to be in the same room. Text messages, internet connections, and social media all enable adultery of the mind.

### Understanding "Lustful Looking"

A crucial distinction: this is not about any passing attraction. An unmarried man finding an unmarried woman attractive is natural—part of how we are made. A glance at someone doesn't constitute adultery. Men are built to notice the physical world—it's simply how our nature works.

However, the Greek construction—"looks at a woman in order to desire"—implies intention and deliberate pursuit. It's not the fleeting attraction, but the choice to entertain and pursue it. Martin Luther captured it well: "You can't stop a bird from flying over your head, but you can stop that bird from building a nest in your hair." You have power to refuse to dwell on such thoughts.

This also connects to the tenth commandment about coveting—desiring what belongs to another. When someone is married, they belong to someone else. There's a ring on the finger. There's a family they're accountable to. You have no right to enter that sacred unit. You are stealing.

### Adultery Beyond the Physical

Adultery happens even without physical contact. Many justify their behavior with rationalizations: "It's just a work lunch." "It's just texting a friend." "We're just joking around." But flirting with someone you're attracted to—pursuing an intimate emotional connection with someone who is not your spouse—is a form of adultery.

As James describes: "Each person is tempted when they are dragged away by their own evil desire and enticed. Then, after desire has conceived, it gives birth to sin" (James 1:14-15).

The desire to steal what belongs to another is at the heart of adultery. It doesn't matter if the marriage feels loveless. It doesn't matter if you've emotionally separated. It doesn't matter what excuse you might offer. The commitment was made not for when love is present, but for when love has faded. That's when the promise is tested.

> "Marriage should be honored by all, and the marriage bed kept pure, for God will judge the adulterer and all the sexually immoral." (Hebrews 13:4)

Adultery is not "falling in love" but always "failing in love."

## The Radical Solution

> "If your right eye causes you to stumble, gouge it out and throw it away. It is better for you to lose one part of your body than for your whole body to be thrown into hell. And if your right hand causes you to stumble, cut it off and throw it away." (Matthew 5:29-30)

This is hyperbole—Jesus is not advocating bodily mutilation. But He is calling for radical action. The point is that we sin in our minds before we sin physically. We plot and scheme. Removing the source doesn't solve an internal problem, since blind and disabled people can still sin in their hearts.

But the principle stands: we must take serious, decisive action. If someone causes you to stumble, confront and challenge the situation. Move to a different department. Change jobs. Don't accept the status quo. The stakes are eternal—God will judge the adulterer.

## The Historical Weight of This Command

Throughout history, societies have recognized adultery's severity. In the Old Testament, both the man and the woman were to be put to death—a sign of how seriously God took this violation (Leviticus 20:10, Deuteronomy 22:22-24).

In medieval Christendom, adulterers were executed. Anne Boleyn was executed for adultery and treason, though notably, King Henry VIII, who committed adultery with her while married to Catherine of Aragon, should have faced the same judgment.

Today, adultery remains illegal in 21 U.S. states, including New York, where it's punishable by a $500 fine or 90 days in jail. Yet Australian law only criminalized adultery until 1994. Even more troubling, contemporary family law now permits multiple concurrent domestic relationships—effectively legalizing what Scripture forbids.

The contrast is striking: societies have historically punished this sin severely because they recognized its power to destroy families and communities. Yet our culture now not only permits but encourages it.

## Hope and Restoration

Despite the severity of this commandment, God offers hope even to those who have failed. We have all committed adultery in our hearts, if not in deed.

> "Return, faithless Israel," declares the Lord. "I will frown on you no longer, for I am faithful... Only acknowledge your guilt—you have rebelled against the Lord your God... Return, faithless people, for I am your husband. I will choose you and bring you to Zion." (Jeremiah 3:12-14)

There is forgiveness for the adulterer. Even after such a terrible thing, God extends grace to those who repent.

## Living as Salt and Light

In a world marked by unfaithfulness, Christians must be different. We are called to be the salt and light of the earth. Our yes must be yes and our no must be no.

For the unmarried: honor marriage by refusing to entangle yourselves with those who are married, and by guarding your own future marriage through sexual purity.

For the married: honor your vows by remaining faithful. Exclusivity in marriage is not a burden but a protection—a sacred trust between you, your spouse, and God.

Be faithful in all you do. Let your life testify to the faithfulness of God.
