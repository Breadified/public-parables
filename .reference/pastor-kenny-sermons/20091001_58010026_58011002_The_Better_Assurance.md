# Hebrews 10:26–11:2; 12:1–13

**Title:** The Better Assurance
**Date:** 8 October 2009
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## The Perseverance of Man

The Christian race demands perseverance in a way that nothing else in life does. Unlike the HSC or other earthly pursuits, where you can simply move on if you fail, the Bible's warning is stark: persevere as a Christian or face spiritual death. This is not meant to frighten us into despair, but to awaken us to the gravity and beauty of what we've been called to.

---

## Warning: The Consequences of Falling Away (10:26–31)

> If we deliberately keep on sinning after we have received the knowledge of the truth, no sacrifice for sins is left, but only a fearful expectation of judgment and of raging fire that will consume the enemies of God. (Hebrews 10:26–27)

The original recipients of Hebrews were Jewish converts facing immense pressure to abandon Christianity. Judaism was officially recognized by Rome with legal protections, while Christianity was considered a dangerous sect. Their families, their culture, their government—all pressured them to return to the temple system, to the familiar comfort of the priesthood.

The author's warning addresses those tempted to apostatize: don't give up on Christ. The sin in view here is not a momentary stumble—not stealing once in a moment of weakness. This is deliberate, conscious, sustained rejection of the truth you've received. It's trampling on the Son of God, treating His blood as unholy, and insulting the Spirit of grace.

When you deliberately persist in rejecting Christ after knowing the truth, there remains no sacrifice for sins. Only judgment awaits.

**The Antibiotic Illustration:** Just as antibiotics work best when they hit the disease early and hard—before the pathogen builds resistance—so too does sin become more entrenched the longer we deliberately indulge it. Hit temptation early. Don't let it establish a foothold.

## The Faithful Persevere: Three Reasons to Hold On (10:32–38)

### Remember Your First Love (10:32–34)

> Remember those earlier days after you had received the light, when you stood your ground in a great contest in the face of suffering. (Hebrews 10:32)

Think back to when you first became a Christian. Remember that joy, that assurance, that relief when doubt finally lifted. You were willing to stand up and be counted. Perhaps you were baptized. Perhaps you gave a testimony.

What you did then wasn't based on emotion alone—it was a rational choice. You understood what you were signing up for. You knew Christianity meant leaving the world behind. You knew it meant submission to the Lord of the universe. You knew it meant following a suffering servant. So what has really changed?

Most people don't abandon their faith because of intellectual challenges to Christianity. They abandon it because they want to keep swearing, because they're tired of the struggle. They want the three G's: girls, games, and glory. Don't throw away your faith for those things. Just a little bit more to go.

### We Share the Same Faith as the Ancients (11:1–2)

> Now faith is being sure of what we hope for and certain of what we do not see. This is what the ancients were commended for. (Hebrews 11:1–2)

You are surrounded by a great cloud of witnesses—Abraham, Isaac, Jacob, Joseph, Moses, even Rahab the prostitute. These weren't perfect people. They had problems. They sinned. But one thing defined them: they stayed loyal to God. They trusted Him when He made impossible promises. They trusted Him through devastating suffering. They trusted Him even when facing death.

These ancients are now witnesses to us, showing us that struggle is normal. Every one of them suffered, and they're okay. The problem isn't that you're struggling—the problem is when you stop struggling, when apathy sets in. Apathy is death. Struggle is life.

Consider where you stand: you haven't even shed blood for your faith.

> In your struggle against sin, you have not yet resisted to the point of shedding your blood. (Hebrews 12:4)

Be honest. You've never been spat upon for being Christian. You've never been imprisoned for your faith. You've never had property confiscated. You probably haven't even been called names for following Jesus. Yet you're terrified of what your friends will think if you mention you're Christian. You're afraid of being ostracized if you say no to an illegal CD or walk away from gossip. Frightened they'll reject you if you invite them to church.

Why are you so afraid? You haven't shed blood yet. This fear is unreasonable. You live in a tolerant country where your friends will accept you if you say you're Christian and talk about faith in a respectful way.

If you can't stand up now, in the safety of youth, how will you stand when harder times come? When you do stand—when you endure even a little suffering—you become one of those witnesses. And when suffering comes, don't view it as martyrdom. View it as discipline.

### We Experience the Sonship of God (12:5–13)

> My son, do not make light of the Lord's discipline, and do not lose heart when he rebukes you, because the Lord disciplines those he loves, and he punishes everyone he accepts as a son. (Hebrews 12:5–6)

Standing in the midst of suffering and persecution isn't punishment—it's discipline. God is treating you as His sons.

Think of your HSC preparation. The struggle is painful now, but what you're gaining will last a lifetime. The study habits you're building, the exam techniques you're learning, the cramming skills you're developing—these will serve you through university and beyond. The actual subject matter? You'll forget that soon enough. But the life skills? Those remain.

You have something those who left in Year 10 don't have: discipline, prioritization, the ability to make quick decisions about what matters. You're more employable, more skilled, more likely to succeed. And during this trial, you pull together, help each other through. You hate it now, but by next year, you'll recognize it as one of the best things you've ever done.

> No discipline seems pleasant at the time, but painful. Later on, however, it produces a harvest of righteousness and peace for those who have been trained by it. (Hebrews 12:11)

Strengthen your feeble arms and weak knees. Man up. Be brave. Be daring.

You've shown incredible courage in your proposals and your work here. I'm amazed you've done it. Now apply that same courage to your Christian life. Work through obstacles and impediments without whining. Take control of the situation. Be strong. Stand up to the moment.

Where are you weak? Where is temptation strongest? You know those areas. There will be opportunities to pray about them. But strengthen yourself. Say no to ungodliness. Deny Christ in nothing. Run this race with everything you've got.

## Can We Be Assured? (Three Clarifications)

### The Warning Is Real—Yet Hypothetical

Don't misunderstand. The warnings in Hebrews are real. They're not empty threats. But just as warning labels on cigarettes are real without compelling you to smoke, so these warnings stand without necessarily describing your fate.

God will hold on to you.

### Personal Assurance Is Possible

While I cannot vouch for anyone else's salvation, you can be personally assured of your own. This assurance doesn't rest on your feelings or your fluctuating spiritual experience. Some of you will go on to bigger and better things next year—join campus ministries, thrive spiritually. Others may struggle. You may feel like you're falling away. But assurance rests not on how you're feeling, but on faith in Christ. He is powerful. He will hold you firm to the end.

Everyone struggles. Those who seem to be doing well will struggle when they enter the workforce. Don't assume struggle means you're falling away.

### Warnings Are Instruments of Our Perseverance

These warnings are not meant to make life hopeless. They're not here to guilt you and tear you down. Rather, they're the very instruments God uses to encourage our perseverance.

We will continue to sin—none of us is sinless. One sin is not the end of the story. But these warnings are there to keep us from the kind of deliberate, sustained rejection of Christ that leads to judgment.

The fact that these warnings are there, and that you listen to them, means you will be saved.

## The Preservation of God

Here's the ultimate comfort: God doesn't just call you to persevere and leave you to your own strength. God Himself preserves you.

In the darkest times of your life, when everything else has failed, when depression settles in and hope seems gone, God still loves you. He cares. He cared for you on the cross, when He was whipped and hung to die. He cared for you when you were a sinner, when you deserved nothing. He's not going to change His mind now. He will persevere even when you think of not.

**The Steven Bradbury Story:** Steven Bradbury won gold in short-track speed skating at the 2002 Winter Olympics—making Australia the only Southern Hemisphere country to win Winter Olympic gold. But Bradbury's path was extraordinary.

At the quarterfinals, he qualified only because another skater was disqualified. At the semifinals, he was off the pace when the competitors ahead crashed, and he advanced. At the finals, he was somewhere in the back of the pack when everyone in front of him crashed. He crossed the finish line first.

After the race, he was asked about his victory. He said: "Obviously I wasn't the fastest skater. I don't think I'll take the medal as the minute-and-a-half of the race I actually won. I'll take it as the last decade of the hard slog I put in."

This is what perseverance is about. Who finishes matters more than how fast you're going. Bradbury had endured terrible suffering—skaters' blades had sliced through his leg, he'd lost four liters of blood, he'd broken his neck in injury. Many times he was tempted to quit, thinking he'd never medal anyway. But he kept his eyes fixed on the end line. He didn't worry about what others were doing. He just kept running.

You're in the HSC of life, which takes about eighty years to run. Last year was assessment after assessment, holiday after holiday with no real break. You sat exhausted in your breaks, guilt-laden because you weren't studying. You longed for final rest. Then came that last minute of the last exam, and you put your pens down. How good will it be at the end?

Don't give up now.

Fix your eyes on Jesus. He is the better revelation, the better man. He offers a better rest. He is better than all the priests. Today, He offers you a better assurance.

Jesus is better. And He will hold you to the end.
