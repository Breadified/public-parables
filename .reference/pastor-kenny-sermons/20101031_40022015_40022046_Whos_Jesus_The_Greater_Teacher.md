# Matthew 22:15-46

**Title:** Who's Jesus? The Greater Teacher
**Date:** October 31, 2010
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction: What Makes a Great Teacher?

When we think of truly great teachers, we recognize that their fruit can only be seen years down the track. A great teacher leaves a lasting impact on their students' lives—not just in the classroom, but in how they shape who their students become.

In this passage, Jesus arrives as a new teacher on the scene, and inevitably, He is tested. The Jewish religious establishment—three key groups—each attempts to trap Him with difficult questions. But Jesus, the Greater Teacher, handles every test with wisdom and turns each challenge into a teaching moment.

## The Herodians: Politics and Allegiance (22:15-22)

### The Setup

The Herodians were a political party aligned with King Herod and, by extension, with Rome. Though Jewish, they had pragmatically decided that friendship with Rome was politically efficient, even if the broader Jewish population resented Roman occupation.

> Then the Pharisees went out and laid plans to trap him in his words. They sent their disciples to him along with the Herodians. "Teacher," they said, "we know you are a man of integrity and that you teach the way of God in accordance with the truth. You aren't swayed by men, because you pay no attention to who they are. Tell us then, what is your opinion? Is it right to pay taxes to Caesar or not?" (Matthew 22:15-17)

### The Trap

On the surface, their compliment seems genuine—they acknowledge Jesus as a teacher of integrity who follows truth rather than public opinion. But this is flattery designed to set a trap.

The question itself is ingenious: "Should we pay taxes to Caesar?" There's no good answer. If Jesus says "yes," He appears to betray God. If He says "no," He commits treason against Rome—an executable offense. Either way, they believe they have Him trapped.

### Jesus's Response

But Jesus knows their evil intent and responds with masterful wisdom:

> Jesus said, "You hypocrites, why are you trying to trap me? Show me the coin used for paying the tax." They brought him a denarius, and he asked them, "Whose portrait is this? And whose inscription?" "Caesar's," they replied. Then he said to them, "Give to Caesar what is Caesar's, and to God what is God's." (Matthew 22:18-21)

The brilliance of this answer lies in understanding ownership. The coin bears Caesar's image, so it belongs to Caesar—give it back to him. But who bears God's image? We do. We are made in the image of God. Therefore, give yourself back to God.

The answer isn't "obey God or obey authorities." It's "obey both." Our ultimate allegiance belongs to God, and we demonstrate that allegiance by respecting the authorities He has established. This is the principle Paul outlines in Romans 13—God has instituted these authorities, and submission to them is part of our obedience to Him.

### Understanding Christian Citizenship

Here's something critical to understand about Christian allegiance: it works differently than other worldviews. Our kingdom is not of this world but belongs to the age to come. Because we don't depend on any earthly government for our ultimate security or identity, we can accept any government's rule.

Our goal isn't to convert the Australian government to Christianity. Our goal is to convert Australians to be Christian. We want politicians to be Christians, but we don't need governments to be Christian. We want judges to be Christians, but the judicial system doesn't need to be Christian. We want legislators to be Christians, but Parliament doesn't need to be Christian.

As Christians, we follow the law of the land—not just when it's convenient, but consistently. We can maintain the separation of church and state without compromising our allegiance to Jesus.

### The Problem We Face Today

The real problem is that we often disrespect both God and earthly authority simultaneously. Like the Herodians, we play them off against each other and follow neither—serving only our own interests while using both institutions to justify ourselves.

Consider these examples:

**Taxes:** We minimize what we owe the state as much as possible—stretching boundaries, even bending rules—so we can give more to God and support Christian ministries. But then when we calculate how much to give at church, we say, "I'd better hold back in case I owe more taxes." In the end, the money goes neither to the state nor to the church—it goes to ourselves.

**Speed limits:** We might exceed the speed limit if we're heading to a Christian event (for God), but if we're just running late to an ordinary meeting, we follow the rules (for the state). We end up creating our own rules and dishonoring both authorities.

**Family and church:** For young people especially, there's often conflict between parental authority and church commitments. Sometimes we say we can't attend a family event because of church, or we can't go to church because of family obligations. But often, neither is actually the issue—it's laziness, poor organization, or social pressure. We dishonor both authority figures and then blame the other for it.

Don't do this. Don't pit God and earthly authority against each other. Don't try to trick God.

## The Sadducees: Resurrection and Eternal Life (22:23-33)

### Who Were the Sadducees?

The Sadducees appear only about twelve times in the New Testament, but they were a significant group. They denied the resurrection of the dead entirely—they believed that when you die, your soul perishes with your body. There's a reason for their name: "the Sadducees were sad, you see!" (because they didn't believe in the resurrection).

Why did they deny resurrection? Because they accepted only the Pentateuch (the first five books of Moses) as authoritative Scripture. They rejected the later writings where resurrection is clearly taught—Isaiah 26:19, Daniel 12:2, Job 19:23-27, and the Psalms all proclaim a future resurrection. Without that scriptural foundation in their accepted canon, they denied it altogether.

### The Trap

The Sadducees approach Jesus with a scenario based on Old Testament law:

> "Teacher," they said, "Moses told us that if a man dies without having children, his brother must marry the widow and have children for him. Now there were seven brothers among us. The first one married and died, and since he had no children, he left his wife to his brother. The same thing happened to the second and third brother, right on down to the seventh. Finally, the woman died. Now then, at the resurrection, whose wife will she be of the seven, since all of them were married to her?" (Matthew 22:24-28)

This scenario is based on the levirate law in Deuteronomy 25:5-6, which required a brother to marry his deceased brother's widow to preserve the family line. It's a clever trap: if you believe in resurrection, how could this possibly work? It would be chaotic and absurd.

### Jesus's Reply

> Jesus replied, "You are in error because you do not know the Scriptures or the power of God. At the resurrection people will neither marry nor be given in marriage; they will be like the angels in heaven. But about the resurrection of the dead—have you not read what God said to you, 'I am the God of Abraham, the God of Isaac, and the God of Jacob'? He is not the God of the dead but of the living." (Matthew 22:29-32)

Jesus gives two answers:

**First:** The rules of heaven operate differently than the rules of earth. There is no marriage in the resurrection. No marriage means no sexual relations, and therefore no children are born. This actually underscores how important evangelism is in this lifetime—we can't make new people in heaven.

**Second:** Jesus quotes directly from the Pentateuch itself—from Exodus 3:6. Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob had been dead for centuries when God spoke to Moses, yet God said, "I am the God of Abraham, the God of Isaac, and the God of Jacob"—not "I *was* the God of these men" but "I *am*." God doesn't speak in past tense about those who have ceased to exist. He is the God of the living, which means Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob are living in God's presence.

The Sadducees had no answer. The crowds were astonished at His teaching.

### Living Like the Sadducees Today

The question for us is: do we live as if the Sadducees were right? Do we live as if heaven operates by earth's rules?

We pursue diplomas, degrees, promotions, titles—rabbi, doctor, reverend. We build careers assuming we can be professional in heaven. But there are no careers in heaven. If the rules of the world to come will be completely different, if the order will be reinvented—would we really invest so much energy in whatever obsession currently grabs us? Our studies, our work, our hobbies, our gadgets, our clothes, our shopping. Everything we accumulate becomes junk. I go to garage sales regularly. I look at what's left at the curb. It's all junk.

But here's the flip side, and it's beautiful: **He is God of the living, not the dead.** There is a resurrection to live for. This life is not all there is.

I know that when I challenge you to give up your lives, your time, your money, your social media, your social networks, your holidays, your fancy things—I feel bad asking you for these sacrifices. I love you, and I don't want you to feel like you're missing out. But you're not missing out. Your hard work isn't wasted. Because there is a resurrection, everything you do in service to God goes toward something eternal. It is remembered by the God of the living and will be celebrated by the living in the presence of God forever.

## The Pharisees: The Greatest Commandment (22:34-40)

### The Question

> Hearing that Jesus had silenced the Sadducees, the Pharisees got together. One of them, an expert in the law, tested him with this question: "Teacher, which is the greatest commandment in the Law?" (Matthew 22:34-36)

The Pharisees were big on law—very big. They had countless commentaries analyzing what every law meant and how it applied. They studied the Talmud, the Mishnah, and the Gemara—centuries of rabbinic opinions. Much like we might collect podcasts from our favorite Bible teachers, the Pharisees collected commentaries.

One thing they debated constantly was the hierarchy of commands. Which is most important? If two commands seem to conflict, which takes precedence? This is a legitimate interpretive question, but it's also a trap. They're asking Jesus to rank the commandments—to say this one matters more than that one.

It's like asking, "Who's your favorite child?" There's no right answer. However you respond, you lose. So Jesus is trapped again, right?

### Jesus's Answer

> Jesus replied: "Love the Lord your God with all your heart and with all your soul and with all your mind.' This is the first and greatest commandment. And the second is like it: 'Love your neighbor as yourself.' All the Law and the Prophets hang on these two commandments." (Matthew 22:37-40)

Jesus gives two commandments, but notice the crucial word: the second is "like" the first. They are of the same nature. Moreover, "all the Law and the Prophets hang on these two commandments"—they're not in tension. They're not in hierarchy. They're consistent with each other. Every commandment in Scripture flows from and is consistent with these two.

There's actually no hierarchy here because there's no contradiction. All of Scripture speaks to the same reality: we are to love God completely and love our neighbor as ourselves. Read them together and they don't contradict each other. No part of Scripture can be read in a way that is repugnant to another part of Scripture—it's all unified around this principle of love.

If you needed one overarching principle for simplicity, Jesus is saying it's love. But notice: these aren't two separate things. Loving God is loving each other. Matthew 25:35-37 makes this crystal clear—when you serve the hungry, the thirsty, the stranger, the sick, the prisoner, you're serving Jesus Himself. Love of God and love of neighbor are inseparable.

### A Critical Qualification

But here's what we must understand: **love is God's definition, not ours.**

We are constantly tempted to redefine love in our own terms. We want to call premarital sex love. We want to call adultery love. We want to call homosexuality love. "How can something that feels so good not be love?" we ask.

I had a young man in my Scripture class at Cherrybrook Tech who came to a difficult realization. He said that heaven would only be heaven if his non-Christian friends were there. When I explained that the Bible teaches non-Christians face judgment, not heaven, he decided he didn't want to be a Christian anymore because that wasn't the heaven he signed up for. I asked him: "Whose definition of heaven is that?" He admitted it was his own.

We are constantly tempted to define heaven, love, and morality in our own terms—definitions that suit us. But God has already revealed His definitions in Scripture. We don't get to rewrite them.

### A Final Note on the Pharisees

There's one more thing to notice: the Pharisees were so focused on getting the law right—all the detailed rules about what constitutes work, what violates the Sabbath—that they lost sight of the central principle. Love God. Love your neighbor. And that principle-centered life would have prevented them from conspiring to kill the very Teacher who embodied that love perfectly.

## Jesus: The Greater Teacher (22:41-46; 23:1-12)

### The Final Question

Now it's Jesus's turn. He turns the tables and asks them a question:

> While the Pharisees were gathered together, Jesus asked them, "What do you think about the Christ? Whose son is he?" "The son of David," they replied. He said to them, "How is it then that David, speaking by the Spirit, calls him 'Lord'? For he says, 'The Lord said to my Lord: Sit at my right hand until I put your enemies under your feet.' If then David calls him 'Lord,' how can he be his son?" No one could say a word in reply, and from that day on no one dared to ask him any more questions. (Matthew 22:41-46)

The Messiah is "the son of David"—greater than David, but in David's line. Yet David himself, moved by the Spirit, calls this coming Messiah "Lord." How can the son be greater than his father? There's only one answer: the Greater Teacher is God Himself.

Silence. From that day on, no one dared ask Jesus any more questions. He had answered every test with perfect wisdom.

### The Character of the True Teacher

> Then Jesus said to the crowds and to his disciples: "The teachers of the law and the Pharisees sit in Moses' seat. So you must obey them and do everything they tell you. But do not do what they do, for they do not practice what they preach. They tie up heavy loads and put them on men's shoulders, but they themselves are not willing to lift a finger to move them. Everything they do is done for men to see: they make their phylacteries wide and the tassels on their garments long; they love the place of honor at banquets and the most important seats in the synagogues; they love to be greeted in the marketplaces and to have men call them 'Rabbi.' But you are not to be called 'Rabbi,' for you have only one Master and you are all brothers. And do not call anyone on earth 'father,' for you have one Father, and he is in heaven. Nor are you to be called 'teacher,' for you have one Teacher, the Christ. The greatest among you will be your servant. For whoever exalts himself will be humbled, and whoever humbles himself will be exalted." (Matthew 23:1-12)

The false teachers lay heavy burdens on others but won't lift a finger to help. They do everything for show. They love status and recognition.

But Jesus—the True Teacher—is completely different. He practices what He preaches. He doesn't just speak about burden-bearing; He actively lifts people's burdens. In fact, He lifted a cross to bear our burden. He seeks no honor, demands no titles, and serves with complete humility.

## Conclusion: Listen to the Greater Teacher

Three groups tested Jesus:

**The Herodians** wanted us to pit obedience to law against obedience to God. Jesus shows us we obey both.

**The Sadducees** lived as if there's no resurrection, no eternity, no ultimate accountability. Jesus reminds us that He is God of the living, and our work echoes into eternity.

**The Pharisees** reduced faith to rules and forgot the central principle: love God, love your neighbor. Jesus calls us back to that unified, principle-centered life.

There's a beautiful scene in the film *Mr. Holland's Opus*. At Mr. Holland's retirement, one of his former students stands up and says:

> "Mr. Holland had a profound influence on my life and on a lot of lives I know. But I have a feeling that he considers a great part of his own life misspent. Rumor had it he was always working on this symphony of his—this was going to make him famous, rich, probably both. But Mr. Holland isn't rich and he isn't famous, at least not outside of our little town. So it might be easy for him to think himself a failure. But he would be wrong, because I think that he's achieved a success far beyond riches and fame. Look around you. There is not a life in this room that you have not touched, and each of us is a better person because of you. We are your symphony, Mr. Holland. We are the melodies and the notes of your opus. We are the music of your life."

That's what it means to listen to the Greater Teacher. That's what it means to serve with eternity in view. You are the work of Christ. You are His opus. Let Him shape you. Let Him teach you. And watch what He will do with your life—not in terms of fame or fortune, but in terms of eternal impact.
