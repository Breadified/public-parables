# Romans 10:1-21

**Title:** The Clarity of Scripture
**Date:** 17 March 2012
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction: The Question of Biblical Clarity

We live in a time when the clarity of Scripture itself is under question. This month we celebrate the Reformation—that pivotal moment on 31 October 1517 when Martin Luther nailed his 95 theses to the church door, calling the medieval church back to its foundations: Grace Alone, Bible Alone, Christ Alone, and Faith Alone.

Yet even within the church today, voices question whether Scripture speaks clearly to our modern questions. Some Christian scholars argue that biblical texts cannot be read as speaking straightforwardly to contemporary issues. They suggest the Bible is obscured—hidden beneath layers of ancient storytelling and present-day dogmatism—with a gap existing between the biblical text and modern Christians that requires constant reinterpretation.

This wasn't unique to contemporary scholars. The medieval church held that Scripture was so difficult to understand that only those in authority—bishops and popes—could be trusted to read and interpret it. They believed Scripture and Sacred Tradition had to be read together, each illuminating the other.

The question is real and personal: When a Bible study becomes controversial and no one can agree? When our daily devotion yields no insight? When we realize the Bible is such a human document, written in ancient cultural contexts vastly different from our own? Is the Bible truly clear, or do we need something more to interpret it?

Scripture addresses this directly, and the answer may surprise you.

## Three False Gaps (and Why They Don't Hold)

### Gap 1: The Transcendence Gap

**The Objection**

The first objection claims that God is transcendent—infinite, beyond time and space, beyond words and language. Human language simply cannot express the reality of God and His purposes without breaking at the seams. How can finite human words describe an infinite God? Isn't insisting on Scripture's clarity a form of reductionism, recasting God within the dimensions of His own creation?

**God Is an Effective Communicator**

But Scripture testifies to something different. In Romans 10, Paul writes:

> The word is near you; it is in your mouth and in your heart, that is, the word of faith we are proclaiming. (Romans 10:8)

Paul is quoting Deuteronomy 30, originally spoken to nomadic Hebrews wandering the desert in the 13th century:

> Now what I am commanding you today is not too difficult for you or beyond your reach. It is not up in heaven, so that you have to ask, "Who will ascend into heaven to get it and proclaim it to us?" Nor is it beyond the sea, so that you have to ask, "Who will cross the sea to get it and proclaim it to us?" No, the word is very near you; it is in your mouth and in your heart so you may obey it. (Deuteronomy 30:11-14)

The original context asks: who will bring God's word down from heaven? Who will bring it up from the depths? These represent human efforts to reach God, to get His attention, to make Him speak to us.

But Christ has done what we cannot do. He came down from heaven. He was raised from the dead. **Christ has come down, taken the word of God, and translated it for us.** The Word is now near you—in your mouth, in your heart. No human effort required. Not in human terms, but in God's terms.

This is crucial: God is the initiator of revelation. Language isn't our desperate attempt to reach God (which would fail the transcendence test). Language is God's initiative. He created it, gave it vocabulary and grammar and syntax—all designed to communicate with us. God made language for accommodation to us.

A parent doesn't speak to a child as to another adult. Parents use simple words, gestures, and patience. God does the same. Language fulfills its function because God designed it to. Therefore, we can read Scripture plainly and trust what it says.

**Receiving God's Word on God's Terms**

Because Scripture is clear, we can obey it, confess it, believe it, and trust it. But we often resist God's terms:

- When we demand that God appear to us personally today so we'll know He's real, we're repeating the error of Romans 10—trying to bring Christ down again. God has communicated on His own terms. He revealed Himself through Christ 2,000 years ago. This is the final revelation (as Hebrews 1 emphasizes). His life, deeds, death, and resurrection are written down for us. Why do we insist on Him appearing personally to billions of people today? That's insisting on our terms.

- When we're unsatisfied with Scripture alone and demand an extra level of interpretation—some mystical "word of the Spirit" beyond the plain reading—we're also resisting God's terms. Bible study doesn't always yield an "aha moment." Sermons don't always inspire. The plain reading of Scripture is often just... plain. And that's acceptable. God's terms of engagement don't require every encounter with His Word to produce shivers or tears.

We must receive God's words on God's terms, not recast them in our own.

### Gap 2: The Cultural Gap

**The Objection**

How can the ancient world of subsistence agriculture possibly compare to our modern capitalist, urban, digital society? Languages changed, customs transformed, social organization evolved. How can we apply ancient commands to modern situations?

Some scholars, like Gordon Fee in *How to Read the Bible for All Its Worth*, propose that:

> Whenever we share comparable particulars (similar specific life situations) with the first century, God's word to us is the same as His word to them. If we don't share the same cultural situation, God's word to us is different. We may translate it, but sometimes we may not. It simply may no longer apply to us.

This view suggests that because we don't understand ancient marriage practices, slavery, or cultural contexts, biblical commands about these matters were addressed only to past epochs. They were right then, but we need to "re-appropriate" them for today. The Bible didn't have modern science or categories like "sexual orientation," so how can it speak to these issues?

**Humans Are the Same Everywhere**

Yet when we examine Scripture itself, we find something remarkable: examples where culture is vastly different but God's word is identical, and examples where culture is similar but God's application changes.

**Different Culture, Same Application:**

Deuteronomy speaks to nomadic Hebrews in the 13th-century desert, written in Aramaic, speaking to a Bronze Age people. Romans addresses Hellenistic Jews and Gentiles 1,400 years later, in an iron-age urban city. They differ in language, custom, dress, education, and religious background. Yet the application is identical. What God said about His word being near them in the desert remains true for them in the city.

Similarly, Genesis establishes that marriage is male and female in creation itself. This transcends all cultural boundaries.

**Same Culture, Different Application:**

Conversely, the Jewish culture in the first century shared significant commonalities with Leviticus regarding animal sacrifice and food laws. Yet Hebrews tells Jewish readers to abandon animal sacrifices entirely. Jesus declares all foods clean, contradicting Old Testament food laws. If similar cultural situation were the key factor, why didn't Jesus simply reinforce the Old Testament pattern?

Culture is a factor in biblical interpretation, but perhaps the least important one.

**Our Cultural Arrogance**

Here's the uncomfortable truth: we tend toward "cultural chauvinism." We believe that because something is old—older than us—it must be inferior, requiring reinterpretation to be "relevant" today. We're bigots about history.

Yet look at our own culture:
- Highest rates of debt
- Highest rates of mental illness
- Highest rates of sexually transmitted diseases
- Highest suicide rates
- We're confused about basic distinctions like gender itself

And yet we convince ourselves our culture is superior to previous generations? We share with the biblical writers the same theological and moral world created by God. We face the same temptations, struggles, and joys. We have access to the same salvation, the same Spirit, the same human nature, and the same challenge to live worthily of our calling.

**There is no hermeneutical gap in the way it's typically spoken of.** Our differences from the original readers are minor. In every sense that matters, we're in the same situation they were.

How should we use cultural knowledge then? At the level of exegesis—understanding what the author communicated to his original audience. We study the language, idioms, historical and geographical context, and cultural dynamics (like Jewish-Gentile relationships). This helps us understand what is actually being said. But once we determine what it says to them, it applies to us also.

### Gap 3: The Complexity Gap

**The Objection**

Perhaps the real issue isn't culture but complexity. We lack information the original readers possessed. The Dark Ages caused knowledge loss. The Library of Alexandria burned. We don't fully understand how biblical theology works—how Christ is the end of the law, when we follow Old Testament commands, when we reappropriate them. The Bible is complex.

This complexity is reflected in:
- Endless theological books and commentaries, yet scholars still can't reach consensus
- Our many denominations, each interpreting texts differently
- Debates within churches about what passages actually mean

**Clarity Means Possible, Not Easy**

But Romans 10 doesn't claim the word is "not difficult." It says the word is "not TOO difficult." We've adopted a modern misconception—that God should speak to us in five minutes while we're bleary-eyed first thing in the morning, perhaps sitting on the toilet, and expecting revelation.

The philosopher Kevin Vanhoozer explains it well:

> The clarity of Scripture means that understanding is possible, not that it is easy. Redeeming the text does not mean reconciling all interpretive conflicts. Understanding does not mean that we will know everything there is to know about the text, but that we will know enough to be able, and responsible, to respond to its subject matter. The clarity of Scripture is not a matter of its obviousness so much as its efficacy; the Bible is clear enough to render its communicative action effective.

Scripture is not a riddle, not obtuse, not a trick, not a mystery. It's all there plainly. It just requires work.

**Understanding Requires Discipline**

- Comprehension requires work
- Looking up references, allowing clearer passages to illuminate less clear ones, requires work
- Making logical connections requires work
- Sometimes thinking outside our presuppositions requires work
- Finding time to do all this requires work
- Learning basic comprehension skills and grammar requires work and training

This is why commentaries, the Christian community, and denominations have value. While not final arbiters of Scripture, they provide training and shortcuts—though we should understand how we arrived at answers from first principles.

The key to understanding all Scripture is this: **Tie it to Jesus.** He is the end of the law. This is what the Ethiopian eunuch learned when Philip explained Isaiah to him (Acts 8:30ff).

The promise is simple: **Do the hard work, and it will become clear over time.**

- Take more than five minutes with your Bible studies
- Perhaps consolidate daily readings into one substantial study session per week
- Read Scripture with others to accelerate understanding
- When teaching others, show them how to reach conclusions from Scripture, not just give them answers
- Consider studying Bible at some point in your life—particularly gain practical knowledge of Greek

**The three alleged gaps aren't really gaps at all. The gap is not between God and the Bible. The gap is not between time and culture. The gap is not in the text.**

## The Real Gap: The Moral Gap

**The Actual Problem**

The real gap we should worry about is not linguistic, cultural, or theological. **The problem is moral.**

Look at Israel's situation in Romans 10:

> Brothers, my heart's desire and prayer to God for the Israelites is that they may be saved. For I can testify about them that they are zealous for God, but their zeal is not based on knowledge. Since they did not know the righteousness that comes from God and sought to establish their own, they did not submit to God's righteousness. (Romans 10:1-3)

Is the problem understanding? Is it a hermeneutical gap? A language barrier? No. Paul continues:

> But not all the Israelites accepted the good news. For Isaiah says, "Lord, who has believed our message?" Did they not hear? Of course they did: "Their voice has gone out into all the earth, their words to the ends of the world." Did Israel not understand? First, Moses says, "I will make you envious by those who are not a nation; I will make you angry by a nation that has no understanding." (Romans 10:16-19)

Israel heard. Israel understood. Their problem was disobedience—they were "a disobedient and obstinate people" (Romans 10:21). **The problem with Israel was not stupidity but obstinacy. A moral gap.**

**A Universal, Timeless Problem**

This moral gap is universal and transcends all cultural boundaries:
- The Semites were sinful 4,000 years ago
- The Hebrews and Gentiles were sinful 2,000 years ago
- This moral gap has no gender barrier—women are equally sinful as men
- We are sinful in the present age

**Why We Fail to Understand**

The scholar Kevin Vanhoozer writes: "We humans know in part, through the glass of language, darkly—not because of some defect in language but because of our unseeing eyes and unclean lips."

The Danish theologian Søren Kierkegaard offers a penetrating observation:

> Suppose it was said in the New Testament that it is God's will that every man should have 100,000 dollars. Do you think there would be any question of a commentary? Would everyone not rather say, "It's easy enough to understand; there's no need of a commentary. Let us for heaven's sake keep clear of commentaries—they could perhaps make it doubtful whether it is really as it is written"? But we prefer it to be doubtful, so we must have commentaries and professors and more commentaries. For it is not a case of risking that it may become doubtful to us—we really wish it to be doubtful, and we have a tiny hope that the commentaries may make it so.

In other words, **we refuse to believe Scripture.** When Scripture aligns with our desires, it's crystal clear. When it challenges us, suddenly it becomes obscure, requiring endless commentary and reinterpretation.

Modern example: In 1 Corinthians 6:9, the list of those who won't inherit God's kingdom is plain. Non-Christian reporters read it and ask, "What's the fuss about?" Only Christians seem to find issues—because it challenges us personally.

The contemporary debate over sexuality illustrates this perfectly. The underlying argument often amounts to: "I want to be and do what I want to do. I have a right to be happy." But where is that in the Bible? Scripture is about restraining sinful desires, not indulging them. Yet instead of repenting, we create complicated hermeneutical frameworks to make Scripture mean something it doesn't say.

**The Solution: Humility and Repentance**

God speaks to this moral gap—this sin, this desire to ask "Did God really say?"—through His Word. **The only hermeneutic we need is faith**, combined with humility and repentance.

- Approach Scripture with humility, not arrogance
- Pray before reading, not after, with awareness of your sinful heart and capacity to rationalize
- Don't dismiss the text as obscure and then grant yourself freedom to decide whatever you want
- Never let your learning position you above the Word of God

When we genuinely repent and submit to Scripture, we understand more. I've witnessed this in my own life. I once argued whether it was bad to be rich, dividing people into categories: "country Australians who shouldn't be rich" versus "Asian immigrants for whom wealth is fine." I wasn't fighting the obscurity of the Word—I was fighting my own sin and reluctance to release my attachment to worldly riches.

## Conclusion: Scripture Is Clear

To approach Bible reading assuming a mighty chasm exists between us and Scripture is both unnecessary and unhelpful. It sidelines the fundamental truth that **the Bible is alive**, that God speaks by His Spirit through what He has spoken. His message is as alive today as it was then.

For evangelicals, our entire approach to Scripture should flow from this doctrine: because it is God who speaks, we trust and obey it. Without this conviction, we will always distort Scripture to make it comfortable. We'll find clever—or not-so-clever—hermeneutical excuses for avoiding what God is saying.

Martin Luther captured this beautifully:

> You are looking for a knot in a bulrush. These expressions do not make Scripture obscure, except that some people are fond of making obscurities where there are none. These are matters of grammar; the sentiment is expressed in figurative words that even schoolboys understand. But we are talking about doctrines, not about figures of speech. Even a ploughboy can read and interpret Scripture.

### You Can Confidently Know God

> Anyone who trusts in him will never be put to shame. For there is no difference between Jew and Gentile—the same Lord is Lord of all and richly blesses all who call on him; for, "Everyone who calls on the name of the Lord will be saved." (Romans 11-13)

Think of it this way: Personhood is obscure, yet it's normal. We don't need an exhaustive manual to know our friends. We know people through relationship, not complete information. Similarly, Scripture offers relational revelation, not exhaustive theological documentation. You can confidently know God through Scripture.

### You Can Confidently Teach Others

> How, then, can they call on the one they have not believed in? And how can they believe in the one of whom they have not heard? And how can they hear without someone preaching to them? And how can they preach unless they are sent? As it is written, "How beautiful are the feet of those who bring good news!" (Romans 10:14-15)

You have been given much: literacy, good schools, understanding, wisdom. Much is expected of you in return. Get out there. Bring this message to your youth groups. Go door-knocking. Show others not just the answers but how to find them in Scripture itself.

**The Bible itself is clearer than often claimed. The real question is not simply what the Bible says, but rather what we do with what it says.**
