# 2 Samuel 17:1–19:29

**Title:** The Downfall of a Nation
**Date:** 28 October 2023
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Ahithophel's Attack

When Absalom's rebellion threatens David's throne, Ahithophel presents a ruthless strategy: take twelve thousand men tonight, pursue the weary king, strike him down while his guard is vulnerable, and return victorious without harming the rest of the people. To Absalom and the elders of Israel, the plan seems flawless—direct, efficient, and guaranteed.

> "I would attack him while he is weary and weak. I would strike him with terror, and then all the people with him will flee. I would strike down only the king and bring all the people back to you."

But Absalom asks to hear another voice. He summons Hushai the Arkite, David's loyal spy planted in the rebellion, to weigh in on the counsel.

## Hushai's Counter-Strategy

Hushai knows his role. He counters that Ahithophel's advice, though strategically sound, underestimates David's military prowess. David is no ordinary fugitive—he and his men are seasoned fighters, fierce as a mother bear defending her cubs. Even now, David is likely hidden in a cave or fortified position. If he strikes first and kills even a few of Absalom's soldiers, panic will spread through the ranks. Men will flee in terror, not because Absalom's army is weak, but because they know David's reputation and the bravery of those loyal to him.

Instead, Hushai urges full mobilization. Gather all Israel from Dan to Beersheba—as numerous as sand upon the seashore. Let Absalom himself lead them into battle. This way, they will overwhelm David wherever he hides, falling upon him like dew covering the ground. Not a single soul will escape.

> "Let all Israel, from Dan to Beersheba—as numerous as the sand on the seashore—be gathered to you, with you yourself leading them into battle. Then we will attack him wherever he may be found, and we will fall on him as dew settles on the ground."

The difference is stark: Ahithophel proposes surgical precision; Hushai proposes overwhelming force. Absalom and his advisors prefer Hushai's vision. They choose full-scale war over a quick strike.

## The Divine Hand

> "Absalom and all the men of Israel said, 'The advice of Hushai the Arkite is better than that of Ahithophel.' For the Lord had determined to frustrate the good advice of Ahithophel in order to bring disaster on Absalom."

What appears to be a political misjudgment is actually the hand of God at work. The Lord frustrates Ahithophel's counsel—which would have succeeded—to ensure Absalom's downfall. The narrative reveals that human wisdom, no matter how clever, is subject to God's sovereign purposes.

## The Message Relayed

Hushai immediately sends word to David through the priests Zadok and Abiathar:

> "Do not spend the night at the fords in the wilderness; cross over without fail, or the king and all the people with him will be swallowed up."

The messengers—Jonathan and Ahimaaz—nearly get caught. A young man spots them, but a woman hides them in a well, covers the opening with cloth, and scatters grain to conceal their presence. When Absalom's soldiers arrive searching, she misdirects them, claiming the men crossed the brook. By nightfall, Jonathan and Ahimaaz emerge and rush to David with the warning. David and all his people cross the Jordan before dawn. Not one remains behind.

## Ahithophel's End

Meanwhile, Ahithophel recognizes the implications. His advice was rejected. The rebellion, which he believed would succeed under his strategy, now faces uncertain odds. Whether moved by pride, prescience, or sensitivity to the moment, he returns to his hometown, puts his house in order, and hangs himself.

> "When Ahithophel saw that his advice had not been followed, he saddled his donkey and set out for his house in his hometown. He put his house in order and then hanged himself."

## Refuge at Mahanaim

David reaches Mahanaim with his people. Absalom, meanwhile, crosses the Jordan with all the men of Israel. Absalom appoints Amasa as commander of his army in place of Joab and camps with his forces in the land of Gilead.

But here, in this moment of apparent triumph for the rebellion, unexpected mercy appears. When David arrives in Mahanaim, three men bring supplies: Shobi son of Nahash from Rabbah of the Ammonites, Makir son of Ammiel from Lo Debar, and Barzillai the Gileadite from Rogelim. They bring bedding, bowls, pottery, wheat, barley, flour, roasted grain, beans, lentils, honey, curds, sheep, and cheese from cows' milk.

> "The people have become exhausted and hungry and thirsty in the wilderness."

In David's lowest moment—fleeing his own son, leading exhausted refugees—God provides through the kindness of foreign allies. This is the God who does not judge by appearance or measure worth by human standards. He sustains the righteous even when they are stripped of power.

Human judgment looks at outward circumstances. We see weakness and assume defeat. We see strength and assume victory. But our God looks at the heart. He weighs our characters and our choices, and He calls us to do the same—to see beyond the surface, to judge justly, and to extend mercy even to those who have failed.
