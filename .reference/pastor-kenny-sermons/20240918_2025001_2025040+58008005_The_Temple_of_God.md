# Exodus 25:1-40; Hebrews 8:5, 9:1-15

**Title:** The Temple of God

**Date:** September 18, 2024

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## God's Desire to Dwell Among His People

The Israelites had been freed from slavery in Egypt—freed to obey God's laws and freed to fellowship with Him. Now God invites them into something remarkable: to build a sanctuary where He will dwell among them.

> Then have them make a sanctuary for me, and I will dwell among them. Make this tabernacle and all its furnishings exactly like the pattern I will show you. (Exodus 25:8-9)

The word "sanctuary" (*qadesh*) means "holy." The "tabernacle" is literally a tent—portable for their journey through the wilderness. This is significant: God's desire to meet with His people is so strong that He travels with them.

## The Purpose of the Furniture

Every piece of furniture in the tabernacle points to one essential truth: **God wants to meet with His people.**

> There, above the cover between the two cherubim that are over the ark of the covenant law, I will meet with you and give you all my commands for the Israelites. (Exodus 25:22)

Consider what the Israelites had just experienced. They'd fled Egypt so hastily they couldn't even finish their meal—had to pack it in takeaway boxes and run. For generations, they'd been enslaved, separated from God. Now, finally, they have the chance to know Him again. The furniture represents this deep longing for fellowship, for face-to-face relationship with their God.

### The Ark of the Covenant

> Have them make an ark of acacia wood—two and a half cubits long, a cubit and a half wide, and a cubit and a half high. (Exodus 25:10)

The ark is a vessel, made of acacia wood and overlaid with pure gold. Inside it would be placed the tablets of the covenant law. The dimensions are given in cubits—an ancient measurement based on the length of a human forearm, a unit that harks back to measuring the world by the human body itself.

The ark has rings on its sides so it can be carried, keeping it portable for the wilderness journey. This design choice matters—when the ark was later carried in a cart (instead of by these prescribed poles), tragedy struck. There is a purpose to how God's presence is treated.

The cover atop the ark—the atonement cover—is of pure gold, with two golden cherubim (winged heavenly creatures) facing each other, their wings overshadowing the cover. This is where God promises to meet His people and speak His commands to them.

### The Table

> Make a table of acacia wood—two cubits long, a cubit wide and a cubit and a half high. Overlay it with pure gold. (Exodus 25:23-24)

Like the ark, it's made of acacia wood and overlaid with pure gold. It too has rings and poles for carrying—it's portable. On this table sits the "bread of the Presence," also called showbread or the "bread of the face." The image is intimate: to eat face to face. God and His people eating together, sharing a meal in His presence. It recalls the manna God provided in the wilderness—His daily sustenance for His people.

### The Lampstand

> Make a lampstand of pure gold. (Exodus 25:31)

The lampstand is described with unusual detail: hammered from pure gold, with a central shaft and six branches extending from it. These branches bear cups shaped like almond flowers, with buds and blossoms—

> Six branches are to extend from the sides of the lampstand—three on one side and three on the other. Three cups shaped like almond flowers with buds and blossoms are to be on one branch, three on the next branch. (Exodus 25:32-33)

At first glance, it seems to be just for light—you'd want light when eating a meal. But the description is far too elaborate for a simple lamp. The language speaks of branches, buds, and flowers. This is not a lamp; **it is a tree**.

I believe the lampstand is a reference to the tree in the Garden of Eden. By including it here, God is reminding His people of more than just the exodus from Egypt. He's calling them to remember the very beginning—when He walked with mankind in the garden, in intimate fellowship. He's reversing history all the way back to creation.

After Adam and Eve sinned and ate the forbidden fruit, God drove them from paradise. Cherubim guarded the gate. No more walking together. No more God's presence. No more intimate communion. Sin had shattered that relationship.

Yet God does not abandon mankind. He takes the initiative to restore fellowship. And the furniture of the tabernacle speaks of this restoration: **God is reversing the curse. He's bringing His people home.**

## A Shadow of What Is to Come

But there's more to understand about this furniture. It's not merely a memory of Eden; it's also a shadow of the future.

> See that you make them according to the pattern shown you on the mountain. (Exodus 25:40)

The word "pattern" can mean a description—here are the instructions, now build it. But it more literally means "copy." The furniture itself is a copy of what is to come. Like a photograph or architectural blueprint, it points to something more glorious—an actual house with real presence, not just a tent.

The New Testament explains this:

> They serve at a sanctuary that is a copy and shadow of what is in heaven. This is why Moses was warned when he was about to build the tabernacle: "See to it that you make everything according to the pattern shown you on the mountain." (Hebrews 8:5)

The tabernacle blueprint becomes a tent. The tent eventually becomes a temple. And God dwells in His temple.

> Now the first covenant had regulations for worship and also an earthly sanctuary. A tabernacle was set up. In its first room were the lampstand and the table with its consecrated bread; this was called the Holy Place. Behind the second curtain was a room called the Most Holy Place, which had the golden altar of incense and the gold-covered ark of the covenant. This ark contained the gold jar of manna, Aaron's staff that had budded, and the stone tablets of the covenant. Above the ark were the cherubim of the Glory, overshadowing the atonement cover. (Hebrews 9:1-5)

Yet here's the ultimate reality: the temple itself was only a shadow. All of it—the furniture, the ceremonies, the sacrifices—pointed to something greater.

## Jesus: The True Tabernacle

> But when Christ came as high priest of the good things that are now already here, he went through the greater and more perfect tabernacle that is not made with human hands, that is to say, is not a part of this creation. He did not enter by means of the blood of goats and calves; but he entered the Most Holy Place once for all by his own blood, thus obtaining eternal redemption. (Hebrews 9:11-12)

The "greater and more perfect tabernacle" is not a building. It is Christ Himself. Jesus is the one in whom God's presence dwells.

> The Word became flesh and made his dwelling among us. We have seen his glory, the glory of the one and only Son, who came from the Father, full of grace and truth. (John 1:14)

Jesus entered heaven by His own blood—His sacrifice was complete, eternal, unrepeatable. While the old priests entered the tabernacle daily, yearly, offering sacrifices that could never fully cleanse the conscience, Christ entered once for all.

> How much more, then, will the blood of Christ, who through the eternal Spirit offered himself unblemished to God, cleanse our consciences from acts that lead to death, so that we may serve the living God! (Hebrews 9:14)

To enter the presence of God now requires purification by His blood—to be washed clean through what Christ accomplished on the cross. Just as you would remove your shoes before entering a fine home, or clean the mud from your feet before stepping inside, we come to God with consciences cleansed by Christ's sacrifice.

> For this reason Christ is the mediator of a new covenant, that those who are called may receive the promised eternal inheritance—now that he has died as a ransom to set them free from the sins committed under the first covenant. (Hebrews 9:15)

## Heaven: The Ultimate Home

Heaven is the ultimate reality toward which all the furniture pointed. On paper, we describe it as a place without sin, hurt, pain, depression, sickness, suffering, or death. But when we arrive, it will exceed our imagination. In heaven, Jesus will have recaptured not just Eden, but the entire cosmos—restored to what God always intended.

## The Church as God's House Today

We gather in a church building and call it "a house of God." But it is not the temple in the old sense. Jesus fulfilled that role completely. We no longer offer blood sacrifices on special days, nor do we try to bribe God with money, songs, or effort.

Yet it is a house of God **in the sense that wherever God's Word dwells, God dwells there.** The church building is not the only place you meet God this week. Your home, the library, a classroom, anywhere believers gather to read Scripture—these are places where God is present.

What we offer is no longer sacrifice, but our lives, serving each other continuously. When we sing, we sing for each other. When we give, we support the ministries here. When we speak, we encourage one another. All of it flows from God's love, and we respond by loving each other.

This church is a shadow—imperfect, facing real challenges—but we long to resemble the church of eternity. The furniture here serves a purpose: it enables us to meet with each other as we meet with God. The tables, the chairs, the platform—all designed for gathering. Many of you serve faithfully in setting up and maintaining these spaces. If you haven't started, please consider helping. You're never too young or too old. Stacking chairs, moving tables, arranging the space—these acts create the environment where community happens. Chat with someone while you serve.

## A Call to Come Home

Some of you feel today that you are far from God. Perhaps you've been doing things you know displease Him. You hear the Ten Commandments and feel the weight of breaking them. Maybe you're lonely, vulnerable, anxious about what's ahead.

Hear God's word to you today: **He calls you home to His presence.**

Just as the furniture of the tabernacle reminded the Israelites of Eden and pointed them toward God's future kingdom, let its familiarity call you home now. Like the prodigal son who remembered his father while sitting among the pigs, remember where you belong. Come home to rest, to comfort, to security. Be anxious no more.

Come home to Jesus.
