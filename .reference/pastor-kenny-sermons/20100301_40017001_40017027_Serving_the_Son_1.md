# Matthew 17:1-27

**Title:** Serving the Son
**Date:** 6 March 2010
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Morphin Time: Understanding Transformation

Just as the Mighty Morphin Power Rangers transform into something greater to serve a purpose, so Jesus demonstrates His glorious transformation—not to remain in glory, but to show us the true nature of that glory: humble, self-sacrificial service.

## 1. The Serving Son

### a. The Glorious Son (Matthew 16:27-17:8)

Jesus first speaks of His coming glory:

> "For the Son of Man is going to come in his Father's glory with his angels, and then he will reward each person according to what he has done. I tell you the truth, some who are standing here will not taste death before they see the Son of Man coming in his kingdom." (Matthew 16:27-28)

Then comes the Transfiguration:

> "After six days Jesus took with him Peter, James and John the brother of James, and led them up a high mountain by themselves. There he was transfigured before them. His face shone like the sun, and his clothes became as white as the light. Just then there appeared before them Moses and Elijah, talking with Jesus." (Matthew 17:1-3)

Peter, overwhelmed, offers to build shelters for each figure. Then a cloud envelops them, and a voice speaks:

> "This is my Son, whom I love; with him I am well pleased. Listen to him!" (Matthew 17:5)

The disciples fall terrified to the ground. Jesus touches them and says, "Get up. Don't be afraid." When they look up, they see only Jesus. As they descend, Jesus instructs them: "Don't tell anyone what you have seen, until the Son of Man has been raised from the dead." (Matthew 17:9)

**Parallels with Exodus 24-25:** This transfiguration echoes Israel's encounter with God on Mount Sinai:

- Both occur after six days (Exodus 24:16; Matthew 17:1)
- Three companions witness the divine glory (Exodus 24:9; Matthew 17:1)
- They stand on a mountain (Exodus 24:12-13)
- A cloud covers the mountain with God's glory (Exodus 24:15-16)
- A voice speaks from heaven (Exodus 24:16; Matthew 17:5)
- The witnesses experience terror (Exodus 24:17; Matthew 17:6)

**But the differences reveal Jesus's uniqueness:**

- This is not merely a glimpse of God's glory—this IS the Son of God (17:5)
- Jesus is transfigured, His very being radiating light (not just external fire)
- The shelter Peter proposes is unnecessary; Jesus Himself is God dwelling among us—no earthly tabernacle is needed
- Rather than forty days on the mountain, Jesus immediately descends to teach His disciples
- The command is not to build but to listen: "Listen to him!"

### b. The Suffering Servant (Matthew 17:9-13; 16:21-24)

Yet immediately after this glorious revelation, Jesus reveals the contradiction at the heart of His mission. As they come down the mountain, the disciples ask about Elijah's coming. Jesus explains:

> "To be sure, Elijah comes and will restore all things. But I tell you, Elijah has already come, and they did not recognize him, but have done to him everything they wished. In the same way the Son of Man is going to suffer at their hands." (Matthew 17:11-12)

He is referring to John the Baptist—and the disciples finally understand that Jesus speaks of His own impending suffering.

Just before the Transfiguration, Jesus had told them plainly:

> "From that time on Jesus began to explain to his disciples that he must go to Jerusalem and suffer many things at the hands of the elders, chief priests and teachers of the law, and that he must be killed and on the third day be raised to life." (Matthew 16:21)

And He calls His disciples into this same path:

> "If anyone would come after me, he must deny himself and take up his cross and follow me." (Matthew 16:24)

The glorious Son does not remain on the mountain enjoying the light. He descends and walks toward His death in Jerusalem. This is the shocking truth: **glory and service are one. True glory is found only in self-emptying love.**

## 2. Serve the Son

### a. Be Little (Matthew 18:1-4)

The disciples, still captivated by the vision of kingdom power, ask:

> "Who is the greatest in the kingdom of heaven?" (Matthew 18:1)

Jesus answers by placing a child before them:

> "I tell you the truth, unless you change and become like little children, you will never enter the kingdom of heaven. Therefore, whoever humbles himself like this child is the greatest in the kingdom of heaven." (Matthew 18:2-4)

**Why a child?** A child listens when called—they have simple faith in their teacher. They possess no status-seeking ambition, no hunger for power. In that culture, children were not a protected class but the lowest rung of society—assigned the dirtiest, most menial work. They were humble by necessity.

Jesus calls His disciples to abandon their obsession with status and become like these little ones: **servants, not lords.**

### b. Welcome the Little Ones (Matthew 18:5-9)

> "And whoever welcomes a little child like this in my name welcomes me. But if anyone causes one of these little ones who believe in me to sin, it would be better for him to have a large millstone hung around his neck and to be drowned in the depths of the sea." (Matthew 18:5-6)

Notice the language: to "cause to sin" is the same Greek word translated as "stumbling block" or "scandal"—it means to block someone's path to God. The disciples are warned with terrifying severity: do not block these little ones. Do not cause them to stumble.

Jesus repeats this warning:

> "Woe to the world because of the things that cause people to sin! Such things must come, but woe to the man through whom they come! If your hand or your foot causes you to sin cut it off and throw it away. It is better for you to enter life maimed or crippled than to have two hands or two feet and be thrown into eternal fire." (Matthew 18:7-8)

**The principle is clear: service means not blocking others from the kingdom. It means welcoming them, accepting them, and lowering yourself for their sake.**

### c. Examples of True Service

#### Service: Not Blocking the Little Ones (Matthew 17:14-23)

A man kneels before Jesus, desperate:

> "Lord, have mercy on my son. He has seizures and is suffering greatly. He often falls into the fire or into the water. I brought him to your disciples, but they could not heal him." (Matthew 17:14-16)

Jesus rebukes the demon, and the boy is healed. Later, the disciples ask privately why they couldn't drive it out. Jesus replies:

> "Because you have so little faith. I tell you the truth, if you have faith as small as a mustard seed, you can say to this mountain, 'Move from here to there' and it will move. Nothing will be impossible for you." (Matthew 17:20)

The disciples lacked faith—not magical power, but the faith that comes from servant hearts. Perhaps they were attempting to cast out demons for the wrong reasons: ambition, status, or the desire to appear powerful. In that failure, they became a stumbling block to the boy who needed deliverance.

**To those who lead or wish to lead: be careful.** Your pride can block others from salvation. Your ambition can prevent healing and wholeness. Service requires humility and genuine faith in Christ's power, not self-seeking.

#### Service: Even Though We Are Exempt (Matthew 17:24-27)

In Capernaum, tax collectors approach Peter:

> "Doesn't your teacher pay the temple tax?" (Matthew 17:24)

Jesus asks Peter: "From whom do the kings of the earth collect duty and taxes—from their own sons or from others?"

Peter answers, "From others."

> "Then the sons are exempt," Jesus said. "But so that we may not offend them, go to the lake and throw out your line. Take the first fish you catch; open its mouth and you will find a four-drachma coin. Take it and give it to them for my tax and yours." (Matthew 17:26-27)

**Here is the paradox of Christian service:** Jesus, the Son of God, is exempt from the temple tax—it is a tax for His Father's house. Yet He pays it anyway. Why? "So that we may not offend them"—literally, "so that we do not block them."

By paying the tax, Jesus humbles Himself. He does not exercise His rights as a Son. Instead, He assumes the role of a servant and stranger. **This is the heart of discipleship: to lower yourself for others, even when you have every right not to.**

#### Service: To the Lost (Matthew 18:12-14)

> "What do you think? If a man owns a hundred sheep, and one of them wanders away, will he not leave the ninety-nine on the hills and go to look for the one that wandered off? And if he finds it, I tell you the truth, he is happier about that one sheep than about the ninety-nine that did not wander off. In the same way your Father in heaven is not willing that any of these little ones should be lost." (Matthew 18:12-14)

The shepherd leaves the secure majority to seek the lost one. This is the service Jesus calls us to: the willingness to go out of your way, to lower yourself, to humble yourself for the sake of those who are lost.

## The Heart of Discipleship: Practical Service

Jesus calls us to transformation—to morph from self-seeking consumers of ministry into humble, self-sacrificing servants. But what does this look like practically?

### The Danger of Glory-Seeking Ministry

In our churches, there is often great respect for visible ministry roles: youth leader, deacon, Bible study leader. It feels glorious. Many are drawn to leadership because their ministries are succeeding, growing, thriving. Success breeds success. We select the best, and they become even more elevated.

But we must examine our hearts: **Why do we serve?**

- To hang out with the cool people?
- To boost our self-esteem and gain social status?
- To have power and control over others?

Or do we serve **out of love, for love's sake—purely to serve others**?

Only the servant disciple persists when ministry becomes mundane: when you're teaching Sunday school with little recognition, when exams press or work demands your time, when the youth group doesn't show up and the work feels pointless. Only the servant disciple remains faithful when there is no glory, no applause, no social benefit.

### What True Service Requires

**Be present.** Like a host at a party who arrives early and stays late, the servant is there. This requires sacrifice:

- Catching an earlier ride to church
- Sleeping earlier on Saturday night
- Showing up consistently to Bible studies and home groups
- Being on campus to connect with others

**Be willing to lose your comfort.** If you sit with the lonely and excluded, you may never sit in your favorite seat again. You may never sit with your clique. Are you prepared?

**Be willing to listen.** You may never get to discuss your interests, your pet topics, your agenda. Instead, you listen to others' concerns and needs. You seek to understand what makes them tick so you can minister the gospel to them more faithfully.

**Be willing to be forgotten.** You might never be the life of the party. Instead, you slowly move around the room, looking for the quiet ones who don't quite fit in. You speak gently. You pray in corners. You serve unseen.

**But this is only bearable—only possible—if your service is motivated by love, not self-interest. If your service is like your Lord's service.**

## The True Nature of Glory

Jesus morphed into glorious light on the mountain. But He showed us what true glory really is: **the glory of service.**

He descended from the mountain. He walked toward rejection, suffering, and death. He paid taxes He didn't owe. He healed those who brought Him no honor. He taught His disciples to welcome children, to seek the lost, to lower themselves for others.

As adopted sons and daughters of God—destined to receive glory, to sit on thrones in heaven, to judge the twelve tribes of Israel—**we are called to morph ourselves into humble and loving servants.**

Not because service is weakness. But because Jesus, the glorious Son of God, showed us that true glory is found only in love poured out for others.
