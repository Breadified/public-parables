# 1 John 4:7-12

**Title:** Love: God's Definition and Our Call
**Date:** 27 May 2017
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Three Definitions of Love

Before we explore what Scripture teaches about love, it's worth considering how the world defines this profound emotion.

**The Evolutionary Definition** suggests that love is fundamentally about physical attraction. Research indicates that for men, a woman's waist-to-hip ratio signals her ability to bear children. For women, a man's broad chest and shoulders represent strength—someone capable of providing and protecting. This view reduces love to biological survival mechanics.

**The Chemical Definition** reveals love as a cascade of hormones and neurotransmitters. When we're searching for a partner, testosterone and estrogen drive us to "get out looking." When we find "the one," dopamine floods our system, making us feel euphoric. Adrenaline quickens our pulse and makes us sweat. Serotonin temporarily clouds our judgment. Finally, oxytocin and vasopressin—the attachment hormones—bond us to our beloved. Love, in this view, is orchestrated chemistry.

These definitions capture something real about human experience. But they fall short of the deeper truth that Scripture offers us.

## 1. God Is Love

> **Dear friends, let us love one another, for love comes from God.** (1 John 4:7)

God is the source of all love. If love originates anywhere, it originates with Him. But John goes further:

> **Whoever does not love does not know God, because God is love.** (1 John 4:8)

Love is not merely an attribute God possesses—something He can set aside when He chooses. It's not a mask He wears that comes off when no one is looking. Love is not a fickle quality that fluctuates with His mood. No—God *is* love. His very nature is love. Every fiber of His being is love. It is His desire, His motivation, His essential identity.

To illustrate: imagine if love were so central to someone's character that if you operated on them and took a biopsy, you would find love. If you cut them, they would bleed love. If you chiseled away a piece, it would be solid love. This is God. His nature is love in its purest, most complete form.

## 2. Jesus Is Love Incarnate

God's love is not abstract—it entered history in person.

> **This is how God showed his love among us: He sent his one and only Son into the world that we might live through him.** (1 John 4:9)

Two thousand years ago, God's love took flesh in Jesus Christ. In Him, we see God's love made visible.

> **This is love: not that we loved God, but that he loved us and sent his Son as an atoning sacrifice for our sins.** (1 John 4:10)

Consider the scandal of this: Jesus was crucified for crimes He did not commit. He died sacrificially on behalf of our sins. God bled. God was pierced. God died. All for our sake.

This love stands in sharp contrast to human nature. While we are capable of love, it is not intrinsic to us. Look beneath the surface of humanity—behind the masks we wear—and what do we find? Selfishness. Hatred. Apathy. When pressure comes, we look out for number one. We protect ourselves. We pursue our interests at others' expense.

But Jesus revealed a different kind of love: other-centered, sacrificial love. He showed us God's love—patient, kind, slow to anger, abounding in mercy. He showed us a God who gives everything, even His life, for those who don't deserve it.

Those who have experienced this love—who have come to know Jesus—understand what it truly costs to love another human being.

## 3. Love One Another

> **Dear friends, since God so loved us, we also ought to love one another.** (1 John 4:11)

God's love flows through those who have received it. When we grasp how deeply we've been loved by God in Christ, that love naturally overflows to others.

This is the love that should sustain a marriage—not merely the chemical rush of attraction, not the evolutionary drive to find a strong provider or fertile mate, but the theological love that God Himself embodies and revealed through Jesus.

True marital love says "I will"—not just "I do" in the momentary sense of today's feelings. It is a commitment extended into tomorrow and all the days beyond. It is unconditional love. It is love that persists when the hormones fade, when physical beauty changes, when the other person fails you. It is love even when he is difficult and she is demanding. It is love even when she is messy and he is withdrawn.

This love was first shown to us by God in Jesus. It is the pattern we are called to reflect in our relationships with one another—patient, sacrificial, and enduring.

Dear friends, since God so loved us, we also ought to love one another.
