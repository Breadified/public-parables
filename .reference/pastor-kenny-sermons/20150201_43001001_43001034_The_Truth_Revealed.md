# John 1:1-34

**Title:** The Truth Revealed
**Date:** 1 February 2015
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction

John begins his Gospel with one of the most profound theological statements in Scripture, setting up a tension that will dominate the opening chapter: How can finite human beings come to know the infinite God? This passage moves through three essential movements: the impossibility of knowing God through human effort, the divine solution through Christ's incarnation, and the invitation to receive Him and become God's children.

## 1. It Is Impossible to Know God (1:1-5)

### The Transcendence Problem

Consider what we face when trying to know someone. At school, university, or church, we might try to meet someone we're interested in. We might exchange letters, emails, or texts. But ultimately, we wish to meet them face to face—to sit down for coffee or a meal together.

If it's this difficult to truly know another human being, how much more impossible is it to know God?

Albert Einstein wrestled with this very question:

> I see a pattern, but my imagination cannot picture the maker of that pattern. I see a clock, but I cannot envision the clockmaker. The human mind is unable to conceive of the four dimensions, so how can it conceive of a God, before whom a thousand years and a thousand dimensions are as one?

Einstein recognized a profound truth: we might study science and learn much about creation, but we cannot know the mind behind it. We cannot discover God's motivations or ultimate causality. He occupies a realm entirely beyond our reach.

### The Word in John 1:1-3

John opens his Gospel in a striking way:

> In the beginning was the Word, and the Word was with God, and the Word was God. He was with God in the beginning.

Why "the Word" rather than simply "God"? John likely employs this language as an echo of Genesis 1, drawing his Jewish readers back to the creation account. Just as Genesis begins "In the beginning God created," John anchors his narrative to that same cosmic foundation. This isn't abstract Greek philosophy, but Jewish theological tradition.

The connection deepens when we read:

> Through him all things were made; without him nothing was made that has been made. In him was life, and that life was the light of all mankind. The light shines in the darkness, and the darkness has not overcome it.

This parallels Genesis 1:3—"And God said, 'Let there be light,' and there was light." The Jewish understanding of creation involves God's *word*—His spoken utterance that brings reality into being. The Psalmist captures this beautifully:

> By the word of the Lord the heavens were made, their starry host by the breath of his mouth.

### God Remains Transcendent

John emphasizes that the Word and God, though distinct, are eternally one:

> The Word was with God, and the Word was God.

This carefully balanced language—repeated three times in verse 1—protects both truths: the Word is intimately identified with God, yet they are not conflated. This foreshadows the Christian doctrine of the Trinity: one God eternally existing as Father, Son, and Holy Spirit.

Yet even with this revelation, Einstein's problem remains unsolved. Verse 18 states plainly:

> No one has ever seen God.

This echoes an Old Testament crisis. When Moses begged to see God's glory in Exodus 33, the Lord replied:

> You cannot see my face, for no one may see me and live. There is a place near me where you may stand on a rock. When my glory passes by, I will put you in a cleft in the rock and cover you with my hand until I have passed by. Then I will remove my hand and you will see my back; but my face must not be seen.

Even seeing only God's back aged Moses dramatically—his hair turned white. The glimpse of transcendent holiness cost him years of his life.

### Why We Cannot Know God

The chasm is insurmountable:

- **Spirit versus flesh.** God is pure spirit; we are bound to physical bodies.
- **Holiness versus defilement.** God's standards of righteousness are infinitely beyond ours. To encounter His holiness unmediated is deadly.
- **Different realms.** God exists in heaven; we exist on earth. Different dimensions, different times.

All human attempts to bridge this gap ultimately fail:

- **Meditation.** Sitting in silence with incense, trying to imagine the divine.
- **Science.** Studying creation, working backwards to understand the Creator.
- **Philosophy.** Reasoning our way to ultimate truth.
- **Feelings.** Seeking emotional or mystical experiences to encounter God's presence.

The record of human civilization confirms our helplessness. Left to our own devices, we gravitate toward cruelty—toward anarchy, injustice, the survival of the strongest. We cannot discover moral truth; we cannot ascend to God.

**It is impossible.**

## 2. It Is Possible to Know God (1:6-9, 1:14-18)

### God's Accommodation

But God does not leave us in our helplessness. In His mercy and grace, He accommodates to our weakness. He intervenes in history.

> There was a man sent from God whose name was John. He came as a witness to testify concerning that light, so that through him all might believe. He himself was not the light; he came only as a witness to the light.

Throughout the Old Testament, God revealed Himself through the prophets—through His word, through signs and miracles, through dramatic interventions in human history. But John the Baptist testifies that he is not the light. The prophets were not the light. The miracles were not the light.

> The true light that gives light to everyone was coming into the world.

Something unprecedented is about to happen.

### The Incarnation: God Becomes Flesh

> The Word became flesh and made his dwelling among us.

This statement would have been shocking to many first-century listeners. The Greeks considered the material body despicable—the worst state of being. Modern Buddhism seeks escape from the body. Islam insists that God would be defiled by entering the physical world.

Yet here is the scandal and the glory of the Gospel: the eternal Word—transcendent, powerful, existing before all things—becomes *flesh*. He is personified in a human being named Jesus.

> He made his dwelling among us—literally, He pitched his tent among us.

The image is deliberate:

- **Temporariness.** Jesus lived on earth for thirty-three years, then was crucified, raised, and ascended back to heaven. He did not permanently settle among us.
- **Meeting place.** The tent evokes the Tent of Meeting in Exodus, where Moses encountered God face to face—though always veiled by cloud.
- **Frailty.** A tent is fragile—a flimsy structure of fabric and sticks. It offers no glamour, no security. It can blow away in the slightest wind.

God limits Himself. He who fills heaven and earth confines Himself to one place, one time—first-century Palestine. He experiences hunger, cold, sorrow. He became vulnerable—capable of being hurt by cruel words, pierced by nails, bleeding for the first time in eternity.

Imagine a global celebrity stepping down from their pedestal: removing the security detail, abandoning designer clothes, wiping off makeup, taking off the jewelry. They put on ordinary clothes and catch the bus to come meet you—humbled, accessible, vulnerable. This is what God did in Christ.

### The Result: Grace Upon Grace

> Out of his fullness we have all received grace upon grace.

From the fullness of the deity, we have received gift after gift, grace heaped upon grace. This is not a transaction; it is pure generosity. God did not come for His own sake. He came to seek and save the lost—for *our* sake.

Unlike all our futile attempts to reach God, here God reaches out to us first. This is the heartbeat of the Gospel: a gracious and compassionate God who takes the initiative.

> We have seen his glory, the glory of the one and only Son, who came from the Father, full of grace and truth. For the law was given through Moses; grace and truth came through Jesus Christ.

The law revealed God's holiness and our need. But Jesus brings both grace and truth together. He is not a liar or a deceiver. In Him, what you see is what you get.

### Jesus Reveals God Completely

> No one has ever seen God, but the one and only Son, who is himself God and is in closest relationship with the Father, has made him known.

God is invisible, but if the invisible God were to take human form, He would look like Jesus. If He were to speak, He would sound like Jesus. If He were to bleed, it would be Jesus's blood. Jesus is the exact image of God's character and nature.

This is why we gather to read Scripture together at church. When we read John, we are reading a record of Jesus. Through His words and deeds recorded in Scripture, we have access to truth itself. We encounter the presence of God, not through imagination or feeling, but through the renewing of our minds by the Spirit.

Some are tempted to look *behind* Jesus—to seek something more transcendent, more mystical:

- Perhaps Jesus came, but there must be something *else* beyond His revelation.
- Perhaps the Bible is not enough; there must be deeper spiritual experiences.
- Perhaps we should return to the old ways: imagining God in solitude, seeking peak experiences on mountaintops, chasing feelings that seem more divine.

But this impulse—however noble—returns us to the old futility. We already have what we seek. In Jesus, we have the fullness of God. What you see is what you get. He *is* the truth about God, not a pointer to some deeper truth beyond.

## 3. Receiving and Believing (1:10-13)

### The Rejection

Yet for all of God's humility and self-emptying, the response was rejection:

> He was in the world, and though the world was made through him, the world did not recognize him. He came to that which was his own, but his own did not receive him.

The Creator was rejected by His creation. Jesus was rejected not only by His own people, the Jews, but by the world itself. Some people are so hardened that no amount of evidence suffices. Even if God were to appear directly before them, they would rationalize it away as hallucination or delusion—just as the first-century Jews, who witnessed God in flesh, rejected Him.

### The Promise: Becoming God's Children

But hope remains:

> Yet to all who did receive him, to those who believed in his name, he gave the right to become children of God.

Two conditions: (1) receive Him, and (2) believe in His name. The result: authority to become children of God.

This language is striking. A "right" or "authority"—as if receiving a legal document, like a birth certificate that entitles one to citizenship. But is this presumptuous? How can we know with certainty that we truly are God's children until we stand before Him in heaven?

This doubt itself reveals our condition. We are accustomed to broken promises. A salesman might guarantee something, only for his supervisor to renege. People change their minds. Circumstances shift.

But Jesus is not a low-level salesman. He is God. He existed before the beginning. His every word is truth. When He declares, "Whoever believes in me shall have eternal life," this is as sure and certain as truth itself. He has given His word, and He will not take it back.

### The New Birth

> Children born not of natural descent, nor of human decision or a husband's will, but born of God.

Ordinary birth involves human choices and decisions—sometimes accidents. But this birth is different. It is not created by human will.

To become a child of God is to be *recreated* by His Spirit:

- **Recreated in spirit** so that we can know the Spirit of God.
- **Recreated in holiness** so that we can meet with the Holy One without fear.
- **Recreated in eternity** so that we can speak to Him who dwells in heaven.

## Conclusion

Some of you hold this hope with confidence. You know that you belong to God. As you navigate the ups and downs of this year, cling to that assurance.

But some of you are uncertain. Perhaps you've never realized this hope was available to you. Perhaps you're not sure where you will stand if you were to die today, next month, or twenty years from now.

That uncertainty need not remain. In light of John's revelation, we can know God. We can know His heart. We can understand His thoughts. By remaining in this Gospel account, you will discover how God created all things and what His purposes are for you.

The God who seemed utterly transcendent and impossible to know has bent down to us in Jesus. He offers you, right now, the right to become His child.

Will you receive Him?
