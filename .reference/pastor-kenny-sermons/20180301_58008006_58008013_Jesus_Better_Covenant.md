# Hebrews 8:6-13

**Title:** Jesus: Better Covenant
**Date:** 18 March 2018
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## The Problem with Following God's Law

We live in a society shaped by unconscious values—we queue, apologize, avoid stealing—without always understanding why. Yet when we examine the difficulties of life, we discover a persistent pattern: motivation is the core issue.

Consider any challenge: tax reform requires economic modeling to find the optimum balance. Helping vulnerable populations means addressing root causes like addiction and family breakdown. University courses demand sustained effort through a structured syllabus. Dental hygiene takes only minutes daily, yet poor oral care cascades into serious health consequences.

The same principle applies to God's law. It's written down. The commands are clear. Yet we struggle to follow them. Why? Because motivation is elusive. Without motivation, even the clearest instructions become burdensome.

## Part One: The Old Covenant

### The Nature of the First Covenant

The writer of Hebrews begins with a comparison between two covenants—two contracts. One is superior to the other.

> But in fact the ministry Jesus has received is as superior to theirs as the covenant of which he is mediator is superior to the old one, since the new covenant is established on better promises. For if there had been nothing wrong with that first covenant, no place would have been sought for another. But God found fault with the people and said: "The days are coming, declares the Lord, when I will make a new covenant with the people of Israel and with the people of Judah." (Hebrews 8:6-8)

The old covenant refers to the agreement God made with Israel at Mount Sinai:

> Then he took the Book of the Covenant and read it to the people. They responded, "We will do everything the Lord has said; we will obey." Moses then took the blood, sprinkled it on the people and said, "This is the blood of the covenant that the Lord has made with you in accordance with all these words." (Exodus 24:7-8)

This covenant was formalized and elaborated when Israel entered Canaan. God set down explicit terms:

> If you fully obey the Lord your God and carefully follow all his commands I give you today, the Lord your God will set you high above all the nations on earth. All these blessings will come on you... You will be blessed in the city and blessed in the country... The Lord will grant you abundant prosperity... (Deuteronomy 28:1-12)

But equally explicit were the curses for disobedience:

> However, if you do not obey the Lord your God and do not carefully follow all his commands and decrees I am giving you today, all these curses will come on you and overtake you. You will be cursed in the city and cursed in the country... (Deuteronomy 28:15-19)

### A Contract with Terms and Conditions

Like any legal contract, the old covenant specified:
- What it meant to fulfill the agreement (blessings)
- What it meant to break the agreement (curses)

Israel broke the contract. As Hebrews declares:

> They did not remain faithful to my covenant, and I turned away from them. (Hebrews 8:9)

The consequence unfolded across centuries. As Israel disobeyed, curses accumulated: famines multiplied, idolatry invited invasion, the kingdom fractured in civil war, foreign powers stripped their wealth. Over a thousand years in Canaan, the nation spiraled into exile. By the time Hebrews was written, the Jews remained under that curse—even those who had physically returned to the land found themselves conquered by the Roman Empire, living in a poor backwater nation.

The curses persisted because disobedience persisted.

### We All Live in Exile

This ancient history is not merely historical. It reveals a universal spiritual reality: all of us begin in exile. We start cursed by God because of our sin. This is why the world contains suffering—none of us is innocent. As Paul writes:

> The wrath of God is being revealed from heaven against all the godlessness and wickedness of people, who suppress the truth by their wickedness... For since the creation of the world God's invisible qualities—his eternal power and divine nature—have been clearly seen, being understood from what has been made, so that people are without excuse. (Romans 1:18-20)

Judgment is not some future abstraction. It is happening now. God's wrath is actively being revealed, warning us of what the future will bring if we persist in disobedience.

### The Link Between Sin and Suffering

Sometimes our personal sufferings trace directly to the consequences of sin. This is what the Apostle James teaches:

> Is anyone among you sick? Let them call the elders of the church to pray over them and anoint them with oil in the name of the Lord. And the prayer offered in faith will make the sick person well; the Lord will raise them up. If they have sinned, they will be forgiven. Therefore confess your sins to each other and pray for each other so that you may be healed. The prayer of a righteous person is powerful and effective. (James 5:14-16)

This is unusual counsel. We expect the modern response: call a doctor, take medicine, rest. Instead James points to the elders of the church and directly connects healing to forgiveness of sin.

Yet Jesus reminds us in John 9 that the link is not always straightforward. When asked about a blind man, "Was it his sin or his parents' sin that he was born blind?" Jesus answered, "Neither." In our chaotic world, the connections between obedience and blessing, disobedience and curse, are sometimes fuzzy.

Still, the principle remains: there is often a spiritual dimension to our suffering. When facing illness or difficulty, a wise first question is: "Am I in sin? Is there something I've begun doing that might be drawing God's correction?" Only after that does it make sense to pursue doctors, medicine, and other remedies. And when nothing changes, we commit to prayer, trusting that some problems lie beyond human solving.

### A Materialistic Spirituality

We struggle with this because we have become practical atheists. We call ourselves theists but live like deists—perhaps God exists, but he left long ago and we're essentially alone. We don't really believe in a metaphysical link between God's character and our circumstances.

Yet the first covenant demonstrates this link vividly. When a nation blesses itself by walking with God, it flourishes. When it turns from God, curse follows. Australia has been blessed—not because the land is inherently great, but because godly people have tilled it, made the desert bloom, and formed a nation under God. But as we turn away from him as a nation, he turns away from us.

Life is difficult at its root because motivation is fractured. We don't want to obey God. And no external law—no matter how perfect—can fix a broken will.

## Part Two: The New Covenant

### The Problem Was Never the Law

Here is the crucial distinction:

> But God found fault with the people and said... (Hebrews 8:8)

God did not find fault with the covenant itself. His laws are perfect and just. The problem was the people. As Hebrews explains:

> If the people were faultless under the first covenant, there would be no need for a new covenant. But God found fault with the people. (Hebrews 8:7-8, paraphrased)

The solution was not to scrap God's laws—that would be foolish, for his ways are perfect. Rather, the solution was revolutionary:

> I will put my laws in their minds and write them on their hearts. (Hebrews 8:10)

### From External to Internal

The commands of God would no longer remain external—written on stone tablets—but would become internal, inscribed on the human heart itself.

This is like the difference between installing antivirus software (which depends on human memory and discipline) and hardwiring protection into the computer itself. The law moves from something we must remember to obey to something we naturally embody.

This transformation mirrors how we develop competence in any domain. We move through distinct stages:

- **Unconscious incompetence:** I don't know what I don't know (I'm struggling but don't even understand why)
- **Conscious incompetence:** I now realize there's a problem, but I don't know how to fix it
- **Conscious competence:** I understand the solution and apply it deliberately
- **Unconscious competence:** The skill is so internalized I perform it naturally without thinking

When you learn piano, you begin playing poorly without understanding why. You struggle, consciously working to improve. Eventually, you master the technique so thoroughly that your fingers find the right keys without deliberate thought. The skill has become internalized.

This internalization begins from birth. A child whose parents teach godly values, model them consistently, and train them in righteousness gradually internalizes God's law. It becomes part of their character, not an external burden.

### Knowledge Becomes Relationship

This internalization produces a dramatic consequence:

> No longer will they teach their neighbor, or say to one another, "Know the Lord," because they will all know me, from the least of them to the greatest. (Hebrews 8:11)

Why do we spend thousands on piano teachers each year? Because the student hasn't yet internalized the skill. Once unconscious competence is achieved, the teacher becomes unnecessary.

Similarly, God's people will no longer need constant religious instruction. They will not be ignorant, gathering curses from disobedience. Instead, having internalized God's commands, they will be blessed. And this "knowing" is not mere intellectual knowledge—it is relational intimacy:

> I will be their God, and they will be my people. (Hebrews 8:10)

### The Transformative Secret: Forgiveness

But how will this internalization happen? Not through more learning—Israel had teachers and training. Not through mystical overnight transformation—sudden spiritual leaps don't explain sustained character change. The answer lies in what comes next:

> For I will forgive their wickedness and will remember their sins no more. (Hebrews 8:12)

This is what has fundamentally changed between the old and new covenants. God has fulfilled his promise to forgive us—through Jesus Christ.

At his last supper, Jesus said:

> I have eagerly desired to eat this Passover with you before I suffer... He took bread, gave thanks and broke it, and gave it to them, saying, "This is my body given for you; do this in remembrance of me." In the same way, after the supper he took the cup, saying, "This cup is the new covenant in my blood, which is poured out for you." (Luke 22:15, 19-20)

Forgiveness is only possible because Jesus takes the punishment for our sin on our behalf. His blood is poured out. His body is broken. And this makes all the difference in the world.

### The Power of Sacrifice to Change Motivation

As we understand the depth of God's love demonstrated in Christ's death, our motivation to obey him transforms from external compulsion to internal desire.

Consider how parental sacrifice can reshape a child's motivation. Some students study hard because of ambition or peer pressure. But others are driven by something deeper—gratitude for their parents' sacrifice. They recognize what their parents gave up: changing countries, learning new languages, facing humiliation, rebuilding their lives from nothing. And in response, students study not from fear of punishment but from a burning desire to honor that sacrifice and prove worthy of it.

Similarly, as we grasp Christ's death more deeply, we love obeying God. We want to please him. We want to honor that sacrifice. The external contract becomes unnecessary because the relationship is characterized by love and trust.

### The Old Covenant Made Obsolete

> By calling this covenant new, he has made the first one obsolete; and what is obsolete and outdated will soon disappear. (Hebrews 8:13)

The first covenant is not obsolete because God's laws are irrelevant—they remain eternally relevant. Rather, it is obsolete because we no longer need it. Contracts are necessary only when parties are suspicious of each other—that's why you need rewards and penalties written in. But when friends agree in genuine relationship, the contract becomes unnecessary. What matters is the bond itself.

## The Fundamental Problem: Motivation

We all long for a better society where we can solve our problems efficiently. But every major difficulty traces back to motivation:

- The problem with tax reform is not the rates or types of taxes. It's that people don't want to pay them.
- The problem with higher education is not the curriculum. It's that students aren't motivated to study material they find irrelevant.
- The problem with dental hygiene is not the unavailability of toothbrushes or toothpaste. It's that people find it tedious—a chore when they'd rather do something else.
- The fundamental problem with God's law is that we don't really want to follow it.

### God's Solution: Dummy-Proofing

God doesn't try to force us to brush our teeth through threats or rewards. Instead, he puts fluoride in the water. The solution is engineered at the source, removing human failure from the equation.

Similarly, Jesus doesn't try to force obedience by threatening harsher punishments. Instead, he circumvents our hard hearts entirely. Rather than working on the problem from the outside, he works from the inside out. He rewires our motivation itself.

Through his death and resurrection, Jesus removes us from the equation. We are no longer the problem. Our motivation no longer depends on our willpower, our discipline, or our strength. It depends on him—on understanding his love, his sacrifice, and his kingdom.

This is what we celebrate next week on Easter Sunday. When we gather to commemorate Christ's resurrection, we celebrate the fact that God has solved our deepest problem—not our circumstances, not our knowledge, but our motivation. He has made us new from the inside out.
