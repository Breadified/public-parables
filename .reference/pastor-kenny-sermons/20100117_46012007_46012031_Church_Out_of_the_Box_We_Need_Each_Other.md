# 1 Corinthians 12:7-31

**Title:** Church Out of the Box: We Need Each Other
**Date:** January 17, 2010
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Big Idea

Same Spirit → Same Lord → Same Unity. We need each other.

## Opening Question

Who is the most important person in this room? The answer isn't the speaker, the musicians, the wealthiest, the most talented, or the longest-serving member. The answer is: **the person next to you**. The other person.

Last week we established three foundational truths:
- We have the **same Spirit**—the same gospel
- As a result, we have the **same Lord**
- Same Lord means **same unity**

---

## 1. Different Gifts, Same Spirit

> Now to each one the manifestation of the Spirit is given for the common good. (1 Corinthians 12:7)

There is one singular manifestation of the Spirit—one message expressed in many different ways. All pointing to the same truth: **Jesus is Lord**. And this message is given for the common good, not for personal use.

> All these are the work of one and the same Spirit, and he gives them to each one, just as he determines. (1 Corinthians 12:11)

The Spirit divides the gifts deliberately and strategically. The same Spirit is at work in every believer, distributing abilities as He sees fit.

### The List of Gifts (verses 8-10)

Paul lists several spiritual gifts: wisdom, knowledge, faith, healing, miracles, prophecy, discernment of spirits, speaking in tongues, and interpretation of tongues.

What are these gifts? **We don't know with certainty.** Even Christian writers in the 4th century AD acknowledged they were uncertain. This is especially true for those who claim to know exactly what each gift is.

### Three Theological Positions

**Full Continuists** believe miraculous gifts continue today as they did in Acts. The Spirit works now with signs and wonders just as then. Jesus is "the same yesterday, today and forever," so healing, prophecy, and tongues are normative.

**Cessationists** hold that miraculous gifts belonged to the apostolic era. The apostles laid the foundation for the church while Scripture was still being written. Now that the Bible is complete, God doesn't need these dramatic signs. Miracles were connected to the apostolic function. Therefore, these gifts either don't apply today or should be understood non-literally—"message of wisdom" as insight, "prophecy" as excellent preaching, "tongues" as speaking Cantonese, "healing" as being a doctor.

**Partial Continuists** sit in the middle—often because they're unsure or based on personal experience.

### Kenny's Perspective

Wherever you land on this spectrum, consider these points:

1. **We don't know.** The text is genuinely unclear.

2. **Based on context,** the miraculous gifts seem to be primarily speaking gifts (prophecy, knowledge, discerning spirits), and this makes sense given Acts was still unfolding at Corinth around AD 50. Later Pauline letters mention signs and wonders less frequently.

3. **These things were remarkable.** Miraculous events in Scripture were never normal—they were linked to specific salvation-historical moments. They were extraordinary, not everyday.

4. **While miracles may happen, they're not gifts.** Even if God performs miracles, not everyone possesses them as permanent abilities.

5. **Personal experience doesn't settle it.** You may have experienced something remarkable, and that's valid, but personal experience doesn't prove something is normative for all believers today.

6. **Consider what transforms us.** The gospel is "the power of God"—but does this power work through learning His Word and being changed by it, or through signs and wonders?

7. **We should be thoughtfully skeptical.** God is supernatural and anything is possible with Him. But God is also a God of order, rationality, sensibility, and knowledge—not a God of chaos disguised as the weirder the better.

8. **Diversity serves unity.** Whatever gifts exist in the church today, they may differ from first-century gifts. Regardless, God uses our differences to achieve His purpose: serving each other under Christ's Lordship for the common good.

## 2. Same Body, Different Parts

> The body is a unit, though it is made up of many parts; and though all its parts are many, they form one body. So it is with Christ. (1 Corinthians 12:12)

We are one body—this is the metaphor for how we function together. We are interdependent and vital to each other.

> For we were all baptized by one Spirit into one body—whether Jews or Greeks, slave or free—and we were all given the one Spirit to drink. (1 Corinthians 12:13)

**Baptized by one Spirit into one body.** In the Jewish tradition, baptism was a washing ritual symbolizing identification with a movement or leader. But here, Paul clarifies this isn't about water baptism—Christ didn't send him to baptize but to preach the gospel (1 Corinthians 1:17).

This is **Spirit baptism**—your identification with Christ through the gospel. When you believed, you were united under His Lordship. The Spirit made you part of His body.

The phrase "given the one Spirit to drink" carries the same meaning (see John 7:37-39). Just as Jesus invited the thirsty to come and drink, signifying internal transformation, we all partake of the same Spirit—the same life-giving truth.

## 3. Many Parts, All Vital

> Now the body is not made up of one part but of many. (1 Corinthians 12:14)
> As it is, there are many parts, but one body. (1 Corinthians 12:20)
> Now you are the body of Christ, and each one of you is a part of it. (1 Corinthians 12:27)

There are two dangerous attitudes that undermine this unity.

### Attitude 1: "I Don't Belong"

> If the foot should say, "Because I am not a hand, I do not belong to the body," it would not for that reason cease to be part of the body. And if the ear should say, "Because I am not an eye, I do not belong to the body," it would not for that reason cease to be part of the body. (1 Corinthians 12:15-16)

We imagine hands as superior—functional, multi-purpose, attractive. Feet bear weight, get smelly, gather only sweat. Eyes seem special; ears less so. If you're a foot, you might think: *I'm not as impressive as a hand. I don't belong here.*

But this is foolish reasoning:

> If the whole body were an eye, where would the sense of hearing be? If the whole body were an ear, where would the sense of smell be? But in fact God has arranged the parts in the body, every one of them, just as he wanted them to be. If they were all one part, where would the body be? As it is, there are many parts, but one body. (1 Corinthians 12:17-20)

If your whole body were an eye, you'd be a grotesque, non-functional blob. If all ears, equally useless. God designed each part exactly as needed. No one cuts off their foot because it's less attractive than their hand. The pinky toe is vital for balance—you discover this the moment you stub it.

**Every part belongs.** Period.

#### Who feels like they don't belong?

- The **newcomer**: This is your first visit. The setting feels strange. You don't know anyone. *I don't belong.*

- The **non-Christian**: You were pressured to come. Everyone here seems so different. *I don't belong.*

- The **sick**: You've never quite fit anywhere. Now you fear people will discover your illness and judge you. *I don't belong.*

- The **parent**: You're desperate for adult conversation in a room of young people. *I don't belong.*

- The **outsider by background**: Everyone has black hair. Everyone seems connected in ways you're not. *I don't belong.*

- The **young**: You're 12 in a room of adults. No one notices you. *I don't belong.*

- The **struggling sinner**: Everyone seems so godly. Your secret shame feels so dark. They'll find out and judge you. *I don't belong.*

- Even the **pastor**: You feel old and out of touch. Did you speak too harshly? Are they gossiping about you? *I don't belong.*

**Here's the truth: we all feel like outsiders.** At an old church, older members complained the congregation was "too young." But the data showed equal representation across ages. The problem? If you're the oldest, you see youth around you. If you're young, everyone looks old. From any position, most people seem "different" from you.

But look past your differences to your unity in Christ. **You are valuable to this body.**

- **Newcomers** teach us how to welcome strangers—the heart of the Good Samaritan.
- **Sick members** teach us compassion and how to show real love.
- **Parents** bring wisdom and multigenerational perspective.
- **The young** represent the future and bring energy.
- **Those struggling** teach us honesty and help us avoid pretense.
- **Those from different backgrounds** expand our understanding.

God gave us each other to teach us how to be one body. How to love.

**Don't disappear on us.** When hard times come, the first casualty is often fellowship—we fear judgment. If that's happened to you, I'm sorry. But don't pull away. We need you. Turn up. Be encouraged and encourage others.

### Attitude 2: "I Don't Need You"

> The eye cannot say to the hand, "I don't need you!" And the head cannot say to the feet, "I don't need you!" (1 Corinthians 12:21)

This is the attitude of the **strong**. You're doing well. Things are working out. You're gifted. So you think: *I don't need this church. I can study Scripture myself. I can attend a conference. I don't need to be here.*

Yes, objectively, you might not *need* the church to survive spiritually. But listen to Paul's counterargument:

> On the contrary, those parts of the body that seem to be weaker are indispensable, and the parts that we think are less honorable we treat with special honor. And the parts that are unpresentable are treated with special modesty, while our presentable parts need no special treatment. But God has combined the members of the body and has given greater honor to the parts that lacked it, so that there should be no division in the body, but that its parts should have equal concern for each other. (1 Corinthians 12:22-25)

**You are strong so you can help the weaker parts.** Your strength isn't for yourself—it's for building others up. When we show honor to the seemingly less important parts, we're saying: *You matter. You belong. We need you.*

#### "I Don't Need to Be Here"

We pack our schedules. Birthday parties, holidays, socials, work, camps, university meetings, recitals, friends' baptisms, weddings. Sunday becomes just another slot to fit in—if there's room.

Often it's because we genuinely don't see church as essential. *I'm fine. I don't need you.*

But here's the problem:

> If one part suffers, every part suffers with it; if one part is honored, every part rejoices with it. (1 Corinthians 12:26)

**We still need you**, even if you don't need us. We can't find another hand to replace you. The body isn't flexible enough to suddenly transform an ear into an eye. Different parts do different things.

When you absent yourself regularly, you destroy what makes church possible:
- **Confessing sin to one another**—can't happen in flux
- **Learning together**—requires consistency
- **Praying for each other**—needs commitment, stability, security
- **Building real relationships**—demands regularity and presence

Relationships don't form in constant transition. People need to know you'll be there next week—then they're safe opening up.

Think of a mobile ministry (like MLM prayer meetings). Different people each week. Different dynamics. Teaching quality fluctuates. Start times are uncertain. You can't challenge each other because you won't see them again. You won't actually know how they're doing. Disagreements get brushed off. It's monstrous.

But imagine instead a congregation where:
- God's word is taught faithfully each week
- People share what they've learned from Scripture
- Struggles, temptations, and difficulties are voiced
- Prayer and help are offered
- Outsiders see love in action

**This requires commitment. Show up. Help. Be present.** Equal concern for each other means reliability, not convenience.

## Closing: Who Matters Most?

We started by asking who's the most important person here. Consider the movie credits—one of the few truly egalitarian things. Key grip. Gaffer. Best boy. Roles with names we don't recognize. **Everyone has a role.**

The most important person in this room is the person next to you. **Each of us is important to each other.**

### Two Calls

**If you feel like you don't belong:**

Give it another chance. You are valuable even if we fail to care for you properly. Take the risk and belong.

**If you think you don't need others:**

People are taking a risk by being vulnerable here. Don't fail them. Show up. Be the hand, foot, eye, or ear that this body needs.

---

## What True Spirituality Looks Like

Being truly spiritual means:
- Recognizing that others need you
- Committing to regular, consistent presence
- Caring equally for all parts of the body
- Building and protecting community
- Making church a safe place to be known and loved

**We are all important to each other. We need each other.**
