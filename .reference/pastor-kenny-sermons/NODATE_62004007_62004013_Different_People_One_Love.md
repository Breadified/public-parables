# 1 John 4:7-13

**Title:** Different People, One Love
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Different People

What a wonderful occasion it is to see so many people gathered here to witness Chris and Khodee's marriage. Weddings are significant events—behind the scenes, there's been tremendous work not just in planning, but in the mental and emotional preparation to live with one another. And if you know these two, you'll notice they are remarkably different people.

If you were to describe Khodee, words like efficient, smart, and direct would come to mind. When I first met her in youth group, everything about her screamed efficiency. She was wearing her school uniform—why buy extra clothes?—and transition lenses that doubled as sunglasses. When I asked how she was doing, she casually mentioned she'd just been in the hospital with tuberculosis and her lungs don't work properly, but she guessed she was okay. That tells you everything about Khodee.

Chris, by contrast, is chill and easygoing—a class clown, but genuinely a good person. When I first met him at church, he was in his classic outfit: a t-shirt, thongs, and those short shorts he loves. I remember him performing an absolutely killer rendition of Rasputin on Just Dance. The contrast couldn't be starker.

This raises an important question: What gives two such different people the confidence to stand here today and make a lifelong commitment to each other? In a moment, they'll exchange vows promising to be husband and wife until death parts them. It's one thing to say "I love you" today, dressed to perfection. But what about ten or twenty years from now? When Chris comes to bed in his sweaty volleyball jersey without changing? When he leaves the gas on and nearly burns the house down? When Khodee forces him to eat mountains of vegetables, or when his carefully planned romantic evening ends at seven o'clock because she's tired?

What keeps opposites together for the long run?

I believe it's what they understand about love.

## God's Love

> Beloved, let us love one another, for love is from God, and whoever loves has been born of God and knows God. Anyone who does not love does not know God, because God is love. (1 John 4:7-8)

Contrary to popular belief, love is not merely a feeling or emotion—not just the butterflies Chris felt when Khodee texted asking if he wanted to go to an escape room. Love is less of a "something" and more of a "someone." God, our Creator, is himself the very definition of love. His nature *is* love. Everything he does flows from love.

Love is what God is like.

When we as human beings show love to one another, we are imitating our heavenly Father. We are made in God's image, and God's law for us is love. Throughout the Bible, the central commandment is clear: love God, and love your neighbor.

But here's the problem: we human beings don't do a very good job of this. Look around at our world—wars, corporate greed, corrupt politicians. We see evidence everywhere that we're missing God's mark. On a personal level, we lie, cheat, and hurt the people around us. If I asked Chris and Khodee to recall the last time the other annoyed them, they wouldn't have to think long. Chris probably gets frustrated every time Khodee messes up their bot lane in League of Legends. Khodee gets frustrated every time Chris steps into the kitchen—and apparently just last week, his Pad See Ew was so horrifically bad it made her cry! A tip for everyone here: six tablespoons of dark soy sauce is indeed too much.

None of us love the people in our lives perfectly the way we should. And we certainly don't love God. We live in a society that increasingly sees God as old news, a fairytale getting in the way of what we want. Instead of respecting our Creator, we make false gods out of our careers, reputations, money, and middle-class comforts.

We have failed to live up to God's standard. We reject God as our Creator and hurt each other through selfish actions. This is what the Bible calls sin. And the consequence for sin is serious: the Bible says the wages of our lovelessness is death and judgment. That's what we deserve.

And yet, look at what happened:

> In this the love of God was made manifest among us, that God sent his only Son into the world, so that we might live through him. In this is love, not that we have loved God but that he loved us and sent his Son to be the propitiation for our sins. (1 John 4:9-10)

Even while we reject God and run his world into the ground, God loved us so much that he extended mercy and sent us the ultimate demonstration of love—his magnum opus. Two thousand years ago, God came to earth in the form of Jesus Christ to give us a way out of the mess we've made. Jesus was the only person to love perfectly, the only one who didn't deserve to die. Yet Jesus, God the Son, willingly died a criminal's death on the cross as propitiation for our sins.

That word "propitiation" means to atone, to pay the price, to make things right. When Jesus shed his blood on the cross, he paid the price for us so that we don't have to. We failed to love, yet God loved us and took the punishment on our behalf. God is the party who was wronged, yet he is the one who made things right, creating a bridge between us and him again, restoring what was broken.

This is a very different kind of love from what we see in the world. Not a love that ebbs and flows with feelings, or keeps records of past wrongs, or exists only as long as there's something to gain in return. This is selfless love. Other-person-centered love. Sacrificial love.

## Not So Different After All

And this is the kind of love that Chris and Khodee believe in. For all their differences, all the ways they might get on each other's nerves, they are completely united in their understanding of God's sacrificial love.

> Beloved, if God so loved us, we also ought to love one another. No one has ever seen God; if we love one another, God abides in us and his love is perfected in us. (1 John 4:11-12)

If you know Chris and Khodee, you know they're committed to living out this verse, showing God's love in their daily lives. Whenever anyone needs help or someone to talk to, they're the first to offer support. They do it in different ways—Chris, with his high emotional intelligence, offers comfort and reassurance; Khodee takes a more practical approach, offering solutions. But in both of them, you can clearly see that they know how to love sacrificially. That's evident in everything they do. Even in their wedding ceremony today, they've requested this message from Scripture so everyone here can know about the wonderful love they've experienced and share in the joy of knowing Jesus.

They will build their marriage on this other-person-centered love.

When storms hit. When life doesn't go smoothly. When their future children test their patience. When Chris doesn't plan properly or when Khodee obsesses over details. What will keep them together is that sacrificial love—that image of Jesus on the cross, giving his life for us. That's what will help them persevere.

As they do this, God promises they will never be alone:

> By this we know that we abide in him and he in us, because he has given us of his Spirit. (1 John 4:13)

God, through the Holy Spirit, will dwell within them, helping them live rightly, ironing out the creases in their character, and making them more like Jesus in their love for one another.

So even though Chris and Khodee are very different people, today they will be united as one under the same God. They do this with courage and joy—not because of their own goodness, but because they know God's sacrificial love. May this love always flow through you to each other and to all those around you, till death do you part.
