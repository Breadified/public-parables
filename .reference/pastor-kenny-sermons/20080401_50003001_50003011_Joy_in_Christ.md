# Philippians 3:1-11

**Title:** Joy in Christ
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew
**Date:** 26 April 2008

## Introduction: Finding True Confidence

Michael Jordan was the greatest basketball player in NBA history. He led the league in scoring, won the MVP award multiple times, and held defensive records with 200 steals and 100 blocks in a single season. He could dunk from the free throw line—earning him the title "Air Jordan." Through his talent and popularity, he single-handedly elevated Nike and the NBA into mainstream consciousness.

Jordan brought the Chicago Bulls to six championships: three consecutive titles from 1991-1993, and three more from 1996-1998. He was the "money man"—the clutch performer you trusted when the game was on the line. When seconds remained and everything depended on one shot, the ball went to Jordan. That's who you put your confidence in on the basketball court.

But as we study Philippians, Paul asks us a deeper question: Who do we put our confidence in for life? Where do we find true joy in a world of suffering? Paul gives us two clear answers: not in the flesh, but in Christ.

## Part 1: Confidence Is Not Found in the Flesh (3:2-8)

### The Warning Against False Confidence (3:2-3)

> Watch out for those dogs, those evildoers, those mutilators of the flesh. For it is we who are the circumcision, we who serve God by his Spirit, who boast in Christ Jesus, and who put no confidence in the flesh.

Paul uses sharp language here to describe the Judaizers—those who insisted that Gentile Christians must also become Jews and be circumcised to be "true Christians." In the original Greek, his words are remarkably strong, employing alliteration and repetition. The warning "watch out" appears three times, creating the sense: "Watch out for those dogs, watch out for those devils, watch out for those destroyers."

**Why these harsh descriptions?** Dogs in antiquity were unclean animals that wandered through towns eating refuse. They symbolized spiritual uncleanness. The Judaizers were "evildoers" and "mutilators" because they relied on works of the law rather than on Christ's righteousness. They performed what they thought were good works, but Paul calls them evil because these works drew attention away from Christ himself.

But Paul reverses their accusation. He proclaims that Christians—even Gentiles without physical circumcision—are the true circumcision. We possess spiritual circumcision, not physical. As Colossians 2:11 explains:

> In him you were also circumcised with a circumcision not performed by human hands. Your whole self ruled by the flesh was put off when you were circumcised by Christ.

The old sign of the covenant (physical circumcision) has been replaced by a new reality. It is no longer about cutting skin; it's about cutting sin. Gentile Christians are fully Christian without physical circumcision. Their lack of that sign nullifies nothing.

### Paul's Impressive Credentials (3:4-6)

If anyone could boast in the flesh, it would be Paul. Look at his resume:

> Though I myself have reasons for such confidence. If someone else thinks they have reasons to put confidence in the flesh, I have more: circumcised on the eighth day, of the people of Israel, of the tribe of Benjamin, a Hebrew of Hebrews; in regard to the law, a Pharisee; as for zeal, persecuting the church; as for righteousness based on the law, faultless.

Paul possessed everything a devout Jew could claim:
- Circumcised on the eighth day (indicating pious parents)
- Of the tribe of Benjamin (descended from Israel's royal line)
- A Hebrew of Hebrews (pure Jewish heritage)
- A Pharisee (the most respected party in Jewish society)
- Zealous in keeping the law—so zealous he persecuted Christians
- Righteous according to the law—faultless in his observance

If anyone had legitimate grounds to find confidence in the flesh, Paul did. He had fulfilled every requirement of being a good Jew.

### Everything Becomes Loss (3:7-8)

Yet what does Paul do with all these credentials?

> But whatever were gains to me I now consider loss for the sake of Christ. What is more, I consider everything a loss because of the surpassing worth of knowing Christ Jesus my Lord, for whose sake I have lost all things. I consider them garbage, that I may gain Christ.

This is remarkable. Paul counts everything he once valued—his reputation, his privileges, his achievements—as rubbish. He uses the Greek word for refuse, waste, the kind of thing you throw into a garbage bin. He would throw away all his certificates.

**Where do we find confidence?**

We often place our confidence in:

1. **Inherited abilities**—our intelligence, talents, physical attractiveness, wealth, or inherited status
2. **Our achievements**—our grades, our job titles, our possessions, our accomplishments
3. **Our dreams**—the promise of a successful future with the right education, the right spouse, the right house, the right income, the right health

But can we really find lasting joy in these things? You can have all the success in the world, and in an instant, it's gone. A test failure devastates a teenager. A job loss shakes us. A health crisis changes everything. These things disappoint us because they are temporary—our achievements are fleeting, our wealth is unstable, our health is momentary. We are subject to physical limitations, sickness, disease, and death.

No wonder we're sad when they fail! Perhaps we should place less hope in them to begin with.

## Part 2: True Confidence Is in Christ (3:9-11)

### The Surpassing Worth of Knowing Christ (3:8-10)

> I consider everything a loss compared to the surpassing greatness of knowing Christ Jesus my Lord.

What replaces all these earthly things? Knowing Christ. Paul doesn't just say "believing in Christ"—he emphasizes *knowing* him.

> I want to know Christ—yes, to know the power of his resurrection and participation in his sufferings, becoming like him in his death, and so, somehow, attaining to the resurrection from the dead.

Knowing Christ means understanding two realities:

**First, the power of his resurrection.** Paul looks forward to our future resurrection. Just as Christ rose from the dead with power and glory, we will be transformed. Philippians 3:21 tells us that Jesus "will transform our lowly bodies so that they will be like his glorious body."

**Second, participation in his sufferings.** To know Christ is to share in what he experienced—suffering for others. This isn't self-harm or flagellation. It's the willing sacrifice of serving others, following his pattern of the cross. As we suffer for Christ and for others, we participate in what he has already accomplished.

### Joy Redefined: Jesus, Our Yokefellow (3:1, 3)

The word "joy" appears throughout Philippians, but Paul uses it in a way we must understand correctly. Joy doesn't mean constant happiness or the absence of suffering. Paul teaches us to redefine joy: **J.O.Y.—Jesus, Our Yokefellow.**

Jesus is the one who carried the yoke before us. He bore the burden of all humanity on the cross. When we suffer for others, we're not doing something unprecedented—we're participating in the pattern he established. He is our fellow traveler, our companion in the burden we carry.

This is why we can rejoice even in difficulty: because our confidence rests on Christ, who has already carried our ultimate burden. Just as Jordan was the money man you could trust in the final seconds—the one whose hands held the winning shot—Jesus is the one we trust in the final moment of life. When the game is nearly over, when all depends on one shot, there is only one person to pass the ball to: Jesus, our Yokefellow.

### The Basis of Our Righteousness (3:9)

> Be found in him, not having a righteousness of my own that comes from the law, but that which is through faith in Christ—the righteousness that comes from God on the basis of faith.

Why should we abandon confidence in circumcision and the law? Because Christ has already done what the law could never accomplish. On his way to the cross, he was whipped, his back raw. On the cross, his hands and feet were pierced and mutilated. He underwent the ultimate cutting—the penalty of sin was cut away from us and placed on him. He was cut off from the land of the living so that we might live.

This is where our righteousness comes from: not from our efforts, but from faith in Christ's completed work. We have been cleansed. We have been made righteous. Therefore, it would be absurd to now seek righteousness from the law.

## Application: Right Perspective on Earthly Things

We must develop a proper perspective on our achievements and privileges. This doesn't mean we should abandon education or career. Rather, we must understand that in contrast to salvation in Christ, these things are rubbish.

**Two critical challenges emerge:**

### 1. Do Not Over-Invest in Rubbish

If earthly things are rubbish compared to Christ, we must be careful not to sacrifice the eternal for the temporary. If we give up knowing Christ or gaining Christ in order to pursue career success, what have we gained? Is an 8-to-6 job in an office really worth eternity? When we stand before God, earthly credentials will mean nothing.

### 2. Do Not Be Distracted by Rubbish

We must not let temporary things distract us from what matters most. Imagine going on a bushwalk and seeing only litter while missing the trees and birds—what's the point? Similarly, we can become so focused on worldly success that we miss the beauty and reality of Christ.

These things will always disappoint us eventually. Our achievements are temporary. Our wealth is fleeting. Our health is momentary. One day we will have transformed bodies in a new heaven and earth that will never disappoint—but for now, we live with physical limitations.

**Ask yourself honestly:**
- Where have I been over-investing? In what have I placed too much hope?
- What major decisions have I made lately—were they for the kingdom and for Christ, or for self-comfort?
- Am I merely following the beat of the world and fleshly desires?
- Am I willing to give up earthly things for the sake of following Christ?

Can we beat peer pressure and take up our cross to follow Jesus? Will we set our face toward Calvary?

## Conclusion: Rejoice in Christ Alone

If you have set your face toward Calvary and committed to carrying your cross, then the opening command makes sense:

> Further, my brothers and sisters, rejoice in the Lord!

And the exhortation of verse 3 becomes real:

> Boast in Christ Jesus.

Rejoice because your confidence is in Christ alone. Rejoice even if everything else is rubbish—for that rubbish is meaningless compared to knowing him. Rejoice because as you suffer for others, you share in what Jesus has already suffered. You are participating in his pattern. He is your yokefellow who has already carried the weight.

When you see the word "joy," remember: **Jesus, Our Yokefellow.** He is the money man, the one you trust when everything is on the line. He is the clutch performer who came through in humanity's darkest hour. And he calls us to trust him in ours.

This is the true joy that Paul teaches—security and assurance rooted not in our achievements, our heritage, or our circumstances, but in Christ alone.
