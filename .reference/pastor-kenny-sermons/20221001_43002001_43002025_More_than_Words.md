# John 2:1-25

**Title:** More than Words?
**Date:** 1 October 2022 (ARISE)
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The First Sign (1-10)

The wedding at Cana in Galilee becomes the setting for Jesus' first miraculous sign. This was no casual gathering—Jewish marriages involved a lengthy process of arrangement, engagement, dowry exchange, and contractual agreements before the wedding itself, which represented the final step toward the consummation of the marriage. When the wine ran out mid-celebration, it was a genuine embarrassment for the groom and his family.

> On the third day a wedding took place at Cana in Galilee. Jesus' mother was there, and Jesus and his disciples had also been invited to the wedding. When the wine was gone, Jesus' mother said to him, "They have no more wine." Jesus replied, "Woman, why do you involve me? My hour has not yet come." His mother said to the servants, "Do whatever he tells you." (John 2:1-5)

Jesus uses the same title for his mother—"Woman"—that he will use again at the cross, suggesting something deeper than mere distance. There is tension in his response, though not harshness. His statement about his "hour" is cryptic here but becomes clearer throughout John's Gospel: the hour refers to his death and glorification, the moment when the Greeks come seeking him. Even then, Jesus was on a divine mission, constrained to preach the good news before his time ran out.

Yet Mary's quiet trust—"Do whatever he tells you"—suggests she understood something of who Jesus was. The servants, standing near six large stone ceremonial water jars used for Jewish ritual washing (each holding twenty to thirty gallons), received their instructions.

> Jesus said to the servants, "Fill the jars with water"; so they filled them to the brim. Then he told them, "Now draw some out and take it to the master of the banquet." They did so, and the master of the banquet tasted the water that had been turned into wine. He did not realize where it had come from, though the servants who had drawn the water knew. Then he called the bridegroom aside and said, "Everyone brings out the choice wine first and then the cheaper wine after the guests have had too much to drink; but you have saved the best till now." (John 2:7-10)

What Jesus did was remarkable not merely for its miraculous power, but for its quality and abundance. This was not a pinch of artificial flavoring or a subtle dye—this was the finest wine, the kind kept for the most honored guests. The abundance itself speaks volumes: water from six massive jars, not a modest pitcher.

**Why did Jesus do this?** Several interpretations offer insight:
- To demonstrate his power and authority
- To spare the groom from shame, especially significant since Jesus symbolically represents the groom in relation to his church
- To signal the replacement of the old ceremonial cleansing laws with something new
- To prefigure the rescue and renewal of the covenant between Christ and his church

> What Jesus did here in Cana of Galilee was the first of the signs through which he revealed his glory; and his disciples believed in him. (John 2:11)

## What a Sign Really Means (11)

In John's Gospel, Jesus' works are deliberately called "signs" (sēmeion), not merely "miracles" or "wonders." The distinction is important. While signs can be miraculous, and miracles can be wonderful, a sign is fundamentally something that *points beyond itself* to a greater reality.

Throughout Scripture, signs serve as pointers to covenant realities:
- The rainbow as a sign of God's covenant with creation
- Circumcision as a sign of the covenant with Abraham
- The infant Jesus wrapped in cloths and lying in a manger—a sign pointing to Isaiah's prophecies of the Messiah

The real power of a sign lies not in what it does, but in what it signifies.

**What does the wine signify?** It points to the fulfillment of Old Testament prophecies about the Messianic age—a time of abundance, joy, and restoration. In the midst of poverty, drought, and despair, the prophets foresaw a day of celebration:

> On this mountain the Lord Almighty will prepare a feast of rich food for all peoples, a banquet of aged wine—the best of meats and the finest of wines. (Isaiah 25:6)

> The threshing floors shall be full of grain; the vats shall overflow with wine and oil. (Joel 2:24)

This eschatological abundance echoes even earlier in Israel's history. When the Israelites faced thirst in the wilderness, God commanded Moses to strike the rock, and water flowed out. Here at Cana, Jesus transforms water into wine—something far greater than Moses' provision. He is not merely meeting a need; he is announcing the arrival of the promised age of blessing.

**The disciples' response was belief:** they saw in this sign the arrival of the Messiah, the fulfillment of generations of longing and prophecy. They recognized that the Word had become flesh, and this flesh was doing the work only God could do.

> Jesus performed many other signs in the presence of his disciples, which are not recorded in this book. But these are written that you may believe that Jesus is the Messiah, the Son of God, and that by believing you may have life in his name. (John 20:30-31)

The signs are recorded so that we, reading them, might follow these signs to their destination: Jesus himself, and through faith in him, eternal life.

## The Response of Rejection (12-25)

Yet not everyone responded with belief. Jesus next goes to the temple in Jerusalem, where he encounters a very different response.

> After this he went down to Capernaum with his mother and brothers and his disciples. When it was almost time for the Jewish Passover, Jesus went up to Jerusalem. In the temple courts he found people selling cattle, sheep and doves, and others sitting at tables exchanging money. So he made a whip out of cords, and drove all from the temple courts, both sheep and cattle; he scattered the coins of the money changers and overturned their tables. To those who sold doves he said, "Get these out of here! Stop turning my Father's house into a market!" (John 2:12-16)

Jesus has just demonstrated who he is through a sign of divine glory. Now he enters his Father's house and cleanses it—a prophetic action declaring his messianic authority. But the response was not universal belief.

> The Jews then responded to him, "What sign can you show us to prove your authority to do all this?" Jesus answered them, "Destroy this temple, and I will raise it again in three days." They replied, "It has taken forty-six years to build this temple, and you are going to raise it in three days?" But the temple he had spoken of was his body. After he was raised from the dead, his disciples recalled what he had said. Then they believed the scripture and the words that Jesus had spoken. (John 2:18-22)

Notice the response: instead of seeing the sign of the cleansed temple as evidence of Jesus' authority, they ask for *another* sign. This is a pattern throughout Scripture—the demand for repeated validation, which reveals not openness to God's revelation but a deeper skepticism and distrust.

> Even after Jesus had performed so many signs in their presence, they still would not believe in him. (John 12:37)

This refusal reveals something crucial about the human heart. The problem is not the absence of signs; it is the absence of genuine faith. When we keep asking God for "just one more sign" to validate what he has already shown us, we are essentially saying: *I don't really trust you. Your word isn't enough for me.*

**Consider the nature of this demand.** In ancient Israel, the pattern was clear:

> Surely all the men who have seen My glory and My signs which I performed in Egypt and in the wilderness, yet have put Me to the test these ten times and have not listened to My voice. (Numbers 14:22)

The Israelites witnessed plagues, the parting of the Red Sea, water from the rock, manna from heaven—yet they repeatedly demanded proof that God was with them. Their constant testing revealed not faith but unbelief.

> For indeed Jews ask for signs and Greeks search for wisdom; but we preach Christ crucified. (1 Corinthians 1:22)

**What does this mean for us today?** We live in an age that mirrors this ancient dynamic. We have the Word of God—the record of all Jesus' signs, their fulfillment of prophecy, and their testimony to his identity. Yet we are tempted to look for more evidence: experiences, miracles, spiritual confirmations. We want to say, "Give me a sign!"

But consider what we are actually saying when we do. Imagine a building with high security, where you must scan your card at every door and checkpoint. Why? Because they don't trust you to belong there. Similarly, when we repeatedly ask God to prove himself, we signal that we haven't truly given him our trust. 

Or think of a marriage where a couple exchanges vows, only to have one spouse insist on renewing them every weekend because of lingering doubts. That marriage has a problem not of commitment, but of belief. It's the same when we say to God, "I believe... but give me just one more sign."

**The good news is this:** We have been given all the signs we need. The signs are written down for us in Scripture—not on marble or stone, but recorded so that we might read them, recognize their fulfillment of Old Testament prophecy, and believe. All the messianic signs Jesus performed point to one destination: faith in him as the Messiah and Son of God.

> Now while he was in Jerusalem at the Passover Festival, many people saw the signs he was performing and believed in his name. But Jesus would not entrust himself to them, for he knew all people. He did not need any testimony about mankind, for he knew what was in each person. (John 2:23-25)

Many saw the signs and believed—but Jesus held back his full trust. He knew human hearts; he understood that belief based on miraculous signs alone is fragile and often self-serving. True belief must rest not on perpetual signs, but on the Word made flesh.

**The challenge for us is clear:** We are called to deliver those signs to others today—not by performing miracles, but by bearing witness to the written record of Jesus' signs, showing how they fulfill prophecy, and inviting people to follow those signs to faith in Christ. The Word of God is sufficient. The signs recorded in Scripture are sufficient. Our task is to help others read them and believe.
