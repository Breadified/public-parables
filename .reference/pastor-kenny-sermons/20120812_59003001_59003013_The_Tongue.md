# James 3:1-13

**Title:** The Tongue

**Date:** August 12, 2012

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Power of the Tongue (3:1-5a)

The tongue is a remarkable organ—small in size yet disproportionately powerful in its effects. James makes this comparison explicit:

> Likewise, the tongue is a small part of the body, but it makes great boasts.

This teaching shouldn't surprise us when we consider the nature of God's creative power. In Genesis 1, we learn that God made the entire universe by speaking. We, made in God's image, reflect that same creative power through our words. A president's words can send aid, change laws, or launch missiles. Death and life are in the power of the tongue (Proverbs 18:21).

James illustrates this principle with two vivid examples:

> When we put bits into the mouths of horses to make them obey us, we can turn the whole animal.

A horse, massive and powerful, is controlled by a small bit placed in its mouth. The rider steers the entire animal through this tiny instrument.

> Although they are so large and are driven by strong winds, they are steered by a very small rudder wherever the pilot wants to go.

A ship, enormous and driven by mighty winds, is directed by a small rudder at its back. Control the rudder, and you control the ship's direction.

Similarly, our words—though they come from such a small part of our bodies—have the power to change the whole direction of a person's life.

### Teachers Are Called to Greater Accountability

James begins his teaching on the tongue by addressing teachers specifically:

> Not many of you should become teachers, my fellow believers, because you know that we who teach will be judged more strictly.

Teachers and leaders speak frequently and therefore wield this power of life and death more significantly. They shape congregations and ministries. This influence brings greater responsibility and stricter judgment.

Yet the principle extends to all believers:

> We all stumble in many ways. Anyone who is never at fault in what they say is perfect, able to keep their whole body in check.

Every person is responsible for the power of their words. The average human speaks over 16,000 words per day—nearly 5.8 million per year. Add to this our digital communications: emails, social media posts, text messages. Each word we speak or type has an impact on ourselves and our hearers. Whether we constantly speak words of anger, complaint, care, or thanksgiving, they shape our souls dramatically. We are our words. What you say is who you are.

This power often goes unappreciated because the tongue is so small. We warn our children about electricity and driving, yet how often do we teach them how to speak to others? We enroll them in language classes but rarely in the art of kind, gracious speech. As parents and believers, we bear responsibility for shaping our words and the words of those we influence.

## The Danger of the Tongue (3:5b-12)

The tongue is extremely dangerous—compared throughout Scripture to a fire capable of spreading destruction:

> Consider what a great forest is set on fire by a small spark. The tongue also is a fire, a world of evil among the parts of the body. It corrupts the whole body, sets the whole course of one's life on fire, and is itself set on fire by hell.

One spark destroys an entire forest. One conversation can bring death to a relationship. One word can ruin a friendship. The tongue corrupts the entire person and sets the trajectory of life aflame.

### The Untamable Tongue

What makes the tongue particularly dangerous is its resistance to control:

> All kinds of animals, birds, reptiles and sea creatures are being tamed and have been tamed by mankind, but no human being can tame the tongue. It is a restless evil, full of deadly poison.

We can tame wild animals. Fish in an aquarium learn to come when we appear, even though they lack intelligence. Yet the tongue remains untamable—a restless evil, a vial of poison, a loaded gun.

### The Contradiction of Blessing and Cursing

The gravity of our speech becomes clear in this stark contradiction:

> With the tongue we praise our Lord and Father, and with it we curse human beings, who have been made in God's likeness. Out of the same mouth come praise and cursing. My brothers and sisters, this should not be.

This is the mark of a double-minded Christian—one who has not grasped that they now possess the implanted word (the gospel that saves). We curse not merely through profanity, but through harsh criticism, insults, slander, and gossip. We curse when we speak in ways that are self-centered, unhelpful, God-ignoring, and lead to death. True hate speech is emotional response that tears down others.

Scripture repeatedly calls us away from such speech:

> Do not let any unwholesome talk come out of your mouths, but only what is helpful for building others up according to their needs, that it may benefit those who listen. And do not grieve the Holy Spirit of God, with whom you were sealed for the day of redemption. Get rid of all bitterness, rage and anger, brawling and slander, along with every form of malice. Be kind and compassionate to one another, forgiving each other, just as in Christ God forgave you. (Ephesians 4:29-32)

And again:

> Let your conversation be always full of grace, seasoned with salt, so that you may know how to answer everyone. (Colossians 4:6)

### What Flows Out Reveals What's Within

James uses the imagery of springs and fruit-bearing trees to illustrate this principle:

> Can both fresh water and salt water flow from the same spring? My brothers and sisters, can a fig tree bear olives, or a grapevine bear figs? Neither can a salt spring produce fresh water.

Our words are like water flowing from a spring or fruit growing on a tree—by necessity they reflect what lies deep within us. An orange tree produces oranges because that is its nature. When we possess the implanted word—the gospel that saves us—our speech should naturally produce life-giving words.

## The Purpose of Our Tongue (3:13)

Our tongue exists for a specific purpose: to bless others.

> Who is wise and understanding among you? Let them show it by their good life, by deeds done in the humility that comes from wisdom.

To bless is simply to speak good words—words that are wholesome, healthy, God-honoring, and life-giving. When we bless God, we praise Him, speaking of His goodness. When God blesses us, He speaks of the good He has done and will do for us. When we bless others, we use our tongues to give life.

You might protest: "I don't have that kind of power." On the basis of God's Word, we must respectfully disagree. You wield power far greater than you realize. The real question is not whether you have power, but how you will use it.

### Practical Application

Let us commit to:

- **Encourage each other**—intentionally look for the good in our brothers and sisters
- **Thank each other**—express gratitude for acts of service and faithfulness
- **Praise good works**—observe and commend the godly deeds we see in one another
- **Correct with care**—when rebuke is necessary, offer it with utmost care and gentleness, perhaps thinking of ten positive things before speaking one critical thing

Remember: Our fellow Christians have been redeemed by Christ's blood. The implanted word lives in them. There is always something good to say about them.

Sundays are precious moments when we gather together, often sacrificing time and energy to be present. Let us use these opportunities to bless each other with kind words. Let us remind one another that we have been saved and loved by God—something we must never take for granted.

## Conclusion: The Words Spoken Over Us

The only way we can speak life-giving words is if we ourselves have been captured and transformed by the words spoken over us.

At the climax of human history, one man came to earth. Rather than curse those around Him, Jesus spoke blessing. His words gave life:

> He committed no sin, and no deceit was found in his mouth. When they hurled their insults at him, he did not retaliate; when he suffered, he made no threats. Instead, he entrusted himself to him who judges justly. (1 Peter 2:22-23)

Why did such a good man suffer? The answer is staggering: Jesus was cursed for us. God the Father spoke the curses we deserved upon Jesus so that He could speak His blessing to us.

If you follow Jesus, God is speaking this over you right now: *You are my beloved child, with whom I am well pleased.* At the end of your life, He will say: *Well done, good and faithful servant! Enter into the joy of your master.*

We deserve curses from God's judgment seat, but if we belong to Jesus, He will speak blessing over us instead. When you truly grasp the magnitude of this reversal—how God speaks to us now because of what Christ has done—you will find yourself using your words for life rather than death. Soaked in God's life-giving words, you will speak out of your soul like a spring pouring forth its water. You will become a person who gives life to those around you.
