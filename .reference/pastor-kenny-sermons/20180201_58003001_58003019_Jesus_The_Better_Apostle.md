# Hebrews 3:1-19

**Title:** Jesus: The Better Apostle

**Date:** 19 February 2018

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Introduction: Being Sent

When we send someone on behalf of our nation—whether as an emissary to strengthen partnerships or represent our interests—we're sending them as our representative. The Greek word *apostle* literally means "the sent one," derived from the same root as "post." Today we're examining how Jesus fulfills this role as God's ultimate apostle, and how He surpasses even the greatest apostle who came before Him: Moses.

## Moses: The Great Apostle

> Therefore, holy brothers and sisters, who share in the heavenly calling, fix your thoughts on Jesus, whom we acknowledge as our apostle and high priest. (Hebrews 3:1-2)

Before we understand Jesus's apostolic office, we must appreciate Moses's greatness. The author compares Jesus to Moses because Moses was sent by God Himself.

> Moses was faithful as a servant in all God's house, bearing witness to what would be spoken by God in the future. (Hebrews 3:5)

God sent Moses at Mount Sinai with a clear commission:

> "So now, go. I am sending you to Pharaoh to bring my people the Israelites out of Egypt." (Exodus 3:10)

Moses held a position so unique that when Miriam and Aaron questioned his authority, God Himself defended him:

> "But this is not true of my servant Moses; he is faithful in all my house. With him I speak face to face, clearly and not in riddles; he sees the form of the Lord." (Numbers 12:7-8)

This intimacy with God was unparalleled. The Scripture affirms:

> "The Lord would speak to Moses face to face, as a man speaks with his friend." (Exodus 33:11)

And later: "Since then, no prophet has risen in Israel like Moses, whom the Lord knew face to face." (Deuteronomy 34:10)

Moses wasn't just a prophet—he was God's friend, spoken to with a clarity and intimacy no other prophet experienced. When familiarity with his family came dangerously close to breeding contempt, God reminded them: don't forget who he is. Moses is God's sent one, and that changes everything.

## The Warning: A Hard Heart

Yet the remainder of this chapter shifts dramatically. The author moves from celebrating Moses to warning us about what happened when God's people failed to listen even to God's greatest apostle.

> See to it, brothers, that none of you has a sinful, unbelieving heart that turns away from the living God. (Hebrews 3:12)

After God freed Israel from Egypt and brought them to the edge of the Promised Land—the place of true rest from their slavery—they encountered the giants occupying Canaan. Despite witnessing God's power over Egypt, despite years of God's provision in the wilderness, the Israelites' hearts hardened with fear and unbelief.

> "Today, if you hear his voice, do not harden your hearts as you did in the rebellion, during the time of testing in the desert, where your fathers tested and tried me and for forty years saw what I did. That is why I was angry with that generation, and I said, 'Their hearts are always going astray, and they have not known my ways.' So I declared on oath in my anger, 'They shall never enter my rest.'" (Hebrews 3:7-11)

What happened in that desert reveals the progressive hardening of hearts:

- **Continual grumbling:** The entire journey from Egypt was marked by complaints—no bread, no water, no meat, "it was better in Egypt"
- **Failure at the threshold:** When the scouts returned with reports of the Canaanite tribes, fear overtook faith, despite God's promise
- **Hardened hearts (v.8):** They refused to trust God's voice
- **Astray hearts (v.10):** Their desires led them away from God's path
- **Unknown ways (v.10):** They rejected God's character and guidance

The consequence was severe: that entire generation wandered forty years in the wilderness without entering God's rest. Only Joshua and Caleb, who trusted God, entered the land.

> "Who were they who heard and rebelled? Were they not all those Moses led out of Egypt? And with whom was he angry for forty years? Was it not with those who sinned, whose bodies fell in the desert? And to whom did God swear that they would never enter his rest if not to those who disobeyed? So we see that they were not able to enter, because of their unbelief." (Hebrews 3:16-19)

This is the warning: unbelief and grumbling are not small matters. They are forms of disobedience that harden our hearts and distance us from God's rest.

## Jesus: The Greater Apostle

> "Jesus has been found worthy of greater honor than Moses, just as the builder of a house has greater honor than the house itself. For every house is built by someone, but God is the builder of everything." (Hebrews 3:3-4)

The author presents two pictures to illustrate why Jesus surpasses Moses:

### The Builder vs. the House

Moses may have been a faithful caretaker of God's house, managing it with integrity. But Jesus is the builder itself. Just as an owner has greater honor than the manager, Jesus—who created all things—has greater honor than Moses. We are the house God builds; Jesus is the one who builds.

### The Servant vs. the Son

> "But Christ is faithful as a son over God's house. And we are his house, if we hold on to our courage and the hope of which we boast." (Hebrews 3:6)

While Moses was faithful as a *servant* in God's house, Christ is faithful as a *son* over God's house. These are not equivalent positions.

A servant in the household has access to all areas—even the most intimate spaces—but enters to work, to serve, to clean. A son, however, has the same access but for a different purpose: for comfort, for belonging, for home. The son has privileges and intimacy the servant, however faithful, does not possess.

Moreover, the son carries responsibilities. Growing into maturity, the son oversees the household, manages its affairs, and carries the weight 24/7. The servant completes his tasks and leaves. But the son is there always.

Moses, however faithful and honored, was a servant. Jesus is the Son. If Moses was God's friend, Jesus is God's Son. And through Jesus, God has spoken to us with finality—His message sealed in blood.

## Today: Hear His Voice and Encourage One Another

This is why the author urges us: "Today, if you hear his voice, do not harden your hearts." (Hebrews 3:7-8)

God's voice comes to us not as audible words but as instruction for our present moment. He has spoken across generations and millennia, and He calls us to listen—today.

The pattern of Israel's failure teaches us what not to do. But how do we live differently? Through mutual encouragement:

> "But encourage one another daily, as long as it is called Today, so that none of you may be hardened by sin's deceitfulness. We have come to share in Christ if we hold firmly till the end the confidence we had at first." (Hebrews 3:13-14)

**Encourage one another:** Not mere positive affirmation, but pointing each other to Jesus. The very word suggests *courage*—helping one another find courage to keep following Christ.

**Daily:** Take every opportunity to build up the church (the people, not buildings) through your words and presence. Whether in formal small groups, informal gatherings, or family conversations, encourage one another.

**All of us:** Every Christian you meet is part of the house. Build them up. Stop grumbling and complaining; instead, invest in others' strength and growth. When we help others succeed, they can help us rise. When we focus on encouraging others rather than our own discontentment, we guard ourselves from unbelief.

**Today:** Don't wait. The opportunity is now. Whether through church gatherings, small groups, or family times of prayer and grace, find ways to speak God's truth to one another in love.

The promise is conditional: "We have come to share in Christ if we hold firmly till the end the confidence we had at first." Our perseverance in faith depends on our willingness to listen to God's voice through His Word and through His people, and to encourage one another to keep trusting Jesus—our greater apostle, our Son, our Lord.
