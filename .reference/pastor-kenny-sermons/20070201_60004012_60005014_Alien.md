# 1 Peter 4:12–5:14

**Title:** Alien  
**Date:** 11 February 2007  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Introduction: The Challenge of Being Different

We naturally resist standing out. Whether it's fashion, technology, or appearance, we want to fit in. Yet sometimes being different brings genuine joy—excelling at something, achieving a distinction, or simply being rare. For Christians, however, being different takes on a deeper significance. As we conclude 1 Peter, we discover that our difference extends to how we understand and respond to suffering.

---

## 1. True Joy in Suffering

### Don't Be Surprised

> Dear friends, do not be surprised at the painful trial you are suffering, as though something strange were happening to you. (1 Peter 4:12)

Peter urges us not to treat suffering as alien or unexpected. The "fiery trial" is not strange—it comes with the territory of following Christ. This is not suffering of any kind, however. Peter clarifies:

> If you suffer, it should not be as a murderer or thief or any other kind of criminal, or even as a meddler. (1 Peter 4:15)

Our suffering should come from doing good and standing for Christ, not from our own wrongdoing or interference in others' affairs.

### Rejoice in Participation with Christ

> But rejoice that you participate in the sufferings of Christ, so that you may be overjoyed when his glory is revealed. If you are insulted because of the name of Christ, you are blessed, for the Spirit of glory and of God rests on you. (1 Peter 4:13–14)

When we suffer for Christ's sake, we participate (*koinonia*—fellowship) with Him. We share in His suffering and join ourselves to Him. This is not about wanting to be different; it's about being willing to follow Christ completely, even when it costs us. We reject "fair weather" faith—those who gladly accept God's gifts but refuse to join Him in His suffering. Jesus Himself modeled this, entering fully into human experience, both blessing and hardship, so that through His death He might destroy the power of death itself (Hebrews 2:14).

### The Order of Glory

> However, if you suffer as a Christian, do not be ashamed, but praise God that you bear that name. (1 Peter 4:16)

There is an order to God's work: suffering comes first, then glory. This present life comes before the next. We live in triumph not through the absence of pain, but through the assurance of what is coming. The hymn "Blessed Be Your Name" captures this truth perfectly:

> When I'm found in the desert place,  
> Though I walk through the wilderness,  
> Blessed be Your name.

Job understood this order. When his wife urged him to curse God and die, he responded:

> Shall we accept good from God, and not trouble? (Job 2:10)

And when his calamities came, he declared:

> The LORD gave and the LORD has taken away; may the name of the LORD be praised. (Job 1:21)

This is not triumphalism—the false belief that God will always grant prosperity and comfort. Rather, it is the deeper joy that comes from hope in God's future, not from the circumstances of the present moment.

### Judgment Begins with God's House

> For it is time for judgment to begin with the family of God; and if it begins with us, what will the outcome be for those who do not obey the gospel of God? If it is hard for the righteous to be saved, what will become of the ungodly and the sinner? (1 Peter 4:17–18)

This is a time of purification for God's people. The "fiery trial" is both judgment and refinement. The righteous go through this purifying fire—like Noah enduring the forty days of the flood—and emerge refined. For the ungodly, the outcome is far worse. Our suffering now is a sign of belonging to God's household, a mark of our salvation.

## 2. Therefore: Commitment and Perseverance

> So then, those who suffer according to God's will should commit themselves to their faithful Creator and continue to do good. (1 Peter 4:19)

When suffering comes as a result of doing good and living according to God's will, we entrust ourselves to our faithful Creator. We do not lose faith; we do not stop doing good. We persevere.

### Two Perspectives on Suffering

**The Secular View:** Unjust suffering is proof that God does not exist. When innocent people suffer unfairly, people conclude that no good God could permit this. But Scripture reverses this logic. Suffering for Christ is not the problem—it is the assurance. It is the sign that we belong to Him.

**The Prosperity Gospel:** Some teach that God never wants us to suffer. But this passage speaks clearly: when you suffer for doing good, you suffer according to God's will. A mature faith understands that suffering for righteousness is God's will, and in it we find assurance of our identity in Christ.

### Suffering in Daily Life

Many young believers face suffering today. Those with non-Christian parents cannot attend certain events, cannot make certain choices. One young woman was forced to choose between badminton and church—and she chose church. Such choices bring real pain.

We also suffer through cultural opposition. The media, through constant repetition, has convinced many that Christians are simpletons without evidence for their faith, that the world and its culture have more to offer than Scripture. This creates an inferiority complex. We must resist this lie and stand firm in what we know to be true.

## 3. Leadership: Sacrificial Shepherding

> To the elders among you, I appeal as a fellow elder, a witness of Christ's sufferings and one who also will share in the glory to be revealed: Be shepherds of God's flock that is under your care, serving as overseers—not because you must, but because you are willing, as God wants you to be; not greedy for money, but eager to serve; not lording it over those entrusted to you, but being examples to the flock. And when the Chief Shepherd appears, you will receive the crown of glory that will never fade away. (1 Peter 5:1–4)

Leaders must be sacrificial shepherds. They serve willingly, not grudgingly. They do not seek comfort or avoid the cost of ministry. They spend time with the flock, look for opportunities to serve, and do not invent excuses to avoid community. When leaders embrace this sacrificial model, those they lead learn what it means to follow Christ.

---

## 4. Followers: Humble Submission

> Young men, in the same way be submissive to those who are older. All of you, clothe yourselves with humility toward one another, because, "God opposes the proud but gives grace to the humble." Humble yourselves, therefore, under God's mighty hand, that he may lift you up in due time. Cast all your anxiety on him because he cares for you. (1 Peter 5:5–7)

As leaders sacrifice, followers practice humility. This is not weakness; it is strength. God opposes pride but gives grace to the humble. When we humble ourselves under God's mighty hand, He will lift us up in His time. We cast our anxieties on Him, trusting that He cares for us.

### Vigilance in Faith

> Be self-controlled and alert. Your enemy the devil prowls around like a roaring lion looking for someone to devour. Resist him, standing firm in the faith, because you know that your brothers throughout the world are undergoing the same kind of sufferings. (1 Peter 5:8–9)

We are not alone in our struggle. Christians everywhere face similar sufferings. This knowledge strengthens us to resist the enemy and stand firm.

### The God of All Grace

> And the God of all grace, who called you to his eternal glory in Christ, after you have suffered a little while, will himself restore you and make you strong, firm and steadfast. To him be the power for ever and ever. Amen. (1 Peter 5:10–11)

Suffering is temporary. God's grace is eternal. After a little while of suffering, God will restore, strengthen, and establish us. This is our hope.

---

## Conclusion: The Alien Life

Peter's letter is summed up in a single word: *alien*. We are aliens in this world because:

- We are different in where we are going (heaven, not this world)
- We are different in how we behave (righteousness, not worldliness)
- We are different in our priorities (doing good, serving others)
- We are different in our view of suffering (not as punishment, but as participation in Christ)

As you move forward—finishing school or university, beginning new jobs, entering new relationships—life will unfold in various ways. Some seasons will be good. Other times you may suffer for no apparent reason, or suffer simply for doing good. Whatever comes, remember: you are part of God's household, marked by His Spirit, and assured of His glory. Stand firm. Blessed be Your name.
