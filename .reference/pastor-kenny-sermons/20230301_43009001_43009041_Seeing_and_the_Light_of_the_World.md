# John 9:1-41

**Title:** Seeing and the Light of the World

**Date:** 5 March 2023 (ARISE); 31 January 2016 (SMAC)

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## The Blind Man's Condition

As Jesus traveled, he encountered a man who had been blind from birth. This was not a temporary affliction—the man had never known sight. His blindness meant more than the absence of vision; in that ancient context, it meant poverty, unemployment, and social shame. A blind person could not work or contribute to society. The man survived by begging.

When the disciples saw him, they asked a question that echoes through the centuries: "Rabbi, who sinned—this man or his parents—that he was born blind?"

## Understanding Suffering and Sin

The disciples' question wasn't unreasonable. Scripture itself connects sin to suffering. In John 5, Jesus tells a healed man, "See, you are well again. Stop sinning or something worse may happen to you." The Old Testament law warns that God will punish the children for the sin of the fathers to the third and fourth generation. Parents' sins can indeed affect their children—sexually transmitted diseases passed from mother to child, for instance, are a tragic reality that causes blindness in countless children worldwide.

Yet Jesus offered a third perspective: "Neither this man nor his parents sinned."

This doesn't deny that sin produces suffering. All suffering traces back to Adam and Eve's original disobedience. But Jesus was saying something crucial: not all suffering has a direct, traceable cause in personal or ancestral sin. Sometimes innocent people, especially children, suffer—and there is no discernible reason we can point to. This is the mystery that troubles us most.

## God's Purpose in Suffering

Jesus revealed the real explanation: "This happened so that the works of God might be displayed in him. As long as it is day, we must do the works of him who sent me. Night is coming, when no one can work. While I am in the world, I am the light of the world."

The answer to suffering lies not in causality but in purpose. God's purposes transcend our ability to trace why suffering happens. There is no innocent suffering outside God's sovereign plan. Sometimes, like in Job's case, God permits suffering as a test. Other times, like Jesus's suffering, it serves redemption. But always—always—God is working toward his purposes.

**When facing suffering, ask yourself:**
- Did I bring this on myself through sin? If so, repent.
- Did my ancestors' sins cause this? If so, honor their memory by living differently and leaving a better legacy.
- Is there no discernible cause? Then trust that God has purposes you may not yet see. Romans 8:28 promises that all things work together for good for those who love God. Be patient. There is a purpose.

## The Miracle and Its Meaning

Jesus then performed a remarkable healing. He spat on the ground, made mud, put it on the man's eyes, and told him to go wash in the Pool of Siloam (which means "Sent"). The man went, washed, and came home seeing.

Why such an elaborate method? Jesus could heal with a word—as he did with the official's son ("Your son will live") and the paralyzed man ("Pick up your mat and walk"). This convoluted process seems deliberate, perhaps to challenge the Pharisees' rigid definitions of Sabbath law.

The healing fulfilled Isaiah 35's promise: "Then will the eyes of the blind be opened and the ears of the deaf unstopped."

## Jesus Under Scrutiny

When the religious authorities questioned the healed man, they focused on one thing: Jesus had made mud and healed on the Sabbath. According to their traditions, this violated the law.

"This man is not from God, for he does not keep the Sabbath," some said. Others countered, "How can a sinner perform such signs?"

The basis of their accusation was simple: keeping the Sabbath is the fourth commandment. God gave rest as a gift to freed slaves—a declaration that humans are not machines, not beasts of burden. The Pharisees had extrapolated this command into thirty-nine specific prohibitions about what constituted "work." But Jesus operated from a different principle: God's work of salvation continues on the Sabbath. Healing is always permissible. Mercy is always right.

Jesus was the light of the world, doing the Father's work during daylight. As he would later say, "Walk while you have the light, before darkness overtakes you. Believe in the light while you have the light, so that you may become children of light."

## The Investigation

The Pharisees brought in the man's parents to verify the miracle was real. But the parents, afraid of being expelled from the synagogue for acknowledging Jesus as Messiah, refused to commit themselves. "Ask him," they said. "He is of age."

Then the Pharisees questioned the man again: "We know this man is a sinner. Tell us the truth."

The healed man responded with remarkable clarity: "Whether he is a sinner or not, I don't know. One thing I do know: I was blind but now I see."

When they pressed him on how Jesus opened his eyes, the man grew bold: "I have told you already and you did not listen. Why do you want to hear it again? Do you want to become his disciples too?"

The Pharisees erupted: "You are this fellow's disciple! We are disciples of Moses! We know that God spoke to Moses, but as for this man, we don't even know where he comes from."

The formerly blind man then made a remarkable theological argument: "Now that is remarkable! You don't know where he comes from, yet he opened my eyes. We know that God does not listen to sinners. He listens to the godly person who does his will. Nobody has ever heard of opening the eyes of someone born blind. If this man were not from God, he could do nothing."

Through simple observation and logic, he concluded what the learned Pharisees refused to see: Jesus must be from God.

## The True Blindness

For this testimony, the Pharisees threw the man out of the synagogue.

But then Jesus found him and asked, "Do you believe in the Son of Man?"

"Who is he, sir?" the man asked. "Tell me so that I may believe."

"You have now seen him; in fact, he is the one speaking with you."

"Lord, I believe," the man said, and he worshiped Jesus.

This was the man's true spiritual awakening. He had been given physical sight, but now he received spiritual sight—genuine faith in Jesus as Lord.

Meanwhile, some Pharisees asked Jesus, "Are we blind too?"

Jesus replied: "If you were blind, you would not be guilty of sin; but now that you claim you can see, your guilt remains."

The irony is profound. The man born blind became the one who could truly see. The Pharisees, with their functioning eyes and their religious training, were the ones who were truly blind. They had seen the evidence repeated over and over—the man's testimony, the neighbors' confirmation, the parents' admission, Jesus's own declarations—yet they refused to believe. Their guilt remained precisely because they claimed to see while rejecting the truth.

## Hearing Over Sight

There is a strange and beautiful truth embedded in this passage. The blind man never actually saw Jesus perform the miracle. He was blind at the time. He went to the pool and washed, and then he could see—but Jesus was already gone. Yet by the end of the chapter, he understood more about Jesus than those with functioning eyes.

How? Through hearing.

The blind man listened to Jesus's words. He heard what others said. He listened to the Pharisees' questions and eventually grew bold enough to answer them with truth. And when Jesus found him and spoke to him, he believed.

When the blind man sarcastically suggested the Pharisees might want to become Jesus's disciples, he was unknowingly describing what faith requires: hearing and believing, again and again.

The Pharisees could see. Seeing can be deceiving. They witnessed miracles but did not listen to what those miracles meant. They heard God's word repeated countless times but refused to hear it.

But for now, in this age, hearing is the most important faculty. One day we will see God face to face, but today faith comes by hearing. The Pharisees saw but did not hear. The blind man heard and believed.

## The Call to Faith

Jesus's final words in this passage challenge us: "If you were blind, you would not be guilty of sin; but now that you claim you can see, your guilt remains."

We cannot claim ignorance. We have heard the gospel. We have seen the evidence of God's work. The question that faces each of us is the same one Jesus posed to the healed man: Do you believe in the Son of Man?

Will you hear his voice as his words are spoken to you today? Will you believe?
