## The Cosmic Conflict

We're looking at one of the most famous feuds in history: the confrontation between Moses and Pharaoh. But this wasn't merely a personal or political conflict—it was fundamentally a clash between the God of Israel and the gods of Egypt. Who would prevail? That's the question at the heart of these passages.

The context is clear: Israel, God's people, were enslaved in Egypt under Pharaoh's rule. God sent Moses to secure their freedom. What unfolds is a series of divine judgments that would reveal the supremacy of the one true God over all the false gods Egypt trusted in.

---

## God's Authority Through Moses

> **Exodus 7:1-5**
> 
> Then the Lord said to Moses, "See, I have made you like God to Pharaoh, and your brother Aaron will be your prophet. You are to say everything I command you, and your brother Aaron is to tell Pharaoh to let the Israelites go out of his country. But I will harden Pharaoh's heart, and though I multiply my signs and wonders in Egypt, he will not listen to you. Then I will lay my hand on Egypt and with mighty acts of judgment I will bring out my divisions, my people the Israelites. And the Egyptians will know that I am the Lord when I stretch out my hand against Egypt and bring the Israelites out of it."

### Moses as God's Representative

When the Lord says "I have made you like God to Pharaoh," He's establishing Moses as His visible representative. This doesn't mean Moses would become divine, but rather that he would act with divine authority in Pharaoh's presence. When Moses spoke, it was as though God Himself was speaking. When Moses acted, it was God acting through him.

Think of it like a power of attorney—when someone holds your legal authority, they can bind you with their decisions. In ancient Near Eastern culture, gods weren't typically encountered directly by ordinary people. Instead, they made themselves known through their representatives and emissaries. Moses became the tangible expression of God's will and power, something Pharaoh could directly confront.

This directly challenged Pharaoh's self-perception. The Egyptian ruler considered himself divine—the Son of Ra, a god himself. But now he would face someone speaking with even greater authority.

### Signs and Wonders as Judgment

God promises to multiply His "signs and wonders" and perform "mighty acts of judgment." These would eventually become the ten plagues—events so devastating that they would strip away Egypt's confidence in its own gods and reveal the supremacy of the one true God.

---

## The Plagues as Judgment on Egypt's Gods

The ten plagues weren't random disasters. They were strategically targeted judgments against the false gods Egypt worshipped and depended upon.

### The Water Turned to Blood

> **Exodus 7:17-21**
> 
> This is what the Lord says: By this you will know that I am the Lord: With the staff that is in my hand I will strike the water of the Nile, and it will be changed into blood. The fish in the Nile will die, and the river will stink; the Egyptians will not be able to drink its water. Moses and Aaron did just as the Lord had commanded. He raised his staff in the presence of Pharaoh and his officials and struck the water of the Nile, and all the water was changed into blood.

The first plague targeted **Hapi**, the Egyptian god of the Nile River. The Nile was the lifeblood of Egyptian civilization—the source of their agriculture, their economy, their very survival. To see it turn to blood, to see the fish die and the water become unusable, was to see one of Egypt's most important deities utterly humiliated. God demonstrated His power over what Egypt trusted most.

### Other Plagues, Other Gods

The plague of frogs was directed against **Heqet**, the goddess of fertility and childbirth. Once considered sacred, frogs became a source of plague and humiliation—a stunning reversal of perceived divine power.

The plague of gnats or lice came from the dust of the earth, attacking the domain of **Geb**, the Egyptian god of the earth itself. The very soil that was supposed to be under divine protection became an instrument of judgment.

One by one, each plague was designed to demonstrate that the gods Egypt trusted in were powerless before the true God. Each plague was a direct challenge to a specific idol, stripping away layer by layer the false confidence the Egyptians placed in their spiritual authorities.

### The Ultimate Declaration

As Exodus 12:12 reveals:

> "On that same night I will pass through Egypt and strike down every firstborn of both people and animals, and I will bring judgment on all the gods of Egypt. I am the Lord."

This was never simply about political liberation. It was a theological confrontation—God declaring His exclusive supremacy and demanding sole allegiance.

## Our Modern Idols

Here's where the ancient narrative becomes intensely personal to us. In many ways, we're not unlike the ancient Egyptians. We too have erected a pantheon of gods—not carved in stone, but deeply embedded in our hearts and daily choices.

### The Gods We Trust

We trust in the financial gods—the ASX, economists, market analysts—to secure our economic future. We worship at the altar of technology and information, bowing to Google and social media platforms to tell us what's true and what positions align with "the right side of history." We look to brands like Coca-Cola and Christian Dior to tell us what to drink and what to wear. We sacrifice time and money to the gods of travel—Flight Centre and Webjet—seeking happiness through pilgrimage to the right destinations. We serve the gods of education and achievement—ATAR and the HSC—believing that performing these sacrifices will guarantee us a blessed life.

And underneath it all, we worship at the great temples of consumerism, materialism, and hedonism. We trust these gods wholeheartedly, almost unquestioningly, throughout our weeks.

### The Exclusive God

But here's what sets the true God apart: He is not merely a Sunday god, confined to our prayers and Bible reading. He claims dominion over Monday through Saturday. He has authority over what we do in the afternoon, at our tutoring sessions, at our sports events, at school, and over what we're learning. He holds opinions about economics, about what truly satisfies, about what leads to genuine blessing.

The critical question becomes: Who are the competing gods in our hearts? When we strip away the surface and ask what we truly trust in and depend on, what do we find? If we're willing to examine ourselves honestly, we might discover that our hearts are divided between the God of Scripture and the modern idols of materialism and pleasure-seeking.

In our moments of rest, do we find ourselves planning how to serve God—reaching out to a struggling coworker, texting someone who's sick, organizing a Bible study—or are we scrolling through Amazon and Facebook Marketplace, asking "What can I buy to feel satisfied?" Do we spend our free time consuming entertainment endlessly, or are we investing in the things of God? Do we plan our vacations around God's purposes, or are we asking "Where can I go that will make me happy?"

The truth is this: there is only one God, and He does not share His throne with anyone or anything else.

## Pharaoh's Hardened Heart

> **Exodus 7:8-16**
> 
> When Pharaoh says to you, "Perform a miracle," then say to Aaron, "Take your staff and throw it down before Pharaoh," and it will become a snake." So Moses and Aaron went to Pharaoh and did just as the Lord commanded. Aaron threw his staff down in front of Pharaoh and his officials, and it became a snake. Pharaoh then summoned wise men and sorcerers, and the Egyptian magicians also did the same things by their secret arts: Each one threw down his staff and it became a snake. But Aaron's staff swallowed up their staffs. Yet Pharaoh's heart became hard and he would not listen to them, just as the Lord had said.

### Testing God Through Demands for Signs

Pharaoh's demand for a miracle reveals a dangerous spiritual posture: asking God to prove Himself through signs is never a healthy foundation for faith. Testing God by demanding miraculous proof is ultimately testing Him out of unbelief.

This pattern appears throughout Scripture. When Herod saw Jesus, he was eager to witness a miracle, hoping Jesus would perform "a sign of some sort." The Pharisees came to Jesus demanding He show them a sign from heaven to validate His claims. Even the devil approached Jesus with this temptation: "If you are the Son of God, throw yourself down. For it is written, 'He will command his angels concerning you, and they will lift you up in their hands.'"

In each case, the demand for a sign revealed a heart condition: skepticism masquerading as reasonableness. Jesus Himself said:

> "Do not put the Lord your God to the test." (Deuteronomy 6:16)

The harsh reality is that signs and wonders alone cannot change a hardened heart. Jesus illustrated this with a parable about the rich man and Lazarus:

> "If they do not listen to Moses and the Prophets, they will not be convinced even if someone rises from the dead." (Luke 16:31)

Why? Because true faith depends on the condition of the heart, not on the accumulation of evidence.

### The Cycle of Hardening

> **Exodus 7:13-16**
> 
> Yet Pharaoh's heart became hard and he would not listen to them, just as the Lord had said. Then the Lord said to Moses, "Pharaoh's heart is unyielding; he refuses to let the people go. Go to Pharaoh in the morning as he goes out to the river. Confront him on the bank of the Nile, and take in your hand the staff that was changed into a snake. Then say to him, 'The Lord, the God of the Hebrews, has sent me to say to you: Let my people go, so that they may worship me in the wilderness. But until now you have not listened.'"

Throughout these chapters, a devastating pattern repeats: a plague strikes, Pharaoh appears to relent, but once the plague is lifted, he hardens his heart again and refuses to release the Israelites. This cycle exposes the deceptive nature of sin and the tragedy of half-hearted, temporary repentance.

When God applies pressure to Pharaoh's heart, what comes out is rebellion and stubbornness. The hardening—whether through Pharaoh's own choice or through God's direct intervention—reveals what's truly inside. And what emerges is refusal, pride, and defiance.

Pharaoh loses every time because his heart remains fixed against God. He makes excuses, he attempts to negotiate, he seeks to limit the damage rather than truly submit. But each time, he chooses resistance over obedience.

### A Warning for Our Hearts

The hardening of Pharaoh's heart is a sober warning for all of us. When we hear God's Word and repeatedly choose to ignore it, when we experience consequences but refuse to genuinely repent, we risk hardening our own hearts. And a hardened heart becomes increasingly resistant to the Holy Spirit's work.

The gospel offers hope precisely here: even the hardest heart can be softened by God's grace. But that grace must be received, not resisted.

## Jesus: God Made Visible

The exodus narrative finds its ultimate fulfillment and meaning in Jesus Christ. Where Moses was a representative of God's authority, Jesus is God Himself made flesh—the invisible God who became visible, embodying the fullness of God's authority and power in human form.

What was once unseen became seen. In Jesus, God didn't communicate through intermediaries or signs alone; He came personally, demonstrating His authority through countless miracles and ultimately through the greatest sign of all: His death and resurrection from the dead.

Jesus performed signs and wonders during His earthly ministry. He healed the sick, cast out demons, multiplied food, calmed storms—displays of divine power that authenticated His identity and mission. But unlike Pharaoh's demand for casual entertainment, these miracles revealed God's compassion and kingdom purposes.

Most importantly, Jesus loved us and died for us. He performed the ultimate sign: His own resurrection. He calls us now to obedience—not blind obedience, but obedience rooted in love and trust. He calls us to take His side, to align ourselves with His kingdom and His purposes.

### The Choice Before Us

Just as ancient Egypt faced a choice—to acknowledge God's supremacy or to harden their hearts—we face the same choice. We must decide: Will we continue trusting in the false gods of our culture? Or will we surrender our hearts entirely to the one true God?

This isn't a question for Sundays only. It's a question that confronts us Monday through Saturday, in our choices about what we consume, where we place our hopes, what we pursue for happiness, and what we're willing to sacrifice for.

The God of the Bible makes an exclusive claim on our allegiance. He is not content to share His throne with our idols. And He proves His supremacy not through coercion, but through the demonstrated power of His love—a love so complete it led Him to the cross and beyond.

The question each of us must answer is simply this: Will we obey?
