# Luke 18:1–14, 19:1–10

**Title:** Nagging for Justice, Mercy, and Jesus
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Nagging for Justice (18:1–8)

We all know what nagging is—that persistent, repeated asking that we hate hearing. But in Scripture, we find nagging can be a powerful spiritual discipline when directed toward God and justice.

Jesus tells a parable of contrast between two characters: an unjust judge and a widow.

> In a certain town there was a judge (krites) who neither feared God nor cared what people thought. And there was a widow in that town who kept coming to him with the plea, "Grant me justice (ekdik-) against my adversary (anti-dik-)." (Luke 18:2–3)

**The Characters**

The judge is powerful—unrestrained by conscience or accountability. He "neither feared God nor cared what the people thought." When men are restricted by neither God nor society, they become dangerous.

The widow is the weakest in society. Without a husband to defend her, she is vulnerable and defenseless. Someone has wronged her—perhaps swindled her money, stolen from her, or physically assaulted her. She comes to the judge asking for justice: "Give me righteousness. Make it right for me. Act against my adversary."

At first glance, this seems impossible. He is powerful; she is nobody. Why would he care about her plea?

> "For some time he refused. But finally he said to himself, 'Even though I don't fear God or care what people think, yet because this widow keeps bothering me, I will see that she gets justice (ekdik-), so that she won't eventually come and attack me!'" (Luke 18:4–5)

Despite his indifference, the widow's relentless persistence wears him down. He finally caves.

**Practical Application: Lobbying for Justice**

This parable teaches us something important about how the world works: if you want justice, you must persistently ask for it. We cannot take vigilante action. We must appeal to legal systems and proper authorities.

When we disagree with laws or governance, we don't simply break them. Instead, we must lobby—petition authorities to change unjust policies. We sign petitions. We voice our concerns. We participate in the democratic process. We advocate for change through proper channels, not through violence or threats.

Consider the recent Coopers brewery controversy. The Bible Society released a video for their 200th anniversary featuring two politicians discussing gay marriage in a civil, respectful conversation using reflective listening. What could be more innocent? Yet a backlash ensued. Through social media pressure and threats, the beer company was forced to distance itself from the Bible Society and issue a statement affirming gay marriage—against their own conscience.

This is how the world operates: through relentless lobbying and pressure. We must learn to do the same, but righteously—without threats or violence. We can petition governments, sign petitions, and advocate for traditional values through organizations like the Australian Christian Lobby.

**A Wider Application: Prayer and Persistence**

But this parable points to something deeper than earthly justice:

> Then Jesus told his disciples a parable to show them that they should always pray (proseuxo) and not give up (not lose heart). (Luke 18:1)

Jesus teaches that we should always pray. When the Lord's Prayer says "Your kingdom come, your will be done," we're asking God to establish His justice on earth. We should cry out to God day and night for justice—not just in our personal circumstances, but for our nation, our justice system, our leaders.

> "Listen to what the unjust judge says. And will not God bring about justice (ekdik-) for his chosen ones, who cry out to him day and night? Will he keep putting them off? I tell you, he will see that they get justice (ekdik-), and quickly." (Luke 18:6–8)

The point is clear: if an unjust judge eventually relents, then certainly the Judge of the Universe—who is righteous and loves His people—will answer. Of course He will bring justice and righteousness.

**The Critical Question**

Yet there's a sobering question at the end of this passage:

> "However, when the Son of Man comes, will he find faith on the earth?" (Luke 18:8)

When Jesus returns to execute final justice, it won't be piecemeal as it is today. He won't just fix your problem. He will fix everyone's problems. He will punish every wrongdoer—including mine and yours. The question becomes: will He find faithfulness? Will He find people who persist in faith?

As one scholar notes, "the faith" here "signifies faithfulness, expressed in unfailing prayer." We must not just nag for justice; we must nag with perseverance and faith until Christ returns.

## Nagging for Mercy (18:9–14)

After asking for justice, we must also ask for something else: mercy. This second parable reveals the danger of spiritual pride.

> Two men went up to the temple to pray (proseuxo), one a Pharisee and the other a tax collector. (Luke 18:10)

**The Characters**

The Pharisee is a do-gooder, a religious person. The tax collector (publican) is a government official—associated with corruption and exploitation.

**The Pharisee's Prayer**

> The Pharisee stood by himself and prayed: "God, I thank you that I am not like other people—robbers, evildoers (adikoi), adulterers—or even like this tax collector. I fast twice a week and give a tenth of all I get." (Luke 18:11–12)

Notice what the Pharisee does: he prays to himself, not to God. There is no request—only comparison and boasting. He sees others as inferior: "I am not like other people" and "not like this tax collector." His righteousness is built on comparison, not on God's standard.

This is virtue signaling. Today's version might be: "Thank God I am not like the rest—the bigots, homophobes, racists, and sexists." We construct our own righteousness based on narrow conditions we set (supporting the right causes) rather than on God's standard of righteousness.

> To some who were confident of their own righteousness (dikai-) and looked down on everyone else, Jesus told this parable. (Luke 18:9)

**The Tax Collector's Prayer**

By contrast, observe the tax collector:

> "But the tax collector stood at a distance. He would not even look up to heaven, but beat his breast and said, 'God, have mercy on me (propitiate me of my sins), a sinner.' (hamatolos)" (Luke 18:13)

Every detail matters. He stands at a distance—aware of his unworthiness. He won't look up to heaven—the posture of someone guilty. He beats his breast—genuine sorrow. And he makes a request: "God, have mercy on me, a sinner."

**The Result**

> "I tell you that this man, rather than the other, went home justified before God. For all those who exalt themselves will be humbled, and those who humble themselves will be exalted." (Luke 18:14)

The tax collector went down from the temple justified—righteous before God. The principle is simple: those who exalt themselves are brought low; those who humble themselves are lifted up.

**A Key Insight**

Christians tend to be more self-reflective than others. In a room of atheists and secular progressives, you'll find self-righteousness and smugness. In a room of Christians, you'll find people wrestling with their sin, aware of their capacity to deceive themselves, genuinely struggling.

This is healthy. Self-reflection matters. But after that reflection, we must remember: we are justified before God through Christ. We are cleansed. We are righteous—not in ourselves, but in Him. So we must also be confident: not in our own righteousness, but in the righteousness Christ has given us.

When facing criticism or doubt, humble yourself first—check yourself. But then return to the cross. Christ has propitiated our sins on our behalf. We are justified. Be confident in that.

## Nagging for Jesus (19:1–10)

The third parable brings it all together. The answer to both justice and mercy is Jesus.

> Jesus entered Jericho and was passing through. A man was there by the name of Zacchaeus; he was a chief tax collector and was wealthy. He wanted to see who Jesus was, but because he was short he could not see over the crowd. So he ran ahead and climbed a sycamore-fig tree to see him, since Jesus was coming that way. (Luke 19:1–4)

**Connections to the Previous Stories**

Notice the parallels:
- We have two characters again
- Zacchaeus is a chief tax collector—the tax collector of tax collectors, rich and powerful
- Like the widow who "kept coming," Zacchaeus is determined to see Jesus
- Like the tax collector in the temple, Zacchaeus climbs—goes up—to pray, but here he climbs a tree to see Jesus
- By being in the tree, he looks down—the posture of the humble

> When Jesus reached the spot, he looked up and said to him, "Zacchaeus, come down immediately. I must stay at your house today." (Luke 19:5)

**The Transformation**

Zacchaeus comes down from the tree "at once and welcomed him gladly." But the crowd grumbles:

> "All the people saw this and began to mutter, 'He has gone to be the guest of a sinner.'" (Luke 19:7)

Zacchaeus then repents:

> "But Zacchaeus stood up and said to the Lord, 'Look, Lord! Here and now I give half of my possessions to the poor, and if I have cheated anybody out of anything, I will pay back four times the amount.'" (Luke 19:8)

And he is justified:

> "Jesus said to him, 'Today salvation has come to this house, because this man, too, is a son of Abraham. For the Son of Man came to seek and to save the lost.'" (Luke 19:9–10)

**The Key Truth**

Notice the beautiful reversal: Zacchaeus sought to see Jesus, but the Son of Man came to seek Zacchaeus. This is grace—God's initiative toward us.

Jesus is both judge and king, but also grace and mercy. Here lies the resolution to our deepest dilemma: if God is judge, how can He let us go free from our sins? But if He doesn't execute judgment, how can He be just? The answer is Jesus.

> "For this reason he had to be made like his brothers in every way, in order that he might become a merciful and faithful high priest in service to God, and that he might make atonement for the sins of the people." (Hebrews 2:17)

God came as a man to take on Himself the punishment of sin. Justice is fulfilled. Mercy is fulfilled. Jesus is the perfect combination of both.

## Application

When we ask for justice and mercy, we are ultimately asking for Jesus. So:

- **Petition your governments for justice.** Lobby persistently for what is right.
- **Petition God for mercy.** Humble yourself, confess your sins, ask for His grace.
- **Petition Jesus to know Him better.** Keep seeking Him. Don't stop. Even if you have questions and doubts, persist in faith.

Like Zacchaeus, keep climbing toward Jesus. He is seeking you. And when you find Him, you will find both the justice you crave and the mercy you desperately need.
