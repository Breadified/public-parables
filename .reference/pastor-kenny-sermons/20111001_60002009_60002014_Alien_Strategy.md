# 1 Peter 2:9-14

**Title:** Alien Strategy
**Date:** 23 October 2011
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction: The Alien Messenger

Last week we explored how Peter uses Old Testament temple imagery to describe Christians today. We are like living stones in God's temple, called to treat one another as sacred. We are like priests serving the new temple, called to love one another. But priests have another crucial task: they carry a message.

Just as SETI dedicates millions of dollars searching for messages from outer space, we as Christians have already received the most important message—from heaven itself. Jesus came to earth bearing a message of hope and salvation. Our task is to declare this message to the world.

## 1. The Priestly Call to Declare God's Praises (v.9-10)

> But you are a chosen people, a royal priesthood, a holy nation, a people belonging to God, that you may declare the praises of him who called you out of darkness into his wonderful light. Once you were not a people, but now you are the people of God; once you had not received mercy, but now you have received mercy. (1 Peter 2:9-10)

### The Ancient Priestly Task

We often think of Old Testament priests primarily as those who offered animal sacrifices. But one of their central duties was something else entirely: they were to declare the praises of God.

The Levites held a unique role in Israel. Moses entrusted them with the Law itself:

> He said of his father and mother, "I have no regard for them." He did not recognize his brothers or acknowledge his own children, but he watched over your word and guarded your covenant. He teaches your precepts to Jacob and your law to Israel. (Deuteronomy 33:9-10)

The prophet Malachi reminds us of this priestly responsibility:

> For the lips of a priest ought to preserve knowledge, and from his mouth men should seek instruction—because he is the messenger of the LORD Almighty. (Malachi 2:7)

### God's Vision: A Priestly Nation

God's intention went beyond the Levites alone. He wanted the entire nation of Israel to function as priests to the gentile world. When God established His covenant with Israel, He declared:

> Now if you obey me fully and keep my covenant, then out of all nations you will be my treasured possession. You will be for me a kingdom of priests and a holy nation. (Exodus 19:5-6)

Isaiah captured this vision beautifully:

> In the last days the mountain of the LORD's temple will be established as chief among the mountains; it will be raised above the hills, and all nations will stream to it. Many peoples will come and say, "Come, let us go up to the mountain of the LORD, to the house of the God of Jacob. He will teach us his ways, so that we may walk in his paths." The law will go out from Zion, the word of the LORD from Jerusalem. (Isaiah 2:2-3)

The promise was clear: nations would be drawn to Jerusalem to hear God's Word proclaimed. In the days of David and Solomon, this actually happened—the world came to hear of Israel's God.

### The Failure and the Fulfillment

Tragically, Israel failed in this calling. The nation became self-indulgent and distracted. The priests themselves grew corrupt, transforming the temple into a den of robbers rather than a house of prayer. They silenced God's Word instead of proclaiming it.

The fulfillment of this priestly calling came through Jesus. He is the true Israel of God, the perfect Priest. He declared God's praises by announcing that the kingdom of God had arrived. He offered the ultimate sacrifice—His own body—to appease God's wrath. He called to sinners, ate with them, and offered them God's blessings.

> If perfection could have been attained through the Levitical priesthood (for on the basis of it the law was given to the people), why was there still need for another priest to come—one in the order of Melchizedek, not in the order of Aaron? Because of this oath, Jesus has become the guarantee of a better covenant. (Hebrews 7:11, 22)

Now, through Christ, we are called to continue this priestly task.

### Understanding "Praise": To Advertise God

When Peter calls us to "declare the praises of God," we must understand what he means by praise. It's not what we typically think.

Praise doesn't mean saying "Hallelujah" or singing worship songs—though those can be expressions of praise. Rather, the word simply means to advertise.

Think of it this way: when you see a friend like Matt do something excellent or display a remarkable quality, and you tell others about it, you're praising him. You're advertising his character or achievements. That's what praise is—making known the excellences of someone to others.

The Psalms demonstrate this beautifully:

> Sing to the LORD a new song; sing to the LORD, all the earth. Declare his glory among the nations, his marvelous deeds among all peoples. For great is the LORD and most worthy of praise; he is to be feared above all gods. (Psalm 96:1-2, 4)

Notice how the command to praise is always followed by the actual declaration of God's mighty deeds. This is what praise truly is.

The problem with much modern "praise and worship" music is that it contains the call to praise but no actual content. We sing "Praise God!" but what are we actually proclaiming about Him? Real praise declares what God has done—His character, His mighty works, His salvation.

### Two Dimensions of Declaring Praise

**Declaring to One Another**

First, we declare God's praises to each other within the church. This is how we encourage our brothers and sisters daily. We tell of God's work in our lives: How is He changing you? What sins have you overcome? What challenges have strengthened your faith? What conversations have shifted your perspective? Share your conversion story. This is advertising God's mighty saving deeds to each other, and when we do so, we are built up together.

We are doubtful creatures who need to hear again and again that the gospel is true and that it works. This mutual encouragement through declaring what God has done is essential.

**Declaring to the World**

But we don't just declare to our Christian friends. We're called to advertise to the whole world what Jesus has done. This is where Israel failed—they became introverted, creating their own little "God-club" with idiosyncratic rules, forgetting their mission to the gentiles.

Consider the challenge: How many non-Christian friends have you told about Jesus this year? If that seems too difficult, how many have you invited to church? If that also seems too much, how many have you prayed for? Do you care enough about our Lord to advertise Him?

You are "the chosen race, the royal priesthood, a holy nation, a people belonging to God" (1 Peter 2:9).

### The Problem of Pre-Evangelism

Many Christians spend too much time in what I call "pre-evangelism." We think, "Let me build a good relationship first. After a year of friendship, then I'll tell them about Jesus or invite them to church." But this strategy backfires. After a year, mentioning Jesus would feel bizarre—like suddenly announcing "By the way, I'm married" to someone you've known for years but never mentioned it. No one does that.

Better to be natural about it from the start. Build genuine relationships, yes, but don't hide who you are. When someone asks "Tell me about yourself," being a committed Christian should come up naturally and relatively quickly, just as your other important commitments would.

The point is to create opportunities. Tell people straight away. If they show interest, wonderful. If not, you've at least been faithful to your calling.

Some of you will go on beach missions. Others intentionally engage in conversations with people in your community about life's deeper questions. Make these invitations now. December will bring special talks relevant for non-Christians. Give your friends advance notice. Those you've wanted to invite all year but were too busy—they won't be too busy now.

## 2. When Words Aren't Enough: Living Good Lives (v.11-15)

What happens when you've tried to declare God's praises but your loved ones ignore you? Your friends close the door? This was the reality in Peter's time. The Roman government wasn't friendly toward Christians. Many worked as servants under pagan masters. Others were married to non-Christian husbands.

When words fail, Peter offers a powerful strategy:

> Dear friends, I urge you, as aliens and strangers in the world, to abstain from sinful desires, which war against your soul. Live such good lives among the pagans that, though they accuse you of doing wrong, by seeing your good deeds they may glorify God on the day he visits us. (1 Peter 2:11-12)

### What "Good Lives" Means

"Good" here doesn't mean the Australian Dream—nice food, nice holidays, nice entertainment. It doesn't mean the Asian Dream—being a professional with a big house. It's not about showing the world how blessed you are materially.

Good lives means living moral lives. It means being distinctly different in your character and conduct. The pagans recognized this difference and even accused Christians of moral wrongness:

> They think it strange that you do not plunge with them into the same flood of dissipation, and they heap abuse on you. (1 Peter 4:4)

Peter's call is radical: continue living righteously even when they slander you. Why? Because one day—on judgment day—they will see those good deeds clearly. God will vindicate your righteousness.

**A Personal Illustration**

I remember in Year 6 when my bus skipped a stop where a classmate was supposed to board. I called out to the driver, ran to the front, and explained we'd miss someone. I got off, called for my classmate, and thought I'd "saved the day." But he ignored me completely. I later learned he was skipping school that day and didn't want to be found.

I got in trouble with my classmate, in trouble with the bus driver, and was embarrassed in front of everyone. No one recognized my good deed that day. But one day in heaven, it will be vindicated.

This is the Christian's confidence: we do what is right not just for righteousness's sake, but knowing that God sees and will vindicate us.

### Two Results of Living Righteously

**A. Silencing Ignorant Talk**

> For it is God's will that by doing good you should silence the ignorant talk of foolish men. (1 Peter 2:15)

Good works silence ignorant accusations. They confound critics and render slander ineffective. When people say Christians are hypocritical, respond by being genuinely humble and kind. Let them know you're a Christian through your actions. That ignorant talk will lose its power.

**B. Creating Opportunities to Declare Praise**

Peter looks ahead to a future moment:

> But in your hearts set apart Christ as Lord. Always be prepared to give an answer to everyone who asks you to give the reason for the hope that you have. But do this with gentleness and respect. (1 Peter 3:15)

Notice: they will ask. As you live moral lives despite their accusations, their voice gradually becomes muted. But something else happens—people begin asking why you're different. They want to understand your hope.

This is where words become essential. Salvation comes through the gospel—through hearing the good news that salvation is finished (Done), not something we do (Do). Good works alone cannot save. In fact, presenting works as the path to salvation is a false gospel.

There's a popular saying among Christians: "Preach the gospel, and if necessary use words." But this gets it backwards. The truth is: "Always preach the gospel, and if you can't, live good lives to create opportunities."

I want to encourage you to actively preach the gospel. But I also recognize that sometimes it's hard. Someone says they're not interested. Your boss has power over you, and it feels awkward. What then? Use your life to create opportunities for conversation. Let your different priorities, values, and choices spark curiosity. Then, when they ask, you'll have the chance to declare what Jesus has done.

### The Core Problem: We're Too Much Like the World

Here's our real challenge: our lifestyle is indistinguishable from the pagan world. We do the same things on Friday nights. We dress the same way. We spend money on the same things. We pursue the same ambitions. We travel to the same places. What's different enough about us to warrant questions?

We are "alien Christians," and that should mean we obviously do things differently.

**Illustrations of Alien Living**

My wife had a wedding in Hong Kong last year. She left Friday and returned Monday—just a weekend trip. Her pagan friends were shocked. "Why so short? If you spend all that money, you might as well stay longer." Here was an opportunity to speak about her values: family comes first, obligations at home matter more than extending a vacation, resurrection hope frees you from worldly FOMO.

Think about how you spend your summer differently than your pagan friends. What conversations come up regularly in your circles? What do people discuss—studies, movies, purchases? Act differently. Let your choices reflect God's values.

How you travel or don't travel, how you spend money or don't, how you prioritize time—these differences will spark questions and create openings for declaring God's praises.

## 3. Submission as Witness (v.13)

> Submit yourselves for the Lord's sake to every authority instituted among men: whether to the king, as the supreme authority, or to governors, who are sent by him to punish those who do wrong and to commend those who do right. (1 Peter 2:13-14)

In Australia, there should be no excuse for disobedience to lawful authority. We have just government institutions. Yet we find temptations everywhere: piracy, dishonesty about sick days at school, driving violations. These small compromises don't bring glory to God.

## Conclusion: Our Alien Strategy

We have received a message from outer space—a message from the heavens. The Lord Jesus came to earth to tell mankind about hope and salvation for the day God returns.

As aliens in this world, let us declare God's praises to the world. And if we can't find the words, let us live such good lives that we create opportunities for others to ask.

> Through Jesus, therefore, let us continually offer to God a sacrifice of praise—the fruit of lips that confess his name. And do not forget to do good and to share with others, for with such sacrifices God is pleased. (Hebrews 13:15-16)
