# Isaiah 44:24 - 45:13

**Title:** Comfort in the Lord of History
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew
**Date:** August 2, 2009

## Introduction: Making Sense of History

History reveals patterns. When we understand what came before, seemingly chaotic events become coherent—whether international conflicts rooted in colonialism and historical grievances, or local geography shaped by its past. But the deepest question remains: How do we make sense of the entire sweep of human history? How do we find comfort in a world marked by war, evil, and suffering when we believe in a good God? How can the Lord of History truly be Lord when so much evil persists?

## 1. God the Redeemer

We stand in the midst of Isaiah 40-66, a section saturated with comfort. The context is salvation. Isaiah himself witnessed the fall of the Northern Kingdom (722 BC to the Assyrians) and now prophesies a darker future: in roughly a century, Babylon will conquer the Southern Kingdom, deporting its people, razing the temple, and carrying away all treasures to a foreign land.

Yet the message remains: **comfort**. God will not abandon His people. He will redeem them from Babylonian captivity—a new Exodus, echoing His deliverance from Egypt centuries before.

> **Isaiah 44:23** — Sing for joy, O heavens, for the LORD has done this; shout aloud, O earth beneath. Burst into song, you mountains, you forests and all your trees, for the LORD has redeemed Jacob, he displays his glory in Israel.

## 2. Cyrus: An Unusual Choice

### The Pagan King

God's solution to the Babylonian exile is startling: He will raise up **Cyrus**, a pagan king, to accomplish His redemptive purposes.

> **Isaiah 45:13** — I will raise up Cyrus in my righteousness: I will make all his ways straight. He will rebuild my city and set my exiles free, but not for a price or reward, says the LORD Almighty.

### Cyrus Described in Messianic Terms

The description of Cyrus is so elevated that he is portrayed using language reserved for Israel's hoped-for Davidic king—the Messiah himself.

**As Shepherd:**
> **Isaiah 44:28** — Who says of Cyrus, "He is my shepherd and will accomplish all that I please; he will say of Jerusalem, 'Let it be rebuilt,' and of the temple, 'Let its foundations be laid.'"

(David was Israel's shepherd-king; this language echoes that royal, redemptive role.)

**As the Anointed One:**
> **Isaiah 45:1** — This is what the LORD says to his anointed, to Cyrus, whose right hand I take hold of to subdue nations before him and to strip kings of their armor, to open doors before him so that gates will not be shut.

**Subduing Nations (echoing Psalm 2):**
> **Isaiah 45:1-2** — I will go before you and will level the mountains; I will break down gates of bronze and cut through bars of iron.

This language parallels the messianic promises of Psalm 2, where God's anointed king subdues all nations. Yet here it describes a pagan conqueror.

**As One Who Prepares the Way (like John the Baptist):**
> **Isaiah 45:2** — I will level the mountains, make the crooked places straight—imagery echoing John's role as forerunner to Christ.

### The Paradox: Pagan Yet Summoned

Here lies the mystery: Cyrus is described in the most exalted, messianic terms, yet he does not even acknowledge God.

> **Isaiah 45:3-5** — I will give you the treasures of darkness, riches stored in secret places, so that you may know that I am the LORD, the God of Israel, who summons you by name. For the sake of Jacob my servant, of Israel my chosen, I summon you by name and bestow on you a title of honor, **though you do not acknowledge me**. I am the LORD, and there is no other; apart from me there is no God. I will strengthen you, **though you have not acknowledged me**.

God honors Cyrus with a title, strengthens him, and uses him to accomplish salvation—all without Cyrus ever recognizing the God who orchestrates these events. This is stunning: God works through those who neither worship Him nor understand His purposes.

## 3. Creator: God's Right to Choose

### Why Would God Do This?

Why would God choose a pagan king—a conqueror who killed thousands, subjugated nations, and eliminated threats to his throne—to accomplish His redemptive work? The answer lies in understanding God's nature as Creator.

### God Is Creator of All

> **Isaiah 44:24** — This is what the LORD says—your Redeemer, who formed you in the womb: I am the LORD, who has made all things, who alone stretched out the heavens, who spread out the earth by myself.

> **Isaiah 44:25-26** — Who foils the signs of false prophets and makes fools of diviners, who overthrows the learning of the wise and turns it into nonsense, who carries out the words of his servants and fulfills the predictions of his messengers.

> **Isaiah 44:27** — Who says to the watery deep, "Be dry, and I will dry up your streams."

Because God is Creator, He is sovereign over **all things**—including the affairs of nations and the actions of men.

### God Is Sovereign Over All, Including Evil

> **Isaiah 45:7** — I form the light and create darkness, I bring prosperity and create disaster; I, the LORD, do all these things.

This is the heart of the matter. God does not merely permit evil; He sovereignly ordains it for His purposes. This does not make Him evil—rather, it reveals His absolute authority. He who creates all things has the right to direct all things toward His ends.

> **Isaiah 45:8** — You heavens above, rain down righteousness; let the clouds shower it down. Let the earth open wide, let salvation spring up, let righteousness grow with it; I, the LORD, have created it.

### God's Plans Cannot Be Questioned

God's sovereignty means His purposes are His alone to determine. His ways are not ours to question or amend.

> **Isaiah 45:9-10** — Woe to him who quarrels with his Maker, to him who is but a potsherd among the potsherds on the ground. Does the clay say to the potter, "What are you making?" Does your work say, "He has no hands"? Woe to him who says to his father, "What have you begotten?" or to his mother, "What have you brought to birth?"

> **Isaiah 45:11-12** — This is what the LORD says—the Holy One of Israel, and its Maker: Concerning things to come, do you question me about my children, or give me orders about the work of my hands? It is I who made the earth and created mankind upon it. My own hands stretched out the heavens; I marshaled their starry hosts.

God alone has the prerogative to direct history. We are clay in the hands of the Potter. Like a child who cannot instruct the parent, we cannot instruct our Maker.

## 4. Addressing the Problem of Evil

### The Classical Problem Reframed

The problem of evil traditionally states:
1. If God is powerful and good, evil should not exist
2. Evil exists
3. Therefore, either God is not powerful, not good, or does not exist

Most Christians affirm God's existence and goodness. The tension usually falls on God's sovereignty. Some theologians, like Arminius in the 16th-17th century, solved this by appealing to human free will: people cause evil through their freedom; God simply permits it. This preserves human responsibility but limits God's sovereignty.

Yet this creates problems. If God does not fully control evil, then:
- My prayers are only partially effective (I pray to a partially sovereign God)
- My confidence in God is incomplete (He cannot stop what evil people freely choose to do)
- My salvation is uncertain (the devil might snatch me away)
- We drift toward dualism (God vs. Free Will, with neither fully in control)

### The Better Answer: Perfect Sovereignty

Calvin understood what Isaiah teaches: **God is perfectly sovereign**. Nothing escapes His control—not even evil. This is not dualism but the majesty of an omnipotent Creator.

This does not mean God is responsible for evil. Rather, **God is so perfectly good and sovereign that He uses even evil for His ultimate glory.**

### God and Evil in Jesus

The fullest demonstration of this principle comes in the crucifixion:

> **Acts 2:22-23** — Men of Israel, listen to this: Jesus of Nazareth was a man accredited by God to you by miracles, wonders and signs, which God did among you through him, as you yourselves know. This man was handed over to you by God's set purpose and foreknowledge; and you, with the help of wicked men, put him to death by nailing him to the cross.

Wicked men killed Jesus—Pharisees, Pilate, soldiers. They bear full moral responsibility for their sin. Yet God had set this outcome from eternity. God's sovereign will and human guilt are not contradictory; they are simultaneously true. The cross was the most evil act in history—the murder of the Son of God—yet God used it to accomplish salvation for all mankind.

God took upon Himself the penalty for sin, purchasing redemption through the death of His own Son. This is the ultimate redemption, surpassing the return from Babylonian exile. This is how God redeems not just a nation from exile, but humanity from sin itself.

## 5. Two Wills of God

### God's Moral Will

God's moral will is His revealed character and commands in Scripture. It tells us what we ought to do and what we ought not do. We need not guess or petition God to reveal it—we simply open the Bible.

God's moral will commanded:
- "Do not commit murder"
- "Do not bear false witness"
- "Love your neighbor as yourself"

These are clear, unchanging, and knowable.

### God's Sovereign Will

God's sovereign will is what He actually brings to pass. It is His eternal purpose, determined before the foundation of the world. If something happens, it was God's sovereign will for it to happen.

Crucially, **God's sovereign will and moral will can differ**. The crucifixion is the clearest example: it violated God's moral will (murder is sin), yet it was God's sovereign will from eternity. Evil men violated God's moral law, yet God's sovereign purposes were accomplished.

This means:
- We must obey God's moral will (do not sin)
- We must trust God's sovereign will (He controls all outcomes)
- We are responsible for our sin, even when God sovereignly ordains it

## 6. Comfort in God's Sovereignty Today

### A. Comfort in Suffering

Many Christians assume good things reflect God's will and bad things do not—or that they come from Satan. But Scripture teaches something more nuanced. God may use suffering to teach us perseverance, to discipline us, to refine our faith, or for purposes we cannot yet see. Job's wife urged him to curse God and die, but Job responded: "Shall I receive good from God and not bad?"

The song "Blessed Be Your Name" captures this sober faith:

> Blessed be your name when I'm found in the desert place / Though I walk through the wilderness, blessed be your name. On the road marked with suffering, though there's pain in the offering, blessed be your name.

This is not triumphalism or denial. It is the honest acknowledgment that God is sovereign over both prosperity and hardship. Our language about suffering should reflect this—not the claim that "God sent this," but the conviction that "nothing escapes God's control, and He will accomplish His purposes even through this."

### B. Comfort in Full Confidence in God

Because God is sovereign, we can trust Him completely.

- **For sin:** Christ died for it. Stop sinning not from fear but from gratitude for His sacrifice.
- **For fear:** Even Hitler, evil as he was, remained under God's control. Even in sickness, God is not surprised or overwhelmed. We need not live in superstition, seeing omens in every event.

Predestination is not a doctrine of hopelessness (as if we are robots with no responsibility). Rather, it is a doctrine of **comfort for Christians**—and of terror for those who reject God. Those who belong to Christ can rest in His sovereign care.

### C. Comfort That God Is Still Just

Evil does not escape judgment. In the end, all injustice will be brought to account. Hitler will answer for his crimes. Cyrus will answer for his conquests. So will we—unless we are forgiven in Christ.

But this raises an important boundary: **We cannot use evil to accomplish good.** "The ends justify the means" is not a Christian maxim. Only God, as Creator, has the right to ordain evil for good purposes. We are not creators; we are creatures. We must obey God's moral will, even when we cannot see how God will accomplish His sovereign will. Trust is the bridge between these two truths.
