# Romans 14:1–15:13

**Title:** Serve the Weak

**Date:** 17 March 2013 (SMAC); 22 June 2024 (ARISE)

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## When Christians Disagree

Throughout church history, believers have wrestled with disputable matters—questions that Scripture doesn't explicitly forbid but where sincere Christians reach different conclusions. These include theological debates like the nature of Christ's blood, the meaning of Holy Communion, church practices around meeting days and music, and dietary restrictions.

Eric Liddell's story illustrates this tension beautifully. Famous for refusing to run on Sundays despite the cost to his athletic career, Liddell was no legalist. When children in a prison camp needed refereeing for their games, he set aside his principle and officiated on Sunday. He would sacrifice a gold medal for himself in the name of truth, yet bend backward for others in the name of grace.

This passage addresses how believers should navigate these differences—not with contempt or judgment, but with love that builds up the church.

---

## Part 1: Accept the Weak, Do Not Despise

### Who Are the Weak and Strong?

> Accept the one whose faith is weak, without quarreling over disputable matters. One person's faith allows them to eat anything, but another, whose faith is weak, eats only vegetables. (Romans 14:1–2)

The "weak" and "strong" in Romans refer to Jewish and Gentile believers adjusting to life together in the Roman church. The weak were likely Jewish Christians bound by Old Testament food laws and Sabbath observance—habits cultivated over generations and not easily abandoned. The strong were those, Jewish or Gentile, confident enough to embrace Christian freedom, understanding that Jesus fulfilled the law and declared all foods clean (Mark 7; Acts 10).

But weakness isn't stupidity—it's conscience. These believers weren't breaking God's law; they were honoring their trained reflexes. Consider how a cockroach triggers revulsion despite being just another insect. Our instincts, shaped by experience, run deep. For weak believers raised in strict dietary traditions, eating unclean food felt like sin, regardless of theological truth.

### The Problem with Contempt

> The one who eats everything must not treat with contempt the one who does not, and the one who does not eat everything must not judge the one who does, for God has accepted them. Who are you to judge someone else's servant? To their own master, they stand or fall. And they will stand, for the Lord is able to make them stand. (Romans 14:3–4)

Paul forbids both despising and condemning. He's not prohibiting discernment—elsewhere he calls us to judge between true and false teaching, to be wise about good and innocent about evil. Rather, he means don't *condemn* those who've done nothing wrong. Don't look down. Don't despise.

The strong often fall into this trap: "Don't you know the gospel? Why live in the past? How immature." This contempt wounds and divides.

### Each Answers to God Alone

> One person considers one day more sacred than another; another considers every day alike. Each of them should be fully convinced in their own mind. Whoever regards one day as special does so to the Lord. Whoever eats meat does so to the Lord, for they give thanks to God; and whoever abstains does so to the Lord and gives thanks to God. (Romans 14:5–6)

Here's the revolutionary truth: **your conviction is between you and God alone**. You are not the congregation's servant. You answer to one Master. When you act according to your sincere conviction—whether abstaining from meat or eating freely—you act for the Lord's eyes.

This doesn't mean truth is relative. Theologically, eating pork is fine. Meat sacrificed to idols is just meat. But each believer must work this out with God and be fully convinced in their own mind. The Reformation recovered this precious doctrine: *sola Scriptura*—Scripture alone. Not the church, not leaders, not tradition ultimately determines your convictions. You and God together discern truth.

## Part 2: Look After the Weak, Do Not Stumble Them

### The Conscience Is Sacred

> I am fully convinced that no food is unclean in itself. But if anyone regards something as unclean, then for that person it is unclean. (Romans 14:14)

This paradox reveals something profound: *objective reality meets subjective conscience*. Food is objectively clean. But if you believe it's unclean, breaking that conviction is sin for you—not because the food is sinful, but because you're violating your own conscience.

Why does this matter? Because conscience is the last line of defense against actual sin. When you train yourself to ignore your conscience—to do what you believe is wrong—you set a dangerous precedent. You teach yourself that conviction doesn't matter. One day you'll need that conscience when facing real temptation, and you'll have already dulled it.

The goal isn't to keep believers weak forever. Over time, as faith matures and understanding deepens, the weak become strong. But this takes time—sometimes decades. A person can't undo twenty years of training in a few weeks of theological argument.

### Don't Destroy with Your Freedom

> If your brother or sister is distressed because of what you eat, you are no longer acting in love. Do not by your eating destroy someone for whom Christ died. (Romans 14:15)

Here's the hard truth for the strong: *your freedom can become their stumbling block*. Yes, you have the right to eat pork. But if doing so in front of your brother causes him anguish, or worse, causes him to violate his conscience and sin, love demands restraint.

> Therefore do not let what you know is good be spoken of as evil. (Romans 14:16)

The issue isn't the food—it's the method. Your liberty becomes evil when it leads others into sin.

### The Kingdom Isn't About Food

> For the kingdom of God is not a matter of eating and drinking, but of righteousness, peace and joy in the Holy Spirit. Because anyone who serves Christ in this way is pleasing to God and receives human approval. (Romans 14:17–18)

Keep perspective. The kingdom of God isn't about dietary rules or calendars. It's about righteousness, peace, and joy in the Spirit. When you surrender your freedom for your brother's sake, you're not diminishing the kingdom—you're building it. You're displaying the righteousness of love, the peace of unity, and the joy of putting others first.

### Practical Application: Alcohol and Gambling

Consider alcohol and gambling—among society's most destructive vices. Theologically, both are neutral. God gives us good gifts to enjoy. But those with a history of addiction, those struggling with these temptations, know the devastation they cause.

You might not know who among your church family is an alcoholic. They likely won't announce it. So what's loving? Church policies that eliminate alcohol and gambling aren't legalistic—they're protective. They say to the weak: "We value your freedom and your conscience. We won't stumble you."

Similarly, consider clubbing—nothing inherently sinful about dancing or darkness. But combine alcohol, dim lighting, and loud music (all designed to lower inhibitions) and you've created conditions where most people will stumble, not stand. Love asks: is this the best environment for your friends' spiritual growth?

### The Stronger Must Bear With the Weaker

> We who are strong ought to bear with the failings of the weak and not to please ourselves. Each of us should please our neighbors for their good, to build them up. (Romans 15:1–2)

> For even Christ did not please himself but, as it is written: "The insults of those who insult you have fallen on me." (Romans 15:3)

Christ is our model. He surrendered His rights constantly for our sake. He went to the cross not for His glory, but for ours. If Christ didn't please Himself, how can we?

## Part 3: Judgment, Humility, and Unity

### Our Judgments Are Always Provisional

> You, then, why do you judge your brother or sister? Or why do you treat them with contempt? For we will all stand before God's judgment seat. It is written: "As surely as I live, says the Lord, every knee will bow before me; every tongue will acknowledge God." So then, each of us will give an account of ourselves to God. (Romans 14:10–12)

Here's a sobering reality: *you will stand before God's judgment seat*. That's the only judgment that ultimately matters. Your judgments of others are always provisional, always subject to God's evaluation. You judge by external appearance; God judges hearts and motives. You're fallible; He's not.

This doesn't eliminate discernment. It humbles it. We can and should work toward truth together. But we hold our judgments lightly, knowing we see dimly now. On the last day, we'll discover we were wrong about some things. Some disputes we thought crucial will seem trivial. Some people we judged harshly were struggling with battles we couldn't see.

### Humility in Our Stance

This calls for three postures:

**Be humble**, not proud. Don't assume your maturity means you understand everything. I might think I know better than someone else, but pride blinds.

**Avoid hypocrisy**. If you find something difficult, extend mercy. You're likely struggling somewhere too—just maybe not where this other person struggles.

**Be slow to judge**. Too often we caricature with a single sentence, assuming we've understood completely. A few more conversations might reveal context that changes everything. Prejudice forms from insufficient information.

### Accept One Another as Christ Accepted You

> Let us therefore make every effort to do what leads to peace and to mutual edification. Do not destroy the work of God for the sake of food. All food is clean, but it is wrong for a person to eat anything that causes someone else to stumble. (Romans 14:19–20)

> Accept one another, then, just as Christ accepted you, in order to bring praise to God. (Romans 15:7)

The standard for acceptance isn't agreement on disputable matters. It's Christ's acceptance. He accepted you while you were still sinning, still confused, still growing. You were weak, and He didn't despise you. He died for you.

Now extend that same grace. Accept those on different parts of the spiritual journey. Don't expect first-year believers to behave like mature disciples. Don't come down harshly because someone hasn't yet worked through what took you years to understand.

### The Gentiles' Hope

> For I tell you that Christ has become a servant of the Jews on behalf of God's truth, to confirm the promises made to the patriarchs so that the Gentiles may glorify God for his mercy, as it is written:

> "Therefore I will praise you among the Gentiles;
>     I will sing hymns to your name."

> Again, it says:

> "Rejoice, O Gentiles, with his people."

> And again:

> "Praise the Lord, all you Gentiles;
>     and sing praises to him, all you peoples."

> And again, Isaiah says:

> "The Root of Jesse will spring up,
>     one who will arise to rule over the nations;
>     in him the Gentiles will hope." (Romans 15:8–12)

Paul grounds unity in God's redemptive history. Christ served the Jews to confirm God's ancient promises. Why? So the Gentiles—outsiders, excluded for millennia—might glorify God for His mercy. The whole arc of Scripture bends toward reconciliation and inclusion.

This is the deepest reason for acceptance: God Himself has accepted both Jew and Gentile, both strong and weak, into His covenant family. If God has accepted them, who are you to reject them?

### The Overflow of Hope

> May the God of hope fill you with all joy and peace as you trust in him, so that you may overflow with hope by the power of the Holy Spirit. (Romans 15:13)

This is the fruit of acceptance and unity. Not division, not contempt, not judgment—but joy, peace, and hope overflowing. This is what happens when Christ's body functions as it should: we strengthen one another, we bear one another's burdens, we build each other up.

---

## Living This Out

The challenge is clear: **look to yourself first**. Before you judge your brother's conscience, examine your own heart. Are you harboring contempt? Are you using your freedom to harm? Are you confident in your own convictions before God?

Remember: you ultimately obey one Master. You perform for an audience of One. That frees you from people-pleasing and frees you to love sacrificially.

And remember too: others are on their own journey with God. The weak believer today might be the strong believer tomorrow. The strong believer might discover areas of blindness where they're actually weak. We're all growing. We're all learning. We're all accountable to God.

So accept one another. Bear with one another's failings. Build up one another. That's the kingdom of God.
