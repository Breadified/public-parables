# Genesis 23:1-20

**Title:** A Burial Site - Faith's Final Promise
**Date:** March 13, 2022
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Opening Reflection

We live in turbulent times, yet there are bright spots. Schools have reopened their Christian groups, the rain has stopped, and life continues. This week we come to the end of the Abraham and Sarah narrative—their deaths in Genesis 23 and 25. It's fitting that we conclude with stories of burial, a practice that speaks to our deepest hopes about resurrection and home.

## 1. A Burial Site (verses 1-4)

Sarah lived 127 years—a remarkably long life. She was barren for so many years, promised a son when she laughed at God's plan, yet bore Isaac at ninety. She should be the poster child for International Day of Women—a woman of faith who saw the impossible become reality.

Now she has died in Hebron. This raises a curious question: Abraham was living in Beersheba, the bordertown of Israel—that phrase "from Dan to Beersheba" marked the nation's north-south boundaries. Yet Sarah died in Hebron, a distance away. Perhaps they maintained homes in both places, or perhaps Abraham was tending to Ishmael while Sarah lived in Hebron where they had originally settled near the great trees of Mamre.

Abraham travels to Hebron to mourn and weep over her—to eulogize, to speak good words about the woman who journeyed with him. Through his grief, he must also attend to the practical business of death. He approaches the Hittites—the sons of Heth—to request property for a burial site. He identifies himself as a "foreigner and stranger" with no land of his own. Though he cannot legally own property as a foreigner, grief and necessity compel him to seek a concession.

## 2. A Cave (verses 5-9)

The Hittites recognize Abraham's status. He has become a "mighty prince among us"—God's promises of wealth and blessing have been fulfilled. He possesses many servants, flocks, and herds. They generously offer him any of their tombs.

Abraham's eye settles on the cave of Machpelah, belonging to Ephron. This specificity matters. Caves served as family tombs in that culture—places where multiple generations could be laid to rest together. In Genesis 25, we read that both Isaac and Abraham himself would eventually be buried in this same cave.

Why a cave? In rocky terrain where burial was difficult, caves provided natural tombs. More significantly, they represent a profound spiritual reality: the body lies in interment, waiting for resurrection. The cave is temporary, like a morgue—a place of rest before the great awakening.

Abraham and his descendants believed in the resurrection of the dead. Ezekiel's vision of dry bones becoming living flesh, muscles, skin, and breath captures this hope. When the time comes, the bones will rattle and flesh will form anew. This is why the Jewish practice forbids cremation—the body must remain intact for resurrection.

Your burial place speaks a message across generations. It's a way of proclaiming the gospel to strangers and descendants who may visit. The gravestone says, without words: "I believed in life after death. I hope in Jesus. I will rise again."

## 3. Price Paid (verses 10-18)

Ephron offers generously at first: "I give you the field, and I give you the cave that is in it. Bury your dead." This may reflect a Hittite bargaining ritual—the dance of offer and refusal, where save-face conversations happen before a true price emerges.

But Abraham refuses the gift. Instead, he insists on paying: "I will pay the price of the field. Accept it from me so I can bury my dead there."

Why pay when offered freely? Abraham refuses to be indebted to the Canaanites. Unknown to them, God had promised this entire land to Abraham—not just a cave or field, but all of it. The descendants of Abraham would one day possess the land and drive out the Hittites. Abraham wants no obligation, no lingering debt between them.

Ephron sets the price at 400 shekels of silver—an exorbitant sum, roughly half a king's ransom. Yet Abraham pays it in full, in front of witnesses. The transaction is recorded, witnessed, deeded. Abraham's purchase ensures he cannot be displaced or manipulated later. The paperwork matters.

## 4. The Deed (verses 16-20)

Abraham receives the field, the cave, and all the trees within its borders. The land is deeded to him—a legal transaction, a property right that cannot be revoked. He buries Sarah in the cave of Machpelah near Mamre, in Hebron, in the land of Canaan.

Here, at the end of their earthly journey, Abraham and Sarah finally possess the land promised to them generations before. Remember Genesis 13:14-15: "Look around from where you are, to the north and south, to the east and west. All the land that you see I will give to you and your offspring forever."

That promise was given in Hebron, where Abraham and Sarah built an altar. Perhaps this is why they returned to Hebron in their final days—to reclaim the center of the promise, not the outskirts where Abraham had settled with Ishmael.

What they receive is not a great city or majestic mountain or land flowing with milk and honey. It is a tomb. And yet, strangely, this tomb becomes their little piece of paradise—not because of what it is, but because of what it symbolizes.

This cave is the perfect deposit on God's promises. It speaks of something deeper than earthly land. It declares that their hope is not ultimately in property that corrupts and decays, but in the resurrection to come. In life after death. The body awaits redemption. The bones await breath. The dead await the resurrection.

## The Gospel In The Tomb

We are like Abraham and Sarah. We too are buried—dead in our trespasses, waiting for redemption. We fret about COVID, about vaccines, about death itself. But listen: we are already dead to this world. We have been sealed with the Holy Spirit, who is the pledge of our inheritance, the promise of what is to come.

We are not citizens of earth. We are enlightened people of heaven. Our home is elsewhere, in the resurrection to come.

Abraham and Sarah came home to be buried. We will go home to be raised. Then, finally, we will be truly home.
