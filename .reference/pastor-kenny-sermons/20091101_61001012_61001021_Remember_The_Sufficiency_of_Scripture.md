# 2 Peter 1:12-21

**Title:** Remember! The Sufficiency of Scripture
**Date:** 30 November 2009
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Opening Reflection on Memory

Memory shapes everything about our lives. Without it, we would lose our capacity to learn from mistakes, to feel gratitude, to hold ourselves accountable, or to maintain any sense of continuity in our relationships and identities. The ability to remember is fundamentally human—it's what allows us to grow, to connect, and to remain faithful to what matters most.

This is precisely why Peter opens this letter with such emphasis on remembrance. He's an old man, aware that his death is near, and his final words to his congregation aren't about personal comfort or legacy—they're about ensuring that people continue to remember Jesus.

## The Importance of Memory (vv. 12-15)

> So I will always remind you of these things, even though you know them and are firmly established in the truth you now have. I think it is right to refresh your memory as long as I live in the tent of this body, because I know that I will soon put it aside, as our Lord Jesus Christ has made clear to me. And I will make every effort to see that after my departure you will always be able to remember these things.

Peter repeats the language of remembrance three times in these verses. His purpose is clear: **to remind, to refresh, to ensure remembrance**. He knows he's dying—his body is a "tent" that will soon be taken down. Yet instead of focusing on his own mortality, he channels his remaining energy toward one goal: making sure his people don't forget Jesus.

This reveals something profound about pastoral leadership. The greatest gift a spiritual leader can give is not instruction alone, but the cultivation of memory—the habit of returning again and again to what matters most.

## The Memory of Jesus (vv. 16-18)

> We did not follow cleverly invented stories when we told you about the power and coming of our Lord Jesus Christ, but we were eyewitnesses of his majesty. For he received honor and glory from God the Father when the voice came to him from the Majestic Glory, saying, "This is my Son, whom I love; with him I am well pleased." We ourselves heard this voice that came from heaven when we were with him on the sacred mountain.

What does Peter want people to remember about Jesus?

- **Jesus is truly God incarnate.** He didn't arrive through clever storytelling or invented mythology. Peter was there. He witnessed Jesus's majesty firsthand.
- **Jesus is the one God delights in.** The Father's own voice came from heaven declaring approval: "This is my Son, whom I love; with him I am well pleased."
- **Jesus is Lord.** He rules our lives and commands our ultimate allegiance.

These aren't abstract truths. They're rooted in Peter's personal testimony—he heard God's voice. He saw Jesus's glory. This is eyewitness testimony to the most important reality in the universe.

## Applying Memory in Our Lives

The challenge is real: our memories fade. We remember things clearly one day, then within weeks—sometimes days—they blur and fade. When life gets hard, we're like the Israelites in the wilderness, forgetting the miracles of the exodus and wanting to return to the slavery we once knew.

**We need regular practices of remembrance.** Here are concrete ways to cultivate memory of Jesus:

### Personal Bible Reading

Scripture itself is a tool for remembrance. The Bible's 66 books contain countless ways to remind ourselves of Jesus's majesty and of God's guidance for how we should think and live. Whether you read systematically through books or follow a guided reading plan, personal engagement with Scripture roots remembrance in your daily life.

### Church Community

Attending a church where the Bible is read, taught, and discussed amplifies your memory. You benefit from learning alongside others and from teachers who've had theological training and pastoral experience. The community itself becomes a container for shared remembrance.

### Student Christian Community

But there's another place where remembrance can happen: right here at school. You spend five days a week in this environment, surrounded by other Christians. Why not create a space where you can gather, read Scripture together, pray together, and remind one another of Jesus?

A school can be a place of immense pressure against Christian faith. Having a refuge—a secure community where you can remember Jesus with friends without fear of ridicule—is invaluable. Imagine a caring Christian community at school where older students look out for younger ones, where you can bring non-Christian friends to hear God's Word in a welcoming context, where your priorities can be known and respected.

## The Cost of Remembrance

Cultivating memory requires commitment. It costs something—time, effort, consistency. You wake up early. You rearrange your schedule. You choose a Christian group over other social options. You risk being identified as a Christian in ways that matter socially.

But this cost is precisely what makes remembrance meaningful. It becomes a concrete statement to yourself and others about where your real priorities lie. It's the difference between vague belief and lived conviction.

This Christmas, remember Jesus. And as you enter the new year, commit to a proactive plan to remember Jesus—through Scripture, through community, through the deliberate practice of faith. Your future self, and your friends, will be grateful.
