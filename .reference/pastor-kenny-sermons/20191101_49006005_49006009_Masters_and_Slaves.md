**Title:** Masters and Slaves

## The Challenge: Understanding Biblical Slavery

When Australian Prime Minister Kevin Rudd was challenged on his Christian faith regarding the definition of marriage, he responded by quoting this passage. His argument was simple: if the Bible endorses slavery ("slaves, obey your masters"), then either we must accept slavery today, or we must abandon the Bible as outdated.

This creates a genuine tension for those of us who love both God and Scripture. We face three options:

1. **Ditch God** and dismiss the Bible as merely a human document
2. **Ditch the Bible** and reinterpret it to fit modern sensibilities
3. **Find a third way** to read and understand what Scripture actually teaches

As Christians, neither of the first two options works. We cannot abandon God, who knows what He is doing even when we don't fully understand. Nor can we treat the Bible as a document we can pick and choose from—if we do, it loses its power to transform us. If Scripture gets this fundamental issue wrong, what else might it be wrong about?

So we must ask: Is there a third way of reading this passage?

---

## Clarifying the Language: What Does "Slaves" Really Mean?

### The Modern Connotation

When we hear the word "slaves," our minds immediately conjure the horrors of American slavery—African men and women bound in chains, robbed of their freedom, treated as less than human. This image carries the stain of profound racism and dehumanization.

However, **this experience of slavery must not skew our understanding of all slavery**. The Greek word *doulos* (slave/servant) does not necessarily carry all those connotations.

### A Surprising Connection

Consider verse 6: "Obey them not only to win their favor when their eye is on you, but as **slaves of Christ**, doing the will of God from your heart."

If slavery is inherently evil, it cannot be something the Bible calls us to be—"slaves of Christ." This parallel suggests that the term carries a different meaning than chattel slavery.

Look at verse 7: "**Serve** wholeheartedly, as if you were serving the Lord, not people..."

Notice the same Greek root. The word "slave" is the same word as "serve." This tells us something important: **a slave is fundamentally a servant.**

### The Distinction: Doulos

When the Bible uses *doulos*, it refers to a bond-servant or indentured servant—someone in a temporary or permanent service arrangement by agreement or circumstance. This is not the same as the kidnapping and trading of human beings that characterized the transatlantic slave trade.

Here's the crucial point: **there is good indentured servitude and bad indentured servitude.**

## Good Indentured Servitude: Biblical Protections

### Servants Were Made in God's Image

The Bible contains detailed instructions on how to treat servants—not because servitude is inherently good, but because **servants are human beings made in the image of God** and must be treated with dignity.

### Legal Protections

**Injury or death:** Harming a servant came with severe penalties—even death of the offender.

> Exodus 21:20 "Anyone who beats their male or female slave with a rod must be punished if the slave dies as a direct result."
>
> Exodus 21:26-27 "An owner who hits a male or female slave in the eye and destroys it must let the slave go free to compensate for the eye. And an owner who knocks out the tooth of a male or female slave must let the slave go free to compensate for the tooth."

**Rest:** Servants were not to work on the Sabbath—they needed rest just as their masters did.

> Exodus 23:12 "Six days do your work, but on the seventh day do not work, so that your ox and your donkey may rest, and so that the slave born in your household and the foreigner living among you may be refreshed."

**Reputation:** You could not slander a servant.

> Proverbs 30:10 "Do not slander a servant to their master, or they will curse you, and you will pay for it."

**Refuge:** Escaped slaves were not to be returned to cruel masters; they were to be welcomed and protected.

> Deuteronomy 23:15-16 "If a slave has taken refuge with you, do not hand them over to their master. Let them live among you wherever they like and in whatever town they choose. Do not oppress them."

**Care:** Masters were even encouraged to treat servants generously.

> Proverbs 29:21 "Whoever pampers his servant from childhood will in the end find him his heir."

### Temporary Arrangement

For Hebrew servants, servitude was **not permanent**. It operated on a seven-year cycle:

> Exodus 21:2 "If you buy a Hebrew slave, he shall serve for six years; but on the seventh he shall go out as a free man without payment."

The **Year of Jubilee** (occurring every seven years) was a reset for the entire economic system. Debts were forgiven, land returned to original owners, and people rested.

> Leviticus 25:40-41 "They are to be treated as hired workers or temporary residents among you; they are to work for you until the Year of Jubilee. Then they and their children are to be released, and they will go back to their own clans and to the property of their ancestors."

### Released with Resources

When servants were released, they weren't sent out empty-handed. They were given the means to start over:

> Deuteronomy 15:12-15 "If any of your people—Hebrew men or women—sell themselves to you and serve you six years, in the seventh year you must let them go free. And when you release them, do not send them away empty-handed. Supply them liberally from your flock, your threshing floor and your winepress. Give to them as the Lord your God has blessed you. Remember that you were slaves in Egypt and the Lord your God redeemed you. That is why I give you this command today."

## Why Servitude Existed: The Historical Context

### When You Ask "Why?", Ask "What's the Alternative?"

To understand biblical servitude, we must understand the alternatives available in the ancient Near East.

### War and Prisoners

When nations conquered enemies in war, they faced a fundamental reality: enemies are either **killed** or they become **enslaved**. There was no third option. There were no prisons, no international humanitarian laws, no ability to feed and guard enemy soldiers indefinitely.

> Deuteronomy 20:10-13 "When you march up to attack a city, make its people an offer of peace. If they accept and open their gates, all the people in it shall be subject to forced labor and shall work for you. If they refuse to make peace and they engage you in battle, lay siege to that city. When the Lord your God delivers it into your hand, put to the sword all the men in it."

King David put conquered Ammonites to forced labor:

> 2 Samuel 12:31 "[David] brought out the people who were there, consigning them to labor with saws and with iron picks and axes, and he made them work at brickmaking. David did this to all the Ammonite towns."

**Viewed in this way, servitude became an act of mercy.** It meant a captured enemy kept his life and had a chance to start over in a new land with employment and provisions.

Consider modern parallels: After America toppled Saddam Hussein, soldiers who were neither imprisoned nor employed became disgruntled and eventually formed ISIS. The alternative to servitude can be far worse.

### Debt and Poverty

Another path into servitude was voluntary—through poverty or debt. This especially applied to Israelites:

> Leviticus 25:39-43 "If any of your fellow Israelites become poor and sell themselves to you, do not make them work as slaves. They are to be treated as hired workers or temporary residents among you; they are to work for you until the Year of Jubilee. Then they and their children are to be released, and they will go back to their own clans and to the property of their ancestors. Because the Israelites are my servants, whom I brought out of Egypt, they must not be sold as slaves. Do not rule over them ruthlessly, but fear your God."

In the ancient Near East, there were no banks offering loans to the poor, no bankruptcy laws, no government assistance. When you had nothing, your only remaining asset was your labor. A poor person could literally "pay off" their debt through their own work and skill.

**This was voluntary.** Notice verse 39: "sell themselves to you." The servant entered into this arrangement by choice, trading temporary freedom for financial survival.

When a modern person declares bankruptcy today, they lose rights—they cannot be a company director, cannot borrow money, cannot hold certain licenses. They lose financial privileges, not their humanity. Similarly, a biblical servant lost certain civil rights but retained fundamental human dignity.

## Bad Slavery: The Trade in Human Beings

### The True Evil: Slave Trading

The real evil condemned throughout Scripture is not indentured servitude but **the slave trade**—the kidnapping and selling of people for profit.

> 1 Timothy 1:9-10 "We also know that the law is made not for the righteous but for lawbreakers and rebels, the ungodly and sinful, the unholy and irreligious, for those who kill their fathers or mothers, for murderers, for the sexually immoral, for those practicing homosexuality, **for slave traders** and liars and perjurers—and for whatever else is contrary to the sound doctrine."

The Bible's penalty for kidnapping was death:

> Deuteronomy 24:7 "If someone is caught kidnapping a fellow Israelite and treating or selling them as a slave, the kidnapper must die. You must purge the evil from among you."
>
> Exodus 21:16 "Anyone who kidnaps someone is to be put to death, whether the victim has been sold or is still in the kidnapper's possession."

Buying and selling human beings for profit is abominable. They are not commodities or derivatives—they are image-bearers of God.

### A Historical Truth We Must Face

The modern slave trade that transported Africans across the Atlantic involved multiple actors. While Europeans purchased enslaved people, historians estimate that **90 percent of those shipped to the New World were enslaved by Africans and then sold to European traders**. European traders themselves rarely captured slaves—they purchased them from African kings, chiefs, and wealthy merchants.

The Arab slave trade also participated heavily in this commerce, transporting both Africans and Europeans.

This doesn't excuse the system, but it shows the complexity: **slavery as a trade is a human evil that transcends race and geography.**

### Modern Slavery Still Exists

Today, the slave trade continues:

- **War-torn regions:** Boko Haram kidnaps child brides
- **Sex trafficking:** People smugglers trap vulnerable individuals in brothels across Asia
- **Forced labor:** Migrants lured with promises of factory work but forced into servitude

The Bible condemns this:

> Revelation 18:10-13 "Terrified at her torment, they will stand far off and cry: 'Woe! Woe to you, great city, you mighty city of Babylon! In one hour your doom has come!' The merchants of the earth will weep and mourn over her because no one buys their cargoes anymore—cargoes of gold, silver, precious stones and pearls; fine linen, purple, silk and scarlet cloth; every sort of citron wood, and articles of every kind made of ivory, costly wood, bronze, iron and marble; cargoes of cinnamon and spice, of incense, myrrh and frankincense, of wine and olive oil, of fine flour and wheat; cattle and sheep; horses and carriages; **and human beings sold as slaves.**"

God promises judgment on those who traffic in human beings. There is hope that one day this evil will end.

## Servants Today: Understanding Our Own Servitude

### A Modern Reality

Unless you are a child, self-employed, or retired, **we are all indentured servants to some degree**. Consider:

- **Work contracts** bind us to specific employers for specific periods
- **Geographical assignments** relocate us far from home
- **Secondments** place us under other firms' authority for seasons
- **Scholarships** restrict our freedom—we cannot leave without forfeiting funds
- **Government bonds** (like my cousin in Malaysia) obligate us to work for years to repay educational investments
- **Overseas domestic workers** in Hong Kong, Singapore, and Malaysia borrow for visas and plane tickets, then remain trapped by contractual obligations
- **Historical indentured servitude** brought immigrants to America—they paid for passage by working years without option to leave or seek other employment

Most of us need our jobs more than our jobs need us. We endure difficult conditions, temperamental bosses, and restrictions on our freedom because we have families, mortgages, and bills.

### Application: How to Work with Integrity

In such circumstances, Scripture teaches us:

> Ephesians 6:5-8 "Slaves, obey your earthly masters with **respect and fear**, and with **sincerity of heart**, just as you would obey Christ. Obey them not only to win their favor when their eye is on you, but as slaves of Christ, doing the **will of God from your heart**. **Serve wholeheartedly**, as if you were serving the Lord, not people, because you know that the Lord will reward each one for whatever good they do, whether they are slave or free."

The emphasis is entirely on **attitude and heart:**

- **Respect and fear** toward your employer
- **Sincerity of heart** (not hypocrisy)
- **From your heart** (genuine devotion)
- **Wholeheartedly** (full commitment)

The teaching is radical: treat your work as service to Jesus Himself. Would you cheat Jesus? Don't cheat your employer. Would you harm Jesus? Don't harm your master—even when tempted by anger or resentment.

This applies even to believing masters who might be tempted to use their faith as justification for poor treatment:

> 1 Timothy 6:1-2 "All who are under the yoke of slavery should consider their masters **worthy of full respect**, so that God's name and our teaching may not be slandered. Those who have believing masters should not show them disrespect just because they are fellow believers. Instead, they should serve them even better because their masters are dear to them as fellow believers and are devoted to the welfare of their slaves. These are the things you are to teach and insist on."

### Practical Living:

- **Turn up for work** with integrity
- **Be a good employee**—honest, diligent, faithful
- **Do it for the Lord**, not merely to impress
- **Be grateful for work** because it provides for your family, supports your church, enables generosity to others

The ancient Romans discovered this truth: it was slaves who became Christian first, and their good witness converted their employers. Slowly, the empire itself became Christian—not through conquest but through the transformed lives of servants doing their work as service to God.

## Instruction for Masters: Accountability Before God

> Ephesians 6:9 "And masters, treat your slaves in the same way. Do not threaten them, since you know that he who is both their Master and yours is in heaven, and there is no favoritism with him."

### The Principle of Sub-Mastership

"Treat your slaves in the same way"—wholeheartedly, as if serving God. Do not threaten their welfare or livelihood.

**No matter how powerful you become, you are only a sub-master.** There is always a Boss over you. God will hold you accountable for how you treat those under your authority.

### No Favoritism Before God

There are no special privileges for being a master rather than a servant. God will evaluate your actions without bias. You cannot hide behind your position or power.

### For Those Becoming Masters

Most of you are not yet in positions of authority, but many will be. Whether as:

- **Employers** managing others
- **Bosses** with hiring and firing authority
- **Leaders** in democracy—you are masters of politicians who must be held accountable
- **Parents** and mentors guiding others

Paul encourages slaves to seek freedom if possible:

> 1 Corinthians 7:21 "Were you a slave when you were called? Don't let it trouble you—although if you can gain your freedom, do so."

**Too many of us have capability but lack ambition.** We coast through life, letting others define our future. If you can improve your position, you should. But improvement requires sacrifice:

- Thrift about materialism and consumption
- Willingness to forgo TVs, furniture, holidays, and cars
- Delayed gratification to build toward freedom

### The Christian Abolition Movement

It was precisely because the Bible condemns the **evil slave trade** that Christians led the abolitionist movement. In 1787, a group of twelve men—mostly Quakers and Anglicans—founded the Committee for the Abolition of the Slave Trade, including:

- **Granville Sharp**, who formulated plans to settle freed African people in Sierra Leone and founded Freetown
- **Thomas Clarkson**, who devoted his entire life to ending slavery despite severe hardships
- **William Wilberforce**, who led the campaign in Parliament
- **John Newton**, whose hymn "Amazing Grace" testified to redemption from his own complicity in slavery

Within twenty years, the slave trade was abolished—not because society evolved on its own, but because **Christians stood on biblical conviction** that human trafficking is abominable.

## The Critical Distinction

The world, including Kevin Rudd, conflates two entirely different things:

1. **Indentured servitude**—a fact of ancient economics and the realities of war, regulated to protect human dignity
2. **The slave trade**—cruel, oppressive kidnapping and sale of human beings for profit

**The Bible does not treat these as the same.** One it regulates with protections; the other it condemns with death penalties.

This distinction matters profoundly for how we read Scripture. The Bible's acceptance of servitude does not mean endorsement of chattel slavery or human trafficking. Rather, it shows how God meets people where they are—in systems that existed—and transforms them through protection, dignity, and hope for freedom.

Human trafficking remains one of the great evils of our age. The church must continue the work that Wilberforce and the abolitionists began: **standing firmly against the trade in human beings** while treating all who work with the dignity they deserve as image-bearers of God.
