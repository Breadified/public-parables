# Luke 19:11-27

**Title:** Riches Found: How to be Rich - Rich in Deeds

**Date:** 23 April 2024

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## The Expectation of Immediacy (v. 11-14)

As Jesus drew near to Jerusalem, his disciples expected the kingdom of God to appear at once. The word "appear" carries a nautical sense—like a ship materializing on the horizon. They imagined that as Jerusalem came into view, God's kingdom would simultaneously arrive.

But Jesus teaches otherwise through a parable about a nobleman of noble birth (Greek: *eugenes*) who must travel to a distant country to be appointed king before returning. This journey will take time. There may be resistance—indeed, his subjects hate him and send a delegation after him protesting: "We don't want this man to be our king."

**The key lesson:** There will be an expected delay before the Master returns.

## While We Wait: Be Busy (v. 12-14)

Yet the nobleman doesn't leave his servants idle during this waiting period. Before departing, he calls ten servants and distributes to each one mina—a pound of silver. His command is clear: "Put this money to work until I come back."

The Greek word for "put to work" (*pragmateuomai*) comes from *pragma*, meaning "deed" or "thing." It speaks of trading, investing, doing business. It means: do something. Make something happen.

This seems counterintuitive. If we believe Jesus is returning, our natural inclination is to do nothing—to settle our affairs, close our shops, and simply wait. History offers a sobering example. During the Second Great Awakening of the 19th century, William Miller calculated that Jesus would return in 1843-44. When a spectacular comet appeared in 1843 with a 200-mile tail visible even in daylight, many believers saw confirmation of his prophecy. They ceased work entirely, closed their businesses, and did nothing as they waited to ascend in what they called the "judgment car" of the comet. When October 22, 1844 came and went without the Second Coming, they experienced what became known as the Great Disappointment.

We cannot blame them for their fatalism. But Jesus teaches the opposite: **Be busy because he is coming back.**

Today's world echoes this same despair. In China, there's the "lying flat" phenomenon—a widespread refusal to strive. In Australia, recent euthanasia legislation reflects a culture of giving up. Many of us lose ambition and prefer to mindlessly scroll through life.

Yet the parable calls us to different action. Jesus expects us to be occupied when he returns—not idle, but actively working.

## The Accounting: Faithfulness and Results (v. 15-19)

When the nobleman finally returns as king, his first order of business is to settle accounts with his servants. He wants to know what they have gained through business and investment.

**The first servant brings good news:** "Sir, your mina has earned ten more."

A return of 1000%—one mina transformed into ten. The king's response is effusive: "Well done, my good servant! Because you have been trustworthy in a very small matter, take charge of ten cities."

Notice the commendation centers on faithfulness, not on the absolute number. The king doesn't measure success solely by the magnitude of profit.

**The second servant reports:** "Sir, your mina has earned five more."

Half the return of the first servant. Yet the king rewards him proportionally: "You take charge of five cities." He doesn't scold the second servant for producing only half. The principle is consistent—faithfulness yields reward.

It's striking that Jesus uses this parable to commend successful business. There's no hint that enterprise is sinful or exploitative. The businessman takes capital and risks it to create profit. This requires courage. Many people see a brilliant idea but never act on it. The businessman is the one who actually takes the risk—who foregoes comfort, steady income, and security; who invests capital and delays gratification to make the idea bear fruit. Woolworths opened in 1924, but by 1925 had more customers than staff. The founders and shareholders had to inject more capital to keep it alive. They took the risk. Today, after nearly a century of employment and growth, Woolworths achieves a 10% return on investment—a pittance for such sustained risk-taking.

## The Servant Who Refused to Risk (v. 20-23)

**The third servant presents a very different report:** "Sir, here is your mina; I have kept it laid away in a piece of cloth."

He did nothing. He wrapped the money carefully and hid it—preserved it, untouched, like an old heirloom. Why? His explanation reveals his heart:

> "I was afraid of you, because you are a hard man. You take out what you did not put in and reap what you did not sow."

Fear paralyzed him. He perceived the king as harsh, exacting, impossible to please. Better to avoid failure than to risk and lose.

The king's response is judicial: "I will judge you by your own words, you wicked servant! You knew that I am a hard man, taking out what I did not put in, and reaping what I did not sow? Why then didn't you put my money on deposit, so that when I came back, I could have collected it with interest?"

The king's logic cuts deep: If you truly believed me to be such a tyrant, you should have at least attempted something minimal. You could have placed it in a bank account earning the risk-free rate. Even that modest effort would have produced some return.

But the third servant's characterization of the master is fundamentally false. We see this in how the king treated the first two servants. He commended them for faithfulness, not merely for results. He rewarded the second servant proportionally, not punished him for producing less. The king is not the harsh man the third servant imagined.

The real issue is this: **The third servant despised the king.** He projected his own selfishness onto the master, accusing him of reaping where he had not sown. But this accusation is itself a projection. The master had sown—he gave each servant the mina! The problem isn't that the master failed to sow. The problem is that the master finds no harvest. The third servant's inaction yielded zero return on investment. He wasted what was entrusted to him.

Moreover, the third servant wanted to reap where he had not sown. He wanted approval and cities without any work, without any risk of failure.

## The Consequence and Principle (v. 24-26)

The king's judgment is swift: "Take his mina away from him and give it to the one who has ten minas."

The onlookers object: "Sir, he already has ten!"

But the king's reply unveils an economic principle: "I tell you that to everyone who has, more will be given, but as for the one who has nothing, even what they have will be taken away."

Capital gravitates toward those who can steward it productively. The one who has demonstrated faithfulness receives more responsibility. The one who produces nothing loses even what he has. Redistributing the third servant's mina to the unproductive would waste it. The productive servant will generate more wealth and benefit all.

This seems harsh, even unjust. Yet it reflects reality. A socialist dream of perpetual redistribution might equalize starting positions, but within a generation, inequality returns—because the issue isn't what people have, but who they are. Everyone receives one mina. The difference is the servant you choose to become.

## The Enemies Destroyed (v. 27)

The parable concludes with darkness: "But those enemies of mine who did not want me to be king over them—bring them here and kill them in front of me."

The third servant, aligned with those who rejected the king's rule, faces destruction along with the rebels.

## Rich in Deeds

We are all like those ten servants. Each of us receives one mina—one life. The king (Jesus) has sown into our lives through creation and redemption. He will return as king. The accounting is certain.

What will he find? Will he discover us idle and afraid, making excuses? Or will he catch us busy—working for his kingdom?

The needs are immense. The gospel needs proclamation. It needs people, resources, buildings, influence, media presence, and believers who are activated and motivated. The wicked have murdered the unborn for decades; now they target the elderly. Soon they will extend their reach further. The harvest is ripe and the laborers are few.

Jesus has purchased our freedom with his blood. The field where our seeds lie is watered by his sacrifice and fertilized by his body. When he rose as king and will return in final judgment, he expects to see fruit.

Sow generously. Don't live for comfort, convenience, or a predetermined life plan that avoids sacrifice and interruption. A life devoted to God's purposes means being available—stopping your game to arrive at church on time, adjusting career ambitions for your children, caring for aging parents, doing a second shift to provide, evangelizing friends, praying for God's kingdom to grow.

The best time to plant was yesterday. The second best time is today.

When he comes, let him find you not idle, but busy:

- Lifting church chairs
- Feeding your children  
- Befriending the lonely
- Caring for the frail
- Providing for your family
- Sharing the gospel
- Praying for his kingdom

Be rich in deeds.
