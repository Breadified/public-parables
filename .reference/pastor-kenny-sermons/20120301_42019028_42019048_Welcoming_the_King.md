# Luke 19:28-48

**Title:** Welcoming the King
**Date:** 11 March 2012
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Best Welcome Ever

What comes to mind when you think of being welcomed? A red carpet at an elegant event. A surprise party with all your friends. Coming home to family waiting at the airport. A five-star hotel with high ceilings, polished floors, and staff opening grand doors to greet you by name. These moments make us feel valued and expected.

Today we encounter the greatest welcome ever given—the arrival of Jesus the King into Jerusalem.

## 1. The Expected King (28-35)

Jesus approaches the end of his long journey through Luke's gospel (chapters 9-19). As he arrives at the Mount of Olives overlooking Jerusalem, he gives his disciples peculiar instructions.

> "Go into the village ahead of you. As you enter it, you will find a young donkey tied there, on which no one has ever sat. Untie it and bring it here. If anyone asks you, 'Why are you untying it?' say this: 'The Lord needs it.'" (Luke 19:29-31)

This is strange. You don't simply take someone's animal. Yet remarkably, it happens exactly as Jesus predicted. The owners release the donkey without resistance—they are expecting him.

**Why the donkey?** This points directly to an Old Testament prophecy. Five hundred years earlier, Zechariah proclaimed:

> "Rejoice greatly, O Daughter of Zion! Shout, Daughter of Jerusalem! See, your king comes to you, righteous and having salvation, gentle and riding on a donkey, on a colt, the foal of a donkey." (Zechariah 9:9)

To Western eyes, a donkey seems an odd choice for royalty. But in Hebrew culture, the donkey carried deep significance:

- **Practical choice**: Israelites were forbidden from importing Egyptian horses. Donkeys suited Israel's mountainous terrain far better.

- **Symbol of wealth and leadership**: Throughout Israel's history, donkeys were the transport of the elite. Judges 10:4 and 12:14 describe rulers "who rode on donkeys" as signs of success and power. Jacob blessed his son Judah (ancestor of Jesus) saying he would "tether his donkey to a vine" (Genesis 49:11)—a picture of prosperity and peace.

- **Symbol of peace**: Zechariah 9:10 continues: "I will take away the chariots from Ephraim and the war-horses from Jerusalem... He will proclaim peace to the nations."

The donkey announces: *This is the expected king who brings peace.*

The disciples place their robes on the donkey as a saddle, honoring Jesus as royalty. The owners simply yield what they possess. This is the reception of one who was anticipated, prepared for, and recognized.

## 2. The Expected Reception (36-38)

As Jesus descends the Mount of Olives, the crowd spontaneously honors him:

> "They were spreading their robes on the road." (Luke 19:36)

This echoes the ancient protocol of coronation. When King Jehu was anointed in 2 Kings 9:13, his followers "quickly took their cloaks and spread them under him on the bare steps. Then they blew the trumpet and proclaimed, 'Jehu is king!'"

The disciples burst into praise:

> "The King who comes in the name of the Lord is the blessed One. Peace in heaven and glory in the highest heaven!" (Luke 19:38)

This song draws from Psalm 118:26—a processional hymn celebrating God's triumph over enemies and the entrance of the righteous into the gates. It echoes the ancient royal processions: after conquering, kings returned to their cities with tributes, soldiers, and celebration.

The people recognize who Jesus is. They welcome him with the reception befitting a long-awaited king.

## 3. The Unexpected Reception (39, 47-48)

But not all respond with celebration.

> "Some of the Pharisees from the crowd told Him, 'Teacher, rebuke Your disciples.'" (Luke 19:39)

The religious leaders reject the proclamation. They don't want Jesus paraded before crowds. They want silence.

This rejection deepens into something darker:

> "Every day He was teaching in the temple complex. The chief priests, the scribes, and the leaders of the people were looking for a way to destroy Him, but they could not find a way to do it, because all the people were captivated by what they heard." (Luke 19:47-48)

The very people who knew Zechariah's prophecy, who studied Psalm 118, who should have recognized the expected king—they plot his death instead.

## 4. The Unexpected King (40-46)

Jesus responds to the Pharisees' demand for silence:

> "I tell you, if they were to keep silent, the stones would cry out!" (Luke 19:40)

His kingship is so obvious, so undeniable, that even dead stones would praise God. This echoes his earlier teaching in Luke 3:8—God can "raise up children for Abraham from these stones." The reality of Jesus as King transcends all human resistance.

This is a king over people and creation itself. A king who commands waves, wind, and stones. The living religious leaders refuse to acknowledge what even the stones would celebrate. Their silence condemns them.

Yet Jesus responds not with judgment alone, but with grief:

> "As He approached and saw the city, He wept over it, saying, 'If you knew this day what would bring peace—but now it is hidden from your eyes. For the days will come on you when your enemies will build an embankment against you, surround you, and hem you in on every side. They will crush you and your children within you to the ground, and they will not leave one stone on another in you, because you did not recognize the time of your visitation.'" (Luke 19:41-44)

Jesus weeps. The rejection of the king brings disaster—siege, destruction, complete devastation. Not even stones will remain standing. This is the consequence of refusing to recognize God's visitation to them.

Then Jesus cleanses the temple:

> "He went into the temple complex and began to throw out those who were selling, and He said, 'It is written, My house will be a house of prayer, but you have made it a den of thieves!'" (Luke 19:45-46)

This connects to Zechariah 14:21—in the day of God's full redemption, there will be no more traders in the house of the Lord.

## The Tension: Recognizing Jesus Today

Many ask: "Why doesn't God appear to me today? If I could see him, I would believe."

Consider: Jesus *did* appear. Two thousand years ago, he stood before the people of Jerusalem in flesh and blood. Yet they rejected him. There's no guarantee that a personal appearance would produce faith—indeed, the majority of those who saw Jesus crucified him.

Instead, Luke 16:27-31 reveals God's expectation: we are to trust in Scripture, not in signs and wonders. We have the Bible—the testimony of eyewitnesses, the reliable historical record of real events. That is sufficient.

The danger lies in refusing to recognize this visitation of God. If you delay, if you demand proof on your own terms, you test God—a dangerous position. His next appearance will not be gentle. He will come with judgment upon those who have not repented.

> "Recognize the time of your visitation." (Luke 19:44)

## Welcoming the King

If you would welcome Jesus now—truly receive him—your reception on his return will be entirely different:

- "Your sins are forgiven" (Luke 5)
- "Your reward is great" (Luke 6)
- "Your faith has made you well" (Luke 8)
- "Eat your fill" (Luke 9)
- "Rejoice, yours is the kingdom" (Luke 10)
- "Here is the kingdom" (Luke 12)
- "Here is the crown of life"
- "Well done, good and faithful servant"

Will you welcome him?
