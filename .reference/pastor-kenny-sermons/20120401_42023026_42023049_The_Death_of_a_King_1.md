# Luke 23:26-49
**Title:** The Death of a King
**Date:** April 1, 2012
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## If You Are King

When we think of royalty, we imagine tremendous privilege. Kings summon and suspend parliaments, appoint ministers, declare wars. They live surrounded by the best of everything—finest food, finest cars, finest education. Most importantly, if you are king, you are not expected to die. You have bodyguards and the best doctors.

But today we encounter a different kind of king.

> There was a written notice above him, which read: "This is the king of the Jews." (Luke 23:38)

This notice hangs above his death. How did we arrive at this moment? Over the past weeks, we've followed Jesus through the final chapters of Luke's gospel. He came as a king riding a donkey, fulfilling prophecy. He was betrayed by his closest disciples. After a show trial, he was condemned to death.

## The Journey to Golgotha

> As the soldiers led him away, they seized Simon from Cyrene, who was on his way in from the country, and put the cross on him and made him carry it behind Jesus. (Luke 23:26)

Simon, a Cyrenian from North Africa, was likely coming in from the fields for the festival, unaware of the chaos unfolding in Jerusalem. He becomes an unwilling participant in history.

A large crowd follows—women mourning and wailing. Two criminals are led out to execution alongside Jesus.

> When they came to the place called the Skull, they crucified him there, along with the criminals—one on his right, the other on his left. (Luke 23:33-34)

Golgotha—the place of the skull—was a public execution site. The name references either the abandoned skulls scattered there or the bones of a nearby cemetery.

### What Luke Doesn't Tell Us

Interestingly, Luke (like the other gospel writers) does not dwell on the physical horror of crucifixion. There is no description of the nails, no graphic portrayal of blood and suffering. While modern retellings focus obsessively on physical torture, the New Testament writers direct our attention elsewhere—to the deeper spiritual reality.

The pain of God on the cross is not primarily physical pain. It is the pain of separation from God, of facing the judgment of all the world. Some Christians attempt to reenact crucifixion as if physical suffering brings them closer to Christ. But crucifixion cannot be duplicated. What Jesus accomplished behind the scenes—the spiritual transaction—cannot be replicated through bodily pain.

## "Save Yourself"

A temptation echoes across Golgotha:

> One of the criminals who hung there hurled insults at him: "Aren't you the Messiah? Save yourself and us!" (Luke 23:39)

The people, the rulers, the soldiers all mock him with the same refrain:

> He saved others; let him save himself if he is God's Messiah, the Chosen One. (Luke 23:35)
> 
> If you are the king of the Jews, save yourself. (Luke 23:37)

This formula—"If you are the Son of God"—echoes the desert temptation. The devil tested Jesus: "If you are the Son of God, throw yourself down from here" (Luke 4:9). And like all temptations, there is a grain of truth in it.

Yes, Jesus could have saved himself. Angels would protect him. As king, he would receive splendor and authority. He could command the elements. The nails would pop free, his wounds would heal instantly. In a moment, he could move from unbearable agony to a lap of luxury—like a reality show contestant suddenly airlifted from suffering to comfort in an air-conditioned studio.

And consider the remarkable results: Tada! A superhero rising into the air! Ready-made audience! Surely everyone would believe. Isn't that what we ask for?

But Jesus doesn't save himself. Why?

The leaders, soldiers, and criminals misunderstand the real problem. It is not whether people believe in God. The problem is sin and the forgiveness of sin. God doesn't need our love; we need God's love.

## "Save Us"

The second criminal grasps something deeper:

> Don't you fear God, he said, since you are under the same sentence? We are punished justly, for we are getting what our deeds deserve. But this man has done nothing wrong. (Luke 23:40-41)

The criminals face the fundamental human problem: punishment for sin. And this is not merely their problem—it is ours.

> Jesus turned and said to them, Daughters of Jerusalem, do not weep for me; weep for yourselves and for your children. For the time will come when you will say, Blessed are the childless women, the wombs that never bore and the breasts that never nursed! Then they will say to the mountains, Fall on us! and to the hills, Cover us! (Luke 23:28-30)

Judgment is coming, and it will be terrible. It will be so catastrophic that childlessness—normally considered a curse—will seem a blessing by comparison. People will cry out for death to escape God's wrath.

Like the thieves, we are in desperate need of rescue.

## Save Yourself OR Us

Here is the puzzle: Jesus could save himself. Jesus could save the thieves and us. Why not both?

Because there is one thing God cannot do. Jesus does not face the reality of saving himself AND us. It is an either/or choice.

Either he saves himself, or he saves us. 

Like the Titanic with one spot on the door—only Jack or Rose. Like the Hunger Games—only one tribute survives. Jesus volunteers as tribute.

> The anger of God against sin and wickedness cannot merely dissipate. It must land on a person.

It lands either on Jesus or on us. If he saves himself, we face punishment. If we face what we deserve, we are destroyed, for we cannot endure God's judgment.

**So he made a choice. Jesus did not save himself. He saved us.**

Faced with a choice between himself and you, he chose you.

> Jesus has done nothing wrong. The centurion, seeing what had happened, praised God and said, Surely this was a righteous man. (Luke 23:47)

An innocent man. Never bullied anyone. Never swore. Never killed. Never warred. He lived in righteousness and purity. And yet he is punished—not for his sins, but for ours.

## The King Remembers

> Then he said, Jesus, remember me when you come into your kingdom. (Luke 23:42)

The criminal's plea is a statement of faith. He acknowledges that Jesus is king. He asks to be saved. And this is what God desires from us: repentance—the acknowledgment of our guilt and a cry for help.

> Jesus answered him, Truly I tell you, today you will be with me in paradise. (Luke 23:43)

The king says yes. The king comes to die for you. Today you will be with me in paradise.

Paradise means garden. The criminal doesn't ask to be remembered for good works or boasting. He asks to be remembered because he doesn't deserve to be remembered. Because what is done for you is already complete.

**This is what it means to truly fear God**—to realize the calamity we face and to act appropriately. Fear of God is not irrational phobia. It is a natural, healthy fear, like our fear of fire or heights. It is the appropriate response to reality. So we must repent.

## The Darkness and the Cross

> It was now about noon, and darkness came over the whole land until three in the afternoon, for the sun stopped shining. And the curtain of the temple was torn in two. Jesus called out with a loud voice, Father, into your hands I commit my spirit. When he had said this, he breathed his last. (Luke 23:44-46)

Light fails. Darkness falls. But this is not the end. Jesus rose on the third day, conquering death and sin. He is truly King now, and he promises to return—next time not to save, but to judge.

## Our Choice

We must decide today. If Jesus is the true King:

- **Believe** that he can save you because he did not save himself.
- **Take up the cross** for his sake. This will be unpopular. At university, in your workplace, in the world—it will cost you.
- **Follow Simon Cyrene's example.** He carried the cross unwillingly at first, but he points to a better choice for us: to willingly give up your life for Jesus.

We face the same either/or choice that Jesus faced. We keep wanting both—to follow Jesus and to follow the world. But it is either/or. We cannot have both.

What will be your response?
