# Luke 13:1-30

**Title:** Repent or Perish
**Date:** December 2, 2007
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## The Narrow Door: Urgency of Decision

### The Question About Salvation

Someone asked Jesus a question that strikes at the heart of human anxiety: "Lord, are only a few people going to be saved?" This question reveals a fundamental misunderstanding about how God's kingdom works.

Jesus answered with both urgency and clarity:

> Make every effort to enter through the narrow door, because many, I tell you, will try to enter and will not be able to.

### What the Narrow Door Means

The Pharisees interpreted the narrow door as symbolic of difficult moral effort—the kingdom as something you earn through being good enough, righteous enough, better than others. They believed they belonged because of their status, their reputation, their achievements. They were Israel's celebrities, full of pride about themselves.

But Jesus redirects their thinking entirely. The narrow door doesn't mean *difficult entry*—it means *limited time*. Notice what follows:

> Once the owner of the house gets up and closes the door, you will stand outside knocking and pleading, "Sir, open the door for us." But he will answer, "I don't know you or where you come from." Then you will say, "We ate and drank with you, and you taught in our streets." But he will reply, "I don't know you or where you come from. Away from me, all you evildoers!" There will be weeping there, and gnashing of teeth, when you see Abraham, Isaac and Jacob and all the prophets in the kingdom of God, but you yourselves thrown out.

The tragedy isn't that the door is hard to pass through. The tragedy is that many will try to enter *after it closes*. The narrow door represents a window of opportunity that will shut.

### The Pharisees' Fatal Mistake

The Pharisees looked at suffering and misfortune and drew a dangerous conclusion. When they heard about the Galileans whose blood Pilate mixed with their sacrifices in the temple, and about eighteen people who died when the tower of Siloam collapsed, they thought: "These people must be worse sinners than everyone else. Their tragedy proves their guilt."

Jesus confronted this logic head-on:

> Do you think that these Galileans were worse sinners than all the other Galileans because they suffered this way? I tell you, no! But unless you repent, you too will all perish.

They were operating on a curved grading system—comparing themselves to those worse off. The Pharisees in their luxury and popularity looked down on the weak, the sick, the suffering, and concluded they were the righteous ones. But God doesn't judge on a curve.

### God Judges by Absolute Standards

The fundamental error is this: God doesn't use a standard deviation graph where the top percentage gets in and the rest are left out. Entry into heaven is not determined by where you rank compared to others.

Think of it like a modern auction. There's a reserve price—an absolute minimum that must be met. You can have the highest bid in the room, but if you haven't reached the reserve price, you don't win. No one gets in.

This is our situation exactly: **No one has met God's reserve price**. No one has met the absolute standard of holiness God demands. "All have sinned and fall short of the glory of God. There is no one righteous, not even one." Whether we're a cripple, a thief, or a proud Pharisee—we're all the same. We're all guilty of ignoring and rejecting God.

### The Way In Is Simple

Yet the way to enter isn't through moral effort at all. Luke 11 reminds us:

> Ask and it will be given to you; seek and you will find; knock and the door will be opened to you. For everyone who asks receives; the one who seeks finds; and to the one who knocks, the door will be opened.

And specifically:

> If you then, though you are evil, know how to give good gifts to your children, how much more will your Father in heaven give the Holy Spirit to those who ask him!

There's no qualification, no earning. You simply ask. When you ask God for His Holy Spirit—for His presence—He will say yes. It's like your children asking for more vegetables or more homework. Of course, yes!

### Don't Delay

The parable of the fig tree drives home the urgency:

> A man had a fig tree growing in his vineyard, and he went to look for fruit on it but did not find any. So he said to the man who took care of the vineyard, "For three years now I've been coming to look for fruit on this fig tree and haven't found any. Cut it down! Why should it use up the soil?" But the man replied, "Sir, leave it alone for one more year, and I'll dig around it and fertilize it. If it bears fruit next year, fine! If not, then cut it down."

Time is short. One more year. This is the deal of a lifetime, yet we find countless ways to delay:

- **Waiting for intellectual certainty**: We demand absolute proof before deciding, when really we never demand absolute certainty for any other major life decision. We live with probable, not absolute, standards in everything else.

- **Overcritical skepticism**: We've had our questions answered, but we keep waiting for the next objection to surface, trying to find fault.

- **Waiting for a feeling**: Wanting Jesus to appear to us in some special, unmistakable way. But when He appears, it will be too late.

- **Postponing for life circumstances**: "After my career is established... after my holiday... after I finish school. Really? You'd miss heaven for the HSC?"

- **Waiting for the right family moment**: "I'll become a Christian when my kids are grown." But why wait? If this is genuinely good news, your family needs to hear it from you. You're the one who will shape their understanding.

This is the ultimate FOMO—fear of missing out. But it's real. Don't delay. If you're not a Christian today, don't wait another day. If you have genuine questions, let's explore them honestly. But don't use questions as an excuse for postponement.

## The Big Kingdom: Scope and Hope

### A Small Door Leads to Something Vast

Just because the door is narrow doesn't mean the kingdom is small. In fact, Jesus illustrates the kingdom's expansive, unstoppable growth:

> What is the kingdom of God like? What shall I compare it to? It is like a mustard seed, which a man took and planted in his garden. It grew and became a tree, and the birds perched in its branches.

And again:

> What shall I compare the kingdom of God to? It is like yeast that a woman took and mixed into about sixty pounds of flour until it worked all through the dough.

A tiny seed becomes a sheltering tree. A pinch of yeast transforms an entire batch. The kingdom starts small, hidden, seemingly insignificant. But it grows, it spreads, it transforms everything it touches.

### The Gathering of Nations

> People will come from east and west and north and south, and will take their places at the feast in the kingdom of God. Indeed, there are those who are last who will be first, and first who will be last.

Consider the numbers: Seven billion people alive today, and historians estimate about 107 billion people have ever lived. Yet Jesus speaks of a vast gathering from every corner of the earth—a feast so large that the hierarchies of this world are completely inverted.

### Your Place in Something Bigger

If you're a Christian, you might feel like a minority. In your family, in your workplace, in your culture. Christianity itself has shifted from being something culturally good, to something merely benign, to something many now view as actively evil—the last obstacle to human freedom, an object of ridicule.

But you're actually part of a massive, hidden movement. You can't see it all at once, just as you can't watch a mustard seed grow in real time, or observe yeast working through dough hour by hour. But it's happening.

> In a very narrow time, the kingdom will be complete.

You've passed through the narrow door. You're part of the people of God. You're caught up in something far larger than the world's opinion about you. That's true FOMO—and it's the opposite of what the world offers. You've chosen the deal of a lifetime.

## The Cost of Rejecting Mercy: Jesus and the Pharisees

### A Dispute Over the Sabbath

On a Sabbath, Jesus was teaching in a synagogue when He saw a woman crippled by a spirit for eighteen years, completely bent over, unable to straighten up. When Jesus saw her, He called her forward:

> Woman, you are set free from your infirmity.

He put His hands on her, and immediately she straightened up and praised God.

But the synagogue leader was indignant:

> There are six days for work. So come and be healed on those days, not on the Sabbath.

Jesus's response was cutting:

> You hypocrites! Doesn't each of you on the Sabbath untie your ox or donkey from the stall and lead it out to give it water? Then should not this woman, a daughter of Abraham, whom Satan has kept bound for eighteen long years, be set free on the Sabbath day from what bound her?

The Pharisees could show mercy to animals on the Sabbath, but they would deny healing to a human being—a daughter of Abraham herself—on the grounds of religious rules. They had lost sight entirely of what the law was meant to protect.

### Jesus's Prophetic Warning

Later, some Pharisees warned Jesus:

> Leave this place and go somewhere else. Herod wants to kill you.

Jesus replied with stunning defiance and clarity:

> Go tell that fox, "I will keep on driving out demons and healing people today and tomorrow, and on the third day I will reach my goal. In any case, I must press on today and tomorrow and the next day—for surely no prophet can die outside Jerusalem!"

And then His lament over the city:

> Jerusalem, Jerusalem, you who kill the prophets and stone those sent to you, how often I have longed to gather your children together, as a hen gathers her chicks under her wings, and you were not willing. Look, your house is left to you desolate. I tell you, you will not see me again until you say, "Blessed is he who comes in the name of the Lord."

### The Pharisee and the Tax Collector

This reflects the tragedy of the Pharisee's posture. Their thinking was: "I am not like other people." And which other people? They found those worse off—the poor, the uneducated, the sick, the social outcasts—and measured themselves against them.

It's like grading on a curve. Some get As, others Bs, Cs, Ds. If you're an A, B, or C student, you think you're in. If you're a D, E, or F, you're out. We mark ourselves by how we compare to others.

But God doesn't judge this way. He doesn't grade on a curve.

### The Truth About Our Condition

The reserve price God demands is nothing less than absolute holiness. Every single person—crippled woman, thief, Pharisee, you, me—has failed to meet it.

That's why the Pharisees were wrong not just in their arrogance, but in their fundamental misunderstanding of God's judgment. The people who died horribly in accidents were indeed sinners. But they weren't *worse* sinners. Everyone falls short. No one is righteous. We're all guilty of the same fundamental sin: ignoring and rejecting God.

The only path forward is repentance—a turning around, a recognition that we cannot earn our way in, and a humble receiving of mercy when it's offered.

That's the narrow door. That's the kingdom. That's the call that echoes through every generation: **Repent, before it's too late.**
