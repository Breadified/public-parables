# Hebrews 6:13-7:28

**Title:** Melchizedek: The Priest Who Lasts Forever  
**Date:** 18 March 2018  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction: The Problem of Obsolescence

Things don't seem to last anymore. Refrigerators that used to run for decades now fail in under ten years. Phones have built-in obsolescence. Cars break down. Even massive infrastructure like bridges designed to last centuries collapse unexpectedly. We live in an age of disposability.

The book of Hebrews addresses a similar crisis among Jewish believers facing persecution. The author writes to people tempted to abandon Jesus and return to Judaism, urging them: don't give up. Jesus is better than the old system, and He offers what the old priesthood could never provide—permanence.

The key to understanding this is a mysterious figure named Melchizedek. Through him, the author proves two essential truths about Jesus:

1. **Jesus is greater** than the entire Jewish system
2. **Jesus is forever**—He will never become obsolete

---

## Part 1: Jesus is Greater

### The Mysterious Melchizedek

Melchizedek appears briefly in Genesis 14, a mysterious figure who seems to emerge from nowhere and disappear just as quickly. Yet the author of Hebrews builds an entire theological argument around him.

> "This Melchizedek was king of Salem and priest of God Most High. He met Abraham returning from the defeat of the kings and blessed him." (Hebrews 7:1)

Two titles define him:

**King of righteousness** (*melek* = king; *tsedek* = righteousness)  
**King of peace** (Salem = peace)

More remarkably, verse 3 describes him in language that echoes Christ:

> "Without father or mother, without genealogy, without beginning of days or end of life, resembling the Son of God, he remains a priest forever." (Hebrews 7:3)

### Abraham's Submission Proves Melchizedek's Greatness

Here's the critical point: when Melchizedek meets Abraham, he blesses him. In ancient protocol, the greater person blesses the lesser. This happened twice:

1. Melchizedek blessed Abraham (Genesis 14:19-20)
2. Abraham gave Melchizedek a tenth of everything he possessed

Why does this matter? Because Abraham was the patriarch—the father of the Jewish nation and Judaism itself. If someone was greater than Abraham, he was greater than the entire system that descended from him.

The author presses this further:

> "Now the law requires the descendants of Levi who become priests to collect a tenth from the people—that is, from their fellow Israelites—even though they also are descended from Abraham. But this man, however, did not trace his descent from Levi, yet he collected a tenth from Abraham and blessed him who had the promises." (Hebrews 7:5-6)

The Levites had the authority to collect tithes from the people. But Melchizedek collected a tithe from Abraham—and through Abraham, from Levi himself, who was still "in the body of his ancestor" when this happened.

**Illustration:** If the Australian Tax Office (ATO) is powerful because it can tax you, imagine an organization powerful enough to tax the ATO. Who's at the top of that organizational chart? Jesus. Because Jesus, like Melchizedek, stands above the entire priestly system.

Jesus confirmed this when confronted about His authority:

> "Before Abraham was born, I am!" (John 8:58)

He claimed to exist before the patriarch, placing Himself in the order of Melchizedek—above Abraham, above the Law, above Judaism itself.

### A Different Kind of Genealogy

The Jews were obsessed with genealogy. Priests had to prove their Levitical lineage through DNA and family records. This created a problem: Jesus came from Judah, not Levi. Moses said nothing about priests coming from that tribe.

But the author of Hebrews reveals a revolutionary truth:

> "He of whom these things are said belonged to a different tribe, and no one from that tribe has ever served at the altar... another priest like Melchizedek appears, one who has become a priest not on the basis of a regulation as to his ancestry but on the basis of the power of an indestructible life." (Hebrews 7:13-16)

Jesus's priesthood isn't based on DNA. It's based on **indestructible life**—resurrection power.

Melchizedek has no recorded father, mother, or genealogy. He simply exists in the text as a timeless figure. Jesus, by contrast, has a known genealogy and parents. But He transcends that through resurrection. Both share the mark of indestructible life.

**Illustration:** In Korea, "Kim" is an incredibly common surname—roughly one in five South Koreans. Around the time surnames became popular, the current king was a Kim. Suddenly, all Kims became related to royalty, not by blood but by a shared connection to the king.

Similarly, we are linked to Jesus. And because Jesus is linked to Melchizedek, we are linked to the highest order of priesthood:

> "Both the one who makes people holy and those who are made holy are of the same family. So Jesus is not ashamed to call them brothers and sisters." (Hebrews 2:11)

If we're related to the King, we're sitting at the top of the food chain. We have authority. We have access. We have hope that is "firm and secure. It enters the inner sanctuary behind the curtain, where our forerunner, Jesus, has entered on our behalf. He has become a high priest forever, in the order of Melchizedek." (Hebrews 6:19-20)

As Christians, we become part of the priesthood of believers—the ones mediating God's Word to the world.

## Part 2: Jesus is Forever

### The Problem with the Old System

The law was never sufficient. It couldn't accomplish what it was supposed to do.

> "If perfection could have been attained through the Levitical priesthood—and indeed the law given to the people established that priesthood—why was there still need for another priest to come, one in the order of Melchizedek, not in the order of Aaron?" (Hebrews 7:11)

The priesthood was weak and useless. It was "not fit for purpose." Priests were humans with human limitations. They had to offer sacrifices for their own sins before they could offer sacrifices for the people. They grew old. They died. And when they died, the entire mediating system depended on their successor.

> "The former regulation is set aside because it was weak and useless (for the law made nothing perfect), and a better hope is introduced, by which we draw near to God." (Hebrews 7:18-19)

A new priesthood was needed. And with it came something radical: **an oath**.

### God's Unchangeable Oath

When people make promises, we swear by someone greater than ourselves to give our words credibility:

> "People swear by someone greater than themselves, and the oath confirms what is said and puts an end to all argument." (Hebrews 6:16)

But God has no one greater to swear by. So God swore by Himself:

> "The Lord has sworn and will not change his mind: You are a priest forever." (Psalm 110:4, cited in Hebrews 7:21)

This oath appears in Genesis 15, where God made an unconditional covenant with Abraham. After Abraham expressed doubt about God's promises, God enacted a covenant ceremony—cutting animals in half and passing between the pieces, a practice that meant "May I be cut in pieces if I break this covenant." God's presence, represented by a smoking firepot and blazing torch, passed between the pieces, signifying that God alone was binding Himself to this promise.

This was a **one-sided oath**—God swearing by Himself, putting His very nature on the line.

### Why Permanence Matters

Most oaths we know don't last forever. Marriage vows end at death. Supreme Court justices eventually retire. The Hippocratic oath is fulfilled when a doctor stops practicing medicine. Even perpetual trusts typically last only three generations. Everything has an endpoint.

But God's oath about Jesus is eternal:

> "Because Jesus lives forever, he has a permanent priesthood. Therefore he is able to save completely those who come to God through him, because he always lives to intercede for them." (Hebrews 7:24-25)

Why does permanence matter? Consider our sin debt. Some sins are so grave, so numerous, that we can never repay them in a lifetime. We'll die still owing a cosmic debt we created.

**Illustration:** The United States national debt can never be repaid in full. But the government can keep paying the interest forever. The debt never goes away, but neither does the ability to service it. That's acceptable for economics, but it's disastrous for the soul facing eternal judgment.

Jesus solves this through His permanence. He doesn't die on the cross repeatedly each week. But He sits at the right hand of God, perpetually interceding on our behalf. Day after day, He petitions the Father for us. His indestructible life means His advocacy never ceases.

### The Perfect Priest

> "Such a high priest truly meets our needs—one who is holy, blameless, pure, set apart from sinners, exalted above the heavens. Unlike the other high priests, he does not need to offer sacrifices day after day, first for his own sins, and then for the sins of the people. He sacrificed for their sins once for all when he offered himself." (Hebrews 7:26-27)

The old priests were weak men who sinned. They offered sacrifices daily, their own sins first, then the people's. It was endless, cyclical, never complete.

Jesus is holy, blameless, pure. He needed no sacrifice for Himself. His single sacrifice on the cross was sufficient for all sins, for all time. The law appointed weak men as priests. But God's oath appointed the Son, who has been made perfect forever.

## Application: The Power to Save

We live in a world saturated with broken promises. Companies promise durability but deliver disposability. Governments promise solutions but deliver bureaucracy. Even well-intentioned people promise help they cannot deliver.

The fundamental question is: **Can He actually save us?**

Wanting to help is not the same as being able to help. Good intentions don't eliminate poverty. Desire doesn't cure disease. And spiritual good intentions don't reconcile us with a holy God.

But Jesus doesn't merely want to save. He has the **power to save completely** because:

1. **He is greater**—His authority supersedes every system, every law, every power that opposes us
2. **He is forever**—His intercession never stops, His priesthood never fails, His covenant never expires

We can trust Jesus. Not because of wishful thinking or sentimental faith, but because He stands in the order of Melchizedek—forever, unchanging, indestructible.

> "Because God wanted to make the unchanging nature of his purpose very clear to the heirs of what was promised, he confirmed it with an oath. God did this so that, by two unchangeable things in which it is impossible for God to lie, we who have fled to take hold of the hope set before us may be greatly encouraged." (Hebrews 6:17-18)

We are no longer subject to obsolescence. We are no longer waiting for a priest who might fail. We have Jesus—the priest who lasts forever.
