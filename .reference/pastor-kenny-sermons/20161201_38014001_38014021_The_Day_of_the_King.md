# Zechariah 14:1-21

**Title:** The Day of the King
**Date:** 25 December 2016
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Anticipating the Day

The end of the year brings mixed feelings. Some of us realize we haven't achieved all our goals. Others have endured a difficult year and simply want to move forward.

The ancient Israelites, two and a half thousand years ago, carried similar longings. Through the prophet Zechariah, God spoke to them in their hopes and disappointments with a message that still resonates: **"Return to me and I will return to you."** The final chapter of Zechariah's prophecy closes with words for both ancient Israel and for us today.

This teaching unfolds around two central themes:

1. **The Day of Escape** (Zechariah 14:1-8, 12-19)
2. **The Day of Peace** (Zechariah 14:9-11, 20-21)

---

## The Day of Escape

### The Siege Begins

The prophecy opens with devastating imagery:

> A day of the Lord is coming, Jerusalem, when your possessions will be plundered and divided up within your very walls. I will gather all the nations to Jerusalem to fight against it; the city will be captured, the houses ransacked, and the women raped. Half of the city will go into exile, but the rest of the people will not be taken from the city.

The language moves from the city itself, to individual homes, to personal violation—mothers, wives, daughters. It's an escalation of horror. Half the population is carried away as captives; the other half remains to endure continued suffering. This is complete devastation: the walls that should protect become places of terror.

### God Intervenes in Suffering

Yet in the darkest moment, God acts:

> Then the Lord will go out and fight against those nations, as he fights on a day of battle.

A strange and wonderful image follows—geological upheaval at God's intervention:

> On that day his feet will stand on the Mount of Olives, east of Jerusalem, and the Mount of Olives will be split in two from east to west, forming a great valley, with half of the mountain moving north and half moving south.

### The Geography of Rescue

Jerusalem sits on a hill, surrounded by hills, backed into a corner by enemies approaching from the west. The people are trapped. But God promises something remarkable: the mountain will split in two, creating an escape route through the valley. This echoes the first great deliverance—the parting of the Red Sea when Israel fled Egypt, where water became their salvation against pursuers.

> You will flee by my mountain valley, for it will extend to Azel. You will flee as you fled from the earthquake in the days of Uzziah king of Judah. Then the Lord my God will come, and all the holy ones with him.

The valley becomes a pathway to freedom, just as Moses divided the waters so Israel could cross. The image continues:

> On that day living water will flow out from Jerusalem, half of it east to the Dead Sea and half of it west to the Mediterranean Sea, in summer and in winter.

Just as water destroyed Pharaoh's armies in the Exodus, so the water fills this valley, completing God's judgment on the invaders.

### The Day Has Come in Jesus

This prophecy finds its fulfillment not in spectacular geological transformation, but in spiritual reality. When Jesus hung on the cross, God's Day of Judgment arrived:

> From noon until three in the afternoon darkness came over all the land. About three in the afternoon Jesus cried out in a loud voice, "Eli, Eli, lema sabachthani?" (which means "My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?") ... And when Jesus had cried out again in a loud voice, he gave up his spirit. At that moment the curtain of the temple was torn in two from top to bottom. The earth shook, the rocks split and the tombs broke open. The bodies of many holy people who had died were raised to life.

The parallels are unmistakable:
- **Darkness** came over the land (Zechariah 14:6-7)
- **The earth shook and rocks split** (Zechariah 14:4)
- **Holy people** were raised (Zechariah 14:5)

All humanity stands backed into a wall of our own making—the judgment of God. We are trapped by sin with no hope of escape. But when hope seemed impossible, a way was opened. The cross became our mountain split in two, our passage to freedom.

When Jesus breathed his last, the earth shook. In that moment, a way opened for us to return to God. His death became our solution, our escape route, our salvation.

## The Day of Peace

### Jerusalem Exalted

With escape comes transformation:

> The whole land, from Geba to Rimmon, south of Jerusalem, will become like the Arabah. But Jerusalem will be raised up high from the Benjamin Gate to the site of the First Gate, to the Corner Gate, and from the Tower of Hananel to the royal winepresses, and will remain in its place. It will be inhabited; never again will it be destroyed. Jerusalem will be secure.

No more siege. No more suffering. The city is raised high—so elevated that it needs no walls because enemies cannot reach it. Security comes not from defensive barriers but from exaltation itself. There will be universal peace:

> The Lord will be king over the whole earth. On that day there will be one Lord, and his name the only name.

### Everything Becomes Holy

The vision extends to the mundane details of daily life:

> Holy to the Lord will be inscribed on the bells of the horses, and the cooking pots in the Lord's house will be like the sacred bowls in front of the altar. Every pot in Jerusalem and Judah will be holy to the Lord Almighty, and all who come to sacrifice will take some of the pots and cook in them.

This is the radical scope of Christ's redemption. Nothing is too ordinary to be sanctified. The bells on horses, cooking pots, the worn kitchen wok—all becomes holy. It's as if every object once touched by Jesus' death becomes treasured. When someone famous dies, we suddenly find value in their possessions; how much more when the God-man dies? His death declares us holy, cleanses us by his blood, and transforms everything we touch.

One day the entire creation—every action, every thing, every moment—will be cleansed and made holy. Sin and suffering will be removed from the world entirely.

### The Call to Faith

Zechariah calls us to trust in this promise:

> Return to me and I will return to you.

Have faith. Believe in what God has said.

---

## The Day Is Coming

### Faith as Trust in God's Rescue

In Matthew 17, Jesus connects faith directly to Zechariah's prophecy:

> I tell you the truth, if you have faith as small as a mustard seed, you can say to this mountain, "Move from here to there" and it will move. Nothing will be impossible for you.

This passage is often misunderstood—as if we can demand anything from God through positive thinking. But that misses the point entirely. God is not Santa Claus. We don't tell God what we expect; God tells us what he expects. He's not a cosmic vending machine but our Father. Like all good fathers, we can ask, but ultimately he decides what is best for us.

What Jesus actually teaches is belief in God's rescue. He's saying:

- Believe in Zechariah's prophecy
- Believe in hope beyond death
- Believe God when he says judgment is coming
- Believe Jesus when he says he can save you from it

> I tell you the truth, if you have faith and do not doubt, not only can you do what was done to the fig tree, but also you can say to this mountain, "Go, throw yourself into the sea," and it will be done. If you believe, you will receive whatever you ask for in prayer.

This is faith in God and his ability to judge and rescue. You don't need enormous faith—just faith as small as a mustard seed. If you are a Christian today, you believe in the Messiah who moved mountains to rescue you. If you're not yet following Christ, if you ask for salvation—if you ask to return to him—that request will be answered, because that is his greatest will for you.

---

## Conclusion

Friends, the day has come. For some, this may catch us by surprise. For others, it's something we've been waiting for.

Zechariah waited 500 years for this prophecy to be fulfilled. The Jewish people waited centuries. And then Christmas came.

> For to us a child is born, to us a son is given, and the government will be on his shoulders. And he will be called Wonderful Counselor, Mighty God, Everlasting Father, Prince of Peace.

God was with us. This Christmas, amid all the stress and celebration and feasting, we mark the 2,000-plus years since the Lord's first coming.

And Jesus will come back again. The day has not quite finished. We await its final conclusion—when there will be peace on earth.

Put your trust in Jesus this Christmas. That day is coming, and it belongs to those who believe.
