# Jonah 1:1-16

**Title:** The Fear of God  
**Date:** 10 January 2021 (ARISE); originally 7 December 2014 (SMAC)  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Scariest Job in the World

Some of the most dangerous professions are farming, forestry, and fishing—industries with fatality rates around 11.2 per 100,000 workers. Mining has seen rates as high as 12.4 per 100,000. Yet there's a job description far more perilous than any of these, one that requires facing genuine danger with nothing but faith as protection.

## Fear of the Mission (1:1-3)

The word of Yahweh came to the prophet Jonah son of Amittai with a clear directive:

> "Go to the great city of Nineveh and preach against it, because its wickedness has come up before me."

Nineveh was no ordinary target. As the capital of Assyria, it was infamous throughout the ancient world for unparalleled cruelty. The Assyrians practiced systematic terror as military strategy—impalement, beheading, dismemberment, and mass torture sessions designed to utterly demoralize their enemies. Their very name was synonymous with violence and barbarism. To be called to preach there was to walk into the mouth of a beast.

Most prophets would complain and then obey. But Jonah's response was unprecedented:

> But Jonah ran away from the Lord and headed for Tarshish. He went down to Joppa, where he found a ship bound for that port. After paying the fare, he went aboard and sailed for Tarshish to flee from the Lord.

He fled. Whether toward Spain or distant Tarsus, the direction mattered less than the fact that Jonah—a man who claimed to fear God—attempted to escape from the God of heaven and earth.

### The Fear That Paralyzes Us

As Christians, we've been given a similar calling through the Great Commission:

> "Therefore go and make disciples of all nations, baptizing them in the name of the Father and of the Son and of the Holy Spirit, and teaching them to obey everything I have commanded you."

We're called to speak of God's goodness, the mercy purchased by His Son, and the assurance the Spirit brings. Yet it terrifies us. We fear losing our popularity. We worry about being associated with Christianity's fringe elements. We're anxious it will cost us time and money, inconvenience us, or go unappreciated. We're frightened by rejection, failure, and irrelevance.

During times of crisis—like the pandemic—our fears compound. We become paralyzed when we should be most active. But consider the logic: if we genuinely believe death is imminent, shouldn't we be more fervent in calling people to repentance? If we're so careful about infecting our loved ones that we restrict every interaction, shouldn't we at least have told them the gospel? Shouldn't we have prepared them for eternity?

> Even though I walk through the darkest valley, I will fear no evil, for you are with me; your rod and your staff, they comfort me.

If we have told them and they know Christ, we can rest in that assurance. If we haven't, our concern for their spiritual eternity should override all other fears. Getting them to hear about God must supersede every other priority. That is our responsibility as followers of Christ. Will we let fear make us escape from our duty, as Jonah did?

## Fear of Nature (1:4-5)

Jonah's disobedience didn't go unaddressed:

> Then the Lord sent a great wind on the sea, and such a violent storm arose that the ship threatened to break up.

The pagan sailors, terrified and grasping for security, cried out to their gods—each to his own deity. In a polytheistic world, there were gods for every contingency: gods of wind, gods of water, gods of the sea. When panic seized them, they turned to the only source of comfort they knew.

> All the sailors were afraid and each cried out to his own god. And they threw the cargo from the ship into the sea to lighten it from upon them.

They were attempting to appease the sea itself, offering sacrifice to placate an angry god. Such desperation is understandable when you have no true God to trust.

### The Illusion of Control

In recent years, we've witnessed a similar phenomenon. Covid felt like an angry god demanding constant appeasement. We followed its instructions religiously—rotating all our decisions around it, checking daily statistics like morning prayers, sacrificing social gatherings, church attendance, education, employment, and personal liberty. We masked ourselves, sanitized our hands until they stung, accepted restrictions we'd never thought possible. We threw our cargo overboard—our values, our priorities, our very way of life—hoping the angry god would finally be satisfied.

Yet no amount of compliance brought peace. Infections continued despite the highest vaccination rates in the world. The storm never truly calmed.

Here's the hard truth: following Covid's rules cannot save us. Panic and sacrifice won't appease a false god. Only the true God can rescue us:

> God alone himself will save us. He offers himself in the person of his Son, who appeases the wrath of God by taking it upon himself.

The question becomes: who truly rules? Who is responsible for creation and circumstance?

## Fear of the God of Heaven, Sea, and Land (1:6-16)

As the storm raged, the captain discovered Jonah asleep below deck and demanded he pray to his god. The sailors, desperate to identify the source of their calamity, cast lots to determine who was responsible. The lot fell on Jonah.

> So they asked him, "Tell us, who is responsible for making all this trouble for us? What kind of work do you do? Where do you come from? What is your country? From what people are you?"

It was an interrogation. Jonah's answers were brief:

> He answered, "I am a Hebrew and I worship the Lord, the God of heaven, who made the sea and the dry land."

The Hebrew word translated "worship" actually means "fear." Jonah declared, "I fear Yahweh." Yet this raises a profound contradiction: if he truly feared the God of heaven and earth—the sovereign ruler of all creation—why was he sleeping peacefully while pagan sailors panicked? Why was he fleeing from the God who commands both heaven and land, who controls wind and waves?

The sailors, upon hearing that Yahweh was Lord, were terrified. They understood the implications: if the sea itself is so powerful it inspires dread, what of the One who created the sea? But they moved from terror to repentance:

> Then they took Jonah and threw him overboard, and the raging sea grew calm. At this the men greatly feared the Lord, and they offered a sacrifice to the Lord and made vows to him.

The pagan sailors came to a proper fear of God. They received His mercy. They responded with worship and vows. In contrast, Jonah—who claimed to fear God—showed through his disobedience that his fear was merely intellectual assent, not lived conviction.

### A Troubling Question

This raises an uncomfortable reality for believers. We see ourselves in Jonah's contradiction. How many of us say we fear God while our actions reveal otherwise? During crises, Christians panic alongside unbelievers. We cancel commitments. We spread fear. We live as though God has lost control of creation.

This is embarrassing. The pagans look at us and see no difference. We claim to follow the God of heaven, sea, and land—yet we act as though we follow false gods and false fears. How does that testify to the world?

The fundamental question is this: **Who fears God enough to do His mission?** Who loves the lost enough to overcome personal anxiety? Who trusts God's sovereignty so completely that earthly threats lose their power?

### The Call to Faithful Mission

Those called to share the gospel face the most dangerous job in the world. Historically, believers have faced imprisonment and martyrdom. Today we face infection and social disapproval. But we cannot let fear paralyze us.

This church exists, in part, to encourage one another toward faithful obedience to Christ's commission. If you sense God's calling on your life to participate in this mission—to grow His kingdom, to call believers and unbelievers alike to follow Jesus—you have accepted the most perilous task imaginable.

Yet we must not shrink back. Our responsibility is clear. Our mission is urgent. Our God is sovereign over every storm.

Fear God. That fear will cast out all other fears and empower you to do what He's called you to do.
