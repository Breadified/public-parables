# Isaiah 55:1-7

**Title:** Can You Afford NOT to Follow Jesus?

**Date:** 16 July 2012

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## The Best Bargain

We all love a good bargain. Whether it's a garage sale find, a deal from Ozbargain.com, or even $2 gravy and rice—there's something deeply satisfying about getting more than we paid for. Isaiah, an ancient Jewish prophet writing some 500 years before Jesus came, understood this too. He knew how to speak to people in terms they understood, and he begins this passage by inviting his people to consider the ultimate bargain.

## 1. Free Food

> "Come, all you who are thirsty, come to the waters; and you who have no money, come, buy and eat! Come, buy wine and milk without money and without cost." (Isaiah 55:1)

Isaiah's people were desperate. Ancient Israel had been ravaged by successive invaders, and the nation was in ruins. To these thirsty and hungry people, Isaiah extends an invitation: come to the waters. Bring no money—literally, no silver. Come and buy wine and milk without money and without cost.

In the ancient Middle East, water was precious beyond measure. The dry, desert lands were often gripped by drought. There were no swimming pools, no ice cubes in drinks, no regular baths. Most homes had no running water, no toilets, no bathtubs. There were no bottled waters at stores, no drinking fountains at schools. Water was survival itself. So when Isaiah offers not just water, but the finest liquids—wine and milk—and he offers them for free, this is an extraordinary bargain.

### The Cost of Bad Bargains

But Isaiah immediately pivots to a hard truth:

> "Why spend money on what is not bread, and your labor on what does not satisfy?" (Isaiah 55:2)

Now that we know there is free food available, spending money on what doesn't satisfy becomes a double tragedy. You've paid for something broken, something counterfeit. We've all felt this sting—the expensive hotel room where the minibar charges $8 for a can of soda you could buy outside for $2, the fancy restaurant with tiny portions and double the price, the full-price purchase that arrives defective.

The heartbreak isn't just the money wasted. It's the labor—your effort, your time, your energy—spent on something that leaves you empty.

### The Soul's Hunger

This is when we realize Isaiah isn't really talking about food at all:

> "Listen, listen to me, and eat what is good, and your soul will delight in the richest of fare. Give ear and come to me; hear me, that your soul may live." (Isaiah 55:2b-3)

Isaiah speaks of the soul—that part of you capable of thoughtfulness and reflection, the very seat of your life. Just as the body needs food, the soul needs nourishment. And this nourishment cannot come from physical comforts alone.

Maslow's hierarchy of needs captures this progression: food, safety, love, esteem, and self-actualization. Yet even beyond self-actualization, we sense there's something more. We know intuitively that we want something beyond the daily grind.

And we know this empirically as well. Australia has never been wealthier, healthier, or more educated. Yet happiness eludes us. Every indicator has climbed—except one. The wellbeing of the soul.

- People reporting very unhappiness have risen 20% since the 1950s
- Depression rates are ten times what they were, so common we've invented a word for it—emo
- Suicide rates are climbing
- Family breakdown is rising

We're among the top five wealthiest nations in the world, with only Switzerland and Scandinavia ahead of us. Yet we are unhappy. Not happy Jan. Something is profoundly wrong.

## 2. A Promise

It is into this spiritual hunger that God brings a promise:

> "I will make an everlasting covenant with you, my faithful love promised to David." (Isaiah 55:3b)

God is referencing an ancient promise made to King David, the shepherd boy whom God chose to rule Israel. David reigned over a prosperous kingdom—a land flowing with milk and honey, overflowing with wine, bread, cheese, and abundance. A land of peace and security.

But there's a problem: David is long dead. In fact, all the kings of Israel are dead, exiled by the Babylonians. So this promise of a new king—this "everlasting covenant"—must point to someone else. Someone greater than David himself.

> "See, I have made him a witness to the peoples, a leader and commander of the peoples." (Isaiah 55:4)

> "Surely you will summon nations you know not, and nations that do not know you will hasten to you, because of the LORD your God, the Holy One of Israel, for he has endowed you with splendor." (Isaiah 55:5)

This new king will not merely rule over Israel. His influence will extend to nations he doesn't know, peoples who don't know him. His authority will be automatic, universal, divinely given. He will be greater than David ever was.

### The Promise Fulfilled

Fast forward 500 years. Around 30 AD, history records the appearance of Jesus of Nazareth. Consider what happens at the feeding of the five thousand:

> "By this time it was late in the day, so his disciples came to him. 'This is a remote place,' they said, 'and it's already very late. Send the people away so they can go to the surrounding countryside and villages and buy themselves something to eat.' But he answered, 'You give them something to eat.' They said to him, 'That would take eight months of a man's wages! Are we to go and spend that much on bread and give it to them to eat?' 'How many loaves do you have?' he asked. 'Go and see.' When they found out, they said, 'Five—and two fish.'" (Mark 6:35-38)

Eight months of wages. Thirty-five to forty thousand dollars in today's money. An impossible sum. But then:

> "Then Jesus directed them to have all the people sit down in groups on the green grass. So they sat down in groups of hundreds and fifties. Taking the five loaves and the two fish and looking up to heaven, he gave thanks and broke the loaves. Then he gave them to his disciples to set before the people. He also divided the two fish among them all. They all ate and were satisfied, and the disciples picked up twelve basketfuls of broken pieces of bread and fish. The number of the men who had eaten was five thousand." (Mark 6:39-44)

Notice the details: he organizes them like a king, in groups of hundreds and fifties. He gives thanks to God. He multiplies the loaves. They all—all five thousand—ate and were satisfied. And there were leftovers—twelve baskets full.

This is no accident. This is Jesus demonstrating that he is the one Isaiah promised. He is the one who can feed your soul.

He can fill your desire for truth. He can answer your longing for certainty. He can satisfy your hunger for meaning. He can clarify your calling and purpose. He can ignite your curiosity about what lies ahead.

But most importantly, he addresses our deepest need: our hunger for love and forgiveness.

Deep within us—when all the distractions fall away, when we're alone with our thoughts—we feel it: the gut-level knowledge that there is a supreme God, and that this God is holy, and that we have wronged him. We have done wrong things to him, to each other, and to ourselves. And we know we will one day answer for it.

If that's you, you don't have to remain hungry anymore.

## 3. Seek God While You Can

> "Seek the LORD while he may be found; call on him while he is near." (Isaiah 55:6)

Notice the urgency: "while he may be found." Like all bargains, this is a limited offer. It is near. The opportunity is present.

> "Let the wicked forsake his way and the evil man his thoughts. Let him turn to the LORD, and he will have mercy on him, and to our God, for he will freely pardon." (Isaiah 55:7)

Repent. Turn around. Forsake the way that doesn't satisfy. And when you do, what do you find? Mercy. Free pardon. Complete forgiveness.

## The True Cost of This Bargain

There's something crucial to understand: this forgiveness is free to you, but it was not free to Jesus.

Jesus fed five thousand with scraps of bread and fish. But eventually, this man would die. Within a couple of years, he would willingly lay down his life as a sacrifice for us. He would go to the cross knowing exactly what he was doing, paying a debt that should have been ours to pay.

That's why it's free. Someone paid.

## Can You Afford NOT to Follow Jesus?

We worry about the cost of Christianity. We think: Can I really afford to follow Jesus? To live a certain way? To give up certain habits? To commit to a belief system? To surrender my autonomy to God?

But let's think about this differently.

**First, it's not really a cost—it's free.** Yes, Christianity involves an opportunity cost: choosing it means not choosing other things. But those other things don't satisfy anyway. Look at the evidence. Money doesn't satisfy—the wealthiest among us often aren't the happiest. University degrees don't satisfy; half of students change courses despite the effort to get in. Holidays don't satisfy; we travel to Japan, Europe, Bali, always chasing the next experience. High test scores don't satisfy; students with perfect scores still sue school boards. Possessions don't satisfy; what matters today gets recycled tomorrow.

We are deceived by advertising agencies, by Hollywood, by corporations and politicians who see us only as consumers, tools to grow their profits and expand the economy. What a tragedy it would be to spend your entire life chasing bread at twice the price, only to discover at the end that it was never really bread at all. All that effort, all that labor, yielding nothing.

So the cost of Christianity—living life as God intended, forsaking evil—is that you won't be ripped off. That's not a cost. That's a rescue.

**Second, even if you think it is a cost, it's not a big one.** We put our lives into strangers' hands all the time for far less reason than a whim. We eat KFC despite meningitis concerns. We hand ourselves to a fifteen-year-old with a pimply face simply because it's "probably ok." We take countless small leaps of faith every day.

Friends, if you can look at your Christian friends and say, "They seem ok. Jesus is probably right"—that's enough. Faith of a mustard seed. Because the benefits far outweigh the costs.

**Third, consider the evidence.** I became a Christian in year 11, not from a churched background but from an essentially atheistic home. At the time, I thought: what if there's a slim possibility this could all be true?

But we live like this constantly. We don't verify our future spouses. We don't check shopping centers for terrorists. We don't ask to see our bus driver's license. We gather a little information, then take a leap. And like most friendships, each leap is confirmed, leading to another leap. It's a relationship of trust.

Yet for Christianity, we have far more than that. We have the best evidence available from the ancient world: multiple attestation, historical origin, internal coherence. Better than all the world religions. Better than atheism. Your Christian friends can point you to this.

And I've been a Christian for twenty years. The proofs get better. To those of you wavering because the world seems so convincing—yes, the world's arguments are the same as always. But our rebuttal improves. Every year we find new manuscripts confirming the Bible. Science—genuine science with multiple different proofs—supports God. Quantum physics reveals a universe far more mysterious than the simplistic materialism taught in high school. Real philosophy of science demands multiple different lines of evidence, which is exactly what Christianity offers: historical documents, philosophical coherence, ethical integrity.

## The Real Question

The passage ends where it began:

> "Come, all you who are thirsty, come to the waters; and you who have no money, come, buy and eat! Come, buy wine and milk without money and without cost. Why spend money on what is not bread, and your labor on what does not satisfy?"

There are leftovers. Plenty of food. And it's for you.

So the real question isn't: "Can I afford to follow Jesus?"

The real question is: **How can you afford NOT to follow Jesus?**

This is the best bargain of your life. Literally.
