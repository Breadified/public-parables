# Numbers 12:1-16

**Title:** Seduced by Arrogance
**Date:** 9 June 2013
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Christianity as Arrogance?

The world often accuses Christians of arrogance. They say we're arrogant when we claim assurance of salvation—how dare we say God will accept us? They say we're arrogant when we proclaim that Jesus is the only way to God. After all, haven't we checked every belief system in the world? How can we be so certain?

Consider Guy Sebastian, the Australian Idol winner who once boldly proclaimed his Christian faith. Ten years ago, he was the church's poster boy, talking about Jesus and claiming his success came from God. But recently, his position shifted dramatically. In his song "Get Along," he sings about getting along with "God, soul, Mary, Mohammed"—suggesting all paths lead to the same place.

In an interview, he explained: "My views are more based on life and discovery and research than just what I'm told. I don't think anyone has the right to say what they believe is more important or more significant." In other words, he now thinks Christians who claim Jesus is the only way are arrogant.

But is this really arrogance? Or is something else going on? Let's examine what Scripture says.

---

## 1. Seduced by Arrogance (Numbers 12:1-3)

> Miriam and Aaron began to talk against Moses because of his Cushite wife, for he had married a Cushite. "Has the LORD spoken only through Moses?" they asked. "Hasn't he also spoken through us?"

Miriam and Aaron, Moses' older siblings, grumbled about Moses' marriage to a Cushite woman (likely from modern-day Ethiopia). But their complaint reveals deeper discontent: Why does God give Moses preferential treatment? They too have heard from God. Miriam is even described as a prophetess in Exodus—one who receives and communicates God's message.

They thought their complaints were private, whispered in the desert away from others. But the Lord heard them.

This is what gossip truly is: saying things about someone behind their back that you wouldn't say to their face. It's character assassination where the victim cannot defend himself. We think no one knows what we say in secret, but God hears everything.

### Humility as the Antidote to Arrogance

What made Miriam and Aaron's behavior wrong? Look at God's assessment of Moses:

> Now Moses was a very humble man, more humble than anyone else on the face of the earth. (Numbers 12:3)

The Hebrew word for humble here also means "weak" or "miserable"—describing those who recognize their complete dependence on God. This is the meekness Jesus spoke of:

> Blessed are the meek, for they will inherit the earth. (Matthew 5:5)

Humility plays a central role in what happens next.

### The Spirit Brings Humility

Earlier in Numbers 11, God dealt with the people's craving for food by appointing seventy elders to share Moses' burden. When God's Spirit came upon them, they prophesied. Even two elders who stayed in camp—Eldad and Medad—received the Spirit and prophesied. Joshua, Moses' young aide, objected: "Moses, my lord, stop them!"

But Moses replied with perfect humility:

> "Are you jealous for my sake? I wish that all the LORD's people were prophets and that the LORD would put his Spirit on them!" (Numbers 11:29)

The people's sin was envy and jealousy—they craved what others had. What's the remedy? Humility.

James later echoes this connection:

> What causes fights and quarrels among you? Don't they come from your desires that battle within you? You want something but don't get it. You kill and covet, but you cannot have what you want. (James 4:1-2)

This mirrors Numbers 11 perfectly: desires lead to conflict. James continues:

> But he gives us more grace. That is why Scripture says: "God opposes the proud but gives grace to the humble." (James 4:6)

God's Spirit brings the character of humility. The Spirit of God is a spirit of humility. Therefore James concludes:

> Humble yourselves before the Lord, and he will lift you up. (James 4:10)

Humility is the antidote to envy, to entitlement, to fights and quarrels, to gossip and slander. Instead of "I deserve this," humility says, "This is grace." Instead of "I earned this," it says, "This is a gift." Instead of "I'm better than you," it says, "But for God's grace, there go I."

As Paul wrote:

> For by the grace given me I say to every one of you: Do not think of yourself more highly than you ought, but rather think of yourself with sober judgment, in accordance with the measure of faith God has given you. (Romans 12:3)

When we think soberly about ourselves, we realize: *I didn't actually deserve this. This is good fortune showered from God.*

Imagine if Miriam had said instead: "Moses has been picked by God. I know he's not perfect—I don't approve of his wife, he's sometimes a coward, he can't speak well—but God picked him for a reason. We've done well under his leadership. A year ago we were slaves in Egypt; now we're a week away from the Promised Land. Yes, we're hungry and the food is boring, but I couldn't have done better. Aaron, let's go help our little brother."

That would be humility.

## 2. God Chooses to Relate Through His Chosen Mediator (Numbers 12:4-8)

Miriam and Aaron's arrogance brought swift judgment. God summoned all three of them to the Tent of Meeting—not in private, but at the entrance where the entire community could see.

> Then the LORD came down in a pillar of cloud; he stood at the entrance to the Tent and summoned Aaron and Miriam. When both of them stepped forward, he said, "Listen to my words: When a prophet of the LORD is among you, I reveal myself to him in visions, I speak to him in dreams. But this is not true of my servant Moses; he is faithful in all my house. With him I speak face to face, clearly and not in riddles; he sees the form of the LORD. Why then were you not afraid to speak against my servant Moses?" (Numbers 12:6-8)

God makes a crucial distinction. Yes, He speaks to prophets through visions and dreams. But Moses is different. Moses is God's faithful servant, and with Moses, God speaks face to face—mouth to mouth, with complete clarity and intimacy, not in riddles.

> The LORD would speak to Moses face to face, as a man speaks with his friend. (Exodus 33:11)

Moses enjoyed a friendship with God that no other prophet experienced:

> Since then, no prophet has risen in Israel like Moses, whom the LORD knew face to face. (Deuteronomy 34:10)

Moses was God's friend. To oppose Moses was to oppose God's chosen mediator.

### Jesus is the Son

The New Testament applies this truth to Jesus:

> Jesus has been found worthy of greater honor than Moses, just as the builder of a house has greater honor than the house itself. For every house is built by someone, but God is the builder of everything. Moses was faithful as a servant in all God's house, testifying to what would be said in the future. But Christ is faithful as a son over God's house. (Hebrews 3:3-6)

If Moses was God's faithful servant, Jesus is God's faithful son. Jesus is greater than Moses.

### Jesus is the Humble One

But Jesus is greater not in pride, but in humility:

> Your attitude should be the same as that of Christ Jesus: And being found in appearance as a man, he humbled himself and became obedient to death—even death on a cross! Therefore God exalted him to the highest place and gave him the name that is above every name. (Philippians 2:5, 8-10)

Was Moses the most humble man on earth? Yes. But Jesus was more humble still. He lowered Himself to wash His disciples' feet, to be persecuted, whipped, spat upon, ridiculed, and bullied. He lowered Himself to die.

And now, exalted to the highest place, He is King.

### Jesus is from the Father

> No one has ever seen God, but God the One and Only, who is at the Father's side, has made him known. (John 1:18)

Jesus knows the Father intimately. He comes from the Father's side with complete knowledge of who God is.

## 3. God is Angry When People Won't Accept His Mediator (Numbers 12:9-12)

> The anger of the LORD burned against them, and he left them. When the cloud lifted from above the Tent, there stood Miriam—leprous, like snow. Aaron turned toward her and saw that she had leprosy; and he said to Moses, "Please, my lord, do not hold against us the sin we have so foolishly committed. Do not let her be like a stillborn infant coming from its mother's womb with its flesh half eaten away." (Numbers 12:9-12)

God judges. Miriam is struck with a disease that makes her appear white as snow—a living death. The description is horrific: like a stillborn infant with flesh half-eaten away. God's judgment is severe.

Why such a harsh punishment for gossip and slander? Because God hates it when people refuse to accept His mediator. God chose Moses. Moses is His friend, His faithful servant, who speaks with Him face to face. Miriam and Aaron thought they could relate to God on their own terms, bypassing God's chosen mediator.

But God won't allow it.

### God Will Judge

This principle applies today:

> For there is one God and one mediator between God and men, the man Christ Jesus, who gave himself as a ransom for all men. (1 Timothy 2:5)

Jesus answered, "I am the way and the truth and the life. No one comes to the Father except through me." (John 14:6)

Notice: this isn't a boast from some arrogant Christian. These are the words of Jesus Himself.

We constantly try to reach God on our own terms. Some seek God through spirituality. Some through Buddha. Some through Muhammad. Some through Mary. Some reject God altogether and seek meaning in humanism.

The world promotes pluralism—the idea that all paths lead to the same God. Like the parable of the blind men and the elephant: each man touches a different part and thinks he's found the whole truth. Surely all religions are just different perspectives on the same reality?

But there's a fatal flaw in this logic: *How do you know there's an elephant at all?* To know you're only touching one part, you must already know the whole exists. You need someone who has actually seen the elephant to tell you.

The problem with pluralism isn't humility. It's ignorance.

By contrast, there is *one* mediator between God and man: Jesus Christ.

Here's a better version of the story: Three blind men seek to discover what an elephant is. One touches a camel and concludes that's an elephant—hairy, with humps, foul breath, thin legs. Another touches an ostrich and thinks that's an elephant—feathered, two legs, dangerous beak. But the third consults the zoo signs and speaks with the zookeepers. He encounters the real elephant and knows what it's actually like.

When the three meet, the first two have strange ideas about elephants, but neither has encountered the real thing. The third man describes the true elephant. One friend believes him and returns to see for himself. The others dismiss him as crazy.

In humanity's search for God, many fumble around blindly, seeking what they cannot grasp alone. Some look in entirely the wrong places. Different interpretations of God aren't necessarily parts of one truth—they're descriptions of entirely different things. Not an elephant, but a camel or an ostrich.

Now, back to Guy Sebastian's statement: "My views are more based on life and discovery and research than just what I'm told."

The problem? *My views.* God isn't interested in Guy Sebastian's views. God is interested in His Son's views.

Jesus isn't arrogant. He's the most humble man who ever lived—lowering Himself to die for us, to wash our feet, to endure ridicule and torture. It isn't Jesus who is arrogant. And Christians who remain faithful to Jesus aren't arrogant either.

It's Guy Sebastian who is arrogant—thinking his views somehow supersede Jesus' views. It's the world's religions that are arrogant—refusing to examine the evidence Jesus left behind.

### A Warning About Drifting

When you became a Christian, you had solid reasons for it. Not just feelings (though you may have had them), not just friends (though you may have had them), but because you were convinced by the evidence: this man Jesus, the humblest man who ever lived, died for many and rose again, defeating sin and death. You believed He loved you and that you owed Him your life.

What's changed since then? Have you discovered new evidence against Him? Did someone find Jesus' bones? Did you examine the claims about the Bible being false?

Nothing has changed. Except you've met more people who don't like Jesus. And you like them. So maybe you don't have to like Jesus so much either.

You've been seduced.

## 4. Humble Christians (Numbers 12:13-16)

Yet here's the beautiful part: Miriam's victim shows grace.

> So Moses cried out to the LORD, "O God, please heal her!" (Numbers 12:13)

God shows grace:

> The LORD replied to Moses, "If her father had spit in her face, would she not have been in disgrace for seven days? Confine her outside the camp for seven days; after that she can be brought back." (Numbers 12:14)

Miriam must undergo the ritual purification, confined outside the camp for seven days. But it's a path to restoration.

The people show grace too:

> So Miriam was confined outside the camp for seven days, and the people did not move on till she was brought back. After that, the people left Hazeroth and encamped in the Desert of Paran. (Numbers 12:15-16)

The entire nation waited for her. Despite her arrogance, despite her slander, despite her judgment—they waited with compassion.

### A Word to Those Drifting

If you find yourself drifting from God, it's not too late. It's not too late to return with conviction to the faith of your youth. Speak to a leader. Speak to God. Ask for forgiveness.

Remember what Guy Sebastian once said, years ago when his faith was strong:

> I can search for the rest of my life, but I know I've found, in Christ, everything that matters to me. And he is the reason why I do everything I do. He has saved me from a time when I called out his name, and he came and he gave me purpose and direction. And because he lives, and because I know we celebrate today that he rose from the dead—out of that grave he didn't only become a man, but became a man that took all my sins, took all my worries. I know I can face any day, no matter what comes, because he lives.

This is the good news. May we return to it. May none of us be seduced by arrogance or pluralism. May we humbly submit to Jesus, God's chosen mediator, and find in Him everything that truly matters.
