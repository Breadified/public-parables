# Romans 11:1-36

**Title:** The Hope of the Remnant
**Date:** 2 June 2024 (ARISE); Originally 17 February 2013 (SMAC)
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## God Has Not Rejected His People (11:1-11a)

Paul opens with a startling question that cuts to the heart of Israel's apparent rejection: "Did God reject his people?" His answer is absolute: "By no means!"

This seems impossible given what precedes it. The Jewish people have hardened themselves against the gospel. Their religious leaders rejected Jesus. They persecuted the early church. Yet Paul insists this is not the final word.

### Paul Himself as Proof

> I am an Israelite myself, a descendant of Abraham, from the tribe of Benjamin. (11:1)

Paul's own conversion is living proof that God has not abandoned Israel. Consider his background: he was a Pharisee of the highest order, zealously persecuting Christians, actively working against the gospel. He was an enemy of God incarnate. If such a man could be transformed by grace, why should we despair for any other Jewish person, no matter how stubborn or resistant?

### God's Faithfulness Through the Remnant

Paul points us to Elijah's story—a moment when Israel appeared completely lost. Under King Ahab and Queen Jezebel, prophets of God were systematically killed and God's altars destroyed. The national religion had turned wholly to Baal worship. Even Elijah believed he was alone, the only faithful one left.

> God did not reject his people, whom he foreknew... "I have reserved for myself seven thousand who have not bowed the knee to Baal." (11:2, 4)

In the darkest hour, God's answer to Elijah's despair was this: you are not alone. There are seven thousand I have kept for myself. 

Paul applies this ancient promise to his own time: just as in Elijah's era God preserved a remnant, so in Paul's day there remains a chosen remnant of Israel who have accepted Jesus. The principle is unchanging—God never completely abandons His people.

> So too, at the present time there is a remnant chosen by grace. (11:5)

### Election by Grace Alone

This remnant exists not because of works but purely through grace:

> If by grace, then it cannot be based on works; if it were, grace would no longer be grace. (11:6)

This is the heart of election. God's choice does not depend on whether people are good or evil, deserving or undeserving. Grace means God's selection is entirely His own merciful choice. He may choose the morally upright or the morally bankrupt. He may choose Jews or Gentiles. The principle remains: it is God's sovereign mercy alone.

Therefore, the Jewish people cannot be wholesale written off. Perhaps God will choose to save some of them. Perhaps He already has. We cannot determine election based on performance or lack thereof. We can only trust in God's inscrutable grace.

There is hope because there is grace, and where grace operates, no one is beyond redemption.

### Application: Hope for the Hardhearted

This teaches us something crucial: as long as someone lives, the end is not yet written. Your hardened friend, the diehard atheist, the stubborn family member—God has not given up on them. We must not either.

We work prayerfully for their salvation. We make gentle appeals, seeking to arrest their attention and plant seeds of reflection. We act as though we truly believe in a God who can reach anyone. This is what it means to be both theologically convinced of God's sovereignty (Calvinistic in our theology) and practically committed to evangelism (Arminian in our effort).

## God at Work in Gentiles (11:11b-24)

Yet while God preserves a Jewish remnant, He is simultaneously at work in an unexpected direction—among the Gentiles.

> Because of their transgression, salvation has come to the Gentiles to make Israel envious. But if their transgression means riches for the world, and their loss means riches for the Gentiles, how much greater riches will their full inclusion bring! (11:11-12)

What appears to be tragedy—Jewish rejection of Christ—becomes the vehicle through which salvation reaches the pagan Gentile world. When synagogue after synagogue rejected Paul's message, he turned to the Gentiles. When Jerusalem closed its doors, he opened them to Rome.

God does not become stuck. He is sovereignly moving His purposes forward, using even human resistance to accomplish His will. The hardened Jewish heart becomes the instrument through which God's mercy flows to the nations.

### The Olive Tree Metaphor

Paul uses the image of an olive tree to explain this reversal:

> If some of the branches have been broken off, and you, though a wild olive shoot, have been grafted in among the others and now share in the nourishing sap from the olive root... (11:17)

The natural branches—ethnic Israel—have been broken off due to unbelief. The wild Gentile branches have been grafted unnaturally into the cultivated tree, receiving nourishment from the ancient root. This is both humbling and astonishing: we wild branches depend entirely on a root we did not create.

> Do not consider yourself to be superior to those other branches... You do not support the root, but the root supports you. (11:18)

The grafted-in Gentiles must maintain profound humility. We stand by faith alone, and faith can be lost:

> If God did not spare the natural branches, he will not spare you either. (11:21)

We have been chosen by grace, not merit. We must never become arrogant about our status. We are supported by a root antedating us—the ancient covenant with Abraham—and we remain only as long as we remain in faith.

### God's Dual Work: Sternness and Kindness

> Consider therefore the kindness and sternness of God: sternness to those who fell, but kindness to you, provided that you continue in his kindness. Otherwise, you also will be cut off. (11:22)

God's sternness toward the fallen Jewish branches and His kindness toward grafted-in Gentiles both serve the same purpose: to teach humility. We Gentiles should never forget that we were not the original plan. We are here by God's gracious recalculation, using what appeared to be failure as the mechanism of our salvation.

God uses everything for His purposes—even negative things, even punishment, even disaster. Like a master chef who wastes nothing, creating an exquisite dish from every ingredient, God transforms suffering and rejection into instruments of redemption.

## God at Work in the Jews (11:23-27)

But here is the stunning conclusion: God is also still working toward the salvation of the very Jews He has set aside.

> But if their transgression means riches for the world, and their loss means riches for the Gentiles, how much greater riches will their full inclusion bring! (11:12)

Paul looks forward with hope to Jewish restoration. Their hardening is not permanent; it is functional—serving God's timeline:

> Israel has experienced a hardening in part until the full number of the Gentiles has come in, and in this way all Israel will be saved. (11:25-26)

The phrase "all Israel will be saved" has been interpreted various ways. But most likely it means: the totality of God's elect from ethnic Israel throughout history will be brought into salvation. Not necessarily every individual Jew, but the full complement of elect Israelites will be saved through the mechanism of Gentile envy and restored faith.

### The Plan's Logic

Just as Jewish rejection led to Gentile salvation, so Gentile salvation may yet lead to Jewish restoration. Paul addresses the Gentiles directly:

> Inasmuch as I am the apostle to the Gentiles, I take pride in my ministry in the hope that I may somehow arouse my own people to envy and save some of them. (11:13-14)

The strategy is this: Gentiles living in radical faith should provoke the Jewish people to wonder. "What do these pagans possess that we have rejected?" This envy, this longing, becomes the catalyst for Jewish return.

If they do not persist in unbelief, they will be grafted back in—more easily than the wild Gentile branches were grafted in originally, because they are branches of the original tree.

### The Root and Firstfruits

> If the part of the dough offered as firstfruits is holy, then the whole batch is holy; if the root is holy, so are the branches. (11:16)

Christ is both the root and the firstfruits. His holiness sanctifies all who are connected to Him—all the patriarchs, all the apostles, all believers both Jewish and Gentile. As the firstfruits of resurrection, He guarantees the resurrection of all who belong to Him.

### The Reversal of Romans 1:16

There is a beautiful reversal here. Romans 1:16 proclaimed: "salvation first for the Jew, then for the Gentile." But the Jews rejected it. So now the pattern reverses: the Gentiles receive salvation first, that they might provoke the Jews to receive it second. The gospel reaches the nations, and through the nations' faith, it circles back to Israel.

### The Mystery Resolved in Mercy

> Just as you who were at one time disobedient to God have now received mercy as a result of their disobedience, so they too have now become disobedient in order that they too may now receive mercy as a result of God's mercy to you. (11:30-31)

Here is the profound symmetry: both Gentiles and Jews arrive at salvation through disobedience and mercy. Neither can boast. Both are bound to receive salvation only through the other's fallen state and God's intervening grace.

> For God has bound everyone over to disobedience so that he may have mercy on them all. (11:32)

## The Mystery of God's Wisdom (11:33-36)

Faced with the complexity and depth of this divine plan—how God uses rejection to accomplish redemption, how He works through human failure to establish His purposes—Paul breaks into doxology:

> Oh, the depth of the riches of the wisdom and knowledge of God! How unsearchable his judgments, and his paths beyond tracing out! (11:33)

We cannot fully comprehend God's ways. His judgments are inscrutable. His paths cannot be traced by human reason. Yet we can trust them, because they flow from infinite wisdom.

> Who has known the mind of the Lord? Or who has been his counselor? Who has ever given to God, that God should repay him? For from him and through him and to him are all things. To him be the glory forever! Amen. (11:34-36)

Paul closes not with answers but with awe. The appropriate human response to God's incomprehensible sovereignty is humble worship and the acknowledgment that all things—all suffering, all rejection, all restoration—flow from Him, through Him, and return to Him.

---

## Application: Living Between the Already and Not Yet

We live in the tension Paul describes. We see both God's sternness (historical Jewish rejection of Christ) and His kindness (salvation offered to both Jews and Gentiles). We must neither despair about any person's hardness nor become arrogant about our own position.

The Gentiles are called to live a life of faith so remarkable that it provokes Jewish longing. This is a high calling. Our witness matters not just for individual conversions but for God's cosmic plan of redemption.

We are Theologically Calvinist (believing in God's sovereign election) and Pragmatically Arminian (working tirelessly as though human choice mattered). Both are true. God is completely sovereign, and yet our faithful witness, our intercession, our outreach may become the very means through which He accomplishes His purposes.

There is a remnant always. There is always hope. God has not finished with His people—Jewish or Gentile. Our task is to live faithfully, to pray earnestly, to witness boldly, and to trust in a God whose wisdom is too deep to measure but whose mercy never fails.
