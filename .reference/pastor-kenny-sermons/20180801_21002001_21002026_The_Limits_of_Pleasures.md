# Ecclesiastes 2:1-26

**Title:** The Limits of Pleasures  
**Date:** 21 August 2018  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Introduction

Some experiments are impossible. We cannot replay the past—there is only one life to live. The road not taken cannot be tested. Some experiments are unethical, confined by moral boundaries we rightly respect. And some experiments are simply too hard to conduct. Consider the 10,000-hour rule claiming that practice alone, not talent, produces expertise. Testing this would require thousands of volunteers, millions of dollars, and people willing to spend 10,000 hours learning skills assigned to them by researchers. It's untestable.

Today we examine an experiment that actually happened—recorded nearly 3,000 years ago in the Book of Ecclesiastes. King Solomon, the wisest and richest man of his era, conducted an experiment to answer a question we all wonder: Can true satisfaction be found in pleasure?

---

## Testing the Limits of Pleasure

> I said in my heart, "Come now, I will test you with pleasure; enjoy yourself." But behold, this also was vanity. I said of laughter, "It is mad," and of pleasure, "What use is it?" (Ecclesiastes 2:1-2)

Solomon tells us his conclusion before describing his experiment. He stands apart from his pleasures, observing himself with detachment—a conscious experimenter examining his own heart.

### Wine and Substances

> I searched with my heart how to cheer my body with wine—my heart still guiding me with wisdom—and how to lay hold on folly, till I might see what was good for the children of man to do under heaven during the few days of their life. (Ecclesiastes 2:3)

Solomon tested the pleasure of wine, fine wine, and mind-altering substances. Whether he pursued this with restraint or abandon, the conclusion remained the same: these pleasures were temporary and ultimately unsatisfying.

### Projects and Achievements

> I made great works. I built houses and planted vineyards for myself. I made myself gardens and parks, and planted in them all kinds of fruit trees. I made myself pools from which to water the forest of growing trees. (Ecclesiastes 2:4-6)

Solomon created grand projects—houses, vineyards, gardens, and irrigation systems. These were tangible accomplishments; he could see the results of his labor. Unlike the fleeting pleasures of wine, achievements gave him something lasting to point to.

Today we pursue similar projects. We build toward degrees, credentials, and accolades. We pursue hobbies that produce visible results—bicycles, photographs, craft projects. We think these accomplishments will bring us happiness and meaning. Yet they too prove empty.

Consider an aquarium project—a 4-foot tank with a complete ecosystem. There's a genuine sense of achievement in building something functional and beautiful. But that satisfaction fades.

### Power and Control

> I bought male and female slaves, and had slaves who were born in my house. I had also great possessions of herds and flocks, more than any who had been before me in Jerusalem. (Ecclesiastes 2:7-7b)

Solomon commanded 153,600 servants during the building of the temple. He possessed power over people and resources on an unimaginable scale. The pleasure of power is real—the ability to command, to control, to make others do our will.

As the philosopher Søren Kierkegaard observed: "If I had a servant in my employ, who when I asked him for a cup of cold water, brought instead the world's costliest wines blended in a chalice, I would dismiss him, for true pleasure consists not in getting my wine but in getting my way."

### Possessions

Solomon owned vast herds and flocks—the transportation and resources of his era. He possessed more than anyone in Jerusalem.

The Sultan of Brunei owns 7,000 cars. His collection includes 574 Mercedes-Benzes, 452 Ferraris, 382 Bentleys, 209 BMWs, and 179 Jaguars. If he drove a different car every day, it would take 19 years to cycle through his entire collection. Yet possessions, no matter how numerous, cannot provide lasting satisfaction.

### Wealth and Entertainment

> I also gathered for myself silver and gold and the treasure of kings and provinces. I got singers, both men and women. (Ecclesiastes 2:8)

Solomon accumulated wealth—silver, gold, and the treasures of kingdoms. He employed musicians to entertain him continually.

### Sexual Pleasure

> ...and many concubines, the delight of the sons of man. (Ecclesiastes 2:8b)

> Now King Solomon loved many foreign women, along with the daughter of Pharaoh: Moabite, Ammonite, Edomite, Sidonian, and Hittite women... He had 700 wives, who were princesses, and 300 concubines. And his wives turned away his heart. (1 Kings 11:1-3)

Solomon pursued sexual pleasure on an epic scale. His wives were diplomatic alliances and strategic relationships, but his concubines—300 of them—existed solely for his enjoyment. With 300 concubines, he could spend time with a different woman every day of the year, excluding the Sabbath and holidays.

Surely this would bring satisfaction. Yet it did not.

## The Verdict: All is Vanity

> So I became great and surpassed all who were before me in Jerusalem. Also my wisdom remained with me. And whatever my eyes desired I did not keep from them. I kept my heart from no pleasure. (Ecclesiastes 2:9-10)

Solomon summarizes his position: He was greater than Hugh Hefner, Warren Buffett, Jeff Bezos, and Donald Trump combined. He denied himself nothing. Every pleasure was available to him.

> Then I considered all that my hands had done and the toil I had expended in doing it, and behold, all was vanity and a striving after wind, and there was nothing to be gained under the sun. (Ecclesiastes 2:11)

After everything—wine and women and Ferraris—Solomon wakes to his own wisdom and declares: vanity. Ephemeral. Fleeting. All of it is like chasing the wind.

The modern entertainer Russell Brand reflected on this same realization:

> When I grew up in a hum-drum town, like a Morrissey lyric, I want to be rich and famous. But I have been inside now, I have been through the looking glass, and I feel empty inside.

**The Pattern**: Pleasure leads to pain, which leads to death. As Scripture says:

> Then desire when it has conceived gives birth to sin, and sin when it is fully grown brings forth death. (James 1:15)

---

## The Deeper Truth: We Cannot Keep Experiences

We know Solomon's conclusion is true. Look at the famous and wealthy. Brad Pitt, Jennifer Aniston, Angelina Jolie—the most beautiful and successful people end in misery. They always need more.

We try to preserve experiences through photos we never look at again. We scramble to capture selfies at Wedding Cake Rock, desperately trying to keep a fleeting moment. But the experience vanishes the moment we leave. You cannot keep experiences. They are like the wind—momentary, intangible, impossible to hold.

This is why we pursue hedonism—holidays, gourmet food, exotic experiences, even illicit substances. We work hard to earn money to buy things and experiences. We think: *Why am I at university? To get a good job. Why? To earn money. Why? So I can buy things. Why? So I can be happy.*

Yet the happiness we chase never comes. We need more. Always more.

---

## The Better Way: Gratitude and Contentment

Yet Solomon also tells us something else:

> There is nothing better for a person than that he should eat and drink and find enjoyment in his toil. This also, I saw, is from the hand of God, for apart from him who can eat or who can have enjoyment? For to the one who pleases him God has given wisdom and knowledge and joy, but to the sinner he has given the business of gathering and collecting, only to give to one who pleases God. (Ecclesiastes 2:24-26)

The pleasure itself is not the problem. The issue is building your life on something that cannot sustain it. You can find genuine enjoyment in simple things—eating, drinking, working with your hands. But only when received as a gift from God, with gratitude.

God's wisdom offers a different path:

> Godliness with contentment is great gain. For we brought nothing into the world, and we can take nothing out of it. But if we have food and clothing, we will be content with that. Those who want to get rich fall into temptation and a trap and into many foolish and harmful desires that plunge people into ruin and destruction. (1 Timothy 6:6-9)

Jesus taught the same truth:

> Watch out! Be on your guard against all kinds of greed; life does not consist in an abundance of possessions... The ground of a certain rich man yielded an abundant harvest. He thought to himself, 'What shall I do? I have no place to store my crops.' Then he said, 'This is what I'll do. I will tear down my barns and build bigger ones, and there I will store my surplus grain. And I'll say to myself, "You have plenty of grain laid up for many years. Take life easy; eat, drink and be merry."' But God said to him, 'You fool! This very night your life will be demanded from you. Then who will get what you have prepared for yourself? This is how it will be with whoever stores up things for themselves but is not rich toward God.' (Luke 12:15-21)

> For everything created by God is good, and nothing is to be rejected if it is received with thanksgiving, for it is made holy by the word of God and prayer. (1 Timothy 4:4-5)

---

## Conclusion

Your life is an experiment that cannot be repeated. You have one shot, one life to live. The question is not whether pleasure is available—Solomon proved it is abundantly available. The question is what you will build your life upon.

Pleasures are momentary. They cannot be kept. They are like the wind—here one moment, gone the next. Yet we pursue them to death, never satisfied, always needing more.

But when you find your satisfaction in God rather than in possessions, achievements, power, or pleasure, you discover true contentment. Not the absence of enjoyment, but joy that doesn't depend on circumstances. Food tastes better when received with gratitude. Work brings fulfillment when it's not desperate striving but a gift to enjoy. Relationships matter when they're rooted in something deeper than appetite.

The experiment Solomon conducted echoes across the centuries to us: Can true satisfaction be found in pleasure alone? No. But can it be found in God? Yes. That is the wisdom that remains.
