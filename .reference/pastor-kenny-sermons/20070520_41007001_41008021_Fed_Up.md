# Mark 7:1–8:21

**Title:** Fed Up  
**Date:** May 20, 2007  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Part 1: Eating Bread with Unwashed Hands (Mark 7:1–13)

The Pharisees and teachers of the law challenged Jesus because His disciples ate bread with unwashed hands. But this was never really about hygiene—it was about tradition and authority.

> The Pharisees and some of the teachers of the law who had come from Jerusalem gathered around Jesus and saw some of his disciples eating food with hands that were defiled, that is, unwashed. (Mark 7:1–2)

The issue went far deeper. The Pharisees had developed elaborate ceremonial washing practices, treating them with the same seriousness as God's actual commands.

> The Pharisees and all the Jews do not eat unless they give their hands a ceremonial washing, holding to the tradition of the elders. When they come from the marketplace they do not eat unless they wash. And they observe many other traditions, such as the washing of cups, pitchers and kettles. (Mark 7:3–4)

In the Old Testament, ritual washings had specific purposes: priests washed before entering the temple, the Levites washed before serving God, and people washed to meet with God or handle disease. These weren't arbitrary—they pointed to the need for holiness and separation from impurity.

But over time, the Pharisees had transformed these genuine spiritual practices into endless human rules, disconnected from their original meaning. What began as wise boundaries became burdensome requirements.

Jesus responded with a direct accusation:

> He replied, "Isaiah was right when he prophesied about you hypocrites; as it is written: 'These people honor me with their lips, but their hearts are far from me. They worship me in vain; their teachings are merely human rules.'" (Mark 7:6–7)

A hypocrite, by definition, says one thing and does another. The Pharisees claimed to honor God but actually served their own traditions. They had become so invested in human regulations that they'd abandoned God's actual commands.

> You have let go of the commands of God and are holding on to human traditions. You have a fine way of setting aside the commands of God in order to observe your own traditions! (Mark 7:8–9)

Jesus illustrated this with a concrete example. The law commanded, "Honor your father and mother," but the Pharisees created a loophole: if someone declared their resources "Corban" (devoted to God), they no longer had to support their parents. They had become so clever at building protective barriers around the law that they ended up nullifying it.

The underlying pattern is striking: when we invest ourselves deeply in human rules—when we've built our identity around them—we naturally defend them even when they contradict what actually matters. We end up doing what we've invented to avoid doing what we've been told.

### The Consequence of Prioritizing Human Rules

This pattern continues in every generation. During the COVID pandemic, we saw this same dynamic play out: good intentions (preventing disease) gradually transformed into rules enforced with moral absolutism. Those who questioned escalating restrictions were branded as murderers. Shaking hands became an act of warfare. Running on the beach became "super-spreading." A gesture of peace—a kiss on the cheek—became indecent.

The Pharisees started with genuine wisdom about health and disease prevention, then turned their precautions into God-like laws. They became so focused on external rules that they ignored real internal dangers. Meanwhile, sexually transmitted diseases—affecting far more people than any virus—were permitted to spread freely. Churches were closed, but brothels stayed open. Hook-up apps operated 24/7.

> Good is evil. Evil is good.

The real danger was never external. True defilement comes from within, not from what we touch or eat.

## Part 2: The Unclean Dogs Eating Crumbs (Mark 7:14–30)

Jesus now shifted His teaching. Defilement isn't about what enters a person from outside—it's about what comes from within.

> Nothing outside a person can defile them by going into them. Rather, it is what comes out of a person that defiles them. (Mark 7:15)

> Don't you see that nothing that enters a person from the outside can defile them? For it doesn't go into their heart but into their stomach, and then out of the body. (Mark 7:18–19)

Jesus made a biological argument. Food passes through the stomach and exits the body. That's simply how bodies work. With this statement, Jesus declared all foods clean—a revolutionary moment for Jewish law.

Sin is not an external problem. Sin is an internal problem.

> What comes out of a person is what defiles them. For it is from within, out of a person's heart, that evil thoughts come—sexual immorality, theft, murder, adultery, greed, malice, deceit, lewdness, envy, slander, arrogance and folly. All these evils come from inside and defile a person. (Mark 7:20–23)

Consider a newborn. No one teaches children to be selfish, to refuse to share, to hurt others. From the moment of birth, after a mother has endured hours of labor, countless injections, and severe pain, the baby's first concern is only self: "Feed me now." Children naturally live this way until we teach them to be kind, patient, and loving.

The danger is not viruses, pork, or forgotten hand-washing. The danger is what Jesus described in verses 21–23. These things separate us from God.

> The heart is deceitful above all things and beyond cure. Who can understand it? (Jeremiah 17:9)

Jesus diagnosed the true division not as Jewish clean versus unclean, but as universal human sin versus God's righteousness. By relocating purity from external observance to internal transformation, Jesus opened the gospel to everyone—not just those who could maintain elaborate ritual systems.

### Jesus Enters Gentile Territory

After this teaching, Jesus traveled to Tyre, a port city inhabited by Gentiles—a Greek woman whose child was possessed by an evil spirit found Him.

> A woman whose little daughter was possessed by an impure spirit came and fell at his feet. The woman was a Greek, born in Syrian Phoenicia. She begged Jesus to drive the demon out of her daughter. (Mark 7:25–26)

At first, Jesus seemed to refuse:

> First let the children eat all they want, for it is not right to take the children's bread and toss it to the dogs. (Mark 7:27)

Here, "children" referred to the Jewish people—God's chosen ones who had received the covenants, the law, the temple worship, and the promises. The Gentiles were the "dogs"—those outside the covenant community, those considered unclean and uncivilized.

In Jewish thought, the barrier between themselves and unwashed Gentiles was absolute.

But the woman didn't accept Jesus's refusal. She answered with faith:

> Lord, even the dogs under the table eat the children's crumbs. (Mark 7:28)

She didn't demand a full meal. She didn't insist on equal treatment. She acknowledged her outsider status—she was willing to be the dog eating crumbs that fell from the table. But she knew that even the scraps from Jesus's ministry held power.

> For such a reply, you may go; the demon has left your daughter. (Mark 7:29)

The woman's desperation revealed her faith. She understood something the privileged disciples hadn't grasped: the gospel is available to everyone, but you must be hungry enough to want it. You must recognize your own internal need rather than looking outward for external enemies to blame.

By welcoming this Gentile woman, Jesus signaled that the exclusive Jewish covenant was expanding. The boundaries were breaking down. The good news was no longer restricted to those who could maintain perfect ritual purity.

## Part 3: Gentiles Eat Bread (Mark 7:31–8:21)

After healing the Gentile woman's daughter, Jesus traveled through Gentile regions and gathered a large crowd.

> Jesus called his disciples to him and said, "I have compassion for these people; they have already been with me three days and have nothing to eat. If I send them home hungry, they will collapse on the way, because some of them have come a long distance." (Mark 8:1–3)

The disciples had been with Jesus three days, hungering and thirsting for His teaching. Now Jesus would feed them.

> How many loaves do you have? Jesus asked.
> 
> Seven, they replied.
> 
> He told the crowd to sit down on the ground. When he had taken the seven loaves and given thanks, he broke them and gave them to his disciples to distribute to the people, and they did so. The people ate and were satisfied. (Mark 8:5–8)

Notice: *satisfied*. This wasn't scraps. This wasn't crumbs. The Gentiles ate the full meal. The promise Jesus had made to the woman—that the dogs would eat the children's bread—was fulfilled.

> Afterward the disciples picked up seven basketfuls of broken pieces that were left over. About four thousand were present. (Mark 8:8–9)

Earlier, when Jesus fed the five thousand (Jewish crowds), twelve basketfuls remained. Now, feeding four thousand Gentiles, seven basketfuls were left. The significance of "seven" appears throughout Scripture—it represents completion, fullness, and restoration. These seven nations (the Canaanites, Amorites, Hittites, Jebusites, Hivites, Perizzites, and Girgashites) whom Israel was commanded to utterly destroy—were now shown mercy through Jesus.

The old covenant's exclusive boundary had shattered. The Gentiles, once called "dogs," now received the full bread of the kingdom.

### The Hardened Heart

But immediately after this miracle, the Pharisees demanded a sign from heaven—as though feeding four thousand wasn't enough.

> The Pharisees came and began to question Jesus. To test him, they asked him for a sign from heaven. He sighed deeply and said, "Why does this generation ask for a sign? Truly I tell you, no sign will be given to it." (Mark 8:11–12)

Jesus's sigh revealed His weariness. He had just demonstrated compassion, multiplied bread, and fed a multitude. The Pharisees, blind to what was right before them, demanded proof.

The disciples, traveling in the boat with only one loaf, worried about having no bread. Jesus warned them:

> Be careful. Watch out for the yeast of the Pharisees and that of Herod. (Mark 8:15)

The disciples misunderstood, thinking He meant literal bread. But Jesus was teaching them about the influence of Pharisaic thinking—the yeast that spreads through everything, expanding human rules and skepticism until it crowds out faith.

> Why are you talking about having no bread? Do you still not see or understand? Are your hearts hardened? Do you have eyes but fail to see, and ears but fail to hear? And don't you remember? When I broke the five loaves for the five thousand, how many basketfuls of pieces did you pick up? (Mark 8:17–19)

> Twelve, they replied.
> 
> And when I broke the seven loaves for the four thousand, how many basketfuls of pieces did you pick up?
> 
> They answered, Seven.
> 
> He said to them, Do you still not understand? (Mark 8:20–21)

Jesus had demonstrated His power twice—to Jews and to Gentiles alike. Both times, abundance remained. Yet the disciples still feared scarcity. They had witnessed the gospel expand beyond all boundaries, yet they couldn't grasp that Jesus's provision knew no limits.

---

## The Core Teaching

The tension throughout this passage is between human regulations and God's righteousness, between external barriers and internal transformation, between scarcity and abundance.

The Pharisees believed purity came from careful external observance. Jesus taught that it flows from the heart. They built walls to keep people out; Jesus broke them down to welcome everyone.

When you're hungry enough—desperate enough—you'll take even the crumbs. But Jesus offers more than crumbs to all who come. The gospel doesn't require perfect purity, perfect practice, or perfect understanding. It requires hunger and faith.

The mercy shown to the Gentile woman and the four thousand was mercy extended to us. We were the outsiders, the unclean, the dogs. Yet through Christ's death and resurrection, we received not just scraps, but the full feast of God's kingdom.

Human rules will always expand, mutate, and ultimately betray us. Only God's word—calling us to internal transformation and radical faith—can set us free.
