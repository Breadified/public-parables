# James 2:14-26

**Title:** Faith That Works  
**Date:** 26 June 2020  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## The Central Tension

For centuries, James and Paul have seemed to be at odds. Martin Luther, the German Reformer, famously dismissed this letter as "strawy"—mere straw—because he believed James contradicted Paul's doctrine of justification by faith alone. The apparent conflict is stark:

**James says:**
> What good is it, my brothers and sisters, if someone claims to have faith but has no deeds? Can such faith save them? (2:14)
> 
> A person is considered righteous by what they do and not by faith alone. (2:24)

**Paul says:**
> For it is by grace you have been saved, through faith—and this is not from yourselves, it is the gift of God—not by works, so that no one can boast. (Ephesians 2:8-9)

The tension deepens when both cite the same Scripture about Abraham: "Abraham believed God, and it was credited to him as righteousness" (Genesis 15:6). Paul uses this to argue for justification by faith apart from works (Romans 4:5). James uses it to argue that faith and deeds must work together (James 2:22-23).

This is not a minor disagreement. The nature of salvation itself seems at stake.

## What This Passage Is NOT About

Before we panic, we must understand what James is addressing. **He is not redefining how we become Christians.** 

Notice: James addresses "brothers and sisters" (v. 14)—he is speaking to those already in the faith, not to seekers wondering how to enter God's kingdom. The thief on the cross needed no deeds; he was saved by faith alone in his final moments. Salvation by faith is not in question here.

Rather, James is addressing the *maturity* and *effectiveness* of faith in the Christian life. The word "save" in verse 14 carries a broader meaning than eternal salvation—it speaks to being saved from a fruitless, useless existence; saved from living like a copy of the world rather than being transformed by the gospel.

## Faith Without Works Is Dead

> In the same way, faith by itself, if it is not accompanied by action, is dead. (2:17)

James uses the Greek word *nekra*—the same word for a corpse. A body without breath is dead. Faith without deeds is equally lifeless.

Consider his illustration: "Suppose a brother or a sister is without clothes and daily food. If one of you says to them, 'Go in peace; keep warm and well fed,' but does nothing about their physical needs, what good is it?" (v. 15-16). Words alone profit nothing. Saying "I have faith" while doing nothing is merely talking the talk without walking the walk.

The Greek word for "useless" here is *arge*—the same word used for idle machinery or an unread book still in its shrinkwrap. A car's engine running in neutral reaches its potential for nothing. Faith untethered to action is equally sterile.

### Even Demons Believe

> You believe that there is one God. Good! Even the demons believe that—and shudder. (v. 19)

This cuts to the heart of mere intellectual assent. Confessing that God exists changes nothing if it doesn't change how you live. A demon believes in God's existence and power; what it refuses is obedience. Knowledge without transformation is demonic, not Christian.

To truly believe in the one God means to live in light of that belief:
- If there is one sovereign God, we need not be anxious about the fog that surrounds us
- If there is one Judge, we must act in righteousness, knowing we will answer to Him
- If there is one true God, our loyalty belongs to Him alone, not to the loud voices of culture that demand our compromise

## Faith Finds Its Completion in Deeds

The turning point comes in verse 22: "You see that his faith and his actions were working together, and his faith was made complete by what he did."

James points to Abraham, the father of faith. But which act justified him? Not merely his intellectual assent to God's existence, but his willingness to offer Isaac on the altar—the moment when faith became action. Into the fog, without answers or logic, Abraham stepped forward in obedience. His faith reached its *telos*—its goal and completion—through what he did.

> Was not our father Abraham considered righteous for what he did when he offered his son Isaac on the altar? (v. 21)

This is the paradox that resolves the apparent contradiction: Abraham was **declared** righteous by faith (Genesis 15:6, cited by Paul). But Abraham was **becoming** truly righteous through his works—his faith was finding its actual completion and effectiveness in his obedience.

God says to Abraham: "Now I know that you fear God, because you have not withheld from me your son, your only son" (Genesis 22:12). The test revealed what faith looks like when it costs everything.

Similarly with Rahab: "Was not even Rahab the prostitute considered righteous for what she did when she gave lodging to the spies and sent them off in a different direction?" (v. 25). She believed the God of Israel was real. She acted on that belief, taking enormous risk. Her faith was made real through her deeds.

## The Challenge to Our Faith

Here is where the teaching becomes uncomfortable. Many of us possess extraordinary biblical knowledge. Christian universities, Bible study groups, theological training—we have the knowledge to fill libraries. Yet what fruit does our faith produce?

Our fears remain the same as the world's: Am I beautiful? Am I popular? Am I liked? Our priorities mirror our unbelieving friends: a comfortable middle-class life, overseas holidays, superannuation. We have become a "cheap China knock-off"—a safer, non-smoking, more polite version of the world, but the world nonetheless.

Our older brothers and sisters in Christ—those whose statues we are now tearing down—they transformed civilization. They didn't make copies. They made a whole new world. We, by contrast, have become "the pious version of the world."

> If you possess these qualities in increasing measure, they will keep you from being ineffective and unproductive in your knowledge of our Lord Jesus Christ. (2 Peter 1:8)

When did you last truly practice what you believe? When did you last step out in faith that cost you something? This is the question James presses into our souls.

## True Faith Proves Itself in Action

Charles Blondin was a legendary tightrope walker who crossed Niagara Falls approximately 300 times in the 1800s. Huge crowds would gather. He would ask them: "Do you have faith that I can cross this rope?" They would roar their approval. "Do you have faith I can do it blindfolded? With a wheelbarrow? While carrying someone on my back?"

"YES!" the crowd would shout. "Of COURSE you can!"

Then Blondin would ask: "Who will volunteer to be carried across?"

Suddenly, silence. That is the moment faith becomes real. That is when belief transforms into action.

This is what James is asking us:

> You see that a person is considered righteous by what they do and not by faith alone. (v. 24)

Justified by faith alone? Yes—we are declared righteous by God through faith in Christ. But faith is never meant to stand alone. It must express itself through what we do.

True faith is:
- A faith that obeys God unequivocally, without doubt
- A faith that risks everything based on a promise from God
- A faith that shows itself real in times of trial and persecution
- A faith that doesn't shrink back when the cost is high

## A Word to Those Still Struggling

If you are a Christian struggling and not yet seeing fruit, do not hear this as a guilt trip. Christ died for you while you were still a sinner, when you had done no good. He doesn't demand that you earn His love through works.

But He also doesn't want you to stay useless. Through trials and sufferings, you can be growing and maturing. Your skin can get thicker. God calls you to be useful for His cause—to be transformed, not conformed.

## Our Charge: Time for Action

The question comes to us now: When the chips are down and persecution comes, will the faith we confess turn to action?

This is our moment. I see a church that:
- Speaks on controversial topics others avoid
- Faithfully cares for the weak and troubled among us
- Willingly bears unpopular witness for truth, suffering swear words and scorn
- Engages politically, sacrificing resources for the slim hope of systemic change
- Stands for theological convictions, willing to abandon comfort and step into the unknown

> But we do not belong to those who shrink back and are destroyed, but to those who have faith and are saved. (Hebrews 10:39)

This is faith that works. This is faith that saves—not from judgment day, but from living a useless, wasted life. This is faith becoming what it was always meant to be.

Well done.

> As the body without the spirit is dead, so faith without deeds is dead. (v. 26)
