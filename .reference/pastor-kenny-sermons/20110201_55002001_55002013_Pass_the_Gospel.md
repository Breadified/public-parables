# 2 Timothy 2:1-13

**Title:** Pass the Gospel  
**Date:** 13 February 2011  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## A Relay Race

The gospel has been passed down through generations—we must not drop it.

Consider the 4 × 100 metres relay in athletics. Four runners each complete 100 meters, passing a baton in sequence. The first team to finish wins. But in the 1952 Helsinki Olympics, the Australian women's sprint relay team—featuring two world champions, Shirley Strickland and Marjorie Jackson—was leading the race. They'd already set a world record in their heat. But on the final exchange, Marjorie Jackson dropped the baton. No matter how well the others had run, that one mistake determined the entire outcome.

Passing the baton matters. Consider too how some Chinese families pass down unique middle names through generations—so many that reciting them in order creates a poem spanning twenty generations. Imagine a family that migrates to Australia, loses touch with their heritage, and names their children incorrectly. The wrong information is lost forever.

Today's passage shows Paul emphasizing this same principle: passing the baton of the gospel.

## 1. Entrust to Reliable Men (2:1-2)

> You then, my son, be strong (empowered) in the grace that is in Christ Jesus. And the things you have heard me say in the presence of many witnesses entrust (commit) to reliable (faithful) men who will also be qualified to teach (competent to teach) others.

Second Timothy is likely Paul's last will and testimony—his final letter before his death in prison. There's an urgency here as he entrusts his legacy to the generation below, addressing Timothy as "my son."

He echoes his earlier reminder:

> What you heard from me, keep as the pattern of sound teaching, with faith and love in Christ Jesus. (1:13)

And in verse 14:

> Guard the good deposit that was entrusted to you—guard it with the help of the Holy Spirit who lives in us.

Notice the four generations mentioned in verse 2:

1. Paul passes the gospel to Timothy
2. Timothy passes it to reliable (faithful) men and women
3. These faithful people become qualified to teach
4. Others receive their teaching

Four generations—the gospel passed down perpetually, with each generation training the next to teach others.

**Why must these teachers be reliable, faithful, and qualified?** Because the gospel is only one generation from extinction. Think of the dodo bird, the Tasmanian tiger—one bad generation, one failure, one dropped baton, and the race is over. Those passing the gospel must be trained and equipped.

**Application:** At our church, we recognize that formal leadership—Bible study leaders, committee members—are qualified people. Though we're all works in progress, we consider them people of sound doctrine, able to teach others, reliable and faithful. They've endured training, testing, and rebuke to serve you. Respect them deeply.

But Paul's command isn't only for formal leaders—it's for all Christians. You are the leaders of tomorrow. You may become future Bible study leaders or pastors. Most certainly, you'll be fathers and mothers, leading your children in the knowledge and love of Christ. The gospel disappears in one generation if parents don't pass it to their children.

Therefore, be someone who can be entrusted with the gospel. Train yourself to be reliable and faithful, competent to teach. Leading a Bible study isn't the ultimate goal—it's training to lead your own families in Scripture. Your Sunday school teaching prepares you to teach your own preschoolers.

**Get trained.** We are richly resourced: small groups, training groups for Bible study leaders, training seminars available. Training exists for any ministry—leading Bible studies, leading Scripture classes, leading someone to Christ, training others to lead someone to Christ.

Yet sometimes we fail to take advantage of these resources and stagnate. We remain content with the level of Christian education we received in Sunday school—with simplistic, childish understandings of church as mere music and ritual, unable to articulate or testify to what we believe. Consider this: we invest professionally in our careers, write sophisticated essays at university, develop high competency at school—yet our Christian education lags far behind. We are the smartest people in the smartest part of the country. We hold high school diplomas, bachelor degrees, even master's degrees, yet our Christian knowledge remains at children's story level.

As the author of Hebrews urges: let us leave the milk and go on to solid food. Our goal must be to know the gospel so thoroughly that we can teach it to others.

If this is you, make this the year you commit to training. And if there's anything Asians know how to do, it's study. So do it.

## 2. Endure Hardship

> Endure hardship (suffer bad) with us like a good soldier of Christ Jesus. (2:3)

Paul presents three pictures of gospel commitment. First: the soldier.

Soldiers endure tremendous hardship. Consider the Roman soldier in Paul's day—rigorous training, constant discipline. Modern military cadets know a fraction of it: no showers, long treks. A Basic Fitness Assessment for Australian Defence Force males under 25 requires 40 pushups, 70 situps, and a 2.4km run in 11 minutes 18 seconds. Immense endurance and suffering.

Moreover, a soldier remains singleminded:

> No one serving as a soldier gets involved in civilian affairs—he wants to please his commanding officer. (2:4)

The soldier doesn't get distracted. In Paul's day, soldiers were cut off from civilian life—no phones, no email. Today, soldiers still require special leave to exit camp. They cannot abandon duty for birthdays or part-time work or fatigue from gaming. The soldier is wholly focused on pleasing the commanding officer.

Yet we resist this metaphor. We don't want to be soldiers of Christ because it's too hard. When the Australian Auditor-General audited the army's readiness for war, he found that more than a third of troops couldn't be deployed. About 7,300 failed basic fitness, 5,800 failed weapons proficiency, 1,500 were medically unfit, and 2,140 had poor teeth. Only about 53 percent were battle ready.

**Are we ready?** Are we distracted from testifying the gospel to our world? Cut out the distractions.

### The Athlete (2:5)

> Similarly, if anyone competes as an athlete, he does not receive the victor's crown unless he competes according to the rules.

Greek wrestling was the most distinguished Olympic sport, the ultimate expression of strength. But it has strict rules: no intentional hitting or kicking, no eye-gouging or biting, no improper finger-twisting. You must get your opponent on his back or out of the ring. Three points wins.

Ian Thorpe coming out of retirement must endure absolutely. No life outside swimming. According to the rules means no steroids, no shortcuts—hard work and discipline.

There are rules to passing the gospel too. We cannot lie. We cannot use shortcuts, flattery, or manipulation. We cannot tell people what they want to hear. We must speak truth in love, through relationships, in careful, painstaking ministry. It would be easier to preach prosperity gospel—that Christianity is suffering-free and God guarantees material wealth. It would be easier to attract crowds through spectacular music. But truth in love demands slow, careful, suffering ministry.

As Paul reminds us:

> Remember Jesus Christ, raised from the dead, descended from David. This is my gospel, for which I am suffering (enduring bad) even to the point of being chained like a criminal. (2:8-9)

### The Farmer (2:6)

> The hardworking farmer should be the first to receive a share of the crops.

Growing up in Malaysia, my ambition was to do anything in an air-conditioned room. But farmers work relentlessly—mending fences, planting seed, fertilizing, irrigating. They watch storms wipe away their crops, then start over again. It's exhausting labor to be qualified to teach others.

**Application:** I wonder if we're prepared for this hard work. Two things:

**First: Hard work in self-control.** Give away distractions—excessive computer games, television, sports, hobbies. Each has its place, but when they consume our time and we cannot give them up, we're enslaved. YouTube surpasses two billion views per day. Australians spend an average of 7 hours 19 minutes on social networking sites. We're being consumed.

**Second: Hard work in prioritizing training.** Imagine if your time training in Christian beliefs equated to the time you spend on organized sports, tutoring, or music lessons. If sport is important, if piano is important—why not Christian training? At school, what if your commitment to Christian knowledge matched your commitment to one subject? Read Christian books as you'd read textbooks. Think critically about Scripture as you would an English text. Research and write essays on your favorite Christian questions.

At university, put as much time into Christian knowledge as you do per subject. Three hours of lectures? Commit to three hours of Christian lectures. A couple hours of tutorials? Give it to Bible study groups. Two textbooks per semester? Read two Christian books.

Why now? Because you're in the training phase of your life. School and university exist for this reason. Your knowledge at university is often your lifetime high point. Make it the highest it can be. Don't keep your Christian qualification at Sunday school level.

For parents and workers, the study phase has passed, but training continues. It's like pursuing a master's degree or professional qualification. If circumstances allow, save time for short study blocks. If possible, take a year for Bible college.

Get trained. Get qualified. Get trusted. Invest the time. It's hard, but worth it—not just for you, but for others, especially the next generation.

## 3. For the Sake of the Elect (2:9-10)

> But God's word is not chained. (2:9)
>
> Therefore I endure everything for the sake of the elect, that they too may obtain the salvation that is in Christ Jesus, with eternal glory. (2:10)

The purpose of training isn't personal glory. It's for others. Endure for the elect, so they may obtain salvation. The motivation is love.

The former principal of Moore College, David Knox, said the purpose of learning is to tell the gospel better. Whatever you learn, the end goal is communicating the gospel more clearly.

The gospel isn't meant to be preserved like a precious heirloom locked away. It's meant to be passed on and lived out, that it may save others.

### The Reward (2:11-13)

> Here is a trustworthy saying:
>
> If we died with him, [also] we will also live with him;  
> if we endure (persevere), [also] we will also reign with him.  
> If we disown him, he will also disown us;  
> if we are faithless, he will remain faithful, for he cannot disown himself.

This passage offers two interpretations of verse 13. One suggests "faithful in forgiving us," but that undermines the warning. Another suggests "faithful to his promise to disown," affecting our assurance. 

But consider the structure as a chiasm—a literary device revealing that you're receiving training even in our sermons! Verses 11a and 13a belong together. Verses 12 and 12b belong together. Thus verse 12 addresses rewards, not salvation. "You will reign" means reward, not eternal destiny.

The context shows this pattern:
- Soldier pleases the commanding officer (4)
- Wrestler receives the victor's crown (5)
- Farmer receives the first fruits (6)

If you deny Him, you're denied rewards. But salvation itself remains secure.

## Pass the Gospel

Be that reliable person.

The race began 2,000 years ago when Jesus appeared, died, and rose to life before witnesses and disciples. These disciples, like Paul, spoke and wrote these things, entrusting them to reliable people. Now, nearly eighty generations later—eighty 100-meter laps—it has come to you. It's your turn to hold the baton.

Don't slack off. Train hard. Be single-minded. Don't be distracted. For the sake of the elect, don't drop it.

Pass the gospel.
