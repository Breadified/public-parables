# Luke 2:21-35

**Title:** The Comfort of God  
**Date:** December 22, 2012  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Christmas is About Waiting

We live in a season of waiting. Queues at the shops, parking lots, traffic, waiting for butchers and seafood counters. Retailers bank on Christmas for 20% of their annual sales. Even when we try to avoid the crowds by shopping online, we find ourselves waiting at post offices for deliveries. And then there are the presents themselves—sitting under the tree, waiting to be opened.

But today's passage tells us about a different kind of waiting. It's about people who waited for something far more significant than holiday shopping or wrapped gifts.

## Judah Waiting for Comfort

### The Background (Luke 2:21-24)

After Jesus was born, His family followed the Law of Moses precisely. On the eighth day, Jesus was circumcised. Then, when the time of purification was complete, Mary and Joseph brought Him to Jerusalem to present Him to the Lord and offer the required sacrifice.

This was no wealthy family offering a lamb. They brought a pair of turtledoves or two young pigeons—the offering of the poor. Yet they were righteous and devout, faithful to God's Law even in their humble circumstances.

The temple would have been crowded with people like them, waiting in queues to complete their sacrifices.

### Simeon's Long Wait (Luke 2:25-32)

Among those in the temple was a man named Simeon. The Scripture describes him simply: "righteous and devout, waiting for the consolation of Israel, and the Holy Spirit was upon him."

**Why did Israel need comfort?** Their history was one of suffering and displacement. About 500 years before this moment, the people of Judah had been taken into exile in Babylon. Though they were eventually freed, many never returned. The land lay in ruins, the buildings destroyed, foreign settlers occupying their homeland. Then Rome came and conquered them, making Judah a client state under foreign domination.

Israel was waiting for relief. Waiting for a rescuer. Who would protect them?

Through the prophet Isaiah, God had promised this comfort:

> Comfort, comfort my people, says your God. Speak tenderly to Jerusalem, and cry to her that her warfare is ended, that her iniquity is pardoned... And the glory of the Lord will be revealed.

Simeon had staked his life on this promise. The Holy Spirit had revealed to him that he would not see death before he had seen the Lord's Christ—the Messiah. How long he had waited, we don't know. But when Mary and Joseph brought the child Jesus into the temple, the Spirit moved upon Simeon, and he recognized Him.

He took the child in his arms and proclaimed:

> Lord, now you are letting your servant depart in peace, according to your word; for my eyes have seen your salvation that you have prepared in the presence of all peoples, a light for revelation to the Gentiles, and for glory to your people Israel.

Simeon was overwhelmed with joy. His entire life—his sole ambition—had been to see the Messiah. It was like all his Christmases had come at once. Now he could die in peace.

But Simeon also understood something profound about what this Child would accomplish. Jesus would come to save the world through a tragic means—through His own death. He would sacrifice His life for many and bear the penalty of sin for His people.

> And Simeon blessed them and said to Mary his mother, Behold, this child is appointed for the fall and rising of many in Israel, and for a sign that is opposed... and a sword will pierce through your own soul also.

### The Application: Ready to Die

Here's what strikes me about Simeon's faith: he had seen the Messiah, and he was ready to die. Not out of despair, but out of completion. His life's purpose was fulfilled.

Christians should be a people ready to die, because like Simeon, we have seen the Messiah—not with physical eyes, but with spiritual eyes. As we read his testimony, it's as though we too have held the baby, marveled at Him, and can say, "Now let me depart in peace."

As Paul wrote:

> For to me to live is Christ, and to die is gain. (Philippians 1:21)

And Jesus said:

> If anyone would come after me, let him deny himself and take up his cross and follow me. For whoever would save his life will lose it, but whoever loses his life for my sake and the gospel's will save it. (Mark 8:34-35)

**Yet we resist this truth.** We cling to our bucket lists—we want to get married, travel the world, visit certain cities, build careers, earn degrees, accumulate possessions. Christmas becomes the climax of our hedonistic desires as we gorge ourselves on presents and food.

But our true contentment should be in Christ alone.

I once knew an older Christian gentleman. Two months after his retirement—the time he'd been waiting for—he was diagnosed with cancer. Six months later, he died. People said, "What a shame. He couldn't enjoy his travels, his break." But here's the thing: he was a Christian. That means he's in a far better place than any city on this earth. To die is gain.

**We don't need to experience everything the world offers.** We don't need to live in fear of missing out—FOMO. Yet we see this everywhere. Students fear missing a single math class because they worry their classmates will get ahead. We fill our children's calendars with tutoring, sports, Chinese school, music lessons, all because we fear they'll miss out if we don't. We tell ourselves, "My child could be a famous singer if only I'd given him lessons earlier." So we pile on class after class.

These aren't bad things in themselves. But ask yourself: if our children don't know Jesus, don't love Him, don't care about Him—is their life really better with more piano lessons? When someone asks our children, "What do your parents really want from you?" would they say, "To follow Jesus," or would they say, "To succeed, to get good grades, to be the best"?

We are unwittingly teaching our children NOT to be ready to die. We're teaching them NOT to be content in the gospel.

Consider Simeon again. He had been promised he wouldn't die until he saw the Messiah. You'd think he might hide away in some remote corner, avoiding people! Instead, he remained at the temple, daily looking for the Messiah. He wasn't worried about death—he was focused on what he would accomplish before he died.

## Israel Waiting for Comfort: Anna

### The Second Witness (Luke 2:36-38)

There was another person in the temple waiting for Christmas: a prophetess named Anna.

This account is rich with symbolism. Anna means "Hannah"—like the prophetess Hannah of the Old Testament. Her father's name was Phanuel, which means "face of God." She was of the tribe of Asher, from the northern tribes—the first to be conquered and scattered.

Anna was advanced in years. She had been married for seven years, and then was a widow for eighty-four years. She never left the temple, but "worshiped with fasting and prayer night and day."

The symbolism is clear: this represents God's faithfulness to all twelve tribes, even to those thought lost and scattered.

> And coming up at that very hour she began to give thanks to God and to speak of him to all who were waiting for the redemption of Jerusalem.

What was this elderly woman doing? Even as she awaited the Messiah herself, she was telling others about Him. She was speaking to all who were waiting for redemption.

### Our Purpose: Proclaiming Comfort

Here's what Anna teaches us: if Judah has received comfort, if Israel has received comfort, then the time has come—as Simeon said—for this salvation to be "a light of revelation for the Gentiles." It's for everyone. It's for us and our friends and family and strangers. The face of God turns favorably toward all.

**We are still living for a reason.** The reason is to do God's work and proclaim His comfort. We should be content doing God's work—telling our friends, setting up opportunities for witness and fellowship. We live to tell others about Christ.

**Secondly, we live to prepare the next generation** to tell the gospel. Whether through training younger believers in youth group, through Sunday school, or through raising godly children in our own homes—this is our calling.

This is why we live. To die is gain, but to live must be for Christ. To tell others of His comfort. To assure them that death is not the end.

## Our Comfort Today

Let me turn to Romans 8 for the comfort that sustains us now.

Paul writes:

> What then shall we say to these things? If God is for us, who can be against us? He who did not spare his own Son but gave him up for us all, how will he not also with him graciously give us all things? Who shall bring any charge against God's elect? It is God who justifies. Who is to condemn? Christ Jesus is the one who died—more than that, who was raised—who is at the right hand of God, who indeed is interceding for us. (Romans 8:31-34)

Paul asks a profound question: **Who shall separate us from the love of Christ?** Tribulation? Distress? Persecution? Famine? Nakedness? Danger? The sword?

> No, in all these things we are more than conquerors through him who loved us. For I am sure that neither death nor life, nor angels nor rulers, nor things present nor things to come, nor powers, nor height nor depth, nor anything else in all creation, will be able to separate us from the love of God in Christ Jesus our Lord. (Romans 8:37-39)

### The Wait is Over

You know what I love about Christmas Day itself? The waiting is over. The streets are quiet. It's peaceful.

Friends, the wait is over. This Christmas is a reminder that **Christ HAS come.** He has come for you.

Our sins have been pardoned. It is a time of comfort.

Your life is complete in Him. You can die in peace, knowing that to die is gain.

Merry Christmas.
