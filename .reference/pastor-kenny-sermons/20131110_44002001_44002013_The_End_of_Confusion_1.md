# Acts 2:1-13

**Title:** The End of Confusion
**Date:** November 10, 2013
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Speaking in Tongues (vv. 2-12)

When Pentecost arrived, something extraordinary happened—a sound came from heaven like a violent, rushing wind that filled the entire house. Tongues of fire appeared and came to rest on each person present. The disciples were filled with the Holy Spirit and began speaking in other languages as the Spirit enabled them.

To understand what's actually happening here, we need to recognize that "tongues" carries two meanings in Scripture. First, it refers to the physical organ in our mouth. Second, and more importantly here, it refers to language itself—which makes sense because we speak with our tongues. This is why we call our native language our "mother tongue."

The word choice throughout this passage demonstrates this parallel usage beautifully. In verse 1, the day is "fulfilled." In verse 2, the wind "filled" the whole house. The disciples "sat" in verse 2, and the fire "sat" (came to rest) on them in verse 3. The pattern suggests "tongues" in verse 3 (the shape of the flames) and "tongues" in verse 4 (languages) are intentionally connected—tongues of fire and tongues of language.

This interpretation makes sense of what follows. When Peter speaks, people hear his message in their own native languages. The crowd—Jews from every nation under heaven, gathered for the festival of Pentecost—each understood in their own language. The diversity is remarkable: Parthians, Medes, Elamites, residents of Mesopotamia, Judea, Cappadocia, Pontus, Asia, Phrygia, Pamphylia, Egypt, Libya, Rome, Crete, and Arabia. Yet each one heard the wonders of God declared in their own tongue.

### Why So Many Languages?

Pentecost was held fifty days after the Passover—hence "Penta-cost"—and attendance was required for all able-bodied Jewish males. But these scattered Jews spoke different languages because centuries of exile and persecution had scattered them across the known world. They're called the diaspora. Despite their geographical separation, they maintained their faith and traveled to Jerusalem to keep the festival. Yet they carried the languages of their adopted homelands with them.

### Not Ecstatic Babbling, But Real Language

Some Pentecostal churches teach that "speaking in tongues" means speaking in a special, ecstatic, heavenly language that sounds like unintelligible babbling. They point to 1 Corinthians 13:1—"If I speak in the tongues of men and of angels"—as evidence. But this passage is deeply hyperbolic. Paul lists impossible scenarios to make a single point: even if you possessed extraordinary gifts, without love you are nothing. He's not describing an actual spiritual gift; he's describing an impossibility to emphasize love's supremacy.

Others cite 1 Corinthians 14:2: "Anyone who speaks in a tongue does not speak to men but to God. Indeed, no one understands him; he utters mysteries with his spirit." But read in context, Paul's concern is unintelligibility harming the church. When everyone speaks their own foreign language in a gathered community—like Sydney's diverse restaurants where Cantonese and Arabic are spoken simultaneously—the church gains nothing. Verse 4 makes clear that prophecy (intelligible speech) edifies the church, while uninterpreted tongues edify only the speaker.

The exegetical evidence points to real languages, not ecstatic babbling. If God wanted to give an unintelligible language, it would contradict the entire theological significance of Pentecost itself.

## The End of Babel—The Reversal of God's Judgment (vv. 6, 12-13)

The most significant aspect of Pentecost is this: initial confusion gives way to complete understanding. People were bewildered by the rushing wind and noise, yet "each one heard them speaking in his own language." Some mocked them, assuming drunkenness. But by the end, when Peter finishes speaking, they understand completely—"cut to the heart," convicted by his words, they ask, "What shall we do?"

The Greek word for "bewilderment" in verse 6 carries profound theological weight. It echoes something from Israel's distant past: the Tower of Babel.

### The Tower and the Scattering

In Genesis 11, after the flood, humanity lived in unity with one language and common speech. As they settled in the plain of Shinar, they conceived an ambitious plan: to build a tower reaching to heaven so they might "make a name" for themselves and avoid being scattered. Their motivation was clear—unity of power and preservation of identity.

But God saw their arrogance. He said, "If as one people speaking the same language they have begun to do this, then nothing they plan to do will be impossible for them." To stop them, He confounded their language so they could not understand one another. The result? The building stopped, and they scattered across the earth, their grand name forgotten. That's why it's called Babel—because there God confused the languages.

Notice the judgment: no name and scattering. Foreign language became their punishment, a barrier separating people who once worked in unity.

### Language as Barrier

Foreign language divides us profoundly. I wanted to know how to order Korean food but felt intimidated in restaurants where English wasn't spoken—it was all just babble to me. Language separates relationships and creates loneliness. I never truly knew my grandmother in Malaysia, though I loved her and she cared for me as a child. We were separated not by distance or intellect, but by language. She spoke Fukien and Mandarin; I didn't. I couldn't ask her about her faith journey, how she raised eight children, what it was like growing up in the early 1900s. When she asked me questions, I had no idea what she was saying. Language divided us.

This division continues today. Estimates suggest between 3,000 and 10,000 living languages exist in the world. Yet imagine if we could suddenly understand one another across all these barriers—if Google Translate worked perfectly in real time. That would be the reversal of babel, the end of misunderstandings and incomprehension.

This is exactly what happens at Pentecost.

### The Solution Found in Abraham

The solution to Babel is found in Genesis 12, when God called Abram: "Leave your country and go to the land I will show you. I will make you into a great nation and I will make your name great, and you will be a blessing." After thousands of years, God's promises to Abraham come true through Jesus.

The "name" made great refers to Acts 2:21: "Everyone who calls on the name of the Lord will be saved." Jesus is the descendant and offspring of Abraham. And at Pentecost, there is no more scattering—they are gathered together. Remarkably, there is no more babble language. After the initial confusion, everyone understands in their own language! When Peter speaks about Jesus, everyone comprehends.

The Table of Nations that scattered at Babel is now reversed at Pentecost. The people from every nation listed in Acts 2:9-11 hear the wonders of God declared in their own tongues. This is not judgment; this is grace. God isn't giving a confusing heavenly language. He's doing the opposite—He's bringing people back together through mutual understanding.

### Language Builds Unity

To promote unintelligible language would be to reverse Pentecost itself. Instead, we must prioritize intelligible speech. Paul says in 1 Corinthians 14:18-19: "I thank God that I speak in tongues more than all of you. But in the church I would rather speak five intelligible words to instruct others than ten thousand words in a tongue."

Language patterns reveal history's winners. If we all spoke Japanese and German today, we'd know we lost World War II. If Russian, we'd know we lost the Cold War. Each conqueror brings their language. The Greeks brought Greek, Romans brought Latin, French brought French, English brought English. Languages mark judgment and conquest.

When foreign tongues appear in Isaiah's prophecy (which Paul quotes in 1 Corinthians 14:21), it signifies invasion and judgment: "Through men of strange tongues and through the lips of foreigners I will speak to this people." In a city under siege, a strange tongue means enemies have conquered—you've lost the war.

But at Pentecost, the fact that everyone understands the gospel means we're no longer under judgment. We're blessed. The curse of Babel is broken. Paul emphasizes this: "Tongues are a sign, not for believers but for unbelievers." If the language remains babble and unintelligible, it's still a sign of condemnation—the judgment of God still rests on them.

But "if an unbeliever comes in while everybody is prophesying, he will be convinced by all that he is a sinner and will be judged by all, and the secrets of his heart will be laid bare. So he will fall down and worship God, exclaiming, 'God is really among you!'" When unbelievers hear the gospel in a language they understand, they encounter salvation.

## Making the Gospel Intelligible

Our gatherings must prioritize intelligibility. This is why we interpret when necessary at combined baptism services—so people can understand. It's why missionary organizations like SIL have devoted enormous effort to translating Scripture into the world's languages. As of 2009, at least a portion of the Bible had been translated into 2,508 of 6,909 different languages.

If you have a gift with languages, cultivate it. Learn Chinese well. Practice it at church gatherings. Consider learning how to present Two Ways to Live in Chinese. On this side of heaven, we can't create one universal language, but we can make the gospel available in every language so that Acts 2:21 becomes reality: "Everyone who calls on the name of the Lord will be saved."
