# John 4:1-42

**Title:** Be a Camel
**Date:** 2014
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Jesus Wants a Drink (4:1-9)

Jesus and his disciples have traveled a long distance and arrive at the town of Sychar in Samaria near Jacob's well. After his journey, Jesus is tired, hot—it's about midday, the hottest part of the day—and thirsty. He sits down by the well while his disciples go into town to buy food.

When a Samaritan woman comes to draw water, Jesus asks her, "Will you give me a drink?"

But immediately there's a hurdle. The woman responds with shock: "You are a Jew and I am a Samaritan woman. How can you ask me for a drink? Jews do not associate with Samaritans."

### The Bad Blood Between Jews and Samaritans

To understand this tension, we need to know the history. Both Jews and Samaritans share a common ancestor in Jacob, whose twelve sons formed the twelve tribes of Israel. Over time, they were ruled by the house of David from Judah. But the nation experienced a civil war, and ten tribes in the northern kingdom broke away from the southern kingdom of Judah, each establishing their own capital and temple.

The smaller southern kingdom of Judah retained Jerusalem and the temple, while the larger northern kingdom of Israel built their own temple in Samaria. After many generations, the Assyrian Empire invaded the northern kingdom. Their foreign policy was brutal: they deported the majority of Israelites across the empire and brought in five foreign races from other conquered lands, who intermarried with the remaining population. This mixing created what would later be called Samaritans—people of mixed heritage who were despised by Jews for not being "pure." Today, fewer than a thousand Samaritans remain, living in just four families, and ironically, they've kept themselves separate precisely because they don't intermarry with other races.

The southern kingdom of Judah was later conquered by Babylon, but they were taken into exile as a unified people and eventually returned together when Persia conquered Babylon. This preserved their distinct identity as people of Judah—or simply, Jews.

When the Jews returned to the land, Jews and Samaritans found themselves living side by side, and the old animosities never died. Jews accused Samaritans of being impure, idolatrous, and disloyal. Samaritans accused Jews of stealing their land and corrupting themselves in Babylon. The hatred ran so deep that Samaritans wouldn't even acknowledge David's kingship or the temple in Jerusalem—they only accepted the Samaritan Pentateuch, their own version of Scripture. In this world of ethnic and religious hostility, a Samaritan woman was essentially saying to Jesus: "How can you, a Jew, ask me, a Samaritan, for a drink when we are enemies?"

## Living Water (4:10-15)

Jesus responds to this hostility not with defense but with an offer—and it's an offer like no other.

> "If you knew the gift of God and who it is that asks you for a drink, you would have asked him and he would have given you living water."

The woman is confused. "Sir, you have nothing to draw with and the well is deep. Where can you get this living water? Are you greater than our father Jacob, who gave us the well and drank from it himself, as did also his sons and his livestock?"

Jesus answers her with a profound promise:

> "Everyone who drinks this water will be thirsty again, but whoever drinks the water I give him will never thirst. Indeed, the water I give him will become in him a spring of water welling up to eternal life."

She's desperate for relief. The daily struggle to fetch water is relentless and exhausting. In the ancient world—and still today in many parts of the world—water collection defines life. In parts of Africa, fetching water is an eight-hour daily task for women. Water is heavy, hard to move uphill, unwieldy to transport, and easily contaminated.

But Jesus isn't offering physical water. He's offering eternal life. Just as water is essential for physical survival, he's promising something that satisfies the deepest longings of the human soul.

The woman responds eagerly: "Sir, give me this water so that I won't get thirsty and have to keep coming here to draw water."

### Application: The Thirst We All Feel

Water is a perfect metaphor for life itself. Without it, death comes quickly. In the Western world, we turn on our faucets and clean water pours out abundantly. We rarely think about the privilege of this.

But Jesus promises something deeper than physical water—he promises eternal life. A life that doesn't require constant effort to maintain, that cannot be contaminated or lost, that will forever satisfy. The thirst that drives us—for meaning, for purpose, for something that lasts beyond this life—can only be quenched by what Jesus offers.

We all feel this thirst. On our clearer days, we sense that something in the world isn't right, that we're looking for a fix that no earthly thing can satisfy. On our less sensible days, we try to fill this thirst with trivial, temporary, or dangerous things, thinking they'll finally satisfy us. But they never do.

## Repenting to the Truth (4:16-26)

Jesus abruptly shifts the conversation: "Go, call your husband and come back."

The woman replies, "I have no husband."

Jesus responds, "You are right when you say you have no husband. The fact is, you have had five husbands, and the man you now have is not your husband. What you have just said is quite true."

What does this reveal about her? Perhaps she's been a widow forced into poverty and desperation. Perhaps she's sought to satisfy her desires through relationships and is deeply compromised. Perhaps she comes to the well at midday alone—the hottest part of the day—because the other women avoid her, gathering instead in the early morning or late evening in groups.

But here's what's remarkable: whatever her past, she admits to the truth. She doesn't deflect or make excuses. She acknowledges the reality of her situation. And Jesus commends her for it: "What you have just said is quite true."

This moment represents her admission of her need. Her confession of failure. Her recognition that she is desperate for this living water. To access what Jesus offers, we must first come to the truth about ourselves.

### Wrong Place, Right Truth

The woman then says, "Sir, I can see that you are a prophet. Our ancestors worshiped on this mountain, but you Jews claim that the place where we must worship is in Jerusalem."

Jesus replies, "Believe me, woman, a time is coming when you will worship the Father neither on this mountain nor in Jerusalem."

The Samaritans were sincere in their worship—but sincerely wrong. They bowed down at Mount Gerizim, where their ancestors had placed the original tabernacle, before David moved it to Jerusalem. They were ignorant of this history because their Samaritan Pentateuch excluded the accounts of David's time.

> "You Samaritans worship what you do not know; we worship what we do know, for salvation is from the Jews."

But Jesus moves beyond geography entirely. The location of worship isn't a mountain or a temple building. The true place of worship is where you encounter truth itself.

> "Yet a time is coming and has now come when the true worshipers will worship the Father in the Spirit and in truth, for they are the kind of worshipers the Father seeks. God is spirit, and his worshipers must worship in the Spirit and in truth."

"Spirit and truth" are not separate ideas—emotional experience versus intellectual assent. Rather, they describe one reality: worship centered on the Spirit of Truth. God is spirit, meaning God is truth itself. God is truthful because God is faithful. As Jesus will say later:

> "I am the way and the truth and the life. No one comes to the Father except through me."

The Samaritan woman grasps something of this. She says, "I know that Messiah (called Christ) is coming. When he comes, he will explain everything to us." This is remarkable—the term "Messiah" isn't even in the Samaritan Pentateuch, yet she's heard of this hope, perhaps through contact with Jews.

Jesus responds with the most direct self-revelation in all of John's Gospel: "I who speak to you am he." Or more literally: "I AM." She has found the truth.

### Application: We Must Be Truth People

Jesus calls us to be truth people. Later, when comforting his disciples, he promises: "I will ask the Father, and he will give you another advocate to help you and be with you forever—the Spirit of truth." And in his prayer for the disciples, he says: "Sanctify them by the truth; your word is truth."

The ninth commandment forbids bearing false witness. Yet we live in an age where contempt for truth has become commonplace.

We justify lies by saying the ends justify the means—that deception serves a greater good. We dismiss inconvenient facts as unknowable rather than refuting them with evidence. We censor ideas we disagree with. We accept lies from media sources because the lies align with what we already believe.

Consider how some religious traditions explicitly permit deception. In Islam, there's a concept called Taqiya—that lying is permissible if it promotes peace. Yet when we observe Muslim-majority countries, we see laws making it illegal to convert from Islam, broad blasphemy laws, and prosecution of apostates. There's a curious disconnect between the claim of tolerance and the actual laws enforced.

We all have contempt for truth in different ways. Instead of refuting good arguments, we dismiss them as unknowable. We declare that the Bible is made up with "no evidence," even though historical scholarship increasingly supports biblical accounts. We silence voices through censorship rather than engaging with their arguments.

But there is no justice without truth. There is no faithfulness without truth. There is no salvation without truth.

We must be active in seeking the truth. We must be bold in telling it. And we must live it in our own lives.

## Telling Others the Truth (4:27-42)

Something remarkable happens next. Leaving her water jar behind—a symbolic gesture that she no longer needs the old way of drawing water—the woman goes back to the town and tells the people:

> "Come, see a man who told me everything I ever did. Could this be the Messiah?"

She leaves her bucket behind. Now that she has drunk the living water, she no longer needs it. Off she goes to tell others about Jesus.

The result is extraordinary:

> "Many of the Samaritans from that town believed in him because of the woman's testimony, 'He told me everything I ever did.' So when the Samaritans came to him, they urged him to stay with them, and he stayed two days. And because of his words many more became believers. They said to the woman, 'We no longer believe just because of what you said; now we have heard for ourselves, and we know that this man really is the Savior of the world.'"

This is why Jesus came into the world. A woman who was marginalized, despised, and compromised becomes a witness to her entire community. Because of her testimony, they encounter Jesus themselves, and their faith goes deeper than secondhand reporting—they know him personally.

Meanwhile, Jesus' own disciples are still caught in temporal concerns:

> "Rabbi, eat something."

But Jesus responds, "I have food to eat that you know nothing about." He's not speaking about physical food. His priorities are completely different:

> "My food is to do the will of him who sent me and to finish his work. Don't you have a saying, 'It's still four months until harvest'? I tell you, open your eyes and look at the fields! They are ripe for harvest. Even now the one who reaps draws a wage and harvests a crop for eternal life, so that the sower and the reaper may be glad together."

Jesus sees the world differently. While his disciples think about lunch, he sees a harvest of souls ready to be gathered. While they focus on their own needs, he focuses on the Father's mission. The work has already been done—Jesus himself is doing the hard work through his life, death, and resurrection. Our task is simply to harvest, to tell others what we know to be true.

We have the same calling today. Through personal conversations, through inviting friends to church, through sharing our faith during natural opportunities, we're simply harvesting what Jesus has already prepared. We might think this is difficult work, but it's far easier than the sowing and the working that Jesus does. Others have done the hard work. Jesus has broken down the barriers—between Jew and Samaritan, between Jew and Gentile, between God and us.

### The Complete Picture

There's something poignant about how this story ends. It begins with a simple request: Jesus is thirsty and asks for a drink. But look at what happens—the Samaritan woman drinks the living water and goes to tell others. The disciples return, the crowds come. Everything happens except that Jesus gets his drink.

For that drink, we must fast forward to John 19:

> "Later, knowing that all was now completed, and so that the Scripture would be fulfilled, Jesus said, 'I am thirsty.' A jar of wine vinegar was there, so they soaked a sponge in it, put the sponge on a stalk of the hyssop plant, and lifted it to Jesus' lips. When he had received the drink, Jesus said, 'It is finished.' With that, he bowed his head and gave up his spirit."

On the cross, Jesus finally drinks. But what he drinks is judgment—the judgment that should have fallen on us. He gave up the living water so that we could drink it. He went thirsty so that we would never have to.
