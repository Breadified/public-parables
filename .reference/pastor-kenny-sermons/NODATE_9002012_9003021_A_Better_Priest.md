# 1 Samuel 2:12-3:21

**Title:** A Better Priest
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Problem: Eli's Sons—Brutal and Godless

The Old Testament presents us with a sobering picture of spiritual failure that echoes through the ages. Eli's sons, Hophni and Phinehas, were scoundrels who had no regard for the Lord. Their story reminds us of history's most brutal dictators and their equally wicked sons—men who wielded power to oppress, intimidate, and abuse those around them.

Like the tyrants of our own time, Eli's sons operated on the basis of force. They were thugs and bullies who took what they wanted by violence:

> If the person said to him, "Let the fat be burned first, and then take whatever you want," the servant would answer, "No, hand it over now; if you don't, I'll take it by force." (1 Samuel 2:16)

The word used to describe them—"scoundrels"—appears elsewhere in Scripture to characterize the most depraved men: those who attempted to sexually assault visitors and those who viciously raped a woman until she died. Their wickedness ran deep.

### Their Sacrilege Against God

But their brutality toward others paled in comparison to their contempt for God himself. As priests, Eli's sons were entrusted with a sacred responsibility: to oversee the sacrificial system through which Israel paid for their sins. When the people brought their finest animals—choice cattle, lambs, or birds—these offerings were meant to turn away God's wrath. The most aromatic part of the sacrifice, the fat, was to be burned on the altar as an offering to God.

Yet Eli's sons perverted this holy system. Before the fat could even be burned, they would demand raw meat for themselves—the choice portions, the best cuts—and take them by force if refused:

> But even before the fat was burned, the priests' servant would come and say to the person who was sacrificing, "Give the priest some meat to roast; he won't accept boiled meat from you, but only raw." (1 Samuel 2:15)

They were fattening themselves on what belonged to God.

> This sin of the young men was very great in the Lord's sight, for they were treating the Lord's offering with contempt. (1 Samuel 2:17)

### Eli's Failure

Yet the problem extended beyond the young men themselves. Eli, their father and high priest, heard of their wickedness but failed to take decisive action. When he confronted them, they simply refused to listen. Instead of exercising his authority to stop them, Eli's passivity enabled their sin to continue.

God's indictment reveals the deeper issue:

> Why do you scorn my sacrifice and offering that I prescribed for my dwelling? Why do you honor your sons more than me by fattening yourselves on the choice parts of every offering made by my people Israel? (1 Samuel 2:29)

Eli had honored his sons above God. Perhaps, as an aging man, he believed he needed to preserve his sons to continue his priestly line. But this reasoning was no solution—it was sin. Just as Abraham once tried to help God fulfill His promises through his own means, Eli attempted to secure his dynasty through ungodly compromise. The ends do not justify the means.

Their action amounted to blasphemy—a defiant rejection of God's word and commandment. For this, they faced the ultimate penalty:

> The time is coming when I will cut short your strength and the strength of your priestly house, so that no one in it will reach old age... Every one of you that I do not cut off from serving at my altar I will spare only to destroy your sight and sap your strength, and all your descendants will die in the prime of life. (1 Samuel 2:31-33)

## The Solution: Samuel—A Faithful Servant

In stark contrast to Eli's sons stands another young man: Samuel. His story offers a profound lesson about faithfulness and the consequences of honoring God above all else.

Samuel's mother, Hannah, had made a remarkable vow. Rather than keeping her long-prayed-for son for herself, she dedicated him entirely to the Lord. Each year she would weave him a little robe and bring it to him at the sanctuary. This was not the action of a mother who loved her child less, but of a mother who loved God more.

> But Samuel was ministering before the Lord—a boy wearing a linen ephod. Each year his mother made him a little robe and took it to him when she went up with her husband to offer the annual sacrifice... Meanwhile, the boy Samuel grew up in the presence of the Lord. (1 Samuel 2:18-21)

While Eli's sons grew in wickedness, Samuel grew in favor with both God and people:

> And the boy Samuel continued to grow in stature and in favor with the Lord and with people. (1 Samuel 2:26)

### Samuel's Call

In those days, the word of the Lord was rare—a silent period when God had said all He intended to say. Yet it was to this young boy that God chose to speak:

> The Lord called Samuel... Then Samuel said, "Speak, for your servant is listening." (1 Samuel 3:10)

What did God reveal to Samuel? The judgment upon Eli's house. God told the boy that He would bring about everything He had spoken against Eli's family:

> For I told him that I would judge his family forever because of the sin he knew about; his sons blasphemed God, and he failed to restrain them. Therefore I swore to the house of Eli, "The guilt of Eli's house will never be atoned for by sacrifice or offering." (1 Samuel 3:12-14)

Samuel feared delivering this message, but Eli received it with humble acceptance:

> Then Eli said, "He is the Lord; let him do what is good in his eyes." (1 Samuel 3:18)

### The Promise of a Better Priest

Yet God's judgment was not His final word. Even as He pronounced the curse on Eli's house, He made a remarkable promise:

> I will raise up for myself a faithful priest, who will do according to what is in my heart and mind. I will firmly establish his priestly house, and they will minister before my anointed one always. (1 Samuel 2:35)

This promise pointed beyond the priesthood alone. The text speaks of one who would minister before "my anointed one"—suggesting that kingship and priesthood would eventually converge in a single figure.

When David became king, he himself began to fulfill priestly functions. He offered sacrifices, wore the priestly linen ephod, and blessed the people:

> Wearing a linen ephod, David was dancing before the Lord with all his might, while he and all Israel were bringing up the ark of the Lord with shouts and the sound of trumpets. (2 Samuel 6:14-15)

Yet even David was incomplete. When he offered to build a house for God, the Lord promised that his son would build it instead. But that son would be punished when he sinned. The promised priest must be someone who would never disobey—someone who could do all that God's heart desired, forever.

## The Ultimate Fulfillment: Jesus, Our Eternal High Priest

The answer to this mystery is found in the New Testament. Only one figure could fulfill this promise—a descendant of David who would serve as priest forever:

> The law appoints as high priests men in all their weakness; but the oath, which came after the law, appointed the Son, who has been made perfect forever. (Hebrews 7:28)

Jesus is that faithful priest. Unlike the sons of Eli, who grew wicked and perverse, Jesus grew in righteousness. Unlike earthly priests who could only mediate between humans, Jesus can do something no other priest could accomplish:

> If one person sins against another, God may mediate for the offender; but if anyone sins against the Lord, who will intercede for them? (1 Samuel 2:25)

Through Jesus's sacrifice, we have an intercessor. Our prayers are heard. Our sins can be forgiven. We have a priest forever.

## Application: The Parental Call

This passage confronts us with uncomfortable truths about parenthood and the choices we make in honoring God.

When children turn from God, does the word of God change to accommodate our love for them? Too often, parents choose to rewrite their theology rather than stand firm. "I love my child," they reason, "so I will change my beliefs to match their behavior." In doing so, they honor their children more than they honor God.

Our calling as parents is to draw the line, to be a rock when our children doubt, and to be prophets calling them away from sin. This requires courage and conviction. We cannot simply enable destructive behavior out of misplaced love.

Consider the parent who gives money to a daughter struggling with drug use and promiscuity—funding the very lifestyle that is destroying her. The parent's love is genuine, but undirected love becomes cruelty. Sometimes the most loving thing we can do is to refuse to enable sin, to let natural consequences teach the lessons that words cannot.

God uses us to call our children toward holiness. But He cannot use us if we simply capitulate to their desires. We must be willing to stand with the truth, even when it costs us the approval we long for. In doing so, we give our children the greatest gift: a pathway home.
