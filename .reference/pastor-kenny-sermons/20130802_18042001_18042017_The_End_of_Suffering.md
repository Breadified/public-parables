# Job 42:1-17

**Title:** The End of Suffering

**Date:** August 2, 2013

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## When Does Suffering End?

We often find ourselves stuck in suffering, wondering when it will end. Perhaps you're facing tragedy, a broken relationship, chronic illness—circumstances where nothing improves and dreams slip away. In such times, we ask: **Where is God?**

The Book of Job addresses this fundamental question from multiple angles. Today we explore how suffering finally ends—not through explanation, but through God's presence and our willingness to trust Him.

---

## 1. God Speaks (Job 42:1-4, 40:6-10)

Throughout the book, Job has desperately wanted an audience with God. He cried out:

> "I desire to speak to the Almighty and to argue my case with God." (Job 13:3)

> "I myself will see him with my own eyes—I, and not another. How my heart yearns within me!" (Job 19:27)

Finally, God speaks—not through Job's three friends, but directly to Job himself. And what God says may surprise us.

### God's Response: Not Explanation, But Challenge

When we ask "why suffering?" we're typically seeking the cause. Science might offer biological mechanisms. Theology might point to the cosmic consequences of sin—that Adam's fall introduced decay and death into creation. Romans 8:20 confirms: "The creation was subjected to frustration...in hope that the creation itself will be liberated from its bondage to decay."

But God doesn't answer Job this way. Instead, God responds with rhetorical questions:

> "Brace yourself like a man; I will question you, and you shall answer me. Would you discredit my justice? Would you condemn me to justify yourself?" (Job 40:7-8)

> "Do you have an arm like God's, and can your voice thunder like his? Then adorn yourself with glory and splendor, and clothe yourself in honor and majesty." (Job 40:9-10)

> "Unleash the fury of your wrath, look at all who are proud and bring them low...crush the wicked where they stand...Then I myself will admit to you that your own right hand can save you." (Job 40:11-14)

**The core question becomes:** Are you God? Can you maintain the universe, bring justice to the world, punish all wrongdoers while saving the innocent?

This isn't cruelty—it's perspective. We live 21, 30, 45, or 95 years and think ourselves experts on life. We criticize decisions we haven't fully understood, pass judgment on complexities we've never navigated. God has existed eternally and sustains all creation. He orders the universe as He sees fit and doesn't owe us an explanation for every decision.

### The Heart of the Matter: Trust, Not Understanding

Even if God explained every reason behind every suffering, we couldn't comprehend it. We would keep asking "why?" forever. **God therefore asks us to trust Him.**

God knows what He's doing. He was there in the beginning. He sustains all things. He will be there in the future. The answer isn't in understanding the plan—it's in knowing the One who holds the plan.

And God does more than speak. He appeared to us in Jesus Christ. Two thousand years ago on a patch of dirt in the Middle East, the God of the universe lowered Himself to be judged so that we don't have to be judged. Blood flowed for our sins.

We trust Him not because we understand the intricacies of His plan, but because **He loves us**. Like a child carried by his mother—no idea where he's going, why here and there—but following because of love.

Rather than conclude that imperfect explanations point to an imperfect God, perhaps we should believe in a good God whose explanations to us mortals are necessarily imperfect. We are not alone.

## 2. Job Moves On (Job 42:5-9)

Having heard from God, Job experiences a profound shift in understanding:

> "My ears had heard of you but now my eyes have seen you." (Job 42:5)

This isn't physical sight—Job witnessed a storm. Rather, it represents a change in understanding. He gets it.

### The Difficult Verse: "Dust and Ashes"

What follows seems puzzling:

> "Therefore I despise myself and repent in dust and ashes." (Job 42:6)

But Job is righteous! God Himself testified:

> "Have you considered my servant Job? There is no one on earth like him; he is blameless and upright, a man who fears God and shuns evil." (Job 1:8)

Later, God rebukes Job's friends and says:

> "You have not spoken of me what is right, as my servant Job has." (Job 42:7-8)

So what does Job repent from? The Hebrew text reveals the translation needs adjustment:

- "Myself" isn't in the original—it's been added for clarity but obscures the meaning
- "Repent **in** dust and ashes" should be "repent **from** dust and ashes"

**The meaning:** I reject and turn away from mourning.

Job is already in dust and ashes—sitting among them, covered in boils, scraping himself. He's been in mourning, lamenting, asking why. Now, having heard from God, he's ready to **move on**. He chooses to stop wallowing, stop questioning endlessly, and trust.

### The Choice Before Job—And Before Us

God has spoken. The choice is Job's:

- Continue wallowing and playing the victim, or
- Move on

Nothing wrong with questioning and grieving—let it out of your system. But **God has now spoken**, saying: Trust me. I was there in the beginning. I'm sustaining you. I'll be there in the future. You've got to let me be God and do my thing.

This is the end of mourning. The end of suffering—not in the sense that pain vanishes, but in the sense that we stop being defined by it.

### Suffering Cannot Be an Excuse

Some of us are stuck in grief and may need a few more moments of questions. But for others, it's time to move on.

Suffering can't excuse:
- Ignoring God
- Using hardship as evidence God is your enemy
- Perpetual doubt and bitterness
- Stopping service to God
- Our own sins

There is a time for mourning. There is also a time to move on. Queen Victoria mourned Prince Albert's death for 40 years, wearing widow's clothes until her own death. But that's not the template for us. We're called to **persevere through suffering, not wallow in it**.

Consider those who overcame:
- Japan, humiliated in defeat, rose to prosperity
- Singapore, abandoned by Malaysia, became a thriving nation
- The Jewish people, after the Holocaust, continued to build and contribute
- Members of our church living with chronic illness, who don't complain but finish their studies, get jobs, meaningfully serve

We all have grievances. We all have disadvantages. Does that mean permanent victimhood? Some are in genuine grief. But many of us are stuck in a "grievance industry"—professional victimhood where we claim someone 150 years ago wronged a relative, so we're entitled to compensation and can blame others for our condition.

The call is clear: **Move on. Trust God. Let's see what He does with our future.**

## 3. The End of Suffering (Job 42:10-17)

Job picks himself up. Things return much as before:

### Job Returns to Service

> "So now take seven bulls and seven rams and go to my servant Job and sacrifice a burnt offering for yourselves. My servant Job will pray for you, and I will accept his prayer." (Job 42:8-9)

Before, Job prayed for his children's sins. Now, he intercedes for his friends. He resumes his role as a righteous man serving others.

### Job Restored to Community

> "All his brothers and sisters and everyone who had known him before came and ate with him in his house. They comforted and consoled him over all the trouble the LORD had brought upon him, and each one gave him a piece of silver and a gold ring." (Job 42:11)

No longer an outcast, Job eats with others. Friends give him silver and gold rings—capital to rebuild his life. God uses the people around him to help Job recover.

### Job's Restoration: Twice As Much

> "After Job had prayed for his friends, the LORD made him prosperous again and gave him twice as much as he had before." (Job 42:10)

> "The LORD blessed the latter part of Job's life more than the first." (Job 42:12)

The restoration includes:
- **Wealth doubled:** Fourteen thousand sheep, six thousand camels, a thousand yoke of oxen, a thousand donkeys
- **Family blessed:** Seven sons and three daughters—with the remarkable detail that even his daughters inherited (verses 14-15), indicating extraordinary wealth
- **Years extended:** Job lived 140 years—twice the typical lifespan—and saw his children to the fourth generation (verses 16-17)

**Important observation:** These blessings exceed what Job lost. They're not merely compensation but gestures of God's grace. Job received undeserved suffering and now receives undeserved blessings.

### The Ultimate Point: God's Last Word Is to Bless

God is in control. As Job himself said:

> "Naked I came from my mother's womb, and naked I will depart. The LORD gave and the LORD has taken away; may the name of the LORD be praised." (Job 1:21)

This is comfort: the God who can give great suffering can also give great blessing. Both come from the same source. And His promise to His elect is **blessing**.

## Jesus on Suffering: Grace, Not Karma

Jesus spoke to the same question:

> "Now there were some present at that time who told Jesus about the Galileans whose blood Pilate had mixed with their sacrifices. Jesus answered, 'Do you think that these Galileans were worse sinners than all the other Galileans because they suffered this way? I tell you, no! But unless you repent, you too will all perish.'" (Luke 13:1-3)

> "Or those eighteen who died when the tower in Siloam fell on them—do you think they were more guilty than all the others living in Jerusalem? I tell you, no! But unless you repent, you too will all perish." (Luke 13:4-5)

The answer isn't karma. It isn't "they deserved it." The answer is grace speaking: **Repent and trust me.**

C.S. Lewis captured this truth:

> "God whispers to us in our pleasures, speaks to us in our conscience, but shouts in our pains: It is His megaphone to rouse a deaf world."

---

## Application: The Promise of Restoration

God has spoken to you through His Word and provided comfort to the suffering. Jesus didn't just speak—He acted. He took the cross and paid the penalty of sin, suffering so that our suffering might one day end permanently.

> "I consider that our present sufferings are not worth comparing with the glory that will be revealed in us. The creation waits in eager expectation for the sons of God to be revealed. For the creation was subjected to frustration...in hope that the creation itself will be liberated from its bondage to decay and brought into the glorious freedom of the children of God." (Romans 8:18-21)

**The end of suffering for those in Christ is not escape or explanation—it is restoration, blessing, and the presence of God.** When you face suffering, look to Job's example and the promise of Christ: Trust God, move forward, and await the glory that awaits.
