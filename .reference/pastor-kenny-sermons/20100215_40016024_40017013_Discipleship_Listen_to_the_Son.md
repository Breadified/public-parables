# Matthew 16:24–17:13

**Title:** Discipleship – Listen to the Son
**Date:** February 15, 2010
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Overview

This passage reveals Jesus as the glorious Son who calls us to follow Him as servants of forgiveness and mercy. We see three interconnected themes:

1. Follow the Son (denying self)
2. Listen to the Son (the Transfiguration, 17:1–13)
3. Serve the Son (welcoming others)

## The Glorious Son: The Transfiguration (17:1–9)

After six days, Jesus took Peter, James, and John up a high mountain. There, something extraordinary happened—Jesus was transfigured before them. His face shone like the sun, and His clothes became as white as light. Suddenly, Moses and Elijah appeared, speaking with Jesus.

Peter, overwhelmed by the moment, suggested building three shelters—one for Jesus, one for Moses, and one for Elijah. But before he could finish, a bright cloud enveloped them, and a voice from heaven declared:

> "This is my Son, whom I love; with him I am well pleased. Listen to him!" (Matthew 17:5)

The disciples fell facedown, terrified. But Jesus came and touched them, saying, "Get up. Don't be afraid." When they looked up, only Jesus remained. As they descended the mountain, Jesus instructed them to tell no one what they had seen until He had risen from the dead.

### Connection to Exodus

This scene echoes a remarkable Old Testament parallel. In Exodus 24, Moses and the elders of Israel went up Mount Sinai and saw the God of Israel. They ate and drank in His presence. Six days later, the Lord called to Moses from the cloud, and Moses entered it, staying forty days and forty nights.

The similarities are striking: companions on a mountain, a cloud covering the peak, a voice from heaven, the mention of "six days," the building of shelters (connecting to the tabernacle instructions that follow). Even Moses' face shone with God's glory (Exodus 34:29–35).

But Jesus is reenacting this scene to reinterpret it—not to repeat it, but to surpass it.

## The More Glorious Messenger (17:5, 8)

### Jesus is the New and Better Moses

God is announcing that Jesus is the fulfillment of what Moses represented. The comparison reveals Jesus' superiority:

- **Greater glory:** Moses' face shone, but Jesus' entire being radiates divine light—not just His face, but His very clothes. His glory is so overwhelming it seems to overflow from within.

- **Greater relationship:** Moses was God's servant and prophet. Jesus is declared "my Son." He is not merely a messenger; He is the Son of God—God Himself in human flesh.

- **Greater reality:** The law came through Moses as written commandments on stone tablets. But Jesus is the living Word. As John writes:

> "The Word became flesh and made his dwelling among us. We have seen his glory, the glory of the One and Only, who came from the Father, full of grace and truth." (John 1:14)

Peter's desire to build shelters misses the point entirely. Jesus doesn't need a tent or tabernacle—He is the true dwelling of God among His people.

### The Command to Listen

The voice from heaven emphasizes one crucial imperative: "Listen to him!"

This echoes God's words to Israel about the angel (messenger/prophet) He would send:

> "See, I am sending an angel ahead of you to guard you along the way and to bring you to the place I have prepared. Pay attention to him and listen to what he says... If you listen carefully to what he says and do all that I say, I will be an enemy to your enemies." (Exodus 23:20–22)

Jesus is that angel. He is the one we must listen to carefully and obey. When the disciples looked up and saw no one except Jesus—no Elijah, no Moses—the meaning was clear: Jesus alone. Listen to Jesus.

## The Challenge: Law and Grace

This raises a profound question: What is the relationship between the Old Testament law and the New Testament teaching of Jesus? How do they apply to us?

### The Problem

Christians have wrestled with this in different ways. Some have tried to follow Old Testament laws literally, attempting to maintain every regulation exactly as written. But this approach is not only impractical—it contradicts the spirit of grace that Jesus brought. It reduces Christianity to mere rule-keeping, as if Jesus were simply another lawgiver rather than a savior.

Others divide Old Testament law into three categories: ceremonial laws (about worship), civil laws (about society), and moral laws (about ethics). They argue that the first two have been fulfilled in Christ and no longer apply, while moral laws continue. This provides some clarity, but it introduces a troubling subjectivity. Who determines what is truly "moral"? Is eating certain foods a moral issue or a cultural one? Where do you draw the line?

### A Better Approach

Perhaps the truest understanding is this: Christians are dead to the Law in its entirety. The Law and all its rules and regulations are completely fulfilled in Christ. As Colossians states:

> "Since you died with Christ to the basic principles of this world, why, as though you still belonged to it, do you submit to its rules?" (Colossians 2:20)

Think of it this way: When we were children, our mothers warned us never to touch the hot kettle on the stove. Now that we're adults, we don't need that command—we have wisdom. Moreover, kettles on stovetops have become obsolete; we use electric ones instead. The old instruction has been superseded by maturity and changed circumstances.

The Old Testament law has ended, but God's eternal principles remain. These principles are best summed up in one word: **Love**—love of God and love of neighbor (Matthew 22:39).

This is the lens through which we read all Scripture. Pork was prohibited not because the food itself was morally wrong, but because loving care sometimes means avoiding things that could cause harm. Homosexuality remains inconsistent with God's design for love because it damages the loving relationships God intends. The reason isn't found merely in Leviticus—it's found in the principle of love that Leviticus expresses and that the New Testament reinforces.

Similarly, in the New Testament, the covering of hair is no longer required, but submission in relationships still matters. The Sabbath as a law is no longer binding, but encouraging one another (which the Sabbath anticipated) remains vital today.

This is complex work because the Bible is occasional—sometimes addressing immediate situations, sometimes revealing timeless principles. But Jesus has given us the key: **Listen to Him.** Take our marching orders from the New Testament, from the person and teaching of Christ. We can read the Old Testament and gain wisdom from it, knowing that Jesus has interpreted it for us.

## The More Glorious Message (17:6–7)

### The Message Is Grace, Not Condemnation

When the people of Israel heard God's voice on Mount Sinai, they trembled with fear:

> "On the morning of the third day there was thunder and lightning, with a thick cloud over the mountain, and a very loud trumpet blast. Everyone in the camp trembled." (Exodus 19:16)

But notice the contrast in Matthew 17:6–7. When the disciples heard the voice and fell facedown in terror, Jesus came and touched them:

> "Get up," he said. "Don't be afraid."

This is the difference between law and grace. Jesus is not coming to pile on more laws to increase your guilt. The Law was already given through Moses, and it could not save us. Instead, Jesus comes with words that rescue:

> "The law was given through Moses; grace and truth came through Jesus Christ." (John 1:17)

### Jesus Fulfills the Law in Our Place

Jesus has not come to condemn but to represent us. He is the truthful and righteous one who can stand in our place. He lived the perfect life the law demands. Then, as our perfect representative, He paid the penalty we deserve. On the cross, He was condemned by the law—not us. He bore the judgment that was ours.

So what should we do with such a Master who has served us? How do we respond to the Christ who denied Himself, carried His cross, and died for our sake?

The answer is simple: **Listen to Him. Follow Him.**

## The Challenge: Are We Really Listening?

### Four Ways We Avoid Listening to Jesus

Yet we are remarkably skilled at not listening. When we encounter something in the Bible that challenges or troubles us, we tend to respond in one of four ways:

**1. Appeal to Ignorance**

We skim quickly over hard passages or ignore them altogether. We find something we don't like to hear and mumble something about it being "too complicated." Then we carry on as we were. We say the Bible is true but act as though we're too lazy to understand it.

**2. Appeal to Tradition**

We appeal to what we've been taught in the past and dismiss what the passage actually says. "Well, my church says this" or "I've always been told that." But Scripture is to be interpreted by Scripture. Two passages cannot be read in contradiction to each other; we must let them inform one another. The question isn't what tradition says—it's what does this passage say?

**3. Appeal to Culture**

We dismiss the Bible's relevance by claiming it only applied to its own time. "That was the cultural context then," we say, as if ancient cultural practices somehow nullify eternal principles. We use this dodge with passages about evolution, homosexuality, and other uncomfortable topics—acting as though Christians have no real answer.

**4. Appeal to Exceptionalism**

We acknowledge the Bible is true for everyone else but not for us. "I'm too young for this. I'm too old for this. I'm an exception to the rule." We qualify and excuse ourselves until the passage means nothing to us—or to anyone.

### A Case Study: Matthew 18:8–9

Consider what we'll read next week:

> "If your hand or your foot causes you to sin, cut it off and throw it away. It is better for you to enter life maimed or crippled than to have two hands or two feet and be thrown into eternal fire. And if your eye causes you to sin, gouge it out and throw it away. It is better for you to enter life with one eye than to have two eyes and be thrown into the fire of hell."

How do we respond?

- **Ignorance:** Too hard to understand.
- **Tradition:** Some theologian says we don't have to take this literally.
- **Culture:** Mutilation was more common back then; we don't do that anymore.
- **Exceptionalism:** "Jesus doesn't really mean that. Besides, I need both arms for my school certificate. I need both eyes for my upcoming exam. I'm an exception."

For many good reasons (which we'll explore), Jesus is almost certainly not commanding literal self-mutilation. But here's the danger: we often jump too quickly to that qualification and miss the point entirely.

### The Better Way: Sit Under the Word

Instead of racing to excuse the text, we should pause and sit under it. Ask yourself: What is God trying to say? Let the force of His words affect you. What would it mean to cut off your arm? It would hurt. You would miss it. You would be disabled. For a particular sin, it might actually help to stop. Why? Because God hates that sin.

The end result isn't necessarily that you literally cut off your arm. But you might:
- Cut off that internet subscription that fuels your temptation
- Stop using your hands to steal, to hit, to harm others
- Put your hands in your pockets and choose peace instead
- Cut off that TV program, put away that useless magazine
- Take drastic action against whatever is causing you to sin

You won't consider any of these options if you've simply dismissed the text as impractical.

### The Call to Radical Discipleship

Jesus says to us: "Deny yourself, take up your cross, and follow me."

What do you do with that? Here's what I'd like you to consider:

What is the most challenging thing you've heard from the Bible lately? What is something another Christian has challenged you about from Scripture? What is the most challenging thing you know God could ask of you right now—something that aligns with Scripture, something you know is probably the right thing to do?

Now ask yourself: What are the real reasons I'm not doing it? Is it ignorance? Tradition? Cultural discomfort? Am I claiming an exception that doesn't actually apply?

We're a bit like Peter, saying "No, Lord" to Jesus' call to deny ourselves and follow Him. But I want to tell you this: Jesus hasn't come to earth to give you more laws and increase your guilt. He has come to remove your guilt. He comes to the disciples and says, "Do not be afraid."

He died for your sake. And when He instructs you, it is also for your sake. So in love and gratitude, listen to Him.

---

## Conclusion

**Listen to Him.** Take the written Word seriously. Sit under it. Let the authority of God shape your life, not the other way around. This is what it means to be a radical disciple of Christ.
