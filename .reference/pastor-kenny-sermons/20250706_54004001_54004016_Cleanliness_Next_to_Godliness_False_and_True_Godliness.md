# 1 Timothy 4:1-16

**Title:** Cleanliness Next to Godliness? False and True Godliness
**Date:** July 6, 2025
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## The Problem: False Godliness (Verses 1-5)

The Spirit clearly warns us that in the last days—the time we're living in now, awaiting Christ's return and the restoration of all things—some will abandon the faith and follow deceiving spirits and demonic teachings. These false teachings come through hypocritical liars whose consciences have become hardened, seared as with a hot iron.

Their doctrine reveals itself through specific practices:

> They forbid people to marry and order them to abstain from certain foods, which God created to be received with thanksgiving by those who believe and who know the truth. (v. 3)

This ascetic approach treats the material world—sex, food, the body—as inherently sinful and corrupting. The underlying philosophy is Gnostic: the physical realm is evil, so we must deny the flesh through rigid rules and self-deprivation.

But consider the flawed reasoning: these teachers argue that because there is no sex in heaven, we shouldn't marry today. By that logic, we shouldn't have children either (for there are no children in heaven), nor should we evangelize (there's no evangelism in heaven). Yet these activities are essential for our present stage of redemptive history. They're not pointless simply because they won't exist in the age to come.

**Modern Manifestations of False Godliness**

This ancient error echoes in our contemporary world. We've simply redirected our ascetic impulses from the ancient Jewish dietary laws to modern moral performance:

- **Food as virtue**: Fair-trade coffee, organic produce, free-range eggs, plant-based diets—these become markers of spiritual superiority rather than simple preferences
- **Health as holiness**: A "beach body" or six-pack abs becomes the visible proof of godliness, as if physical discipline were equivalent to spiritual maturity
- **Lifestyle choices as morality**: We attach spiritual significance to choices that are spiritually neutral—where we shop, what we eat, how we exercise

We see people who maintain no biblical sexual morality yet become fiercely judgmental about food choices. There's an interesting inversion: when a society abandons moral clarity about sexuality, it often channels that moral energy into rigid food rules. Some claim no binding morality exists regarding sexual conduct, yet insist there is binding morality regarding what one eats.

The danger is that these external rules become a *substitute* for true godliness—a mask of righteousness covering an unchanged heart. It's like the Pharisaic "whitewashed tombs": beautiful on the outside, full of decay within.

**The Divine Perspective**

Yet Paul provides the corrective:

> For everything God created is good, and nothing is to be rejected if it is received with thanksgiving, because it is consecrated by the word of God and prayer. (vv. 4-5)

This echoes throughout Scripture. Genesis declares God's creation "good." Jesus Himself declared all foods clean. Peter's vision shows us not to call unclean what God has made clean. The principle is clear: *nothing* created by God is inherently corrupting.

But this freedom isn't permission to do anything. It addresses those false teachers who deny the goodness of creation. It doesn't mean we should consume everything at a buffet simply because we can. The question becomes: among all the good things God has created, how do we choose wisely?

## The Solution: True Godliness (Verses 6-16)

### Nourishment Through Sound Teaching (Verses 6-7)

> If you point these things out to the brothers and sisters, you will be a good minister of Christ Jesus, nourished on the truths of the faith and of the good teaching that you have followed. (v. 6)

To be a good servant of Christ, one must be *nourished*—fed consistently—on sound doctrine and faithful teaching. This isn't passive consumption; it's deliberate cultivation of truth.

The response is clear:

> Have nothing to do with godless myths and old wives' tales; rather, train yourself to be godly. (v. 7)

The Greek word for "train" (gymnasia) is the origin of "gymnasium." Just as athletic training requires discipline and repetition, so does spiritual growth. Godliness is cultivated through deliberate practice and habitual submission to God's Word.

### Godliness Versus Physical Training (Verse 8)

> For physical training is of some value, but godliness has value for all things, holding promise for both the present life and the life to come. (v. 8)

Physical exercise has some benefit—you might achieve it through regular walking, manual labor, or modest activity without expensive gym memberships. But godliness—devotion to God in all of life, making decisions that prioritize His glory—yields profit that extends to both this age and the age to come.

Godliness is not about achieving a particular aesthetic or moral performance standard. It's about alignment with God Himself.

### The Hard Labor of True Godliness (Verses 10-12)

> That is why we labor and strive, because we have put our hope in the living God, who is the Savior of all people, and especially of those who believe. (v. 10)

The words "labor" (κοπιῶμεν) and "strive" (ἀγωνιζόμεθα) convey genuine struggle and effort. This is particularly true for leaders—and especially for young leaders.

> Don't let anyone look down on you because you are young, but set an example for the believers in speech, in conduct, in love, in faith and in purity. (v. 12)

Young leaders face particular pressure. The antidote isn't defensive posturing but *exemplary living* across five dimensions: your words, your behavior, your love, your faith, and your sexual purity. These aren't rules to follow; they're a pattern to embody.

### Perseverance and Progress (Verses 15-16)

> Be diligent in these matters; give yourself wholly to them, so that everyone may see your progress. Watch your life and doctrine closely. Persevere in them, because if you do, you will save both yourself and your hearers. (vv. 15-16)

Notice the word "progress"—this is visible, observable spiritual growth. Godliness is not static; it develops through persistent attention to both your life and your doctrine.

The promise of "salvation" here is not about earning justification but about living out the good works God has prepared for you. As Philippians 2:12 reminds us, we "work out your salvation"—not to earn it, but to express it through faithful living.

## The Heart of It All

The fundamental question Paul addresses is this: **Are you holy, or are you godly?**

You can avoid sin and remain technically righteous while still drifting with the world, doing only what is not forbidden rather than what is best. True godliness means:

- Making choices out of *devotion to God*, not mere avoidance of rules
- Among the many good things available, selecting those that serve the gospel
- Receiving God's gifts—including food, marriage, the goodness of creation—with genuine thankfulness
- Recognizing that when we know the gospel, our eyes open to God's generosity and our hearts align with His purposes

When godliness guides us, we use the world's resources for their intended purposes. The goods of creation, placed in the hands of those devoted to God, become instruments of His kingdom.

## The Mystery of True Godliness

> Beyond all question, the mystery from which true godliness springs is great:
> 
> He appeared in the flesh,
> was vindicated by the Spirit,
> was seen by angels,
> was preached among the nations,
> was believed on in the world,
> was taken up in glory. (v. 16)

True godliness is not self-generated discipline or moral performance. It springs from the gospel itself—from Christ incarnate, vindicated, exalted, and proclaimed to all nations. Godliness is the natural response of a heart transformed by the mystery of Christ.
