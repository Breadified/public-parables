# Luke 24:13-46

**Title:** Return of the King  
**Date:** 1 April 2012  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Nature of Returning

When we return to our homeland after time away, there's a checklist of things we must do. Whether it's visiting loved ones, enjoying familiar foods, or rekindling old memories—we tick off those meaningful items before we leave again. But what about Jesus' return? What is He checking off?

## A Question on the Road (13-24)

On the very day of the resurrection, two disciples were walking to the village of Emmaus, about seven miles from Jerusalem. As they walked, they were engaged in intense discussion about everything that had happened—discussing, debating, and disputing what the events might mean. Yet despite the wonderful news of the resurrection itself, these two seemed entirely unmoved. One of them, named Cleopas, even rebuked the stranger who joined them:

> "Are you the only one visiting Jerusalem who does not know the things that have happened there in these days?"

They knew the facts. They recounted how Jesus was a prophet, powerful in word and deed before God and all the people. They knew the leaders had handed Him over to be sentenced to death and crucified Him. They even knew that some of the women had gone to the tomb early in the morning and found it empty, reporting that they had seen a vision of angels saying He was alive.

Yet here was the tragic irony: **they possessed all the evidence of the resurrection, yet believed none of it.**

## The Key to Understanding (25-27)

Jesus rebuked them not for missing some miraculous sign, but for missing the Scripture itself:

> "How foolish you are, and how slow to believe all that the prophets have spoken! Did not the Messiah have to suffer these things and then enter his glory?"

Their real problem was threefold:

1. **Grief had clouded their understanding.** Their faces were downcast; they were spiritually discouraged and confused.

2. **They did not understand the nature of the Messiah.** They expected a triumphant redeemer, not a suffering one. Jesus was just "a great prophet" to them—one figure among many, no different in kind from John the Baptist or any other spiritual teacher.

3. **They had not read the prophets properly.** They missed what Scripture clearly revealed about the coming suffering Messiah.

Jesus' solution was revolutionary:

> "Beginning with Moses and all the Prophets, he explained to them what was said in all the Scriptures concerning himself."

By "Moses and all the Prophets," Jesus referred to the entire Hebrew Bible—the Torah, the Prophets, and the Writings (the full TANAK). He wasn't adding new information; He was opening their eyes to what had always been there. The Old Testament is fundamentally about one Person: Jesus Christ.

## A New Understanding (28-35)

As they approached Emmaus, Jesus acted as though He would continue on. But the disciples urged Him to stay with them—the day was nearly over. When they sat at table together, something extraordinary happened:

> "When he was at the table with them, he took bread, gave thanks, broke it and began to give it to them. Then their eyes were opened and they recognized him, and he disappeared from their sight."

Notice: it wasn't the breaking of bread itself that opened their eyes, as if by magic. Rather, as they reflected on everything Jesus had explained to them from Scripture, recognition suddenly dawned:

> "Were not our hearts burning within us while he talked with us on the road and opened the Scriptures to us?"

**The understanding was the turning point, not the miracle.**

## The Same Understanding Repeated (36-43; 44-45)

This pattern of incomprehension followed by Scriptural explanation was repeated with the eleven and those gathered with them. Again, Jesus appeared and they were terrified, thinking they saw a ghost. Again, Jesus pointed them to the physical reality—"Look at my hands and my feet. Touch me and see"—and even ate fish in their presence to prove His bodily resurrection.

Yet still they struggled to believe. So once more, Jesus returned to Scripture:

> "This is what I told you while I was still with you: Everything must be fulfilled that is written about me in the Law of Moses, the Prophets and the Psalms."

Finally, in verse 45:

> "Then he opened their minds so they could understand the Scriptures."

## The Central Application: Know the Old Testament

The entire Bible is the story of one Person. This is what biblical theology teaches: **the theology of the Bible is Jesus Christ.** 

When you read Genesis and encounter Noah—you're reading about Jesus. When you study Leviticus with its elaborate sacrificial system—that's about Jesus. When you read about King David—you're reading about Jesus. From Malachi's final promise to the Psalms' prayers of the suffering righteous one, the Old Testament points to Christ.

Consider what the Bible is *not*:
- **Not a moral textbook**, though it addresses morality
- **Not a self-help manual**, though it speaks to our decisions
- **Not a fortune-teller's guide**, though it speaks of our future
- **Not primarily motivational**, though it inspires
- **Most importantly: not about you**, though it certainly affects you—**it is about Jesus**

Scripture points to Christ through multiple channels:

1. **Direct mention** of Jesus Christ
2. **Typology**—types and shadows like the lamb, the temple, the priesthood
3. **Predictive prophecy**—explicit predictions of His coming and suffering
4. **Preparatory work**—the sacrificial system pointing to His ultimate sacrifice; the law serving as a tutor leading us to Him
5. **Reflective of Christ's work**—passages about grace, forgiveness, and redemption that echo His redemptive work
6. **Resultant of Christ's work**—our ability to approach God in prayer, our standing before Him, our transformation

**This week, as you read Scripture, work harder to ask: How does this point to Jesus?** Don't settle for five minutes of surface reading. Set aside additional time to sit with the text and work out its Christological meaning. Read consecutively for narrative context. This requires commitment, but it unlocks the Bible's true power.

## The Resurrection: More Than a Miracle

We must understand something critical: **the power of the resurrection does not lie in its miraculous nature.** Yes, Christ rose from the dead after three days—that is miraculous. But many ancient myths speak of immortality and resurrection. People have been medically resuscitated after brief periods of clinical death.

**The significance of Christ's resurrection is that it fulfills Old Testament prophecy.** This is why Jesus consistently pointed His disciples to Scripture rather than just to the empty tomb.

In Luke 16, Abraham tells the rich man in Hades: "They have Moses and the Prophets; let them listen to them." When the rich man protests that his relatives won't believe unless someone rises from the dead, Abraham's response is telling:

> "If they do not listen to Moses and the Prophets, they will not be convinced even if someone rises from the dead."

**Belief in Scripture is sufficient.** You need not be a personal eyewitness to the resurrection. You may never encounter the risen Jesus face-to-face in this age. But if you have confidence in Scripture—if the Bible raises no fundamental doubts in your mind—then that is enough. You can trust in Jesus without requiring a resurrection appearance.

## What's Next? (46-53)

The fulfillment of one prophecy triggers the beginning of another:

> "This is what is written: The Messiah will suffer and rise from the dead on the third day, and repentance for the forgiveness of sins will be preached in his name to all nations, beginning at Jerusalem."

With the resurrection, death itself has been dealt with. Forgiveness of sins is now possible. And the disciples are commissioned as witnesses:

> "You are witnesses of these things. I am going to send you what my Father has promised; but stay in the city until you have been clothed with power from on high."

After His blessing, Jesus ascended into heaven. The disciples returned to Jerusalem with great joy and remained continually at the temple, praising God—waiting for the Holy Spirit who would empower them to carry the message to all nations.

## The Commitment We Need

Jesus returned to have a Bible study with His disciples. Not entertainment, not mere fellowship, but the opening of Scripture. If you claim to follow Jesus, this must shape your commitment too.

- **Commit to studying Scripture together.** Don't come to church for games and entertainment—they're available elsewhere. Come to be equipped, to read God's Word, to grow in understanding.
- **Prepare beforehand.** Read the passage in advance. Ask genuine questions that help the discussion, not clever ones designed to impress.
- **Meet one-on-one with others.** As Jesus opened the Scriptures with His disciples, so we should open them with one another. If you've been coming for some time and never had someone walk you through the Bible personally, speak with us. We're eager to do this.
- **Maintain commitment through seasons.** Camps are wonderful, but afterward, commit again to another term of studying God's Word together.

## The King Has Come—Will You Be Ready?

He came. He was rejected. He rose on the third day according to Scripture. He appeared to His disciples, opened their eyes through Scripture, and ascended to heaven. And He promised to return.

The question for each of us is: **Will you be ready when He returns again?**

That readiness begins now, with opening your Bible and believing what it says about Jesus. The key has been given to us. Let us use it.
