# Amos 4:1–5:17

**Title:** Judgment on the Wealthy
**Date:** 22 November 2015
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Our Wealth and Responsibility

We Australians live in extraordinary privilege. According to research by KPMG, Australia commands the resources of an entire continent with just 24 million people—a claim no other nation, not even America, can make. We should be a wealthy people for generations to come.

This wealth is reflected in our health too. Our diseases are those of affluence: heart disease, diabetes, and colon cancer from rich foods; dementia and Alzheimer's from living longer; lung cancer from smoking. We live insulated from the diseases of poverty that ravage developing nations. When disease does strike us, it often arrives with migrants from poorer countries.

With such wealth comes tremendous responsibility before God. Amos speaks directly to us as modern Australians living in comfort while others suffer.

## The Empty Religion of the Wealthy (Amos 4:4–5:4)

> Hear this word, you cows of Bashan on Mount Samaria, you women who oppress the poor and crush the needy and say to your husbands, "Bring us some drinks!"

The prophet speaks with cutting sarcasm. These wealthy women—and by extension, the comfortable class—have constructed an empty religion. They maintain the external forms of worship while their hearts remain hardened toward justice. Around Sydney we see grand Anglican buildings, carefully maintained yet spiritually hollow. They exist because the wealthy keep them standing, not because anyone truly believes.

When the Lord tells them, "Go to Bethel and sin; go to Gilgal and sin yet more," this is not permission but prophecy. It is His way of saying: "Continue in your false religion. Bring your sacrifices every morning, your tithes every three years. Burn leavened bread and brag about your freewill offerings." The rhetoric cuts deep—they love their religion precisely because it allows them to feel righteous while ignoring injustice.

But false religion carries a price. The Lord has sworn:

> The time will surely come when you will be taken away with hooks, the last of you with fishhooks. You will each go straight out through breaches in the wall, and you will be cast out toward Harmon.

This is the consequence of worship without repentance, of ritual without righteousness.

## Suffering as God's Megaphone (Amos 4:6–5:3)

> I gave you empty stomachs in every city and lack of bread in every town, yet you have not returned to me.

God repeatedly sends trials to wake us from our spiritual slumber. He withholds rain. He sends plagues and crop failures. He strikes down the young and fills the camps with stench. Each time, the refrain echoes: "Yet you have not returned to me."

This reflects a principle found in Romans 1: God does not force obedience. When we persistently ignore Him, He allows us to experience the natural consequences of our rebellion. This is not permission to sin—it is punishment. God gives us over to the pathway we have chosen so we might finally feel the weight of our choices and turn back.

Suffering, then, is God's megaphone to a deaf people. It is His way of saying: "You are in error. The path you are walking leads to destruction. Return to me."

The question becomes personal: How far down must you go before you learn? How deep into addiction, failure, and disappointment must you sink before you recognize your need? The wise person learns early; the stubborn person must be broken.

Consider the gift of pain. Dr. Paul Brand, a missionary physician, discovered that leprosy's terrible deformities did not come from the disease itself but from the loss of pain sensation. Patients without pain would unknowingly injure themselves—burning their hands, stepping on broken bones, abrading their skin—until gangrene set in. Pain, Brand realized, is a gift. It warns us of danger and protects our bodies. A person without pain is, in many ways, a person without hope.

So too with the pain of consequences. It is God's mercy speaking. When we refuse to listen to His word, He allows us to experience the logical outcome of our choices. This is not cruelty—it is love. It is the only way to wake us from the dream that we can live well without Him.

## The Call to Return (Amos 5:4–17)

But there is hope. Before judgment falls, God extends an invitation:

> Seek me and live; do not seek Bethel, do not go to Gilgal, do not journey to Beersheba. For Gilgal will surely go into exile, and Bethel will be reduced to nothing. Seek the Lord and live, or he will sweep through the tribes of Joseph like a fire.

The false centers of worship—Bethel, Gilgal, Beersheba—will fall. But God Himself remains. To seek Him is to find life.

Amos then identifies the specific sins of the wealthy:

> There are those who turn justice into bitterness and cast righteousness to the ground.

The wealthy have perverted the courts. They oppress the innocent and take bribes. They impose taxes on the poor's meager grain while building stone mansions for themselves—mansions they will never inhabit. They have forgotten that justice is not optional; it is the foundation of any society that claims to follow God.

Yet judgment is not inevitable. There remains a path forward:

> Seek good, not evil, that you may live. Then the Lord God Almighty will be with you. Hate evil, love good; maintain justice in the courts. Perhaps the Lord God Almighty will have mercy on the remnant of Joseph.

Notice the word "perhaps." God does not guarantee mercy to those who refuse to change. But He holds out the possibility. The door remains open. Repentance is always possible.

## The Choice Before Us

We face a choice as a wealthy nation. History shows us two paths: nations that embrace Christian principles and justice flourish; nations that abandon God and embrace atheism or false religion crumble from within. The Soviet Union debunked the promise of atheism through its own collapse and brutality. Other nations continue their own descent.

Some might ask: Why should we give up our comfortable false religion? Why abandon the ways of the world that have served us well?

The answer is simple: because you already know where that path leads. If you have walked it before, you recognize its ugliness. Sin may glitter for a moment, but it always ends in heartache, failure, and disappointment. God offers something better—not comfort, necessarily, but meaning, purpose, and the peace of knowing we are right with Him.

The Pilgrims understood this. They fled to the New World in search of religious freedom, willing to endure hardship for the sake of worshiping God rightly. Their first winter killed half their number. Yet one man—Squanto, a Patuxet who had been kidnapped, enslaved, freed, and trained in London—returned to guide them. Against all odds, he became the bridge between the settlers and the native peoples, helping establish a peace that lasted fifty years.

Governor William Bradford recognized this as God's hand. Squanto was "a special instrument sent of God," a living answer to the Pilgrims' prayers. When Squanto died, he left his possessions to the Pilgrims as "remembrances of his love." From tragedy and loss came unexpected blessing.

This is what Thanksgiving celebrates: God's provision for those who seek Him faithfully.

## A Question for Our Time

We live in an age of judgment—or at least, we think we do. We judge each other constantly. On social media, we declare who is right and who is wrong about marriage, taxation, immigration, and every other issue. We become offended. We swear. We demand peace while practicing condemnation.

But maybe our real problem is that we have lost the ability to make righteous judgments at all. To gain knowledge requires judgment. We must be willing to say: "I believe you are wrong, and I believe I am right." We must also be willing to examine ourselves and admit: "I was wrong. You were right."

Without judgment, there is no conflict—but also no growth, no justice, no truth. The question is not whether we judge, but whether we judge rightly.

## The Urgent Call

Prepare to meet your Maker. This is not a threat but an invitation. God has already prepared a way for us to meet Him—through repentance, through faith, through the pursuit of justice and mercy.

The choice is ours. Will we learn from our suffering? Will we turn from our false religions and our comfortable compromises? Will we seek God and live?

Or will we wait until judgment falls, until our comfortable houses become empty tombs, until our vineyards produce no wine?

The time to return is now.
