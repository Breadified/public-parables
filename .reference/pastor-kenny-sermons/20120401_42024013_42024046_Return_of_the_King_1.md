# Luke 24:13-24:46

**Title:** Return of the King
**Date:** 1 April 2012 (SMAC) / 27 April 2025 (ARISE)
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction: Interpreting Scripture

The Reformation gave us a profound principle: *"even the ploughboy can interpret Scripture."* This wasn't arrogance but a response to the papal claim that only special clergy could authoritatively explain God's Word. When the Bible became widely available and people began reading it, they discovered the Pope and his priests could be wrong. The Church needed reformation, and the reformers recovered a vital truth—Scripture is accessible to ordinary believers who approach it with diligence and humility.

Today we explore how Jesus himself modeled Scripture interpretation and what that teaches us about knowing Him.

---

## 1. The Problem: Not Recognizing Jesus (Luke 24:13-24)

On resurrection day, two disciples walked toward Emmaus, about seven miles from Jerusalem—roughly a two- to three-hour journey. They were discussing the extraordinary events of recent days: their master crucified, buried, and now rumors of a missing body.

> 13 Now that same day two of them were going to a village called Emmaus, about seven miles from Jerusalem. 14 They were talking with each other about everything that had happened.

Their discussion was intense—repeating four times throughout this passage: discussing, reasoning, arguing, disputing. Yet their arguments yielded no real answers. Then, remarkably, Jesus joined them unrecognized.

> 15 As they talked and discussed these things with each other, Jesus himself came up and walked along with them; 16 but they were kept from recognizing him. 17 He asked them, "What are you discussing together as you walk along?" They stood still, their faces downcast.

The Greek word for "recognizing" is *epignōnai*—to know deeply. To them, Jesus was a stranger. The irony cuts deep: they didn't know the One they'd been discussing.

### Their Knowledge—and Blindness

The disciples possessed facts about Jesus:

> 19 "About Jesus of Nazareth," they replied. "He was a prophet, powerful in word and deed before God and all the people. 20 The chief priests and our rulers handed him over to be sentenced to death, and they crucified him;

They knew He was a prophet. They knew His power. They knew of His arrest and crucifixion. But knowledge of events didn't translate to understanding His person or purpose. Their problem was deeper:

> 21 but we had hoped that he was the one who was going to redeem Israel. And what is more, it is the third day since all this took place.

They'd expected redemption—freedom from Roman oppression and sin. It was the third day. Surely something should have happened by now. Instead:

> 22 In addition, some of our women amazed us. They went to the tomb early this morning 23 but didn't find his body. They came and told us that they had seen a vision of angels, who said he was alive. 24 Then some of our companions went to the tomb and found it just as the women had said, but they did not see Jesus.

No body. Rumors of resurrection. But no Jesus. No redemption. No evidence they could touch and verify. They had information but not understanding.

## 2. The Answer: Scripture Reveals Jesus (Luke 24:25-27)

Then Jesus rebuked them:

> 25 He said to them, "How foolish you are, and how slow to believe all that the prophets have spoken! 26 Did not the Messiah have to suffer these things and then enter his glory?"

He calls them "foolish"—not for lacking magical powers but for failing to understand what they should have known. They didn't grasp that the Messiah would suffer *before* entering glory. They missed the full testimony of the prophets.

The solution? Scripture itself:

> 27 And beginning with Moses and all the Prophets, he explained to them what was said in all the Scriptures concerning himself.

The Greek word *diermēneusen* means "interpreted" or "hermeneutics." Jesus opened the Old Testament—from the Torah (Moses) through the Prophets, encompassing all of Scripture—and showed how it pointed to Him. Not every single verse speaks of Jesus in direct prophecy, but the scriptural narrative, read as a coherent whole, testifies to the coming Messiah who would suffer and rise.

### Theology: How It All Fits Together

Kenny's personal testimony illustrates this. As a child in Malaysia, he attended Methodist Sunday School and Catholic primary school. He heard Bible stories—crafts, Easter celebrations—but nothing made sense. No one explained how the pieces fit together. "That's called theology," he reflects. *The narrative arc of Scripture reveals God's purposes.*

Similarly, when Philip encountered the Ethiopian eunuch reading Isaiah 53, the eunuch was puzzled:

> 34 The eunuch asked Philip, "Tell me, please, who is the prophet talking about, himself or someone else?" 35 Then Philip began with that very passage of Scripture and told him the good news about Jesus.

The eunuch needed interpretation. He needed someone to show him that this suffering servant passage pointed to Jesus—that Jesus is the climax and fulfillment of all Scripture has written.

## 3. Recognition Through Scripture: A New Understanding (Luke 24:28-32)

As they approached Emmaus, Jesus made as if to continue on:

> 28 As they approached the village to which they were going, Jesus continued on as if he were going farther. 29 But they urged him strongly, "Stay with us, for it is nearly evening; the day is almost over." So he went in to stay with them. 30 When he was at the table with them, he took bread, gave thanks, broke it and began to give it to them. 31 Then their eyes were opened and they recognized him, and he disappeared from their sight.

What opened their eyes? Many assume miraculous revelation at the breaking of bread. But verse 32 tells us what actually happened:

> 32 They asked each other, "Were not our hearts burning within us while he talked with us on the road and opened the Scriptures to us?"

Their hearts burned with understanding as Jesus *explained the Scriptures*. The opening of their eyes followed the opening of Scripture. Recognition came through theological comprehension—seeing how Jesus fulfills God's promises.

## 4. The Pattern Repeats: From Doubt to Understanding (Luke 24:36-45)

The disciples returned to Jerusalem with joy and reported the encounter. But when Jesus appeared to the Eleven, confusion and doubt returned:

> 36 While they were still talking about this, Jesus himself stood among them and said to them, "Peace be with you." 37 They were startled and frightened, thinking they saw a ghost. 38 He said to them, "Why are you troubled, and why do doubts rise in your minds? 39 Look at my hands and my feet. It is I myself! Touch me and see; a ghost does not have flesh and bones, as you see I have."

Even seeing the risen Jesus in the flesh, touching His hands and feet:

> 41 And while they still did not believe it because of joy and amazement, he asked them, "Do you have anything here to eat?" 42 They gave him a piece of broiled fish, 43 and he took it and ate it in their presence.

Jesus proved He had a physical body. Yet belief still wavered. So once again, He taught them Scripture:

> 44 He said to them, "This is what I told you while I was still with you: Everything must be fulfilled that is written about me in the Law of Moses, the Prophets and the Psalms."

And finally:

> 45 Then he opened their minds so they could understand the Scriptures.

Understanding came through Scripture, not primarily through miraculous signs.

### Why the Resurrection Matters

This is crucial. We often think the power of the resurrection lies in its miraculous character—a man rising from death, an incredible supernatural feat. But people are revived medically. Miracles alone don't guarantee belief.

The true significance of the resurrection is *prophetic fulfillment*. Jesus' rising from the dead proves that God is faithful to His Word. It validates all Scripture has promised. When Abraham rejected the rich man's plea that a resurrection would convince his brothers, his answer was stark:

> 31 "He said to him, 'If they do not listen to Moses and the Prophets, they will not be convinced even if someone rises from the dead.'"

Truth convinces. Scripture convinces. Not miracles alone, but the truthfulness of God demonstrated through fulfilled prophecy.

Believers in the Old Testament never saw Jesus in the flesh, yet they trusted Him, looking forward to an unseen Messiah. We look back to remember an unseen Messiah. When we know Him through Scripture, we are empowered to carry out His purposes.

---

## 5. The Task: Proclaim and Witness (Luke 24:46-53)

Understanding Scripture leads to action:

> 46 He told them, "This is what is written: The Messiah will suffer and rise from the dead on the third day, 47 and repentance for the forgiveness of sins will be preached in his name to all nations, beginning at Jerusalem.

Jesus as Savior offers forgiveness. Jesus as Lord commands that this message extend to all nations—a multi-generational task. The gospel isn't merely about personal salvation; it's about the lordship of Christ over all things. Scripture speaks to morality, decisions, the future, history, science, and politics.

> 48 You are witnesses of these things. 49 I am going to send you what my Father has promised; but stay in the city until you have been clothed with power from on high.

The disciples are brought into the story. The power promised isn't for comfort or self-fulfillment but for service. Jesus has ascended; the work continues through His people.

> 50 When he had led them out to the vicinity of Bethany, he lifted up his hands and blessed them. 51 While he was blessing them, he left them and was taken up into heaven. 52 Then they worshiped him and returned to Jerusalem with great joy. 53 And they stayed continually at the temple, praising God.

The Acts narrative will unfold what happens next. But this conclusion is clear: understanding Scripture produces worship, joy, and faithful witness.

## Application: Study and Explain Scripture Together

Remarkably, when Jesus rose from the dead, the first thing He did was conduct a Bible study. He didn't call for a celebration or dwell on the miraculous nature of His resurrection. He opened Scripture.

The principle of "the ploughboy can interpret Scripture" needs clarification. It doesn't mean that no instruction is needed or that mentors are unnecessary. Individualism—figuring everything out from scratch alone—misses the point. Rather, it means that with effort and faculties intact, there is no supernatural barrier to understanding. Interpretation requires explanation and teaching. Scripture has been passed down to us; we pass it to others.

**This demands commitment to Bible study.** If you want to know Jesus and live rightly, you must:

- **Open the Bible.** Take initiative. If you've attended church but never studied Scripture seriously, now is the time.
- **Study together.** Explanation and teaching happen best in community. Join a study group. Discuss, reason through difficult passages, and apply Scripture to life.
- **Let Scripture shape your vision.** Our life's aim is not comfort or career fulfillment according to the world's script. We follow God's purposes, which Scripture reveals.

We can know Jesus. We can understand how to live rightly. The risen King has shown us the way—through the opened Scriptures and opened hearts.
