# Ecclesiastes 4:1-16

**Title:** Two Is Better Than One
**Date:** 11 May 2014
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction: Work and Relationships

Mother's Day reminds us to honor the women who have shaped our families and society. Yet the irony is striking—we celebrate motherhood on a day that's already a holiday, so we don't have to take actual time away from work. This reveals something deeper about our culture: work has begun to eclipse relationships, including family bonds.

The Book of Ecclesiastes explores this tension with remarkable realism. Solomon, who achieved everything—he was king, built massive projects, expanded trade and diplomacy, and made his nation exceptionally wealthy and influential—concluded that it was all meaningless, a chasing after the wind. Why? Because his work sprang from envy, not purpose. Today we'll look at what Solomon teaches about finding the right balance between work and relationships.

## Part 1: One Is Better Than Two—The Three Attitudes to Work

> And I saw that all labor and all achievement spring from man's envy of his neighbor. This too is meaningless, a chasing after the wind. (Ecclesiastes 4:4)

Solomon identifies three problematic attitudes toward work: the lazy, the crazy, and the daily.

### a. The Lazy: Folded Hands

> The fool folds his hands and ruins himself. (Ecclesiastes 4:5)

Folded hands symbolize laziness and idleness. The Book of Proverbs, also written by Solomon, describes the sluggard—someone characterized by refusal to work, endless excuses, love of sleep, and wasted time. The sluggard even believes he is wise, though he is a fool. Like Newton's first law of motion, a lazy person tends to remain at rest, unable to overcome inertia.

For some of us, this becomes a serious problem: the inability to overcome inertia when we need to, always late, always failing to complete assignments, never taking responsibility but blaming circumstances instead—the school, the teachers, the weather, everyone but ourselves.

Scripture is serious about this. Proverbs says:

> The sluggard's craving will be the death of him, because his hands refuse to work. (Proverbs 21:25)

In a subsistence economy, no work meant hunger and starvation. The medieval church listed sloth as one of the seven deadly sins precisely because it leads to death. Paul reinforces this:

> If a man will not work, he shall not eat. (2 Thessalonians 3:10)

If laziness doesn't lead to physical death, it leads to deathly habits. An unwilling person becomes a stealer—stealing from someone else's productivity by not contributing. When we don't pay our bills, we are stealing.

The solution is not idleness but obedience:

> He who has been stealing must steal no longer, but must work, doing something useful with his own hands, that he may have something to share with those in need. (Ephesians 4:28)

The work doesn't need to be prestigious or highly paid. Even wiping floors at a fast-food restaurant is honorable when done with your own hands. The key is doing something useful and being willing to contribute.

### b. The Crazy: Two Handfuls with Toil

> Better one handful with tranquility than two handfuls with toil. (Ecclesiastes 4:6)

On the other extreme are those with two hands constantly grasping—the greedy, the envious, the overworked. They grab everything they can reach, but at enormous cost.

In Japan, this has become so severe that they have a word for it: *karoshi*—death from overwork. There are as many as 30,000 victims each year. Since 1990, the Japanese government has been forced to provide restitution to karoshi widows. The government conducted studies confirming what should be obvious: employees cannot work twelve or more hours a day, six or seven days a week, year after year, without suffering physically and mentally.

In Australia, studies show that workers complete more than two billion hours of unpaid overtime annually—$72 billion in foregone wages. Why this toil?

Sometimes it's necessary—a season for new graduates needing to prove themselves, or a particular deadline. But some of us have chosen this crazy life because of envy. We want to look better than our neighbors, to outdo them, to have what they have.

> And I saw that all labor and all achievement spring from man's envy of his neighbor. This too is meaningless, a chasing after the wind. (Ecclesiastes 4:4)

Consider the absurdity: nations compete to build the tallest buildings—the Petronas Towers, Taipei 101, Dubai—for bragging rights that last two months. Meaningless. China builds empty cities so councils look good on paper. Meaningless. Billionaires wear $1,300 track pants with a designer label—the same style you can buy at a discount store for $20—just to display status. Meaningless.

If you overwork to impress others, you're playing a losing game. Your neighbor overworks. You both outduel each other in stress and exhaustion, both taking sick leave, both losing. In Japan, employees work long hours not for productivity but for *appearance*—to look good in front of coworkers. It's meaningless and unprofitable.

The housing crisis illustrates this vividly. A generation ago, families saw their neighbors buy bigger houses and felt envious. To afford the bigger house, both parents had to work. But then every other couple saw this and wanted the same thing, so they too bought bigger houses on double incomes. Now house prices have doubled, and every family works two incomes just to stay in the same place. Meanwhile, the extra toil and stress have followed. Children don't have their mothers at home. Care gets outsourced to strangers. Families become chaotic from exhaustion. Sunday gets consumed with catching up on chores, so we can't go to church.

For what? So we can have more? So we can look good to our neighbors? So our children can have two hobbies instead of one, two languages instead of one, two rooms with televisions? Is two handfuls with toil really worth it?

### c. The Daily: One Handful with Contentment

> Better one handful with tranquility. (Ecclesiastes 4:6)

This doesn't mean literally grabbing with one hand instead of two. Rather, it represents contentment—one hand full and the other saying "enough" and "no thank you." The word *tranquility* means peace and quietness.

> Make it your ambition to lead a quiet life, to mind your own business and to work with your hands, just as we told you, so that your daily life may win the respect of outsiders and so that you will not be dependent on anybody. (1 Thessalonians 4:11-12)

Don't be lazy. Don't be frantically crazy. Just look to the daily. Daily work wins respect—not through envy but through integrity. Daily work provides for yourself and for others.

We pray for our daily bread. We work for our daily needs. That's ultimately why we are working. In obedience to God, we work to meet the daily needs of those around us—our families, our churches, our friends, our communities, those in need.

Notice how different this is from working for meaning, for satisfaction, for significance, for gain, for status, for envy. We work for the daily. We choose contentment over riches. We choose one over two.

**A Word to Young Women**

Young women are often pressured to work outside the home because of envy—the envy of men. Men get careers, corporate lunches, suits, and status. Why can't women have the same? The grass looks greener on the other side.

But there may be a season when caring for family is the calling. No one pretends that changing nappies is glamorous or feels significant in the way a career feels significant. But it is important to God and to those you serve.

**A Word to Young Men**

It might be easier for your future wives if men didn't make such a big deal out of work. When we're asked "What do you do?", our answer is our occupation—we define ourselves by our career. But consider what we really do: provide the daily necessities for different layers of society. You bring home the bacon. Or in your cases, the charsiu, the tieji bulgolgi.

Christians don't have careers; we have jobs.

If we look at the purpose of work rather than the status of work, then the occupation and label we're all envious over becomes meaningless. It no longer matters whether you're a lawyer, a doctor, a garbage man, a plumber, a nurse, or a carpenter. You won't be labeled that way in heaven. So why be preoccupied with those labels today, especially at the expense of relationships?

You see, relationship is better than riches. Two is better than one.

## Part 2: Two Is Better Than One—The Value of Community

> Again I saw something meaningless under the sun: There was a man all alone; he had neither son nor brother. There was no end to his toil, yet his eyes were not content with his wealth. "For whom am I toiling," he asked, "and why am I depriving myself of enjoyment?" This too is meaningless. (Ecclesiastes 4:7-8)

This is the workaholic—the man who sees his bank account rise but is never content. He watches houses rise from the ground, his position climb the corporate ladder, his portfolio grow, and still he is not satisfied. The question haunts him: For whom am I toiling? Why am I depriving myself of good?

Paul Ramsay, Australia's healthcare tycoon and the 11th richest man in Australia, died recently. He was never married and had no heirs—just an impersonal charity to receive his $3 billion. For whom was he toiling?

Working to serve others is good. Obeying God in different seasons of life is good. Fearing the Lord is good. But working obsessively while isolated is meaningless.

Having one handful leaves the other hand free—free to serve others, free to give. We are not to envy our neighbor but to help our neighbor.

### Two Are Better Than One

> Two are better than one, because they have a good return for their work. If one falls down, his friend can help him up. But pity the man who falls and has no one to help him up! Also, if two lie down together, they will keep warm. But how can one keep warm alone? Though one may be overpowered, two can defend themselves. (Ecclesiastes 4:9-12)

The benefits of community are practical and profound:

- **Work together produces better results.** Partnership multiplies effectiveness—think of Batman and Robin, the concept of a wingman.
- **Mutual support when we fall.** Life knocks us down; we need friends to help us up. The man who falls alone has no one.
- **Shared warmth.** Two lying down together keep warm. One alone shivers in the cold.
- **Collective strength.** One person can be overpowered; two can defend themselves.

Isn't it clear that relationship—family and friendship—is better than wealth? Yet we're losing this in our pursuit of wealth and status.

### The Loneliness of Our Culture

There is great loneliness in the modern world:

- Our houses are sophisticated with automatic garage doors so we can drive straight inside, never interacting with neighbors.
- Our apartments are locked with advanced security systems, keeping others out.
- We have bigger houses but no common rooms, just bigger closets, gyms, and entertainment rooms.
- Voluntary associations—charities run by neighbors, community canteens run by mothers—have declined. Governments and paid professionals now do what communities once did together.
- Gathering places have disappeared. Coffee shops where people once sat together now have signs discouraging conversation.
- Sit-down restaurants have given way to fast food. Instead of going out to meet people, we sit exhausted in our entertainment rooms watching reruns of *Friends* instead of actually having friends.
- We watch *How I Met Your Mother* at work instead of having time to actually meet our future spouses.

For whom are you toiling? Is it worth sacrificing relationships for the things the world promises but can never deliver? It's a wild goose chase without a goose. It's a rat race where the only winners are rats.

It is meaningless.

### A Cord of Three Strands

> A cord of three strands is not quickly broken. (Ecclesiastes 4:12)

The language shifts from ones and twos to three. It's as though Solomon is saying: it's not just that two is better than one—three is better than one. In fact, four, five, six, seven, eight is better than one. All of us together are better than one.

That's what we're trying to do here at church: a community of Christians looking out for each other and for those not yet part of us. Friends are wonderful. I didn't have many friends in high school, but church became my friends—I was welcomed, looked after, had great conversations, and felt I belonged somewhere.

Relationships aren't perfect. They can be limited, even meaningless sometimes (which may be what the rest of chapter 4 explores). But we have an opportunity today to welcome each other, to encourage each other to stick with Christ, and to invite others to join us. It's a time to be content and not worry about working more or studying more. Instead, invest in relationships.

It's a time to remember that Christ died for his friends—us—to make us into one family. Jesus has brought us together.

## Conclusion: Contentment Over Commercialization

Anna Jarvis founded Mother's Day to honor mothers and motherhood, not profit. But she soon became resentful as companies commercialized the holiday. By the 1920s, Hallmark and others were selling Mother's Day cards. Jarvis became so embittered by what she saw as exploitation that she protested, organized boycotts, and even tried to rescind the holiday she had fought so hard to establish. She crashed a candy makers' convention and was arrested for disturbing the peace—all because the holiday had become about profit, not sentiment.

This Mother's Day, remember your mothers and all your relationships rather than the commercialization and grabbing that pervades our culture.

**One is better than two. Two is better than one.**
