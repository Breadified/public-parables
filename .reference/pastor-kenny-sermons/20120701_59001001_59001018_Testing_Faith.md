# James 1:1-18

**Title:** Testing Faith  
**Date:** 18 July 2012  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction

Have you ever faced a situation where your trust in Jesus and God's goodness felt tested? Not necessarily persecution, but the plain trials of life—struggles at home, relationship conflicts, academic pressure, unemployment, chronic illness, or loss. We don't like these trials. We simply want them over.

Years ago, a friend from Bible college experienced profound tragedy. His wife was pregnant with twins, due to be induced on a Saturday at 1pm. At noon, she began bleeding profusely. The babies were stillborn, suffocating from lack of oxygen before they were even due to be born—an hour before their scheduled arrival. At the funeral, he described it simply: "A bandit came into my home, stole my babies, leaving a puddle of blood behind." His faith was being tested in the most unbearable way.

The question that haunts us: How are we to respond to trials, suffering, disaster, and tragedy when they come our way? James gives us three surprising truths.

## 1. God's Testing Leads to Life

### Enjoy Your Trials (1:2)

James writes something that seems almost shocking:

> Consider it pure joy, my brothers, whenever you face trials of many kinds.

Joy? In the midst of trials? This seems incomprehensible until we understand that we are complex creatures, capable of holding multiple emotional states at once. Even in disappointment, frustration, and sadness, we can find joy—because trials serve a good purpose.

### Trials Produce Perseverance (1:3-4)

The testing of your faith develops perseverance. The word for testing (dokimos) is the same word used to verify the genuineness of something. Just as testing separates quality products from duds, suffering develops our spiritual endurance—our ability to keep trusting God and obeying Him no matter what circumstances surround us.

But perseverance must finish its work. It must have its full effect to accomplish God's goal:

> Perseverance must finish its work so that you may be mature and complete, not lacking anything.

Consider the gym. If your goal is building muscle, exercise is the test. But the testing must continue. The most tempting moment to stop is when it starts to hurt—but that's precisely when the old muscle breaks down and new strength builds. That's the very reason you started. If you quit, you don't complete.

Or consider a course of antibiotics. If you don't persevere to complete the full treatment, the medication never achieves its full effect. The cold returns.

God's method is not strange when we see it elsewhere in life. He has determined that perseverance—steadfastness under pressure—is how He molds us into maturity and completeness. How He shapes us to be like His Son.

### Ask God for Wisdom (1:5)

Instead of giving up, we can ask for help:

> If any of you lacks wisdom, he should ask God, who gives generously to all without finding fault, and it will be given to him.

God is not stingy or judgmental. He is entirely approachable. The wisdom we need is the ability to navigate the trial, to grow in perseverance, to reach maturity and completeness. God will provide it generously.

### Better Than Those Left Without Trial (1:9-12)

James then makes a counterintuitive statement. In his context, the church faced conflict between rich and poor Christians. The poor were oppressed; the rich took advantage. James says:

> The brother in humble circumstances ought to take pride in his high position.

Why? Because the poor believer who perseveres will become mature and complete, lacking nothing—united with Christ. He has been chosen for an exalted position. This is something to be proud of.

> But the one who is rich should take pride in his low position, because he will pass away like a wild flower. For the sun rises with scorching heat and withers the plant; its blossom falls and its beauty is destroyed.

The wealthy, comfortable, untested—they fade away like flowers in scorching heat. Without resistance, without trial, they atrophy spiritually. Like muscles weakened by weightlessness in space, they collapse when returned to earth.

Those who persevere through testing become strong. Those who avoid all difficulty become brittle.

> Blessed is the man who perseveres under trial, because when he has stood the test, he will receive the crown of life that God has promised to those who love him.

The crown—that ancient symbol of victory, of triumph—is life itself. Eternal life. The crown of life is another word for salvation, for heaven. And it is promised to those who endure.

The writer of Hebrews echoes this:

> Endure hardship as discipline; God is treating you as sons. For what son is not disciplined by his father? Moreover, we have all had human fathers who disciplined us and we respected them for it. How much more should we submit to the Father of our spirits and live! No discipline seems pleasant at the time, but painful. Later on, however, it produces a harvest of righteousness and peace for those who have been trained by it.

### Application: Don't Give Up the Crown

Joni Eareckson Tada has been a quadriplegic for nearly five decades, paralyzed from a diving accident in her youth. She lives with chronic pain. Recently, she was diagnosed with breast cancer. Hers is a tough, tested life. Yet she writes:

> Only God knows why I was paralyzed. Maybe He knew I'd be ultimately happier serving Him. If I were still on my feet, it's hard to say how things have gone. I probably would have drifted through life—marriage, maybe even divorce—dissatisfied and disillusioned. When I was in high school, I reacted to life selfishly and never built on any long-lasting values, and almost always at the expense of others.

The trial revealed what really mattered. It refined her.

John Calvin observed: "If we are given gold, would we not test it to determine its value? If we doubted its genuineness, we would test it by fire. And so God with us. The fire of affliction reveals the quality of our faith."

Here's the hard truth: we are not good at perseverance. We lose the war of attrition. This is especially true in comfortable societies like Australia, where life is too easy. We subcontract everything. We demand instant gratification—apps, credit cards, immediate results. We've lost the discipline of waiting.

If you're not currently facing a trial, find ways to practice endurance. Delay gratification. Choose the harder path sometimes. Keep your commitments even when it's inconvenient. Maintain your disciplines—your Bible reading, your church attendance, your responsibilities—especially when everything in you wants relief.

God wants a resilient people who remain steadfast when times grow hard.

C.S. Lewis, in *The Screwtape Letters*, captures how Satan views perseverance:

> He leaves the creature to stand up on its own legs—to carry out from the will alone duties which have lost all relish. It is during such trough periods, much more than during the peak periods, that it is growing into the sort of creature He wants it to be. Do not be deceived, Wormwood, our cause is never more in danger than when a human, no longer desiring, but still intending, to do our Enemy's will, looks round upon a universe from which every trace of Him seems to have vanished, and asks why he has been forsaken, and still obeys.

That is when true faith is built. When you obey not because you feel good, but because you have committed yourself to God's will.

## 2. Testing God Leads to Death

### Testing God (1:6-8)

But there is a reciprocal danger. While God's testing of us leads to life, testing God leads to death.

> But when he asks, he must believe and not doubt, because he who doubts is like a wave of the sea, blown and tossed by the wind. That man should not think he will receive anything from the Lord; he is a double-minded man, unstable in all he does.

When we ask God for wisdom to endure trials, we must ask in faith—believing that God will do what is good for us. Doubting here doesn't mean asking honest questions or struggling with confusion. It means being suspicious of God's intentions. It means believing He's out to get you. Such a person is double-minded, wavering between loyalty to God and loyalty to self-protection.

You cannot expect God to give you the wisdom to persevere while simultaneously doubting His goodness and character. The request cancels itself out.

### Desire Leads to Sin, Sin to Death (1:13-17)

Here's the crucial distinction James makes:

> When tempted, no one should say, "God is tempting me." For God cannot be tempted by evil, nor does he tempt anyone; but each one is tempted when, by his own evil desire, he is dragged away and enticed.

When you're tested, you might be tempted to blame God. "God made me poor, so it's not my fault if I'm stingy and selfish." "God gave me a terrible boss, so it's okay if I cheat the company." "God didn't give me a spouse, so it's fine to date a non-Christian." "God's system is unfair, so it's okay if I steal software."

But this thinking is a deadly trap:

> Then, after desire has conceived, it gives birth to sin; and sin, when it is full-grown, gives birth to death.

The progression is clear: temptation → desire → sin → death.

We are responsible for our own desires. They come from within us, not from God. God does not entrap us in sin. In fact, God is entirely good:

> Every good and perfect gift is from above, coming down from the Father of the heavenly lights, who does not change like shifting shadows.

God is not double-minded. He doesn't wake up merciful one day and cruel the next. He's not playing games with us like the Greek gods toying with human emotions. He is consistent, good, and trustworthy.

When trials come, you cannot justify sin by blaming your circumstances. If you sin, you've already failed in perseverance. You've chosen the path of death instead of life.

## 3. God Has Already Been Tested

### His Goodness Is Proven (1:18)

Here's why we can trust God completely:

> He chose to give us birth through the word of truth, that we might be a kind of firstfruits of all he created.

By His own will and initiative, God has given us salvation through the gospel. He has made us a new people—firstfruits of His creation—designed to love Him and do good works.

God's goodness doesn't need to be tested by us. It has already been demonstrated:

> God demonstrates his love for us in this: While we were still sinners, Christ died for us. (Romans 5:8)

Jesus Himself underwent every trial we face. He suffered. He was tested. But He did what we cannot do—He persevered to the end without wavering, without doubting God's goodness, without sin.

When Jesus cried out, "My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?"—He asked the hardest question. Yet He remained obedient on that cross. He never questioned where His loyalties lay. He never doubted His Father's character. He endured every test perfectly.

This is the God we must keep trusting, keep persevering for, no matter what circumstances life holds.

## Conclusion

Two paths lie before us:

**The path of trials and perseverance** leads to maturity, completeness, life, and the crown that God has promised.

**The path of doubt and blame** leads to double-mindedness, sin, and death.

What is your attitude tonight? When trials come, will you respond with joy and steadfastness? Or with resentment and doubt?

Will you pride yourself on the high position God has given you through testing—maturing into the likeness of Christ? Or will you fade away like a flower in the scorching sun?

Do not be deceived. God's testing leads to life. God's goodness has already been proven in Christ. Trust Him. Persevere. Receive the crown of life.
