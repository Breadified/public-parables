# 1 Corinthians 12:7-31

**Title:** Church Out of the Box: We Need Each Other

**Date:** April 26, 2020

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## The Question: Who's Most Important?

In every sphere of life—movies, hospitals, accounting firms, sports teams—we struggle with the same question: who matters most? The Corinthian church faced this exact tension. Paul's answer is radical: no one person or gift is more important than another.

## 1. Many Gifts, One Spirit (12:7-11)

Paul begins by acknowledging the sheer variety of spiritual gifts distributed throughout the church:

> To one there is given through the Spirit a message of wisdom, to another a message of knowledge by means of the same Spirit, to another faith by the same Spirit, to another gifts of healing by that one Spirit, to another miraculous powers, to another prophecy, to another distinguishing between spirits, to another speaking in different kinds of tongues, and to still another the interpretation of tongues. All these are the work of one and the same Spirit, and he distributes them to each one, just as he determines.

**The Gifts Are Speaking Gifts**

The precise meaning of some gifts—like "gifts of healing" or "working of miracles"—remains lost to history. Even church fathers in 400 AD admitted uncertainty. However, Kenny observes an important pattern: most of these gifts center on *speaking God's word*.

Notice the language:
- "Message of wisdom" 
- "Message of knowledge"
- "Prophecy"
- "Distinguishing between spirits"
- "Speaking in tongues"
- "Interpretation of tongues"

**What About Healing and Miracles?**

Some theologians argue these miraculous gifts ceased with the apostles—that signs and wonders were limited to laying the church's foundation while Scripture was being written. Since the Bible is now complete, such gifts became rare rather than characteristic of believers.

Yet other passages suggest healing operates through prayer and godly words:

> Is anyone among you sick? Let them call the elders of the church to pray over them and anoint them with oil in the name of the Lord. Therefore confess your sins to each other and pray for each other so that you may be healed. The prayer of a righteous person is powerful and effective. (James 5:14-16)

And in Proverbs: "The human spirit can endure in sickness, but a crushed spirit who can bear?" (18:14)

Similarly, Paul writes of God's power working through proclamation: "We proclaim Him, admonishing and teaching everyone with all wisdom, so that we may present everyone perfect in Christ. To this end I strenuously contend with all the energy Christ so powerfully works in me." (Colossians 1:28-29)

**Speaking Gifts in Church Today**

Think about all the ways God's word is spoken in our church:
- Sermons and teaching
- Prayers and intercession
- Bible reading aloud
- Questions asked and answered
- Comment cards and chat discussions
- Breakout room conversations
- Songs and hymns sung together
- Bible studies and small groups
- Informal conversations during fellowship
- Digital sharing over Facebook, WhatsApp, email
- Care and encouragement given through words
- Even disagreement that keeps us honest

**All of these are speakings of God's word.** All build up Christ's body. All exercise the spiritual truths given for the common good.

## 2. One Spirit, One Message (12:7-13)

The phrase "of the Spirit" appears repeatedly because Paul emphasizes *unity of message*, not just origin of gifts:

> Now to each one the manifestation of the Spirit is given for the common good.

The repeated references to "the Spirit" describe the nature of the message—that it's the same breath, the same speaking, the same good news regardless of method.

**Different messengers, same message: Jesus is Lord.**

And we need *all* of these speakings. All must speak.

### The One Body (12:12-13)

> Just as a body, though one, has many parts, but all its many parts form one body, so it is with Christ.

The body operates as a unified whole despite being made of many different parts. How did we become one body?

> For we were all baptized by one Spirit so as to form one body—whether Jews or Gentiles, slave or free—and we were all given the one Spirit to drink.

**The Meaning of Baptism**

Baptism originally meant ritual washing that identified the person with a movement—like John's baptism identified people with his message. But in Corinth, this caused division: people were baptized "into the name of Paul" or "into the name of Apollos" (1 Corinthians 1:13).

Paul corrects this:

> Christ did not send me to baptize, but to preach the gospel—not with wisdom and eloquence, lest the cross of Christ be emptied of its power. (1 Corinthians 1:17)

True baptism is spiritual—identification with Christ's lordship and the Spirit of truth. When we believed in Jesus, we were baptized into this spiritual reality. We identify with Christ as our master and are united under the same spiritual truth.

**"One Spirit to Drink"**

The phrase conveys internal transformation, echoing Jesus' promise:

> On the last and greatest day of the festival, Jesus stood and said in a loud voice, "Let anyone who is thirsty come to me and drink... By this he meant the Spirit." (John 7:37-39)

This is not external water baptism but internal transformation. The Spirit dwells within us.

**A Timely Illustration**

Just as the problem with COVID-19 is not external contamination on surfaces—it's the virus *inside* you that causes harm—so it is with sin. Greed, self-centeredness, and lust are not external problems. They're already within us. Left untreated, they destroy us spiritually.

But unlike COVID, we have been given the antidote: Christ himself. He bore the penalty of sin and took the disease upon himself, sparing us. Do you have the vaccine in you? If not, this message is for you: find life in Christ before it's too late.

## 3. All Belong (12:15-20)

> Now if the foot should say, "Because I am not a hand, I do not belong to the body," it would not for that reason stop being part of the body. And if the ear should say, "Because I am not an eye, I do not belong to the body," it would not for that reason stop being part of the body.

**The Temptation to Feel Insignificant**

Society assigns worth based on visibility and prestige. A hand is beautiful, functional, multi-purpose—insured for millions in the modeling industry. A foot is low, hidden, carries weight, often neglected. An eye is celebrated; who praises an ear?

Yet:

> If the whole body were an eye, where would the sense of hearing be? If the whole body were an ear, where would the sense of smell be? (12:17)

**No one would intentionally amputate a foot because they dislike it.** We need the parts we don't celebrate.

> But in fact God has placed the parts in the body, every one of them, just as he wanted them to be. (12:18)

Even the little pinky toe, seemingly insignificant, is crucial for balance. You discover its importance when you stub it—the pain far exceeds its size.

> If they were all one part, where would the body be? As it is, there are many parts, but one body. (12:19-20)

A congregation made of only "important" people—everyone speaking at their pleasure, no one listening, everyone thinking only of themselves—would be monstrous. No welcome, no shared prayer, no confession, no genuine care. Just eyes obsessed with self-importance. Such a church would look ugly and function terribly.

**Don't let our church become like that.**

## The Reality: Many Feel They Don't Belong

Listen to the voice of doubt:
- **The newcomer:** This place looks strange, I don't know anyone—*I don't belong.*
- **The non-believer:** Someone pressured me to come, these Christians are so different—*I don't belong.*
- **The sick:** I've never quite fit anywhere; people will judge me if they know—*I don't belong.*
- **Parents with unruly children:** My kids are running around; I must be out of place—*I don't belong.*
- **The young:** I'm so little here, no one notices me—*I don't belong.*
- **The struggling sinner:** Everyone seems so godly; they'll judge me if they know my secret—*I don't belong.*
- **Even the pastor:** I feel old and out of touch; my jokes seem lamer—*I don't belong.*

**The Truth:** We all feel out of it. We are all minority groups, even in a minority church.

One pastor showed his congregation an age distribution chart—equal numbers across all age groups. Yet the oldest saw only young people behind them, the young saw only old people, and those in the middle saw no one their age. Everyone felt out of place.

As Ayn Rand observed: "The smallest minority on earth is the individual. Those who deny individual rights cannot claim to be defenders of minorities."

We tend to focus on our differences and feel disqualified. Instead, look to our unity in Christ. You will see that we are valuable to each other:

- **Newcomers** teach us how to love and welcome a stranger
- **The sick** teach us compassion and intercession
- **Those who frustrate us** help us grow in patience and grace
- **The elderly** bring wisdom; the young bring vitality
- **Unbelievers** keep us honest with tough questions

**So don't say you don't belong. You do belong.** Turn up to church to be encouraged and to encourage others.

When hard times hit, we often withdraw from fellowship—feeling judged or inadequate. We apologize if that's happened to you. We're committed to making this a safe, caring place. Please don't disappear. We need each other.

## 4. We Need Each Other (12:21-26)

> The eye cannot say to the hand, "I don't need you!" And the head cannot say to the feet, "I don't need you!" (12:21)

**The Temptation of the Strong**

It's tempting for those who are gifted and thriving to think: "I'm doing well spiritually. I don't need this church. I can study the Bible myself, attend conferences elsewhere. I don't need you."

While it's true you may not need us in that sense, we still need you.

> On the contrary, those parts of the body that seem to be weaker are indispensable, and the parts that we think are less honorable we treat with special honor. And the parts that are unpresentable are treated with special modesty, while our presentable parts need no special treatment. But God has put the body together, giving greater honor to the parts that lacked it, so that there should be no division in the body, but that its parts should have equal concern for each other. (12:22-25)

You are strong *in order to help the weaker parts.* As Paul writes:

> We who are strong ought to bear with the failings of the weak and not to please ourselves. (Romans 15:1)

We cannot simply replace you with someone else. The foot cannot morph into a hand; the ear cannot become an eye. Every body part has a unique, irreplaceable function for the common good.

**The Interdependence of Suffering and Joy**

> If one part suffers, every part suffers with it; if one part is honored, every part rejoices with it. (12:26)

This mutual concern creates the health of the whole. When someone is struggling, we grieve together. When someone celebrates, we celebrate together.

> Now you are the body of Christ, and each one of you is a part of it. (12:27)

## The Most Important Person: The One Next to You

Look at movie credits—every role matters. Key grips, gaffers, best boys, production assistants, script supervisors. Everyone has a role. The body works only when every position is filled.

**The most important person in this room is the person next to you. The most important person is the other person.** We are all important to each other. We need each other for the proper functioning of this body.

We can hear God's word faithfully not just from the pulpit but also from each other—as we share what we've learned, confess our struggles, offer help and prayer. As outsiders observe us, they see how love operates among God's people.

This is what it means to be truly spiritual: a community bound together by the Spirit, where every voice matters, every gift serves the common good, and everyone belongs.
