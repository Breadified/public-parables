# Luke 18:9-17

**Title:** Good Guy, Bad Guy
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Setting the Scene

Jesus tells a parable set in first-century Jerusalem, under Roman occupation around 30 AD. It's a story that challenges our assumptions about who is righteous and who isn't—a reversal of the "good guy wins, bad guy loses" narrative we're familiar with from movies.

## The Pharisee: The "Good Guy"

The first character is a Pharisee—one of the decent, moralistic folks of his day. Think of them as the well-to-do citizens of Sydney's lower North Shore or Eastern Suburbs: educated in law or commerce, respectable, law-abiding volunteers who never harm anyone.

Listen to what he says in his prayer:

> "God, I thank you that I am not like other people—robbers, evildoers, adulterers... I fast twice a week and give a tenth of all I get." (Luke 18:11-12)

His attitude is unmistakable: *I am a good guy, and I know it.* He's not breaking rules. He's going beyond the law—fasting twice weekly, tithing generously, living a clean life. By every external standard, he's righteous.

## The Tax Collector: The "Bad Guy"

The second character is a tax collector. In Roman-occupied Jerusalem, these men were doubly despised. Rome hated them for not being Roman; the Jewish people hated them for collaborating with the occupier. They were seen as traitors, living in the same moral category as robbers, evildoers, and adulterers. They didn't enter the temple—they would be thrown out.

More than that, they were corrupt. While the Pharisee gave away a tenth of his wealth, the tax collector *extorted* it from others. He was, by every measure, a bad man—low of the low.

But here's what's remarkable: his prayer is radically different.

> "God, have mercy on me, a sinner." (Luke 18:13)

He won't even look up to heaven. He beats his breast in genuine remorse. He doesn't defend himself or list his virtues. He simply admits the truth: he is a sinner.

## What Is Sin?

The word "sin" gets misunderstood. We think of it as breaking rules or doing bad things. But it's deeper than that. Sin is rebellion against God—not just rule-breaking, but *authority-breaking*. 

Think of it this way: you might work under a boss and break small rules—wear jeans instead of business casual, take personal emails, forget to charge your time. But rebellion would be going to Level 16, disposing of the leader, making the laws instead of following them. That's sin. It's not just about what you do; it's about who you recognize as authority in your life.

The tax collector understands this. He is a sinful man—truly.

## The Shocking Reversal

Here's where Jesus shocks his listeners:

> "I tell you that this man, rather than the other, went home justified before God." (Luke 18:14)

The tax collector—the extortionist, the traitor, the corrupt official—is right with God. The Pharisee—the moral, generous, law-keeping citizen—is not.

This feels unfair. The Pharisee has done so much good. Surely his goodness counts for something? And the tax collector's humility and confession, while admirable, can't possibly undo years of corruption and extortion. How is that just?

## The Good News

Yet Jesus himself declares it. And the reason is this: **God can show mercy to us through his Son, Jesus.**

Jesus was raised to life and now rules over the universe as our boss and God. He has the authority to show mercy—to let us off the hook—if we humble ourselves and ask him for it.

This is where the parable becomes personal. Who do you relate to? Are you the confident Pharisee, trusting in your own goodness? Or are you the broken tax collector, aware of your rebellion and desperate for mercy?

If you recognize yourself as the sinful tax collector, here is the good news: Jesus offers you mercy. Not because you deserve it, but because he can give it.

But consider this: many of us in this room are educated, decent citizens. We pay our taxes, live by the rules, don't commit serious crimes. By the world's standards, we are good people. Yet Jesus says we are facing judgment just like the Pharisee.

The question isn't whether God has made a mistake. The question is: have we?
