## Samuel's Sons and the Failure of Hereditary Leadership

Samuel grew old and appointed his sons as Israel's judges. Yet they did not follow his ways. Joel and Abijah turned aside after dishonest gain, accepted bribes, and perverted justice—a direct contradiction of God's law in Deuteronomy:

> Do not pervert justice or show partiality. Do not accept a bribe, for a bribe blinds the eyes of the wise and twists the words of the innocent. Follow justice and justice alone. (Deuteronomy 16:19-20)

In this moment, Samuel repeated the tragic pattern of Eli. Both men were old and failed to discipline their sons. Both saw their children reject their ways and walk into corruption. The difference was that Israel now demanded change—not gradual reform, but a complete restructuring of government. The elders gathered at Ramah and made their petition clear:

> You are old, and your sons do not follow your ways; now appoint a king to lead us, such as all the other nations have. (1 Samuel 8:5)

---

## The Problems with Kingship

### 1. Hereditary Rule vs. Merit

The core problem with kingship is that it is hereditary rather than meritocratic. A kingdom passes to your sons whether they are godly or wicked, righteous or corrupt. Prince Charles will be the next king regardless of the people's judgment of his character. Samuel had tried to start a dynasty of judges, but this was precisely the wrong direction. Israel should have been selecting leaders based on merit and God's calling, not bloodline.

Yet the Israelites weren't improving matters—they simply wanted to choose their own king instead of accepting Samuel's line. They desired a dynasty, not justice.

### 2. Rejecting God as King

More fundamentally, Israel's demand for a king represented a rejection of God's kingship. This is the heart of the matter. God reminded Samuel:

> Listen to all that the people are saying to you; it is not you they have rejected, but they have rejected me as their king. (1 Samuel 8:7)

In the Book of Judges, God repeatedly raised up judges as circumstances demanded. When the people cried out, God sent deliverers—Gideon, Samson, Deborah. These judges were not standing armies or permanent bureaucracies. They were men whom God called to lead when needed. Remarkably, God Himself was Israel's true Judge and King. Jephthah called Yahweh "the judge" (Judges 11:27), and Scripture repeatedly testifies that Yahweh "fought for Israel" and gave them victory (Exodus 14:14; Deuteronomy 1:30; Joshua 10:42).

When Gideon was tempted to become a permanent ruler, he refused:

> I will not rule over you, nor will my son rule over you. The Lord will rule over you. (Judges 8:23)

God's vision was a layered structure: judges ruled locally, priests interceded at a higher level, and God reigned over all. This reflected a profound theological principle: ultimate authority belonged to God alone, not to any human institution.

### 3. Being Like "Other Nations"

Israel was created to be different. Before they were a nation, they were slaves in Egypt. God called them to be a people set apart—spiritually distinct, ethically separate, culturally distinct from surrounding nations:

> I see a people who live apart and do not consider themselves one of the nations. (Numbers 23:9)

> When you enter the land the Lord your God is giving you, do not learn to imitate the detestable ways of the nations there. (Deuteronomy 18:9)

God gave Israel the Ten Commandments and the Law to guide them into a completely different way of life. They were to be holy—separated unto God. Yet Israel repeatedly looked longingly at the other nations and envied them rather than remaining distinct.

In particular, they wanted military power like other nations. They didn't trust God's protection. They wanted a standing army commanded by a king:

> Then we will be like all the other nations, with a king to lead us and to go out before us and fight our battles. (1 Samuel 8:20)

This was deeply misguided. God had repeatedly proven His faithfulness in battle. In the previous chapter, Yahweh threw the Philistines into confusion and defeated them before Israel (1 Samuel 7:10). God had brought victory over the Philistines and peace with the Amorites under Samuel's leadership. Yet this was not enough for Israel.

Behind their demand for a king was not merely a practical need, but something more troubling: a fundamental lack of faith in God. They wanted independence from Him. They wanted to trust in human strength and military machinery rather than divine protection.

## The Consequences of Kingship: Slavery

When Israel insisted on a king despite Samuel's warnings, God told Samuel to warn them of what they would face. Samuel's description of a king's "rights" is deeply sobering:

> He will take your sons and make them serve with his chariots and horses, and they will run in front of his chariots. (1 Samuel 8:11)

Your sons will become conscripted soldiers, forced to die in the king's wars. They will be the front-line infantry running ahead of the royal chariots.

> Some he will assign to be commanders of thousands and commanders of fifties, and others to plow his ground and reap his harvest, and still others to make weapons of war and equipment for his chariots. (1 Samuel 8:12)

A centrally planned military requires a centrally planned economy. Once a king has military power, he naturally extends his control over everything.

> He will take your daughters to be perfumers and cooks and bakers. He will take the best of your fields and vineyards and olive groves and give them to his attendants. He will take a tenth of your grain and of your vintage and give it to his officials and attendants. (1 Samuel 8:13-15)

The king will confiscate your best productive assets for his own use.

> Your male and female servants and the best of your cattle and donkeys he will take for his own use. He will take a tenth of your flocks, and you yourselves will become his slaves. (1 Samuel 8:16-17)

This is a feudal system. Your entire life becomes service to the king—for his welfare, his comfort, his enjoyment. You will have just enough to survive, kept healthy like livestock so you can serve his every need. This is slavery, plain and simple. You came out of Egypt only to create a new Egypt.

> When that day comes, you will cry out for relief from the king you have chosen, but the Lord will not answer you in that day. (1 Samuel 8:18)

God made clear: if you reject Me as king and choose a human ruler, I will not rescue you. You will have made your choice.

## Application: The Modern King

We do not call our leaders kings, yet modern governments exercise the same powers. Consider how contemporary society mirrors Samuel's warning:

**Military conscription** exists in many nations. Young men are compelled to fight in wars they may not believe in, serving the state's geopolitical interests rather than their own conscience.

**Taxation** far exceeds the tenth mentioned in Scripture. Income tax was introduced in Australia during World War I as a temporary emergency measure—it was supposed to be temporary. Yet it became permanent and has steadily increased, now consuming far more than ten percent of earnings.

**Centrally planned economies** direct industries through subsidies and regulations. Governments tell people what jobs they should pursue through incentives and loans. They dictate what children should be taught in schools. When families choose Christian education instead of government schools, funding is cut off.

**Control of conscience and speech** has become pervasive. Citizens are expected to conform to government-approved thoughts and use politically correct language. Social pressure enforces compliance with state-sanctioned narratives.

We are well on our way to becoming slaves—not in the ancient sense, but in the modern equivalent. We have given our "king" (it doesn't matter whether he is righteous or not) ever-expanding power. If a government can lock down your business for three months, what prevents it from locking you down for three years? History shows that temporary emergency powers rarely remain temporary.

**The root problem is theological, not economic or political.** We see symptoms—education failures, media manipulation, demographic shifts—but the disease is spiritual: as a nation, we have rejected God as king and replaced Him with godless rulers. We reap the consequences of that choice.

When we act like the other nations, we are governed like the other nations. There is no escape through political reform alone.

## The Promise: A Different Kind of King

Yet God did not leave Israel without hope. Though the people insisted on a human king, God gave them a vision of what this king should be—radically different from the kings of other nations.

> When you enter the land the Lord your God is giving you and have taken possession of it and settled in it, and you say, "Let us set a king over us like all the nations around us," you may indeed set a king over you whom the Lord your God chooses. (Deuteronomy 17:14-15)

First, **the Lord will choose the king's line**—not hereditary succession based on blood, but selection based on God's wisdom.

Second, **he must be from among your fellow Israelites. Do not place a foreigner over you, one who is not an Israelite.** (Deuteronomy 17:15) The king must be one of the people, specifically separated from the pagan nations.

Third, **the king must not acquire great numbers of horses for himself or make the people return to Egypt to get more of them, for the Lord has told you, "You are not to go back that way again."** (Deuteronomy 17:16) No military alliances with Egypt. No reliance on chariot warfare. The king must trust God, not weapons.

Fourth, **he must not take many wives, or his heart will be led astray.** (Deuteronomy 17:17) No harem of wives that represent political alliances with foreign powers. The king's loyalty must be undivided.

Fifth, **he must not accumulate large amounts of silver and gold.** (Deuteronomy 17:17-18) No corruption, no plunder of the nation's wealth. Either minimal taxation or none at all.

Sixth, **when he takes the throne of his kingdom, he is to write for himself on a scroll a copy of this law, taken from that of the Levitical priests. It is to be with him, and he is to read it all the days of his life so that he may learn to revere the Lord his God and follow carefully all the words of this law and these decrees.** (Deuteronomy 17:18-19) The king stands under the law of God, not above it.

Finally, **he is not to consider himself better than his fellow Israelites and turn from the law to the right or to the left. Then he and his descendants will reign a long time over his kingdom in Israel.** (Deuteronomy 17:20) A constitutional monarchy of sorts—the king reigns among his people under the law, equal before God's Word.

This king would not be like the other nations' kings at all. He would be fundamentally different.

## Where Is This King Found?

The fulfillment of this promise comes in Jesus Christ. When Jesus began His ministry, He announced:

> The time has come. The kingdom of God has come near. Repent and believe the good news! (Mark 1:14-15)

Jesus is the King of the Jews, the One before whom every knee will bow. But He is a king unlike any other:

- He did not tax the citizenry or confiscate their food. Instead, He gave freely—multiplying bread and fish to feed the hungry.

- He rode not on warhorse and chariot, but on a donkey into Jerusalem.

- Rather than sending men to their deaths in his wars, He stood in front of them, defending them. In Gethsemane, He was taken captive on their behalf. He suffered a lonely death upon the cross for His subjects' sake.

- He battles against sin itself and defeats evil through His resurrection.

- He now sits on the throne in heaven, and we await His return to judge the living and the dead.

We look forward to the day when the law of God will be written on every person's heart. When men and women are ruled by His Word from within, there will be no need for earthly kings or governments. Only Christ—our only King in everything—will reign.

## Limited Government and Divine Kingship

The form of government matters far less than the character of those who govern. Whether monarchy, democracy, or constitutional republic—if the leaders are godly, the system works. If they are ungodly, disaster follows regardless of the system's design.

We must be skeptical of those who promise that removing a ceremonial figurehead will solve systemic problems. Our current governments already wield the powers of ancient kings. If leaders do not believe themselves accountable to God, good luck when they rule over you.

Some have idealistic visions that a big federal government will solve all our problems—medical crises, housing shortages, environmental challenges. Yet government is run by humans who are limited, fallible, and often motivated by greed. We need to think ahead: if they can take this power now, what might they do with it later?

The fundamental issue is not economic or political—it is theological. As long as we reject God as our true King and place our hope in human rulers, we will experience the consequences. The solutions to our deepest problems are not found in policy reforms or political movements. They are found in God and His Word.

This truth has lifted humanity out of barbarism and poverty throughout history. It will continue to do so if we would but trust it. Our hope must be in Christ Our Only King.
