# Romans 5:12-21

**Title:** The Reconciliation of God
**Date:** 2 December 2012 (SMAC) / 24 December 2023 (ARISE)
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Power of One Man to Change the World

One person's actions can reshape history. In 1914, Gavrilo Princip, a Serbian nationalist, assassinated Archduke Franz Ferdinand of Austria-Hungary. This single act triggered a chain reaction that plunged Europe into war. Sixty-five million people died in World War I, redrawing the map of Europe and the world. The collapse of the Ottoman Empire led directly to the Israeli-Palestinian conflict that persists today. World War I then became the catalyst for World War II, claiming another seventy million lives.

One unremarkable man, acting in a single moment, brought death to millions across generations. Yet this historical truth teaches us a spiritual principle: just as one man can bring destruction, one man can bring restoration. Today we encounter two men who changed the universe—Adam, who ruined it all, and Jesus Christ, who fixed it.

## Part One: The Man Who Ruins It All (Romans 5:12-14)

> Therefore, just as sin entered the world through one man, and death through sin, and in this way death came to all people, because all sinned.

The "one man" refers to Adam in the Garden of Eden. God gave him a simple command: do not eat from a certain tree. Adam disobeyed. Sin entered the world, and with it came death. This was not merely Adam's personal consequence—his action brought death to all humanity.

This seems profoundly unfair. Why should we inherit the consequences of someone else's sin? We did not choose to be part of Adam's failure. Yet consequences are inherited all the time. When Gavrilo Princip pulled the trigger, millions who never knew him suffered the results for generations. In the same way, when Adam sinned, we inherited the consequences of death.

But we are not innocent bystanders. We do not merely inherit death; we inherit the tendency toward sin itself. Death itself produces sin in us.

### The Vicious Cycle: Death Produces Sin

In the original Greek, Romans 5:12 uses "therefore" rather than "because." This subtle distinction reveals a profound truth. Look at 1 Corinthians 15:56:

> The sting of death is sin, and the power of sin is the law.

We naturally think the sting of *sin* is death. But Paul reverses this: the sting of *death* is sin. Death makes us sin. We are trapped in a cycle:

**Sin → Death → Sin → Death**

Between Adam and Moses, there was no written law, yet people still died. Death existed before the Law was given. What caused sin in those without the law? Death itself. Mortality strips away our peace and security, driving us toward greed, jealousy, and selfishness. Because we will die, we grasp at everything we can get. We fight over toys and food, we compete ruthlessly, we hoard wealth—all driven by the terror of our mortality.

This pattern is visible especially during the Christmas season. Black Friday and Boxing Day sales reveal human nature at its most desperate. We abandon our responsibilities and our care for others because we feel entitled to grab what we can while we still can. We relax our moral commitments and pursue material satisfaction. Mortality makes us morally fail.

We are born over a grave, each day moving closer to death. Every single person is part of this chain reaction—inheriting both Adam's death and our own sinful responses to mortality. We cannot escape this vicious cycle on our own.

## Part Two: The Man Who Fixes It All (Romans 5:15-21)

But here is the gospel: if one man can wreck creation, one man can restore it.

> But the gift is not like the trespass. For if the many died by the trespass of the one man, how much more did God's grace and the gift that came by the grace of the one man, Jesus Christ, overflow to the many!

Paul spends verses 15-19 comparing Adam and Jesus:

**Adam's Disobedience → Condemnation and Death**
**Jesus's Obedience → Justification and Life**

Adam broke God's command for mere food and lost life itself. Jesus demonstrated perfect obedience to the Father, following God's will even unto death on the cross. In that single righteous act, Jesus demonstrated his goodness, his generosity, his complete selflessness. He is the righteous one.

And then he gives this righteousness to us—freely, as a gift.

### The Best Gift Ever

Adam's "gift" to us is the worst imaginable: inherited sin and death. Jesus's gift is the best—eternal life through grace, given freely to us who deserve it least. Imagine receiving a traffic fine with double demerits over Christmas, while your friend receives the news that someone has paid off their entire mortgage. Jesus gives us the mortgage payment. The debt of sin is paid.

Instead of death reigning, life now reigns for those who receive God's abundant grace. Jesus rose from the dead and conquered death itself. He gives us that victory. The Bible calls this "grace"—unmerited favor, a gift we could never earn.

### The Reversal of Everything

Looking at Romans 5:17:

> How much more will those who receive God's abundant provision of grace and of the gift of righteousness reign in life through the one man, Jesus Christ!

Death reigned through Adam. Now life reigns through Christ. The penalty of sin is reversed. But more than that—we are transferred from death to life, from condemnation to justification.

Consider Steve Jobs and Apple. When Jobs founded Apple, everything depended on him. The company was his vision, his genius. Years later, the board fired him. Apple's fortunes immediately collapsed. In his eleven years away, Jobs founded Pixar and created extraordinary things, but Apple continued to decline. Finally, the company realized their mistake and brought him back. With Jobs' return, he created the iPod and other revolutionary products. One man's presence transformed the company's trajectory. The shares soared. One man saved the company.

It only takes one man to rescue a company. It only takes one man to rescue humanity. Jesus Christ is that one man. He defeated death. He reversed the curse. He offers us life.

But this requires understanding who we are and what he has done for us.

## Part Three: Christ Died for Us While We Were Still Weak (Romans 5:6-9)

> For while we were still weak, at the right time Christ died for the ungodly... but God shows his love for us in that while we were still sinners, Christ died for us.

Notice the timing. Jesus died for us:
- While we were weak
- While we were ungodly
- While we were sinners

This is crucial because it reveals God's love. We would rarely die for a righteous person. Perhaps for someone good, someone might sacrifice themselves. But for someone weak, ungodly, sinful? No.

The world operates on merit. We get hired based on our best resume. We gain admission to university based on our highest test scores. Houses sell for their best price. Children get into competitive schools based on their best performance. The world always evaluates us at our best.

God does something radically different. God looks at our worst.

### The Paradox: God Chooses the Worst

This is the paradox of grace. When the world looks at you, it picks the best. When God looks at you, he sees the worst—your sins, your weakness, your failures—and he loves you anyway. He sends his Son to die for you while you are still at your lowest point.

Imagine being asked to take the place of a convicted criminal in prison, someone guilty of terrible crimes. Most would refuse. Yet Jesus takes our place. He swaps his perfect life for our sinful lives.

Here is the wonderful news: if you only present your best resume, you have nowhere to go but down. If you claim to be a chess champion when you are not, the interviewer asks you a chess question in proper notation and you collapse. Your best becomes your ceiling.

But if you admit your worst—"I am lazy, I will waste time, I fail at everything"—then you can only improve in that person's eyes. They have already seen the bottom. They can only be pleasantly surprised. God has seen our worst. And he forgives us anyway. He declares us righteous despite our sin.

## The Foundation of Christian Assurance

> Since, therefore, we have now been justified by his blood, much more shall we be saved by him from the wrath of God.

We have been declared righteous (justified) in the present through Jesus's blood. This means we can be assured of salvation in the future.

Why? Because God accepted us when we were enemies, when we were at our absolute worst. If he justified us then, why would he change his mind now? The grounds for his acceptance are his mercy and grace, not our performance. Even if we do not improve (and we do fail repeatedly), the reason God took us in remains the same—his undeserved grace.

This is mathematical certainty: if God proved his love by dying for us while we were sinners, he will certainly not abandon us now that we are his children.

### How This Changes Everything

With this assurance, we can hold the world loosely. We no longer need to grasp desperately at life because we have something better to look forward to. We do not fear missing out because we will have eternal life. We do not need to fear mortality because we will have new bodies that do not decay.

The hope of resurrection transforms how we live now. We can be generous instead of greedy. We can serve others instead of clawing at possessions. We can take risks for the kingdom instead of playing it safe. We can endure suffering knowing it produces character and hope.

## A Peace That Transcends Death

Our hearts will eventually fail us—the one organ that barely regenerates. Yet despite the body's amazing ability to renew itself, death waits at the end. We will all face it.

Perhaps, like many, you fear death. I do not look forward to it. But I know this: when that day comes, no matter what happens, I will be at peace. Not the peace of being dead and insensible, but peace in the deepest sense—the war between God and me is over. Christ has won. I am reconciled to God.

This is what it means to be Christian: we are people loved and forgiven by God despite our sin, given eternal life through Jesus's righteous act, and assured of God's faithfulness because he proved his love when we were most unlovable.

This Christmas season, we have an opportunity to reflect on our priorities. Will we live for ourselves, grasping and competing? Or will we live secure in God's love, free to love others? The choice is ours, but the foundation is sure: one man has already fixed what one man broke. Jesus Christ is Lord, and his grace is sufficient.
