# Hebrews 12:1-13

**Title:** In Light of Such Witnesses
**Date:** March 20, 2009
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Recap: Jesus is Better

Over the past five weeks, we've journeyed through Hebrews and arrived at a powerful conclusion: **Jesus is better**. Better than angels. Better than any human. Better than the Old Testament prophets. Better than the Old Testament priests. Better sacrifice. Better temple. This foundational truth sets the stage for what the author writes next.

## Persevere! (v.1)

With this foundation established, the author of Hebrews draws a natural conclusion:

> Therefore let us throw off everything that hinders and the sin that so easily entangles. Let us run with perseverance the race marked out for us. (v.1)

The imagery is vivid and athletic—a race. An Olympic athlete stripping away everything unnecessary. This metaphor continues throughout the passage: don't grow weary and lose heart (v.3), endure hardship as discipline (v.7), strengthen your feeble arms and weak knees so you can run straight (v.12).

The Christian life is a race. We are called to run without the baggage of sin, without being stuck in Old Testament modes of thinking, moving forward with everything unnecessary stripped away.

## The Original Context: Pressure to Abandon Faith

These weren't abstract metaphors for the original readers. The letter was written to Jewish converts facing immense pressure—from their families, their culture, their government, their religious tradition. The temptation was real and compelling: return to the temple system, to the priests, to what was familiar and culturally acceptable. Abandon this new faith in Christ.

## Our Modern Pressures

We face similar pressures today, though they take different forms:

- **Family pressure:** Studies demand all our attention; Christian practices get pushed aside. Generations of family religious tradition clash with our new faith commitment.

- **Cultural pressure:** Schools and peer groups dismiss Christianity as dubious or silly. Yet these critics rarely engage seriously with Scripture. They've never actually sat down and read the Bible as an adult, let alone engaged in genuine conversation about its claims.

- **Romantic pressure:** A relationship begins with someone who isn't Christian and shows no interest in becoming one. The implicit bargain: drop the "silly Christian things" and just spend time together. Notice the asymmetry—the non-Christian is never asked to read the Bible or pray; only the Christian is expected to compromise, to abandon their "quaint beliefs."

## The Slow Unraveling

What often happens is a gradual erosion:

1. **Personally**, we give up the habits that sustain us—Bible reading, prayer
2. **Publicly**, we stop meeting with other believers, beginning to isolate ourselves
3. **Finally**, we give up Christ altogether

The deceitfulness of the world and of sin is patient and cunning. It entangles us, chokes us slowly. But the call is clear: **run**. Leave sin behind. Don't bow to pressure to abandon Christ. Don't isolate yourself from the community of faith. (This is why community was so important in last week's teaching.)

## Why Persevere? Two Reasons

### Reason One: The Cloud of Witnesses (v.1)

> Therefore, since we are surrounded by a great cloud of witnesses... (v.1)

The previous chapter—Hebrews 11—contains a hall of faith: Abraham, Isaac, Jacob, Joseph, Moses, and even Rahab the prostitute. These weren't perfect people. They were sinful, flawed, struggling. But they had one thing in common: **they stayed loyal to God**. They trusted God even when His promises seemed crazy. They trusted Him through suffering. They trusted Him facing death.

Now they surround us like witnesses in a stadium. They endured. They made it. They're okay. Their faithful witness across the centuries says to us: *You can do this too. Persevere.*

### Reason Two: Fix Your Eyes on Jesus (v.2-3)

> Let us fix our eyes on Jesus, the author and perfecter of our faith, who for the joy set before him endured the cross, scorning its shame, and sat down at the right hand of the throne of God. Consider him who endured such opposition from sinful men, so that you will not grow weary and lose heart. (v.2-3)

Jesus didn't bypass suffering—He endured it. And where did His faithfulness lead? To the right hand of God. Exaltation. Victory. This is where perseverance leads.

## Standing in Hardship as Discipline (v.7-11)

> Endure hardship as discipline; God is treating you as sons. For what son is not disciplined by his father? If you are not disciplined (and everyone undergoes discipline), then you are illegitimate children and not true sons. Moreover, we have all had human fathers who disciplined us and we respected them for it. How much more should we submit to the Father of our spirits and live! Our fathers disciplined us for a little while as they thought best; but God disciplines us for our good, that we may share in his holiness. No discipline seems pleasant at the time, but painful. Later on, however, it produces a harvest of righteousness and peace for those who have been trained by it. (v.7-11)

Here's something profound: your suffering isn't punishment—it's *discipline*. It's evidence that you're God's child, not an illegitimate outsider. A father disciplines his children because he loves them and wants them to grow. God disciplines us for our good, so that we might share in His holiness.

The original readers hadn't even faced the ultimate test. They hadn't shed blood. There had been no martyrs among them. Yet they were already forgetting this encouragement—already growing weary under what amounted to social pressure.

## A Honest Reflection

How are we different? We talk about persecution from our non-Christian friends. But let's be honest:

I am still alive. I've never been spat upon for being Christian. I've never been jailed. I've never had my property confiscated. I've probably never even been called names for my faith.

Yet I'm terrified. I'm afraid of what friends will think when I mention I'm Christian. I'm scared of being ostracized when I say no to something illegal or walk away from gossip. I'm frightened they'll think less of me if I invite them to church. 

Why? I haven't even shed blood yet. My fear is unreasonable. We live in a tolerant country where people generally respect others' faith commitments. Our friends will likely be fine if we're honest about being Christian and talk about it respectfully.

If there's even a little pain to endurance—a dip in popularity, awkward moments—consider it *discipline*, not martyrdom. But here's what concerns me: if you can't stand firm now, in the safety of your school environment with minimal real cost, what will you do when genuine hardship comes? And it will come. When you finally stand up and persevere, you become one of those witnesses for the next generation.

## Man Up

> Strengthen your feeble arms and weak knees. (v.12)

In other words: **man up**. 

Don't be a coward. Be brave. Be daring. Work through impediments and obstacles without whining. Take control of the situation. Be strong. Stand up to the moment.

- Take control of sin in your life
- Take control of temptation to stray from faith
- Say no to ungodliness
- Say no to denying Christ
- Stand firm against peer pressure

You know the areas where you're weak. There will be opportunities to pray about those later. But for now: strengthen those feeble arms and legs. Run this race. Persevere.
