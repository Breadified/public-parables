# 1 Peter 3:18-4:6

**Title:** Waiting for Christmas
**Date:** February 11, 2007
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction: The Season of Waiting

We all know the experience of waiting—especially at Christmas. Whether it's waiting to open presents, celebrating the new year, or reuniting with loved ones we haven't seen in months, waiting shapes our experience of the holiday season.

But there's a deeper kind of waiting that matters far more than any Christmas morning. The passage we're studying today speaks to the experience of waiting for God—and what that means for those of us who follow Jesus.

We've been working through 1 Peter as a church, and we're finishing up these final weeks. This passage is admittedly strange, but it speaks directly to us today, especially to those of us who call ourselves Christians.

## Part One: Waiting for Jesus Before Jesus

> For Christ also suffered once for sins, the righteous for the unrighteous, to bring you to God. He was put to death in the body but made alive in the Spirit. After being made alive, he went and made proclamation to the imprisoned spirits to those who were disobedient long ago when God waited patiently in the days of Noah while the ark was being built. In it only a few people, eight in all, were saved through water. (1 Peter 3:18-20)

This passage contains one of the strangest verses in Scripture: the idea of Christ preaching to "spirits in prison." What does this mean?

Over the centuries, theologians have proposed various interpretations:

- Some suggest purgatory—a state between heaven and hell where sins can be purged. But this contradicts verse 18, which tells us that Christ has already brought us to God. His death is sufficient and effective. Purgatory would suggest there's still something more to pay, that we haven't truly been brought to God.

- Others propose Christ metaphorically preached in judgment during Noah's time.

- Some connect it to fallen angels in Genesis 6, though this seems convoluted and lacks context.

- A more recent interpretation—one I find compelling—suggests that this passage speaks of people who waited for Jesus *before Jesus came*.

### The Promise in Isaiah

If we search Scripture carefully, this idea of preaching to captives isn't strange at all. The prophet Isaiah, five hundred years before Jesus was born, prophesied about it:

> The Spirit of the Sovereign LORD is on me, because the LORD has anointed me to preach good news to the poor. He has sent me to bind up the brokenhearted, to proclaim freedom for the captives and release from darkness for the prisoners. (Isaiah 61:1)

When we think of "prisoners," we might imagine criminals. But justice demands that those who do the crime pay the time. No—Isaiah is speaking of a more terrible prison than any jail cell. He's speaking of death itself.

### Sheol: The Prison of Death

The Old Testament describes a place called *Sheol* or *Hades*—the realm of the dead. It's not described with the same fiery torment as *Gehenna* (hell), which Jesus speaks of in the gospels. It's simply where people go when they die. It's not a positive place, for death itself is never positive. But it is where the dead wait.

The Psalms speak of God's redemption from this prison:

> But God will redeem my soul from the power of Sheol, for He will receive me. (Psalm 49:15)

> For Your lovingkindness toward me is great, and You have delivered my soul from the depths of Sheol. (Psalm 86:13)

### The Question of the Old Testament Saints

Peter is writing to Jewish believers, reminding them that all God's promises have been fulfilled in Christ—the glorious eternal inheritance, the worldwide ruling Christ, redemption, the new temple, the holy nation, the royal priesthood. Everything they'd been waiting for had finally arrived.

But here's the question that must have haunted many: *What about those who died before Jesus came?* What about the faithful ones of the Old Testament exile who lived and died before experiencing these promises? Did they miss out?

The answer is no. They did not miss out.

Christ, by His Spirit, goes back to them. He proclaims the gospel of Isaiah 61 to the ancient exiles—*the time has finally come*. This is what the Apostles' Creed means when it says, "He descended into the dead." These people do not get left behind.

### A Question We've All Asked

Here's a question every Sunday school class and youth group eventually asks: *What happened to people who died in the Old Testament before Jesus? They never heard the gospel. They didn't even know the name of Jesus.*

Here's the answer: **Jesus doesn't forget about them.**

The brilliance of this passage is in what it reveals:

- **The Old Testament people had faith too.** Their faith was in the future—in the God who promised to come.
- **Our faith is in the past.** We look back to the cross and resurrection.
- **But it's the same faith in the same Christ.**

Being born in a different era doesn't disqualify you from salvation. Faith in Christ is effective for those born before He came, just as it will be effective for those born after. Christ is the center of all history—the one toward whom the past points and from whom the future flows.

## The Faithfulness of God: The Noah Illustration

Now Peter moves to the example of Noah, and this isn't random:

> Who disobeyed long ago when God waited patiently (while they were disobedient) in the days of Noah while the ark was being built. In it only a few people, eight in all, were saved through water. (1 Peter 3:20)

God patiently judged the world. He didn't carelessly chuck everything out. Instead, He took His time—judging the world yet carefully sparing eight souls.

The prophet Isaiah himself connected this promise to the exiles he was addressing:

> In a surge of anger I hid my face from you for a moment, but with everlasting kindness I will have compassion on you, says the LORD your Redeemer. To me this is like the days of Noah, when I swore that the waters of Noah would never again cover the earth. So now I have sworn not to be angry with you, never to rebuke you again. (Isaiah 54:8-9)

The world was judged, yet Noah's family were saved. The waters that brought death to the world became waters of salvation for them. There's no need to fear. God saves amidst judgment. God keeps His promises. You have been brought to God through the death of Christ.

## Part Two: Waiting for Jesus After Jesus

Now Peter calls us to patience in our own time:

> Therefore, since Christ suffered in his body, arm yourselves also with the same attitude, because whoever suffers in the body is done with sin. As a result, they do not live the rest of their earthly lives for evil human desires, but rather for the will of God. (1 Peter 4:1-2)

### The Impatient Life

Notice what characterizes those who don't wait:

> You have spent enough time in the past doing what pagans choose to do—living in debauchery, lust, drunkenness, orgies, carousing and detestable idolatry. They are surprised that you do not join them in their reckless, wild living, and they heap abuse on you. (1 Peter 4:3-4)

These words describe the life of the impatient—those who cannot delay gratification. They live only for the moment, for their passions and lusts. They don't think about long-term consequences or anyone but themselves. Reckless. Wild. Whatever feels right.

There's a profound spiritual discipline in waiting:
- Sex before marriage? *Wait.*
- Assets before expenses? *Wait.*
- Investment before your house? *Wait.*
- Work before holiday? *Wait.*

We teach our children delayed gratification—opening one present per month at Christmas, not all at once. We're teaching them to wait, to trust that something meaningful comes to those who can delay what they desire.

### The Impatience of This Age

Those who live without faith think we're foolish for not joining them. They mock our restraint. But we know something they don't: judgment is coming.

> They will have to give account to him who is ready to judge the living and the dead. (1 Peter 4:5)

## The Two Christmases

### The First Christmas: Salvation

The first Christmas brought salvation:

> For Christ also suffered once for sins, the righteous for the unrighteous, to bring you to God. (1 Peter 3:18)

This prophecy comes from Isaiah, who spoke seven centuries before Christ's birth:

> He was pierced for our transgressions, he was crushed for our iniquities; the punishment that brought us peace was upon him, and by his wounds we are healed. We all, like sheep, have gone astray, each of us has turned to his own way; and the LORD has laid on him the iniquity of us all. (Isaiah 53:5-6)

The righteous One died for the unrighteous. He was brought to God so that He could bring *us* to God.

### Baptism: Identifying with His Death and Life

Peter speaks of baptism in a way that clarifies its true meaning:

> This water symbolizes baptism that now saves you also—not the removal of dirt from the body but the pledge of a good conscience toward God. It saves you by the resurrection of Jesus Christ, who has gone into heaven and is at God's right hand. (1 Peter 3:21-22)

Notice what Peter emphasizes: baptism isn't about the water itself or the ritual of washing. It's about the pledge—your conscious identification with Christ's death and resurrection.

When Noah entered the ark, he publicly declared: *I am on God's side, despite the ridicule.* Similarly, baptism is your declaration: *I am joined with Christ's death. I am joined with His life.*

This changes everything—not just your future, but transforms how your past is viewed. It's like a Facebook profile picture that changes your entire timeline. Your past and future are rewritten by this identification with Christ.

### The Last Christmas: Judgment

I've been out in the community, and it can feel ugly toward Christians. In my anxiety, I sometimes wonder: *What's taking so long?* But Peter is clear: the offer of salvation won't remain open forever.

> But there will be a second coming—not to bring salvation, but to bring judgment. This will be the last Christmas. (Application of 1 Peter 4:5-6)

We're approaching the 2,000th anniversary of that first Christmas. This time is for us to get right with God—to sort out whether our final journey will be upward to heaven or downward into eternal judgment.

## A Message of Hope

To those who might not know Jesus personally yet: I invite you to get to know Him. There will be a last Christmas. When He visits us once more, it won't be to bring salvation. It will be to bring judgment.

But today—this day—is still a day of grace. God is patient with you, not wanting any to perish but all to come to repentance.

Merry Christmas. And thank you.
