# Romans 1:18–2:16

**Title:** The Wrath of God  
**Date:** November 4, 2012  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Who Is This Passage About?

Paul addresses three possible audiences in Romans 1:19-23. Each group has been given clear knowledge of God, yet each has failed to honor Him. Understanding which audience Paul primarily addresses shapes how we interpret the doctrine of natural revelation and human accountability.

### 1. The Ignorant (1:19-23)

A common interpretation suggests this passage applies to everyone who hasn't heard the gospel. The logic seems straightforward:

> For what can be known about God is plain to them, because God has shown it to them. For his invisible attributes, namely, his eternal power and divine nature, have been clearly perceived, ever since the creation of the world, in the things that have been made. So they are without excuse. (Romans 1:19-20)

**The three movements:**
- They have knowledge of God—what can be known is plain to them
- God reveals it through creation—His eternal power and divine nature are clearly perceived
- They are without excuse—the conclusion is inescapable

#### Natural Revelation

In Christian theology, *natural revelation* refers to the discovery of God's attributes through creation. Unlike written revelation—where God speaks directly through Scripture—natural revelation relies on the principle that creation bears God's fingerprints. Because creation was made by God, we should be able to discern His mind and character from nature.

The reasoning seems elegant. Consider a beautifully designed red house. From examining its careful details, excellent architecture, and coherent design, you can conclude three things: there was a designer; the designer was skilled; the designer preferred red. If we apply this logic to the universe itself—knowing it had a definite beginning and that cause precedes effect—we can infer a First Cause. That cause must be God. Since everyone has access to creation, everyone knows God's attributes. Therefore, no one has an excuse.

### 2. Adam (1:19-23)

A second option is that Paul is actually describing Adam's fall. Several clues point to this interpretation.

First, Romans 1:23 echoes Genesis 1:26 remarkably:

> [Romans 1:23] exchanged the glory of the immortal God for images resembling mortal man and birds and animals and creeping things.

> [Genesis 1:26] Then God said, Let us make man in our image, after our likeness. And let them have dominion over the fish of the sea and over the birds of the heavens and over the livestock and over all the earth and over every creeping thing that creeps on the earth.

The shared vocabulary—images, man, birds, animals, creeping things—indicates Paul is deliberately invoking Genesis.

**Adam's three advantages:**
- He had direct knowledge of God—God walked with him in the garden
- He witnessed God's creative power—he named the animals and saw Eve created
- He had no excuse—he knew God intimately

Yet Adam failed spectacularly. Although he knew God, he did not honor Him or give thanks. Presented with one simple command—eat from any tree except one—he chose exactly the forbidden tree. His thinking became futile; his heart darkened. He believed the serpent's lie that God was withholding something good.

Claiming to be wise, he became a fool. His fall stemmed from his desire to be like God, to possess knowledge of good and evil (Genesis 3:5). In his pride, Adam exchanged the glory of the immortal God for a creature—a snake, a creeping thing.

This created a terrible reversal. Man was made in God's image, but now man makes God in his image. Mankind was given dominion over animals, but now animals become gods. The snake commanded, and Adam obeyed. He turned his back on truth and gave his allegiance to a creature rather than the Creator.

### 3. Israel (1:19-23)

The third option identifies this passage as describing Israel's rebellion. Israel, like Adam, was given direct revelation.

God appeared to the Israelites at Mount Sinai and spoke to Moses and the people. Psalm 106:19-21 describes their response:

> They made a calf in Horeb and worshiped a metal image. They exchanged the glory of God for the image of an ox that eats grass. They forgot God, their Savior, who had done great things in Egypt.

This parallels Romans 1:23 precisely. Additionally, Deuteronomy 4:15-18 warns Israel against making carved images of animals—the very prohibition they violated by creating the golden calf.

**Israel's three advantages:**
- They had direct knowledge of God—Moses brought the knowledge of Yahweh; God's voice boomed from the mountain
- They witnessed God's creative power—the ten plagues, the parting of the Red Sea, the pillar of fire and cloud, manna from heaven
- They had no excuse—they knew God through unmistakable miracles

Yet Israel failed. Although they knew God, they did not honor or thank Him. They complained about food and longed to return to Egypt. Their thinking became futile; their hearts darkened. They suspected God had abandoned them and conspired against His prophet. They claimed to be wise but became fools.

Most damningly, at Mount Sinai itself—where they were warned against idolatry—they built a golden calf and worshiped it. Israel was given all knowledge of God. Therefore, Israel is without excuse, and condemnation is just.

## Which Interpretation?

We have three candidates: the ignorant, Adam, or Israel. The answer isn't "the ignorant."

### Why Not the Ignorant?

**First reason:** Paul just established that God's righteousness is revealed in the gospel, in the person of Jesus (Romans 1:17). Grace comes through Jesus Christ—not through nature. If we must know Jesus to access God's grace, then nature alone cannot save us.

**Second reason:** Natural revelation doesn't actually work. 

When we observe a building, we might infer a designer, but our conclusion is really just educated guessing. How do we know the design wasn't randomly generated? How do we know there's one designer and not multiple? How do we determine if it's good design? Our reasoning depends entirely on assumptions we bring to the evidence.

Without direct revelation, we have no reliable information. Looking at creation only helps us understand God if we already know Him. To those who don't know God, creation simply becomes an object of worship. A cow becomes useful and worthy of worship. A tree becomes a god.

Eastern belief systems illustrate this perfectly. Buddhism and Hinduism teach that we can discover God through meditation and introspection. Each person arrives at their own understanding of the divine. There is no objective truth—only individual visions. This is why Hinduism embraces millions of gods and why conversations about faith with Buddhists or Hindus prove so frustrating. We appeal to truth and evidence; they answer that truth is irrelevant.

This is precisely what Paul means by the suppression of truth. Knowledge of God isn't derived from what we think He should be like. It comes from truth He reveals directly. He has spoken. He has written. In the Bible, we have the truth.

Christianity is fundamentally a religion of truth. This is why churches have Bible talks, study groups, and Sunday schools—to help people discover truth. If we stop reading, discussing, and reasoning, we suppress the truth. We must keep opening our Bibles.

### The Answer: Both Adam and Israel

The most compelling interpretation identifies both Adam and Israel as the passage's subjects. Paul isn't choosing between them; he's describing the same human failure across generations.

If you are Jewish, your condemnation comes as an Israelite. Israel received the truth revealed and ignored it. You are without excuse.

But if you protest, "I am not an Israelite," the answer is simple: you are condemned because you are of Adam. Adam received the truth and rejected it. You are without excuse.

If you further object, "I was never presented this choice" or "I've never read the Bible or known Christians," the response is still: we all inherit both the wisdom and the sins of our ancestors. This is simply how the world works. No one starts with a clean slate.

This may sound unfair, but it isn't. Consider when Kevin Rudd apologized to Aboriginal Australians for the Stolen Generation—children forcibly removed from their families. I had never stolen anyone's child. I wasn't even born fifty years ago. Yet when he apologized, it became my apology. As an Australian citizen, I accept all that comes with citizenship: the wonderful climate, the advantages of an Australian passport, government welfare, free education, accessible universities, rule of law, democracy, minimum wage, and first-world living standards. I inherited these good things without earning them. We wouldn't trade them for anything.

But this raises a question: how can I inherit all the good things but refuse the bad? If I accept the benefits of being Australian, I must also accept the burdens. Just as I receive good legacies through citizenship, I must accept bad legacies too.

Similarly, we are in Adam. We inherit everything about being human—all the good and all the bad. One of those bad things is that our ancestor Adam sinned, and through him, we are all condemned. The conclusion for Adam and the conclusion for Israel is the same for us: **we have no excuse.**

## The Good News and the Warning

But here is the good news—and we will see this more fully in chapter 5.

If we are in Adam and so share in sin together, the wonderful upside is that **we can be in Christ and rise with Him together**—based on nothing we have done. God's righteousness has been revealed in the gospel.

But in Jesus, God's wrath is also revealed. On the cross, God's punishment fell on Jesus on behalf of those who follow Him. For those who do not believe, that same punishment awaits.

If you doubt that judgment is coming, look around. God's judgment is already visible in the world.

**Idolatry:** People exchange God's truth for lies and worship creatures rather than the Creator.

**Sexual immorality:** God gives people over to dishonorable desires. Women exchange natural relations for unnatural ones; men abandon natural relations with women and burn with passion for one another, committing shameless acts and receiving the due penalty for their error.

**All manner of wickedness:** People are filled with unrighteousness, evil, covetousness, malice, envy, murder, strife, deceit, gossip, slander, hatred of God, insolence, haughtiness, boasting, disobedience to parents, foolishness, faithlessness, heartlessness, and ruthlessness. They know God's righteous decree that such things deserve death, yet they not only practice them—they applaud others who do.

This is a world truly deserving of judgment. We are part of this world. We deserve condemnation too.

## Escape: Specific Revelation

The only way to escape this judgment is through the revealed gospel of Jesus Christ.

Christians are people who know this truth and have turned back to God. What distinguishes the wicked from us? We sin just as they do. The difference is that **we are forgiven**. The difference is that **we do not suppress the truth**.

So don't suppress it now. Tell your friends and family about Jesus. Don't be ashamed of the gospel.

There is no other revelation for them. They will not work out Jesus by themselves. They will not gaze at the stars and one day realize God exists. They will not bump into a tree and think, "There must be a God." They will not even observe your life and decide to repent. They need you to speak.

Your friends and family need you to tell them directly the message of the Bible. Without that message, they will not be saved from the judgment to come.
