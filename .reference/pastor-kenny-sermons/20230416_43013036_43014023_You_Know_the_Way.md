# John 13:36–14:23

**Title:** You Know the Way  
**Date:** April 16, 2023 (ARISE); March 13, 2015 (SMAC)  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Farewell Discourse

We find ourselves in one of Scripture's most intimate moments—Jesus' final night with His disciples. Having just washed their feet as an act of humble service, He now announces His departure. This isn't an abandonment but a purposeful going away, and His words address the deep anxiety His disciples feel.

The disciples' concern is understandable. When someone close to us leaves—whether a child's first day of school, a newlywed's first night away from parents, or a young person leaving for camp—we experience real loss and uncertainty. When Jesus tells them, "Where I am going, you cannot come," their fear escalates. Peter's protest is immediate and passionate: "Lord, why can't I follow you now? I will lay down my life for you."

But Jesus' response cuts through their panic with a call to faith: "Do not let your hearts be troubled. You believe in God; believe also in me."

## Part 1: Where Are You Going? (John 13:36–14:4)

### Jesus Is Preparing a Place

Jesus answers Peter's anxious question with reassurance. He's not simply disappearing—He has a reason for leaving. He has a task to accomplish for His disciples.

> "My Father's house has many rooms; if that were not so, would I have told you that I am going there to prepare a place for you?"

On the surface, this suggests Jesus is going to heaven to prepare physical dwelling places. Many interpreters understand it this way—that Jesus ascends to construct mansions and buildings He'll bring us to when He returns.

But there's a puzzle here. If the rooms already exist (the text says "many rooms"), why would preparation take so long? This suggests we should look deeper.

### The True Temple: Jesus' Body

When we search Scripture for this phrase "My Father's house," we find it used earlier in John's Gospel—in the temple cleansing account.

> "Get these out of here! Stop turning my Father's house into a market!"

And when challenged about His authority, Jesus says:

> "Destroy this temple, and I will raise it again in three days."

The disciples didn't understand at first. They thought He meant the physical temple in Jerusalem—that magnificent structure that took forty-six years to build. But John clarifies: "the temple he had spoken of was his body."

Josephus, an eyewitness historian of Jesus' day, describes the temple as having many apartments—chambers and rooms arranged on multiple stories, with passages connecting them. But Jesus is redefining what "My Father's house" means. It's not the stone building in Jerusalem. It's His own body.

This reinterpretation is crucial. Jesus is saying: "I am preparing my body for sacrifice. I am preparing myself for death."

### Perhaps Many Rooms Means Many Sufferings

If we understand "preparing the place" as preparing His body for death, then perhaps "many rooms" speaks metaphorically of the stages of suffering ahead. He will enter a room where He's mocked, another where He's beaten, another where He's pierced with nails, and so on. Each stage a room in the Father's house—a place of sacrifice and atonement.

This is why He tells Peter, "you cannot follow me now." Peter's willingness to die for Jesus is admirable but insufficient. The sacrifice for sin is something only Jesus can accomplish. Your closest friend cannot pay your debt to God. Your parents, however much they love you, cannot atone for your sins. Only Jesus—the Lamb of God—possesses the power to pay sin's ransom.

### A Room for Each of Us

By His death, Jesus has prepared a place for each believer. The Father's house contains "many rooms"—room enough for the whole world. Jesus' sacrifice is so complete, so infinite in its value, that it covers the sins of all who believe.

But how can we be sure we belong in that house? Many of us carry deep guilt. We've made serious mistakes. How could God possibly let us in?

The answer is humble and beautiful: we enter not based on our merit but on Christ's merit. We escape judgment not because we're good enough, but because Jesus is good enough, and His goodness becomes ours through faith.

## Part 2: How Do We Know the Way? (John 14:5–23)

### The Disciples' Confusion

Thomas expresses what the others are thinking. Jesus has promised a place with Him, but now His disciples are lost: How can we know where He's going? What path do we take to get there?

Jesus answers with one of Scripture's most profound declarations:

> "I am the way and the truth and the life. No one comes to the Father except through me."

### Echoes of John's Beginning

This language of "the way" recalls John the Baptist's ministry at the beginning of this Gospel. John was calling people to repent because "the Lord is coming on the way." Prepare the path. Someone is approaching over the horizon.

And that someone, we now discover, is Jesus Himself. Though the Father and the Son are two distinct persons, they are one in essence and purpose. To know Jesus is to know God. To see Jesus is to see God. To believe in Jesus is to believe in God.

### Three Ways They Are One

**They speak the same words.** Jesus says: "The words I say to you I do not speak on my own authority. Rather, it is the Father, living in me, who is doing his work."

**They possess the same authority.** The works Jesus does are the Father's works. When Jesus commands nature, heals the sick, and raises the dead, the Father is doing these things through Him.

**They share the same intimacy.** This intimacy is marked by the profound language of mutual indwelling: "I am in the Father, and the Father is in me."

Philip breaks in with his own request: "Lord, show us the Father and that will be enough for us." But Jesus' response carries gentle rebuke: "Don't you know me, Philip, even after I have been among you such a long time? Anyone who has seen me has seen the Father."

The disciples have spent three years with Jesus. They've watched His works. They've heard His words. They've touched Him. Yet still they want something more, something external, something dramatic. But Jesus is saying: you already have what you're looking for. I am the revelation of God you seek.

### Exclusive Access to God

Just as Jesus alone can atone for sins, Jesus alone gives access to the Father. This is like the Coca-Cola vault in Atlanta—guarded, exclusive, containing one irreplaceable secret. But Jesus gives His followers exclusive access to the greatest secret of all: intimate knowledge of God the Father.

### The Return and the Home

Strange as it might seem, Jesus shifts from preparing rooms to preparing His body, and now He speaks of His return:

> "If I go and prepare a place for you, I will come back and take you to be with me that you also may be where I am."

When will this happen? If we understand the preparation of the place as the immediate fulfillment of preparing His body through death and resurrection, then His return also finds immediate meaning: Jesus will rise from the dead and manifest Himself to His disciples.

But the promise extends beyond the resurrection appearances. We await our ultimate resurrection when He finally comes. Yet even now, we are seated with Christ in the heavenly realms (Ephesians 2:6).

The crucial point is this: heaven is not primarily a location. It's wherever Jesus is. As one puts it perfectly to their beloved: "Sweetheart, wherever you are—that's my favorite place." So it is with us and Jesus. His presence is our home.

### The Promise of the Holy Spirit (John 14:12–21)

Jesus then reveals how this intimate relationship will continue after His ascension. Through the work of the Holy Spirit, believers will experience the same kind of connection with Jesus that the Father and Son share.

Just as the Son does great works, so will believers—even greater works through the power of the Spirit.

Just as the Father answers the Son's prayers, so will the Son answer believers' prayers made in His name.

The Father will send another advocate—the Greek word *paraclete*, meaning "one who comes alongside"—a counselor, defender, and helper. This is the Holy Spirit, the Spirit of Truth. While the world cannot receive Him, believers know Him intimately. "He lives with you and will be in you."

This is the essence of Christian intimacy: the same presence that dwells in Jesus now dwells in us through His Spirit.

### Love as the Marker of Discipleship

Jesus then circles back to His original command:

> "Whoever has my commands and keeps them is the one who loves me."

What commands? He reminds them: "A new command I give you: Love one another. As I have loved you, so you must love one another. By this everyone will know that you are my disciples, if you love one another."

This is how the world will come to know Jesus—not through spectacular displays or external miracles, but through the radical love of Christ's followers for one another. Like a master apprentice whose excellent work reflects the master's skill, so our love for each other reflects Jesus' love to the watching world.

## Part 3: Why Not Show Yourself to the World? (John 14:22–23)

### The Final Objection

Judas (not Iscariot) presses the obvious question: "Lord, why do you intend to show yourself to us and not to the world?"

It's a fair question. If God is Jesus, shouldn't God reveal Himself to everyone? Didn't Jesus die for the world? Don't people deserve to know about these many rooms in the Father's house? Why restrict the revelation to a small band of disciples?

Jesus' answer strikes at the heart of faith:

> "Anyone who loves me will obey my teaching. My Father will love them, and we will come to them and make our home with them."

Notice the condition: "Anyone who loves me." Faith is not compelled by force or spectacle. It's a response to love. Those who love Jesus are those who submit to His teaching. And to such people, God reveals Himself intimately—not through overwhelming external displays, but through the indwelling presence of the Father and Son via the Holy Spirit.

### Our Task as Witnesses

But there's more here about visibility. Jesus doesn't withdraw from the world—He withdraws from the world through the disciples. We are His witnesses. We are His visible presence now.

This is Jesus' strategy: His disciples will manifest His character through their love for one another. The world will see disciples reflecting Jesus' character, and through that reflection, see Jesus Himself.

This calls us to a demanding but beautiful task. We must:

- **Put away envy and jealousy.** Stop comparing ourselves with one another. Stop competing for status or approval.

- **Put on generosity, kindness, and genuine goodwill.** Actively want good for one another. Serve sacrificially.

- **Put away apathy and self-centeredness.** Replace indifference with real commitment to one another's wellbeing.

- **Put away thin-skinned defensiveness.** Learn humility and accountability. Be willing to be corrected and to correct in love.

As we do this—as we embody Christ's love through our words and actions—we become the way, the truth, and the life to those around us. Through us, others encounter Jesus.

The Christian Union at Lancaster University understood this. An observer noted: "They're really lovely and don't care who knows it." Through their genuine love, their thoughtful discussions about hard questions like "Is the Bible sexist?" and "Where's God in mental illness?"—through their free lunches and their simple kindness—they got the ear of the campus atheist editor.

## Coming Home

Death frightens us. We wonder what it will be like, whether there's pain, whether consciousness continues. These are the deepest human fears.

But Jesus' resurrection assures us: death is not the end. Christ has defeated the final enemy. Even the terror of death cannot shake us, because we know we're going home—to wherever Jesus is.

It will be like coming home.
