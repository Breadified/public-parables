# Romans 5:1-21

**Title:** The Reconciliation of God
**Date:** 2 December 2012
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Problem: Death and Sin

### Why We Die

The human body is remarkable—it continuously renews itself. Our skeleton replaces itself every four years. Blood cycles through three times annually. Skin sheds and regenerates monthly. Even the gut lining refreshes every three days. Most of our bodies are technically less than ten years old.

Yet despite this constant renewal, we age and die. The question haunts us: if our bodies perpetually replace themselves, why can't we simply remain as we are?

Science offers several explanations. Some say cellular copies degrade over time. Others point to free radicals—unstable molecules created by oxygen metabolism that corrode our bodies. Still others invoke entropy, the second law of thermodynamics: all things naturally progress from order to disorder. Our bodies, too, inevitably move toward breakdown and death.

But Scripture gives us an answer—actually, two answers and a solution.

### The Root Cause: Sin Brings Death

> Therefore, just as sin came into the world through one man, and death through sin, and so death spread to all men because all sinned. (Romans 5:12)

Adam's sin in the Garden was not merely a personal failure. When God commanded him not to eat from the tree, and Adam disobeyed, sin entered the world. And with sin came death—not just for Adam, but for all humanity.

This is the tragic irony: it only takes one person to bring down an entire system.

Consider Gavrilo Princip—a nobody, unremarkable in every way. Yet in 1914, this Serbian assassinated Archduke Franz Ferdinand, heir to the Austro-Hungarian throne. This single act triggered a chain reaction that plunged all of Europe into World War I, killing up to 65 million people—roughly 3% of the world's population. One foolish man's decision reshaped the entire map of civilization and, through the unresolved tensions it left behind, sparked World War II and another 70 million deaths.

Adam was our Gavrilo Princip. And we are his descendants, bearing the genetic inheritance of his sin: death itself.

Even if we lived perfectly—never sinning, never breaking God's law—we would still die. We cannot escape this legacy. You cannot undo history. You cannot bring back the millions. You cannot unscramble an egg.

### A Troubling Question

Yet here lies a puzzle. Verse 13 tells us:

> but sin is not counted where there is no law. (Romans 5:13)

This echoes Romans 4:15: "For the law brings wrath, but where there is no law there is no transgression."

If there is no law, there is no sin. If there is no sin, there is no death. So what about those who lived before the law was given? What about Gentiles who never received God's law? Why should they die when they've broken no law?

Yet the text says:

> for sin indeed was in the world before the law was given, and sin was not counted where there was no law. Yet death reigned from Adam to Moses. (Romans 5:13-14)

This is the paradox. Death reigned universally, even over those without the law. If sin requires a law to be counted, and these people had no law, from whence came their sin and death?

### The Reversal: Death Brings Sin

Paul's answer is radical and often overlooked. He suggests that the causality runs in a different direction than we might expect:

> The sting of death is sin, and the power of sin is the law. (1 Corinthians 15:56)

We typically think: sin causes death. But Paul says the sting of death is sin. Our mortality makes us sinners.

Think about it. Our finite lives create pressure. Knowing we have limited time, we become driven to grab what we can while we can. We hoard. We become selfish, greedy, envious, jealous. We fear missing out. Our mortality breeds desperation, and desperation breeds sin.

This creates a vicious cycle. Death makes us sin. Our sin deepens our condemnation to death. Which makes us sin more. Round and round—trapped in a cycle with no escape. We are humans giving birth over graves.

### We Are Surrounded by Death

Look around. In just the past year, our community has experienced the loss of grandparents and grandmothers. Young students have died. Most of us carry fresh wounds of loss.

And the pattern continues relentlessly: first our grandparents die, then our parents, then us, then our children. Death surrounds us. There is no escape.

Who will save us from this?

## The Solution: One Man's Righteousness

### A Better Comparison

> But the free gift is not like the trespass. For if many died through one man's trespass, much more have the grace of God and the free gift by the grace of that one man Jesus Christ abounded for many. (Romans 5:15)

Just as one man's sin brought death to all humanity, one man's righteousness can reverse it all.

Consider Steve Jobs. He founded Apple Computer based on a simple, beautiful vision. The company grew around him. When the board sacked him, Apple's fortunes collapsed immediately. Jobs went on to found Pixar, creating Toy Story. Then Apple realized its mistake, brought him back, and he gave the world the iPod. With Jobs, Apple's stock soared and the company thrived again.

One man rescued the entire company.

Similarly, it takes only one man to rescue humanity from death. But not just any man. Jesus is God himself, come to reverse the effects of sin. Through his divine nature, his death on the cross possesses infinite power. His sacrifice undoes what Adam broke and restores what was lost.

The contrast is absolute:

| Adam | Christ |
|------|--------|
| One trespass | One act of righteousness |
| Brought condemnation | Brought justification |
| Death reigned | Life reigns |
| Many made sinners | Many made righteous |

> Therefore, as one trespass led to condemnation for all men, so one act of righteousness leads to justification and life for all men. For as by the one man's disobedience the many were made sinners, so by the one man's obedience the many will be made righteous. (Romans 5:18-19)

### Christ Died for You at Your Worst

> For while we were still weak, at the right time Christ died for the ungodly... but God shows his love for us in that while we were still sinners, Christ died for us. (Romans 5:6, 8)

The timing of Jesus's sacrifice reveals his love. He died not for the righteous, but for the ungodly. Not for the strong, but for the weak. Not when we had cleaned ourselves up, but while we were still sinners.

This is extraordinary because it runs counter to everything in our world.

The university picks you based on your best ATAR scores. Employers hire you based on your best resume. We sell our houses for the best price. Children enter schools and sports teams based on their best performance.

The world always demands your best.

But God does something radically different. God looks at your worst and says, "I love you."

Consider this: Two Australian lovers were arrested in Malaysia. The girl was caught with drugs—allegedly her boyfriend's—and faced execution. Did the boyfriend return to fight for her? No. He flew back to Australia alone. Even romantic love has limits.

But Jesus accepts what no one else would accept. He embraces your worst self. Your ungodliness. Your weakness. Your sin. Your guilt.

This is why the crucifixion is such good news.

### Why This Gives Us Assurance

> Since, therefore, we have now been justified by his blood, much more shall we be saved by him from the wrath of God. For if while we were enemies we were reconciled to God by the death of his Son, much more, now that we are reconciled, shall we be saved by his life. (Romans 5:9-10)

Here lies our confidence for the future. God accepted you when you were his enemy. God forgave you when you were at your absolute worst. He paid the price for your sins when you could offer nothing in return.

So ask yourself: if God took you in when you were your worst, why would he change his mind now? What reason would he have to reject you in the future when the very grounds for accepting you remain unchanged—his grace and mercy?

If you fear you're not good enough for God's future salvation, you're asking the wrong question. The question is not whether you're good enough. It's whether God is merciful. And he has already proven his mercy by accepting you while you were ungodly.

Assurance works like mathematical induction. The pattern has been established: God forgave your worst. Therefore, the pattern continues into the future.

### When Doubt Comes

When you doubt, resist the temptation to look inward. Looking at yourself—"I am such a sinner"—only multiplies doubt.

Instead, look to Christ. See his extraordinary goodness. Reflect on the fact that he justified you while you were a sinner, an enemy, at your absolute worst. If he accepted that version of you, he will certainly stand by you in your better moments.

Your assurance rests on two truths: (1) you were as low as you could possibly be, and (2) Christ is infinitely higher than you can imagine. Hold these truths, and you have every confidence for the future.

## The Response: Rejoice and Rest

> Therefore, since we have been justified by faith, we have peace with God through our Lord Jesus Christ. Through him we have also obtained access by faith into this grace in which we stand, and we rejoice in hope of the glory of God. (Romans 5:1-2)

> Not only that, but we rejoice in our sufferings, knowing that suffering produces endurance, and endurance produces character, and character produces hope, and hope does not put us to shame, because God's love has been poured into our hearts through the Holy Spirit who has been given to us. (Romans 5:3-5)

Knowing that you are reconciled to God changes everything. The war is over. Christ has won. You are at peace—not the peace of death, but the peace of a restored relationship with your Creator.

This peace reshapes how you face suffering and loss. You can hold the things of this world more loosely. You can endure hardship with purpose, knowing it produces character. You can face death itself without ultimate fear.

Because you know what lies beyond.
