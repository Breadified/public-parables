# James 3:14–4:12

**Title:** Faith of the Humble  
**Date:** November 4, 2012  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Sin of Envy

### Understanding Envy

Envy is the sin of comparison—desiring what others have because they possess it. From our earliest moments reaching for toys to our final days competing for cemetery plots, envy is woven into human nature. The world even celebrates it: economists justify envy as the engine of progress, and advertisers exploit it relentlessly. We've given envy a color—green—because the grass always appears greener on the other side.

But as Christians, James calls us to something radically different. Scripture warns us:

> Who is wise and understanding among you? Let them show it by their good life, by deeds done in the humility that comes from wisdom. But if you harbor bitter envy and selfish ambition in your hearts, do not boast about it or deny the truth. (James 3:13–14)

There is nothing to boast about when our hearts are consumed by envy and selfish ambition. Such "wisdom" does not come from God.

### The Nature of Worldly Wisdom

James is clear about the source of this kind of thinking:

> Such "wisdom" does not come down from heaven but is earthly, unspiritual, demonic. For where you have envy and selfish ambition, there you find disorder and every evil practice. (James 3:15–16)

When we live by comparison and covetousness, we embrace worldly thinking—the very opposite of God's wisdom. This produces disorder, conflict, and spiritual emptiness.

### Envy in Our Culture

We live in a culture saturated with envy. Social media, advertising, and consumer culture constantly whisper: *Your neighbor has this, so you should too.* We see someone's vacation and suddenly need to travel there. We witness a friend's child in piano lessons and feel pressure to do the same. We scroll through Instagram and accumulate a list of things we didn't know we wanted until we saw someone else enjoying them.

The solution is radical: eliminate unnecessary dissatisfaction. Consider cutting away the sources of comparison—unsubscribe from advertising, limit social media, block those who consistently boast. When temptation to compare feels overwhelming, give yourself permission to step back for thirty days.

## From Envy to Violence

### The Escalation of Desire

Envy doesn't stop in the heart. It spills outward into our relationships and communities:

> What causes fights and quarrels among you? Don't they come from your desires that battle within you? (James 4:1)

When we want what someone else possesses, the next step is taking it. The progression is brutal: from wanting, to quarreling, to fighting, to stealing, to killing.

> You desire but do not have, so you kill. You covet but you cannot get what you want, so you quarrel and fight. You do not have because you do not ask God. When you ask, you do not receive, because you ask with wrong motives, that you may spend what you get on your pleasures. (James 4:2–3)

This seems extreme until we look at Scripture itself. The Bible repeatedly traces envy to violence.

### Biblical Examples of Envy's Destruction

**Cain and Abel:** Cain's offering was rejected while Abel's found favor with the Lord. Consumed by envy, Cain murdered his own brother in the field.

**Joseph and His Brothers:** The patriarchs were jealous of Joseph because their father favored him. They plotted to kill him, ultimately selling him into slavery in Egypt instead.

**Saul and David:** When David returned from battle, the women sang, "Saul has killed his thousands, and David his ten thousands." This simple comparison ignited jealousy in Saul's heart. He attempted to pin David to the wall with his spear, driven by envy of David's growing popularity and favor.

### Envy Disguised as Righteousness

We witness the same pattern today, though we often disguise it as justice:

- When someone successful receives a parking fine, we think they deserve it
- When a celebrity faces media harassment, we say they're too popular
- When we hear of a wealthy person's troubles, we judge them as wasteful or arrogant
- When someone excels academically, we suspect cheating
- We attribute others' success to privilege rather than celebrating their blessing

These are not righteous acts. They are acts of envy dressed in the language of fairness.

When a movement loses its moral foundation and begins to destroy, steal, and harm others, it has transformed from protest for equal opportunity into the politics of envy—*how can I take from you?* rather than *how can we rise together?*

## The Battle Within and Without

The fights outside us originate in the battles within us. Our desires wage war against our hearts, and we carry that conflict into our relationships.

Envy particularly festers in places of equality—among school friends, family, church members. We are most envious of those we consider our peers. At a school reunion, you encounter people once like you—the same age, same education, same teachers. Yet now some have surpassed you. The comparison stings because the baseline seemed equal.

Church can become a dangerous place for envy for this very reason. We are brothers and sisters in Christ, children of the same Father, yet when some experience greater material success or spiritual advancement, bitterness can creep in. We compare marriages, houses, cars, children, achievements—and resentment grows.

The Lord, however, gives grace as He apportions it. He is under no obligation to distribute blessings equally. More is given, more is expected. It is not our place to judge others by their circumstances.

### Envy Sets Us Against God

The deepest tragedy is this: when we embrace the world's values of accumulation and comparison, we make ourselves enemies of God.

> You adulterous people, don't you know that friendship with the world means enmity against God? Therefore, anyone who chooses to be a friend of the world becomes an enemy of God. (James 4:4)

Envy was the very motivation behind Jesus's persecution. The chief priests and teachers of the law were envious of the attention Jesus received, the influence He wielded, His growing popularity. They envied Him, sought to outdo Him, came to hate Him, and finally orchestrated His death. When we are envious, we align ourselves with those who put Jesus to death.

## The Antidote: Humility

### God's Grace to the Humble

Scripture teaches a profound reversal:

> But he gives us more grace. That is why Scripture says: "God opposes the proud but gives grace to the humble." (James 4:6, quoting Proverbs 3:34)

God's Spirit and grace are not given to the proud. They flow to the humble. The spirit we possess is meant to be a spirit of humility, not envy. God jealously guards that humility in us, longing for us to reflect His character of dependence on Him, not grasping for more.

### The Path of Humility

James calls us to a specific sequence of actions:

> Submit yourselves, then, to God. Resist the devil, and he will flee from you. Come near to God and he will come near to you. Wash your hands, you sinners, and purify your hearts, you double-minded. Grieve, mourn and wail. Change your laughter to mourning and your joy to gloom. Humble yourselves before the Lord, and he will lift you up. (James 4:7–10)

To resist the devil is not to engage in direct spiritual combat—we are not called to exorcisms or deliverance ministries. Rather, we resist the attitudes of the world and the devil that he represents. We resist *envy*. We resist *pride*. Just as God resists the proud, we resist the worldly attitudes that ensnare us. In doing so, the things of the world flee from us.

Submission to God involves:
- **Washing our hands:** Cleansing ourselves from worldly desires
- **Purifying our hearts:** Dealing with double-mindedness and divided loyalties
- **Grieving and mourning:** Recognizing the weight of our sin
- **Humbling ourselves:** Acknowledging our need for God's mercy

And the promised reward? God will lift you up. He will restore you. He will exalt you not because you deserve it, but because His grace reaches to the humble.

### The Example of Christ

Look to Jesus as our model:

> Who, being in very nature God, did not consider equality with God something to be grasped, but made himself nothing, taking the very nature of a servant, being made in human likeness. (Philippians 2:6–7)

Christ emptied Himself. He humbled Himself. He became nothing so that we might become heirs of God. When we humble ourselves before Him, confessing our sins and pleading for mercy, we receive the grace that He alone can give—forgiveness and restoration.

## Living as the Humble

### From Judgment to Love

Once we are humble before God, we can extend humility to others:

> Brothers and sisters, do not slander one another. Anyone who speaks against a brother or sister or judges them speaks against the law and judges it. When you judge the law, you are not keeping it, but sitting in judgment on it. (James 4:11)

Instead of wishing our neighbor harm because of envy, we are called to seek their good. Love does not envy. Love celebrates another's blessing. Love is marked by gratitude for what God has given us and contentment in our own portion, not comparison with another's.

### The Only Judge

> There is only one Lawgiver and Judge, the one who is able to save and destroy. But you—who are you to judge your neighbor? (James 4:12)

Jesus is the one who is able to **save** us. Though we may lose our possessions, our reputation, even our life, we will not lose God, for He will save us. Through the sacrifice of His Son, we have been saved from God's judgment. Jesus paid the price and satisfied what we truly desire: forgiveness of our sin and rebellion. He has saved us then, and He will save us again when He returns.

---

## A Final Word

If you are not yet a Christian, let go of your own self-determination. Put your life into God's hands. Humble yourself, and He will lift you up.

If you are a Christian, recognize the envy that creeps into your heart. It disguises itself as justice, as ambition, as reasonable desire. Be filled instead with gratitude for what God has already given you. Be content. Do not compare. Therefore, do not be envious. Be humble toward God and toward each other.

This is the faith of the humble—the faith that trusts God's provision, celebrates others' blessings, and rests in the security that our worth is not determined by what we own or achieve, but by the grace of God in Christ.
