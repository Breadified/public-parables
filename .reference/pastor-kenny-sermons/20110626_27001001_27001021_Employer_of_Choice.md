# Daniel 1:1-21

**Title:** Employer of Choice
**Date:** June 26, 2011
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Finding Yourself in a Foreign Land

When I first came to Sydney in October, I was overwhelmed. The cold air made my lips sore as I sat on my mother's lap, watching shops that seemed utterly foreign—everything was in English, the televisions looked different, and the culture was so distinctly Western. Back then, you'd notice when you saw another Asian person at the shopping center. I missed home deeply.

Daniel found himself in a similar position. He too was displaced in a foreign land, far from everything familiar. The Book of Daniel can feel foreign to us as well—with its multiple genres blending history and apocalyptic literature, its dates jumping around, and its mix of languages and versions. Yet at its core, this book is about finding yourself when you're displaced in a foreign land.

## Part 1: Thoroughly Defeated

Daniel's journey begins in devastation. Jerusalem has been besieged, and Judah has suffered complete defeat.

> In the third year of the reign of Jehoiakim king of Judah, Nebuchadnezzar king of Babylon came to Jerusalem and besieged it. (Daniel 1:1)

**The Exile**

The first exile came in 605 BC when the royal blood was taken. Jerusalem fell into trouble, and there would be a second exile in 597 BC, followed by another in 587 BC.

**The Plundering**

> And the Lord delivered Jehoiakim king of Judah into his hand, along with some of the articles from the temple of God. These he carried off to the temple of his god in Babylonia and put in the treasure house of his god. (Daniel 1:2)

Not only did they lose the war—they lost the sacred objects of worship. In that pre-Christian era, the temple was everything. It was how you demonstrated faith, how sins were forgiven, and how you worshiped God. To have the temple's treasures removed was like serving a meal without its essential ingredient. Worse than that, it was a judgment on God himself—His house's treasures now resting in the house of a foreign god. It was utterly embarrassing.

**The Assimilation**

> Then the king ordered Ashpenaz, chief of his court officials, to bring in some of the Israelites from the royal family and the nobility—young men without any physical defect, handsome, showing aptitude for every kind of learning, well informed, quick to understand, and qualified to serve in the king's palace. He was to teach them the language and literature of the Babylonians. The king assigned them a daily amount of food and wine from the king's table. They were to be trained for three years, and after that they were to enter the king's service. (Daniel 1:3-5)

The Babylonians had a deliberate policy of assimilation. They took the best young men—the cream of Judah's youth—and removed them to serve their conquerors. It was systematically devastating.

**Identity Stripped Away**

> Among these were some from Judah: Daniel, Hananiah, Mishael and Azariah. The chief official gave them new names: to Daniel, the name Belteshazzar; to Hananiah, Shadrach; to Mishael, Meshach; and to Azariah, Abednego. (Daniel 1:6-7)

They even changed their names. This is an age-old mechanism of assimilation—erasing identity at the most fundamental level. In my own experience with immigrants, I've seen this pattern repeated. When you're renamed, your very identity is being rewritten by those in power.

Daniel appears utterly defeated. The God of Babylon has won. The invaders have triumphed completely.

This parallels the tragic history of indigenous peoples. When Aboriginal children in Australia were taken and re-educated, when their language was stripped away and they were assimilated into white culture, it was devastating. Similarly, the impact of slavery in America left wounds so deep that people still struggle to find their identity. When even your name comes from the oppressor, when the very language you use to express your frustration is borrowed from those you oppose, identity becomes fractured.

Some of you reading this may identify with being immigrants or displaced people yourselves.

**But Here's the Spiritual Reality**

As Christians, we are in the same defeated position as Daniel. We are exiles.

> Peter, an apostle of Jesus Christ, To God's elect, strangers in the world, scattered throughout Pontus, Galatia, Cappadocia, Asia and Bithynia (1 Peter 1:1)

This world is not our land. These are not our names. This is not our culture—even if you're Asian, that identity isn't ultimately yours either. The music isn't us. The entertainment isn't us. You might be popular and fit in well in your environment, but you are an alien, a stranger. You might be respected at work, have great conversations, and help your colleagues—yet they are not your true friends, not the people you'll spend eternity with. Your study is not yours. Your career is not yours. Your university is not yours. Your school is not yours. Whatever you're proud of as your identity—if it comes from the world—it's not truly yours.

It's almost like we're living in a matrix. We don't recognize we're in a foreign land because it feels so normal. Aboriginal children didn't realize they were being assimilated because they had nothing to compare it to. We befriend our enemies, which is why we find the judgment of non-Christians so distasteful. We say, "Why would God judge the nations?" We forget: *they are enemies*.

This is like Stockholm syndrome. In 1973, bank robbers held employees hostage, and those victims became emotionally attached to their captors, defending them even after escape. We've done the same spiritually—we've grown comfortable with our captors.

**But your boss tempting you with promotions in exchange for your integrity is not your friend.** The classmate encouraging you to compromise your conscience is not your friend. The colleague espousing tolerance and telling us we all just need to get along is not your friend. **Nebuchadnezzar is not your friend.**

The world is a foreign place for Christians. The world doesn't owe us anything. When you think of "the world," picture it as Babylon—a place of defeat. The earth is not heaven on earth; it's Babylon. Even if the world were wealthier and better educated, even if someone like Barack Obama—rich and president—were in charge, you still wouldn't belong. That was his struggle too.

## Part 2: Victory

Now contrast that picture of defeat with what happens by the end of Chapter 1.

**Daniel Became Healthier**

> At the end of the ten days they looked healthier and better nourished than any of the young men who ate the royal food. So the guard took away their choice food and the wine they were to drink and gave them vegetables instead. (Daniel 1:15-16)

**Daniel Became More Knowledgeable**

> To these four young men God gave knowledge and understanding of all kinds of literature and learning. And Daniel could understand visions and dreams of all kinds. At the end of the time set by the king to bring them in, the chief official presented them to Nebuchadnezzar. The king talked with them, and he found none equal to Daniel, Hananiah, Mishael and Azariah; so they entered the king's service. In every matter of wisdom and understanding about which the king questioned them, he found them ten times better than all the magicians and enchanters in his whole kingdom. (Daniel 1:17-20)

None were equal to Daniel. Imagine being ten times better than your classmates in every subject.

**Daniel Outlived, Outplayed, and Outlasted**

> And Daniel remained there until the first year of King Cyrus. (Daniel 1:21)

Through the entire Book of Daniel, we'll see that he lived through four kings. He was the ultimate survivor.

Meanwhile, his enemies fell into trouble:

> In the second year of his reign, Nebuchadnezzar had dreams; his mind was troubled and he could not sleep. So the king summoned the magicians, enchanters, sorcerers and astrologers to tell him what he had dreamed. When they came in and stood before the king, he said to them, "I have had a dream that troubles me and I want to know what it means." Then the astrologers answered the king in Aramaic, "O king, live forever! Tell your servants the dream, and we will interpret it." (Daniel 2:1-4)

God looked after Daniel. He provided the miracle of health despite a different diet. He gave knowledge and understanding. He sent Cyrus. He sent the dreams that would exalt Daniel and expose the limitations of Babylon's wisest men.

## Part 3: Standing Out—The Process Behind Victory

The reason Daniel experienced victory was simple: **one young man stood up for God.**

Like Joseph. Like Moses.

> But Daniel resolved not to defile himself with the royal food and wine, and he asked the chief official for permission not to defile himself this way. Now God had caused the official to show favor and sympathy to Daniel, but the official told Daniel, "I am afraid of my lord the king, who has assigned your food and drink. Why should he see you looking worse than the other young men your age? The king would then have my head because of you." Daniel then said to the guard whom the chief official had appointed over Daniel, Hananiah, Mishael and Azariah, "Please test your servants for ten days: Give us nothing but vegetables to eat and water to drink. Then compare our appearance with that of the young men who eat the royal food, and treat your servants in accordance with what you see." So he agreed to this and tested them for ten days. (Daniel 1:8-14)

Daniel's refusal was rooted in the food laws of Leviticus. He determined not to defile himself—and notice God worked through the official, causing him to show favor. Daniel was making the first vegan argument in Scripture, trusting that God would sustain them on vegetables. And God did.

In the midst of defeat, one man stood up for God. That's why victory came. That's the pattern.

Six hundred years later, another man stood up. Jesus. Another wise man. He too did not defile himself—through sin, through disobedience. And He trusted that God would look after Him.

**How Do We Stand Up?**

The food laws aren't binding anymore—Jesus fulfilled them, and we Gentiles are included in God's grace. But the principle is eternal: standing up means trusting God by keeping His commands.

> Your boasting is not good. Don't you know that a little yeast works through the whole batch of dough? Get rid of the old yeast that you may be a new batch without yeast—as you really are. For Christ, our Passover lamb, has been sacrificed. Therefore let us keep the Festival, not with the old yeast, the yeast of malice and wickedness, but with bread without yeast—the bread of sincerity and truth. (1 Corinthians 5:6-8)

**Standing up looks like:**

- Not cheating on your tax return; paying your GST
- Not being stingy; financially supporting your church
- Giving to the poor
- When I decided to enter ministry, I gave up full-time work for six years in the middle of marriage and raising children. Part-time ministry work was enough. It might collapse tomorrow, but so far, God has cared for us.

**And trust that God will look after you.**

Some of you are wrestling with idols—and part of you fears you'll miss out if you let them go.

- **Holidays, fun, parties, friends:** You think you'll lose friends, but you'll gain better ones. Trust God and see.
- **Study, wealth, parents, career:** Don't put all your eggs in one basket. Invest in people. Trust God and see.
- **Relationships—marriage:** Stick with your spouse. Trust God and see.
- **Relationships—choosing a partner:** Stay away from immature Christians and non-Christians. If you're already in such a relationship, break it off. Trust God and see. It's okay not to be in a relationship—look at your leaders. They're excellent examples of this. No messy relationship drama, no jumping in and out of commitments.

## The Model Survivor

Daniel was a man who realized this world is not our home. He longed for heaven. We'll see further examples of Daniel and his friends standing up for God in a world set against God.

That's the victory that comes from one simple choice: **to stand apart, to refuse to be assimilated, and to trust God.**
