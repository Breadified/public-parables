# Acts 4:1-20

**Title:** Keep Speaking

**Date:** 7 August 2016 (PH Baptist CDP); 19 May 2026 (CIC)

## Our Authority Comes from God, Not Government

The disciples faced fierce opposition after healing the lame beggar at the temple gate. The religious authorities—the priests, temple captain, and Sadducees—confronted Peter and John while they were still speaking to the crowds.

> The priests and the captain of the temple guard and the Sadducees came up to Peter and John while they were speaking to the people. They were greatly disturbed because the apostles were teaching the people, proclaiming in Jesus the resurrection of the dead. (Acts 4:1-2)

The disciples were doing three things that provoked the authorities: **speaking, teaching, and proclaiming**—all centered on Jesus as Lord and His resurrection. Even their miracles flowed through speech; they weren't performing magic tricks but declaring God's truth.

Their core message was unmistakable: **Jesus is Lord.** This wasn't merely a spiritual platitude about heaven after death. It was a political claim—that Jesus is King of the entire universe, that He will judge the world, and that His Lordship supersedes every earthly authority. For those who repent, salvation is available. They were calling people to live as new citizens of a new kingdom under a new Lord.

When brought before the Sanhedrin, Peter declared boldly:

> "Rulers and elders of the people! If we are being called to account today for an act of kindness shown to a man who was lame and are being asked how he was healed, then know this, you and all the people of Israel: It is by the name of Jesus Christ of Nazareth, whom you crucified but whom God raised from the dead, that this man stands before you healed. Jesus is 'the stone you builders rejected, which has become the cornerstone.'" (Acts 4:8-11)

Peter's reply directly challenged their authority. He was essentially saying: Your power is not ultimate. The Lordship of Christ supersedes your rulership. That is our **permission and authority to speak**.

### The Question of Authority

We hear this objection constantly: "By what right do you impose your values on us?"

The answer is simple: we're not imposing anything—we're speaking. And more fundamentally, our authority doesn't come from government. It comes from God Himself, who has established His laws, created marriage as He designed it, set boundaries for human flourishing, and calls all people to repentance. If people call that imposing, so be it. We call it encouraging, warning, and challenging.

We are God's prophets on earth, His instruments. Some places enjoy explicit freedom of speech protections—America's First Amendment, for instance, was established by those fleeing religious persecution who built a system to protect Christian liberty. Other nations, like Australia, don't have explicit constitutional guarantees but recognize freedom of political communication through High Court interpretation and state human rights legislation.

**But here's the crucial point: depending on government for our religious freedom is counterproductive.** Government doesn't *give* us our freedoms—it *recognizes* them. If we believe our fundamental rights come from the state, we become enslaved to its whims. We'll be tempted to restrict the gospel to private, spiritual matters: nice platitudes about heaven when you die, obedience to authorities, and nothing more.

This is precisely what authoritarian regimes like China's accept. They're perfectly comfortable with Christianity that stays quiet about injustice, teaches submission to state power, and limits itself to funeral services promising heavenly reward. That poses no threat.

But Jesus was offensive—not because He preached niceness and afterlife comfort, but because He was political. He proclaimed His Lordship *now*, not just in some distant heaven. "The Kingdom of Heaven is here," He taught. He confronted the religious and political leaders of His day and told them they were wrong.

### The Universality of Christ's Lordship

Here's what transformed my understanding: **Jesus' Lordship is over everything.** Just as sin permeates every sphere of our lives—in every aspect and every relationship—so too does Christ's rule extend into every nook and cranny.

This means the gospel speaks to *all* areas of life:
- John the Baptist called King Herod to account over his marriage
- Jesus had opinions on taxes
- Paul spoke to the nature of welfare and work
- Scripture speaks to economics, law, governance, defense, innovation, and every sphere of human society

The gospel is *political*—not in the partisan sense, but in the original sense: it relates to the *polis*, the city, everything about how we live together. There is no such thing as a "gospel issue" separate from other topics. Everything is ultimately a gospel issue; the difference is only in timing.

When we understand Christ's universal Lordship, we recognize that we, collectively as God's people, have a biblical perspective on all areas of life. We have permission to speak with authority on everything.

This isn't about dominating politics. It's about ensuring that when decisions affecting everyone are made at the highest levels—in parliaments and councils—the godliest voices are present to speak biblical truth. But that's just one place. We speak everywhere:
- In political parties and community organizations
- At school meetings and workplace gatherings
- At family discussions and university cafeterias
- On social media and in everyday conversations
- Within our churches and Christian campus groups

Tragically, our churches—where we have the most permission to speak—are often the most censored. We need to be informed, equipped, and bold enough to engage in every sphere. **We have to speak.**

## We Are Censored, But We Speak with Courage

The authorities arrested Peter and John overnight, then brought them before the full council. The problem was undeniable—everyone in Jerusalem knew a genuine miracle had occurred. They couldn't deny the facts. So they turned to intimidation:

> "To stop this thing from spreading any further among the people, we must warn them to speak no longer to anyone in this name." Then they called them in again and commanded them not to speak or teach at all in the name of Jesus. (Acts 4:17-18)

What amazed the council wasn't Peter and John's eloquence or education. It was their courage:

> When they saw the courage of Peter and John and realized that they were unschooled, ordinary men, they were astonished and they took note that these men had been with Jesus. (Acts 4:13)

These were ordinary people—no formal religious training, no status, no credentials. But they had something far more powerful: **courage**. And that courage came from a higher allegiance.

When commanded to be silent, Peter and John responded with breathtaking clarity:

> "Which is right in God's eyes: to listen to you, or to him? You be the judges! As for us, we cannot help speaking about what we have seen and heard." (Acts 4:19-20)

The authorities tried the carrot first (intimidation), and when that didn't work, they were prepared to move to the stick (violence). But the disciples understood something fundamental: there is a higher authority than any earthly power, and allegiance to God transcends fear of man.

**Courage transforms ordinary people into the extraordinary.**

I think of a brave woman arrested this week for offering help to women considering abortion outside a fertility clinic in Melbourne, operating in direct violation of "Safe Access Zone" laws. She's an ordinary mum—perhaps with thirteen children, a mortgage to pay, the normal responsibilities of life. But she obeyed God's law instead of man's law. She took an enormous risk, and her courageous actions deserve our applause and our emulation.

### The Call to Extraordinary Courage

There was a time—perhaps a generation ago—when an ordinary Christian life was sufficient. You could be faithful in your family, honest in your work, regular in church attendance, and live a quiet, unremarkable life without making waves. Perhaps such times existed.

But this is not one of those times.

As the saying goes:

> "Perhaps there are times and places in the history of the world in which it is possible to go through life as just an ordinary, good person. Perhaps. But perhaps [this time] is not one of those times and places. Rather, in our contemporary society, the only way to be good is to be heroic. Failing to act with heroism inevitably makes us complicit in grave evils."

For years, I lived as an ordinary Christian. I'd been a believer for over thirty years, doing church activities, supporting evangelism, trusting that Christian education would happen elsewhere. The world seemed manageable. Our church rented space from a secular school; they didn't care what we believed or taught.

But the dominos have fallen quickly. The same-sex marriage debate exposed abortion issues. That exposed religious freedom concerns. Those exposed attacks on parental rights and safe school policies. The cascade continues. **We no longer have the luxury of being ordinary Christians.**

Some say they'll act later, when the stakes are higher. I don't believe them. History shows that those who remained silent when they could have spoken become the ones who report dissenters to authorities when repression comes.

Where is your red line? When will you speak? When will you act?

We must become **extraordinary Christians** for extraordinary times. If you've been voting, that's no longer enough. If you've been handing out how-to-votes, that's no longer enough. If you've been quiet, you can't remain quiet anymore.

We cannot help speaking about what we have seen and heard.

Speak on talkback radio. Speak in your workplace. Speak at city council meetings. Speak in your family. Speak with courage, informed by Scripture, guided by prayer, and trusting in the God who raised Jesus from the dead.

That is our calling. That is our privilege. That is our responsibility.
