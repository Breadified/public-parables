# Luke 12:35-48

**Title:** Division and Preparation
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Part One: Jesus Brings Division

### The Unexpected Teaching

At first glance, it seems strange that Jesus would bring division. We think of Him as the Prince of Peace, and at Christmas we celebrate "peace on earth and goodwill towards men." Yet Jesus plainly states:

> Do you think I came to bring peace on earth? No, I tell you, but division. From now on there will be five in one family divided against each other, three against two and two against three. They will be divided: father against son and son against father, mother against daughter and daughter against mother, mother-in-law against daughter-in-law and daughter-in-law against mother-in-law.

This is jarring. Why would the Messiah bring such fracturing into families and communities?

### Why Division Occurs

The answer lies in the nature of truth itself. God's Word is alive and active:

> For the word of God is alive and active. Sharper than any double-edged sword, it penetrates even to dividing soul and spirit, joints and marrow; it judges the thoughts and attitudes of the heart. (Hebrews 4:12)

Jesus came as a sword—not to wage war, but to judge. And what do swords do? They divide. A sharp instrument separates truth from lies, good from evil. Jesus' mission was to restore the proper understanding of good and evil that had been inverted since the Garden of Eden, where Adam and Eve redefined the forbidden fruit as "pleasing to the eye and good to eat" rather than recognizing it as the path to death.

When truth is proclaimed, people respond in fundamentally different ways. Paul captures this reality:

> For we are to God the pleasing aroma of Christ among those who are being saved and those who are perishing. To the one we are an aroma that brings death; to the other, an aroma that brings life. (2 Corinthians 2:15-16)

One response to God's truth ranges from genuine belief to cautious interest to sincere consideration. But another response is hostile rejection—offense, accusations of brainwashing, demands to have nothing to do with the messenger or the message.

### Jesus Never Compromised Truth

Jesus did not adapt His message to please people or avoid offense. He spoke what was true, not what people wanted to hear. He never sought to be "politically correct." Because He refused to compromise truth, His message created outrage, violent protests, and eventually His own execution.

This same pattern continues today. When we honestly speak truth, we should expect division. Christians should actually be suspicious when there is no division at all. In meetings where everyone simply agrees, you may have groupthink rather than genuine conversation. Conflict—non-violent, honest conflict—is a sign that real truth-telling is happening. It's a zone of authentic conversation.

### Sharp Lines Between Good and Evil

Jesus drew very sharp distinctions:

> Woe to those who call evil good and good evil, who put darkness for light and light for darkness. (Isaiah 5:20)

He spoke of those who "loved darkness instead of light because their deeds were evil" (John 3:19). At the end of the age, His angels will separate "everything that causes sin and all who do evil" from "the righteous will shine like the sun in the kingdom of their Father" (Matthew 13:40-43).

Jesus refused to blur the line between good and evil. Modern teaching often seeks to blur this line, but when we do, we invite all kinds of evil. We urgently need to return to Jesus' clarity: some things are genuinely good, and some things are genuinely evil.

### Division in the Future

Jesus also speaks of future division:

> I have come to bring fire on the earth, and how I wish it were already kindled! But I have a baptism to undergo, and what constraint I am under until it is completed! (Luke 12:49-50)

This "fire on the earth" refers to future judgment and destruction. Jesus speaks of His own "baptism"—not water baptism, but the baptism of fire through which He will take upon Himself the sins of the world on the cross. The Son will be separated from the Father at Calvary for our sakes, experiencing the ultimate division so that we might be reconciled to God.

## Part Two: Jesus Brings Unity

Yet there is another side to this teaching. If Jesus brings fire in the future, what about today?

### Peace for Those Who Believe

Immediately after His warning about division, Jesus calls for reconciliation:

> As you are going with your adversary to the magistrate, try hard to be reconciled on the way, or your adversary may drag you off to the judge, and the judge turn you over to the officer, and the officer throw you into prison. I tell you, you will not get out until you have paid the last penny. (Luke 12:58-59)

This passage hints at the possibility of reconciliation. Jesus speaks of peace—but it is peace rooted in our relationship with God:

> When you enter a house, first say, 'Peace to this house.' If someone who promotes peace is there, your peace will rest on them. (Luke 10:5-6)

True peace is our response to God. When we are reconciled with God, peace naturally flows between individuals.

> Peace I leave with you; my peace I give to you. Not as the world gives do I give to you. (John 14:27)

### Seek Reconciliation with God Now

Jesus urges His hearers to put things right while there is still time. The illustration is sobering: God is our adversary because of our guilt. Like a wise attorney counseling clients, Jesus tells us: plead guilty now, settle the case now. You don't want to take your chances with the judge. The penalty will destroy everything.

This is why God's judgment has not yet occurred. Peter reminds us:

> The Lord is not slow in keeping his promise, as some understand slowness. He is patient with you, not wanting anyone to perish, but everyone to come to repentance. (2 Peter 3:9)

God has granted us a window of time for reconciliation. The resurrection of Jesus is the sign that time is limited—His triumph demonstrates that the end will come.

### The Signs Are Clear

Jesus rebukes the crowd for their blindness:

> When you see a cloud rising in the west, you say, 'It's going to rain,' and it does. And when the south wind blows, you say, 'It's going to be hot,' and it is. Hypocrites! You know how to interpret the appearance of the earth and the sky. How is it that you don't know how to interpret this present time? (Luke 12:54-56)

People understood the weather signs of their day. They could read the natural world. Yet they could not read the signs of God's kingdom present in Jesus Himself. When John the Baptist sent messengers asking if Jesus was the promised one, Jesus answered:

> Go back and report to John what you have seen and heard: The blind receive sight, the lame walk, those who have leprosy are cleansed, the deaf hear, the dead are raised, and the good news is proclaimed to the poor. (Luke 7:22)

The evidence was undeniable. Two thousand years of scholarship have not disproven the historical evidence for Jesus' death and resurrection. The truth is as clear as the sky.

## Part Three: Be Prepared

### The Servants Waiting

> Be dressed ready for service and keep your lamps burning, like servants waiting for their master to return from a wedding banquet, so that when he comes and knocks they can immediately open the door for him. It will be good for those servants whose master finds them watching when he comes. Truly I tell you, he will dress himself to serve, will have them recline at the table and will come and wait on them. (Luke 12:35-37)

Jesus prepares His disciples for what is coming, much like a coach psyches up a team before the game. The imagery is that of servants waiting alertly for their master's return. The master is away at a celebration, and the servants must remain ready—just as employees are tempted to slack when the boss is away, or students when the teacher leaves the room.

### Ready for His Departure

The context of Luke 9-12 is crucial. Peter has just confessed that Jesus is the Messiah, the promised King. But rather than coming in power and majesty, Jesus surprises them:

> The Son of Man must suffer many things and be rejected by the elders, chief priests and teachers of the law, and he must be killed and on the third day be raised to life. (Luke 9:22)

From this point, Jesus sets His face toward Jerusalem, increasingly spending time with His disciples to prepare them for what will come. He teaches them what true kingship means—suffering, rejection, death.

Therefore, when Jesus says "the Son of Man will come at an hour when you do not expect him" (Luke 12:40), the context suggests He is speaking of His going away, His departure. In Greek, the word can mean either "come" or "go"—and contextually, His departure makes more sense. Jesus is preparing them: I will be taken from you suddenly, violently. Will you be ready? Will you remain faithful? Will you continue to proclaim God's Word?

### Instructions to the Disciples

Peter asks directly: "Lord, are you telling this parable to us, or to everyone?" (Luke 12:41)

Jesus' answer clarifies that this parable is specifically for the disciples—those entrusted with leadership of God's flock:

> Who then is the faithful and wise manager, whom the master puts in charge of his servants to give them their food allowance at the proper time? It will be good for that servant whom the master finds doing so when he returns. Truly I tell you, he will put him in charge of all his possessions. But suppose the servant says to himself, 'My master is taking a long time in coming,' and he then begins to beat the menservants and maidservants and to eat and drink and get drunk. The master of that servant will come on a day when he does not expect him and at an hour he is not aware of. He will cut him to pieces and assign him a place with the unbelievers. (Luke 12:42-46)

The disciples are given a sacred trust: to remember Jesus' words, to pass them on to others, and to write them down for future generations. They must be prepared for the violence and opposition that will come. They must not get distracted or run away. They must hold fast to what they have learned.

### What About Us Today?

We live in a different time. The game has already been played, and victory belongs to Jesus. On the cross, He defeated death and sin. Salvation is finished; the kingdom has come.

Although Jesus' words about being ready were spoken in a specific moment to His immediate disciples, we have the words of the disciple Peter directed to us:

> The day of the Lord will come like a thief. The heavens will disappear with a roar; the elements will be destroyed by fire, and the earth and everything in it will be laid bare. Since everything will be destroyed in this way, what kind of people ought you to be? You ought to live holy and godly lives as you look forward to the day of God and speed its coming. But in keeping with his promise we are looking forward to a new heaven and a new earth, the home of righteousness. So then, dear friends, since you are looking forward to this, make every effort to be found spotless, blameless and at peace with him. (2 Peter 3:10-14)

### Living Between Two Worlds

We live in a peculiar tension. We don't know when Christ will return—it could be tomorrow or not in our lifetime. So we invest in this world: we study, work, build houses, raise children. Yet we hold it all loosely, knowing that "the heavens will disappear with a roar, the elements destroyed by fire, and the earth and everything in it will be laid bare."

We work and build and plan—but we do so with holy and godly lives. We make every effort to be found spotless, to put away sin. We live for the next world while faithfully stewarding this one.

> If it is possible, as far as it depends on you, live at peace with everyone. (Romans 12:18)

This is the paradox of Christian hope: we remain vigilant and holy, yet at peace. We live as those who have already won the victory, awaiting its final revelation.
