# Luke 15:11-24

**Title:** Lost and Found (Easter)
**Date:** February 26, 2017
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction

Welcome to church, especially if this is your first time. I'm Kenny, and I'd love for you to join us for lunch afterward so we can get to know you.

We've all lost something. I'm rather absent-minded and constantly misplace my keys. Once, I even drove home and forgot one of my children! But today, we encounter a story about a man who loses something far more precious—his son.

## 1. The Father Loses His Son (Luke 15:11-12)

> A man had two sons. The younger one said to his father, "Father, give me my share of the estate."

We don't know what caused the rift between them. Was there a quarrel? An escalating conflict? What we do know is that the divide had grown so severe that the younger son demanded, "Give me my inheritance now."

In Jewish law, a father could designate his estate before death, but the sons only received it after he died. So when this son asked for his inheritance, he was essentially saying: "I wish you were dead." It's a stunning request—the ultimate rejection.

### What Could the Father Have Done?

The father had many options:
- He could have cursed and beaten his son
- He could have disowned him and cut off his inheritance
- In biblical times, he could have brought him to the authorities to be stoned to death (Deuteronomy 21:18-21)

But instead, something remarkable happens:

> He divided his property between them.

The Greek word here literally means "he divided his life." Fifty years of labor, blood, and sweat—liquidated. Half his capital, gone. Half his business model, destroyed. The means to support his wife, daughters, and remaining son—severed. But worst of all, his beloved son was leaving him.

A son is your life. You gave him life, invested in him, loved him—and now your family is torn apart.

### Application

This is what we do to God. He is our Creator, and we tell Him we're leaving. We're dissatisfied with the life He's given us. We're tired of His laws and His ways. We're weary of being told what to do.

As a society, we've done exactly this. Nietzsche said, "God is dead, and we killed him." After the Enlightenment, Western society decided we were too clever to be governed by God—we'd guide ourselves through logic and reason. But the consequences are profound: not only has the father lost his son, but the son has lost his father.

## 2. The Son Loses His Father (Luke 15:13-16)

> Soon after, the younger son gathered all he had and traveled to a distant country, where he squandered his wealth in wild living.

He went to a **distant place**—far from his father, his relatives, his village. Somewhere no one knew him. He could reinvent himself with a new identity, unburdened by his past, free from his father's oversight. No one would ask, "Aren't you somebody's son?" or "Didn't you used to go to church?" There was nothing to hold him accountable.

But this distance wasn't merely physical. It was emotional and spiritual—a chasm that would nearly destroy him.

He squandered his wealth in **wild living** (asotia in Greek)—recklessness, excess, self-indulgence. And then reality struck:

> He spent everything. Then a severe famine spread through that whole country, and he began to be in need.

Without savings, when the economic collapse came, he couldn't escape it. His fair-weather friends abandoned him. Desperate and alone, he hired himself out as a servant:

> He went and hired himself out to feed pigs. He longed to fill his stomach with the pods the pigs were eating, but no one gave him anything.

For a Jewish man, this was dehumanizing. Pigs were unclean animals. He was so hungry he wanted to eat what the pigs ate—and even that was denied him.

His trajectory was catastrophic:
- From wealth to rags
- From luxury to filth
- From wine to pig slop
- From penthouse to pigpen

The further he traveled from his father, the longer he stayed away, the worse his condition became. He had lost the source of wealth, health, and relationship, and descended into poverty, disease, and despair.

### Application

Just as our society has abandoned God, so too have we spiraled into poverty, sickness, and hopelessness. Australia doesn't look like it—we started with great wealth—but bit by bit, year after year, generation by generation, as we've moved further from God, we've suffered more:

- You see it in society's margins, among those money cannot save
- You see it in the mental health crisis of younger generations, who lack moral formation
- At our current birthrate, we won't even have a next generation

We've grown poorer. We've grown sicker. We've grown lonelier.

**Is there any way out of this spiral?**

## 3. Finding Each Other (Luke 15:17-24)

The rebellious son did something extraordinary—he came to his senses.

> He said to himself, "How many of my father's hired servants have food to spare, and here I am starving to death!"

He compared his situation to his father's servants. If he must work, why not work for his father? At least he wouldn't starve. If pig food works for animals, surely his father's provisions would work better—and they'd be kosher. He realized a profound truth: we are all servants. The choice is whether we serve evil or serve God.

As the Psalmist says:

> One day in your courts is better than a thousand elsewhere. I would rather be a doorkeeper in the house of my God than dwell in the tents of the wicked. (Psalm 84:10)

Better to suffer under God than to prosper outside of Him. Better to be a taxi driver in Australia than a doctor in poverty elsewhere.

### The Son Prepares His Speech

> I will arise and go to my father, and will say to him: "Father, I have sinned against heaven and against you. I am no longer worthy to be called your son; make me like one of your hired servants."

These are words of genuine humility and true repentance. He expects judgment. He no longer claims sonship—he'll accept servanthood.

But something extraordinary happens:

> While he was still a long way off, his father saw him and was filled with compassion for him; he ran to his son, threw his arms around him and kissed him.

The father saw him. Had he been watching every day, scanning the horizon? The father **ran** to his son, bridging the distance between them. Rather than raising his arms in anger, he **embraced** him. Rather than cursing him, he **kissed** him.

The son's speech was cut short. He had planned to end with, "Make me like one of your hired servants," but the father interrupted him—because he knew exactly what status his son should have.

> The father said to his servants, "Quick! Bring the best robe and put it on him. Put a ring on his finger and sandals on his feet."

These weren't arbitrary gifts—they were declarations of sonship:

- **The best robe:** Position in the family. Servants didn't wear robes.
- **The ring:** A family signet ring, authority and identity.
- **Sandals:** Servants went barefoot; only family members wore sandals.

The father was saying: "No, I won't receive you as a servant. I receive you as my son."

Then:

> Bring the fattened calf and kill it. Let's have a feast and celebrate. For this son of mine was dead and is alive again; he was lost and is found.

A fattened calf was a feast worthy of the whole village. The father wanted everyone to know. Why?

> Because my son was dead and is alive again; he was lost and is found.

The lost son was found. The dead son was alive again. The message for all of us: don't die in your sins—live in God.

Have you repented? Are you seeking to be found by God? In a moment, I'm going to pray a prayer. To become a Christian, you must come to God through Jesus Christ. We know this is possible because God did this for His Son.

## Found!

At Easter, we celebrate the death and resurrection of Jesus. God lost His Son for our sake. On Good Friday, Jesus died for the sins of the world. But on Easter Sunday, the Son rose from the dead. He lived again.

Therefore, we have confidence that we who are dead in sin will be raised to life in Christ.
