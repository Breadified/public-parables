# Mark 5:21-6:56

**Title:** The One - Last Minute Scramble to Find the Remnant of Israel
**Date:** May 20, 2007
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction: The Urgency of the Moment

We find ourselves at a turning point in Mark's gospel. Jesus is in a race against time, making a last-minute scramble to reach the Jewish people before the window closes. Like accountants working frantically before a deadline, or a serving team rushing to prepare a meal, there's an unmistakable sense of urgency here. The passage itself mirrors this scramble—Jesus is searching, reaching out, looking for that faithful remnant who will truly believe in him.

## Part One: The Bleeding Woman (5:21-34)

When Jesus returned from across the lake, a large crowd gathered around him. A synagogue leader named Jairus came and fell at his feet, pleading desperately: "My little daughter is dying. Please come and put your hands on her so that she will be healed and live."

Before Jesus could reach Jairus's home, the crowd pressed around him. In the middle of this chaos, a woman who had been bleeding for twelve years made her way through the masses. For over a decade, she had suffered greatly under the care of many physicians, spending all her money only to grow worse. Under Jewish law, her condition made her ritually unclean—she was essentially exiled from community and worship.

Yet this woman had heard about Jesus. She thought to herself, "If I just touch his clothes, I will be healed." She came up behind him in the crowd and touched his cloak. Immediately, her bleeding stopped, and she felt in her body that she was freed from her suffering.

Jesus sensed that power had gone out from him. He turned around and asked, "Who touched my clothes?" The disciples, bewildered, asked how he could identify one touch among so many in a crowded throng. But Jesus kept looking around, searching for her. When the woman realized what had happened to her, trembling with fear, she came and fell at his feet and told him the whole truth.

Jesus said to her, "Daughter, your faith has healed you. Go in peace and be freed from your suffering."

### The Deeper Meaning

What's remarkable here is Jesus's reaction. Rather than anger at the unwanted transfer of power, Jesus was actively looking for her—not in condemnation, but in recognition. He was searching for faith. Among all the crowds following him for the wrong reasons, here was someone with genuine faith in him.

This woman represents the faithful remnant of Israel. Her twelve-year affliction parallels Israel's own spiritual sickness. Just as the bleeding rendered her unclean under the law, Israel had become spiritually contaminated by sin. The religious leaders had tried to fix the problem by adding more rules, more regulations, more layers of law—treating the symptoms while ignoring the disease.

But Jesus is the true doctor. He didn't come merely to heal physical ailments or bring order to the world. He came to forgive sin—to address the root problem, not the symptoms. And he paid the price himself. To stop the bleeding of the woman, Jesus bled himself. His blood, shed on the cross, is the ultimate cure. The religious leaders were like physicians treating gangrene with bandages on the toes while ignoring the poisoned blood supply underneath. Jesus bled to fix what no law could fix.

## Part Two: The Sleeping Girl (5:35-43)

While Jesus was still speaking, messengers came from Jairus's house saying, "Your daughter is dead. Why bother the teacher anymore?" 

Jesus, overhearing them, said to Jairus, "Don't be afraid; just believe." Faith, not fear—this is the constant theme.

He brought only Peter, James, and John with him to Jairus's house. When they arrived, Jesus saw the commotion—people crying and wailing loudly. He said to them, "Why all this commotion and wailing? The child is not dead but asleep." They laughed at him.

He put everyone out except the child's father and mother and the disciples with him. Then he took the little girl's hand and said, "Talitha koum!" (which means "Little girl, I say to you, get up!"). Immediately the girl stood up and began to walk around. She was twelve years old.

### The Symbolic Significance

Two features of this story shouldn't be missed. First, the parallel to the bleeding woman: both stories involve the number twelve. The woman had been bleeding for twelve years; the girl was twelve years old. This is not coincidence. The number twelve represents Israel itself—from the twelve tribes of Israel. In Scripture, twelve is the symbol of God's people, his covenant nation. We see it in the twelve disciples (the new people of God), in the 144,000 of Revelation (12 × 12), and here in Mark's carefully woven symbolism.

Second, notice the terminology. The woman is called "Daughter" by Jesus. The girl is called Jairus's "daughter." In the Old Testament, "Daughter of Zion" is a term for the nation of Israel itself. We see this in Psalms, Isaiah, and Lamentations—Israel is personified as the Daughter of Zion.

The book of Lamentations describes the Daughter of Zion during the exile: "All the splendor has departed from the Daughter of Zion... her princes are like deer that find no pasture; in weakness they have fled before the pursuer." Israel had become filthy and unclean through sin. God's anger had separated her from him, leaving her without comfort.

The girl in this story represents Israel dead—or more precisely, Israel asleep, awaiting resurrection. The bad doctors—the teachers of the law, the Pharisees, the scribes—were part of the problem, not the solution. They offered salvation through the law but delivered only condemnation. They multiplied laws and regulations, making people more guilty rather than more righteous. They were like parasitic worms, taking from the people in the temple but giving nothing in return.

But Jesus came as the true doctor. He alone could awaken the sleeping nation. His touch brought her back to life.

## Part Three: The Remnant of Israel (6:1-56)

### Rejection in His Hometown

Jesus then went to his hometown, where he began teaching in the synagogue on the Sabbath. Many who heard him were amazed at his wisdom and the remarkable miracles he performed. They asked, "Where did this man get these things? What's this wisdom that has been given him? Isn't this the carpenter? Isn't this Mary's son and the brother of James, Joseph, Judas and Simon?"

They took offense at him. A prophet, Jesus said, is not without honor except in his own town, among his relatives, and in his own home. He could not do any miracles there except lay his hands on a few sick people and heal them. He was amazed at their lack of faith.

The rejection was complete. Israel, his own people, rejected him.

### The Urgent Mission

After this rejection, Jesus began traveling from village to village, teaching. He called the Twelve to himself and began sending them out two by two, giving them authority over impure spirits. This is the third time "twelve" appears in just two chapters—emphasizing the theme of the twelve tribes, the people of Israel.

He gave them specific instructions: "Take nothing for the journey except a staff. Wear sandals but not an extra shirt. Take no bread, no bag, no money in your belts."

What do these restrictions tell us? The trip would be short and quick. They weren't packing for a long journey or an extended mission. They were to go from place to place, staying in one house per town, and if people rejected them, they were to shake the dust off their feet and move on. Don't waste time. Time is short.

The twelve went out, preaching repentance. They drove out demons and healed many sick people with oil.

### John the Baptist's Death—The Timetable Accelerates

King Herod heard about Jesus, for his name had become well known. Some thought John the Baptist had been raised from the dead, others that Jesus was Elijah, still others that he was a prophet like the ancient prophets.

But Herod knew the truth. He had beheaded John the Baptist—and now Jesus's fame was spreading.

This detail is crucial. John the Baptist, the forerunner, had been executed. The countdown had begun. Jesus himself would be next. The timetable was accelerating.

### The Last Minute Scramble

The apostles returned to Jesus and reported all they had done. Because so many people were coming and going that they didn't even have time to eat, Jesus said to them, "Come with me by yourselves to a quiet place and get some rest."

They got into a boat and went to a solitary place. But many saw them leaving, recognized them, and ran on foot from all the towns, arriving ahead of them. When Jesus landed and saw the large crowd, he had compassion on them, because they were like sheep without a shepherd. So he began teaching them many things.

By this time it was late in the day. The disciples came to him and said, "This is a remote place, and it's already very late. Send the people away so they can go to the surrounding countryside and villages and buy themselves something to eat."

Jesus said, "You give them something to eat."

The disciples were shocked. "That would take more than half a year's wages!"

Jesus asked, "How many loaves do you have? Go and see." They found five loaves and two fish.

### The Feeding and the New Exodus

Jesus directed them to have all the people sit down on the green grass in groups. They sat down in rows of hundreds and fifties—an echo of Exodus 18, where Moses organized Israel's people under thousands, hundreds, fifties, and tens.

The green grass itself is significant. It recalls Psalm 23: "The Lord is my shepherd, I lack nothing. He makes me lie down in green pastures, he leads me beside quiet waters."

This was a new Exodus. In the original exodus, God provided manna from heaven to feed his people in the wilderness. Now Jesus was providing a new, better manna.

Jesus took the five loaves and two fish, looked up to heaven, gave thanks, and broke the loaves. He gave them to his disciples to distribute. He also divided the two fish among them all. They all ate and were satisfied. The disciples picked up twelve basketfuls of leftover bread and fish. The number of men who had eaten was five thousand.

### The Problem with the Leftovers

Here's where the symbolism deepens. Everyone ate and was satisfied. Yet there were twelve basketfuls of leftovers. Why?

The answer points to the tragedy of this moment. The leftovers symbolize the rejection. Jesus had prepared a messianic banquet for all Israel—the people represented by the number twelve. But many didn't show up. The feast was prepared for them, but they refused the invitation. The majority of Israel rejected Jesus.

This last-minute scramble was a successful failure. Jesus gathered five thousand, yes—but it was only a remnant, a shadow of what might have been. The mission worked for a few, but most of Israel would not come.

## Conclusion: The Urgency of Our Time

Throughout this passage, there runs an undercurrent of urgency. Jesus is racing against time. He knows John the Baptist is dead, that his own crucifixion is approaching. He is making a desperate, last-minute scramble to find the faithful within Israel—the woman who believes when no one else will, the synagogue ruler who trusts when his daughter seems lost, the few who respond to his call.

The remnant have come in. Some true daughters of Zion, the five thousand hungry Jews, a synagogue ruler, the bleeding lady—each one precious to Jesus. For us, the urgency remains. We too live in the last days. Time is short. The question Jesus asks us is the same he asked then: Will you believe? Will you be part of the faithful remnant, or will you remain in the crowds, following for the wrong reasons?
