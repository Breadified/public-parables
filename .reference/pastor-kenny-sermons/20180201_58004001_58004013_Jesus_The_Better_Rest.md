# Hebrews 4:1-13

**Title:** Jesus: The Better Rest
**Date:** 25 February 2018
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## The Paradox of Rest

One of the most perplexing passages in Hebrews presents us with what seems like a contradiction:

> Now we who have believed enter that rest, just as God has said, "So I declared on oath in my anger, 'They shall never enter my rest.'" (Hebrews 4:3)

How can we both enter the rest and still be making our way toward it? The answer is beautifully biblical: **both are true**. We have entered God's rest, and yet we have not fully entered it. This tension between the "already" and the "not yet" defines the Christian life.

---

## We Have Entered the Rest

### The Work of God is Complete

God's work of creation finished at the beginning of time. Notice what Genesis tells us:

> By the seventh day God had finished the work he had been doing; so on the seventh day he rested from all his work. Then God blessed the seventh day and made it holy, because on it he rested from all the work of creating that he had done. (Genesis 2:2-3)

The remarkable truth is that the seventh day has no ending in Scripture. Unlike the first six days, which conclude with "and it was good," the seventh day continues perpetually. We are living in God's rest—the eternal Sabbath that began at creation.

### Rest Through the Sabbath Commandment

This reality found expression in God's law:

> Remember the Sabbath day by keeping it holy. Six days you shall labor and do all your work, but the seventh day is a sabbath to the Lord your God. On it you shall not do any work... For in six days the Lord made the heavens and the earth, the sea, and all that is in them, but he rested on the seventh day. Therefore the Lord blessed the Sabbath day and made it holy. (Exodus 20:8-11)

Throughout Israel's history, the principle of rest became foundational. Every seventh day, Israelites ceased from work. Every seventh year, the land rested. Every fiftieth year—seven times seven—came the Jubilee, when debts were forgiven, slaves were freed, and the entire nation reset itself. This wasn't mere ritual; it was a radical act of faith. In an economy where you didn't work and you didn't eat, to rest meant trusting God completely. For forty years in the wilderness, God provided manna without requiring labor on the Sabbath. He proved that His provision sustains us when we cannot work.

### Rest in Jesus

Jesus Himself embodied this truth:

> Come to me, all you who are weary and burdened, and I will give you rest. Take my yoke upon you and learn from me, for I am gentle and humble in heart, and you will find rest for your souls. For my yoke is easy and my burden is light. (Matthew 11:28-29)

The writer of Hebrews reminds us:

> When this priest had offered for all time one sacrifice for sins, he sat down at the right hand of God. (Hebrews 10:12)

Jesus sat down because His work was finished. On the cross, He declared the words that complete all labor: **"It is finished."** (John 19:30)

The work of creation required rest on the seventh day. The work of redemption is complete in Christ. We have entered this rest through faith. We are no longer striving to earn God's favor—it is already ours.

### The Rest We Live In Daily

While Sunday is not technically the Sabbath (it's the first day of the week, celebrated by the early church to commemorate the resurrection), the principle of Sabbath applies to us in a fuller way. As Paul wrote:

> Do not let anyone judge you by what you eat or drink, or with regard to a religious festival, a New Moon celebration or a Sabbath day. (Colossians 2:16)

The change is this: the Sabbath is no longer about a day, but about a person. To honor the Sabbath is to honor Jesus, the Lord of the Sabbath. This is why we must remember daily that work is not all there is. Behind every task, there is a greater picture—a rest that awaits, a God who provides, a redemption already accomplished.

### The Problem of Forgetting

Yet many of us live as though we haven't entered this rest at all. We work frantically, thinking everything depends on us. We take expensive vacations only to return more exhausted than before, then work harder to pay off the debt. We've forgotten why we work.

There's an old story of a fisherman content with his simple life. A business consultant approached him with a plan: expand your business, hire more workers, build a factory, export internationally, make millions, then retire. The fisherman asked, "Why?" The consultant replied, "So you can do what you want to do—work in the morning, then go home to enjoy your family and support your church." The fisherman smiled. He already had exactly that.

**Workaholism is the disease of our age.** We work for the sake of working, as though productivity is the ultimate good. In Japan, this has become so severe that there's a word for it: *karoshi*—death from overwork. People remain at the office long after their tasks are complete, simply because everyone else is there. The fourth commandment is needed more than ever: there is something beyond work. There is rest.

## We Have Not Yet Entered the Rest

### The Rest is Eschatological

But there's another truth we must hold equally: **the fullness of rest still awaits us**. This is not yet our eternal home.

> There remains, then, a Sabbath-rest for the people of God; for anyone who enters God's rest also rests from his own work, just as God did from his. Let us, therefore, make every effort to enter that rest, so that no one will fall by following their example of disobedience. (Hebrews 4:9-11)

The author is calling us to "make every effort" to reach a future rest. The Israelites were offered rest repeatedly—in Eden, in the Promised Land, in the kingdom under David—yet they missed it. Why? Disobedience and unbelief.

### Israel's Pattern of Rejection

Israel had multiple opportunities for rest but chose slavery instead. Adam and Eve enjoyed the rest of Eden until they sinned, and toil entered the world. Their descendants were freed from Egypt's bondage, yet when God offered them the Promised Land, they refused to enter through faith. Instead, they wandered forty years in the wilderness.

After finally entering Canaan under Joshua, they still didn't find ultimate rest. David's kingdom was great, but it wasn't the final rest. Why? Because the Scripture says:

> If Joshua had given them rest, God would not have spoken later about another day. (Hebrews 4:8)

God continues to call us with the same words: "Today, if you hear his voice, do not harden your hearts." (Hebrews 4:7)

The physical land of Israel was never the true rest. We are looking for something greater—heaven itself, the eternal dwelling place of God's people.

### The Work of Perseverance

So why does verse 11 say "make every effort"? This seems contradictory—how do we work to enter rest? The answer lies in understanding what work is required. It's not the work of earning salvation; that's finished. It's the **work of character**, the discipline of perseverance.

Billy Graham understood this. He had one unbreakable rule: "I will never be alone in a private room with a woman who is not my wife." This single commitment protected him from the moral falls that destroyed many Christian leaders—Jim Bakker, Jimmy Swaggart, and others. While he preached to over 215 million people and was rated by Gallup as one of the ten most admired men in the world fifty-one times, he never compromised his character. He finished the race.

But you don't have to be Billy Graham. You simply must finish. Like Steven Bradbury, the Australian speed skater who won gold in 2002 when everyone ahead of him crashed, what matters is being there at the end. Bradbury had suffered terrible injuries—his skate blade cut through his leg, causing him to lose four liters of blood; he broke his neck. Yet he kept making every effort. After winning, he said: "I won't take the medal for the minute-and-a-half of the race I actually won. I'll take it as the last decade of the hard slog I put in."

This is the work required: **the work of killing sin before it kills you**. Like the Eskimo hunter who freezes a sharp blade in sugared ice for wolves to lick, sin numbs us gradually until we bleed to death without feeling it. We must actively resist, actively pursue holiness, actively keep our eyes fixed on Jesus.

---

## The Call to Perseverance

### For Now

We live in the tension. We have tasted God's rest through Christ's finished work, yet we press toward the ultimate rest that awaits. This means:

- **Keep inviting your friends.** Don't give up on those who need to hear.
- **Keep training your children.** Instill in them the hope of Christ.
- **Take your breaks, yes.** Rest is not sinful; it's commanded. But then return to the race.
- **Remember daily why you work.** It's not for its own sake. You work for the Kingdom.

Even Billy Graham, in his final years, despite Parkinson's disease that caused him to fall repeatedly, breaking his hip and pelvis, kept going back out to preach. He knew the race wasn't finished. He made every effort.

### For Eternity

But take heart: the race has an end, and the prize is sure. Billy Graham has arrived. He has entered his rest. So will we, if we remain faithful.

> Let us, therefore, make every effort to enter that rest, so that no one will fall by following their example of disobedience. (Hebrews 4:11)

The rest is coming. Jesus has already done the work. We simply must not harden our hearts. We must believe. We must persevere. And one day, we will enter into that eternal Sabbath, where all labor ceases and we see our Savior face to face.
