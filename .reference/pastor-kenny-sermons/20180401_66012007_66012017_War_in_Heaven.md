# Revelation 12:7-17

**Title:** War in Heaven
**Date:** 29 April 2018
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## A War in Heaven

God is not against war. Scripture reveals an ancient cosmic conflict between Christ and Satan that continues to shape our world and our faith.

> 7 Then war broke out in heaven. Michael and his angels fought against the dragon, and the dragon and his angels fought back.

### Understanding the Combatants

**Michael represents Jesus.** While some interpret Michael as merely one angelic leader, passages like Daniel 10:21 and 12:1 describe him as "the great prince," titles that apply uniquely to Christ. The name "Michael" means "like God"—a fitting designation for the one who embodies God's character. Though Jesus is not an angel but the non-angelic leader of God's army, Michael represents his divine authority and power.

**The dragon represents Satan—the ancient serpent, the devil, the accuser.** In Greek, "drakon" refers to a great serpent or serpent-like creature. The titles are revealing: "devil" (diablos in Greek) means "accuser" or "one who throws against"; "Satan" (Hebrew) also means "accuser." This captures Satan's essential nature—he is the slanderer, the one who deceives through lies and half-truths, just as he did in the Garden of Eden when he told Eve, "You surely will not die." Jesus himself identified Satan as fundamentally dishonest:

> John 8:44 — [the devil] was a murderer from the beginning, not holding to the truth, for there is no truth in him. When he lies, he speaks his native language, for he is a liar and the father of lies.

War exists when diplomacy has failed, when one side refuses reason and chooses only deception. When a side cheats, steals, and murders, righteousness can only prevail through the use of force. This is the nature of the war in heaven.

### A Complete Victory

Jesus wins. Satan loses. This is unambiguous.

> 8 But he [the dragon] was not strong enough, and they [dragon and his army] lost their place in heaven.

Jesus is the stronger man—the one who can enter a strong man's house, bind him, and plunder his possessions (Mark 3:27). The victory is total and decisive. To emphasize Satan's complete defeat, the word "hurled" repeats three times:

> 9 The great dragon was **hurled down**—that ancient serpent called the devil, or Satan, who leads the whole world astray. He was **hurled to the earth**, and his angels with him.
>
> 10 For the accuser of our brothers and sisters, who accuses them before our God day and night, has been **hurled down**.

Imagine pottery hurled against concrete and shattered into pieces. Satan's defeat is complete.

Yet from this victory comes hope for us. The triumph of Jesus over evil means our triumph over evil:

> 10 Then I heard a loud voice in heaven say: "Now have come the salvation and the power and the kingdom of our God, and the authority of his Messiah."

Jesus is seated at the right hand of God with all authority and all power. The world is his kingdom. Throughout his earthly ministry, Jesus demonstrated this supremacy: he fought against the powers of evil and won. He resisted the devil's temptations in the wilderness and won. He drove out demons and won. And this victory belongs to us.

> They triumphed over him by the blood of the Lamb and by the word of their testimony; they did not love their lives so much as to shrink from death.

Therefore rejoice! Our brothers and sisters in heaven rejoice, and you who dwell on earth can rejoice too. The victory is vicarious—just as we benefit from the sacrifices of soldiers who fought for our freedom without personally fighting in those battles, so we benefit from Christ's victory. A million young Australians enlisted in World War One. They fought, bled, were maimed and crippled, came home with mental illness, so that we could enjoy freedoms we did nothing to earn. Yet we rejoice in their victory. Similarly, Jesus has won the ultimate battle, taking upon himself the crushing accusations of the devil so that we are free. Because the accuser is defeated, we are free from his lies, his false guilt, his deceptive temptations.

**Jesus wins. We win. We are free.**

## War on Earth

But here we encounter a problem: **Why does the victory look so incomplete?**

If the battle in heaven is definitively over, why are there still wars on earth? Why is Christianity the most persecuted religion in the world? Eleven Christians are killed every hour—273 per day, 100,000 every year. In Western countries, Christians face increasing marginalization, legal pressure, and social hostility. Personally, many of you face attacks from circumstances, society, friends, even family. It seems like Satan is winning.

The answer lies in understanding the timing of Satan's expulsion:

> 11 But woe to the earth and the sea, because the devil has gone down to you! He is filled with fury, because he knows that his time is short.

Satan has been hurled down to earth, but he has not been destroyed. He knows his time is limited, and this knowledge fills him with rage. His strategy shifts: unable to defeat Christ in heaven, he now pursues the woman—the people of God.

> 13 When the dragon saw that he had been hurled to the earth, he pursued the woman who had given birth to the male child.

The woman represents God's people, as described earlier in Revelation 12. Satan cannot harm Jesus (he is beyond his reach), so he turns his fury on Christ's followers:

> 17 Then the dragon was enraged at the woman and went off to wage war against the rest of her offspring—those who keep God's commands and hold fast their testimony about Jesus.

### The War Continues, But Victory is Assured

Consider the Japanese soldiers found decades after World War Two ended, still fighting in the jungles because they didn't believe the war was over. Though the battle was decisively concluded, they continued in futile combat, their weapons rusted, their uniforms tattered. They could still harm with their dulled bayonets, but they could not kill.

This is Satan's present condition. The war in heaven is over. He is defeated. Yet the effects of that war continue on earth. Satan roams like a toothless lion—he can still give a fierce roar and deliver painful bites, but he cannot devour you. He cannot kill you spiritually. He cannot separate you from God's love.

> 14 The woman was given the two wings of a great eagle, so that she might fly to the place prepared for her in the wilderness, where she would be taken care of for a time, times and half a time, out of the serpent's reach.

This time reference—time, times, and half a time (or 1,260 days, or three and a half years)—appears throughout Scripture and carries significance. If seven represents fullness, then three and a half represents the halfway point. The worst is behind us. We are in the downhill stretch. Satan's time is short, and we are protected in the meantime.

Satan is allowed to persecute, to mislead, to hurt us temporarily—but he cannot hurt us permanently or take us away from God. His rage is the rage of a defeated enemy, thrashing about in his final moments.

### Victory is Complete

Sometimes when we look at the world, it appears that good and evil are equally matched forces. Sometimes it looks like evil is winning through wars, personal defeats, and persecution. But this is an illusion.

The real battle has been won. God has won. Good has prevailed. Jesus has triumphed over evil. Nothing can separate us from God's love—not Satan's accusations, not persecution, not fear. Not because we are stronger than evil, but because **Jesus is stronger.**

## Christian Perseverance in the Ongoing Battle

Yet we must also recognize that Satan remains on earth for a season, and as long as evil exists, there is a legitimate need for resistance—even warfare. This may seem harsh, but it reflects biblical reality. God is called "Lord of Hosts"—the God of armies. He is not opposed to armies or military forces. Throughout Scripture, when the righteous face oppression from the wicked, God raises up deliverers:

> Judges 2:18 Whenever the Lord raised up a judge for them, he was with the judge and saved them out of the hands of their enemies as long as the judge lived; for the Lord relented because of their groaning under those who oppressed and afflicted them.

In Nehemiah, the builders fortifying Jerusalem worked with one hand and held weapons in the other. Some killings are lawful—they are acts of justice, not murder. As Augustine wrote in the fifth century, those "who have waged war in obedience to the divine command...have represented in their persons the public justice or the wisdom of government, and in this capacity have put to death wicked men" without violating the commandment against killing.

War, when just—whether to defend the innocent, protect one's nation, or resist tyranny—is not evil. It can be an expression of love and charity. To refuse to defend the innocent when able is cowardice, not virtue. Yet wars must be entered thoughtfully: they should be declared through proper authority, pursued only by willing volunteers persuaded of their righteousness, and waged with just cause and proportion.

### Honor Those Who Fight

This is why we honor the Anzac soldiers. They did not want to fight—they would have preferred to stay home, play cricket, get married, have children. But they didn't. They identified themselves with righteousness, gave up their material desires, set aside their fears, and fought. Teenagers on the beaches of Gallipoli, waves of them cut down by artillery, yet they stood fast. They fought so we could enjoy freedoms we did nothing to earn.

All they ask is that we preserve their legacy: don't squander the freedom they purchased with their blood, and be prepared to defend it again if it is threatened.

**Similarly, honor Jesus.** He has purchased our freedom through his blood. He asks only that we preserve his legacy—that we live as his witnesses, that we testify to his victory, and that we be willing to sacrifice, just as he did.

> 11 They triumphed over him by the blood of the Lamb and by the word of their testimony; they did not love their lives so much as to shrink from death.

This is the call for this term and beyond. Some of you will have great times. Others may be tested beyond what you think you can bear. Do not shrink from persecution. Do not love your life, your reputation, your comfort so much that you abandon your faith. Be brave. Stand fast.

Christians have endured persecution for centuries. We grow weary. But help is coming. Continue to persevere. The victory in heaven is complete, and soon—very soon in God's timeline—the final chapter will be written. Until then, the devil roams with fury and a short time. Hold fast to the blood of the Lamb and the word of your testimony.

**Rejoice. You win. You are free.**
