# 2 Corinthians 3:7–4:6

**Title:** The Glorious Ministry
**Date:** 22 July 2009
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## The Desire for Glory

We all want to be someone. We work up the ranks of leadership, hoping to achieve glory and make a name for ourselves. Yet the Bible surprises us—it says to go for glory. The question is: what kind of glory are we pursuing?

Paul addresses this in his comparison of Old Testament ministry to New Testament ministry. He invites us to reconsider what true glory really is.

## The Glory of the Old Covenant

> Now if the ministry that brought death, which was engraved in letters on stone, came with glory, so that the Israelites could not look steadily at the face of Moses because of its glory, fading though it was, will not the ministry of the Spirit be even more glorious? (2 Corinthians 3:7-8)

Paul begins by acknowledging the genuine glory of the Mosaic ministry. When Moses descended from Mount Sinai with the two tablets of the Testimony, his face shone with radiance because he had spoken with the Lord:

> When Moses came down from Mount Sinai with the two tablets of the Testimony in his hands, he was not aware that his face was radiant because he had spoken with the LORD. When Aaron and all the Israelites saw Moses, his face was radiant, and they were afraid to come near him. (Exodus 34:29-30)

The giving of the law was glorious in multiple ways:
- The law was written in fancy letters on stone
- The prophet himself bore a shining face as evidence of God's presence
- His brightness was so intense the Israelites could not look upon him steadily

So impressive was this ministry that Moses veiled his face:

> When Moses finished speaking to them, he put a veil over his face. But whenever he entered the LORD's presence to speak with him, he removed the veil until he came out. And when he came out and told the Israelites what he had been commanded, they saw that his face was radiant. Then Moses would put the veil back over his face until he went in to speak with the LORD. (Exodus 34:33-35)

## The Surpassing Glory of the New Covenant

Yet Paul insists the ministry of Christ is far greater:

> If the ministry that condemns men is glorious, how much more glorious is the ministry that brings righteousness! For what was glorious has no glory now in comparison with the surpassing glory. And if what was fading away came with glory, how much greater is the glory of that which lasts! (2 Corinthians 3:9-11)

The law brings condemnation and is temporary—fading away. Christ's ministry brings righteousness and is eternal. The comparison is staggering.

## An Unveiled Ministry

Paul makes a crucial distinction. Unlike Moses, who veiled his face to conceal the fading radiance, those who minister in Christ operate with unveiled faces:

> We are not like Moses, who would put a veil over his face to keep the Israelites from gazing at it while the radiance was fading away. (2 Corinthians 3:13)

A veil blocks the light. But our ministry in Christ is not hidden. We reveal God's glory directly, without obstruction.

This is the nature of our new access to God. In the Old Covenant, the Israelites knew God through Moses—at a distance, mediated by law. But now:

> Now the Lord is the Spirit, and where the Spirit of the Lord is, there is freedom. And we, who with unveiled faces all reflect the Lord's glory, are being transformed into his likeness with ever-increasing glory, which comes from the Lord, who is the Spirit. (2 Corinthians 3:17-18)

We are those with unveiled faces. Through Christ, we have direct, personal knowledge of God—knowledge unencumbered by barriers. We stand in God's presence as Moses did, yet without the need for a veil. And as we behold God's glory, we are transformed to reflect it.

## What Is Glory?

What exactly is this glory? The Psalms give us a clue. When Moses asked God to show him His glory, the Lord responded:

> The LORD replied, "My Presence will go with you, and I will give you rest." (Exodus 33:14)

Moses pressed further:

> Then Moses said to him, "If your Presence does not go with us, do not send us up from here. If your Presence does not go with us, what will distinguish me and your people from all the other people on the earth?" (Exodus 33:15-16)

And God said:

> I will do the very thing you have asked, because I am pleased with you and I know you by name. (Exodus 33:17)

Then Moses made his request:

> Show me your glory. (Exodus 33:18)

And the Lord's answer reveals the heart of what glory means:

> I will cause all my goodness to pass in front of you, and I will proclaim my name, the LORD, in your presence. (Exodus 33:19)

Glory is God's self-revelation—His presence, His goodness, His character, His name. It is the knowledge of who God truly is. When Moses saw God's glory, he encountered not a dazzling light display, but the revelation of God's nature: compassionate, gracious, slow to anger, abounding in love and faithfulness.

This is the glory we now share and reflect. Not achievement, not status, not the applause of others. But knowledge of God Himself.

## Our Response: Bold Proclamation

Given this understanding, Paul calls us to bold action:

> Therefore, since we have such a hope, we are very bold. (2 Corinthians 3:12)

We are to proclaim boldly, not hide. We reflect the Lord's glory to the world with unveiled faces, continuously being transformed by the knowledge of Christ:

> For God, who said, "Let light shine out of darkness," made his light shine in our hearts to give us the light of the knowledge of the glory of God in the face of Christ. (2 Corinthians 4:6)

Freedom marks our ministry. The veil is gone. We are called to shine out to the world, not conceal the light within us.

## Redefining Glory in Life Choices

Here is the pastoral application: glory is not about career advancement, accomplishment, or recognition in the world's eyes. Playing drums well, working a camera skillfully—these are not inherently glorious. Even service itself becomes glorious only when it flows from love and points to Christ.

The real question for each of us: When we make life decisions, are we pursuing the knowledge of God and the proclamation of Christ? Or are we pursuing worldly success, thinking we can add God to it afterward?

There is nothing wrong with being a doctor. But make that decision after giving glory to God. Seek first His kingdom. Let the knowledge of Christ shape your path. Then whatever work you do becomes part of your unveiling of God's glory to the world.

This is the glorious ministry—not the externals, but the transformation that comes from knowing God and reflecting His character to a watching world.
