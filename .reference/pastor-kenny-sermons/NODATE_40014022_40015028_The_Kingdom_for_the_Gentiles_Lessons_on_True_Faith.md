# Matthew 14:22-15:28

**Title:** The Kingdom for the Gentiles: Lessons on True Faith
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## A Parable on Cleanliness (15:10-20)

Jesus called the crowd to listen and understand a profound parable about what truly makes a person unclean.

> "What goes into a man's mouth does not make him unclean, but what comes out of his mouth, that is what makes him unclean." (Matthew 15:11)

### The Call to Listen

Parables serve two critical purposes. First, they call people to truly listen to Jesus. His opening command—"Listen and understand"—demands active engagement, not passive hearing. Second, parables create division among people through their responses. Some hear but don't comprehend, while others listen and understand. This distinction separates genuine disciples from pretenders.

The Pharisees exemplify this division. When they heard this teaching, they were offended—the same reaction as the seed that falls on shallow ground, unable to take root. Their offense reveals their spiritual condition: they were not truly listening to Jesus.

### The Outside and the Inside

The Pharisees had challenged Jesus because his disciples ate without washing their hands, violating Jewish tradition. But Jesus redirected the conversation entirely. According to the Old Testament law, cleanliness was about acceptability before God. The unclean could not approach the temple or participate fully in Israel's life. Ritual washing was the remedy for contamination from sores, menstruation, or touching the dead.

The Pharisees treated these laws as if physical contamination determined spiritual status. But Jesus taught something revolutionary: uncleanness doesn't come from outside a person.

> "Don't you see that whatever enters the mouth goes into the stomach and then out of the body? But the things that come out of the mouth come from the heart, and these make a man unclean. For out of the heart come evil thoughts, murder, adultery, sexual immorality, theft, false testimony, slander." (Matthew 15:17-19)

True uncleanness originates in the heart—in our desires, thoughts, and intentions.

## No Faith: The Fallen Away (14:34-15:9)

Before teaching this parable, Jesus had confronted the faithlessness of the religious leaders. Just before the hand-washing controversy, crowds brought their sick to touch even the edge of his cloak, and all were healed. Yet the Pharisees and teachers of the law immediately attacked Jesus over tradition.

Jesus exposed their hypocrisy with a striking example. They condemned the disciples for breaking the tradition of hand-washing, yet they themselves broke God's direct commandment. Some used a legal loophole—dedicating their property to God—to avoid supporting their aging parents. They elevated their human traditions above God's clear instruction to "honor your father and mother."

> "These people honor me with their lips, but their hearts are far from me. They worship me in vain; their teachings are but rules taught by men." (Matthew 15:8-9)

The core problem was this: the Pharisees had substituted traditions for genuine faith. They kept external rules while their hearts remained distant from God. This is the essence of faithlessness—trusting in blind tradition rather than in a living relationship with God.

## Great Faith: The Canaanite Woman (15:21-28)

In stark contrast to the faithless Pharisees, Jesus encounters a Canaanite woman whose faith astonishes him.

> "A Canaanite woman from that vicinity came to him, crying out, 'Lord, Son of David, have mercy on me! My daughter is suffering terribly from demon-possession.'" (Matthew 15:22)

Jesus tested her repeatedly. He said nothing at first. His disciples urged him to send her away. Then he said he was sent only to the lost sheep of Israel. But this woman refused to be discouraged. She knelt before him and cried, "Lord, help me!"

Jesus responded with words that seem harsh: "It is not right to take the children's bread and toss it to their dogs." (Matthew 15:26)

### The Desperation of the Dogs

In Jewish culture, "dogs" was a term of profound insult—associated with evil, foolishness, and uncleanliness. Dogs were scavengers, eating dead bodies and licking blood. By any measure of the law, this Canaanite woman was utterly unclean, unworthy of the kingdom's blessings.

But she was desperate. She had nowhere else to turn. Instead of being offended by Jesus's words, she embraced the metaphor:

> "'Yes, Lord,' she said, 'but even the dogs eat the crumbs that fall from their masters' table.'" (Matthew 15:27)

What remarkable faith! She admitted her unworthiness. She asked only for the scraps—the leftovers, the crumbs. Yet she understood something profound: even the smallest blessing from Jesus's kingdom surpasses the greatest blessing outside of it. To be a dog eating crumbs from the Master's table is better than being a king outside the kingdom.

Jesus's response was immediate and filled with admiration:

> "Woman, you have great faith! Your request is granted." (Matthew 15:28)

Her daughter was healed that very hour.

### The Reversal

Here is the gospel's stunning reversal: the woman everyone considered unclean was declared to have great faith. Her heart was clean because it was filled with trust, humility, and desperation for Jesus.

This echoes Isaiah's prophecy of a time when God would do wonderful things—when the deaf would hear, the blind would see, and the humble would rejoice in the Lord (Isaiah 29:17-19). Yet judgment awaited those who rejected God's purposes: "Woe to the obstinate children," declares the Lord (Isaiah 30:1).

## Little Faith: The Disciples (14:22-33)

Between the Pharisees' no faith and the Canaanite woman's great faith, we find the disciples with little faith.

After feeding five thousand, Jesus made the disciples get into a boat while he went up the mountain to pray. During the fourth watch of the night, with the wind against them and the boat being buffeted by waves, Jesus came to them walking on the water.

The disciples were terrified. "It's a ghost!" they cried out in fear.

> "But Jesus immediately said to them: 'Take courage! It is I. Don't be afraid.'" (Matthew 14:27)

Peter's response shows his faith—he asked to walk on the water toward Jesus. But when he saw the wind, fear overtook him, and he began to sink.

> "Immediately Jesus reached out his hand and caught him. 'You of little faith,' he said, 'why did you doubt?'" (Matthew 14:30-31)

Jesus's words weren't harsh condemnation but gentle correction. Peter had faith—genuine faith that prompted him to step out of the boat. But his faith was small, vulnerable to doubt when circumstances seemed threatening.

For those struggling with small faith, there is encouragement here. Little faith is not the same as no faith. The disciples' faith, though wavering, was real. They eventually worshiped Jesus in the boat, declaring, "Truly you are the Son of God."

Jesus invites us to trust even when we don't have all the answers, even when doubts arise. What matters is that we keep our eyes on Jesus and trust in him, step by step.

---

## Application: From Tradition to True Faith

The contrast Matthew presents is unmistakable: the Pharisees with their no faith in blind tradition, the disciples with their little faith but genuine trust, and the Canaanite woman with her great faith rooted in desperation and humility.

We often fall into the Pharisees' trap without realizing it. We develop spiritual routines that feel meaningful—quiet times, worship traditions, church rituals—but we perform them mechanically, without the heart engagement they require. It's tokenism. We do one good thing to avoid another; we keep up appearances while our hearts remain distant.

This is especially dangerous because we can feel spiritually accomplished while growing further from God. We honor God with our lips while our hearts are far from him.

The Canaanite woman models a better way. She approaches God with honest humility, admitting her unworthiness and desperately seeking his mercy. She doesn't demand rights; she asks for crumbs. And she finds that the smallest portion of God's kingdom is greater than anything the world offers.

Where do you stand? Are you going through spiritual motions like the Pharisees? Are you wavering like the disciples, wanting to follow but held back by fear and doubt? Or are you approaching God with the humble desperation of the Canaanite woman?

If you're not yet a Christian, consider her example. Humble yourself. Admit that you are unclean, separated from God by sin. Repent and seek his mercy. The youngest in God's kingdom is greater than the mightiest outside it.

If you are a Christian, examine your traditions. Do they draw your heart closer to Jesus, or have they become hollow routines? Press past your little faith. Trust Jesus even in the storms. Keep your eyes on him, step by step.
