# Matthew 21:18-22:14

**Title:** Who's Jesus? The Greater Judge

**Date:** 24 October 2010

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction: Understanding Anger and Judgment

Last week we discussed anger. It's important to understand that while God does get angry—His anger burned against Israel, making them wander in the desert for forty years (Numbers 32:13)—His anger is fundamentally different from ours. God's anger is righteous, patient, and purposeful. He is "slow to anger, abounding in love" (Exodus 34:6). Our anger, by contrast, is often unrighteous and impatient. Paul warns us repeatedly: "Do not let the sun go down while you are still angry" (Ephesians 4:26), and "everyone should be quick to listen, slow to speak and slow to become angry, for man's anger does not bring about the righteous life that God desires" (James 1:19-20).

Yet the real issue we're examining today isn't merely anger—it's violence and judgment. We see this in Jesus clearing the temple, fashioning whips and driving out animals (John 2:15). And we see it again in the passage before us today.

## 1. Jesus the Judge

### The Curious Incident of the Fig Tree (21:18-19)

> Early in the morning, as he was on his way back to the city, he was hungry. Seeing a fig tree by the road, he went up to it but found nothing on it except leaves. Then he said to it, "May you never bear fruit again!" Immediately the tree withered. (Matthew 21:18-19)

This is a strange moment. Jesus speaks to a tree and curses it—and the tree withers. Why? What is Jesus really doing here?

The answer is that Jesus is not against trees. **The fig tree is an Old Testament symbol for Israel.** Throughout Scripture, a fig tree represents the prosperity and peace of God's people. During Solomon's reign, "Judah and Israel, from Dan to Beersheba, lived in safety, each man under his own vine and fig tree" (1 Kings 4:25). When Isaiah speaks of God's blessing to Judah, he promises: "every one of you will eat from his own vine and fig tree" (Isaiah 36:16). The fig tree symbolizes abundance, security, and divine favor.

But in Joel's prophecy of judgment, we see the reversal: "The vine is dried up and the fig tree is withered... Surely the joy of mankind is withered away" (Joel 1:12). The destruction of the fig tree is the destruction of Israel's glory.

Jesus finds this fig tree full of leaves—it appears healthy, promising, full of life—yet it bears no fruit. It is all appearance and no substance. He curses it, and it withers. **He is pronouncing judgment on Israel.**

### The Meaning of Moving Mountains (21:20-22)

> When the disciples saw this, they were amazed. "How did the fig tree wither so quickly?" they asked. Jesus replied, "I tell you the truth, if you have faith and do not doubt, not only can you do what was done to the fig tree, but also you can say to this mountain, 'Go, throw yourself into the sea,' and it will be done. If you believe, you will receive whatever you ask for in prayer." (Matthew 21:20-22)

The disciples are bewildered. Jesus speaks of moving mountains and throwing them into the sea. What does this mean?

Listen carefully: **this mountain** is not just any mountain. The temple was built on the temple mount. Jesus is speaking of the temple itself—the symbol of Israel's might, the visible sign of God's presence with His people. Earlier, when he drove out the money changers, He cleansed this very place.

When Jesus says "throw this mountain into the sea," He is using the language of judgment we see throughout Matthew. When He teaches about sin, He says, "If your eye causes you to sin, gouge it out and throw it away" (Matthew 18:9). When He describes the final judgment, He says the angels "will throw them into the fiery furnace, where there will be weeping and gnashing of teeth" (Matthew 13:42). To throw something into the sea is to utterly destroy it.

And indeed, Jesus Himself confirms this in Matthew 24: "Do you see all these things? I tell you the truth, not one stone here will be left on another; every one will be thrown down" (Matthew 24:2). The temple—this stumbling block to true faith—would be destroyed in AD 70.

But notice what Jesus says: "If you have faith and do not doubt... you will receive whatever you ask for in prayer." Many misread this verse as a promise of prosperity or material blessing. But **faith that moves mountains is faith in God's judgment.** It is trusting that God will judge injustice, will remove the obstacles to His kingdom, will make all things right. Do you believe in judgment? Do you believe God will judge the world?

### Authority to Judge (21:23-27)

> Jesus entered the temple courts, and, while he was teaching, the chief priests and the elders of the people came to him. "By what authority are you doing these things?" they asked. "And who gave you this authority?" (Matthew 21:23)

The religious leaders demand to know: by what authority does Jesus do these things—cleanse the temple, curse the fig tree, teach with such boldness? Who gave Him this right?

Jesus responds with a question: "John's baptism—where did it come from? Was it from heaven, or from men?" (Matthew 21:25). The leaders are trapped. If they say John's baptism was from heaven, Jesus will ask, "Then why didn't you believe him?" If they say it was from men, they fear the crowd, because everyone holds that John was a prophet. So they answer, "We don't know" (Matthew 21:27).

And Jesus says, "Neither will I tell you by what authority I am doing these things" (Matthew 21:27).

**Both John and Jesus are prophets. Both came from heaven.** That is their authority.

## 2. Why Does Ancient Israel Deserve Judgment?

Between these statements about Jesus' authority lie two parables, both describing Israel as a vineyard. Both answer the question: why does Israel deserve judgment?

### The Parable of the Two Sons: Judging Their Insincerity (21:28-32)

> "What do you think? There was a man who had two sons. He went to the first and said, 'Son, go and work today in the vineyard.' 'I will not,' he answered, but later he changed his mind and went. Then the father went to the other son and said the same thing. He answered, 'I will, sir,' but he did not go. Which of the two did what his father wanted?" "The first," they answered. (Matthew 21:28-31)

Think about this: which son would you prefer? The first says no, then repents and does the work. The second says yes, then goes back on his word. Clearly, the father prefers the first son.

Here is the tragedy of Israel: **Israel was like the second son.** God commanded them to follow Him, and they answered, "Yes, we will!" They built a magnificent temple. They brought sacrifices. They praised God with their lips. But their hearts were not in it. They were insincere—they said yes, but they meant no.

By contrast, the tax collectors and prostitutes—those whom Israel despised—were like the first son. They had initially rejected God, living in sin. But when John came preaching repentance, when Jesus came calling, they repented and believed. Matthew himself, the author of this gospel, was a tax collector who became an apostle. Mary Magdalene, a woman known for her sins, became a devoted follower.

> Jesus said to them, "I tell you the truth, the tax collectors and the prostitutes are entering the kingdom of God ahead of you. For John came to you to show you the way of righteousness, and you did not believe him, but the tax collectors and the prostitutes did. And even after you saw this, you did not repent and believe him." (Matthew 21:31-32)

Israel had the form of godliness, the facade of piety, the magnificent temple and elaborate sacrifices—but nothing underneath. No genuine faith. No true repentance. And so **the kingdom is taken from them and given to the tax collectors and prostitutes, to the outsiders and the despised.**

Israel deserves judgment because of their insincerity—their refusal to genuinely repent and believe.

### The Parable of the Tenants: Judging Their Fruitlessness (21:33-45)

> "Listen to another parable: There was a landowner who planted a vineyard. He put a wall around it, dug a winepress in it and built a watchtower. Then he rented the vineyard to some farmers and went away on a journey. When the harvest time approached, he sent his servants to the tenants to collect his fruit." (Matthew 21:33-34)

God plants a vineyard. He builds everything necessary for it to flourish. Then He entrusts it to tenants—Israel—and goes away. At harvest time, He sends His servants to collect the fruit. What does He find?

> "The tenants seized his servants; they beat one, killed another, and stoned a third. Then he sent other servants to them, more than the first time, and the tenants treated them the same way. Last of all, he sent his son to them. 'They will respect my son,' he said. But when the tenants saw the son, they said to each other, 'This is the heir. Come, let's kill him and take his inheritance.' So they took him and threw him out of the vineyard and killed him." (Matthew 21:35-39)

Instead of producing fruit, the tenants commit crimes. They beat and kill the landowner's servants—the prophets God sent to call them back to righteousness. Finally, the landowner sends his own son, hoping they will respect him. But they see only an opportunity: kill the heir, and the vineyard is theirs.

> "Therefore, when the owner of the vineyard comes, what will he do to those tenants?" "He will bring those wretches to a wretched end," they replied, "and he will rent the vineyard to other tenants, who will give him his share of the crop at harvest time." (Matthew 21:40-41)

**God demands fruit.** Not just the appearance of godliness—actual fruit. "You say you are God's people? Show me the fruit!" When God comes to collect His harvest, He finds rebellion, violence, and refusal. The tenants have not only failed to produce fruit; they have actively resisted God and killed His messengers.

So what will God do? He will "bring those wretches to a wretched end" and give the vineyard to others—the Gentiles, the outsiders—who will actually produce fruit.

Israel deserves judgment because of their fruitlessness and their violence against God's prophets.

## 3. The Judgment of Israel and Salvation of the Gentiles (22:1-14)

### The Parable of the Wedding Banquet

> Jesus spoke to them again in parables, saying: "The kingdom of heaven is like a king who prepared a wedding banquet for his son. He sent his servants to those who had been invited to the banquet to tell them to come, but they refused to come." (Matthew 22:1-3)

The king has prepared an extraordinary feast—a wedding banquet for his son. Everything is ready. He invites the honored guests. And they refuse.

> "Then he sent some more servants and said, 'Tell those who have been invited that I have prepared my dinner: My oxen and fattened cattle have been butchered, and everything is ready. Come to the wedding banquet.' But they paid no attention and went off—one to his field, another to his business. The rest seized his servants, mistreated them and killed them." (Matthew 22:4-6)

It's almost incomprehensible: a free feast from a king, and they refuse. Not only do they refuse—they abuse and kill the king's servants.

> "The king was enraged. He sent his army and destroyed those murderers and burned their city." (Matthew 22:7)

The king's response is swift and severe. **He destroys those murderers and burns their city.** This is Jerusalem in AD 70, when Roman armies destroyed the temple and the city in response to Jewish rebellion. But more importantly, it is Jesus pronouncing judgment on Israel for their rejection of Him, the Son of God.

### The Salvation of the Gentiles

> "Then he said to his servants, 'The wedding banquet is ready, but those I invited did not deserve to come. Go to the street corners and invite to the banquet anyone you find.' So the servants went out into the streets and gathered all the people they could find, both good and bad, and the wedding hall was filled with guests." (Matthew 22:8-10)

This is the turning point—and the good news for us. **What Israel rejects, the Gentiles receive.** The invitation now goes to the outcasts, the ordinary people, the "bad" as well as the "good." The wedding feast is filled with unexpected guests.

> "But when the king came in to see the guests, he noticed a man there who was not wearing wedding clothes. 'Friend,' he asked, 'how did you get in here without wedding clothes?' The man was speechless. Then the king told the attendants, 'Tie him hand and foot, and throw him outside, into the darkness, where there will be weeping and gnashing of teeth.' For many are invited, but few are chosen." (Matthew 22:11-14)

Yet there is a warning embedded here. Many are invited to the feast, but few are chosen. One guest arrives improperly dressed—not in wedding clothes. He has entered the feast but has not truly prepared himself, has not honored the occasion. And he is cast out into darkness.

## The Warning to Us

We must not fall into Israel's trap. While there is a great difference between ancient Israel and the church today, we are warned by their mistakes.

**From the Parable of the Two Sons:** We can easily become insincere. We say we believe, we claim to follow Christ, we do Christian things—but our hearts are not truly in it. We serve for status rather than from genuine faith. We make commitments and then renege on them. We have the form of godliness but lack the reality.

Ask yourself: Do you truly repent? Do you truly believe? Or are you merely going through the motions? Judgment awaits those who claim faith but do not possess it.

**From the Parable of the Tenants:** We can fail to produce fruit. The fruit of the Spirit is "love, joy, peace, forbearance, kindness, goodness, faithfulness, gentleness and self-control" (Galatians 5:22-23). Yet we often show the opposite—anger without righteousness, unforgiveness, refusal to serve, hoarding what God has given us. We refuse to give our Master what is His due: our time, our talents, our treasure, our obedience.

And we can become obstacles to God's kingdom. Israel's temple became a stumbling block, a place of corruption and false religion. We too can become stumbling blocks if we claim to follow Christ but live in contradiction to His teachings.

> "The king came in to see the guests, he noticed a man there who was not wearing wedding clothes."

**The warning is simple: be ready.** Don't be found in God's presence improperly dressed—unprepared, unfaithful, fruitless. Many are invited to the kingdom. But few are chosen—those who genuinely repent, truly believe, and produce the fruit of righteousness.

## A Word on Righteous Anger

Before we conclude, one important clarification. In emphasizing judgment, we must remember: "In your anger do not sin; do not let the sun go down while you are still angry, and do not give the devil a foothold" (Ephesians 4:26-27).

God's anger is righteous. Ours must be too. There is a place for righteous anger—at injustice, at sin, at cruelty. When we see marriages destroyed, when we see the vulnerable exploited, when we see injustice reign, we should be angry. But our anger must be deliberative, not merely emotional. It must lead to action rooted in love, not revenge.

Paul reminds us: "Do not repay anyone evil for evil... Do not take revenge, my friends, but leave room for God's wrath, for it is written: 'It is mine to avenge; I will repay,' says the Lord" (Romans 12:17-19).

**Vengeance belongs to God. Justice belongs to God. Judgment belongs to God.** Our role is to pursue what is right, to live at peace where possible, and to trust that God will ultimately judge all things.

And this is where faith in moving mountains comes full circle. We who follow Christ find ourselves in the same position as the faithful Jews of the first century—we await the Messiah's return to judge injustice and sin. We pray for His kingdom to come, for His will to be done on earth. We trust that the mountains of injustice will indeed be thrown into the sea, that evil will not triumph forever, that God's righteous judgment will ultimately prevail.

Have faith. Believe in His judgment. The day is coming.
