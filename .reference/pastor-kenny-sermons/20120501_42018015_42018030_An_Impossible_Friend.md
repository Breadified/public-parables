# Luke 18:15-30

**Title:** An Impossible Friend?  
**Date:** 19 May 2012  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## The Question: How Do We Inherit Eternal Life?

> A certain ruler asked him, "Good teacher, what must I do to inherit eternal life?" (v.18)

This is a question that resonates with all of us who believe there is life beyond this world. Whether we've experienced loss through death in our families or simply recognize our own mortality, we all wonder about eternal life. The ruler asking this question is someone of importance and influence—he carries authority and power over others. Yet unlike the Pharisee in the parable we studied last week (Luke 18:9-14), this ruler approaches Jesus with humility rather than arrogance. He doesn't assume he already has eternal life; he genuinely asks how to obtain it.

### The Law as the Path to Life

Jesus responds by pointing to the commandments:

> "You know the commandments: 'You shall not commit adultery, you shall not murder, you shall not steal, you shall not give false testimony, honor your father and mother.'" (v.20)

These are part of God's law—the Hebrew summary of God's moral requirements. The message seems straightforward: do the law and you will live. This same answer appears elsewhere when someone poses the identical question (Luke 10:25-28). The law itself is meant to bring life.

Many Christians unfortunately overlook the Old Testament, yet three-quarters of the Bible is the Old Testament. When we claim "the Bible doesn't say anything about that," we often haven't checked what Scripture actually reveals through the ancient texts.

### The Ruler's Confident Response

The ruler's answer is striking:

> "All these I have kept since I was a boy," he said. (v.21)

Since his youth—not just recently, but consistently throughout his life—he claims to have followed these commandments. Most of us could say something similar when judged by the letter of the law rather than its spirit:

- We haven't committed adultery (especially if we're not married)
- We haven't murdered anyone
- We haven't physically stolen from others
- We haven't perjured ourselves in court
- We show respect to our parents, attend family gatherings, and protect our family's honor

By these external measures, the ruler—and perhaps we ourselves—can claim, "All this I have kept."

### The Shocking Verdict

Yet Jesus' assessment startles everyone:

> "You still lack one thing. Sell everything you have and give to the poor, and you will have treasure in heaven. Then come, follow me." (v.22)

Despite keeping all the commandments listed, the ruler is still lacking. There's a wordplay here: he has kept *all* these things, yet he lacks *one* thing, and Jesus tells him to sell *all* he has.

This reveals a profound truth. In verse 19, Jesus asked, "Why do you call me good? No one is good—except God alone." Jesus is suggesting that no one has truly kept all the commandments, and therefore no one can inherit eternal life through the law alone.

The ruler's particular lack is evident in his refusal to follow Jesus. When Jesus says "follow me," the ruler becomes deeply sad and presumably walks away, unwilling to abandon his great wealth.

## Two Aspects of the Ruler's Failure

### He Lacked Love for God

Among the Ten Commandments are those that define our relationship with God:
- No gods before Me
- Do not make idols
- Do not misuse God's name
- Remember the Sabbath

The ruler kept the commandments relating to other people (adultery, murder, stealing, false testimony, honoring parents), but he neglected those that speak to our relationship with God. His refusal to follow Jesus reveals this lack of trust in God. He was willing to be nice to others, but not to surrender everything and follow the one who calls him.

**Application:** Our church community is wonderfully hospitable and caring toward one another. You welcome newcomers, write letters to each other, make phone calls, and support one another through crises. This is beautiful. But all of this would be meaningless—no different from a caring hospital, charity, or hotel—if it wasn't rooted in our decision to follow Jesus. We do these things because we follow Him, not simply to be nice. That is what makes our fellowship distinctly Christian.

### He Failed the One Thing That Mattered

The ruler faced his Achilles' heel. In Greek mythology, Achilles' mother dipped her infant son into a magical river to make him invulnerable, but the heel where she held him remained unprotected. That single vulnerability ultimately destroyed him.

Similarly, a student might excel in every subject except one required course, which becomes the barrier to graduation. It's that one point of vulnerability that changes everything.

For this ruler, wealth was his vulnerability. It was his idol—the thing he put before God, the thing he would abandon the Sabbath for, the thing he could not let go of.

**Application:** What is your one thing? What part of your life do you refuse to let God touch?

Is it a relationship? A boyfriend or girlfriend that means so much you'd nearly give up God for it?

Is it success? The relentless drive for more, thinking just one more rung on the ladder will satisfy?

Is it your studies or HSC? Consuming all your time and energy so you have nothing left for God or His people?

Is it your career? Your identity so wrapped up in professional achievement that you compromise relationships, family, and spiritual health to climb higher?

What is the one thing you never want to give up for God? What is your Achilles' heel?

God, in His wonderful sense of humor, will challenge you precisely in these areas. He does this not because He's mean or picky, but because He's inoculating you. He's trying to dip that heel so you won't be vulnerable to it anymore—so it won't be a stumbling block, won't enslave you with regrets, won't waste your life, and won't become your destruction.

God is doing you a favor.

Friends, put that one thing away. You think it's important, but it's not. God has better things in store for you.

## The Impossible and the Possible

### The Obstacle: Money and a Divided Heart

> "How hard it is for the rich to enter the kingdom of God! Indeed, it is easier for a camel to go through the eye of a needle than for someone who is rich to enter the kingdom of God." (v.24-25)

Jesus uses the most extreme comparison: the smallest opening with the largest animal of the Middle East. He's describing the impossible.

Throughout Scripture, Jesus emphasizes the problem with wealth:

> "No servant can serve two masters. Either he will hate the one and love the other, or he will be devoted to the one and despise the other. You cannot serve both God and Money." (Luke 16:13)

You might object: "I don't love money. I just love what it buys." Or, "I don't love money; I just don't want to let it go."

Friends, we do love money. We love it personally and as a nation. When our government allocates aid at 0.35% of our Gross National Income—roughly 35 cents for every $100 earned—that's how generous we are as a nation. This despite being one of the world's richest nations, largely avoiding the global financial crisis, with low unemployment. We hold tightly to what we have.

The wealthy have a particular vulnerability: they don't need God. The richest suburbs of Sydney don't host churches that promise prosperity through giving. They're already so wealthy they don't sense their need for God.

It is an impossible friendship—between wealth and the kingdom of God.

### The Solution: What God Makes Possible

> "Those who heard this asked, 'Who then can be saved?' Jesus replied, 'What is impossible with man is possible with God.'" (v.26-27)

God makes the impossible possible through Christ, who gave everything.

Christ came to earth already poor—not as a prince with trappings, but as a carpenter. Yet He gave up even that. He surrendered His claims, His rights, His dignity, and ultimately His life itself on the cross, pouring out His love for everyone.

Because of this, Peter can declare:

> "We have left all we had to follow you!" (v.28)

And Jesus affirms this is possible:

> "Truly I tell you, no one who has left home or wife or brothers or sisters or parents or children for the sake of the kingdom of God will fail to receive many times as much in this age, and in the age to come eternal life." (v.29-30)

What must you do to inherit eternal life? Follow Jesus. Seek His kingdom first.

> "But seek his kingdom, and these things will be given to you as well." (Luke 12:31)

## Living as a Follower: Holding Wealth Loosely

Don't misunderstand "sell all you have." Jesus isn't saying that every time you earn money, you must immediately give it away. Rather, this is an acknowledgment that we should hold our possessions loosely, not as permanent fixtures in our lives.

We willingly liquidate wealth for:
- Holidays and family experiences
- A home investment
- Expensive purchases
- Migration to a new country

We can equally use our wealth for gospel reasons:
- Supporting missionaries entering ministry
- Practicing generosity as God has been generous to us

This tests whether you sit loosely with your wealth or grip it tightly.

### Use Wealth to Gain Treasure in Heaven

Money itself isn't evil. The point isn't that wealth is bad, but that we should *use* it wisely:

> "Sell everything you have and give to the poor, and you will have treasure in heaven." (v.22)

Give to the poor. Exchange earthly wealth for heavenly treasure. Don't hoard or accumulate endlessly.

Consider a child with new toys on her birthday—delighted today, tomorrow's junk. Enjoy what you have. Thank God for it. But stop investing in future junk. Invest in treasures in heaven.

Material accumulation is not worth it. The toys fade, hobbies lose their appeal, possessions decay. But treasures in heaven are eternal.

The ruler spent his life accumulating good deeds and great wealth. Yet when faced with following Jesus, he walked away sad, unwilling to surrender what he had built.

### A Generous Friend Makes Friendship Possible

For those not yet Christians, the cost of following Jesus may seem high. But it is worth it.

For those already following Jesus, you've already decided to sit loosely toward this world's wealth and delights. You knew that whatever the world offers, the real treasure is in heaven. Now it should be easier to use your wealth when asked for support.

Merit might determine *whether* to give, but never *why* you withhold support. "He had great wealth" should never be your excuse. Unwillingness born from fear of loss doesn't make sense when God has demonstrated such generosity toward us.

## The Call

> "Let the little children come to me, and do not hinder them, for the kingdom of God belongs to such as these. I tell you the truth, anyone who will not receive the kingdom of God like a little child will never enter it." (Luke 18:16-17)

What is stopping you today?

Perhaps you're asking: What must I do to inherit eternal life?

The answer: Give up all—make earthly things secondary—and follow Him. Be friends with God.
