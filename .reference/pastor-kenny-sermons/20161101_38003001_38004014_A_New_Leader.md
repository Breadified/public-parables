# Zechariah 3:1-4:14

**Title:** A New Leader
**Date:** 20 November 2016 (SMAC); 22 November 2020 (ARISE)
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction

We're reading from Zechariah, a book full of visions and dreams. Dreams are always strange, but today we'll explore the fourth and fifth visions—particularly chapter 4's teaching on the separation of powers, then chapter 3's exploration of leadership.

The big theme throughout Zechariah is God's call to the returning exiles: "Return to me and I will return to you." After their exile in Babylon, the Jewish people have returned to their homeland under the Persian empire. But a fundamental question remains: **Who will lead the reconstruction of the temple and Jerusalem?**

## Part One: Two Leaders Will Rebuild

### The Vision of the Golden Lampstand

> Then the angel who talked with me returned and woke me up, like someone awakened from sleep. He asked me, "What do you see?" I answered, "I see a solid gold lampstand with a bowl at the top and seven lamps on it, with seven channels to the lamps. Also there are two olive trees by it, one on the right of the bowl and the other on its left." (Zechariah 4:1-3)

This vision presents a striking image. The lampstand and bowl together symbolize **God's unity and constant presence**. The seven lamps represent completeness, and the seven channels that feed oil to the lamps speak of God's never-ceasing care. In the temple instructions, these were meant to burn continually, reminding Israel that God is always there.

The lamps come from an unexpected source:

> These are the two who are anointed to serve the Lord of all the earth. (Zechariah 4:14)

Two olive trees supply oil through golden pipes to keep the lamps burning. The word "anointed" carries a play on words—it means "oiled," connecting to the concept of messiah. These two figures represent two distinct leadership lines who will work together to sustain God's presence among the people.

### Zerubbabel: The Kingly Leader

The first of these two leaders is **Zerubbabel**, the governor of Judah:

> So he said to me, "This is the word of the Lord to Zerubbabel: 'Not by might nor by power, but by my Spirit,' says the Lord Almighty. What are you, mighty mountain? Before Zerubbabel you will become level ground. Then he will bring out the capstone to shouts of 'God bless it! God bless it!'" (Zechariah 4:6-7)

Zerubbabel's role is building. He lays the foundation and will complete the temple:

> The hands of Zerubbabel have laid the foundation of this temple; his hands will also complete it. (Zechariah 4:9)

Zerubbabel was a descendant of the kings of Judah—his father Shealtiel was descended from Jehoiachin. He represents the kingly line, the executive authority that will oversee the physical reconstruction.

### Joshua: The Priestly Leader

The second leader appears in chapter 3:

> Then he showed me Joshua the high priest standing before the angel of the Lord, and Satan standing at his right side to accuse him. (Zechariah 3:1)

The name "Joshua" evokes the great leader of old who brought God's people into the promised land. This new Joshua, the high priest, embodies the spiritual leadership of the people. He represents the priestly line and serves as head of the temple—the legislative and spiritual authority.

## Part Two: The Problems These Leaders Face

### Problem One: A Corrupt Priest

Joshua stands before God, but there's a critical problem:

> Now Joshua was dressed in filthy clothes as he stood before the angel. (Zechariah 3:3)

The filthy garments symbolize spiritual corruption and unfitness for office. Satan accuses him on these grounds—if your leader is unclean, how can he represent you before God? Jeremiah reminds us: "The heart is deceitful above all things and beyond cure. Who can understand it?" (Jeremiah 17:9)

What use is a priest clothed in sin? He cannot represent the people before God, let alone before the world.

### Problem Two: A Cursed King

Zerubbabel faces his own obstacle. His lineage traces back to King Jehoiachin, but there was a curse upon that line:

> Record this man as if childless, a man who will not prosper in his lifetime, for none of his offspring will prosper, none will sit on the throne of David or rule anymore in Judah. (Jeremiah 22:30)

Yet here is Zerubbabel, seemingly destined to lead. The curse seems to prohibit his very role.

### Problem Three: A Priest Acting as King

Even more troubling is the mixing of roles. In chapter 6, something unprecedented happens:

> Take the silver and gold and make a crown, and set it on the head of the high priest, Joshua son of Jozadak. Tell him this is what the Lord Almighty says: 'Here is the man whose name is the Branch, and he will branch out from his place and build the temple of the Lord. It is he who will build the temple of the Lord, and he will be clothed with majesty and will sit and rule on his throne. And he will be a priest on his throne. And there will be harmony between the two.' (Zechariah 6:11-13)

This violates fundamental principles. The law explicitly separated these roles. Priests came from the tribe of Levi and had no inheritance of land—they were supported by offerings. Kings came from Judah and could own property and pass it to heirs. Genesis 49:10 promises the scepter will not depart from Judah, while Numbers 3:10 reserves the high priesthood for Aaron's descendants alone.

Even King Uzziah learned this lesson harshly when he presumed to burn incense:

> It is not right for you, Uzziah, to burn incense to the Lord. That is for the priests, the descendants of Aaron, who have been consecrated to burn incense. (2 Chronicles 26:18)

## Part Three: The Separation of Powers

What we're seeing in Zechariah is a **biblical principle of separated authority**: one leader from the kingly line (executive power), one from the priestly line (spiritual/legislative authority). This isn't chaos—it's design.

History shows us the danger of consolidated power. In the 14th century, the papal schism created first two popes, then three, as different parties tried to resolve conflicting claims. When Rwanda's Anglican church split, one faction claimed millions of followers, another just thousands—both claiming legitimacy. Consolidated authority invites such disasters.

The Western tradition of separating powers—legislative, judicial, and executive—didn't originate in England or America. The Jews had it thousands of years earlier.

### Why Separation of Powers Matters

We might think fragmented authority is inefficient. Queensland abolished its upper house; New South Wales consolidated local councils. But this pursuit of efficiency carries real danger.

Consider: If all power concentrates in one place, you risk tyranny. Judge, jury, and executioner become one. Your data lives in a single database. One day, that unified government turns evil—and there's no check to stop it.

History teaches us that **power corrupts, and absolute power corrupts absolutely**. The separation of powers isn't a flaw—it's a feature. It's designed to slow government down, to make radical change difficult, to protect ordinary citizens from concentrated authority.

The choice before any society is stark: Either government is **ineffective**, wasting energy fighting other centers of power and competing with families, churches, and voluntary associations. Or it becomes **dangerously effective**, acting as one coordinated force—with all the peril that entails.

Decentralized governance—churches, families, businesses, local associations all having real authority—is biblically wiser and practically safer than concentrating power in government hands.

## Part Four: The Solution—A Perfect King and Priest

The mixing of roles, the corruption, the curse—all these problems point toward a future resolution. The text hints at a coming figure who will embody both positions in harmony.

### Cleansing and Renewal

God addresses the corruption directly:

> The Lord said to Satan, "The Lord rebuke you, Satan! The Lord, who has chosen Jerusalem, rebuke you! Is not this man a burning stick snatched from the fire?" (Zechariah 3:2)

Joshua is a burning stick pulled from the fire—representing judgment, but judgment that ends. Israel experienced exile but was not destroyed. And then:

> Take off his filthy clothes. Then he said to Joshua, "See, I have taken away your sin, and I will put fine garments on you." Then I said, "Put a clean turban on his head." So they put a clean turban on his head and clothed him, while the angel of the Lord stood by. (Zechariah 3:4-5)

God removes the filthy garments and replaces them with clean priestly vestments. Sin is taken away. Holiness is restored.

### The Coming Branch

God's covenant with Joshua is renewed with a promise:

> If you will walk in obedience to me and keep my requirements, then you will govern my house and have charge of my courts, and I will give you a place among these standing here. Listen, High Priest Joshua, you and your associates seated before you, who are men symbolic of things to come: I am going to bring my servant, the Branch. See, the stone I have set in front of Joshua! There are seven eyes on that one stone, and I will engrave an inscription on it, says the Lord Almighty, and **I will remove the sin of this land in a single day.** (Zechariah 3:7-9)

The Branch—a messianic title pointing to a future shoot from David's line who will embody both king and priest.

### Jesus: The Perfect Union

Within five hundred years, this promise finds fulfillment in Jesus Christ.

Jesus is a **true king** because He descended from Judah:

> For it is clear that our Lord descended from Judah, and in regard to that tribe Moses said nothing about priests. (Hebrews 7:14)

Jesus is a **true priest**, but not from the Levitical line. Instead, He serves in the order of Melchizedek:

> You are a priest forever, in the order of Melchizedek. (Psalm 110:4, cited in Hebrews 7:17)

Most importantly, Jesus is the only leader who is **sinless**:

> Such a high priest truly meets our need—one who is holy, blameless, pure, set apart from sinners, exalted above the heavens. (Hebrews 7:26)

In Jesus, the separation of powers finds its ultimate resolution. He combines perfect justice (the king's role) with perfect mercy (the priest's role). There is no conflict, no corruption, no curse—only harmony.

### The Future Hope

Because Jesus is the perfect leader—both king and priest, both just and merciful—the need for separated powers dissolves. In His coming kingdom, we will have:

- One perfect leader over all
- No more politics or elections
- No more partisanship
- No more tyranny

We long for that day. Until then, we remain grateful for separated powers on earth—an imperfect safeguard against humanity's tendency toward corruption. But our ultimate hope rests in one leader alone: Jesus Christ, the Branch, the sinless Priest-King.

## Application: Who Is Leading You?

Here's the hard truth: **No priest from Aaron onward—including this Joshua—ever met the criterion of sinlessness.** No king from Saul to Zedekiah ruled without sin. They all fell short.

But Jesus is different.

In a world where leaders constantly disappoint, Jesus remains constant. Other leaders come and go, voted in and out, subject to age and decline. Not so with our Leader:

- He is never voted out
- He will never lose capacity
- He always acts for our good
- He rules both in season and out of season

If you must follow multiple earthly leaders (pastors, politicians, elders), that's understandable—we live in a fallen world. But ask yourself: **Who is your ultimate leader?**

World leaders rise and fall. Only one Leader endures. Only one has earned absolute trust.

Let us follow God's chosen leader—Jesus—in and out of season, secure in the knowledge that He alone is worthy of our ultimate allegiance.
