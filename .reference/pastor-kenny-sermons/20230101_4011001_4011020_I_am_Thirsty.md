# Numbers 11:1-20

**Title:** I am Thirsty
**Date:** 8 January 2023
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction: The Journey and Our Complaints

We grumble on long journeys, don't we? A six-hour drive with complaining children—"Are we there yet?" "I'm hungry." "I'm thirsty." "Too hot, too cold." One child even complained, "My bum's itchy." A flight to Hong Kong with six kids brought more of the same: complaints about the TV, the stuffiness, the food.

The book of Numbers tells a similar story. The Israelites were escaping slavery in Egypt, heading toward the promised land. What should have been an eleven-day journey became forty years. Why? Because they grumbled and had cravings.

## 1. Cravings Enslave Us (Verses 4-9)

> Now the people complained about their hardships in the hearing of the Lord, and when he heard them his anger was aroused. Then fire from the Lord burned among them and consumed some of the outskirts of the camp. (Numbers 11:1)

Just three days into an eleven-day walk, the people began to complain. But notice the deeper issue: they didn't just wish for something—they "craved cravingly," or as we might say, "lusted lustfully." That's never good.

### What They Craved

> The rabble with them began to crave other food, and again the Israelites started wailing and said, "If only we had meat to eat!" (Numbers 11:4)

> We remember the fish we ate in Egypt at no cost—also the cucumbers, melons, leeks, onions and garlic. (Numbers 11:5)

They wanted to return to Egyptian food—a Greek salad of vegetables, fresh fish from the Nile. But they were in the desert. What they craved was impossible and unreasonable.

### What They Actually Had: Manna

> But now we have lost our appetite; we never see anything but this manna! (Numbers 11:6)

Before reaching Sinai, God had given them manna—miraculous bread from heaven. Look at what Scripture tells us about it:

> The manna was like coriander seed and looked like resin. The people went around gathering it, and then ground it in a handmill or crushed it in a mortar. They cooked it in a pot or made it into cakes. And it tasted like something made with olive oil. When the dew settled on the camp at night, the manna also came down. (Numbers 11:7-9)

Consider what manna offered them:
- **Attractive:** Golden-colored like honey or resin
- **Convenient:** It fell regularly, the first takeaway food—no farming required
- **Versatile:** Could be ground like flour, cooked in a pot, or made into cakes
- **Tasty:** Like cake baked with olive oil
- **Clean:** It fell on a protective layer of dew

A good staple diet. No reason to complain.

### The Real Problem: Distorted Memory and Ungrateful Hearts

Yet they exaggerated their plight. They claimed they had *only* manna, but this was false. From Exodus 12:38, we know the Israelites brought flocks and herds of sheep, goats, and cattle. They had regular supplies of milk, butter, cheese, and yogurt—and meat, if they rationed properly.

They didn't just speak evil about their situation—they craved something unreasonable. In the desert, they expected the luxuries of Egypt's fertile Nile valley. They were like someone in the middle of the Arabian Peninsula going to the canteen and expecting fine dining.

The Israelites might have been bored, stressed, tired, or frightened. But they were not hungry, and they had other food. It was adequate. It was fine.

### Grumbling Defined: Speaking Evil of Good

> Now the people complained about their hardships (speaking evil) in the hearing of the Lord. (Numbers 11:1)

This is grumbling: speaking evil of what is good. It's forgetting how good we have it and forgetting how bad things were.

We do this too. We complain about being poor while owning smartphones. We whinge about education costs while being richer than our parents were at our age. We forget that nothing in life is truly free—everything is paid for by someone, somewhere. Instead of appreciating what we have, we crave after things related to the old way of life.

### The Danger: Cravings Lead to Internal War

> What causes fights and quarrels among you? Don't they come from your desires (hedone) that battle within you? You want (epithymia) something but don't get it. You quarrel and fight. When you ask, you do not receive, because you ask wrongly, that you may spend what you get on your pleasures. (James 4:1-3)

Our cravings create internal conflict that spills outward:
- We crave success and status, leading us to competition and cheating at school
- We crave sexual satisfaction, leading us to struggle against God's design
- We crave more possessions, willing to steal—openly from shops, or more sophisticatedly through illegal downloads
- This is the way of the world, and it leaves us in a terrible state.

## 2. Cravings Result in Judgment (Verses 10, 33)

> Moses heard the people of every family wailing, each at the entrance to his tent. The LORD became exceedingly angry. (Numbers 11:10)

God's anger was kindled. And what followed reveals the terrible consequence of being enslaved to cravings.

### Judgment: Getting What We Asked For

> Tell the people: "Consecrate yourselves in preparation for tomorrow, when you will eat meat. The LORD heard you when you wailed, 'If only we had meat to eat! We were better off in Egypt!' Now the LORD will give you meat, and you will eat it. You will not eat it for just one day, or two days, or five, ten or twenty days, but for a whole month—until it comes out of your nostrils and you loathe it—because you have rejected the LORD, who is among you, and have wailed before him, saying, 'Why did we ever leave Egypt?'" (Numbers 11:18-20)

God gave them what they asked for: meat for an entire month. An all-you-can-eat buffet that became nauseating. They would eat until the meat came out of their nostrils—vomit flowing from their mouths and noses. It's disgusting.

This illustrates what it means to be enslaved to your cravings. When God gives us over to our sin, when He allows us to become enslaved, it's terrible.

### The Israelites Should Have Remembered

The Israelites had been slaves for only a year and a half. Yet they had trouble remembering how bad it was. They said:

> We remember the fish we ate in Egypt at no cost. (Numbers 11:5)

At no cost? They were *slaves*. The food was free only because they had *already paid with their freedom*. It's like saying, "I get free food in prison!" That's not a good thing. It's like being in front of a firing squad and looking forward to the free bullets.

They did not eat at no cost. They paid dearly with their freedom, their dignity, their humanity. Look at what they had endured:

> So they put slave masters over them to oppress them with forced labor, and they built Pithom and Rameses as store cities for Pharaoh. They made their lives bitter with hard labor in brick and mortar and with all kinds of work in the fields; in all their hard labor the Egyptians used them ruthlessly. (Exodus 1:11, 14)

> Then Pharaoh gave this order to all his people: "Every boy that is born you must throw into the Nile, but let every girl live." (Exodus 1:22)

That was the cost of their "free" food. Their souls.

### Death Came Swiftly

> But while the meat was still between their teeth and before it could be consumed, the anger of the LORD burned against the people, and he struck them with a severe plague. Therefore the place was named Kibroth Hattaavah (Graves of Cravings), because there they buried the people who had craved other food. (Numbers 11:33-34)

A plague struck them. People died while the meat was still in their mouths. The place was named "Graves of Cravings"—a monument to the fatal consequences of enslaving yourself to your desires.

God, the sinless judge, cannot let sin go unpunished. This is the consequence of sin. And yet, not all the people died.

> From Kibroth Hattaavah the people travelled to Hazeroth and stayed there. (Numbers 11:35)

The people survived as a nation. Why? Because someone stood in the gap for them.

## 3. The Intercessor (Verses 10-15)

Moses was troubled when he heard the people wailing. He cried out to God:

> Why have you brought this trouble on your servant? What have I done to displease you that you put the burden of all these people on me? Did I conceive all these people? Did I give them birth? Why do you tell me to carry them in my arms, as a nurse carries an infant, to the land you promised on oath to their forefathers? Where can I get meat for all these people? They keep wailing to me, "Give us meat to eat!" I cannot carry all these people by myself; the burden is too heavy for me. If this is how you are going to treat me, put me to death right now—if I have found favor in your eyes—and do not let me face my own ruin. (Numbers 11:11-15)

At first glance, Moses seems to be grumbling too. But this is not complaining—this is intercession. Moses was doing something remarkable: he stood between God's justice and the people's judgment.

### How Moses Interceded

**First,** Moses redirected God's attention from the people to himself. Notice the first-person pronouns throughout: "Why did you trouble *me*?" "What did *I* do?" "Did *I* give them birth?" His questions shifted God's gaze from the guilty people to himself.

**Second,** Moses reframed the people's complaint. Where they had demanded meat from God, Moses reframed it as a burden on himself—making it a question about his adequacy rather than their rebellion.

**Third,** Moses reminded God of His own nature. His questions subtly pointed back to God's character:
- "Did I conceive these people?" No—*God* did.
- "Am I their nurse?" No—*God* nurtured them.
- "Can I bring them to the promised land?" This is *your* promise, God. You brought them out of Egypt. These are *your* people.

Moses highlighted God's compassionate nature as a nursing father, His covenant promises, His love for this people. The implication: Don't let your previous efforts go to waste. These are your children, and they depend on you.

This is what Psalm 106:23 celebrates:

> So he said he would destroy them—had not Moses, his chosen one, stood in the breach before him to keep his wrath from destroying them. (Psalm 106:23)

Moses was not a whinger—he was their savior.

### God Seeks Intercessors

This dynamic is what God desires. He looks for people who will stand in the gap:

> I looked for a man among them who would build up the wall and stand before me in the gap on behalf of the land so I would not have to destroy it, but I found none. So I will pour out my wrath on them and consume them with my fiery anger, bringing down on their own heads all they have done, declares the Sovereign LORD. (Ezekiel 22:30-31)

Throughout Israel's history, God had to search for intercessors. And often He found none. When that happened, judgment came.

### Jesus: The Perfect Intercessor

Here's the good news: We are those under God's judgment, caused by sin and cravings. But Jesus stands in that gap between us and judgment.

Jesus intercedes for us:
- He says to the Father, "Take me instead. Not my friends."
- He takes sin upon Himself: "Father, forgive them, for they do not know what they are doing."
- He feeds us perfectly, as a better Moses: "Have the people sit down...Jesus then took the loaves, gave thanks, and distributed to those who were seated as much as they wanted." (John 6:10-11)

Jesus repeats the feeding of manna, but better this time. With fish and bread. He mediates perfectly for us.

> I am the bread of life. Your forefathers ate the manna in the desert, yet they died. But here is the bread that comes down from heaven, which a man may eat and not die. I am the living bread that came down from heaven. If anyone eats of this bread, he will live forever. This bread is my flesh, which I will give for the life of the world. (John 6:48-51)

Therefore, we have no fear of judgment. We are invited to join under the intercessor—not to be cast out.

## 4. Contentment with God

### Remembering Without Romanticizing

When I speak with visitors from Malaysia, we end up talking about food. I start to long for Malaysian hawker stalls—imagine getting a meal for spare change in the middle of the night! But then I catch myself. I do have it good here. I can eat Malaysian food once a month at a local restaurant. Yes, it's pricier than in Malaysia, but wages are higher here too. The hygiene is better. I'm not worried about sanitation. And if I really wanted food in the middle of the night, I could get it.

But why would I want to go back to Malaysia? The weather is unbearable. The culture was difficult. Certain races were treated as second-class citizens. Education was hard to access. There were good reasons we came to Australia.

Friends, we similarly can long for a past that no longer exists—but if we think about it honestly, we wouldn't want to go back anyway.

### The Danger of Backward Glances

We might see what our non-Christian friends get up to. They seem to have more fun. They make their own rules. They indulge their cravings freely. We start thinking back to before we were Christians—a time when we had more freedom, didn't have to think about consequences, didn't have to ask if God would approve, when we didn't have a conscience to wrestle with.

But that life wasn't freedom—it was slavery. It's like a trafficked slave thinking her former enslavement was better because she was "fed" and had "parties." She was not free.

### The Truth: We've Been Rescued

> But thanks be to God that, though you used to be slaves to sin, you wholeheartedly obeyed the form of teaching to which you were entrusted. You have been set free from sin and have become slaves to righteousness. (Romans 6:17-18)

We have been rescued from slavery to sin. We were slaves to the devil. We were on a road to eternal death. We might have been enjoying ourselves, perhaps gaining the whole world, but we were not free. We were paying for it all the time—it was costing us our soul.

No matter how hard the journey from our former enslavement to heaven, it's far better than being enslaved to sin. Don't go back. Don't slide back. Don't decrease in fervor. Don't decrease in commitment. Stay firm in your church attendance, your fellowship, your spiritual disciplines.

The fruit of the Spirit is self-control. Addiction enslaves. And the consequences follow us into this life and the next.

### The Craving That Satisfies

No longer thirst for sin. Instead, crave the flesh of God—not the things of the world. We have an intercessor. We have been fed with better bread than manna. We have hope that cravings won't be the end of us.
