# John 5:1-47

**Title:** Willing for Life  
**Date:** 24 April 2005  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction: What Do Miracles Signify?

A crucial question emerges as we study the Gospel of John: What is the purpose of miracles? Some argue they exist primarily to impress—to demonstrate God's power and convince skeptics to believe. Others suggest miracles are simply compassionate responses to immediate human need, with no greater theological significance.

But John presents a different understanding. Throughout his Gospel, miracles function as *signs*—pointers to the identity of Jesus as the promised Christ. They are neither random acts of compassion nor mere displays of power. Rather, each miracle communicates a specific spiritual truth about who Jesus is and what He accomplishes.

Consider the pattern: the first sign at Cana was not simply to provide wine for a wedding celebration, but to signify the arrival of new salvation replacing the old covenant. The healing of the royal official's son pointed to the inclusion of Samaritans in God's kingdom. And as John consistently shows us, these signs are often followed by explanation—Jesus or the narrator clarifies the spiritual meaning we might otherwise miss.

As we approach the healing in John 5, we must ask ourselves: What is this miracle pointing to? What is Jesus trying to teach us about Himself and His authority?

## 1. A Willing Jew (5:1-9)

### The Setting: Jerusalem at Bethesda

After the events of chapter 4, Jesus returns to Jerusalem for a Jewish feast. The last time He was in the city, His zeal for God's house moved Him to drive out the temple merchants—a dramatic confrontation revealing His passion for the holiness of God's dwelling place.

This time, the scene is calmer but no less significant. Near the Sheep Gate lies a pool called Bethesda, covered by five roofed colonnades. The pool was reputed to have healing properties, attracting a steady stream of society's most vulnerable: the blind, the lame, the paralyzed. These are not people resting comfortably—they are sprawled across wet stone, some on simple mats, the poor and marginalized clinging to hope for a second chance at life.

Among them lies a man who has been paralyzed for thirty-eight years.

### The Symbolism: Israel's Wandering

On the surface, this appears to be a straightforward healing miracle. But several details suggest a deeper significance. John is painting a portrait of something larger—the condition of the nation Israel itself.

Notice the context: Jesus is in Jerusalem, at a Jewish feast, in a distinctly Jewish setting. The pool name is translated from Aramaic—a detail John includes specifically for Hebrew readers. And most intriguingly, the man has been ill for thirty-eight years.

Thirty-eight years. This number echoes a pivotal moment in Israel's history. In Deuteronomy 2:14, we learn that Israel wandered in the wilderness for thirty-eight years. After Moses led them out of Egyptian slavery, they stood at the threshold of the promised land, but fear gripped them. Rather than trust God and enter, they refused. God's response was to make them wander in circles, until that faithless generation died and a new generation could enter the land with faith.

After thirty-eight years of wandering, Israel was again given a second chance to enter the promised land and inherit the promise.

This chapter is fundamentally about the Jewish situation. It begins with Jesus in Jerusalem at a Jewish feast and will conclude with Moses—the founder of the Jewish nation and the giver of their law. Following chapter 4's account of Samaritans (half-Jews) coming into the kingdom, chapter 5 poses the question: Will the full-blooded Jews also accept Him? Will they take their second chance?

### The Question: Do You Will to Be Healed?

Jesus approaches the invalid and asks a striking question: "Do you want to be healed?"

The man's response is revealing—rather than expressing desire for healing, he complains about his inability to reach the pool when the water stirs. He focuses on his obstacles rather than his longing. Yet when Jesus commands him to stand up, take his mat, and walk, he obeys. The man is healed. Here is a willing Jew, taking his second chance at life.

The healing itself points to a profound truth: not earthly pools or ritual waters will bring true life—not the pool at Bethesda, not the water from Jacob's well, not the purification jars of Jewish tradition. Jesus alone is the bringer of life. He offers not temporary relief, but lasting restoration.

We expect the narrative to continue with celebration and the influx of Jewish believers, just as the Samaritans had believed in chapter 4. But we are interrupted by a single narrator's comment that shifts everything.

*Now that day was the Sabbath.*

## 2. Opposing the Father's Will (5:9b-18)

### The Sabbath Law and Its Expansion

The Sabbath was God's gift to Israel—the fourth commandment, a day of rest given to teach trust in God's provision. In the wilderness during their thirty-eight years of wandering, God provided manna six days a week, with a double portion on Friday so the people could rest on the Sabbath without laboring for food. This earthly rest was meant to point them toward an eternal rest—a future day when God would provide all things and His people could cease from their earthly struggles.

The Sabbath was never meant to be merely idle. Ceremonies, rituals, and sacrifices still required performance. But the core principle was clear: no work to obtain food. The people were to trust God's provision and use their rest to remember the eternal rest to come.

However, over more than a thousand years, Jewish leaders developed extensive oral traditions to define precisely what "work" meant on the Sabbath. The simple principle became buried under layers of interpretation. If you were lying down but thinking about plowing the field, did that constitute work? If your donkey fell into a pit on the Sabbath, should you rescue it? If firewood fell on your foot, were you permitted to remove it?

To illustrate how detailed this became, consider the Australian Income Tax Act—ten thousand pages long, with new laws constantly added to address loopholes. At the current rate of expansion, it would eventually require three million years simply to read through it. Similarly, the Sabbath law developed into thirty-nine separate categories of regulation. One of these prohibitions: carrying your mat.

### The Clash: Law vs. Life

The Jews confront the healed man immediately. "It is the Sabbath. You cannot carry your mat." It is shocking how completely they miss the miracle. Instead of witnessing resurrection-like healing—a sign that God's kingdom is breaking into history—they fixate on a technical violation. They strain at a gnat and swallow a camel.

The healed man's response is telling: "The man who healed me told me to take up my bed and walk." He correctly identifies his healer as the authority behind his action. But the Jews are unmoved.

### Jesus' Troubling Response

When Jesus later finds the healed man in the temple, His words carry an unexpected weight: "See, you are well! Sin no more, that nothing worse may happen to you."

This statement introduces an unsettling note. What has this man been doing? Sinning in what way? More disturbing still, when asked who healed him, the man goes away and reports Jesus to the Jewish leaders. Is this naive gratitude or calculated betrayal? Has this "willing Jew" proven unwilling after all? His true character will be revealed as we continue.

### The Escalation: From Persecution to Murder Plot

The conflict intensifies rapidly. The Sabbath violation leads to persecution. But when Jesus responds, "My Father is working until now, and I am working," the stakes multiply.

The Jews understand perfectly what Jesus is claiming. By saying He works as His Father works, He is asserting an equality with God that seems blasphemous. The first commandment demands exclusive worship: "You shall have no other gods before me." Yet here stands someone claiming the same authority, the same power, the same divine prerogative.

Their response escalates from persecution to a murderous plot. The Sabbath—that day meant to culminate in rest and life—becomes the occasion for planning death. The irony is devastating: Jesus gives life on the Sabbath while the Jews scheme to take it.

## 3. Living the Father's Will (5:19-29)

### The Nature of True Equality

But here is the critical point: Jesus is not making Himself equal to God. Rather, He is already equal with God—but He demonstrates this equality through radical dependence and obedience to the Father.

This stands in stark contrast to humanity's first rebellion. Adam and Eve sought to "make themselves" like God, to know good and evil independently, to establish their own rules. Their equality was self-asserted and represented rebellion against God's authority.

Jesus' equality is entirely different. His own testimony is insufficient:

> "If I alone bear witness about myself, my testimony is not true." (5:31)

Instead, His testimony derives from another:

> "There is another who bears witness about me, and I know that the testimony that he bears about me is true." (5:32)

When Jesus says the Father is working, so He is working, He means exactly this: "I am not doing My own thing. I am not making up the rules. I am following My Father's instructions" (5:30).

### The Father's Attestation Through Works

> "Truly, truly, I say to you, the Son can do nothing of his own accord, but only what he sees the Father doing. For whatever the Father does, that the Son does likewise. For the Father loves the Son and shows him all that he himself is doing. And greater works than these will he show him, so that you may marvel." (5:19-20)

Here is the foundation of the miracle and its meaning. Jesus demonstrates absolute dependence on the Father. He can do nothing independently. He watches what the Father does and does likewise. It is a relationship of intimate love and perfect learning—like a master craftsman training his son in the family trade.

The Father attests to the Son's qualifications and gives Him works to perform as His seal of approval. This healing of a man paralyzed for thirty-eight years is the first attestation. But greater works are coming, because the Father grants the Son authority over two divine functions: the power to give life and the power to judge.

### Authority Over Life

> "Truly, truly, I say to you, an hour is coming, and is now here, when the dead will hear the voice of the Son of God, and those who hear will live. For as the Father has life in himself, so he has granted the Son also to have life in himself." (5:25-26)

Jesus is the giver of life. This is His supreme prerogative as God's Son. He heals the paralyzed man, but His ultimate authority extends to raising the dead—not merely in body, but in spiritual resurrection. To hear His voice and respond is to live. This is why John's Gospel declares: "For God did not send his Son into the world to condemn the world, but in order that the world might be saved through him" (3:17).

> "For as the Father raises the dead and gives them life, so also the Son gives life to whom he will." (5:21)

### Authority Over Judgment

> "And he has given him authority to execute judgment, because he is the Son of Man. Do not marvel at this, for an hour is coming when all who are in the tombs will hear his voice and come out, those who have done good to the resurrection of life, and those who have done evil to the resurrection of judgment." (5:27-29)

Jesus also possesses all judgment authority:

> "The Father judges no one, but has given all judgment to the Son, that all may honor the Son, just as they honor the Father. Whoever does not honor the Son does not honor the Father who sent him." (5:22-23)

The Father, as author of life and judge of the world, has entrusted both functions to the Son. Jesus is the gatekeeper of eternity. He determines who enters the resurrection of life and who faces the resurrection of judgment. Yet He does so always under the Father's authority, seeking not His own will but the will of the One who sent Him.

> "Truly, truly, I say to you, whoever hears my word and believes him who sent me has eternal life. He does not come into judgment, but has passed from death to life." (5:24)

### Application: Jesus Is the Only Way

The church is growing, and I'm grateful you're here. Most of us likely believe in God—we're part of the eighty-five percent of Australians who do. But here's the striking disconnect: nearly the same percentage do not believe in Jesus.

I visit mission fields regularly and am amazed at conversations I can have about God that never mention Jesus. 

"Do you believe in God?" Yes.  
"What about Jesus?" Don't know. Don't care. Nice guy, maybe.  
"I'm religious, just not in Jesus."

There's a fundamental misunderstanding here. You cannot honor God without honoring His Son. Jesus comes under the full authority of the living God. There is no access to God that bypasses Jesus. To worship God "in Spirit and truth" means to worship through Jesus.

If you honor God but refuse to honor Jesus, you stand exactly where the skeptical Jews stood. They were willing to acknowledge God while rejecting His Son. This is an impossible position. To be in with God means to be in with Jesus. To refuse Jesus is to place yourself outside God's covenant, in opposition to Him.

The Father and Son act in perfect unity. Jesus holds the key to salvation and judgment on the Father's behalf. You cannot get right with God while remaining at odds with the Son. Not through Mary, not through saints, not through any other divine being. The Son alone holds authority over life and judgment. Everything eternal depends on your relationship with Him.

## 4. The Unwilling Jews (5:31-47)

### John's Testimony: A Burning Lamp

> "You sent to John, and he has borne witness to the truth. Not that the testimony that I receive is from man, but I say these things so that you may be saved." (5:33-34)

John the Baptist pointed his followers directly to Jesus, bearing witness to His true identity. Yet the Jews were willing only to "rejoice for a while in his light" (5:35). They enjoyed John's charisma and message temporarily, but refused to follow where John pointed—to Jesus.

### The Father's Testimony: The Works

> "But the testimony that I have is greater than that of John. For the works that the Father has given me to accomplish, the very works that I am doing, bear witness about me that the Father has sent me. And the Father who sent me has himself borne witness about me." (5:36-37)

The works Jesus performs are superior testimony. Each miracle is the Father's signature, attesting that Jesus has been sent from God to accomplish His purposes. The Father Himself bears witness through these signs.

Yet:

> "His voice you have never heard, his form you have never seen, and you do not have his word abiding in you, for you do not believe the one whom he has sent." (5:37-38)

The Jews have never encountered God directly, never seen His form, never heard His voice—because they reject the One in whom God's word and character are fully embodied. Their problem is not intellectual but spiritual: the word of God does not dwell in their hearts.

### The Paradox: Searching Scripture but Missing Its Message

> "You search the Scriptures because you think that in them you have eternal life; and it is they that bear witness about me." (5:39)

The Jewish leaders were diligent students of Scripture, searching it daily. Yet they missed what it was meant to reveal. Like Calvin from the comic strip, so focused on scanning the ground for a dropped penny that he fails to notice the dollar bill floating just above his head, the Jews examined the law so minutely that they overlooked the eternal rest it pointed to—a time when the paralyzed would walk and the dead would hear the voice of God's Son.

Why did they miss Jesus throughout the Old Testament?

> "You do not have the love of God within you. I have come in my Father's name, and you do not receive me." (5:42-43)

The barrier was not intellectual but volitional—a matter of the will. They did not love God genuinely. They did not seek His glory but their own. Therefore, they could not recognize His Son when He appeared.

> "Do not think that I will accuse you to the Father. There is one who accuses you: Moses, on whom you have set your hope. If you believed Moses, you would believe me; for he wrote of me. But if you do not believe his writings, how will you believe my words?" (5:45-47)

Even Moses, the founder of Israel and the giver of the law, becomes their accuser. The very laws given to enhance life and point to heaven condemn them through their misuse and distortion. Their problem extends far deeper than their present rejection of Jesus—it reaches back through their history as a nation, beginning in the desert with their grumbling and faithlessness after God delivered them from Pharaoh's might.

### The Fatal Refusal

> "Yet you refuse to come to me that you may have life." (5:40)

This is the heart of their sin: they *will not come*. Literally, they refuse to will Jesus' truth and authority. They have become unwilling.

Now we understand the riddle of the healed paralyzed man. Returning to verse 7, his first response was not eagerness for healing but complaint about his inability to reach the water. Where was his willingness? And when Jesus later commands him to "sin no more," we see his answer: he dishonors Jesus by reporting Him to the Jewish leaders. Rather than glorifying his healer, he betrays Him.

The Jews were invalids—blind, lame, and paralyzed—wandering in the spiritual desert for thirty-eight years. God gave them another chance. He brought them life in the promised land. He sent His Son. Yet their lack of love for God, their lack of genuine worship, their lack of charity, their refusal to let God's word dwell in their hearts—all of this led them to plot the murder of His Son.

They simply continued to sin. They pursued their own will rather than God's. They sought their own righteousness rather than God's. Through persistent sin, they forfeited what was rightfully theirs.

### Application: The Warning to Believers

As Christians, we stand in a vastly different position. Christ has been revealed to us. We have believed in Him. We have been born again and saved.

Yet we must hear the same command Jesus issued to the healed man: *Sin no more.*

> "For it is impossible to restore again to repentance those who have once been enlightened... if they then fall away." (Hebrews 6:4-6)

We must not jeopardize the salvation so painfully purchased for us by Christ's blood. The warning is grave.

What decisions are you making that could compromise your honoring of Jesus? How you treat others? Involvement with pornography? Spending extended time with influences that draw you away from Christ? The temptation to date a non-believer, knowing that path leads to spiritual danger? The impulse to postpone serious Christianity while pursuing academic achievement or career advancement?

You may be an exception, but few survive putting Jesus on hold. I know of young people who went away for a summer holiday in Hong Kong or Malaysia and never fully returned to their faith. One young woman traveled to Europe for a year and never came back to church. Another took a year off church to study for his HSC and never returned—it's been four years now. He's abandoned his faith.

If you have tasted the goodness of a genuine relationship with Jesus Christ, do not casually throw it away for career advancement or worldly exploration. Do not expect a third chance.

Chapter 5's conflict centers on the Sabbath. Perhaps a helpful reflection is to consider: What are you trying to achieve? How does it fit in light of the eternal rest to come? What are you doing with your second chance at life? Are you keeping the Sabbath in its true sense—resting your anxious labor and trusting God—or is your life consumed by the here and now?

In heaven, unlimited opportunity awaits. You can travel to places we can't yet imagine. If you want to pursue academic excellence, heaven will offer infinitely greater learning. What are you doing now that could compromise your honoring of Jesus and cause you to forfeit salvation?

I must be careful not to create legalism—a new set of rules about what's permissible. "How long is too long a holiday? Two weeks? Three? Six months? Does it depend on the destination?" That would be returning to the Jewish pattern of endless regulations.

We live under the grace our Lord purchased through His own blood. This is the Jesus who has been given authority over life and judgment, who has brought you back from spiritual death to life. He is your master, and all of us must give account to Him on the final day. He has granted you salvation, all wisdom, and His word to guide you. Therefore, honor Him. Sin no more.

If you're struggling with these issues and fear you're making decisions that could compromise your honoring of Jesus, please speak with your leaders, staff, or a Christian you respect. We will not judge you—that belongs to Jesus alone—but we want to help. We are all fallen creatures who have received God's mercy, and He has given us to one another that we might encourage each other to persevere.

## Conclusion: Willing for Life

Miracles are signs. They point to Christ. They are never meant primarily to impress, for miracles alone never produce lasting belief.

For the Jews, the problem was never whether miracles could happen. They knew miracles happened. They saw the healings. They remembered their own miraculous salvation from Egypt. Their skepticism was never about the miraculous itself.

The problem was always this: willingness. Were they willing to accept that these signs pointed to Jesus? Were they willing to honor Him? It was a matter of the heart, not the intellect. They simply *would not* accept Jesus.

So the question comes to each of us: Do you will?

If yes, then: Sin no more.
