# Matthew 17:1-13

**Title:** Discipleship — Listen to the Son

**Date:** 15 February 2010

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## The Glorious Son

After six days, Jesus took Peter, James, and John up a high mountain by themselves. There, Jesus was transfigured before them—his face shone like the sun, and his clothes became as white as light. Suddenly, Moses and Elijah appeared, talking with Jesus.

This strange and wonderful moment echoes an earlier scene in Scripture. Looking at Exodus 24, we find remarkable parallels: Moses also went up a mountain after six days, took three companions with him, experienced a cloud covering the mountain, and heard the voice of God. Jesus is deliberately reenacting this Old Testament scene, but with significant upgrades.

**What makes Jesus's transfiguration more glorious?**

His brilliance exceeds Moses's. When Moses came down from Mount Sinai, his face shone—but Jesus's entire being radiates with the glory of the sun itself. His clothes glow white with light. The glory is so complete it overflows from his person.

More importantly, the voice from heaven declares something Moses never received: "This is my Son, whom I love; with him I am well pleased." Jesus is not merely a prophet or lawgiver. He is God's own Son.

When Peter sees this, he instinctively wants to build three shelters or tabernacles—one for Jesus, one for Moses, one for Elijah. He's echoing the Old Testament pattern where God's presence dwelt in the tabernacle. But his impulse is gently corrected. There is no need to build structures to house God's presence.

> John 1:14 reads: "The Word became flesh and made his dwelling among us. We have seen his glory, the glory of the One and Only, who came from the Father, full of grace and truth."

Jesus himself is the tabernacle. He is God dwelling with us.

## Listen to the Son

As the disciples stand terrified by this overwhelming display of divine power, Jesus does something Moses never did. He touches them and says, "Get up. Don't be afraid."

In Exodus, when the Israelites heard God's voice, they trembled in fear. When Aaron and the elders saw Moses's radiant face, they were afraid to come near him. The law brings terror; the presence of God's holiness is fearful.

But Jesus brings comfort. He is an authority, yes—but a loving authority. His command to listen comes wrapped in reassurance.

> Matthew 17:5 says: "While he was still speaking, a bright cloud covered them, and a voice from the cloud said, 'This is my Son, whom I love; with him I am well pleased. Listen to him!'"

This echoes Exodus 23:20-22, where God promises to send an angel (messenger/prophet) to guard Israel and bring them to the promised land. "Pay attention to him and listen to what he says," God instructs. That angel is Jesus. He is the one whose words lead us to our true promised land.

Yet Jesus comes bringing grace, not condemnation. The law was given through Moses; grace and truth came through Jesus Christ. He has not come to add new laws that condemn us. That's already been done. Instead, he comes with words of rescue.

Jesus fulfills the law's requirements in our place. As the perfect representative of humanity, he takes upon himself the world's penalty and judgment. On the cross, he was condemned by the law—not us. This is the gospel: we are freed from the law's curse because he bore it for us.

## When They Looked Up

> Matthew 17:8 says: "When they looked up, they saw no one except Jesus."

Moses and Elijah are gone. Only Jesus remains.

This is the critical move: Jesus alone has the words of eternal life. He is the sole authority we must heed. Not the voices of culture that oppose him. Not the voices of ideology that claim his teachings are outdated. Not the voices of those who doubt Scripture's reliability.

Listen to Jesus. To him alone.

## Listening in a Confused World

We live in a cloud of confusion, bombarded by competing voices claiming authority over our lives and beliefs:

- **Cultural voices** tell us what to think about identity, morality, and truth. They dismiss biblical teaching as "homophobic," "racist," "sexist"—without argument, only assertion.

- **Scientific voices** sometimes claim to contradict Scripture, as if science and faith are inherently opposed.

- **Friends and loved ones** who don't follow Jesus may tempt us to abandon our faith for their sake.

When we encounter strange or difficult passages in Scripture—and the Bible contains many—we face three temptations:

**Appeal to Ignorance:** We skip hard passages, mumble that they're "too complicated," and move on. *The Bible is true, but I'm too lazy to understand it.*

**Appeal to Culture:** We dismiss biblical teaching as culturally bound. *The Bible was true for that age, but not for ours.* We use this excuse for passages on sexuality, justice, and countless other matters.

**Appeal to Exceptionalism:** *The Bible is true for everyone else, but not for me.* I'm too young. Too old. Too busy. Too gifted to apply this. We qualify the Bible until it means nothing.

The antidote is a posture of humility. When we encounter something in Scripture that seems strange, the solution is not dismissal but deeper learning. We must ask: What is God trying to say? What spiritual principle lies beneath these words?

Consider a difficult passage: "If your hand causes you to sin, cut it off and throw it away" (Matthew 18:8). Most of us shouldn't take this literally. But we shouldn't dismiss it either. Let the force of Jesus's words affect us. What would it mean to cut off my hand? It would hurt. I would grieve the loss. I would be disabled. 

The point is this: God hates sin so much that we should be willing to make radical sacrifices to fight it. Maybe you won't cut off your arm, but perhaps you'll cancel that internet subscription, stop stealing with your hands, or refuse to use them to harm others. You won't gouge out your eye, but you might turn off the program that tempts you or put away the material that enslaves you. None of this happens if you've simply dismissed the text.

## The Message of Grace

Jesus comes not with condemnation but with grace. Yet his grace is not cheap. He calls us to:

> Matthew 16:24-25: "Whoever wants to be my disciple must deny themselves and take up their cross and follow me. For whoever wants to save their life will lose it, but whoever loses their life for me will find it."

This is the call. This is the cost. We are invited into transformation—a true "morphing" that comes only through submission to Jesus's lordship.

He knows us completely. He knows what we have done. He knows our resistance, our fears, our half-hearted commitments. And still, he extends the invitation: Take up your cross. Follow me. Die to yourself so that you might truly live.

## Application

**What is the most challenging thing you've heard from Scripture lately?** What biblical truth makes you uncomfortable? What is God asking you to do that you know is right, yet you're hesitant to obey?

Don't dismiss it. Don't rationalize it away. Don't claim it's culturally irrelevant or meant for someone else.

Instead, sit under it. Let it challenge you. Ask what God is trying to communicate. Then—in grace, because you follow a loving Master who died for you—begin to obey.

It's morphin' time. Christ has died for the penalty of our sin. Christ has risen to give us new life. So transform. Become who you're called to be.

Will you take the call—not from Zordon, but from God? And will you be transformed?
