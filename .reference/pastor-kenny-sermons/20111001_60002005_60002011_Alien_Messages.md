## 1. Christians Are Metaphorical Priests

> But you are a chosen people, a royal priesthood, a holy nation, a people belonging to God. (1 Peter 2:9)

Peter draws on Old Testament imagery to show that Christians today are living stones in a metaphorical temple, continuing the identity of a holy priesthood. To understand what this means, we must look at how priesthood functioned in the Old Testament.

### The Levites in the Old Testament

The Levite tribe was set apart from the other eleven tribes of Israel to serve as priests. This separation reveals what priesthood meant:

**They were set apart morally and spiritually.** The Levites received no land inheritance like other Israelites—their inheritance was the Lord. They were dedicated to God in place of the firstborn of Israel (Numbers 8:15-18), making them a distinct people wholly consecrated to God's service. The book of Leviticus details how they were to maintain this separation.

**They cared for the temple and its sacred duties.** In Moses' time, the Levites transported the tabernacle. Later, they exclusively carried the Ark of the Covenant and eventually maintained the Jerusalem temple itself. Their daily work included standing "every morning to thank and praise the LORD" and performing all the sacred functions of worship (1 Chronicles 23:28-31).

**They served as judges and counselors to the nation.** In Israel's early years without a king, the Levites functioned as a central authority. People brought difficult legal cases to them, and they used the Urim and Thummim to seek God's guidance (Deuteronomy 17:8-9). They also collected tithes and administered justice.

**They proclaimed God's word.** Moses gave the law to the Levites and elders (Deuteronomy 31:9). The blessing of Moses over Levi reveals their central calling: they "watched over your word and guarded your covenant" and taught God's precepts throughout Israel (Deuteronomy 33:8-10). As Malachi reminds us, "the lips of a priest ought to preserve knowledge, and from his mouth men should seek instruction—because he is the messenger of the LORD Almighty" (Malachi 2:7).

### Israel as a Priestly Nation

God's intention, however, extended beyond the Levites alone. He desired that the entire nation of Israel would function as priests to the world:

> If you obey me fully and keep my covenant, then out of all nations you will be my treasured possession. You will be for me a kingdom of priests and a holy nation. (Exodus 19:5-6)

Just as the Levites were set apart by their morality and righteousness, so too the whole nation was to be distinguished by their ethical behavior. Through Israel, God intended to draw the nations to Himself. Isaiah captured this vision of eschatological ingathering:

> In the last days the mountain of the LORD's temple will be established as chief among the mountains; it will be raised above the hills, and all nations will stream to it. Many peoples will come and say, "Come, let us go up to the mountain of the LORD, to the house of the God of Jacob. He will teach us his ways, so that we may walk in his paths." The law will go out from Zion, the word of the LORD from Jerusalem. (Isaiah 2:2-3)

Yet the Old Testament priests failed this calling. Rather than declaring God's praises, they became silent, pursuing idolatry and self-indulgence. They transformed the temple into a den of robbers instead of a beacon to the nations.

### Jesus: The Greater Priest

Jesus came as the literal fulfillment of the priesthood. He is "a priest forever, in the order of Melchizedek" (Hebrews 7), superior to the entire Levitical system. Unlike the Levites, who traced their authority through human descent, Jesus' priesthood rests on His eternal nature and divine character. He alone perfectly embodied the holiness, dedication, and proclamation of God's word that the priesthood was meant to represent.

### Christians: The New Priestly People

Peter now applies this priestly language metaphorically to us. Just as the Levites had no land but possessed a vital task, Christians today are aliens in this world—we have no permanent dwelling place, yet we carry a mission. We are the new priesthood continuing the work Jesus began.

**We are set apart morally and ethically.** Peter urges us: "Dear friends, I urge you, as aliens and strangers in the world, to abstain from sinful desires, which war against your soul" (1 Peter 2:11). Our separation from the world is never about external rules or ceremonial observance—Jesus fulfilled and transcended the food laws and festivals. Rather, our holiness is internal: "What comes out of a person is what defiles them. For it is from within, out of their hearts, that evil thoughts come" (Mark 7:20-21). Only Jesus can cleanse our hearts.

**We care for one another as living stones.** Just as the Levites maintained the physical temple, we build up the spiritual temple—the church—through mutual encouragement and support.

**We govern through our witness and influence.** Though we don't hold earthly political authority, our lives and words shape the moral and spiritual landscape around us.

**We proclaim God's word and declare His praises.** This is our central task and the focus of verses 9-11.

## 2. Christians Declare the Praises of God

> You are a chosen people, a royal priesthood, a holy nation, a people belonging to God, that you may declare the praises of him who called you out of darkness into his wonderful light. (1 Peter 2:9)

### Understanding Praise

There is much confusion about what "praise" means in contemporary Christianity. We often equate it with singing—we think of praise songs, worship music, or the atmosphere created when we close our eyes and raise our hands during a church service. But this misses the biblical meaning entirely.

The Hebrew word *tehilim* (translated "praise") simply means **to advertise**. It means to tell others about the excellent qualities and mighty deeds of someone. When we praise God, we are advertising who He is and what He has done.

Consider how praise actually functions in the Psalms. Psalm 96 illustrates this perfectly:

> Sing to the LORD a new song; sing to the LORD, all the earth. Sing to the LORD, praise his name; proclaim his salvation day after day. Declare his glory among the nations, his marvellous deeds among all peoples. For great is the LORD and most worthy of praise; he is to be feared above all gods. (Psalm 96:1-3, 4)

Notice the pattern: the command to praise is always followed by the content of that praise—declarations of God's greatness, power, and works. Praise without content is empty.

### The Content of Praise

What should we advertise about God? Peter provides the answer in verse 10:

> Once you were not a people, but now you are the people of God; once you had not received mercy, but now you have received mercy.

This echoes Hosea's prophecy: a people who were not God's people have now become His people. Those who received no mercy now receive abundant mercy. The content of our praise is **God's transformative grace in our own lives and in the lives of those around us**.

Praise is a **confession and testimony**. It's a declaration of what God has done—how He has called us from darkness into His wonderful light, how He has shown us mercy, how He has changed us. True praise announces these works to others, not through flattery directed at God, but through honest testimony about God's character and action.

### Praise as Public Witness

Praise is fundamentally a public activity. While it's possible to express gratitude to God in private, biblical praise is about telling a third party. Think of it this way: if someone compliments you directly, it might be mere flattery. But when you hear others speaking well of you in public, when they're advertising your good qualities to others—that's what truly brings joy and honor.

Similarly, we praise God not by saying "we praise you, God" in His presence, but by **telling others about God's wonderful works in history and in our lives**.

Declaring praise to our brothers and sisters in the church means we:

- Share how God is changing us and working in our lives
- Testify about anger we've overcome, malice we've laid down
- Tell of events that helped us grow
- Share how sermons or conversations have challenged us
- Speak of the witness and example of Christian friends
- Recount our conversion and reflect on how we've been transformed

This mutual exhortation "day after day" (Hebrews 3:13, 10:24-25) builds us up. We are a people prone to doubt, constantly needing to hear again that the gospel is true and that it works in real human lives.

### Praise Beyond the Church Walls

But we don't limit our advertising to fellow believers. The Christian message is radical: **Done, not Do.**

The religions of the world proclaim Do: do these works, observe these laws, eat or don't eat these foods, perform these rituals—all to earn God's approval and a clean conscience. Christianity alone announces **Done**: Jesus has completed the work. God's love is unconditional. We need not earn what He freely gives. Everything we do now flows from grateful love, not from desperation to appease a distant God.

This message of hope and salvation must reach everyone, especially non-Christians. We are to advertise God's praises to the whole world. Yet the Jewish people, despite being chosen for this priestly role, became introverted. They developed their own religious club with idiosyncratic rules, failing to declare God's greatness to the nations.

Jesus Himself embodied this outward-focused mission:

> Accept one another, then, just as Christ accepted you, in order to bring praise to God. For I tell you that Christ has become a servant of the Jews on behalf of God's truth, to confirm the promises made to the patriarchs so that the Gentiles may glorify God for his mercy... Therefore I will praise you among the Gentiles; I will sing hymns to your name. (Romans 15:7-9)

### A Practical Call

As we close the year, ask yourself: How many non-Christian friends have you attempted to tell about Jesus? How many have you invited to church? How many have you prayed for intentionally?

If you truly care about praising the Lord, you must care about telling those still in darkness. Age doesn't matter—whether you're in year seven, university, working, or raising a family—you have the same calling.

**Do not delay with excessive pre-evangelism.** Many of us spend years building a relationship before mentioning Jesus, thinking "When we know each other better, then I'll tell them." But after a year of silence about something so central to our identity, mentioning Jesus would indeed be strange. We don't wait a year before mentioning we're married. We bring it up naturally and much more quickly. The same applies to our faith.

Build genuine, natural relationships, and let your Christian identity emerge naturally. When someone asks "Tell me about yourself," your commitment to Christ should arise early in the conversation. Look for open doors: people asking genuine questions about meaning, purpose, morality. Be ready.

Every day around you presents an opportunity to help someone move from darkness into God's wonderful light.

## 3. Declaring Praise Despite Opposition

> To this you were called, because Christ suffered for you, leaving you an example, that you should follow in his steps. (1 Peter 2:21)

What happens when the government opposes Christianity? What if your employer forbids your faith? What if those around you are hostile to the gospel? Can we still declare God's praises?

The answer is yes—and Peter addresses this in verses 12-25, showing us how submission and witness work together even in the face of persecution. This is the subject for the weeks ahead.

## Summary

Christians are aliens in this world, carrying an alien message—the good news that Jesus has transformed us from darkness into light. Like the priests of old, we are set apart not by ceremonial dress or tribal identity, but by our moral transformation and our dedication to proclaiming God's word.

Our calling is to advertise God's praises: to testify to His mercy, His power, and His transforming grace. We do this in the church through mutual encouragement, and we do this in the world through bold witness and invitation.

If you are an alien with this message, speak up. If you are not yet a Christian, listen. The message of hope and salvation originates from heaven itself and is now carried by those of us who have experienced it.
