# 1 Corinthians 12:1-7

**Title:** So You Think You Can Be Spiritual? True Spirituality

**Date:** 15 November 2010

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## What Is True Spirituality?

The Corinthian church wrestled with this question deeply. Corinth was a wealthy, multicultural harbor city—a crossroads between northern and southern Greece with access to both the Mediterranean and Asia. With prosperity came all the trappings of pagan culture: sexual immorality, idolatry, greed, and hedonism. Yet Paul had planted a church there.

But this church struggled badly. Paul himself told them: "Brothers, I could not address you as spiritual but as worldly—mere infants in Christ" (1 Corinthians 3:1). The Corinthians reflected their city too closely. Their problems were staggering: sexual immorality of every kind, divisions and lawsuits against one another, idolatry, selfishness, greed, and—most shamefully—people going hungry while others feasted. Some had swung to the opposite extreme, teaching strict asceticism: the body and all physical pleasure were evil; only poverty was truly spiritual.

Confused about what spirituality actually meant, the Corinthians wrote to Paul asking for clarity. His response addresses not "spiritual gifts" but something more fundamental: the spirituals—spiritual truths themselves.

## 1. Concerning the Spirituals (verse 1)

The Greek text simply says "the spirituals," not "spiritual gifts." This is crucial. English translations add the word "gifts," but the original is more elusive. *Spiritual what?* Truths? People? Blessings? We must determine the meaning from context.

### The Case Against "Spiritual Gifts"

It's tempting to read "spirituals" as spiritual gifts. After all, the Corinthians clearly had issues with how gifts were used. But several problems emerge:

- The phrase "spiritual gifts" (plural) does not exist in the New Testament
- "Spiritual gift" (singular) appears only once: Romans 1:11, where Paul wants to impart a "spiritual gift" to Rome—later revealed to be the gospel itself, not some special talent
- Throughout 1 Corinthians, when gifts are discussed, the Spirit is often absent from the conversation
- Chapter 2 discusses the Spirit's work with no mention of gifts
- Chapter 7 describes various gifts of life (marriage, singleness) without mentioning the Spirit
- Chapters 12:27-30 discuss gifts extensively but say nothing of the Spirit

The most telling evidence is this contradiction: Paul says the Corinthians "do not lack any spiritual gift" (1:7), yet he tells them "I could not address you as spiritual" (3:1). If spirituals meant gifts, Paul would be contradicting himself. This suggests the connection between spirituals and gifts is far thinner than popular teaching suggests.

### The Case for "Spiritual Truths"

A better interpretation: *spirituals refer to spiritual truths*—specifically, the communication of spiritual truth to one another.

Consider what "spirit" actually means. The Greek word *pneuma* literally means wind or breath (as in pneumonia, pneumatic tires). The Hebrew *ruach* carries the same meaning. Throughout Scripture, God's Spirit is God's breath—His creative, life-giving power. When God speaks, He breathes out truth.

To understand what "spiritual" means, look at 1 Corinthians 2, where Paul contrasts the spirit of the world with the Spirit of God:

> What the Spirit is NOT: eloquence, superior wisdom, wise and persuasive words, human wisdom, worldly wisdom
>
> What the Spirit IS: the message of Jesus Christ and Him crucified, God's power, God's hidden wisdom now revealed, understanding what God has freely given us, words taught by the Spirit, the mind of Christ

By contrast, spiritual truth is the opposite of worldly wisdom. It is the gospel itself—the good news of Christ's death and resurrection. The breath of God is the words of God.

This interpretation fits perfectly with Paul's statement: "If we have sown spiritual seed among you, is it too much if we reap a material harvest from you?" (9:11). Speaking spiritual truth. Communicating spiritual truths to one another.

When the Corinthians argued over "who should speak?" and "in what order should we speak the words of God?" they were wrestling with how to communicate spiritual truth effectively. That's the real issue Paul addresses in chapter 14.

## 2. Don't Be Ignorant of Spiritual Truths (verse 1-2)

Paul's first command is stark: "I do not want you to be ignorant." True Christian spirituality is not ignorance. It is not:

- Empty meditation or mindless feeling
- Stargazing, tarot cards, or psychic predictions
- Emotionalism disguised as faith
- Anti-intellectualism
- Miraculous signs divorced from character

Rather, Christian spirituality is *knowledge*—knowledge that transforms. Paul says Christ was crucified for us; this truth must be known, understood, and lived.

Verse 2 reinforces this: "You know that when you were pagans, somehow or other you were influenced and led astray to mute idols." As pagans, they followed dumb idols—pieces of wood or silver that couldn't speak. But the living God speaks. He reveals truth. He breathes His word.

**For those on holiday break:** Don't plan your summer around parties and festivities. Plan it around what you'll read, which books you'll study, what your church is teaching, what Bible conferences are offered. Know. Learn. Don't postpone spiritual growth until the holidays—the holidays are now.

## 3. The Spiritual Confesses the Lordship of Christ (verse 3)

If God speaks spiritual truths, His people do too. Verse 3 gives us the sign of true spirituality:

> No one who is speaking by the Spirit of God says, "Jesus be cursed," and no one can say, "Jesus is Lord," except by the Holy Spirit.

This is not a formula to chant. It is a wholehearted commitment to the gospel. The sign of authentic Christianity is submission to Jesus as Lord.

The gospel is good news: Christ died for us and rose to reign as Lord of the universe. If you don't believe Jesus is Lord over your life, you do not have the Spirit. You are not a Christian if you refuse His lordship.

This doesn't mean Christians live perfectly. It means you *want* to follow. You aim toward obedience, even when you stumble. Piety and emotionalism don't compensate for refusing His rule.

True lordship looks like consistency: the same person on Sunday and Monday, the same integrity on Saturday night and Sunday morning. The person living a double life—one way at church, another way in the world—hasn't surrendered to Christ's lordship.

**Consider this:** If you believe the gospel, if you've truly committed your life to trusting that Jesus died for you, then the Spirit lives in you. You are spiritual. You have spiritual truth.

Many Christians have forgotten the greatest gift of all: eternal life itself. They chase emotional experiences or miraculous signs, thinking these prove spirituality. But all believers share the same Spirit, whether emotional or reserved. We are bonded by the same Lord, the same God, the same Spirit, the same spiritual truths.

### A Word on False Teaching

Years ago, some taught "second baptism" heresy: your first baptism made you a Christian; your second baptism of the Spirit came later, empowering you to speak boldly and serve confidently. You weren't truly spiritual until you received it.

This is false. The Spirit came to you when you first trusted Christ. There is no second baptism. When you wake up and decide to get serious about following Jesus, that's not the Spirit arriving—that's you finally responding to the Spirit already within you.

## 4. The Ultimate Spiritual Truth and Power of God Is the Gospel (verses 4-6)

Paul's final emphasis: the gospel is the ultimate spiritual truth. All believers share the same Spirit because all have received the same gospel.

> There are different kinds of gifts, but the same Spirit. There are different kinds of service, but the same Lord. There are different kinds of working, but the same God works all of them in all men.

Some say certain Christians only believe in the Father, Son, and Holy Bible—as if lacking emotional fervor means lacking the Spirit. This misses the point entirely. The Holy Spirit is the breath of God, and God's breath is His word. Is the Word of God not the sword of the Spirit?

Yes, there is a proper place for emotions. But emotionalism does not define spirituality. You are not spiritual because you cry in church or speak with passion. You are spiritual because you've been saved by the gospel of Christ.

This truth unites all believers. Whether you're reserved or exuberant, whether you speak in public or serve quietly, you share the same Spirit through the same gospel. This is the unity of the Spirit—bonded by being common recipients of eternal life.

## True Spirituality

True spirituality happens when believers gather to speak spiritual truth to one another. You'll find it wherever the Bible is read, the gospel proclaimed, and believers remind each other: "Jesus is Lord. You are His servant."

It looks like Christians bonded by the spiritual truths that saved us, encouraging one another about how the lordship of Christ shapes our lives. It sounds like believers asking each other, "How did God help you through that struggle?" and "How has Christ challenged you lately?"

There's nothing spooky about it. There's nothing that requires tears or ecstatic experiences. There's simply the power of knowing God, speaking His truth, and submitting to Christ's lordship together.

**So you think you can be spiritual? Go for it.** Know Scripture. Confess Christ as Lord. Speak spiritual truth. Love one another. That's as spiritual as it gets.
