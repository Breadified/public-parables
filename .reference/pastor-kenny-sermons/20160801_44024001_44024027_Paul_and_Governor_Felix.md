# Acts 24:1-27

**Title:** Paul and Governor Felix

**Date:** 21 August 2016

**Speaker:** Kenneth Chan

---

## Big Question

How do Christians respond in the face of persecution?

## Big Answer

In the face of persecution, we:
- Speak the truth
- Live in line with Scripture
- Keep a clear conscience before God and men

---

## Background & Context

Before we examine the passage, it's helpful to understand the historical setting and the key figures involved.

### Governor Felix

Paul was being sent to His Excellency Governor Felix of Judea for trial. Felix had a fascinating background: he was once a slave to Antonia, the mother of Emperor Claudius. His brother Pallas served as secretary of the treasury to the Emperor, and through Pallas's petition, Felix became governor of Judaea around AD 52.

### Drusilla: A Complicated Story

We meet Felix's wife Drusilla in Acts 24:24. She was born in AD 38 and was the daughter of Herod Agrippa I, who killed the apostle James (Acts 12:1-23). Drusilla was the granddaughter of Herod the Great—the same king who murdered the infant boys during Jesus' birth in Matthew 2 and Luke 1.

According to Roman historian Josephus, Drusilla was promised in marriage as a young girl to Epiphanes, the son of Antiochus, King of Commagene, on the condition that he convert to Judaism. He refused, so the marriage never happened.

After her father's death in AD 44, her brother Herod Agrippa II arranged her marriage to Azizus, King of Emesa, who consented to be circumcised. But this marriage did not last long.

When Felix met Drusilla at one of her brother's estates, he became captivated by her beauty. Being a pagan, he knew she was a devout Jew, so he sent a Jewish friend to persuade her to break her Jewish laws and marry him. She was only sixteen years old at the time. According to Josephus, Drusilla agreed partly because she longed to escape the jealousy and mistreatment from her sister Bernice, who had an incestuous relationship with their brother Herod Agrippa II.

### Felix's Character

According to the Roman historian Tacitus, Felix indulged in "every kind of barbarity and lust" (Histories 5.9). He was known for brutality, corruption, and the suppression of all opposition. We see this reflected in Acts 24:26-27:

> At the same time he was hoping that Paul would offer him a bribe, so he sent for him frequently and talked with him. When two years had passed, Felix was succeeded by Porcius Festus, but because Felix wanted to grant a favor to the Jews, he left Paul in prison.

### The Christian Witness in a Corrupt World

The lesson of this passage is clear: the Christian life does not insulate us from the corrupt and degenerate world around us. Instead, we learn from Paul's example how to maintain integrity even under persecution. Paul demonstrates three essential practices that allow Christians to stand firm.

## 1. We Speak the Truth

### The Charges Against Paul

When Paul appeared before Felix, the Jewish leaders presented their case through a lawyer named Tertullus. They brought three serious charges:

1. **He was a troublemaker**: Stirring up riots among Jews throughout the world and disturbing the peace of Rome
2. **He was a heretical leader**: He was the ringleader of the Nazarene sect, an illegal movement not recognized by the Roman government
3. **He was desecrating the temple**: He attempted to profane the holy temple, which could warrant execution by Jewish law

Tertullus opened his address with elaborate flattery, praising Felix's wisdom and reforms. But understanding Felix's true character—his brutality and corruption—his words were pure manipulation. The Jewish leaders were willing to use every tool at their disposal, including a hired lawyer's false compliments, to achieve their goal of punishing Paul.

### Paul's Truthful Defense

Unlike Tertullus and the Jewish elders, Paul gave a calm, straightforward, and truthful response. He refuted each charge methodically:

> When the governor motioned for him to speak, Paul replied: "I know that for a number of years you have been a judge over this nation; so I gladly make my defense. You can easily verify that no more than twelve days ago I went up to Jerusalem to worship. My accusers did not find me arguing with anyone at the temple, or stirring up a crowd in the synagogues or anywhere else in the city. And they cannot prove to you the charges they are now making against me. (Acts 24:10-13)

To the second charge about leading a heretical sect:

> However, I admit that I worship the God of our ancestors as a follower of the Way, which they call a sect. I believe everything that is in accordance with the Law and that is written in the Prophets, and I have the same hope in God as these men themselves have, that there will be a resurrection of both the righteous and the wicked. (Acts 24:14-15)

To the third charge about desecrating the temple:

> After an absence of several years, I came to Jerusalem to bring my people gifts for the poor and to present offerings. I was ceremonially clean when they found me in the temple courts doing this. There was no crowd with me, nor was I involved in any disturbance. (Acts 24:17-18)

Paul even pointed out a flaw in Roman law—that his accusers should have been present to testify (Acts 24:19). This demonstrated both his knowledge of justice and his confidence in speaking truth.

Most importantly, Paul did not compromise on the core truth that had gotten him arrested:

> Unless it was this one thing I shouted as I stood in their presence: "It is concerning the resurrection of the dead that I am on trial before you today." (Acts 24:21)

The resurrection of the dead was the truth that had sparked Jewish opposition, and Paul would not back down from it, even facing injustice.

### Application: Speaking Truth in Our Age

We are commanded in Scripture to put off falsehood and speak the truth (Ephesians 4:25). The devil is the father of lies (John 8:44), but God is the God of truth who cannot lie (Titus 1:1-2). Jesus declared, "I am the way, the truth, and the life. No one comes to the Father except through me" (John 14:6). As His followers, we must become people who speak the truth, even when we face accusations and opposition.

In recent years, speaking Christian truth has become increasingly costly. When we acknowledge that Jesus is the only way to the Father, we face accusations of intolerance. When we speak about biblical morality, we're labeled as hateful or bigoted. Yet Paul's example shows us that integrity begins with truthfulness—not harsh or judgmental truthfulness, but clear, factual, humble truthfulness about what God has revealed.

How often do we shy away from explaining to people that there is only one way to God, because Jesus is the only way? We must ask ourselves: Are we willing to speak the truth when it matters?

## 2. We Live in Line with Scripture

Paul asserted his faithfulness to God's Word when he told Felix:

> I believe everything that is in accordance with the Law and that is written in the Prophets (Acts 24:14)

### More Than Belief: Living It Out

It's one thing to say you believe in God's Word. It's quite another to actually live it out. Paul walked the talk. We see this evident in his actions: he made himself ceremonially clean for the temple, he brought gifts and offerings to Jerusalem (Acts 24:17-18), and he lived with the kind of integrity that characterized all his teachings in the New Testament.

Paul wrote in 2 Corinthians 1:12:

> For our proud confidence is this: the testimony of our conscience, that in holiness and godly sincerity, not in fleshly wisdom but in the grace of God, we have conducted ourselves in the world, and especially toward you.

And in Titus 1:11-12:

> For the grace of God appeared, bringing salvation to all men; instructing them to deny ungodliness and worldly desires and to live sensibly, righteously and godly in the present age.

Paul identified himself as a follower of "the Way"—not the Nazarene sect (the derogatory term used by non-Christian Jews). "The Way" was how early Christians identified themselves as followers of Jesus Christ. Paul served the God of the Old Testament according to the way of Jesus and His followers.

### The Hypocrisy Problem

In conversations with non-Christian coworkers, there's one consistent complaint about Christianity: Christians who claim to follow God's way don't seem to live it out. They call them hypocrites. The objection isn't usually to Christian teaching itself, but to the gap between what Christians profess and how they actually live.

Non-believers observe when we say we're loving and friendly but then ignore the newcomer at church. They see when we claim to love our wives and children but pursue adultery. They notice when we preach morality to our friends but speed, download illegal music, or cut corners on what we claim to believe. They watch when we say we're generous but become selfish when stress hits.

In 2 Timothy 3:16-17, Paul reminds us:

> All Scripture is God-breathed and is useful for teaching, rebuking, correcting and training in righteousness, so that the servant of God may be thoroughly equipped for every good work.

God has given us everything we need to live as His servants. He has given us His Word so that we can live in obedience to Him. But do we truly believe everything in it to the point where we actually live it out?

## 3. We Keep a Clear Conscience Before God and Men

### Paul's Commitment to Conscience

In light of his hope in God and the judgment to come, Paul made an explicit commitment:

> I have the same hope in God as these men themselves have, that there will be a resurrection of both the righteous and the wicked. So I strive always to keep my conscience clear before God and man. (Acts 24:15-16)

The Greek word for "strive" is *askeo*, originally used for training the body in athletic pursuits and skills. It was easily adapted to describe intellectual and moral training. Paul wasn't claiming to be sinless. He was saying that he worked hard, disciplined himself, and continuously practiced obedience to God.

What's particularly striking is that Paul cared about having a clear conscience before *men* as well as God. He understood that his responsibility to live God's way wasn't just a private matter between him and the Almighty—it was also about how he related to others and what testimony his life gave to those around him.

This is reinforced in 2 Corinthians 8:21:

> For we aim at what is honorable not only in the Lord's sight but also in the sight of man.

### The Contrast with Felix

Paul's commitment to maintaining a clear conscience before God and men stands in stark contrast to Governor Felix, who lived in a culture of deceit and corruption, pursuing only what was good for his own interests.

Yet Paul recognized that even Felix needed to hear the gospel. In Acts 24:24, we see that Felix sent for Paul frequently and listened to him speak about faith in Christ Jesus. Paul didn't just talk about faith—he also spoke to Felix about righteousness, self-control, and the judgment to come.

### Application: Standing Firm in an Age of Relativism

In our contemporary culture, moral values are often viewed as relative and subjective. Sin is reframed as alternative lifestyles, psychological dysfunctions, addictions, or disease. In this environment, the Christian witness must find ways to speak of God's righteousness with clarity and conviction. We must speak meaningfully about responsible moral self-control and personal accountability before God.

Even though Paul was wrongly accused, even though he faced injustice before a corrupt governor, he stood firm. He spoke the truth, lived in line with Scripture, maintained a clear conscience before God and men, and continued to share the gospel with those who seemed far from faith.

How often do we write off friends, colleagues, or acquaintances as too far gone in their sinful ways, deciding it's pointless to share the gospel with them? Paul's faithfulness to Felix—meeting with him repeatedly, speaking to him about righteousness and judgment—challenges us to maintain hope and continue witnessing even to those who seem most unlikely to respond.

---

## Conclusion

When Kenny was detained at customs in Macau wearing his warm navy jacket, he had a clear conscience and simply spoke the truth when questioned. A week later in Hong Kong, an unshaven appearance led to another police encounter—and again, he spoke truthfully with a nervous but clear conscience and was released.

In an age when society ridicules, mocks, and even attempts to silence Christians from speaking Christian values, it becomes even more essential for us to:

- **Speak the truth** with clarity and conviction
- **Live in line with Scripture** by walking the talk, bridging the gap between profession and practice
- **Strive to keep a clear conscience** before both God and men, understanding that our integrity is a witness to the world

This is how Christians respond in the face of persecution. Not with fear, not with compromise, not with silence—but with truth, obedience, and conscience.
