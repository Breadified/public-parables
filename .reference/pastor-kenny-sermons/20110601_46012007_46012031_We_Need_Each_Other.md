# 1 Corinthians 12:7-31

**Title:** We Need Each Other  
**Date:** 4 June 2011  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## The Question: Who Is Most Important?

When we look at a movie, we see the star—emphasized through bigger names, front billing, special treatment. But in a room full of people with different talents and gifts, who is most important? The speaker? The musician? The one who gives the most money? The oldest member? The newest? The most talented?

This question sits at the heart of Paul's teaching to the Corinthians about spiritual gifts and what it means to be the body of Christ.

---

## 1. We Are One

### One Gospel (v.7)

Paul begins by reminding us of our fundamental unity:

> Now to each one the manifestation of the Spirit is given for the common good. (1 Corinthians 12:7)

The word "manifestation" means disclosure or announcement. In context, Paul is saying that all Christians have received the same gospel—the same essential message, though it may come to us in different ways. That message is: **Jesus is Lord.**

> All these are the work of one and the same Spirit, and he gives them to each one, just as he determines. (1 Corinthians 12:11)

The same Spirit divides these gifts among us, much like an inheritance is divided among heirs. We're united by one gospel delivered in different ways.

### Different Speaking Gifts (v.8-10)

Yet within this unity, Paul acknowledges remarkable variety:

> To one there is given through the Spirit the message of wisdom, to another the message of knowledge by means of the same Spirit, to another faith by the same Spirit, to another gifts of healing by that one Spirit, to another miraculous powers, to another prophecy, to another distinguishing between spirits, to another speaking in different kinds of tongues, and to still another the interpretation of tongues. (1 Corinthians 12:8-10)

**The Mundane Gifts:** Message of wisdom, message of knowledge, prophecy—these are spiritual truths spoken in normal ways.

**The Miraculous Gifts:** Faith, powers, distinguishing between spirits, tongues, interpretation—these raise questions about their nature and continued operation today.

Interpreting these gifts depends largely on how we approach the miraculous:

- **Full Continuists** believe miracles continue as they did in Acts. They quote Hebrews: "Jesus is the same, yesterday, today and forever." However, if this were true, where has God's power been for centuries?

- **Cessationists** argue that miracles accompanied the apostolic era when Scripture was being written and the foundation of the gospel was being laid. Since the apostles have died and the canon is closed, these particular gifts no longer function in the same way. Miracles were linked to specific historical and salvation events, not everyday life.

- **Partial Continuists** hold a middle position, often shaped more by personal experience than theology. Those who've experienced the supernatural lean toward continuism; those who haven't lean toward cessationism.

**My Humble Assessment:**

First, we must admit: we don't know. Even the church fathers in 400 AD wrote that they didn't understand this list of gifts. The information is lost to us, so humility is required.

Second, based on context and the nature of "spiritual truths," these are likely speaking gifts. Acts is still unfolding in Corinthians' time (AD 50—still the apostolic era). Later New Testament letters say very little about signs and wonders.

Third, even if miraculous gifts operated then, they were never normative. They were special, linked to specific moments in God's saving history. You may have experienced something supernatural, and personal experience is hard to argue against, but that doesn't make it normative for all Christians everywhere.

Fourth, consider this: Is the power of God found in miraculous signs, or in the gospel itself—the message that changes hearts? Our God is a God of order, rationality, sensibility, and knowledge, not a God of whatever is weird and wonderful.

### All Gifts for the Common Good (v.7)

Whatever these gifts are, they share one purpose:

> For the common good.

Think of the Commonwealth of Australia—one nation where all parts work together. When one region prospers, the benefits spread. When one struggles, others help bear the load. That's the model for the church.

**Application:** We are not united to put on a good show or based on shared history. We are united for the common good—to build each other up and serve one another.

## 2. The Illustration: The Human Body (v.12-20)

### One Body, Many Parts

> The body is a unit, though it is made up of many parts; and though all its parts are many, they form one body. So it is with Christ. (1 Corinthians 12:12)

A human body works as a cooperative unit. Hormones, enzymes, blood, organs, and muscle all function together. Just so, Christ's body—wherever Christians gather—operates as one.

> For we were all baptized by one Spirit into one body—whether Jews or Greeks, slave or free—and we were all given the one Spirit to drink. (1 Corinthians 12:13)

When Paul speaks of baptism here, he's not referring to water baptism (Paul himself said, "Christ did not send me to baptize but to preach the gospel"). He means Spirit baptism—being identified with Christ through the truth of the gospel. When you believed, you were washed and identified with the Lordship of Christ.

This echoes Jesus' words:

> If anyone is thirsty, let him come to me and drink. Whoever believes in me, as the Scripture has said, streams of living water will flow from within him. (John 7:37-38)

The water is no longer external but internal. One repentance, one Spirit, one gospel, one truth unite us. We have a bond greater than anything else—and that bond must be reflected in our common gatherings.

### Yet We Are Many Parts (v.14)

> Now the body is not made up of one part but of many. (1 Corinthians 12:14)

We are one, yes. But we are also vitally different. And we need each other.

## 3. Two Dangerous Attitudes

### "I Do Not Belong!" (v.15-16)

> If the foot should say, "Because I am not a hand, I do not belong to the body," it would not for that reason cease to be part of the body. And if the ear should say, "Because I am not an eye, I do not belong to the body," it would not for that reason cease to be part of the body. (1 Corinthians 12:15-16)

There's an assumption here: a hand is more valuable than a foot. A hand is functional, has opposable thumbs, is multi-purpose, looks nice. A foot bears weight, gets sweaty, doesn't turn heads. Similarly, an eye seems more important than an ear. You admire someone's eyes, but rarely their ears.

It's tempting to feel like you don't belong:

- **Newcomers:** You've just arrived, the setting feels strange, you know no one. *I don't belong.*
- **Non-Christians:** Someone pressured you to come, everyone seems so different. *I don't belong.*
- **The Sick:** You feel out of place, afraid people will judge your illness. *I don't belong.*
- **The Lonely Parent:** Everyone else seems younger or more connected. *I don't belong.*
- **The Outsider by Race or Age:** You stand out in visible ways. *I don't belong.*
- **The Struggling Sinner:** Everyone seems godly; you're secretly battling sin. *I don't belong.*

But Paul's response cuts through this:

> If the whole body were an eye, where would the sense of hearing be? If the whole body were an ear, where would the sense of smell be? But in fact God has arranged the parts in the body, every one of them, just as he wanted them to be. If they were all one part, where would the body be? As it is, there are many parts, but one body. (1 Corinthians 12:17-20)

An all-eye body would be monstrous. No one thinks rationally, "My foot is ugly and stinky—let me chop it off." Each part belongs because God arranged it that way. Even your pinky toe matters for balance; you know this when you stub it and the pain is disproportionate to its size.

The weaker, less visible, unpresentable parts are actually indispensable:

> Those parts of the body that seem to be weaker are indispensable. (1 Corinthians 12:22)

How would we learn to love the stranger without newcomers? How would we grow in compassion without the sick among us? God has given us each other to teach us how to be one body. The person who frustrates you makes you a better person. The young bring energy; the old bring wisdom. Even the non-Christian keeps us honest with tough questions.

**To Those Who Feel They Don't Belong:** You do belong. Turn up to church. Our common life together exists to encourage and be encouraged. When hard times hit, the first thing to disappear is fellowship—we feel judged, unable to show our face. But don't pull away. We need you. Be vulnerable with us.

### "I Don't Need You!" (v.21-26)

> The eye cannot say to the hand, "I don't need you!" And the head cannot say to the feet, "I don't need you!" (1 Corinthians 12:21)

This is the attitude of the strong and self-sufficient:

- *I can study the Bible better alone than in this small group.*
- *I can go to a better conference than what we offer here.*
- *My friends elsewhere matter more; I don't need to invest here.*
- *I'm doing well spiritually; I don't need church.*

Yes, you might be right. You don't technically need us. But listen:

> The parts that we think are less honorable we treat with special honor. And the parts that are unpresentable are treated with special modesty, while our presentable parts need no special treatment. But God has combined the members of the body and has given greater honor to the parts that lacked it, so that there should be no division in the body, but that its parts should have equal concern for each other. (1 Corinthians 12:23-25)

You are strong for a reason: to help the weaker parts. The unpresentable parts need your strength not to hide them, but to support them.

Many of us pack our Sundays too full—birthday parties, holidays, work, camps, university meetings, weddings, friends' confirmations. We treat our time together like a favor we do for others ("they owe me one"). But the point is this: while you don't need us, we still need you. We can't find another hand to replace you. The ear cannot spontaneously become an eye. The whole essence of a body is its interdependence.

> If one part suffers, every part suffers with it; if one part is honored, every part rejoices with it. (1 Corinthians 12:26)

Think about the difference between a church with committed, consistent members and one where people drift in and out. In the drifting church, you can't challenge each other, can't know how the other person is really doing, can't pray together meaningfully. Relationships don't form in chaos; they require stability and regularity.

**To Those Who Are Strong:** Turn up to help. Or do you only show concern when it fits your schedule?

## Conclusion: Who Is Most Important?

We opened with a question: Who is most important in this room?

Look at movie credits—everyone is listed. The key grip, the gaffer, the best boy—every role has a name because every role matters.

**The answer is this: The person next to you is most important.**

We are all important to each other. We need each other for the proper functioning of this body.

Imagine a congregation where nobody cares about others—self-indulgent, everyone doing what they want, newcomers ignored, no prayers offered, no deep conversations, no shared life. Why would anyone be vulnerable there? Why would anyone risk being known?

Now imagine instead a place where God's word is preached faithfully each week, where we share what we've learned from our Lord, where we confess our troubles and temptations, where we hear each other and offer prayer and help. Where outsiders see how love actually operates.

All of us have a role in this. And this relationship requires commitment and regularity.

This is what it means to be truly spiritual: to need each other and to show that you need each other through your presence, your prayer, your care.
