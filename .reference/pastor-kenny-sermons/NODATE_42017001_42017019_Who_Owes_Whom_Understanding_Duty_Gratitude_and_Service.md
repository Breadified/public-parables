# Luke 17:1-19

**Title:** Who Owes Whom? Understanding Duty, Gratitude, and Service

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## God Does Not Owe Us (verses 7-10)

### The Parable of the Servant

Jesus tells a straightforward parable about a master and his servant. The servant has spent a hard day's work in the field—plowing the soil, turning over the earth to bring fresh nutrients to the surface while burying weeds, or caring for sheep, running about all day feeding them, rescuing them from rivers, walking miles, shearing and milking them, occasionally defending them against wolves and lions.

After such exhausting labor under the Middle Eastern sun—sweat, muscle pain, sore back, blisters, thirst, hunger—the servant naturally expects rest and a meal. He feels entitled to sit down and eat. This seems only reasonable.

> "Suppose one of you has a servant plowing or looking after the sheep. Will he say to the servant when he comes in from the field, 'Come along now and sit down to eat'? Won't he rather say, 'Prepare my supper, get yourself ready and wait on me while I eat and drink; after that you may eat and drink'?" (verses 7-8)

But the master's response is different. The work is not finished—the master has not yet had his dinner. The servant must prepare the meal and continue serving, waiting on the master while he eats. Only then, after all the work is truly complete, may the servant have his own meal.

### No Thanks Are Owed

Furthermore, Jesus makes clear this is not extra work deserving commendation:

> "Will he thank the servant because he did what he was told to do?" (verse 9)

The answer is no. Of course not. The servant is not doing the master a favor—he is doing what he is supposed to do. He has merely fulfilled his obligation. We would not thank a lecturer for submitting an assignment, nor would a boss thank an employee simply for showing up to work. The servant has done what was expected, nothing more.

### Duty: What We Owe

> "So you also, when you have done everything you were told to do, should say, 'We are unworthy servants; we have only done our duty.'" (verse 10)

The Greek word here means "what we owe, under obligation." The servant has merely done his duty—nothing extraordinary, nothing that demands gratitude.

We often misunderstand the word "duty." It sounds forced, coerced, insincere—the opposite of genuine love. But duty is far more profound than that. Duty is not the absence of love; it is love expressed through self-discipline.

When a mother presses forward despite exhaustion to care for her child, she acts from duty rooted in deep love. When parents remain together not from Hollywood romance but from commitment to their family, that is love. When a soldier marches into battle despite fear, giving his life for his country and family, that is love expressed as duty. When Jesus went to the cross—not wanting to die, but following His Father's will and His love for us all the way to Calvary—that was duty rooted in infinite love.

Duty cannot be forced upon us; rather, it comes from a decision we made long ago to do what is right. It is self-discipline planning ahead to overcome our future weakness, temptation, and cowardice. Duty is when we are tempted to run away but we stay and love anyway. It is replacing fear and sadness with joy, trust, and anticipation. It is striving to do what is right, not what feels right, disciplining our bodies and desires to align with God's will.

But where does such duty come from? Not from force—it comes from a deeper place. It comes from thankfulness.

## Our Entitled Culture

Our generation particularly struggles with this. We feel that others owe us something.

**My parents owe me** an education, a car, the latest phone to ignore them at dinner. They should clean up after me and make my bed.

**Australia owes me** welfare (which we've even labeled "entitlements"), a house at the price I want, the highest education, protection from ideas I find offensive, and the lifestyle of the rich and famous.

**And we think God owes us**: a beautiful spouse, health, wealth, a certain dream or ambition. We think God needs us to keep Him company and owes us for our service.

But this inverts the proper order. Who is the master? Who is the servant?

## We Owe God (verses 11-19)

### The Ten Lepers

Jesus illustrates this principle with a powerful story:

> "Now on his way to Jerusalem, Jesus traveled along the border between Samaria and Galilee. As he was going into a village, ten men who had leprosy met him. They stood at a distance and called out in a loud voice, 'Jesus, Master, have pity on us!'" (verses 11-13)

These ten men lived as living death—separated from society, banned to live in the desert away from civilization, unable to see their families, living in poverty and longing. Leprosy, a disease of the nervous system, causes those infected to lose feeling in affected areas, leading to injuries and gangrene without any sensation of pain. Ancient people, believing it highly contagious, feared and avoided lepers completely.

Standing at a distance—socially distanced—they called out desperately: "Jesus, Master, have pity on us!"

> "When he saw them, he said, 'Go, show yourselves to the priests.'" (verse 14)

Jesus shows them pity. To be shown to the priests was not a command to be cleansed but confirmation of healing, the certification needed to be accepted back into community and family.

### The Healed Lepers and Gratitude

Imagine their transformation as they walked toward the priests. Their sores dried up and closed. Collagen restored their skin. Wrinkles disappeared. Feeling returned to their limbs. They were healed—a Hollywood-like transformation.

> "One of them, when he saw he was healed, came back, praising God in a loud voice. He threw himself at Jesus' feet and thanked him—and he was a Samaritan." (verses 15-16)

But there was a problem:

> "Jesus asked, 'Were not all ten cleansed? Where are the other nine? Has no one returned to give praise to God except this foreigner?' Then he said to him, 'Rise and go; your faith has made you well.'" (verses 17-19)

Nine forgot. They took their healing for granted. They did not feel they owed anyone anything—they simply took and walked away. But one returned, and he was a Samaritan, of all people—someone the Jews considered an outsider.

### Thankfulness: The Motivation for Service

Only one returned to praise God. Only one gave what was owed. Only one did his duty. Only one was **thankful**.

Thankfulness is why we serve. Thankfulness is the motivation for service. Thankfulness is the motivation to do our duty.

When someone has served us, we serve others in return. Consider the remarkable work ethic of many students—studying hard, driven not merely by parental pressure or ambition, but fundamentally by **thankfulness**. They have been given much; their parents have spent resources on their education and future. They serve back through diligent study, honoring those who have served them.

God has served us too. Through Jesus, He gave His life as a ransom for many. Out of thankfulness for such grace, we serve Him back.

### Understanding the Earlier Passage (verses 1-6)

This understanding of thankfulness illuminates the earlier verses about forgiveness:

> "If your brother or sister sins against you, rebuke them; and if they repent, forgive them. Even if they sin against you seven times in a day and seven times come back to you saying 'I repent,' you must forgive them." (verses 3-4)

We who have been forgiven much must forgive others much. The disciples, overwhelmed by this call, responded:

> "The apostles said to the Lord, 'Increase our faith!' He replied, 'If you have faith as small as a mustard seed, you can say to this mulberry tree, "Be uprooted and planted in the sea," and it will obey you.'" (verses 5-6)

The problem is not faith. The leper had faith—small faith, simple faith—and it made him well. We do not know when God calls us on a journey what will happen, but we trust. The same faith that healed the leper is the faith we are to have in serving and forgiving others.

Jesus also warned:

> "Things that cause people to stumble are bound to come, but woe to anyone through whom they come. It would be better for them to be thrown into the sea with a millstone tied around their neck than to cause one of these little ones to stumble." (verses 1-2)

We must not prevent others from accessing forgiveness—neither through our judgment, nor through our selfishness, nor through our silence about the gospel. In Luke 18, Jesus says, "Let the little children come to me, and do not hinder them." We must not hinder those seeking God by failing to serve.

The rich man in the parable of Lazarus had great material wealth but was not generous. We have been given great spiritual wealth—forgiveness through Christ—and we must be generous with it. We cannot say we are too busy when the person in need stands right before us, in our classroom, at our workplace, in our backyard. To withhold the gospel from them is to allow them to remain in their sin.

## Our Commission to Serve

The work is not finished. We still have a duty on this earth. That duty is to tell our friends about Jesus, to support this church whose primary aim is to proclaim Christ, to serve one another and the world out of thankfulness for what Christ has done.

This new year brings many opportunities for service: youth groups, Sunday school, Bible classes, ministries to international students, Scripture classes, mothers' groups, seniors ministry, campus ministries, and workplace ministries. All of these exist because the work is incomplete. We wait for Christ to return, and in that waiting, we extend mercy and forgiveness to others.

God will use our little church—inadequate and fallible people, but thankful people—to accomplish His purposes. So come and join us in serving one another and those outside the church who need to hear the message of forgiveness.

## A Final Word of Blessing

Whatever you do, work at it with all your heart, as for the Lord and not for men, knowing that from the Lord you will receive the inheritance as your reward. You are serving the Lord Christ.

Go in peace to love and serve the Lord.
