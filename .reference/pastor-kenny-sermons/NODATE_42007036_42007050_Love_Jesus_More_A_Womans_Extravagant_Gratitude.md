# Luke 7:36-50

**Title:** Love Jesus More: A Woman's Extravagant Gratitude

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Scene: A Pharisee's Dinner

Jesus accepted an invitation to dine at a Pharisee's house. While reclining at the table, a woman from the town—one known for her sinful life—learned where he was. She came with an alabaster jar of expensive perfume and positioned herself at his feet. Overwhelmed, she wept, her tears falling on his feet. She wiped them with her hair, kissed them repeatedly, and anointed them with the perfume.

Simon, the Pharisee who invited Jesus, watched this scene unfold. His thoughts betrayed his skepticism: *If this man were truly a prophet, he would know exactly who is touching him and what kind of woman she is—a sinner.* His logic seemed sound, his judgment secure. But he was about to be gently rebuked by the very man he doubted.

## The Parable: Debts and Love

Jesus turned to Simon and said, "I have something to tell you."

"Speak, teacher," Simon replied.

Jesus told a simple but profound story: "Two men owed money to a certain moneylender. One owed five hundred denarii, the other fifty. Since neither could repay, the lender graciously canceled both debts. Tell me—which debtor will love the moneylender more?"

Simon, sensing a trap, answered cautiously: "I suppose the one whose larger debt was canceled."

"You have judged correctly," Jesus affirmed.

## The Contrast: What Love Looks Like

Then Jesus turned toward the woman and addressed Simon directly. The contrast he drew was devastating in its simplicity:

> "Do you see this woman? I came into your house. You did not give me water for my feet, but she has wet my feet with her tears and wiped them with her hair. You did not give me a kiss of greeting, but she has not stopped kissing my feet since I entered. You did not anoint my head with oil, as was customary, but she has poured perfume on my feet."

Jesus was not simply criticizing Simon's hospitality. He was revealing the difference between formal obligation and genuine love. Simon had extended the minimum social courtesy to a traveling teacher. The woman had offered the devotion of a forgiven soul.

## The Truth: Forgiveness Produces Love

Then came the central teaching: "Therefore, I tell you, her many sins have been forgiven—for she loved much. But he who has been forgiven little loves little."

This is not to say the woman's love earned her forgiveness. Rather, her extraordinary love demonstrated the reality of her forgiveness. She loved much *because* she had been forgiven much.

Notice the beauty in what the woman did. She anointed Jesus's feet with perfume. Why his feet, of all things? Because according to Isaiah, *how beautiful are the feet of those who bring good news*—and Jesus brings the ultimate good news of forgiveness. Her extravagant act was her way of honoring the one through whom restoration had come to her.

The perfume itself carries deeper significance. Perfume was used to prepare bodies for burial. In anointing Jesus, the woman was prophetically pointing to what would secure her forgiveness: his death. Jesus cancels our debts through his sacrifice. This is how he forgives sins. This is the foundation of everything.

## The Pronouncement: Faith and Peace

Jesus then spoke directly to the woman: "Your sins are forgiven."

The other guests were scandalized. "Who is this who even forgives sins?"

But Jesus's final word to the woman transcended their shock: "Your faith has saved you; go in peace."

Her faith—her trust in who Jesus was and what he could do—had been her salvation. And now she could go forward in the peace that only forgiveness brings.

## Application: How Will We Respond?

The question facing us is the same one embedded in Jesus's parable: *What have we been forgiven of?*

Like the woman, we are all debtors. Romans 3:10 reminds us that none of us is righteous—all have sinned and fall short of God's glory. Our debt is not fifty denarii or five hundred. Our debt is incalculable. We owe what we can never repay.

Yet in Christ, that debt has been canceled. Every sin, every failure, every moment of rebellion against our Creator—forgiven. The woman in this story stands as a mirror for all of us. We are all people who have lived lives marked by sin. We all owe much. And we have all been offered the same cancellation of debt.

The question, then, is this: *How much will we love?*

Simon's cold formality reveals the poverty of a heart that has never truly grasped what it means to be forgiven. But the woman's extravagant devotion—her tears, her hair, her perfume—reveals the overflow of a heart transformed by mercy.

When we pause to consider what we have been forgiven of—the patterns of sin we've been rescued from, the shame that's been lifted, the death we deserved but will not face because of Jesus's sacrifice—we cannot help but love. We love Jesus because he has given us peace in place of condemnation. We love others because we have been shown the same grace we offer them.

This is the call: Let us be profoundly grateful. Let us love much. Let us show through our devotion—in how we live, how we treat others, how we speak of Jesus—that we have truly grasped the magnitude of our forgiveness. For in loving Jesus and his people, we are simply responding appropriately to the mercy we have received.
