# 1 Timothy 4:1-16

**Title:** Cleanliness Is Next to Godliness? Distinguishing False Piety from True Godliness

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Phrase's Origins

The saying "Cleanliness is next to godliness" doesn't appear in Scripture. It originated as an ancient proverb, later appearing in modified form in Sir Francis Bacon's *Advancement of Learning* (1605), where he wrote that bodily cleanliness flows from reverence for God. Nearly two hundred years later, John Wesley popularized the exact phrasing we know today in his sermon of 1791: "Slovenliness is not part of religion. Cleanliness is indeed next to godliness."

But the real question before us is: **Is cleanliness itself godliness?**

## 1. False Piety: When Rules Become Righteousness

### The Warning (1 Timothy 4:1-3)

> The Spirit clearly says that in later times some will abandon the faith and follow deceiving spirits and things taught by demons. Such teachings come from hypocritical liars, whose consciences have been seared as with a hot iron. They forbid people to marry and order them to abstain from certain foods, which God created to be received with thanksgiving by those who believe and who know the truth.

Paul speaks of the "later days"—the end times in which we now live, the age between Christ's first and second comings. In these times, deceptive spirits and false teachers will lead people astray. These false teachers are hypocrites whose consciences have been hardened, dulled to spiritual reality.

Their teachings reveal themselves in two commands: forbidding marriage and commanding abstinence from foods. Historically, these represented ascetic heresies—some gnostic philosophies rejected the physical body as inherently sinful, arguing that to be spiritual one must deny the flesh. Their logic: since there is no sexuality in heaven, Christians shouldn't marry; since the flesh is corrupt, certain foods must be avoided.

This reasoning contains a fatal flaw. Just because something doesn't exist in heaven doesn't make it meaningless now. We won't have children in heaven—does that make procreation meaningless today? We won't evangelize in heaven—does that make gospel work pointless now? These present realities are necessary stages leading to our heavenly completion.

### False Piety in Our Time

The ascetic heresy hasn't died; it has simply found new expression. Today's false piety operates through a different mechanism: **moral virtue signaling through dietary and lifestyle choices.**

Consider how these ancient errors reshape themselves:

- **"Forbidding marriage"** becomes celebrating singleness as inherently more spiritual, creating an anti-marriage culture
- **"Abstaining from foods"** becomes treating dietary choices as moral statements—as markers of spiritual superiority

We tell ourselves that coffee must be fair-trade from independent shops, not franchises. We declare certain meats evil; we elevate plant-based eating as the righteous path. Gluten-free and dairy-free become badges of spiritual maturity rather than medical necessities. We speak of acai bowls and protein shakes as paths to moral goodness. We virtue-signal through organic, locally-sourced, non-GMO, free-range choices.

At its core, this is the same error: **elevating external observances into measures of spiritual worth.** We've made "clean, pure, healthy" equivalent to "godly."

Yet observe something striking: **There's a deep connection between sexual ethics and food ethics.** As culture abandons moral restraint in sexual matters, it often redirects that moral energy toward food and lifestyle choices. Some maintain complete moral permissiveness regarding sexuality while becoming increasingly rigid about dietary morality. It's a substitution of moral energies—using food restrictions to signal virtue where other virtues have been abandoned.

This is Corban all over again—the ancient Pharisaic practice of dedicating resources to the temple while neglecting parents. It's false piety masquerading as righteousness, allowing us to feel virtuous while our hearts remain unchanged.

### The Corrective Word (1 Timothy 4:4-5)

> For everything God created is good, and nothing is to be rejected if it is received with thanksgiving. For it is consecrated by the word of God and prayer.

Paul anchors his response in creation itself. Genesis declares God's creation "good"—not once, but repeatedly. Jesus himself proclaimed all foods clean (Mark 7:19). Peter's vision in Acts 10 emphasized: "Do not call anything impure that God has made clean."

The Greek word here is crucial: nothing is to be "rejected" (apodokimazo). But notice the qualifier—all things are good "if received with thanksgiving." Food becomes a means of grace when we receive it as God's gift, not when we treat it as a moral achievement.

This doesn't mean "anything goes." The text presents a paradox: all things are permissible in theory, but not all things are beneficial in practice. At a buffet, you may eat everything, but wisdom asks which choices serve you best.

Here's the crucial distinction: **The problem is not what we eat or don't eat. The problem is when external practices become substitutes for true godliness.**

## 2. True Godliness: Training Yourself for What Matters

### The Call to Teach (1 Timothy 4:6-7)

> If you point these things out to the brothers and sisters, you will be a good minister of Christ Jesus, nourished on the truths of the faith and of the good teaching that you have followed. Have nothing to do with godless myths and old wives' tales; rather, train yourself to be godly.

Timothy's task is to teach these distinctions to the church. The word "nourished" (entrephō) means to be built up and strengthened by sound doctrine. A good minister teaches what strengthens God's people spiritually.

Teaching carries risk. There's the risk of being seen as legalistic. The risk of offending. The risk of being misunderstood. Yet it remains essential work.

### Understanding True Godliness (1 Timothy 4:7)

The Greek word for "godliness" here is *eusebeia*—more than mere morality or rule-keeping. Godliness means **devotion to God, living with the awareness that God is watching, making decisions with God's priorities first.**

This distinguishes itself from other virtues:
- **Holiness** = avoiding sin
- **Reverence** = fearing and honoring God
- **Godliness** = ordering all of life around God's glory, choosing not just between right and wrong, but between good and best

Paul illustrates this: "I have the right to do anything—but not everything is beneficial. I have the right to do anything—but not everything is constructive" (1 Corinthians 10:23).

### Physical Training and Spiritual Training (1 Timothy 4:8)

> For physical training is of some value, but godliness has value for all things, holding promise for both the present life and the life to come.

Some of the false teachers in Timothy's context advocated harsh treatment of the body as a path to spirituality. Paul acknowledges that physical discipline has some value—but he contrasts it sharply with godliness.

The word translated "value" (opheleia) means profit or benefit. Physical training offers limited value; godliness offers infinite value. Godliness is so rich it spans two ages—this present age and the age to come. It yields dividends both now and eternally.

This cuts against modern inversions where we treat fitness as spirituality. There's nothing wrong with physical exercise. But if we're spending $50 weekly for a gym membership three times a week in pursuit of a "beach body," while neglecting prayer, Bible study, and service to others—are we pursuing true godliness? Or are we following a worldly ideal dressed up as spiritual discipline?

### The Promise of Godliness (1 Timothy 4:9)

> This is a trustworthy saying that deserves full acceptance.

Paul stakes his credibility on this: godliness, though it may look weak and foolish to the world, is ultimately the most profitable investment possible. It transcends both this age and the age to come.

### Why This Matters: Our Hope (1 Timothy 4:10)

> This is why we labor and strive (for this we toil and struggle), because we have put our hope in the living God, who is the Savior of all people, and especially of those who have believed.

The words "labor" (kopiaō) and "struggle" (agonizomai) convey effort, strain, wrestling. Why would Timothy labor so hard at this? Because our hope rests in the living God—not in hollow rules, not in visible piety, but in a God who is eternally alive and genuinely saves.

Paul emphasizes that God is Savior of all people, yet specifically of believers. This echoes the truth that while God's grace extends to all humanity, salvation comes to those who trust Christ.

## 3. Leadership and Progress: The Practical Expression

### A Challenge to Young Leaders (1 Timothy 4:11-14)

> Command and teach these things. Don't let anyone look down on you because you are young, but set an example for the believers in all you do, in your speech, your life, your love, your faith and your purity. Until I come, devote yourself to the public reading of Scripture, to preaching and to teaching. Do not neglect your gift, which was given you through prophecy when the body of the elders laid their hands on you.

Timothy faces a real struggle—his youth makes some question his authority. Paul's response is direct: don't let age become an excuse. Instead, become the example in five areas:

1. **Speech**—How you talk reflects your heart
2. **Conduct**—Your actions speak louder than words
3. **Love**—Genuine pastoral care for the flock
4. **Faith**—Deep trust in God's purposes
5. **Purity**—Sexual and moral integrity

The public reading of Scripture, preaching, and teaching form the heart of Timothy's calling. This is leadership work—work that shapes the community's understanding of God. Paul emphasizes that these tasks require the full devotion of a called and commissioned leader (evidenced by the laying on of hands).

### Progress Through Diligence (1 Timothy 4:15-16)

> Be diligent in these matters; give yourself wholly to them, so that everyone may see your progress. Watch your life and doctrine closely. Persevere in them, because if you do, you will save both yourself and your hearers.

Notice the emphasis on *progress*—prokoē, meaning advancement, growth that others can observe. This isn't about sudden perfection; it's about visible movement toward godliness.

The call to "watch your life and doctrine" emphasizes integration. Godliness isn't compartmentalized—it includes both who you are and what you teach. And Paul's final word carries weight: "Persevere in them." The Greek word suggests endurance, holding firm under pressure.

The promise is remarkable: "You will save both yourself and your hearers." This doesn't mean salvation through works. Rather, it means that as you persist in living out and teaching true godliness, you embody the gospel's power. Your own life becomes secure in Christ, and your faithful teaching guides others toward saving faith.

## The Core Issue: Outward Compliance or Inward Transformation?

Consider the contrast in Matthew 23:27-28, where Jesus denounces the Pharisees as "whitewashed tombs." Outwardly beautiful, but inside filled with dead bones and everything unclean. Outwardly righteous, but inwardly full of hypocrisy and wickedness.

Our contemporary challenge mirrors this. We debate the gray areas: Should I take this vacation? Where should I give my money? When should I have children? And while we're right to say we're free in Christ—we are, through His blood—that freedom exists so we might serve Him better, not so we might serve ourselves with a clear conscience.

Here's the penetrating question: **You may be holy, but are you godly?** Do we do things merely because they're not technically sinful? Or do we do them out of devotion to God? In the vast array of permissible good things, are we selecting what's best for the kingdom? Are we committed to God and His gospel, or are we drifting with the world, sin-free but still worldly?

Choosing the best from among the good requires constant teaching, discussion, and even disagreement. But it's possible. It requires opening our eyes to God's goodness—understanding that when we know the gospel, we see creation itself differently. We see God's gifts rightly. And we use them rightly.

Creation is in the right hands when it's held by those who understand that every good gift points us back to the Giver. That's not the spirit of Pharisaism. That's the spirit of true godliness.

## The Mystery of Godliness (1 Timothy 4:16)

> Beyond all question, the mystery of godliness is great:
> 
> He appeared in the flesh,
> was vindicated by the Spirit,
> was seen by angels,
> was preached among the nations,
> was believed on in the world,
> was taken up in glory.

The passage closes not with more rules but with Christ Himself. True godliness isn't found in what we forbid or what we cultivate externally. It's found in union with the One who became incarnate, was raised by the Spirit, is proclaimed throughout the world, and sits in glory at the Father's right hand.

That's the foundation. That's the mystery. And that mystery—the gospel itself—is what shapes us into godliness. Not rules. Not appearances. But the transforming reality that God became man, died for our sins, rose from the dead, and now calls us to follow Him with our whole hearts.
