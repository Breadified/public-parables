# 1 Samuel 16:1-23

**Title:** The Spirit on David: An Unlikely Leader  
**Date:** 4 June 2017  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The End of Saul's Reign

The opening of this chapter marks the beginning of the end for King Saul. God has rejected him as Israel's king, and Samuel struggles with this divine decision. When God commands Samuel to go to Bethlehem to find and anoint a new king, Samuel's first question reveals the danger at hand: "How can I go? If Saul hears about it, he will kill me." This shows how insecure and violent Saul has become—a king who will murder the very prophet who anointed him rather than accept God's judgment.

God provides Samuel with a cover story: he is to take a heifer and announce he is coming to sacrifice to the Lord. The strategy succeeds. When Samuel arrives in Bethlehem, even the elders tremble at his presence, asking nervously, "Do you come in peace?"

## God's Criteria vs. Man's Criteria

When Jesse brings his sons before Samuel for inspection, Samuel immediately fixes his attention on Eliab, the eldest. Eliab is physically impressive—tall, strong, clearly suitable by human standards for kingship. Samuel's first thought is certain: "Surely the Lord's anointed stands here before the Lord."

But God corrects him sharply:

> "Do not consider his appearance or his height, for I have rejected him. The Lord does not look at the things people look at. People look at the outward appearance, but the Lord looks at the heart."

This is a lesson Samuel has apparently forgotten since his selection of Saul, who was also chosen partly on account of his impressive physical stature. The contrast is stark: men evaluate based on what they see—strength, height, bearing—but God evaluates the heart, the inner character and intention.

One by one, Jesse presents his sons. Seven pass before Samuel, and each time Samuel says the same thing: "The Lord has not chosen this one." When Samuel asks if these are all of Jesse's sons, Jesse answers that there is still the youngest, currently tending the sheep. Samuel insists he not sit down to eat until this youngest son is brought.

## The Unlikely Choice

When David arrives, fresh from the fields, the text describes him this way: he is ruddy (red-faced, likely from exertion getting back from the sheep), has beautiful eyes, and is handsome. This description has puzzled many—God just said He doesn't look at outward appearance, yet here comes a description emphasizing appearance. But Kenny suggests the Hebrew is more subtle than our English translations convey.

Looking at the original language: David is described as red (literally, "red like Edom"), with "beautiful eyes," and with something that might be translated as "good vision" or "good sight." The picture that emerges is not of a warrior prince but of a young musician—the kind of boy who would start the chess club and join the school band, not captain the sports team. He plays the lyre and will eventually compose the Psalms. He is, in Kenny's words, "a little nerdy boy."

This is the most unlikely candidate imaginable for a king who must defend Israel against its enemies. His own father didn't even think to present him. Yet:

> "Then the Lord said, 'Rise and anoint him; this is the one.'"

And the text tells us: "So Samuel took the horn of oil and anointed him in the presence of his brothers, and from that day on the **Spirit of the Lord came powerfully upon David.**"

## Understanding "a Man After God's Heart"

The phrase "a man after God's own heart" appears in 1 Samuel 13:14, but Kenny clarifies what this actually means. It's easy to misunderstand: we might think it means David has a naturally good heart. But David's later life shows otherwise—he commits adultery, murder, theft, corruption. The phrase cannot refer to the natural virtue of David's character.

Rather, "according to God's heart" means according to God's own intentions and purposes. God Himself will shape David's heart and destiny. It speaks not of David's innate goodness but of God's sovereign purpose to transform an unlikely shepherd boy into a warrior king and the greatest king in Israel's history.

God's perspective spans eternity. He sees not what David is in that moment, but what He will make David to become through the power of His Spirit.

## David Comes to Saul's Service

While David has been anointed, he begins his rise to prominence in an unexpected way. Some time passes, and Saul, now afflicted by an evil spirit that torments him, seeks someone who can play the lyre to soothe his troubled mind. One of Saul's servants recommends David:

> "I have seen a son of Jesse of Bethlehem who knows how to play the lyre. He is a brave man and a warrior. He speaks well and is a fine-looking man. And the Lord is with him."

What a transformation! The shepherd boy with his lyre has become known as a brave warrior, a skilled speaker, a man of good appearance—and most importantly, "the Lord is with him." The Spirit of God that came upon David at his anointing is already visible to those around him.

Saul summons David, and David enters the king's service. He becomes one of Saul's armor-bearers, and a deep affection grows between them. Saul likes David very much.

When the evil spirit comes upon Saul, David takes up his lyre and plays. The music brings relief; Saul feels better, and the spirit withdraws from him.

## The Spirit Upon David, The Spirit Departing from Saul

There is a tragic parallel unfolding: as the Spirit of the Lord comes powerfully upon David and transforms him, the Spirit of the Lord departs from Saul, leaving him tormented. One man is being prepared for his calling; the other is being abandoned to despair.

This raises the question many find troubling: how can God send an evil spirit? Kenny addresses this honestly. Throughout Scripture, God is portrayed as the source of all that happens—good and evil—without Himself being evil. The Old Testament shows God sending evil spirits in service of His purposes: a spirit of falsehood to lure false prophets (1 Kings 22), spirits of jealousy and discord (Numbers 5:14; Judges 9:23-24), and more.

This reflects a worldview fundamentally different from dualism. There is only one God. God predestines all that comes to pass. In the realm of eternity, God ordains all things. But God's nature is goodness itself. The apparent tension—that an all-good God ordains evil—is one Scripture does not resolve but presents as a mystery. For God, what appears evil to us serves His righteous purposes. For us as creatures, we cannot act this way; we cannot do evil to accomplish good. But for God, whose perspective is eternal and whose purposes are inscrutable, it is permissible.

## Application: You Are God's Chosen Instrument

Kenny draws the teaching toward a profound application. Notice that David is chosen not because of his own virtue but because of God's choice. His anointing does not depend on his worthiness but on God's sovereign purpose to use him.

This same principle applies to every believer. Before the foundation of the world, God chose us in Christ:

> "In him we were also chosen, having been predestined according to the plan of him who works out everything in conformity with the purpose of his will, in order that we, who were the first to put our hope in Christ, might be for the praise of his glory." (Ephesians 1:11-12)

God's choice of you did not depend on looking at your heart—at least not in the sense of evaluating your character. Rather, He chose you before you existed, in Christ, according to His own purposes. When God picked you as a Christian, He did not evaluate your heart's condition, for what is naturally in our hearts is sin. Instead:

> "For it is by grace you have been saved, through faith—and this is not from yourselves, it is the gift of God—not by works, so that no one can boast. For we are God's handiwork, created in Christ Jesus to do good works, which God prepared in advance for us to do." (Ephesians 2:8-10)

Your life has a purpose. You are not random. God has prepared good works in advance for you to accomplish. You are going to be part of God's plan to transform this world and the lives within it.

Perhaps you see yourself as weak, poor, without self-control, without intelligence, without power—as just a nerd with no one to believe in you. You ask, "What can I do?" But that is the wrong question.

The better question is: "What can God do through me?"

This is the lesson of David. God saw a young shepherd boy, overlooked by his own family, and transformed him into a mighty warrior and the greatest king Israel would know. The power was never David's. It was God's Spirit coming powerfully upon him, reshaping him for His glory.

And He has done the same for you. The Spirit of God dwells in you. You have been chosen. You have been sealed. You have been given His Holy Spirit as a deposit, guaranteeing your inheritance. You are His workmanship. And through you—unlikely as you may seem to yourself or to the world—God will accomplish His purposes and transform the world around you.
