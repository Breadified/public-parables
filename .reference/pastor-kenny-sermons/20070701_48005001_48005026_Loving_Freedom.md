# Galatians 5:1-26

**Title:** Loving Freedom
**Date:** 8 July 2007
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Problem with Freedom

Our world is obsessed with freedom. America stands as the epitome of it—a country built on the ideal of liberty. And yet, for all our celebration of freedom, something seems fundamentally wrong. We see countries with great freedom struggling with deep social problems: poverty goes uncared for, healthcare is inadequate, and a culture of self-interest means the vulnerable are left behind. Freedom without direction becomes a hollow promise.

The same tension exists in the Christian life. We believe passionately in freedom—it's part of our heritage. William Wilberforce fought for the equality and freedom of all men. But how do we make sense of Christian freedom? What does it actually mean to be free in Christ?

## Freedom in Christ

> **Galatians 5:1** — "It is for freedom that Christ has set us free. Stand firm, then, and do not let yourselves be burdened again by a yoke of slavery."

Paul writes with unmistakable clarity: Christ has liberated us. But from what? The background is crucial to understand. In the early church, Jewish believers faced immense pressure from teachers insisting that Gentile converts must first become Jewish—they must be circumcised and observe all the temple rituals. These weren't minor traditions; they were control mechanisms that determined who had access to God and who didn't. Think of the stories Jesus rebuked—how religious leaders had twisted the law to abuse people like the bleeding woman, controlling access to God through endless requirements.

But the cross changed everything. In Christ, there is no longer a Jew-first, then-Christian pathway. All true Christians are made so by faith alone. The yoke of slavery—circumcision, temple rituals, endless rules—has been broken.

## The Choice Before Us

This creates a decisive moment. Paul addresses Gentile believers directly: "Do not let yourselves be circumcised!" Why? Because what truly matters is not our outward observance but our faith. As Paul says:

> **Galatians 5:6** — "For in Christ Jesus neither circumcision nor uncircumcision has any value. The only thing that counts is faith expressing itself through love."

Paul's language grows sharp when addressing those who would reimpose these burdens:

> **Galatians 5:2-4** — "Mark my words! I, Paul, tell you that if you let yourselves be circumcised, Christ will be of no value to you at all. Again I declare to every man who lets himself be circumcised that he is obligated to obey the whole law. You who are trying to be justified by law have been alienated from Christ; you have fallen away from grace."

The challenge is real. Every human institution—every law, every system—tries to add conditions and qualifications. Find me one place where something is truly free without a hidden "but." There's always a catch, a loophole, a legal fiction buried in the terms.

## Freedom from Control

What Paul is attacking is something far deeper than circumcision. He's confronting the human tendency to control others through spiritual leverage. We do this constantly:

- We tell young people they must have daily quiet times to be "real Christians"
- We measure spirituality by whether someone goes to church or takes Communion
- We use salvation itself as leverage, implying: "If you were truly grateful, you'd do things my way"
- We create invisible hierarchies: the full-time minister is more Christian than the layperson; the ascetic is more spiritual than the ordinary believer

All of these are yokes of slavery. What do we use yokes for? To control animals. To direct them. To dominate them.

Paul's point is radical: **In the matter of salvation, no human being has authority over you.** No pastor, priest, or parent can blackmail you into God's kingdom or coerce you into righteousness. No one can keep you from God except God himself.

Yes, life is full of legitimate authorities. Your employer controls your working hours. Teachers direct your education. Parents guide their children. These are necessary structures. But there is one realm that belongs entirely to you: your standing before God.

## Freedom For, Not Just Freedom From

But Paul doesn't stop there. Freedom in Christ isn't meant to be a blank check for selfishness. Listen to his next words:

> **Galatians 5:13-15** — "You, my brothers, were called to be free. But do not use your freedom to indulge the sinful nature; rather, serve one another in love. The entire law is summed up in a single command: 'Love your neighbor as yourself.' If you keep on biting and devouring each other, watch out or you will be destroyed by each other."

This is the crucial reorientation. We are free **for** something, not just **from** something. Our freedom has a purpose: to love.

The whole law—all those regulations and rules—finds its fulfillment in a single command: love your neighbor as yourself. That's not less demanding than the old system; in many ways, it's more demanding. Love requires far more than mere external obedience. It demands that we lay down our rights and serve one another.

## The Work of the Spirit

How do we live this out? Through the Spirit of God. The Spirit's work is not mysterious or disconnected from God's Word—they work together. The Spirit doesn't operate on feelings alone. Think of it this way: if your bus is late by one minute and you happen to catch it, that's God's providence. But if you miss the bus? That's God too. The Spirit's work isn't about the buzz we feel after a good worship session or the warm emotions that follow a beautiful song. That's real, but it's not the Spirit's primary work.

The Spirit transforms us from the inside out, conforming us to Christ. The evidence isn't how we feel; it's how we live—the fruit of the Spirit working through us to love one another.

## The Invitation

Here's what you're signing up for when you accept Christ's freedom: the call to love. If you don't want that—if you're not willing to lay down your rights and serve others—then salvation itself might be a waste of time. Because a person who comes to heaven unwilling to love would simply make heaven into hell.

But if you accept that invitation, you've been set truly free: free from the tyranny of human judgment, free from earning God's favor, and free to love as you've been loved.
