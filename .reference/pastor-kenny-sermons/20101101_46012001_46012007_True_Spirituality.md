# 1 Corinthians 12:1-7

**Title:** True Spirituality

**Date:** 15 November 2010

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## What is True Spirituality?

We live with many misconceptions about what spirituality looks like. Some imagine the TV evangelist with spectacular miracles. Others picture the meditating monk in a quiet monastery. Perhaps the missionary who left everything to serve overseas, or the eager believer attending every church event. Some think it's the person with daily quiet times, or one who wakes at 5am to pray. For many of us, it's emotionalism—the passionate, spontaneous, vocal, fervent person at church.

But what does Scripture actually teach?

The Corinthian church wrestled with this same question. Corinth was a prosperous harbor city, a crossroads between north and south Greece. With its wealth came moral corruption: sexual immorality, idolatry, greed, and hedonism saturated the culture. Yet a church existed there—Paul had planted it—but it reflected the city's problems far too closely. Sexual immorality, divisions, lawsuits, idolatry, and selfishness infected the congregation. Some believers even swung to the opposite extreme, embracing strict asceticism and claiming that poverty marked true spirituality.

The Corinthians wrote to Paul asking: what is spirituality? Over the next three weeks, we'll explore his answer, beginning with the most challenging material.

## Point 1: Concerning "The Spirituals"

Notice verse 1 carefully:

> Now about the spirituals, brothers, I do not want you to be ignorant.

The Greek simply says "the spirituals"—not "spiritual gifts." This is crucial. The word is an adjective left incomplete in Greek, forcing us to ask: spiritual what? Blessings? People? Truths?

### Is it "Spiritual Gifts"?

The popular interpretation suggests "spirituals" means "spiritual gifts." This seems reasonable at first—after all, the Corinthians had problems with how gifts were used. But several problems emerge with this reading:

**The term "spiritual gifts" doesn't exist in the New Testament.** The singular "spiritual gift" appears only once—in Romans 1:11, where Paul says he wants to impart a spiritual gift to Rome. Yet later we learn this "gift" is the gospel itself, not some special talent.

**When gifts are discussed, the Spirit often isn't mentioned.** In 1 Corinthians 2, Paul discusses the work of the Spirit but says nothing about gifts. In chapter 7, he describes life gifts (marriage, singleness) without mentioning the Spirit. Chapter 12:27-30 discusses many gifts but not the Spirit. The converse is also true—when the Spirit is discussed, gifts aren't emphasized.

**A fatal contradiction emerges.** In 1 Corinthians 1:7, the Corinthians "do not lack any gift." Yet in 3:1, Paul says "I could not address you as spiritual but as worldly—mere infants in Christ." If spirituals equal gifts, Paul contradicts himself: they have everything yet have nothing. This suggests "spirituals" and "gifts" are distinct concepts.

Therefore, the connection between the Spirit and gifts in popular Christian teaching is greatly exaggerated—a product of poor teaching and unhelpful translation. The entire Godhead gives gifts, not the Spirit alone.

### The Correct Interpretation: "Spiritual Truths"

I propose instead that "the spirituals" refers to **spiritual truths**—specifically, the communication of spiritual truths among believers. Paul and the Corinthians are addressing how to speak God's words to one another.

To understand what "spiritual" means, consider what it's not. In chapter 2, Paul contrasts the spirit of the world with the Spirit of God:

| **Spiritual is NOT** | **Spiritual IS** |
|---|---|
| Eloquence or superior wisdom | Jesus Christ and him crucified |
| Wise and persuasive words | Demonstration of Spirit and power |
| Men's wisdom | God's power |
| Wisdom of this age | Message of wisdom for the mature |
| | God's secret wisdom now revealed |
| Spirit of the world | Spirit of God, understanding God's gifts |
| Words taught by human wisdom | Words taught by the Spirit |
| Unable to accept things from the Spirit | Discerning all things, knowing the mind of Christ |

From this contrast, spiritual truths are those centered on Christ crucified, God's power, His revealed wisdom, and understanding His grace. The Spirit is the breath of God—and God's breath is His words.

This interpretation is consistent with 1 Corinthians 9:11:

> If we have sown spiritual seed among you, is it too much if we reap a material harvest?

"Spiritual seed" is the gospel message Paul planted. By extension, "the spirituals" are spiritual truths—the communication of the gospel's truths to one another.

## Point 2: Don't Be Ignorant of Spiritual Truths (1-2)

Paul's first command: "I do not want you to be ignorant."

Many equate "spiritual" with ignorance. We see this in practices that demand we "feel our way to truth"—tarot cards, horoscopes, meditations that empty the mind. We see it in anti-intellectualism and emotionalism, where spontaneity without thought supposedly proves spirituality. We see it in false signs: miraculous displays, people "slayed in the Spirit," prosperity promises, and emotional fervor.

**But Christian spirituality is neither ignorance nor emotionalism.** Emotions are gifts and valuable, but they don't indicate spirituality. Paul calls us to knowledge—the kind that transforms us. Knowledge of the gospel.

Verse 2 illustrates this:

> You know that when you were pagans, you were influenced and led astray to mute idols.

As pagans, they "listened" to wooden and silver statues that couldn't speak. Dumb idols—not merely foolish, but literally unable to communicate. By contrast, **God is the Spirit God who speaks and reveals.**

**Application:** Know the Scripture. Read the Bible. Read Christian books. Plan your summer not around holidays and festivities, but around what you'll read, which books you'll finish, what your church teaches, which Bible conferences you'll attend. If you've told yourself during school "I'll catch up on faith during the holidays"—the holidays are here. Make it happen.

## Point 3: The Spiritual Confesses the Lordship of Christ (3)

The test of spirituality is found in verse 3:

> No one speaking by the Spirit of God says "Jesus be cursed," and no one can say "Jesus is Lord" except by the Holy Spirit.

This isn't a formula to chant. It's wholehearted commitment to the gospel—the gospel of Christ's death and resurrection as Lord of the universe.

**The sign of a Christian is submission to Christ's lordship.** If you don't believe Jesus is Lord over your life, you don't have the Spirit. You're not a Christian if you refuse submission to Him.

I'm not saying Christians live perfectly. But we should desire to follow His lordship, even when we stumble. Piety and emotionalism cannot compensate for disobedience.

Some live a Sunday/Monday Christianity—worshiping on Sunday, cheating on Monday. Saturday partying, Sunday piety. **Such people do not have the Spirit.** Christ's lordship demands consistent allegiance.

**Application:** If you believe the gospel, you have it within you. You are a Christian. You possess spiritual truth and are therefore a spiritual person. Don't forget the greatest gift of all—eternal life. All Christians share this common Spirit, whether emotional or reserved.

## Point 4: The Gospel is the Ultimate Spiritual Truth (4-6)

Paul emphasizes this clearly:

> There are different kinds of gifts, but the same Spirit. There are different kinds of service, but the same Lord. There are different kinds of working, but the same God works all of them in all people.

Among Christians debating who has the true Spirit, Paul says: you have the gospel. I sowed it. You received it. You share the same Spirit.

The connection between the Holy Spirit and gifts in popular teaching has become distorted—the idea that the Spirit is the "Santa Claus of the Trinity," dispensing special talents. But the Godhead works together. The Word of God is the Sword of the Spirit. If you want the Spirit, find Him in the Word of God.

While emotions have their proper place, **emotionalism does not define spirituality.** The false idea that passion and excitement prove you're in the Spirit echoes the old "second baptism" heresy—the notion that some Christians lack the Spirit until they receive a second, more powerful experience. This demeans the gift of eternal life that all Christians possess.

## True Spirituality

At our gathering today, we haven't performed many formalities. There's been little singing, no tears. Some might say "this group isn't spiritual!" But that's precisely backwards.

I see Christians bonded by the Spirit—by the spiritual truths of the gospel that saved us. We're united as common recipients of an amazing gift: eternal life. This is the unity of the Spirit. We're bonded by the same Lord, the same God, the same Spirit—the same spiritual truths. Here, the Bible is read, the Word proclaimed, where we speak spiritual truths to one another.

**That's as spiritual as it gets.**

As we continue together, let's say "Jesus is Lord" to each other—not as formula, but by sharing how His lordship has shaped us. Speak spiritual truths to remind one another that Jesus is Lord and we are His servants. Tell each other how you've been challenged. Ask: "How did God help you through this season?"

That's what it means to be spiritual.

So you think you can be spiritual? Go for it.
