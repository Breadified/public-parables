# 1 Corinthians 13:1-13

**Title:** The Common Ministry: Love
**Date:** 24 January 2010
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction: Good but Useless

Trivia can be fascinating—we might know that the fastest wombat moves at 42 km/h—but here's the problem: it's often useless for 99% of our daily lives. In fact, Trivial Pursuit has become a popular game at garage sales precisely because people are giving it away.

The same principle applies to the gifts God gives us. We can exercise our spiritual gifts with great skill and proficiency, but if they're not accompanied by love, they become equally useless. Last week we learned that all Christians have the Spirit of God within them—that's what it means to be spiritual. But having the Spirit and exercising gifts isn't enough. Paul shows us there's a more excellent way.

## 1. Gifts Are Useless Without Love (v.1-3)

> If I speak in the tongues of men and of angels, but have not love, I am only a resounding gong or a clanging cymbal. If I have the gift of prophecy and can fathom all mysteries and all knowledge, and if I have a faith that can move mountains, but have not love, I am nothing. If I give all I possess to the poor and surrender my body to the flames, but have not love, I gain nothing.

Paul presents an equation: **Gifts × Love = Profit**. Without love, our accomplishments become nothing but noise—a resounding gong, a clanging cymbal. We gain nothing. It's literal: the word means "profitless."

Think of an orchestra without a conductor. Each musician might be talented, but without unity and direction, the result is cacophony. Your talents are good. Your time and effort are valuable. But if in the end they're not done with love, then they're useless.

The key ingredient is love. So what is this love that Paul describes?

## What Is Love? (v.4-7)

> Love is patient, love is kind. It does not envy, it does not boast, it is not proud. It is not rude, it is not self-seeking, it is not easily angered, it keeps no record of wrongs. Love does not delight in evil but rejoices with the truth. It always protects, always trusts, always hopes, always perseveres.

This is what we need to bring to our service of one another. Love provides the proper focus for our gifts and talents. Love provides the motivation by which we judge our activity.

Notice the specifics:
- **Patience and kindness** address our relational difficulties with one another
- **Freedom from envy, boasting, and pride** counter our individualism
- **Not rude, not self-seeking** concern our treatment of others
- **Not easily angered, keeping no record of wrongs** define our forgiveness
- **Rejoicing with truth, protecting, trusting, hoping, persevering** shape our commitment to one another's good

When you read this list, you see Jesus there. We do this not because we have naturally learned to love, but because He has loved us first. His love is our model and our motivation.

## The Concern for the Body of Christ

Jesus makes this clear in Matthew 25. When the Son of Man sits in judgment, He will separate the nations like a shepherd separates sheep from goats. To those on His right, He will say:

> Come, you who are blessed by my Father; take your inheritance, the kingdom prepared for you since the creation of the world. For I was hungry and you gave me something to eat, I was thirsty and you gave me something to drink, I was a stranger and you invited me in, I needed clothes and you clothed me, I was sick and you looked after me, I was in prison and you came to visit me.

And to those on His left:

> Depart from me, you who are cursed, into the eternal fire prepared for the devil and his angels. For I was hungry and you gave me nothing to eat, I was thirsty and you gave me nothing to drink, I was a stranger and you did not invite me in, I needed clothes and you did not clothe me, I was sick and in prison and you did not look after me.

The defining mark of the righteous is their concern for the vulnerable and forgotten. Therefore, we must now do things out of genuine concern for others. **The test always is: Does it exhibit thoughtful love? Or is it the unrestrainedly brute exercise of gifts?** Nothing at our church should be done without concern for the body. The body is more important than the exercise of any individual gift.

## Love Applied: Examples from Corinthians

### 1 Corinthians 8: The Stumbling Brother

Paul addresses a real situation: food sacrificed to idols. Some Christians knew that idols were nothing but wood—mere superstition had no real power. But others, whose consciences were still weak, couldn't eat such food without feeling they were participating in idolatry.

> Knowledge puffs up, but love builds up. Be careful, however, that the exercise of your freedom does not become a stumbling block to the weak. When you sin against your brothers in this way and wound their weak conscience, you sin against Christ. Therefore, if what I eat causes my brother to fall into sin, I will never eat meat again, so that I will not cause him to fall.

Paul's application of love is radical: possess the knowledge that idols are nothing, but restrict your freedom out of care for your brother. That's love—being patient, kind, other-seeking, and willing to limit your liberty for the sake of the weaker believer.

### 1 Corinthians 11: The Meal Together

When the church gathered for the Lord's Supper, some were eating their own meals while others went hungry. Paul rebukes them:

> When you come together to eat, wait for each other. If anyone is hungry, he should eat at home, so that when you meet together it may not result in judgment.

Again, love requires us to consider others. Every activity of our life—the course we choose, the meal we eat, when we eat it, what we wear, what we say, what beverages we serve at a party, what games we play—is judged by the standard of love. Love must provide the direction. Love supersedes our rights and restrains the exercise of our gifts.

## Serving at Church: A Matter of Love

In the past, we used the acronym FAT.Love to describe who should serve—we identified people who were gifted, available, and talented, and then hoped they would learn to love their service. But I made a mistake. If they don't learn to love, then no matter how gifted, available, and talented they are, the result is a big fat zero. Useless.

The correct priority—though unhealthy sounding—is **LOVE.FAT**: Love first, then giftedness, availability, and talent.

### Why Serve?

It's tempting to serve for many reasons:
- For a new experience and fun
- To build your resume
- To be with friends and the cool leaders
- To feel better about yourself and gain self-esteem
- For personal training and skill development

These things can happen, and they're not necessarily bad. Serving does have its joys. You'll let employers know how you spend your time. You'll make friends serving together. You'll feel better about yourself and improve your skills, especially with people. These are real benefits.

**But these cannot be your primary reason for serving**—not even a close second place. As Bryan Adams sings, you must do it all for love. These other reasons don't last. Only love does.

### Objection 1: "Why Else Would God Give It to Me?"

Some object: Didn't Paul tell us to exercise our gifts? Didn't God give them to me? Surely I should use what God gave me. Why would He give it to me otherwise?

Consider an analogy. A student gets a high ATAR score and everyone assumes God is calling them to medicine or law. But maybe God is telling them to do a simple arts degree to show that you don't need a flashy career. Maybe it's to become a teacher, because we need smarter teachers. Maybe it's to go into politics where they're needed. Or maybe to drop out altogether and pursue independent study because they're smart enough to do so.

The mark by itself is nothing. It's only a means to achieve a goal—whether that's making money, building a fancy career, winning popularity, pleasing parents, or helping society. And your reason for pursuing that goal will determine what you do with that tool.

Gifts are like tools. What do you want to do? And if you love, it is never wasted. Doing a course well below your ATAR level—if that course is what's good for others—can never be wasted. For our young people especially: just because you're good at something is not enough reason to do it.

### Objection 2: "I Have No Other Gifts to Exercise"

Don't despair. There will always be a need to love. Don't know where to serve? First, ask: What are my gifts? But by needs, I don't mean only traditional official roles. I mean love needs.

Look around you. What can you do? Who needs care? Who needs to hear the gospel? Who needs encouragement? Who needs someone to talk to? If you can speak, you have the gift of hello. That opens doors for growth in the gospel and for God's Word to penetrate deep into our hearts. Talking to one another is hard—it means opening up, it means getting rid of small talk about the latest thing you bought or the last movie you watched. But it's essential.

We all have multiple gifts precisely in order to love:

- **Mouths** to speak the words of God to each other
- **Legs** to walk toward someone
- **Memory** to remember others' needs and pray for them during the week
- **Eyes** to see who sits alone or rushes out because they fear being alone
- **Arms** to lift up chairs, pull in garbage bins, or give someone a hug
- **Hands** to scribble a note or type an SMS saying "Keep following Jesus"
- **Driving license** to give people lifts
- **Internet access** to email about youth group or check in on someone who's been absent
- **Wallets** and jobs to give generously
- **Houses** to host a dinner party
- **Mobile phones** to pray on

All of these have been given by God. Don't say you have no gifts. Open your mind to the roles. They all exist for love.

### The Language of Opportunities

This is why I'm keen to move away from the language of "gifts" to the language of "opportunities." That's how Paul uses the word in 1 Corinthians 7, speaking of single versus married people. The way to approach life is not by asking "What am I good at?" but by asking "What needs are around me?" Then fill them. Opportunities are gifts that actually do common good and actually profit.

The problem is that we keep thinking about the glamorous upfront things that perhaps should be only 1% of church activity. How can all of us do that 1%? We can't all be eyes. We can't all be ears. These formal, more glorious roles are important, but they're not everything.

Some seminars and conferences on spiritual gifts start at the wrong place: "What am I good at and what looks impressive?" This invariably leads to music, singing, and leadership—as if God only gave a few dozen upfront gifts. I challenge any such conference to come up with "the gift of taking the garbage in," which one of our brothers here has discovered. Whatever gift it is, it's useless without love.

I'd rather run a love seminar. Gifts will work themselves out. **Make the decision to love first, and gifts will follow.**

## 2. Love Is More Important Than Gifts (v.8)

> Love never fails. But where there are prophecies, they will cease; where there are tongues, they will be stilled; where there is knowledge, it will pass away.

What does it mean that love never fails? It means love endures. One day all the other things will disappear.

### Love Is the Fruit That Lasts

For our musicians, ministry will one day become boring. For our Bible leaders, this Christian culture we're part of is very popular now, especially with young people. It's tempting to ride that popularity. But will you still lead the narrow way when it's no longer cool?

For those helping with morning tea, packing up, ushering and welcoming—will you still serve?

All those other reasons—because it's fun, because it looks good on your resume, because it makes you feel better about yourself, because you're with your friends—will be gone. Service will become hard work. Your resume will take a beating when you quit that job for the sake of love or take up something less glamorous. Your self-esteem will be low when you're taking the flak for trying to act godly and serve others' interests. It won't feel cool when you're alone packing up chairs or cleaning after Bible study.

**Will you still serve then? Out of love?**

Our missionary friends face this constantly. They have preferences—they'd rather stay home than go. They do things they don't like—because Jesus did that for us.

Here's a danger for young people in church: it's very cool to join a group. Young people move in packs. But your loyalty—you who have the Spirit of Christ in you—has to be to the Lord Jesus first. It's not to your group. As a result, your loyalty is to serving the body of Christ, not to peer pressure. So you'll still look after individuals when the group abandons them.

If you find yourself burning out in a ministry, or you're not doing it out of love anymore, or you're doing it only because someone pushed you to—please don't continue. Come talk to me or the other leaders.

### A Mature Understanding of Gifts

> When I was a child, I talked like a child, I thought like a child, I reasoned like a child. When I became a man, I put childish ways behind me. Now we see but a poor reflection as in a mirror; then we shall see face to face. Now I know in part; then I shall know fully, even as I am fully known. And now these three remain: faith, hope and love. But the greatest of these is love.

In the end, one day SMAC will disappear. You will forget what you learned. People won't remember your deeds. **But love will count.** If you did things out of genuine concern for others, if you were not self-seeking, if you loved—that will count forever.

In the end, who cares how talented we are or how wonderfully this ministry might grow? It only matters if it counts toward love. **True spirituality is love. Love is true spirituality.** God knows your motivation. It doesn't matter if the ministry is successful or not—what matters is whether it was done in love.

When is a gift not a gift? When it's not done in love.

## The Best Trumps the Good

We face the tyranny of choice. We're surrounded by so many good things, so many good options, so many good people. A friend once told me: "The good is the enemy of the best."

Do the best thing, not just the good thing. Love. Don't trivialize your opportunities. Make it count. Love.
