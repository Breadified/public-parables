# Galatians 2:1-20

**Title:** Gospel Not of Works  
**Date:** 25 July 2021  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Mixed Messages

We live in a time of confusion and mixed messages. In the early days of lockdown, tensions are rising—people positioning themselves on different issues, particularly around vaccination. Government authorities sent conflicting signals: one day saying the AstraZeneca vaccine should be available to everyone, the next day restricting it due to rare clotting risks. Those over 50 didn't want it. Those under 50 were advised against it. Meanwhile, everyone felt compelled to take a stance and judge others for theirs.

This confusion mirrors a deeper spiritual crisis in the early church. Paul writes to the Galatians about a far more serious mixed message regarding the gospel itself. The controversy centered on whether Gentile Christians had to follow Jewish laws—specifically, whether they needed to be circumcised to be saved.

Mixed messages are dangerous. But Scripture speaks clearly on this matter.

## The Gospel: Freedom Through Grace, Not Works

Peter, Jesus' closest disciple and leader of the Jerusalem church, had received a vision from God in Acts 10. The Spirit told him that the barrier between clean and unclean—between Jews and Gentiles—had been removed. Jesus had declared all foods clean. Peter understood: in Christ, the separation is gone.

Based on this revelation, the apostles in Jerusalem confirmed Paul's gospel message. When Paul brought Titus, a Greek Gentile, before the leaders, they agreed: Titus did not need to be circumcised. The apostles recognized Paul's calling to the Gentiles just as Peter's calling was to the Jews. They gave the right hand of fellowship. All was well.

The good news was clear: **inclusion without conditions**. No amount of following the law could make us perfect before God. The gospel is fundamentally different from every other religious system.

Every other religion operates on the principle of "do, do, do." Follow the rules. Earn your standing. Keep performing. Even modern secular systems operate this way—endless rules about what you must eat, wear, believe, and say. When you fail to comply, you face shame and cancellation.

But the gospel is different: **"Done."** Jesus has completed the work. He has paid the penalty. Salvation is not something we earn through our efforts. It is something we receive through faith—like opening a gift that someone else bought and paid for.

## Compulsion: When the Gospel Is Undermined

But then something changed. Certain men arrived from James in Jerusalem, and Peter's courage wavered. He began withdrawing from Gentile Christians, eating separately, avoiding fellowship. His change wasn't based on theology. He hadn't reread Scripture. He wasn't responding to a pastoral concern. **He changed because of peer pressure.**

The other Jewish believers followed his example. Even Barnabas, Paul's traveling companion, was led astray by the hypocrisy.

This is the essence of the problem Paul addresses: **compulsion masquerading as conviction.**

Peter used his influence to pressure Gentiles to follow Jewish customs—to become circumcised, to observe food laws. He made obedience to the law compulsory for salvation. In doing so, he was saying that Jesus' death wasn't enough. You must add your own works. You must become Jewish to be fully accepted.

Paul confronted him directly: "You are a Jew, yet you live like a Gentile. How is it, then, that you compel Gentiles to follow Jewish customs?"

We recognize this pattern in our own time. An employer welcomes a Christian organization. But then public pressure builds. Suddenly there's "a change in policy"—not based on principle, but on fear of those with influence. When this happens in government, we call it the whim of a dictator. One day this rule. Tomorrow that rule. Everything presented as an emergency, all for your "safety."

When it happens in the church, it's anti-gospel.

One day the message is: "Jesus died for you by grace."  
The next day: "You must stop eating certain foods and be circumcised for Jesus' death to be effective for you."

This reverses the gospel. We've regressed to Old Testament times, where you had to sacrifice bulls and goats, follow circumcision laws, observe food restrictions—all to maintain your standing with God. The good news becomes the old news. We're back to the era of the Pharisees.

But Paul uses a crucial word in verse 3: **"compelled."** When the theology is sound, Titus was not compelled to be circumcised. It was optional—a matter of preference or tradition, not salvation.

There are rituals in the church today. Many come from good, honest places. Attending church is important and beneficial. But we must distinguish between what is helpful and what is **compulsory for salvation**. The gospel cannot compel. God's way is persuasion, not force. He presents truth and lets us choose. He respects our freedom because freedom is core to who He is.

Yet the circumcision group wanted to compel. And when Peter gave in to their pressure, he was saying: "Your standing with God depends on your obedience to these laws."

That's not gospel. That's slavery.

## Freedom: The Heart of the Gospel

Paul writes:

> This matter arose because some false believers had infiltrated our ranks to spy on the freedom we have in Christ Jesus and to make us slaves. (2:4)

The word is deliberate. If you compel someone to be circumcised as an adult, they become a slave. In the Old Testament, adult circumcision marked slaves and prisoners of war.

But Paul would not yield, even for a moment:

> We did not give in to them for a moment, so that the truth of the gospel might be preserved for you. (2:5)

The leaders in Jerusalem—James, Peter, John—held high esteem. But Paul says:

> Whatever they were makes no difference to me; God does not show favoritism. They added nothing to my message. (2:6)

The gospel cannot be improved. It cannot be added to. It is complete in itself. If you add to it, you corrupt it. It's like pure virgin olive oil—add a preservative, add flavoring, and you've lost its value.

Throughout history, there have been two types of heresies:

**Gospel-minus:** Remove the resurrection. Remove the cross. Remove Jesus.

**Gospel-plus:** Add circumcision and food laws. Add works and Christian traditions. Add political correctness.

Both corrupt the gospel. Both make it something it isn't.

When Paul finally confronts the core issue in verse 15-16:

> We who are Jews by birth and not sinful Gentiles know that a person is not justified by the works of the law, but by faith in Jesus Christ. So we, too, have put our faith in Christ Jesus that we may be justified by faith in Christ and not by the works of the law.

**Justified** means to be declared righteous before God—as if standing in a courtroom where the verdict is "not guilty." This declaration comes not through our works, not through our obedience, but through **faith in Jesus Christ**.

Faith is not a work. Faith is receiving a gift. When someone gives you a present, you don't earn it by saying thank you. You didn't perform a work. You simply received what was given.

The Jews, the original people of God, the ones who kept the law most carefully—even they are not justified by the law. So how could Gentiles, who lived outside the law, be expected to earn righteousness through it?

It makes no sense. It's like expecting someone from another culture to celebrate traditions they've never kept, when the original keepers of those traditions have themselves moved beyond them.

## Freedom and the Crucified Life

Paul's final words capture the transformed life that gospel freedom produces:

> For through the law I died to the law so that I might live for God. I have been crucified with Christ and I no longer live, but Christ lives in me. The life I now live in the body, I live by faith in the Son of God, who loved me and gave himself for me. (2:19-20)

And he concludes with shattering clarity:

> I do not set aside the grace of God, for if righteousness could be gained through the law, Christ died for nothing! (2:21)

The gospel is this: **Christ alone. Grace alone.** A message of freedom from the tyranny of works. Freedom from endless performance. Freedom from the shame of never being good enough.

In our own time, we must be careful about anything that compels. We must resist the urge to shame and guilt people into obedience. The gospel operates by persuasion, not compulsion. And when we cannot persuade, we must respect the freedom that the gospel proclaims.

Let's act as if we truly believe we are free.
