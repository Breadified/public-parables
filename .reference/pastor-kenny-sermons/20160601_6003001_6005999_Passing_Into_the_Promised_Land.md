# Joshua 3-5

**Title:** Passing Into the Promised Land
**Date:** 26 June 2016
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## God Passes Over (3:1-14)

Israel stands on the threshold of the Promised Land, poised to enter uncharted territory. They've never been this way before. For forty years, an entire generation has died in the wilderness because they refused to trust God. Now, their children—the new generation—are about to experience what their parents could not claim by faith.

Joshua calls the people to prepare. For three days they camp at the Jordan, waiting for God's instructions. The command comes through clearly: follow the ark of the covenant.

> "When you see the ark of the covenant of the Lord your God, and the Levitical priests carrying it, you are to move out from your positions and follow it... Then you will know which way to go, since you have never been this way before." (Joshua 3:3-4)

The ark represents God's presence dwelling with His people. In the wilderness, the ark traveled in the middle of the camp so all could see it. When Moses entered the tent of meeting, God would speak to him from above the ark. When the ark moved, God moved. This is why the ancient prayer declares: "Rise up, O Lord! May your enemies be scattered" (Numbers 10:35).

The profound truth here is that **God goes first**. Joshua is a great leader—God will exalt him before all Israel—but God Himself will be the one leading them forward. Like a parent entering a dark house first to guide their children, like an army captain who leads his troops into battle, God takes the initiative. He provides guidance, sets the example, and demonstrates His love.

The obstacle before them is overwhelming. The Jordan runs at flood stage during harvest time, churning with chaotic water. The enemy nations—the Canaanites, Hittites, Hivites, Perizzites, Girgashites, Amorites, and Jebusites—stand ready on the other side. Yet God says to the priests: step into the water.

> "Tell the priests who carry the ark of the covenant: 'When you reach the edge of the Jordan's waters, go and stand in the river... As soon as the priests who carry the ark of the Lord set foot in the Jordan, its waters flowing downstream will be cut off and stand up in a heap.'" (Joshua 3:8, 13)

God has moved on our behalf as well. We were powerless, still sinners, incapable of saving ourselves. God didn't wait for us to get our act together—we'd still be waiting. Instead, He acted first.

> "You see, at just the right time, when we were still powerless, Christ died for the ungodly... But God demonstrates his own love for us in this: While we were still sinners, Christ died for us." (Romans 5:6-8)

The crossing of the Jordan represents the threshold between death and life. It is at the cross that we have passed from death into life. God made the first move. He took the initiative. He went first so that we might follow.

## Israel Passes Over (3:15-4:24)

What happens next is nothing short of miraculous. The priests step into the water, and immediately the impossible becomes reality.

> "Now the Jordan is at flood stage all during harvest. Yet as soon as the priests who carried the ark reached the Jordan and their feet touched the water's edge, the water from upstream stopped flowing. It piled up in a heap a great distance away... while the water flowing down to the Sea of the Arabah was completely cut off. So the people crossed over opposite Jericho. The priests who carried the ark of the covenant of the Lord stopped in the middle of the Jordan and stood on dry ground, while all Israel passed by until the whole nation had completed the crossing on dry ground." (Joshua 3:15-17)

This crossing bookends Israel's wilderness experience. The first bookend was the Red Sea crossing in Exodus, when water stood up on either side as they fled Egypt. Now, as they enter their inheritance, God repeats the miracle. The wilderness has ended. The season of manna is over. They now eat from the land of milk and honey.

> "The manna stopped the day after they ate this food from the land; there was no longer any manna for the Israelites, but that year they ate the produce of Canaan." (Joshua 5:12)

These miracles are not everyday occurrences. In Israel's history, one might estimate miracles occurred roughly once every three hundred years—and then a cluster of them when Jesus came to earth. God's miracles mark critical junctions in salvation history. They are rare, specific, and unmistakable signs of His power.

But God does something remarkable to preserve this memory. He commands Israel to take twelve stones from the riverbed and set them up as a memorial.

> "Choose twelve men from among the people, one from each tribe, and tell them to take up twelve stones from the middle of the Jordan, from right where the priests are standing, and carry them over with you and put them down at the place where you stay tonight... These stones are to be a memorial to the people of Israel forever." (Joshua 4:2-3, 7)

Imagine twelve of Israel's strongest men, each wading through the dry riverbed, lifting massive stones onto their shoulders, and carrying them to the other side. Like a souvenir from the Great Barrier Reef or a piece of the Berlin Wall—something tangible that proves you were there. River rocks, smooth from the current, unmistakably from the Jordan itself. Future generations would look at these boulders and know: *The water stood here. The river ran dry. God made it possible.*

Joshua places the stones at Gilgal and explains their significance for generations to come:

> "In the future when your descendants ask their parents, 'What do these stones mean?' tell them, 'Israel crossed the Jordan on dry ground.' For the Lord your God dried up the Jordan before you until you had crossed over. The Lord your God did to the Jordan what he had done to the Red Sea when he dried it up before us until we had crossed over. He did this so that all the peoples of the earth might know that the hand of the Lord is powerful and so that you might always fear the Lord your God.'" (Joshua 4:21-24)

Notice the pronouns shift to the second person: *your God, you crossed, so that you might always fear the Lord your God.* This memorial is not just for the people who witnessed it. It's for every generation after. Through God's Word—through the Bible—we relive the history of yesterday. We experience it ourselves.

> "By faith the people passed through the Red Sea as on dry land; but when the Egyptians tried to do so, they were drowned... By faith the walls of Jericho fell, after the army had marched around them for seven days." (Hebrews 11:29-30)

Faith is not superstition. It is trust rooted in the record God has given us. The Bible is not a dead document. It is alive and effective because the promises it holds still apply. Joshua crossed the river once—he won't cross it again. Jesus died for our sins once—He will not be crucified again. These are accomplished facts of God's salvation history, recorded for our benefit.

Think of it like a graduation degree. You pass your exams once. You don't sit them again to prove you can pass them. The degree is your memorial—it proves you achieved what it says. If someone doubts you, you point to the document. So it is with Scripture. If you doubt what God has done, look to the written record. Witnesses saw the crossing. Witnesses saw the cross. They recorded it for us. The promises of yesterday apply to us today.

## Death Passes Over (5:1-15)

Here we encounter something strange. Fresh from their miraculous crossing, with their enemies terrified and ready to collapse, God commands something that seems militarily foolish: circumcision.

> "Make flint knives and circumcise the Israelites again." (Joshua 5:2)

Why? Because the entire generation born in the wilderness had never been circumcised. Circumcision is the sign of God's covenant, instituted by Abraham and required of all his descendants. Those who were not circumcised could not be God's people. Yet for forty years, this sign had been neglected.

Now, on the eve of conquest, God says the time has come. Every man must be circumcised. This leaves them vulnerable—physically weak and defenseless for days. From a military standpoint, it seems absurd. Yet God says: don't worry. I will protect you.

> "And after the whole nation had been circumcised, they remained where they were in camp until they were healed." (Joshua 5:8)

The timing reveals something profound. God does not command circumcision *before* they cross—when it might serve as a work they accomplish to earn His favor. Instead, He commands it *after* they've crossed, *after* they've entered the Promised Land, *after* they've been blessed. Circumcision becomes a sign of blessing received, not a work required to receive blessing.

> "Then the Lord said to Joshua, 'Today I have rolled away the reproach of Egypt from you.'" (Joshua 5:9)

They celebrate with Passover, remembering their deliverance from Egypt when the angel of death "passed over" the homes marked with lamb's blood. Now, in the Promised Land, they recall: *death has passed over us.*

Before the conquest begins, Joshua encounters the Commander of the Lord's army:

> "Now when Joshua was near Jericho, he looked up and saw a man standing in front of him with a drawn sword in his hand... 'Neither [for you nor for your enemies],' he replied, 'but as commander of the army of the Lord I have now come.' Then Joshua fell facedown to the ground in reverence... The commander of the Lord's army replied, 'Take off your sandals, for the place where you are standing is holy.'" (Joshua 5:13-15)

This echoes Moses at Sinai—a reminder that what comes next is not a military campaign won by human strength, but a conquest directed by God Himself.

There is a pause in the campaign. Memorials to erect. Circumcisions to observe. Passover to celebrate. Why? Because the victory for this land will not be won by flesh and bones, but by God. The preparation is not the sharpening of swords, but the renewal of covenant—removing the uncircumcision of Egypt, remembering the deliverance God has already accomplished, and standing on holy ground.

For us who know Jesus, our circumcision is no longer physical but of the heart. It is the removal of sin and rebellion, accomplished through His work.

> "A person is not a Jew who is one only outwardly, nor is circumcision merely outward and physical." (Romans 2:28)

We are circumcised at heart when we remember that death has passed over us because of Christ crucified—when we claim the reality of His finished work and live in gratitude for a salvation we did not earn and cannot lose. This is how we enter our inheritance, not through human achievement, but through faith in what God has already accomplished.
