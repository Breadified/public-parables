## The Power of Words

Words shape our world. A few carefully spoken words can alter the course of history and transform lives. Kennedy's commitment to reach the moon by decade's end launched humanity to Mars. A declaration of war sets nations on a destructive path. On a personal level, the words "we are moving" or "I will" can redirect an entire life.

This sermon series explores the core values that define our church: **All Arise, Receive, Inspire, Serve**. Today we focus on **Inspire**—God's word, spoken and shared, animates everything we do.

## The Power of God's Words

Paul approaches his letter to the Romans with profound seriousness. He writes "quite boldly," not as an ordinary correspondent, but as a **minister** of Christ Jesus—a sacred responsibility with the weight of priestly duty.

> Romans 15:16: "to be a minister of Christ Jesus to the Gentiles with the priestly duty of proclaiming the gospel of God, so that the Gentiles might become an offering acceptable to God, sanctified by the Holy Spirit."

Though Paul descends from the tribe of Benjamin—no priestly lineage—he sees his work as fundamentally priestly. He brings reconciliation between God and humanity through the proclamation of God's word.

> Romans 15:17: "Therefore I glory in Christ Jesus in my service to God."

This exultation stems not from personal greatness (Paul elsewhere identifies himself as the worst of sinners) but from the **ultimate importance of his task**: conveying God's word to humanity.

### The Creative Power of God's Word

God's words possess creative force. The universe itself emerged not through labor or programming, but through divine speech:

> Genesis 1:3: "And God said, 'Let there be light,' and there was light."

This divine speaking reached its climax in Jesus Christ:

> Hebrews 1:1-2: "In the past God spoke to our ancestors through the prophets at many times and in various ways, but in these last days he has spoken to us by his Son."

> John 1:14: "The Word became flesh and made his dwelling among us. We have seen his glory, the glory of the one and only Son, who came from the Father, full of grace and truth."

Jesus himself declared:

> John 15:3: "You are already clean because of the word I have spoken to you."

Today, we continue receiving these powerful words through Scripture—God's authoritative voice preserved for all generations.

> 2 Timothy 3:16: "All Scripture is God-breathed and is useful for teaching, rebuking, correcting and training in righteousness, so that the servant of God may be thoroughly equipped for every good work."

### Scripture's Civilizational Impact

The Bible has shaped Western civilization's foundational values: our judicial systems, rule of law, and cherished protections for the vulnerable and poor. Yet modern society increasingly disrespects this Book, picking and choosing which parts to believe.

Scripture must be taken wholly, not partially. Half-measures produce spiritual Pharisees—externally compliant but inwardly corrupt. As a church, we commit to teaching God's complete counsel, including the unpopular passages. We address where worldly thinking creeps into Christian life, and we learn from Scripture what it truly means to love one another, live in community, forgive, and be forgiven.

## The Power of Speaking God's Words

If God's word is powerful, how much more should we speak it? Paul doesn't believe the word stops with him:

> Romans 15:14: "I myself am convinced, my brothers and sisters, that you yourselves are full of goodness, filled with knowledge and competent to instruct one another."

The Roman church—full of problems like any congregation—nonetheless possesses what matters most: **goodness, knowledge, and competence to teach one another**.

In the first century, without widely available Bibles, believers transmitted Scripture orally:

> Ephesians 4:15: "Speaking the truth in love, we will grow to become in every respect the mature body of him who is the head, that is, Christ."

### Mutual Encouragement Through God's Word

When we gather as church, we're not merely eating, singing, or performing ancient rituals together—as valuable as these are. We're called to **encourage one another** through the words we've learned from Scripture.

> 2 Timothy 3:16: "All Scripture is God-breathed and is useful for teaching, rebuking, correcting and training in righteousness."

We teach, rebuke, correct, and train one another in different contexts: with each other at church, with our families at home, and with the world around us. In each setting, we proclaim God's gospel.

### The Weight of Our Words

Studies suggest we speak approximately 20,000 words daily. Are these words marked by wisdom and patience, or by malice and carelessness? We must steward our God-shaped capacity for speech.

This gathering offers a particular opportunity. Perhaps the sermon sparked a question. Perhaps reading the passage yourself triggered an insight. Maybe during the week, God stirred a thought in your heart, or you have a genuine question to ask.

**Share these with one another.** As you speak, you'll instruct, comfort, and assure each other of God's forgiveness.

Some will do this naturally; others must be intentional. Consider making it a goal to have at least one meaningful conversation about God each time you gather. Instead of generic pleasantries ("How's church?" or "How's your Christian life?"), ask specifically: "What have you been thinking about God lately?" or "What did God teach you this week?"

If appropriate, pray for one another.

**Be intentional.** As God's Spirit inspired Scripture, let us inspire one another to persevere in faith.
