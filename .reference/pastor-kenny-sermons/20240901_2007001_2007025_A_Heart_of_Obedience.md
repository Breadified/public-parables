# Exodus 7:1-25

**Title:** A Heart of Obedience  
**Date:** September 1, 2024  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## God's Authority Through Moses (vv. 1-7, 17-25)

The Lord said to Moses: "See, I have made you like God to Pharaoh, and your brother Aaron shall be your prophet. You shall speak all that I command you, and your brother Aaron shall tell Pharaoh to let the people of Israel go out of his land."

### "Like God to Pharaoh"

Moses is given divine authority and becomes God's representative before Pharaoh. This is not merely a diplomatic appointment—when Moses speaks or acts, it is as though God Himself is speaking and acting. This authority is crucial because Pharaoh considers himself divine. He must recognize that Moses operates under the power and authority of the one true God.

Through Moses, God arranges a direct confrontation with Pharaoh. Moses becomes the visible representative of the invisible God, embodying God's will and power in a way that Pharaoh can directly experience. For an ancient world where gods were understood as powerful yet unseen, such a representative role was essential. Pharaoh views himself as divine or semi-divine, and encountering someone who operates "like God" challenges his very claim to godhood. This is God's way of dismantling Pharaoh's self-perception and the Egyptian belief that their ruler is the supreme deity. Through Moses, God will demonstrate that true power and divinity belong to Yahweh alone, not to Pharaoh.

### The Hardening of Pharaoh's Heart

"I will harden Pharaoh's heart, and though I multiply my signs and wonders in the land of Egypt, he will not listen to you... When I stretch out my hand against Egypt and bring the Israelites out of it, the Egyptians will know that I am the Lord."

The central theme throughout these chapters is the hardening of Pharaoh's heart. The narrative repeatedly mentions it—sometimes as his own choice, sometimes as God's direct intervention. This dual aspect raises profound theological questions and offers important lessons.

One perspective is that God squeezes Pharaoh's heart to reveal what lies within. What emerges is rebellion and stubbornness. Pharaoh's hardness may stem from his pride and belief in his own divine status. The idea of submitting to the Hebrew God is unthinkable to him. Yet a pattern emerges: a plague comes, Pharaoh's heart seems to soften, but once the plague lifts, he hardens his heart again and refuses to let Israel go. This cycle repeats, highlighting the deceptive nature of sin and the danger of half-hearted repentance.

Ultimately, these are mere excuses. Now is the time for submission. Now is the time for judgment.

## The Signs and Wonders (vv. 3-7)

"I will multiply my signs and wonders in the land of Egypt... I will perform great acts of judgment, bring out my people the Israelites, and the Egyptians will know that I am the Lord."

The ten plagues demonstrate God's supremacy over nature, over the Egyptian gods, and over Pharaoh's will. Scholars have noted various patterns in the sequence: some organize them in groups of three, each set escalating in severity. Others recognize numerical patterns—the number seven appears frequently throughout the narrative. Still others suggest the plagues target specific Egyptian deities.

> "When that night comes, I will pass through Egypt and strike down every firstborn of both people and animals, and I will bring judgment on all the gods of Egypt. I am the Lord." (Exodus 12:12)

### The First Plague: Blood

"Thus says the Lord, 'By this you shall know that I am the Lord.' Behold, I will strike the water that is in the Nile with the staff that is in my hand, and it shall turn into blood. The fish in the Nile shall die, and the Nile will stink, and the Egyptians will find it difficult to drink water from the Nile."

The Nile River is the center of Egyptian life, religion, and economy. It was worshipped as a god—Hapi, the deity controlling the river's vital inundations. Imagine living in a society where the very source of life and commerce suddenly becomes poisoned. The devastation would be catastrophic, but the spiritual impact would be even greater. As the Egyptians depended on the Nile, we often depend on material resources, technological advancement, or human institutions as our ultimate source of security. Yet God's power transcends these things, reminding us that we must ultimately rely on Him alone.

### The Plague of Frogs

The frogs are connected to Heqet, the goddess believed to aid women in childbirth and symbolizing fertility. This plague is especially humiliating for the Egyptians—creatures once considered sacred become sources of affliction and torment. What was holy becomes repulsive.

### The Plague of Gnats or Lice

Swarms of gnats descended upon all people and animals. This plague attacks Geb, the Egyptian god of the earth. The fertile soil that sustains life becomes a source of suffering and annoyance. The magicians, for the first time, acknowledge this as "the finger of God" (Exodus 8:19), yet Pharaoh's heart remains hard.

### The Magicians' Failed Counterfeit

The magicians of Egypt used their secret arts to do the same things. They produce their own miracles initially—when Aaron's staff becomes a snake, Pharaoh's magicians do likewise. But when the plague of gnats comes, something changes. The magicians attempt to produce gnats by their secret arts, but they cannot.

> "The coming of the lawless one will be in accordance with how Satan works. He will use all sorts of displays of power through signs and wonders that serve the lie." (2 Thessalonians 2:9)

> "But when the magicians tried to produce gnats by their secret arts, they could not." (Exodus 8:18)

The magicians' power has limits. Their counterfeit wonders fail where genuine divine power succeeds. This reveals an important truth: no false power—whether magic, deception, or the schemes of evil—can ultimately stand against God's authority.

## The Heart of the Matter: Who Is Our God?

The question before us is not simply who God is on Sundays, during prayer and Bible reading. The real question is: Who is God on Monday through Saturday? During the 9-to-5? From 5 p.m. to 9 p.m.? Which god competes for our hearts and allegiance?

If we examined ourselves honestly, we might discover that the "god" of modern life is often materialism or hedonism. Our leisure time is not planned around serving God—how can I serve Him? Is there someone I should speak with? Is someone sick and in need of a text or prayer? Could we start a Bible study? Instead, we scroll through Amazon and Facebook Marketplace thinking about what purchase will satisfy us. We browse Flight Centre and Webjet wondering where we could travel to find satisfaction. Worldliness is ultimately about the self.

But there is only one God, and He does not share His throne. Pharaoh is about to discover this truth.

## Pharaoh's Idolatry (vv. 8-16)

Pharaoh viewed himself as divine—a son of Ra. When he says to Moses and Aaron, "Perform a sign or wonder," he is testing God. Requesting miracles as a test of God's authenticity has never been wise.

> "When Herod saw Jesus, he was greatly pleased, because for a long time he had been wanting to see him. From what he had heard about him, he hoped to see him perform some sign." (Luke 23:8)

The Pharisees who sought to kill Jesus did the same:

> "The Pharisees and Sadducees came to Jesus and tested him by asking him to show them a sign from heaven." (Matthew 16:1)

Even the devil attempted this:

> "The devil led him to the highest point of the temple. 'If you are the Son of God,' he said, 'throw yourself down. For it is written: He will command his angels concerning you, and they will lift you up in their hands, so that you will not strike your foot against a stone.'" (Matthew 4:5-6)

Requesting miracles to test, requesting out of unbelief, demanding that God prove Himself—these are all warnings Scripture gives us:

> "Jesus answered him, 'It is also written: Do not put the Lord your God to the test.'" (Matthew 4:7)

> "Do not put the Lord your God to the test as you did at Massah." (Deuteronomy 6:16)

Miracles and wonders alone do not produce faith. They are signs of judgment upon those who refuse to believe:

> "He answered, 'Then I beg you, father, send Lazarus to my family, for I have five brothers. Let him warn them, so they will not also come to this place of torment.' Abraham replied, 'They have Moses and the Prophets; let them listen to them.' 'No, father Abraham,' he said, 'but if someone from the dead goes to them, they will repent.' He said to him, 'If they do not listen to Moses and the Prophets, they will not be convinced even if someone rises from the dead.'" (Luke 16:27-31)

The issue is always the heart. If it is hardened, no sign will matter.

### Pharaoh's Obstinate Heart

The narrative emphasizes this repeatedly:

- Verse 13: "Pharaoh's heart became hard; he would not listen to them, just as the Lord had said."
- Verse 14: "Pharaoh's heart is unyielding; he refuses to let the people go."
- Verse 16: "And the Lord said to Moses, 'Go to Pharaoh in the morning... until now you have not listened.'"

A military operation succeeds only when every soldier strictly follows the commander's orders. If even one person disobeys, the entire mission is compromised. Similarly, obedience to God's instructions is essential for accomplishing His purposes in our lives. Obedience is not optional; it is essential. We are called to follow God's direction, even when it is difficult or when we do not fully understand His plan. Through obedience, we demonstrate our faith and trust in God, and it is through obedience that we experience His blessing and protection.

The hardening of Pharaoh's heart serves as a warning to all who hear the gospel. Just as Pharaoh repeatedly rejected God's command and experienced its consequences, so too will those who harden their hearts against the message of salvation. Yet the gospel also offers hope: even the hardest heart can be softened by God's grace.

## A Call to Self-Examination

Pharaoh's story calls us to examine our own hearts. Are we resisting God's will in certain areas or refusing to listen to His voice? The good news is that God's grace is available to all who turn to Him in repentance. We are called to respond to God's call with soft hearts, ready to receive His word and obey Him.
