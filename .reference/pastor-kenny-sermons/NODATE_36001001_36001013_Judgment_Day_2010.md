# Zephaniah 1:1-13

**Title:** Judgment Day: 2010  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## A Sweeping Judgment (verses 2-4)

### The World

God declares through the prophet Zephaniah:

> "I will sweep away everything from the face of the earth," declares the LORD. "I will sweep away both men and animals; I will sweep away the birds of the air and the fish of the sea. The wicked will have only heaps of rubble when I cut off man from the face of the earth," declares the LORD.

Zephaniah prophesied around 640-609 BC during the reign of King Josiah, in the final decades before Babylon would invade Jerusalem. His message is stark: God's judgment is coming, and it will be total.

Notice the breadth of this judgment—it encompasses all creation. This echoes the account of Noah's flood, when God saw humanity's wickedness and decided to cleanse the earth. The language of "sweeping away" recalls Genesis 6:5-7, where God grieved over human sin and determined to wipe mankind from the earth.

More precisely, Zephaniah's prophecy reverses the order of creation itself. On Day 5, God created the creatures of the sea and the birds of the air. On Day 6, God created the animals and humans. Now, in judgment, these will be unmade—swept away entirely. Unlike Noah's flood, where the fish survived, this judgment will leave nothing behind. Creation itself will be undone.

### Jerusalem

But God's judgment focuses particularly on Jerusalem:

> "On that day," declares the LORD, "a cry will go up from the Fish Gate, wailing from the New Quarter, and a loud crash from the hills. Wail, you who live in the market district; all your merchants will be wiped out, all who trade with silver will be ruined."

Listen to the sounds of judgment: a cry, a wail, a crash. These are not the sounds of children waking in the night. These are the sounds of invasion and destruction.

The Fish Gate stands in Jerusalem's northern wall—likely one of the main entrances to the city. The geography of Jerusalem makes this significant: the city sits on a high plateau, surrounded by valleys and ravines on the west, east, and south. Any invading army must come from the north. The Babylonians will breach the northern gate, sweep through the new quarters inside the wall, and like a flood of water, work their way downward through every district, destroying all in their path. The marketplace will fall silent as merchants vanish. Those who trade in silver will be ruined.

### Personal Judgment

Yet this is no impersonal catastrophe. God will personally inspect His judgment:

> "At that time I will search Jerusalem with lamps and punish those who are complacent, who are like wine left on its dregs, who think, 'The LORD will do nothing, either good or bad.'"

The word "punish" literally means God will "visit" Jerusalem with punishment. He will search every cranny and nook with lamps, like a mother scrubbing a toilet, bleaching every crevice. No one will hide. No sanctuary will protect them.

God's particular judgment falls on the complacent—those like wine left on its dregs, the leftovers who have settled into apathy. These people believe God is neutral, that He does nothing either good or bad. They treat Him like an Easter Bunny: indifferent, non-judgmental, simply handing out gifts with no care for righteousness. But God is not neutral. He judges. He cares deeply about how His people live.

## The Root: Idolatry (verses 4-6)

The reason for this coming judgment is spiritual adultery. God reveals what Judah has done:

> "I will stretch out my hand against Judah and against all who live in Jerusalem. I will cut off from this place every remnant of Baal, the names of the pagan and the idolatrous priests—those who bow down on the roofs to worship the starry host, those who bow down and swear by the LORD and who also swear by Molech, those who turn back from following the LORD and neither seek the LORD nor inquire of him."

Judah has abandoned the God who delivered them from Egypt and given them the land. Instead, they have embraced false gods.

**Baal** represents the gods of fertility—Ashtaroth and Asherah—the gods of life and prosperity.

**Molech** represents the god of death and the underworld, a god pleased by child sacrifice.

Whether these gods promised life or death made no difference. All were idolatry in Yahweh's sight.

### The Double Love

But some tried to hedge their bets. They swore by Yahweh *and* by Molech simultaneously. Surely God would accept a divided heart?

No. In any true relationship, exclusivity is essential. If you are not wholly with Yahweh, you do not truly have Him at all. There is no such thing as double love.

This problem was not confined to superstitious common folk. It involved political leaders—the princes and the king's sons who had adopted foreign clothes and foreign alliances. It involved religious leaders who performed pagan rituals and filled the temples with violence and deceit. All of Judah participated in this spiritual adultery.

## The Promise Reversed (verse 13)

Because of their idolatry, the ancient promises would be undone:

> "Their wealth will be plundered, their houses demolished. They will build houses but not live in them; they will plant vineyards but not drink the wine."

Recall Deuteronomy's promise: God would give them cities they did not build, houses filled with good things, wells they did not dig, vineyards and olive groves they did not plant. All the fruits of labor without the labor itself—a blessing for obedience.

Now this promise reverses into a curse. They will build, but not enjoy. They will plant, but not harvest. Why? Because they forgot the God who gave them everything. They did not learn from their northern cousins' destruction by Assyria. They repeated Judah's sins and now would face the same fate.

---

## Dreadful Expectation (verse 7)

The verdict is final:

> "Be silent before the Sovereign LORD, for the day of the LORD is near. The LORD has prepared a sacrifice; he has consecrated those he has invited."

What can you do when God has pronounced His verdict? Only silence remains. The dreadful expectation of coming judgment.

God has prepared a sacrifice—Judah and Jerusalem themselves. He has consecrated those He has invited—the Babylonians, come to feast on the main course of Jerusalem's destruction.

For the ancient Jews, it was too late. All they could do was wait in silence.

## Application for Us Today

We may think we are far removed from Judah's ancient idolatry. We do not openly bow to Baal or Molech. But consider what commands our hearts:

- Some worship at the altar of materialism—a television that tells us what we need to buy to be happy
- Some worship at the altar of self-gratification—cable modems and endless digital distractions
- Some bow down repeatedly at golf courses or press down at gyms, preoccupied with sports and our physical appearance
- Some are captivated by the stars of Hollywood, following glossy gossip magazines that peddle astrology
- Some parents, in the name of achievement, sacrifice their children's Christian formation—church attendance becomes optional, but tutoring and academic advancement never are. They work extra jobs to feed their smallest god: the wallet
- Some worship the literal god or goddess—a person we cannot give up even when they tempt us away from Jesus
- All of us tend the "little leather god" in our pocket or purse—we feed it with part-time work, we protect it fiercely, and when generosity calls, we shield it like nothing else

We claim to love Jesus and follow God. But like those who swore by Yahweh *and* Molech, do we maintain a double love? Do we pledge loyalty to God with one hand while honoring another god with the other?

Let us repent of these divided hearts. Let us give up these false gods. Let us follow the one true God through Jesus Christ. That means everything else—everything—must be far, far second.

## Judgment Is Coming

This is what idolatry produces: **judgment**.

The ancient Jews thought they had time. King Josiah, a good king, found the Law hidden in the temple and attempted reforms. But despite his efforts, Judah was rotten at the core. Too little, too late. The fundamental transformation needed could only come through judgment.

We find ourselves in a similar position. Peter wrote to the early church:

> "In the last days, scoffers will come, scoffing and following their own evil desires. They will say, 'Where is this coming he promised? Ever since our fathers died, everything goes on as it has since the beginning of creation.' But the heavens and earth that now exist are reserved for fire, being kept for the day of judgment and destruction of ungodly men... The day of the Lord will come like a thief. The heavens will disappear with a roar; the elements will be destroyed by fire, and the earth and everything in it will be laid bare."

Do we truly believe this? Or does our double love leave us conflicted—believing the world will end, yet acting as though it will continue forever? We build investment portfolios and retirement plans as though we have decades of certainty. We accumulate possessions as though our houses will stand eternally.

Judgment is assured. The question is: are you ready?

## Called to Holiness

Consider what happened in a recent flood—water damage that ruined homes, shops flooded with destruction. Now imagine the tsunamis magnified, waves covering Mount Everest itself. Ponder everything we treasure: Michelangelo's painting on the Sistine Chapel, the Christ the Redeemer statue in Rio, the White House, our homes, our schools, everything we own.

Something like this is coming. We know because of Easter.

Jesus, by defeating death itself, rose to be the Lord of judgment. Therefore, the countdown has begun. It could be 2010. It could be tomorrow. We do not know the day or hour, but we know it will come—not necessarily after you've fulfilled your ambitions, not when you graduate or marry or retire, but any time now.

The apostle Peter challenges us:

> "Since everything will be destroyed in this way, what kind of people ought you to be? You ought to live holy and godly lives as you look forward to the day of God... We are looking forward to a new heaven and a new earth, the home of righteousness."

Be ready. Invest in holiness and godliness, not materialism. Look forward to the new creation, not the next gadget or vacation or party.

---

## Hope in Christ

There is one more truth Zephaniah speaks to those who follow Jesus.

> "Be silent before the Sovereign LORD, for the day of the LORD is near. The LORD has prepared a sacrifice; he has consecrated those he has invited."

For us, this passage takes on a different meaning entirely.

Yahweh has prepared a sacrifice—His own Son, Jesus Christ.

So that He might invite us—a different set, both Jews and Gentiles—to join Him at His table.

The day of judgment is coming. But those who have repented and followed Christ will not face condemnation. Instead, we look forward to a new heaven and a new earth—the home of righteousness, where we will dwell with our God forever.

Therefore, be ready. Repent. Follow Jesus, the ultimate Judge.
