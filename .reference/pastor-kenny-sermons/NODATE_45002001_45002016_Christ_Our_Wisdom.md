# Romans 2:1-16

**Title:** Christ Our Wisdom
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction

When joining the school council, I was struck by how refreshing it is to witness a learning community that explicitly centers Christ. Coming from the public school system, where Christian expression often requires special permission while other worldviews receive automatic accommodation, I found myself grateful for a place where teachers pray in their classrooms and Christian support for students is simply woven into the fabric of the school.

The school's motto—"Christ Our Wisdom"—points us directly to one of Scripture's most foundational passages about what true wisdom actually is.

## Christ as Wisdom: The Core Message

> When I came to you, brothers and sisters, I did not come with eloquence or human wisdom as I proclaimed to you the testimony about God. For I resolved to know nothing while I was with you except Jesus Christ and him crucified. I came to you in weakness with great fear and trembling. My message and my preaching were not with wise and persuasive words, but with a demonstration of the Spirit's power, so that your faith might not rest on human wisdom, but on God's power. We do, however, speak a message of wisdom among the mature, but not the wisdom of this age or of the rulers of this age, who are coming to nothing. No, we declare God's wisdom, a mystery that has been hidden and that God destined for our glory before time began. None of the rulers of this age understood it, for if they had, they would not have crucified the Lord of glory. (1 Corinthians 2:1-8)

Paul presents to the Corinthian church a radical claim: the message of wisdom is Jesus Christ and him crucified. But this is not merely historical content—a factual statement about what happened. Rather, Christ crucified serves as the **key principle** by which all knowledge and wisdom must be understood. It is the **presupposition and epistemological framework** through which we interpret reality itself.

The resurrection confirms that Christ is Lord of all creation, which means all knowledge and all disciplines fall under his lordship. This changes everything.

## The Radical Implication: No Neutral Education

There is a temptation to think that subjects like mathematics, science, geography, or economics are somehow neutral—independent of worldview. But this misses something crucial. The very foundations upon which these disciplines rest require a Christian presupposition:

- **Philosophy of science** assumes a Lawgiver who established the laws of nature we discover
- **Philosophy of mathematics** assumes objective, unchanging truths that exist independent of human preference
- **Philosophy of history** assumes a meaningful arc to human events, not merely random accidents—a meta-narrative from which we can learn
- **Philosophy of language** assumes words carry objective meaning, not merely subjective interpretation

These foundational assumptions are not neutral. They are distinctly Christian. There is no such thing as education without a worldview. There is Christian education, and there is non-Christian education. The difference is real, even if our post-Christian culture makes it harder to see.

## The Cost of Abandoning Wisdom

We are already witnessing the fractures in a society that has rejected Christ as the source of wisdom. Our carefully reasoned arguments—about marriage, euthanasia, school funding, fundamental ethics—no longer persuade because we are operating under fundamentally different presuppositions about what constitutes an argument.

A proper argument requires:
- Recognition that logic and reason matter
- Willingness to hear the strongest case for the other side
- Belief in objective truth that exists independent of our subjective feelings
- Assumption that truth and evidence matter to all parties

When these foundations crumble, argument itself becomes impossible. We assume others value what we value—truth, evidence, open-mindedness—when in fact we are operating according to entirely different wisdoms.

## A Refuge for Wisdom

As we seek excellence in education, we must ask: what kind of excellence? Excellence toward what end? By what standard?

I came to this council because I see this school as something increasingly rare: a place that consciously holds to the wisdom of Christ in an age of confusion. If the present cultural crisis deepens, institutions like this may become sanctuaries where the ancient wisdom of God can be preserved and passed on—monasteries of Christ's wisdom in the midst of increasing darkness.

This is both a privilege and a profound responsibility.
