# Luke 17:1-19

**Title:** First World Problems and Last World Problems
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## First World Problems

Have you heard of "first world problems"? It's a term describing the complaints of people in wealthy nations—problems that take for granted the luxuries most of the world considers unattainable. We complain about forgotten earphones, broken treadmills, too many seeds in our watermelon, or which university degree to pursue. Meanwhile, we remain blind to genuine human suffering around us.

This passage concerns people who feel entitled—who take God's gifts for granted. It's a sobering reminder of where our hearts truly are.

## God Does Not Owe Us (7-10)

> Suppose one of you had a servant plowing or looking after the sheep. Would he say to the servant when he comes in from the field, "Come along now and sit down to eat"? (Luke 17:7)

The servant has worked hard all day. He's spent hours plowing the soil—turning over the upper layer, bringing fresh nutrients to the surface while burying weeds. Or perhaps he's been shepherding, spending his day running after sheep, feeding them, rescuing them from rivers, walking miles, shearing them, milking them, occasionally defending them against wolves and lions.

After such a day under the Middle Eastern sun—sweat, muscle pain, a sore back, blisters, and thirst—the servant naturally wants to sit down and enjoy his reward. But the master has other expectations.

> Would he not rather say, "Prepare my supper, get yourself ready and wait on me while I eat and drink; after that you may eat and drink"? (Luke 17:8)

The work isn't finished. The master hasn't eaten yet. The servant must prepare the meal and continue serving—only then can he have his own dinner.

> Would he thank the servant because he did what he was told to do? So you also, when you have done everything you were told to do, should say, "We are unworthy servants; we have only done our duty." (Luke 17:9-10)

The answer is no. The servant has merely done his duty. There's no special thanks required—just as you wouldn't thank a toaster for making your toast or thank a lecturer for accepting your assignment. The servant has done what was expected.

### The Issue of Duty

In our culture, "duty" sounds burdensome—something forced upon us against our will. We prefer service to be spontaneous and freely chosen. But we misunderstand duty.

Some years ago, youth leaders complained about having to lead the youth group when their hearts weren't in it. They reasoned it would be disservice to God to minister without sincere feeling. But that's backwards. While it's true we shouldn't pursue long-term ministries we don't care about, duty means doing what you've committed to, even when it's hard.

Consider soldiers. They make a commitment for a set period. They can't decide spontaneously to resign when war breaks out because they've lost enthusiasm. They've committed. That's their duty.

The Christian perseveres in service—they see things through, keep their word, commit, and do their duty. They anticipate hardship. The path is narrow. We take up our cross and follow.

Yet here's the danger many young people face: thinking "I've done my Christian bit." "I packed up chairs at church—I'm done." "I served at church, so I don't need to think about evangelism at work." "I led one Bible study; now it's my year off ministry." And then one year off becomes several, then a career focus, then retirement playing golf and traveling overseas.

But consider John Chapman, a beloved Christian worker in Sydney who died at 82. Though he retired from the Anglican department of evangelism fifteen years earlier, his retirement was anything but. He remained an itinerant speaker throughout Australia, with speaking engagements booked right until his dying days. He died serving the Lord—not playing golf, not on holiday. He did his duty, and now he is at rest.

## Rather, We Owe God

We forget that it is not God who owes us, but we who owe God—as our Creator, as our Redeemer, and especially as servants of the gospel.

### The Scandal of Unforgiveness (1-2)

> Things that cause people to sin are bound to come, but woe to that person through whom they come. It would be better for him to be thrown into the sea with a millstone tied around his neck than for him to cause one of these little ones to sin. (Luke 17:1-2)

Jesus speaks with shocking severity. The "scandal" he references is unforgiveness—refusing others the forgiveness God has extended to us.

### Forgive (3-4)

> If your brother sins, rebuke him, and if he repents, forgive him. If he sins against you seven times in a day, and seven times comes back to you and says, "I repent," forgive him. (Luke 17:3-4)

We must extend forgiveness to those who wrong us. This doesn't mean instant emotional healing, but it means heading toward reconciliation. We forgive because we have been forgiven.

When we forgive, we shouldn't view ourselves as heroes. Rather, we should remember the parable of the rich king and his servant in Matthew 18—we have been forgiven an enormous debt. How much more must we forgive our brothers?

But forgiveness extends beyond those who wrong us. In the context of Luke 16, the Rich Man and Lazarus, we see another dimension: we must tell others about God's forgiveness in Christ. Not telling someone about Christ isn't merely a passive sin of omission—it's a profound failure to offer access to God's mercy.

Even Penn Jillette, the atheist magician, understood this:

> If you believe that there's a heaven and a hell, and people could be going to hell or not getting eternal life, and you think that it's not really worth telling them this because it would make it socially awkward—how much do you have to hate somebody to not proselytize? If I believed, beyond the shadow of a doubt, that a truck was coming at you, and you didn't believe that truck was bearing down on you, there is a certain point where I tackle you. And this is more important than that.

By not telling people about Christ, we're indirectly hating them. We're causing them to stumble. We're denying them access to the forgiveness that alone saves.

As servants of the gospel, we owe God the proclamation of His forgiveness and reconciliation.

### The Problem Is Not Faith (5-6)

> The apostles said to the Lord, "Increase our faith!" He replied, "If you have faith as small as a mustard seed, you can say to this mulberry tree, 'Be uprooted and planted in the sea,' and it will obey you." (Luke 17:5-6)

The disciples' response reveals their misdiagnosis. They thought they lacked faith. But Jesus corrects them—the problem isn't faith. Faith as small as a mustard seed is sufficient. Even the tiniest faith, when directed at God's power, can accomplish the seemingly impossible.

The real issue is deeper.

### Thankfulness (7-15)

The passage transitions to an illustration that captures the heart of the problem:

> Now on his way to Jerusalem, Jesus traveled along the border between Samaria and Galilee. As he was going into a village, ten men who had leprosy met him. They stood at a distance and called out in a loud voice, "Jesus, Master, have pity on us!" When he saw them, he said, "Go, show yourselves to the priests." And as they went, they were cleansed. One of them, when he saw he was healed, came back, praising God in a loud voice. He threw himself at Jesus' feet and thanked him—and he was a Samaritan. Jesus asked, "Were not all ten cleansed? Where are the other nine? Was no one found to return and give praise to God except this foreigner?" Then he said to him, "Rise and go; your faith has made you well." (Luke 17:11-19)

Ten men were healed. Only one returned to give thanks—a Samaritan, an outsider. Where were the other nine?

The problem isn't faith. The problem is ingratitude. We take for granted the gospel that has saved our souls. We forget that not long ago, we were lost but were found. Or if we grew up in Christian families, we fail to remember how the gospel came to our families against great odds—how we were on the other side of that great chasm, desperately needing God's mercy, and He mercifully plucked us out. He gave us purpose. He gave us a reason to live.

Why do Christians serve? Not to earn our way to heaven. Not because we're trying to do works-righteousness. But out of gratefulness. Our service flows from a thankful heart that remembers what God has done.

Jesus is the person who did His duty and more—and He did it out of love for you and me. In view of His mercy, let us offer our lives as living sacrifices.

## Last World Problems

We are a spoiled generation—producing Peter Pans who refuse to grow up. We delay responsibility. Boys who don't want to be fathers. Girls who don't want to be mothers. We'll end up growing old without growing up.

We create modern versions of first world problems while the world desperately needs the gospel:

- "I can't be generous because I need to buy yet another phone."
- "I can't commit to summer missions because I'm booking flights to Korea."
- "I can't serve in this ministry because I'm skiing in Canada."
- "I don't have time—I only get four months off for holidays."
- "I'd hang out with that lonely person, but I don't like the food at their restaurant."

But these aren't first world problems. They're *last world problems*—the problems of those who've forgotten why they were redeemed.

We're born again to another world. We don't live for travel, money, food, wine, gadgets, or fashion. We've left the old world and its selfishness behind. Now we are servants of the gospel of forgiveness and reconciliation.

University is a great time to learn commitment. When campus groups recruit in February, come. Commit to it. Rearrange your timetable around their activities. Reach out to the lost on your campus.

No more entitlements. No more excuses. We have a duty. Let's go do it together.
