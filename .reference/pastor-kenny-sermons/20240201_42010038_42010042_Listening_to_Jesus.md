# Luke 10:38-42

**Title:** Listening to Jesus  
**Date:** 18 February 2024  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Contrast: Martha and Mary

> As Jesus and his disciples were on their way, he came to a village where a woman named Martha opened her home to him. (Luke 10:38)

Jesus arrives at Martha's house after sending out seventy-two disciples to prepare the way for him. Martha welcomes him warmly, opening her home with genuine hospitality. She represents the ideal response—someone ready to serve and provide.

> But Martha was distracted by all the preparations that had to be made. She came to him and asked, "Lord, don't you care that my sister has left me to do the work by myself? Tell her to help me!" (Luke 10:40)

Yet Martha becomes overwhelmed. The word "distracted" (*periespato*) means "drawn away"—her focus shifts from her guest to the endless tasks. She feels the weight of preparing the perfect meal alone, and frustration builds. Her complaint reveals the deeper issue: she interprets Jesus' silence as indifference. If he cared, wouldn't he help? Wouldn't Mary pitch in?

> "Martha, Martha," the Lord answered, "you are worried and upset about many things." (Luke 10:41)

Jesus responds with gentle correction. He sees her anxiety—the same word used in Philippians 4:6 for anxious worry. Martha is caught in the trap of overinvestment in external things, just like a mother at Christmas time, stressed about creating the perfect celebration while missing the joy of the moment.

## The Root Issue: Misplaced Priorities

The real problem isn't the work itself. Food and hospitality are good. The issue is that Martha has confused the means with the end. She's so focused on the meal that she's neglecting the reason for the meal—Jesus himself.

This connects to a deeper truth found in Deuteronomy 8:3:

> "He humbled you, causing you to hunger and then feeding you with manna, which neither you nor your ancestors had known, to teach you that man does not live on bread alone but on every word that comes from the mouth of the Lord."

We live in material abundance—in one of the most prosperous countries in the world—yet anxiety and depression grip us. Despite having everything, we lack peace. Why? Because we've inflated the importance of material things far beyond their actual value. We don't need just clothes; we need fashion and status. We don't need shelter; we need a house filled with things we don't use. We don't need food; we need culinary perfection. We chase experiences just to say we've had them. And all this striving leaves us exhausted.

But life is more than these things. The word of God speaks to how we live now and how we live eternally. That's what truly matters.

## Mary's Better Choice

> She had a sister called Mary, who sat at the Lord's feet listening to what he said. (Luke 10:39)

Mary chooses differently. She sits at Jesus' feet—the posture of a disciple, a place of humility and learning. This phrase echoes through Luke's gospel:

- The sinful woman who anointed Jesus' feet with perfume and tears (Luke 7:36-38)
- Jairus, who fell at Jesus' feet pleading for his daughter's life (Luke 8:41)

All these figures came to Jesus' feet in their need and desperation, and they found mercy.

Mary isn't in the kitchen. She isn't worried about food. She isn't distracted by the endless list of tasks. She's listening to Jesus himself—to his words, his teaching, his presence.

> "Blessed rather are those who hear the word of God and obey it." (Luke 11:28)

The privilege of listening to Jesus' words cannot be overstated. Even the Queen of Sheba traveled from the ends of the earth to hear Solomon's wisdom. How much greater is it to hear from one greater than Solomon?

And notice: Jesus' words have lasting power.

> "Heaven and earth will pass away, but my words will never pass away." (Luke 21:33)

## The Verdict

> "But few things are needed—or indeed only one. Mary has chosen what is better, and it will not be taken away from her." (Luke 10:42)

The "one thing" is sitting at Jesus' feet and listening to his word. This is what cannot be taken away. This is what matters eternally.

To understand the weight of this saying, consider: Jesus himself is present in Martha's home. He has arrived. The King of the universe is there. Would you ignore him to work in the kitchen? Of course not. Yet we do this spiritually all the time when we have his word—the Bible—and we find distractions instead.

This word built Western civilization through the wisdom of Jesus. This word gives hope beyond this earthly life through the death and resurrection of Jesus. And yet we remain anxious. That reveals what we truly believe about what matters.

## Living Out the Truth

The point isn't that work is bad or that we should neglect practical service. Rather, Jesus calls us to keep first things first. Mary's choice—listening to Jesus—is what sustains us spiritually and emotionally.

Some of us worry about work and promotion. Others are anxious about property and investments. Many fret over our children's education and their future. Jesus says: don't be anxious.

> "Therefore I tell you, do not worry about your life, what you will eat; or about your body, what you will wear. For life is more than food, and the body more than clothes. Consider the ravens: They do not sow or reap, they have no storeroom or barn; yet God feeds them. And how much more valuable you are than birds! Who of you by worrying can add a single hour to your life?" (Luke 12:22-25)

The answer is simple: no one. Worry accomplishes nothing.

This doesn't mean we ignore practical needs. But it means we recognize them for what they are—temporary provisions for temporary bodies. Meanwhile, the word of God addresses both our present and our eternal future. It matters infinitely more.

Like a baby who is fed and kept warm but receives no words, no attention, no eye contact—that child will not flourish. Food alone isn't enough. We need the word that comes from the mouth of God.

## Application

**Listen to Jesus.** This means reading and studying the Bible—not just as a rule, but as an act of devotion. Sundays are a gift: a time to gather and hear God's word explained, to pray in response, to be encouraged alongside others.

**Keep the important things important.** When we gather as a church, the priority is God's word. We provide food and hospitality—generously—but as a means to an end: so people will stay, listen, and encounter Jesus through his word.

**Find your contentment there.** Whatever anxieties press upon you today, remember: you are sorted for this life through practical provision, and you are sorted for the next life through the cross and resurrection of Jesus. That's what should relieve your anxiety.

Man does not live by bread alone. He lives by the word of God. Remember that as you enjoy this meal.
