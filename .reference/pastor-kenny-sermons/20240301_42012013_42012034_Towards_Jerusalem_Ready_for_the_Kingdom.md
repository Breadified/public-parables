# Luke 12:13-34

## When Will He Find Us Worrying?

Jesus is traveling toward Jerusalem, preaching the kingdom of heaven, healing the sick, and casting out demons. As he walks, he is interrupted by a man in the crowd with a very earthly concern.

> **Luke 12:13** "Someone in the crowd said to him, 'Teacher, tell my brother to divide the inheritance with me.'"

### Jesus Refuses to Divide Possessions

The word "divide" (Greek: *meros*) means to share or apportion. One brother has received an inheritance and refuses to split it—understandably, since a whole property is more valuable than divided parts. The other brother appeals to Jesus as judge to force the division.

But Jesus declines to intervene:

> **Luke 12:14** "Jesus replied, 'Man, who appointed me a judge or an arbiter between you?'"

The word "arbiter" carries the same root: a divider of possessions. This is not Jesus' calling. Instead, he launches into a profound teaching about money and the human heart.

> **Luke 12:15** "Then he said to them, 'Watch out! Be on your guard against all kinds of greed; life does not consist in an abundance of possessions.'"

The warning is sharp and urgent. Covetousness—the desire to possess more and more for oneself—is a spiritual danger. Life is not measured by what we own.

### The Parable of the Rich Fool

Jesus illustrates this with a parable about a man whose land produces an abundant harvest:

> **Luke 12:16-19** "The ground of a certain rich man yielded an abundant harvest. He thought to himself, 'What shall I do? I have no place to store my crops.' Then he said, 'This is what I'll do. I will tear down my barns and build bigger ones, and there I will store my surplus grain and my goods. And I'll say to myself, "You have plenty of grain laid up for many years. Take life easy; eat, drink and be merry."'"

Notice what this man does: he talks to himself. His entire internal conversation is with himself—my crops, my barns, my goods, myself. There is no thought for others, no gratitude to God, no recognition that his abundance came from the ground, not from his own effort.

His fundamental error is this: he believes life consists in the abundance of possessions.

> **Luke 12:20-21** "But God said to him, 'You fool! This very night your life will be demanded from you. Then who will get what you have prepared for yourself? This is how it will be with whoever stores up things for themselves but is not rich toward God.'"

The problem is not wealth itself—it is what he did with it. He stored up for himself. He was not rich toward God. He forgot that his life belonged to God. He forgot that the abundance was God's gift, not his achievement.

### Living in a Materialistic World

We live in a world captivated by consumption. Statistics show that most Australians believe our consumption habits harm society, yet nearly half still feel compelled to buy new things. The psychological rush of purchasing—retail therapy—keeps us returning for more.

We accumulate beyond measure. Closets overflow into spare rooms. Garages overflow into driveways. We build larger and larger houses, not for more people, but for more possessions—kitchens filled with appliances we rarely use, counters laden with gadgets.

Jesus' critique is precise: does our life consist in the abundance of possessions? Matthew records his teaching:

> **Matthew 6:19** "Do not store up for yourselves treasures on earth, where moths and vermin destroy, and where thieves break in and steal."

Eventually, the brand new clothes fade, become fashionable again, then obsolete. We must ask ourselves: Do I really need it? What will I have to give up for it? Am I turning my home into a barn?

## The Call to Not Worry

With covetousness comes worry. These are intimately connected. The Greek word for "worry" (*merimnaō*) means literally to be "divided in mind"—to be split, distracted, pulled in different directions.

> **Luke 12:22-23** "Then Jesus said to his disciples: 'Therefore I tell you, do not worry about your life, what you will eat; or about your body, what you will wear. For life is more than food, and the body more than clothes.'"

Humans alone among God's creatures worry. We possess the terrible capacity for catastrophic thinking, imagining disasters that will never occur. Studies suggest eighty percent of our worries concern things that never happen.

### Consider the Ravens

> **Luke 12:24** "Consider the ravens: They do not sow or reap, they have no storeroom or barn; yet God feeds them. And how much more valuable you are than birds!"

The raven is a common scavenger—an unclean bird according to Leviticus. Yet God cares for it. The Bible testifies to this:

> **Job 38:41** "Who provides food for the raven when its young cry out to God and wander about for lack of food?"

> **Psalm 147:9** "He provides food for the cattle and for the young ravens when they call."

Using the logic of lesser to greater: if God loves and sustains even the lowly raven, how much more will he care for you, his image-bearer?

### Consider the Wildflowers

> **Luke 12:27-28** "Consider how the wild flowers grow. They do not labor or spin. Yet I tell you, not even Solomon in all his splendor was dressed like one of these. If that is how God clothes the grass of the field, which is here today, and tomorrow is thrown into the fire, how much more will he clothe you—you of little faith!"

The grass of the field is temporary, fleeting. Yet God adorns it with beauty. If he clothes what is here today and gone tomorrow, how much more will he clothe you, who will remain for eighty years?

The problem is not God's provision. The problem is us. "Little faith" means a divided mind—torn between trusting God's promises and trusting our own fearful imaginings.

> **Romans 14:23** "Everything that does not come from faith is sin."

### Don't Be Like the World

> **Luke 12:29-30** "And do not set your heart on what you will eat or drink; do not worry about it. For the pagan world runs after all such things, and your Father knows that you need them."

The nations of the world must seek anxiously because their gods are not fathers. Their gods are harsh masters, slave drivers. They must worry about providing even for their gods. (In some cultures, people burn paper money to appease ancestral spirits—such is the desperation of those without a loving Father.)

If we worry like the world, we will face the world's problems. Anxiety feeds hoarding. The English word "worry" originally meant to seize by the throat and tear. Worry strangles us.

> **Matthew 13:22** "The seed falling among the thorns refers to someone who hears the word, but the worries of this life and the deceitfulness of wealth choke the word, making it unfruitful."

A divided kingdom cannot stand. A divided mind cannot stand.

## Seek First the Kingdom

Rather than fighting worry by merely trying not to worry, Jesus calls us to something positive:

> **Luke 12:31** "But seek his kingdom, and these things will be given to you as well."

King Solomon exemplifies this principle. When God asked what he desired, Solomon asked for wisdom to govern justly—not for long life, wealth, or the death of his enemies. God was pleased:

> **1 Kings 3:11-13** "So God said to him, 'Since you have asked for this and not for long life or wealth for yourself, nor have asked for the death of your enemies but for discernment in administering justice, I will do what you have asked. I will give you a wise and discerning heart... Moreover, I will give you what you have not asked for—both wealth and honor—so that in your lifetime you will have no equal among kings.'"

This is the promise: seek the kingdom first. Make God's priorities your priorities. Live God's way. Leave the supply of other things to God—and he will provide.

> **Luke 12:32-34** "Do not be afraid, little flock, for your Father has been pleased to give you the kingdom. Sell your possessions and give to the poor. Provide purses for yourselves that will not wear out, a treasure in heaven that will never fail, where no thief comes near and no moth destroys. For where your treasure is, there your heart will be also."

"Sell your possessions and give to the poor" does not mean vow yourself to poverty. That would create a vicious cycle. Rather, it calls for simplicity and charity—freedom from materialism. The contrast is between hoarding in barns and distributing to those in need.

Jesus comforts us: "Do not be afraid, little flock." He calls us lambs. The kingdom is already yours—it is the Father's good pleasure to give it to you.

This is why Jesus could not be bothered mediating between the brothers. They were quarreling over peanuts—a relatively tiny earthly inheritance—while completely missing the inheritance that matters: the kingdom of God.

## Two Practical Applications

### Investing in God's Kingdom

The church is eager for God's kingdom to grow. We seek to reach more believers and equip them for ministry. This year, we hope to employ permanent staff to serve alongside our student ministers. Additionally, one family has already sold possessions to launch our building fund, and we continue saving toward future expansion.

If you wish to give more to reflect changed circumstances, or to begin giving, this is a tangible way to invest in the kingdom's advance.

### Young People and Simplicity

To young people and young couples, I encourage you: do not let material things become obstacles to seeking the kingdom.

The pressure begins early. You must get into an OC school—but why? To attend a selective school. But why? To earn a degree. But why? To get a high-paying job. But why? To buy clothes, phones, cars, houses. And to attract someone who wants you for those possessions.

None of these goals are inherently wrong. Hard work and perseverance are virtues. But consider the reason. When you trace it backward, the ultimate purpose seems to be: to buy more.

At Castle Hill High, I spoke with a Year 9 girls' group. When asked about their life goals:
- What do you want to do? *To be rich.*
- What's the purpose of life? *To be rich.*
- Want to be a lawyer? *To be rich.*
- Want to be a model? *To be rich.*
- Want to be a builder? *To be rich.*

Jesus' critique is accurate: our lives do seem to consist in the abundance of possessions.

South Korea, one of the richest nations on earth (per capita income $35,000), has seen marriages drop forty percent in ten years. Women delay childbearing until an average age of 33.6—the oldest in the OECD. The government has invested $270 billion in incentive programs, yet couples resist. The excuse? Children are too expensive. Education is expensive. Housing is expensive. Childcare is expensive. And taking a mother out of the workforce costs half the family income.

But this is not new. What has changed is our spending habits. We want more—from luxury brands to cheap trinkets. We upgrade perfectly good appliances. We fly around the world multiple times yearly. We consume and consume and consume.

How is it that the richer we become, the less able we feel to have children? The reality is that nothing of importance has changed. Motherhood has always involved sacrifice. Houses have always been expensive (in Manhattan, no one buys; they rent). One generation has always had to defer consumption so the next could thrive.

What has changed is our unwillingness to sacrifice our lifestyle. We refuse to give up our spending for the next generation.

## The Question Before Us

What does your life consist of? What are your true priorities? Are you seeking the abundance of things, or are you seeking the kingdom?

Is your life made up by the number of possessions you own? Or do you belong to a Father who delights to give you his kingdom?
