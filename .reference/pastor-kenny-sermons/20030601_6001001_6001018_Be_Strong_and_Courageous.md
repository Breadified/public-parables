# Joshua 1:1-18

**Title:** Be Strong and Courageous
**Date:** 12 June 2003
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Context: A Historic Parallel

This week marks the 72nd anniversary of D-Day—June 6, 1944. When the Allied forces launched the largest invasion in world history against Nazi-occupied France, waves of paratroopers and soldiers descended on the beaches of Normandy. But for the first assault companies, it was a catastrophe.

The pre-assault bombing meant to silence German artillery missed because of cloud cover. The amphibious tanks that should have destroyed enemy positions sank in rough seas. As soldiers poured from their landing craft, German machine guns—engineered to fire twice as fast as Allied weapons—cut them down. Those who tried to escape by jumping overboard drowned under the weight of their equipment in just a few meters of water. Some entire companies lost everyone in the first five minutes without firing a shot. The Allies lost 5,000 people in those first few hours.

Yet despite this carnage, the soldiers pressed forward. By day's end, they had established a beachhead. Within 77 days, they would help win the entire war. Those men had to be strong and courageous. Today, we hear another story of invasion—and the same call to courage.

## 1. The Promised Land (Joshua 1:1-5)

> After the death of Moses the servant of the Lord, the Lord said to Joshua son of Nun, Moses' aide: "Moses my servant is dead. Now then, you and all these people, get ready to cross the Jordan River into the land I am about to give to them—to the Israelites.

Forty years have passed since the Israelites escaped Egyptian slavery. Now they finally stand at the threshold of the promised land. Moses, the leader who brought them out, has died. Joshua must take his place. But the heart of the land lies across the Jordan River—they must cross it with an army of 500,000 men.

> I will give you every place where you set your foot, as I promised Moses. Your territory will extend from the desert to Lebanon, and from the great river, the Euphrates—all the Hittite country—to the Mediterranean Sea in the west.

This wasn't just the traditional boundaries of Israel. The promise extended from south to north (Lebanon), east (the Euphrates), and west (the Mediterranean)—territory that had been promised to Abraham centuries before.

### The Covenant Behind the Promise

God's promise goes back to Genesis 15, when the Lord spoke to Abraham:

> "Do not be afraid, Abram. I am your shield, your very great reward."

Abraham questioned how this could happen—he had no children. God took him outside and said, "Look up at the sky and count the stars. So shall your offspring be." And remarkably: "Abram believed the Lord, and he credited it to him as righteousness."

God then made a formal covenant—a blood covenant—with Abraham. In that ancient ritual, animals were cut in half and the covenant partners would walk between the pieces, saying, "If I break this promise, may I be cut in half like these animals." But God alone walked between the pieces (appearing as a smoking firepot with a blazing torch), while Abraham slept. This was a one-sided, unconditional promise. God would accomplish it.

God also told Abraham: "Know for certain that for four hundred years your descendants will be strangers in a country not their own and that they will be enslaved and mistreated there. But I will punish the nation they serve as slaves, and afterward they will come out with great possessions." This prophecy describes Egypt's bondage and the exodus.

Now, after 450 years of waiting—from Abraham's call to Joshua's conquest—the promise is finally being fulfilled.

### God's Faithfulness

> No one will be able to stand against you all the days of your life. As I was with Moses, so I will be with you; I will never leave you nor forsake you.

When God makes a promise, He takes responsibility for fulfilling it. It's like having a wealthy, generous relative in your family. When they're in town and invite you to dinner, you don't worry—they always pay. They cover everything: the organization, the food, the bill, even the cleanup. You simply enjoy their company and their provision.

Or like coming home to your mother. When she invites you over for dinner, she takes care of everything—the cooking, the dishes, even gives you a doggie bag to take home.

Similarly, when God makes a promise, you don't worry. He'll organize it, provide for it, cover all the costs. Your only responsibility is to trust His word.

## 2. Be Strong and Courageous (Joshua 1:6-9)

> Be strong and courageous, because you will lead these people to inherit the land I swore to their ancestors to give them.

> Have I not commanded you? Be strong and courageous. Do not be afraid; do not be discouraged, for the Lord your God will be with you wherever you go.

This command appears three times in the context—once from God to Joshua, once from Moses to the nation, and once from Moses directly to Joshua. The Hebrew words capture the full meaning: *chazaq* (be firm, confident, strong) and *amatz* (be stout, bold, alert). Together they mean: hold your ground, don't waver.

### A Lesson from the Past

Why does God repeat this command so emphatically? Because the last time Israel faced this moment, they failed.

> They gave Moses this account: "We went into the land to which you sent us, and it does flow with milk and honey! Here is its fruit. But the people who live there are powerful, and the cities are fortified and very large. We even saw descendants of Anak there."

The twelve spies reported the truth: the land was good, but the inhabitants were strong. Then came the crucial moment:

> Then Caleb silenced the people before Moses and said, "We should go up and take possession of the land, for we can certainly do it." But the men who had gone up with him said, "We can't attack those people; they are stronger than we are." And they spread among the Israelites a bad report about the land they had explored. They said, "The land we explored devours those living in it. We saw the Nephilim there. We seemed like grasshoppers in our own eyes, and we looked the same to them."

Fear conquered faith. The people wept and said: "Why is the Lord bringing us to this land only to let us fall by the sword? Wouldn't it be better for us to go back to Egypt?" They wanted to choose a new leader and return to slavery.

Only Joshua and Caleb stood firm:

> Joshua son of Nun and Caleb son of Jephunneh said to the entire Israelite assembly, "The land we passed through and explored is exceedingly good. If the Lord is pleased with us, he will lead us into that land, a land flowing with milk and honey, and will give it to us. Only do not rebel against the Lord. And do not be afraid of the people of the land, because we will devour them. Their protection is gone, but the Lord is with us. Do not be afraid of them."

The nation chose fear over faith. That decision cost them forty years of wilderness wandering. Everyone over twenty who refused to trust God died without seeing the promised land—except Joshua and Caleb.

Now, Joshua must lead a new generation into the same land. God's repeated command—"Be strong and courageous"—echoes that painful history. The outcome depends not on military prowess or the size of the enemy, but on whether they will trust God this time.

### What True Courage Looks Like

> Be strong and very courageous. Be careful to obey all the law my servant Moses gave you; do not turn from it to the right or to the left, that you may be successful wherever you go. Keep this Book of the Law always on your lips; meditate on it day and night, so that you may be careful to do everything written in it. Then you will be prosperous and successful.

Courage here isn't about the battlefield—it's about obedience. God asks Joshua to be courageous in following His Word, meditating on it continually, and doing everything written in it. Success comes through faithfulness, not military victory.

### A Modern Illustration

Imagine standing at the top of the Questacon free fall slide in Canberra. You hang from a bar overlooking a six-meter drop. The physics are flawless—the curved slide will safely decelerate you and bring you gently to a horizontal landing. It's completely safe. But hanging there, looking down, you need courage to let go.

It's the same with roller coasters. Even if you take your hands off, the engineering will keep you safe. The danger isn't real. But you still need courage to trust the system.

Joshua faced something similar. God had already promised victory. The outcome was assured. But Joshua and the people still needed the courage to step forward in faith, to obey God despite their fears.

### Courage in Our Own Lives

What frightens you? Perhaps:

**Evangelism.** We might fear rejection or ridicule. But the elect are already known to God—we simply need to share the gospel courageously. We don't have to manipulate people into believing. If they're interested, wonderful. If not, that's between them and God. But we can't let fear silence us.

**Standing for biblical truth when it costs.** In our culture, certain truths face fierce opposition. It may cost us friendships, reputation, or career advancement. We might be tempted to stay silent for safety. But Christian courage means speaking truth—lovingly but clearly—regardless of earthly cost, because we trust God to sustain us.

As John Piper has written: "Christian courage is the willingness to say and do the right thing regardless of the earthly cost, because God promises to help you and save you on account of Christ."

The opposite of courage is cowardice—sinful unbelief. This is what condemned the generation in Numbers. They didn't lack intelligence or size or resources. They lacked faith in God's promise.

### Courage for Today

Consider those facing a cultural moment where speaking up could cost them. Some Christians discourage this: "Don't make yourself such a target. We're going to lose anyway. Let's just cultivate friendships." But there's a tragic irony in this advice. Some Christians lecture others about their tone and approach—from the comfort of their own chair, risking nothing themselves. 

If we believe something is true and important, do we run away? Or do we stand? Yes, let's sharpen each other in how we communicate. But let's do that while both standing up, not one person lecturing from the sidelines.

It's like the tribes of Reuben and Gad discouraging the conquest from across the Jordan, complaining about how things were going. Their discouragement spread to the whole nation and cost forty years. But contrast that with genuine encouragement—when your brothers and sisters back you up as you stand for truth, how strengthened you become.

## 3. We Need to Encourage Each Other (Joshua 1:10-18)

> So Joshua ordered the officers of the people: "Go through the camp and tell the people, 'Get your provisions ready. Three days from now you will cross the Jordan here to go in and take possession of the land the Lord your God is giving you for your own.'"

Joshua moves forward with confidence. But there's a complication:

> But to the Reubenites, the Gadites and the half-tribe of Manasseh, Joshua said, "Remember the command that Moses the servant of the Lord gave you after he said, 'The Lord your God will give you rest by giving you this land.' Your wives, your children and your livestock may stay in the land that Moses gave you east of the Jordan, but all your fighting men, ready for battle, must cross over ahead of your fellow Israelites. You are to help them until the Lord gives them rest, as he has done for you, and until they too have taken possession of the land the Lord your God is giving them."

These two and a half tribes had chosen their inheritance on the east side of the Jordan because their herds and flocks were large and the land was suitable for livestock. Understandably, they preferred not to cross the river and fight. But Joshua reminds them: you must support your brothers in the conquest first. Don't rest until everyone is safely in the promised land.

This is critical. The absence of these tribes would be discouraging. Seeing others give up, seeing soldiers run away—this saps courage. We can either share bravery or spread cowardice. There's no neutral ground.

But look at their response:

> Then they answered Joshua, "Whatever you have commanded us we will do, and wherever you send us we will go. Just as we fully obeyed Moses, so we will obey you. Only may the Lord your God be with you as he was with Moses. Whoever rebels against your word and does not obey it, whatever you may command them, will be put to death. Only be strong and courageous!"

Remarkably, they don't just comply. They turn around and encourage Joshua: "Only be strong and courageous!" Even as they commit to a burden, they lift up their leader with words of strength.

### The Power of Mutual Encouragement

As Paul would later write:

> See to it, brothers and sisters, that none of you has a sinful, unbelieving heart that turns away from the living God. But encourage one another daily, as long as it is called "Today," so that none of you may be hardened by sin's deceitfulness.

You might be scared yourself, but show courage for others. Encourage them. Support those trying to do the right thing.

When someone stands up for biblical truth and faces criticism from the world, our first response should be to back them up, not to distance ourselves or lecture about their tone. When you post about faith or biblical values on social media and others attack you, but your friends rally to your defense—doesn't that encourage you deeply? Then you must do the same for others. When anyone stands up to obey God, encourage them. Let them know the fight isn't alone.

In contrast, the culture war that rages on social media is brutal—and tragically, much of the discouragement comes from other Christians, not just from the world. But genuine community means standing together, sharpening each other while both engaged in the struggle, not criticizing from safety.

## 4. The Ultimate Model: Jesus, the True Joshua

> The Lord has one who is powerful and strong.

Jesus is the Joshua who is powerful and strong. He displayed courage on the cross, taking all the enemy's ammunition, enduring all of Satan's assault. In that event, Satan was mortally wounded.

## The Closing Illustration: D-Day's Second Wave

Despite the catastrophe of the initial assault, the soldiers of D-Day advanced. By day's end, they had established a beachhead. But halfway through, the Allied commanders had doubts. Looking at the casualty rates, they considered pulling back.

Then they showed strength and courage. They led forward. And what they didn't know was providential: by that point, the Germans had run out of ammunition. Their efficient machine guns had consumed bullets twice as fast as standard weapons. The subsequent waves faced increasingly lighter fire.

As the day progressed, it got easier. But that wasn't the time to give up! The beach was theirs. They just needed courage to advance and claim what had already been secured.

It's the same spiritually. Jesus has already died for our sakes. He was the first wave, absorbing all the enemy's fire. Satan has been mortally wounded. His ammunition is spent.

Now is not the time to fear. How foolish would it be, after the greatest sacrifice has been given and the enemy is running out of ammunition, to give up now? No. Now is the time to have strength and courage and advance.

## The Challenge

The call to Joshua is the call to us: **Obey God. Be strong and courageous. And encourage one another as we go.**
