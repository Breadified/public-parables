# Genesis 22:1-19

**Title:** Sacrificing the Youth
**Date:** 6 March 2022
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Test of Faith

> Some time later God tested Abraham. He said to him, "Abraham!"
> "Here I am," he replied.

God's command to Abraham is shocking and incomprehensible: take his beloved son Isaac—the child of promise, the fulfillment of decades of waiting—and sacrifice him on Mount Moriah. To understand Abraham's response, we must first grasp what Isaac meant to him and Sarah.

Isaac's name means "laughter" in Hebrew, a reminder of the joy Abraham and Sarah felt when God finally fulfilled His promise. After years of disappointment, after Abraham's attempt to create a son through Hagar, Isaac finally arrived. He was their only son, their sole heir, the repository of all their hopes and God's covenant promises. Yet Abraham received this devastating command without hesitation.

### Abraham's Unwavering Obedience

> Take your son, your only son, whom you love—Isaac—and go to the region of Moriah. Sacrifice him there as a burnt offering on a mountain I will show you.

What strikes us most is Abraham's response: "Early the next morning Abraham got up and loaded his donkey." There is no delay, no argument, no request for clarification. Abraham moves with purpose—a striking change from the man who had previously acted with doubt, who had laughed at God's promise, who had tried to manufacture his own solution through Hagar.

The phrase "Here I am" echoes his first response to God's call. Abraham is a dutiful soldier of the faith.

### The Cruel Details of the Journey

Abraham takes everything necessary: a donkey for the journey, two servants, and wood for the burnt offering. Isaac himself carries the wood—an unbearable detail that foreshadows what will come. As they walk, Isaac asks the question that cuts to Abraham's heart:

> "Father?" "Yes, my son?" Abraham replied.
> "The fire and wood are here," Isaac said, "but where is the lamb for the burnt offering?"

Abraham's answer—"God himself will provide the lamb for the burnt offering, my son"—reveals the depth of his faith. He does not know how God will resolve this impossible situation, yet he speaks with certainty. The two of them continue together, bound by purpose and trust.

On the third day, Abraham sees Mount Moriah in the distance. Three days to think, to reconsider, to question. Yet he tells his servants: "Stay here with the donkey while I and the boy go over there. We will worship and then we will come back to you." Notice the promise: "we will come back." Abraham speaks in the first-person plural, not singular. He believes he will return with Isaac.

## Why Abraham's Faith Was Tested

This test reveals something profound about Abraham's heart. It was not genuinely about killing Isaac—God would never command such a thing. Rather, the test asked: Will Abraham trust God's promises alone, or will he continue to trust in himself?

Throughout his life, Abraham had struggled with self-reliance:
- He laughed when God promised he could father children at age ninety-nine
- He took matters into his own hands with Hagar, creating Ishmael
- He trusted his own instincts and wisdom rather than God's word

Now, with Isaac—the very fulfillment of God's promise—on the altar, Abraham faces his ultimate test. As the writer of Hebrews explains:

> By faith Abraham, when God tested him, offered Isaac as a sacrifice. He who had embraced the promises was about to sacrifice his one and only son, even though God had said to him, "It is through Isaac that your offspring will be reckoned."

Yet Abraham reasoned that God could even raise the dead, and so in a manner of speaking he did receive Isaac back from death. Abraham's faith had matured beyond hope in God's promises to faith in God's power over death itself.

## The Nature of Testing

A test is fundamentally a choice. We can only pick one path when push comes to shove. The world speaks of nuance and balance, but in times of genuine pressure, we reveal what we truly believe. This applies to all of us:

- Career versus God
- Money versus God
- Success versus God
- Safety versus God
- Our children versus God

For parents especially, the test becomes acute. When we make our ultimate priority the safety or earthly success of our children rather than their walk with Christ, we have failed the test. We are saying, in effect, that we do not want them to be Christians—because God's call is to follow Him first, above all.

The COVID pandemic was such a test. When offered the opportunity to reopen our churches and spread the Gospel, what excuse did we hear most? The protection of the vulnerable. In prioritizing fear over faith, we revealed what we truly believed about God's sovereignty. Some sent their children to school but kept them from church. Does this not show a certain priority about what we believe matters?

Each year presents tests:
- In your child's HSC year, will you prioritize their exam success or their fellowship with other Christians?
- During Selective school testing or OC exams, will you encourage them to study textbooks or read Scripture?
- When a friendship group goes the wrong direction, will you defend your child or help them stand firm in faith?

There is never a "safe" time to be a Christian. If safety and earthly success are your number one priorities as a parent, then you are not prioritizing what God calls you to prioritize.

### Children as Idols

We must examine our hearts honestly. Do our children function as idols in our lives?

- **We live vicariously through them.** We relive our past through their achievements in sports or academics. Their victories become our victories in a twisted way. We gain a sense of identity and worth through their success.

- **We compromise our standards to please them.** We bend rules and allow them to get away with things we know are wrong. We want to be their friend rather than their parent. We stop having the tough conversations. We never say no.

- **We constantly worry about them.** We choose worry over trust, which means we are really saying that God is not fully capable of caring for our children as well as we are. When we choose worry, our children control our thinking rather than the Lord and His Word.

- **We feel overwhelming abandonment at the thought of them leaving.** This is not normal parental feeling but a refusal to relinquish our children's futures to the Lord. The child is filling a hole in our life that was never meant to be filled by them.

- **We consistently side with them over our spouse.** The child has replaced the spouse in God's desire for oneness. We defend them against our spouse and believe our spouse does not understand them as we do.

> Little children, guard yourselves from idols.

We want the best for our children. The best thing we can give them is to teach them to follow Christ, and then to send them off—even when it costs us—so that their faith can be tested and proven genuine.

## The Provision

Just as Abraham raised the knife, the angel of the Lord called out:

> "Abraham! Abraham!" "Here I am," he replied.
> "Do not lay a hand on the boy," he said. "Do not do anything to him. Now I know that you fear God, because you have not withheld from me your son, your only son."

This command answers a critical question: Is God evil? No. God is not. Isaac was not harmed. This was a test unique to Abraham in salvation history—a description of what God did, not a prescription for our actions. Abraham heard God's voice directly; we do not have that luxury. If anyone claims to have a vision from God to harm their children, that is a false vision. They should be reported to the police and the child rescued immediately.

God subsequently revealed His will through commandments:

> You shall not murder.

> Do not give any of your children to be sacrificed to Molek, for you must not profane the name of your God.

Then Abraham looked up and saw a ram caught by its horns in a thicket. He took the ram and sacrificed it as a burnt offering instead of his son.

> So Abraham called that place The Lord Will Provide. And to this day it is said, "On the mountain of the Lord it will be provided."

God provided a substitute. A sacrifice was still needed, but not Isaac. This principle appears throughout Scripture:

> No temptation has overtaken you except what is common to mankind. And God is faithful; he will not let you be tempted beyond what you can bear. But when you are tempted, he will also provide a way out so that you can endure it.

## The Foreshadow: Jesus as the Ultimate Sacrifice

But the ram was not the final provision. Genesis 22 points forward 4,000 years to Jesus Christ.

The ram appears because the Lamb of God had not yet come. When John the Baptist later pointed to Jesus, he cried out: "Look, the Lamb of God, who takes away the sin of the world!" The parallels are unmistakable:

- Abraham's "one and only son" mirrors God's "one and only Son"
- Isaac carried the wood for his own sacrifice; Jesus carried the wood of the cross
- The third day marked Abraham's hope in resurrection; the third day marked Christ's resurrection
- The sacrifice was for the penalty of our sins and our idolatry

What was a test for Abraham the father became a reality for God the Father. God did not withhold His own Son. He allowed the sacrifice to be completed. What Abraham was spared from doing, God actually did—out of love for us.

> For God so loved the world that he gave his one and only Son, that whoever believes in him shall not perish but have eternal life.

The command was fulfilled, not by Isaac, but by Jesus.

## The Blessing

Abraham passed the test. More importantly, God passed His own test. The angel called a second time:

> I swear by myself, declares the Lord, that because you have done this and have not withheld your son, your only son, I will surely bless you and make your descendants as numerous as the stars in the sky and as the sand on the seashore. Your descendants will take possession of the cities of their enemies, and through your offspring all nations on earth will be blessed, because you have obeyed me.

From one man, considered as good as dead, came descendants as numerous as the stars of heaven and the grains of sand by the seashore. This is the hope of the resurrection—not just Abraham's faith in God's power to raise the dead, but our faith that through Christ's sacrifice and resurrection, all nations will be blessed.

## A Word on Modern Sacrifice

We do not kill our children. But we do raise and train them, and we send them away as willing adults who will sacrifice for others. As parents and as a church community, we must support and encourage them in this calling. The greatest gift we can give them is not safety or earthly success, but the faith to follow Christ, whatever the cost.

> These have come so that the proven genuineness of your faith—of greater worth than gold, which perishes even though refined by fire—may result in praise, glory and honor when Jesus Christ is revealed.
