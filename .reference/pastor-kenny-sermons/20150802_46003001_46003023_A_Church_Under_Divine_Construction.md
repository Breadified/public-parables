# 1 Corinthians 3:1-23

**Title:** A Church Under Divine Construction  
**Date:** August 2, 2015  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Opening

Heavenly Father, we thank you for our church and for the fruit of the labors of many people before us and with us. We pray that this part of the Bible might help us be a better church. For your son's sake. Amen.

## 1. A Divided People (vv. 1-8)

The Corinthian church was facing a serious problem: division. Different groups were forming around different leaders.

> For when one says, "I follow Paul," and another, "I follow Apollos," are you not mere human beings? (v. 4)

Different factions had emerged—some following Paul, who founded the church, and others following Apollos. We know little about Apollos beyond what Paul tells us, but Acts describes him as a Jew from Alexandria, an eloquent speaker, and a man well versed in Scripture. Perhaps his reputation for wisdom sparked the very divisions Paul addresses throughout this letter.

**The Problem: Acting Like the Flesh, Not the Spirit**

Paul diagnoses the root issue. The church is acting according to worldly ways—the exact opposite of living by the Spirit.

> Brothers and sisters, I could not address you as people who live by the Spirit but as people who are still worldly—mere infants in Christ. I gave you milk, not solid food, for you were not yet ready for it. Indeed, you are still not ready. You are still worldly. For since there is jealousy and quarreling among you, are you not worldly? Are you not acting like mere humans? (vv. 1-3)

"Worldly" appears three times in these verses. It means acting according to the ways of the world—opposed to living by the nature of Christ and the mind of Christ. This is a contrast between a way of life and a way of death.

Galatians 5:16-23 shows us what this looks like. The flesh produces "hatred, discord, jealousy, fits of rage, selfish ambition, dissensions, factions and envy." But the Spirit produces "love, joy, peace, forbearance, kindness, goodness, faithfulness, gentleness and self-control."

**Babies, Not Mature Believers**

Paul calls the Corinthians babies—and this is meant as rebuke, not affection. While the Bible elsewhere uses baby imagery positively (Jesus said "become like little children," and Paul expressed tender care for the Thessalonians as a nursing mother), here Paul uses it as an insult. The Corinthians think they're acting with sophistication, but they're engaging in immature behavior: quarreling over who's popular, jostling for position, keeping score of slights. It's high school politics dressed up in spiritual language.

Like babies, they're selfish. They think only of their own needs and their own group's benefit, not the greater good. James asks the right question: "What causes fights and quarrels among you? Don't they come from your desires that battle within you?" (James 4:1)

The irony is devastating: the Corinthians think they're wise and spiritual, but Paul exposes them as acting exactly like those who do not have the Spirit.

**Application: The Problem of Human Leaders**

This reveals an inherent danger in any church: human leaders are still under the flesh, prone to ego and wrong desires. They're vulnerable to the very worldliness that divides believers.

Leadership can become worldly in how it solves problems. It can become worldly in how it manages people. It can become worldly in who rises to positions of authority. And when we take this worldliness into our disputes, we create splits that wound not just our congregation but neighboring churches too—sowing seeds of dissension wherever we go.

Our leaders are prone to this. I am prone to it. And you, as you take the lead to serve each other, are prone to it.

The solution? Ask God to reveal the bigger picture beyond our small conflicts.

## 2. One Field, One Building, One Temple (vv. 9, 16-23)

There is something far more important than individual egos.

> For we are co-workers in God's service; you are God's field, God's building. (v. 9)

**Multiple Workers, One Project**

While there are many workers, there is only one field and only one building. These are metaphors for the church—God's church, not ours.

A field is "an area of land used for cultivation." It's been worked on, tended, prepared for harvest. The building metaphor suggests something permanent, something being constructed according to a plan. Both images emphasize that the church is God's work, not humanity's possession.

**God's Temple**

Most crucially, Paul calls the church God's temple:

> Don't you know that you yourselves are God's temple and that God's Spirit dwells in your midst? (v. 16)

The temple originally referred to the Jerusalem temple—God's dwelling place. But Jesus became the temple, the one "torn down but rebuilt in three days." Through Christ, we inherit the privilege of becoming God's temple ourselves.

Peter captures this beautifully: "You also, like living stones, are being built into a spiritual house to be a holy priesthood, offering spiritual sacrifices acceptable to God through Jesus Christ" (1 Peter 2:5).

Paul's language echoes throughout Scripture. Hebrews tells us "we are his house, if indeed we hold firmly to our confidence." Ephesians speaks of how "the whole building is joined together and rises to become a holy temple in the Lord. And in him you too are being built together to become a dwelling in which God lives by his Spirit" (Ephesians 2:21-22).

Through God's Spirit, He dwells with us. The church is God's project—His construction, His dwelling place.

**The Seriousness of Harming the Temple**

This is why Paul's warning is so severe:

> If anyone destroys God's temple, God will destroy that person; for God's temple is sacred, and you together are that temple. (v. 17)

When church splits occur, the damage is devastating. A young Christian witnessing intense discord within his church begins to wonder about everything he's been taught. He questions whether Christianity can truly deliver on its promises. If the church can't handle its own internal conflicts, how can it offer redemption from sin?

Many believers deeply hurt by church splits withdraw entirely, reluctant to invest again for fear of being betrayed. Church divisions leave members bitter and disillusioned, and they convince non-Christians that Christianity is a fraud.

Even if you're completely right in your position during a church conflict, you must be careful about how you pursue it. Being right doesn't justify any method of resolution. You may be completely correct in doctrine and still be completely wrong in your behavior toward others.

> Do not deceive yourselves. If any of you think you are wise by the standards of this age, you should become "fools" so that you may become wise. For the wisdom of this world is foolishness in God's sight. (vv. 18-19)

## 3. It's Not About the Leaders (vv. 5-8, 4:6-7, 3:21-23)

Here's what truly matters: the work belongs to God, not to His servants.

> What, after all, is Apollos? And what is Paul? Only servants, through whom you came to believe—as the Lord has assigned to each his task. I planted the seed, Apollos watered it, but God has been making it grow. So neither the one who plants nor the one who waters is anything, but only God, who makes things grow. (vv. 5-7)

**God Is Everything**

This is the revolutionary heart of Paul's message. Yes, Paul and Apollos worked. Paul planted, Apollos watered. But "God has been making it grow." The growth—the actual life and power of the church—comes from God alone. This is a miracle of divine grace.

Preachers may labor long and hard, pressing many gifts into service, but in the end, the Lord gives the growth. Apart from their assigned tasks, servants are nothing. God is everything.

This means the church is really God's church, God's ministers belong to God. Those "in charge" should never view the church as theirs. Neither should the people regard the church and its workers as theirs, no matter how much they've invested.

To God alone we owe our loyalty.

**No Boasting, No Rivalry**

Paul drives this home with brutal clarity:

> Now, brothers and sisters, I have applied these things to myself and Apollos for your benefit, so that you may learn from us the meaning of the saying, "Do not go beyond what is written." Then you will not be puffed up in being a follower of one of us over against the other. For who makes you different from anyone else? What do you have that you did not receive? And if you did receive it, why do you boast as though you did not? (4:6-7)

Everything you have, you received. Where is the boasting then?

Paul makes the application crystal clear:

> So then, no more boasting about human leaders! All things are yours, whether Paul or Apollos or Cephas or the world or life or death or the present or the future—all are yours, and you are of Christ, and Christ is of God. (3:21-23)

Think of a major construction project—a rail link, for instance. Each worker is replaceable. Each firm is replaceable. But the project itself is not. So there's no boasting. There's something bigger than ourselves, bigger than our human loyalties.

Consider soldiers in war. One soldier is much like another. But it would be stupid for one soldier to backstab another, claim superiority, and split the platoon. You might win the ego fight but lose the war. And it's especially dumb to harm the mission itself for the sake of your ego.

We're all working toward something greater: the growth of the church—not just SMAC, but the heavenly church. We're not rivals but co-workers sharing a mutual purpose that unites our efforts.

---

## Conclusion: A Church Under Divine Construction

The church is under divine construction—built by God's word and Spirit, for God's purposes. This is why Paul says:

> Don't follow leaders. Follow Christ. Work the Bible out yourselves.

This is not brainwashing. Open the Bible. That limits the damage any one person can do. It's why we have question time—a chance to clarify and test what's taught against Scripture.

**What will next week bring?** We'll explore how leaders still matter—how different builders can work on the same project, how variety is necessary, and how each will be judged according to their work. But always, always under the headship of Christ, always in service of His temple.

> For the kingdom of God is not a matter of talk but of power. (4:20)

That power is seen in changed lives. People who live and die for others. People who endure suffering. That's real power. That's the power Paul speaks of in 1 Corinthians 1 and 2.
