# Hebrews 12:14-17

**Title:** Disqualification in the Race
**Date:** August 26, 2018
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## 1. Pursue (Verse 14)

### Context: The Christian Race

We're in the midst of a race—the Christian life depicted as both a marathon and a wrestling match. To finish is not enough; we must also avoid disqualification. The context is one of discipline, pain, and the need to run correctly.

### "Make Every Effort" (dikoksts)

> Make every effort to live in peace with everyone and to be holy; without holiness no one will see the Lord. (Hebrews 12:14)

The Greek word *dikoksts* ("make every effort") carries the energy of pursuit—to chase, to pursue, to prosecute. It's often used negatively to describe persecution or harassment of an opponent. But here, it takes on a redemptive meaning: *pursue peace*.

This is somewhat ironic. A persecuted congregation is urged to "pursue" peace—to chase after it with the same intensity others might chase them. There's a paradox at work here.

### Pursuing Peace with Everyone

Perhaps we can understand this through the example of Rahab. In Joshua 2, when Joshua's spies came to her, she could have been hostile. Instead, she welcomed God's people. She received them in peace, which is why she's commended in Hebrews 11:31.

Life is inherently political. Some people mesh with your temperament; others don't. Personalities differ. Sometimes you get your way; sometimes you don't. Yet we're called to make every effort toward a peaceful life—not to see the world as a zero-sum game of winners and losers, but to aim for win-win outcomes.

**Practically, this means:**
- Giving people the benefit of the doubt
- Extending grace to those we disagree with
- Resisting the cultural pressure toward constant accusation

There's a current of thought flowing from university campuses—imported from the West—that encourages excessive criticism of one another, assuming the worst of people. If you disagree with someone, you label them: racist, sexist, Holocaust denier. If you won't accept their views, you compare them to Hitler. This rhetoric has real consequences. We've seen assassination attempts born from such comparisons. One may have legitimate criticisms of a political figure, but comparing them to someone who killed sixteen million people is absurd and dangerous.

This is not living in peace. This is living in war. We are called to live in peace with one another. And I believe we should give more grace to those closest to us—our brothers and sisters in the faith deserve more rope.

### Pursuing Holiness

We are also called to pursue holiness. The Greek word for "holy" (*hagios*) means to be set apart from the world, separated from the common, consecrated to God.

In the temple, there were sacred places—the holy place where only priests could enter, and the holy of holies where only the high priest could go. God is separate, unable to touch evil.

> No one can see my face and live. (Exodus 33:20)

God is so holy, so wholly other, that to see Him in our sinful state would mean death. But in Jesus—God made flesh—the incarnate God finally adapted Himself to sinful, mortal humanity. His sacrifice on the cross paid the price for us to see God. When Jesus died, the curtain separating people from God was torn in two. Now ordinary people can meet the holy God. Christians are those who see God through Jesus.

### Peace and Holiness Together

It might seem random to pair peace and holiness, but there's deep logic here. We might link "toward all people" (peace) with "toward God" (holiness). The great commandments say it perfectly:

> Love the Lord your God with all your heart and with all your soul and with all your mind. This is the first and greatest commandment. And the second is like it: Love your neighbor as yourself. All the Law and the Prophets hang on these two commandments. (Matthew 22:37-40)

The pursuit of peace and holiness resonates with Jacob's character in the Old Testament. In Genesis 32, Jacob pursued peace—he was reconciling with his brother Esau, whom he had wronged. Jacob had deceived Esau out of his birthright and fled for his life. Now, afraid of Esau's vengeance, he sent gifts ahead—two hundred female goats, twenty male goats, two hundred ewes, twenty rams, thirty female camels, forty cows, ten bulls, twenty female donkeys, and ten male donkeys—each group separated so that Esau would see them one after another.

> I will pacify him with these gifts I am sending on ahead; later, when I see him, perhaps he will receive me. (Genesis 32:20)

Jacob's persistence was rewarded. In that night of wrestling with God at Peniel, Jacob struggled until dawn. The mysterious figure said to him:

> Your name will no longer be Jacob, but Israel, because you have struggled with God and with humans and have overcome. (Genesis 32:28-30)

Jacob's character changed. He limped away, his hip dislocated, bearing a physical mark of his encounter with God. But he also received a blessing and saw God face to face without dying.

Jacob's perseverance demonstrates the path: **pursuit, discipline and pain, and then the vision of God**.

**Application:** We must endure discipline. In the race, this means the pounding of our feet and the running itself. Pursue peace and holiness.

## 2. Watch That No One Falls (Verses 15-16)

### The Contrast

> See to it that no one falls short of the grace of God. (Hebrews 12:15)

The word "see to it" (*skopos*) means to look carefully, to observe closely, to search earnestly. This contrasts sharply with "pursue"—here we're on guard against those who are falling away, stumbling, being disqualified from God's grace.

Where we once pursued peace, now we watch lest a bitter root grows up to cause trouble.

> See to it that no bitter root grows up to cause trouble and defile many people. (Hebrews 12:15)

In Deuteronomy 29:18, Moses warned Israel:

> Make sure there is no man or woman, clan or tribe among you today whose heart turns away from the Lord our God... See to it that there is no root among you that produces such bitter poison.

Bitterness here is idolatry—turning from God to worship other gods. It's the poison that breaks covenant relationship. The word "defile" means to become unclean. And "many people" suggests that one person's bitterness doesn't stay private—it contaminates the community.

### The Example of Esau

> See that no one is sexually immoral, or is godless like Esau, who for a single meal sold his inheritance rights as the oldest son. (Hebrews 12:16)

The reference to Esau seems strange if we think only of sexual immorality. But the description is more profound. Esau sold something infinitely precious for something worthless. The Greek word *porneia* (sexual immorality) is related to "selling" (*pernemi*), evoking prostitution—trading away something sacred for a pittance.

> The boys grew up, and Esau became a skillful hunter, a man of the open country, while Jacob was content to stay at home. Isaac, who had a taste for wild game, loved Esau, but Rebekah loved Jacob.

> Once when Jacob was cooking some stew, Esau came in from the open country, famished. He said to Jacob, "Quick, let me have some of that red stew! I'm famished!" (That is why he was also called Edom.)

> Jacob replied, "First sell me your birthright."

> "Look, I am about to die," Esau said. "What good is the birthright to me?"

> But Jacob said, "Swear to me first." So he swore an oath to him, selling his birthright to Jacob.

> Then Jacob gave Esau some bread and some lentil stew. He ate and drank, and then got up and left. So Esau despised his inheritance rights. (Genesis 25:27-34)

Esau's godlessness (*bebelos*—literally "walking on the threshold," common, profane, the opposite of holy) is revealed in this act. His hunger, his fleshly desire, blinded him. He valued food more than God's spiritual blessing. He despised God's gift.

His lack of piety produced an appetite so consuming that he traded away his sacred inheritance for lentil stew. What a terrible bargain. He exchanged God's blessing for something worse. His true character showed itself in that moment—he saw no value in what God had promised him. It simply fell away from him.

### The Consequences of Idolatry

When we abandon the pursuit of peace and holiness, we exchange eternal things for temporal things:

- We trade our inheritance for cash. Do you not think you'll be rich in heaven?
- We trade it for things. Do you not believe heaven has better things?
- We trade it for sex. That satisfaction lasts mere minutes.
- We trade it for pleasure. What can earth offer when you possess eternity?

## 3. DNF—Did Not Finish (Verse 17)

> Later, as you know, when he wanted to inherit this blessing, he was rejected. Even though he sought the blessing with tears, he could not change what he had done. (Hebrews 12:17)

"Later" means immediately—the moment arrived for the blessing, and Esau fell. He was disqualified. He did not finish the race.

Hear the sorrow in his cry:

> When Esau heard his father's words, he burst out with a loud and bitter cry and said to his father, "Bless me—me too, my father!" (Genesis 27:34)

A bitter root produces bitter fruit. His tears came too late. The blessing could not be undone. His brother Jacob had received it through deception, yes, but it was done.

> Your brother came deceitfully and took your blessing. (Genesis 27:35)

> Esau said, "Isn't he rightly named Jacob? He has deceived me these two times: He took my birthright, and now he's taken my blessing! Haven't you reserved any blessing for me?" (Genesis 27:36)

> Isaac answered Esau, "I have made him lord over you and have made all his relatives his servants, and I have sustained him with grain and new wine. So what can I possibly do for you, my son?"

> Esau said to his father, "Do you have only one blessing, my father? Bless me too, my father!" Then Esau wept aloud. (Genesis 27:37-38)

He was disqualified. He did not finish.

Jacob represents the perseverant, faithful people who arrive at Mount Zion. Esau represents those who reject the life of faith and are finally rejected at Mount Sinai.

Hear the warning: Those who fall away from grace, having tasted heavenly gifts and shared in the Holy Spirit, having tasted the goodness of God's word and the powers of the coming age—if they fall away, it's impossible to bring them back to repentance (Hebrews 6:4-6).

But this is not your destiny. God is not unjust. He remembers your work, your love shown to His people. We hope each of you shows the same diligence to the very end, so that what you hope for may be fully realized. We are not calling you to laziness, but to imitate those who through faith and patience inherit what has been promised (Hebrews 6:9-12).

To imitate (*mimetes*) means to copy, to emulate. To persevere. If we become entangled, distracted by desires, we cannot finish the race. Our hope rests not in running perfectly, but in pressing forward—in pursuing peace and holiness, in watching lest we fall, in keeping our eyes fixed on Jesus until we cross the finish line.
