# Jonah 2:10-3:10

**Title:** Turning and Returning
**Date:** 12 June 2022 (ARISE); 21 December 2014 (SMAC)
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction: Going the Wrong Way

Have you ever found yourself heading in the wrong direction? It happens to all of us. I remember when my daughter first got her learner's permit—she turned right directly into oncoming traffic at a busy intersection. Or that time on a long weekend when we missed a turn on the way to church camp. What should have been a one-hour drive became two hours.

This is the story of Jonah. He went the wrong way.

## Jonah Repents (Jonah 2:10-3:4)

> And the Lord spoke to the fish, and it vomited Jonah out upon the dry land. Then the word of the Lord came to Jonah the second time, saying, "Arise, go to Nineveh, that great city, and call out against it the message that I tell you." So Jonah arose and went to Nineveh, according to the word of the Lord. Now Nineveh was an exceedingly great city, three days' journey in breadth. Jonah began to go into the city, going a day's journey. And he called out, "Yet forty days, and Nineveh shall be overthrown!" (ESV)

After his dramatic rescue from the fish, Jonah appears to have repented. God commands him again, and this time Jonah obeys—he gets up and goes toward Nineveh, where God wants him.

But notice the problems in how he carries out this mission:

**He only works one day.** The city required three days' journey to traverse, yet Jonah only spends one day proclaiming. He's slack, dragging his feet, reluctant.

**His message is minimal.** It's literally five words in Hebrew: "Yet forty days, and Nineveh shall be overthrown!" Where is God's character? Where is the call to repentance? The message lacks substance.

**His heart isn't in it.** Jonah is clearly procrastinating, going through the motions without genuine commitment. He's the reluctant prophet, still not truly keen on his assignment.

The number "forty" echoes throughout Scripture as a period of judgment and testing. But Jonah's minimal effort raises a troubling question: How many of us procrastinate when facing difficult obedience? We delay checking medical results when we're afraid. We postpone repentance because we're not ready. We drag our feet in serving God when we'd rather be elsewhere.

## Nineveh Repents (Jonah 3:5-9)

> And the people of Nineveh believed God. They called for a fast and put on sackcloth, from the greatest of them to the least of them. The word reached the king of Nineveh, and he arose from his throne, removed his robe, covered himself with sackcloth, and sat in ashes. And he issued a proclamation and published through Nineveh, "By the decree of the king and his nobles: Let neither man nor beast, herd nor flock, taste anything. Let them not feed or drink water, but let man and beast be covered with sackcloth, and let them call out mightily to God. Let everyone turn from his evil way and from the violence that is in his hands. Who knows? God may turn and relent and turn from his fierce anger, so that we may not perish." (ESV)

Despite Jonah's reluctant and incomplete proclamation, God uses even this minimal effort to accomplish His will. The Ninevites believed God—not Jonah, but God Himself.

### True Repentance: From Greatest to Least

The text emphasizes that repentance touched everyone, **from the greatest to the least**. This speaks to genuine, universal transformation:

**The greatest:** The king himself—the most powerful person in the city—rose from his throne, that seat of power and majesty. By removing his royal robes and covering himself with sackcloth, he acknowledges that God alone is king. He exchanges silk for calico, the finest garments for the roughest cloth. He sits in dust, the lowest position.

**The least:** Everyone participated—man, woman, child, servant, slave. Even the animals were clothed in sackcloth. This wasn't performative; it was total.

### The Meaning of Their Actions

In ancient culture, fasting, wearing sackcloth, and sitting in dust were the outward expressions of genuine repentance. Today we use baptism—simple white cloth signifying cleansing and newness. But repentance is far more than clothing or ritual.

The Ninevites sincerely repented. They **turned from their evil ways and from the violence in their hands**. They turned from worldliness, from the devil's ways, from selfishness and cruelty.

Repentance is not a one-time event but a daily practice—day after day, we turn more fully toward Jesus, shedding our old patterns.

### The Danger of Shallow Repentance

We live in an age of shallow commitment. We offer apologies that are merely words. "I'm sorry" has become so easy to say, so quick to forget. We practice "slacktivism"—activism reduced to clicking buttons or performing stunts. Real repentance requires years and years of consistent transformation. True solutions demand time, steadiness, and the hard work of turning from sin again and again.

Yet the miracle here is stunning: **an entire city repented**. From the king on his throne to the least slave, from people to animals, the whole of Nineveh turned. This has happened throughout history—in Rome, London, New England, throughout Africa. Kings and cities have turned from child sacrifice, debauchery, and pagan worship to follow the true and living God. It can happen again.

## God Repents (Jonah 3:10)

> When God saw what they did, how they turned from their evil way, God relented of the disaster that he had said he would do to them, and he did not do it. (ESV)

But here lies the greatest miracle of all: **God Himself repented.**

God relented from His judgment. He turned. He changed His mind.

This is extraordinary. When a king encounters a peasant on the road, who must give way? The peasant, of course. God is perfectly just to judge sin. Nineveh had earned destruction through violence and wickedness. Yet out of compassion—when He saw their genuine turning—God changed His mind and forgave.

### God's Mercy in Christ

Two thousand years ago, this divine mercy was fully revealed in Jesus. God the King rose from His throne and came down to earth. He sat in the dust, dressed first in swaddling clothes as a helpless baby, then in linen cloths as He hung on the cross for our sake.

Jesus became the payment for sin. He did not punish us but punished Himself instead. The Lamb of God was sacrificed so that we might be free from the penalty of sin.

**God has turned.** He made the first move toward us.

The Ninevites could only hope: "Who knows? God may turn and relent." But we know. It is proven in Christ. We have the certainty they lacked.

## Returning to God

Friends, we have all walked far away from God. We've gotten ourselves into messes of our own making. But the message of Jonah is clear: **we can change our minds and return to God. He promises to change His mind toward us.**

If you haven't returned to God, perhaps today is the day. Life is fleeting. Make peace with your Maker before it is too late. Just as that driving lesson taught me about paying attention to the road ahead, let this story teach you about the direction your life is heading. Are you going the right way? Will you turn and return to God?
