# Hebrews 13:1-6

**Title:** Running Together  
**Dates:** September 9, 2018 (SMAC) / August 11, 2024 (ARISE)  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Introduction

We're concluding our marathon journey through Hebrews. This final chapter takes a noticeably different tone—more prescriptive and practical than the theological argumentation that precedes it. Some scholars question whether it belongs, but there's no textual evidence for separation. Instead, the author is drawing out the implications of all he's taught, especially building from Hebrews 12:28: "how to serve God acceptably."

The chapter unfolds in three movements: welcoming certain people, rejecting certain attitudes, and trusting God through it all.

---

## I. Welcome These People (13:1-3, 7-8)

### Love One Another as Brothers and Sisters

> Keep on loving one another as brothers and sisters. (13:1)

The original Greek contains a single compound word: *philadelphia*—literally, "brother-love." This word carries profound significance. The city of Philadelphia itself was named after this ideal. When William Penn, a persecuted Quaker, founded the colony, he chose the name to express his vision: a place where people of all denominations could worship freely together.

For early Christians, this love was radical and concrete. Through faith in Christ, they were bound not by blood but by a shared commitment to Jesus. Each person belonged to him, and all belonged together. This wasn't sentimental affection—it was a working, practical love.

The broader context in Hebrews clarifies what this means:

> Let us consider how we may spur one another on toward love and good deeds, not giving up meeting together... but encouraging one another. (Hebrews 10:24-25)

And more powerfully:

> God will not forget your work and the love you have shown him as you have helped his people and continue to help them. (Hebrews 6:10)

This is the heart of it: loving God's people is loving God himself.

Jesus made it plain:

> A new commandment I give you: Love one another as I have loved you. By this everyone will know that you are my disciples, if you love one another. (John 13:34-35)

Love as Christ loved means sacrificial care—putting others' interests above your own. This happens everywhere: mothers caring for children, babysitters serving families so others can teach Scripture, the practical work of community on Sundays—parking, sharing meals, clearing bins, stacking containers. A simple thing, but it matters. It reflects the hidden love that holds a church together.

Speak to one another, encouraging each other toward love and good works. Thank those who serve quietly—like the aunties who bring soup each week. This is the language of brotherhood.

### Show Hospitality to Strangers

> Do not forget to show hospitality to strangers, for by so doing some people have shown hospitality to angels without knowing it. (13:2)

The Greek word is *philoxenia*—the love of strangers. In the ancient world, there were no safe inns except those infamous for drunkenness and worse. Safe lodging existed only in homes. Travelers and foreigners depended entirely on the hospitality of strangers.

Why does this command make sense for Christians? Because we are strangers ourselves:

> All these people were still living by faith when they died. They did not receive the things promised; they only saw them and welcomed them from a distance, admitting that they were foreigners and strangers on earth. (Hebrews 11:13)

The reference to angels echoes Abraham's story. When three strangers appeared at his tent, Abraham didn't hesitate. He ran to greet them, bowed low, offered food and rest. Those "strangers" turned out to be God's messengers bringing covenant promises. The point is simple: how we treat the stranger reflects our faith in God.

### Remember Those in Prison

> Continue to remember those in prison as if you were together with them in prison, and those who are mistreated as if you yourselves were suffering. (13:3)

In the first-century context, prison was often the holding place before judgment or execution—especially for Christians. "Remember" means far more than thinking about them. It means praying, identifying with their suffering, sending help. It means "as if you were bound with them."

The tragedy is that Christians remain the most persecuted religion in the world today, primarily in Muslim-majority regions of the Middle East and Africa, and in places like India where churches are burned. Behind the statistics are real witnesses and martyrs—people who, like the heroes of Chapter 11, have sacrificed everything to remain faithful to Christ.

We cannot merely offer thoughts and prayers. We must suffer alongside them, bearing their burden as our own. As Hebrews 10:32-35 reminds us:

> Remember those earlier days after you had received the light, when you endured in a great conflict full of suffering. You suffered along with those in prison and joyfully accepted the confiscation of your property, because you knew that you yourselves had better and lasting possessions.

We are in a race together. It is not one party running while others sleep at home. If soldiers fight overseas for our freedom while we let freedom die at home, what are they fighting for?

### Remember Your Leaders

> Remember your leaders, who spoke the word of God to you. Consider the outcome of their way of life and imitate their faith. Jesus Christ is the same yesterday and today and forever. (13:7-8)

The Greek word for leaders is *hegemonenous*—those who lead or guide. It can also mean "those who think." These are not titles but descriptions of action. They are the ones doing this work. Think of them as coaches—people who have shown you the way and now call you to follow.

Observe how they finished. Look at the trajectory of their lives. See what Christ produced in them. Then imitate their faith.

## II. Do Not Welcome These Things (13:4-5)

### Keep the Marriage Bed Pure

> Marriage should be honored by all, and the marriage bed kept pure, for God will judge the adulterer and all the sexually immoral. (13:4)

We welcome brothers and sisters in Christ into our lives, but not into the marriage bed. Marriage is the exclusive covenant between a man and woman for life.

"Honored by all" means honored by society and by yourselves. By society: don't let friends poison a couple against each other. Never counsel divorce regardless of circumstances. By yourselves: don't take revenge, don't remarry in bitterness. Trust God to judge.

The marriage bed must remain pure. This is sacred ground. No one else can enter it—no other man, no other woman. Both partners cannot agree to a third person. What happens in that intimacy stays there. God created sexual union as a gift for husband and wife alone. To misuse it is to defile the marriage bed.

History recalls Bishop Hugh Latimer, who once gave King Henry VIII a gift—not gold, but a New Testament wrapped in cloth. On the cloth was written: "Whoremongers and adulterers God will judge." Even kings are not exempt.

### Keep Your Lives Free from the Love of Money

> Keep your lives free from the love of money and be content with what you have, because God has said, "Never will I leave you; never will I forsake you." (13:5)

The Greek word *aphilargyros* means literally "not a silver-lover." The contrast is sharp: don't be a silver-lover; be a brother-lover instead. Covetousness is not welcomed into the Christian life.

Instead, use what God has given you generously. If you have a house, open it to welcome guests and children. If you have a car, use it to transport those who cannot transport themselves. If the market gave you profit this week, buy someone lunch. If you lost, let someone buy you lunch.

Money is a tool God has entrusted to you. Use it well. This is not easy. It requires dying to self-centeredness and the idols of the world. It means facing the world's criticisms, temptations, and pressures—and saying no.

But we do this hard thing because we trust God.

## III. Trust God (13:5b-6, 9-14)

### The Foundation: God's Faithfulness

> "Never will I leave you; never will I forsake you." So we say with confidence, "The Lord is my helper; I will not be afraid. What can mere mortals do to me?" (13:5b-6)

The double negative—never... never—is emphatic. God's promise is absolute. Our confidence rests not on circumstances but on God himself. This is the whole burden of Hebrews: trust God, not idols. Your treasure and inheritance lie not on earth but in heaven.

### Do the Hard Thing

> Do not be carried away by all kinds of strange teachings. It is good for our hearts to be strengthened by grace, not by eating ceremonial foods, which is of no benefit to those who do so. (13:9-10)

The author warns against false teachers who would lure believers back to empty rituals. We have Christ. We need nothing else.

The sacrificial system pointed forward to Jesus:

> The high priest carries the blood of animals into the Most Holy Place as a sin offering, but the bodies are burned outside the camp. And so Jesus also suffered outside the city gate to make the people holy through his own blood. (13:11-12)

Jesus was executed as a criminal, outside the city, bearing shame. Yet it was there—in that place of disgrace—that our redemption occurred. The irony is profound: outside, in shame, we find redemption.

> Let us, then, go to him outside the camp, bearing the disgrace he bore. (13:13)

We are called to follow him outside, to share his reproach. But look at the precedent: when Israel danced around the golden calf, God said his presence would no longer dwell in the camp. Those who sought him had to go outside.

> Moses used to take a tent and pitch it outside the camp some distance away, calling it the "tent of meeting." Anyone inquiring of the Lord would go to the tent of meeting outside the camp. (Exodus 33:7)

The author of Hebrews finds in this history a crucial principle: to meet the living God, you must be willing to go outside. You must be willing to step away from comfort, safety, and approval.

> For here we do not have an enduring city, but we are looking for the city that is to come. (13:14)

This present world is not our final home. We are strangers and pilgrims, like Abraham, looking for a city whose builder and maker is God.

### The Climax: Living as Disciples

The character of a Christian is faithfulness, commitment, grit—what Jesus called discipline. To be a disciple is to have *discipline*. This requires:

- Righteousness and holiness now
- Self-control, generosity, compassion, serving
- Great reserves of willpower when confronted by the world's distractions

These are not optional virtues. They are the marks of a life that has truly encountered Jesus Christ. They demand that we:

- Keep loving one another even when it's inconvenient
- Welcome the stranger even when it costs us
- Remember the suffering even when it's uncomfortable
- Honor marriage even in a culture of disposability
- Reject greed even in an age of acquisition
- Follow Jesus outside the camp even when it brings disgrace

> Through Jesus, therefore, let us continually offer to God a sacrifice of praise—the fruit of lips that openly profess his name. And do not forget to do good and to share with others, for with such sacrifices God is pleased. (13:15-16)

---

## Conclusion: Running to the Finish

We are heading not to Los Angeles, not to worldly achievement, but to Philadelphia—the city of brotherly love. We are heading to Mount Zion, to the church of the firstborn, to joyful angels, to the heavenly Jerusalem in God's presence.

The race is on. Ready, set, go.

Like Abebe Bikila, the Ethiopian marathoner who continued running on a badly injured knee when wisdom said to stop—finishing not first but finishing—we must finish the race. His nation sent him not merely to start but to finish. So Christ has sent us. We run together, brothers and sisters, with the cloud of witnesses cheering us on, with Christ himself as our coach, and with God's promise never to leave us.

The goal is godliness. The goal is good works. The goal is a life sacrificed for the greater good.

Father, we are in the race of our lives. Knowing you are with us, along with our brothers and sisters past, present, and future, around the world, give us courage and strength to run the race faithfully to the end.

Amen.
