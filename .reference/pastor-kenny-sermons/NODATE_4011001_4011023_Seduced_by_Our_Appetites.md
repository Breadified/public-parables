# Numbers 11:1-23

**Title:** Seduced by Our Appetites
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Grumbling on the Journey

Grumbling is especially toxic during a journey. I once drove our family to Cooma, and the entire trip was miserable—constant complaints. "Dad, are we there yet?" "I'm hungry." "I'm thirsty." "It's too hot." "It's too cold." One of my children even said, "My bum's itchy." (We don't go on holidays anymore!)

In Numbers, what should have been an eleven-day journey became a forty-year journey. Why? Because the people grumbled.

## Cravings (Numbers 11:4-9)

> Now the people began to crave other food, and again the Israelites started wailing and said, "If only we had meat to eat!" (Numbers 11:4)

Just three days into their journey. The "rabble"—other slaves and outsiders who had joined Israel at the exodus—started craving the foods of Egypt. Their discontent spread to the Israelites themselves, who began longing for the past.

> We remember the fish we ate in Egypt at no cost—also the cucumbers, melons, leeks, onions and garlic. (Numbers 11:5)

> But now we have lost our appetite; we never see anything but this manna! (Numbers 11:6)

The Israelites were dissatisfied with their current provision. God had given them manna—a miraculous provision described in verses 7-9:

> The manna was like coriander seed and looked like resin. The people went around gathering it, and then ground it in a handmill or crushed it in a mortar. They cooked it in a pot or made it into cakes. And it tasted like something made with olive oil. (Numbers 11:7-8)

The manna was actually quite remarkable: attractive (honey-colored like resin), convenient (freely available and requiring only gathering), versatile (could be ground, cooked, or made into cakes), flavorful (tasted like bread made with oil), and pure (covered with protective dew). It was a perfectly adequate diet.

Moreover, their complaint misrepresented their true situation. They weren't eating only manna. From Exodus 12:38, we know they brought flocks and herds—sheep, goats, and cattle—which would have provided milk, butter, cheese, yogurt, and meat. They had more than enough.

Their request was also unreasonable. They were in the Arabian desert. Where would they find fish like those from the Nile? Where would they grow the melons, cucumbers, leeks, onions, and garlic of Egypt's fertile plains? It's like traveling to Eastwood and expecting good steaks, or going to Cherrybrook and expecting authentic Chinese food.

**The truth:** They were not hungry. They were not lacking adequate food. They were bored, stressed, frightened—but not starving. Yet they complained.

### The Nature of Grumbling

> Now the people complained about their hardships (literally: spoke evil) in the hearing of the LORD. (Numbers 11:1)

Grumbling means speaking evil. It's whining about our circumstances while forgetting how good we actually have it and how bad things used to be.

This pattern repeats in our modern context. We whinge about being poor while possessing luxuries beyond the dreams of previous generations. We complain about outdated phones. We forget we're wealthier than we were a decade ago, wealthier than our parents were at our age. We see this in Australia, among students, among young people—a constant refusal to appreciate what we have.

The root issue is the same with modern cravings:
- Success and status
- Sexual fulfillment (pornography, relationships)
- Material possessions and "more"

We who have left behind sin, guilt, and shame—who have rejected the world's value systems—must resist these same cravings today.

## Cravings Result in Judgment (Numbers 11:10, 33)

God's response to grumbling is clear: judgment.

> The LORD heard this and his anger was aroused. Then fire from the LORD burned among them and consumed some of the outskirts of the camp. (Numbers 11:1)

> But while the meat was still between their teeth and before it could be consumed, the anger of the LORD burned against the people, and he struck them with a severe plague. (Numbers 11:33)

**The consequence of sin is death.** People died.

God even gave them what they demanded—meat for a whole month:

> Tell the people: 'Consecrate yourselves in preparation for tomorrow, when you will eat meat. The LORD heard you when you wailed, "If only we had meat to eat! We were better off in Egypt!" Now the LORD will give you meat, and you will eat it. You will not eat it for just one day, or two days, or five, ten or twenty days, but for a whole month—until it comes out of your nostrils and you loathe it—because you have rejected the LORD, who is among you, and have wailed before him, saying, "Why did we ever leave Egypt?"' (Numbers 11:18-20)

This is God allowing them to indulge in their cravings until those very cravings destroyed them. Like the boy in *Matilda* who is force-fed cake until he's sick.

### The Deception of Nostalgia

The Israelites remembered eating the fish of Egypt "at no cost." But that cost was their freedom—they were slaves. Consider the reality:

> So they put slave masters over them to oppress them with forced labor, and they built Pithom and Rameses as store cities for Pharaoh. They made their lives bitter with hard labor in brick and mortar and with all kinds of work in the fields; in all their hard labor the Egyptians used them ruthlessly. (Exodus 1:11, 14)

> Then Pharaoh gave this order to all his people: "Every boy that is born you must throw into the Nile, but let every girl live." (Exodus 1:22)

"At no cost"? They paid with their souls.

This mirrors our own temptation. We see our non-Christian friends seemingly having more freedom and fun. We remember pre-Christian days when we made our own rules, indulged our desires, lived without conscience. We forget the despair, the emptiness, the spiritual death.

**But we have been set free from sin:**

> Thanks be to God that, though you used to be slaves to sin, you wholeheartedly obeyed the form of teaching to which you were entrusted. You have been set free from sin and have become slaves to righteousness. (Romans 6:17-18)

The fruit of the Spirit includes self-control, not enslavement to appetites. Do not slide back. Do not decrease in fervor. Do not slacken in your commitment to God and your church community.

## The Intercessor (Numbers 11:10-15)

Yet the judgment was not fatal. Why? Because someone interceded for the people.

Moses saw the Israelites' situation and was troubled:

> He asked the LORD, "Why have you brought this trouble on your servant? What have I done to displease you that you put the burden of all these people on me? Did I conceive all these people? Did I give them birth? Why do you tell me to carry them in my arms, as a nurse carries an infant, to the land you promised on oath to their forefathers? Where can I get meat for all these people? They keep wailing to me, 'Give us meat to eat!' I cannot carry all these people by myself; the burden is too heavy for me. If this is how you are going to treat me, put me to death right now—if I have found favor in your eyes—and do not let me face my own ruin." (Numbers 11:11-15)

At first glance, this sounds like Moses is also complaining. But elsewhere Scripture reveals his true role. When the people first cried out:

> When the people cried out to Moses, he prayed to the LORD and the fire died down. (Numbers 11:2)

And the Psalmist explains Moses' broader role:

> They forgot the God who saved them, who had done great things in Egypt, miracles in the land of Ham and awesome deeds by the Red Sea. So he said he would destroy them—had not Moses, his chosen one, stood in the breach before him to keep his wrath from destroying them. (Psalm 106:21-23)

Moses stands in the breach. He intercedes.

### How Moses Intercedes

**First, Moses redirects God's attention.** His questions focus on himself rather than the people—"Why have you brought this on me?" "What did I do?" "Did I conceive them?" "Did I give them birth?" "Am I their nurse?" He makes himself the issue, giving God another target and drawing judgment toward himself rather than the nation.

**Second, Moses reframes the complaint.** He subtly shifts their words. They demanded meat from God ("who will give us meat?"), but Moses reports that they are begging *him* ("they keep wailing to me, 'Give us meat to eat!'"). This redirects the focus.

**Third, Moses reminds God of His character.** His questions are rhetorical—they all point back to God's responsibility:

- "Did I conceive these people?" No, God did.
- "Am I their nurse?" No, God nurtured them.
- "Can I fulfill the promise to bring them to Canaan?" No, God promised.

The implication is clear: they are *your* people, not mine. They depend on *you* for nourishment, not me. Don't let your previous work go to waste. Take pity on your chosen leader and show mercy to your people.

This shows that God actually desires intercessors. He is looking for someone to stand in the gap:

> I looked for a man among them who would build up the wall and stand before me in the gap on behalf of the land so I would not have to destroy it, but I found none. (Ezekiel 22:30)

**And here is the good news:** Jesus is the better Moses. He stands in the gap between God and humanity, created by our sin and our cravings.

Where Moses prayed and the fire died down, Jesus intercedes perfectly and eternally. He takes our place. In the Garden, He prayed, "Take me instead." On the cross, He said, "Father, forgive them, for they do not know what they are doing."

Just as Jesus is the better Moses, He is also the better manna:

> I am the bread of life. Your forefathers ate the manna in the desert, yet they died. But here is the bread that comes down from heaven, which a man may eat and not die. I am the living bread that came down from heaven. If anyone eats of this bread, he will live forever. This bread is my flesh, which I will give for the life of the world. (John 6:48-51)

The Israelites received the feeding of the five thousand—fish and bread multiplied beyond measure—pointing to Jesus as the satisfying provision. And more importantly, we need not fear judgment because Jesus has interceded for us.

## Contentment with God

The place where the people died was named Kibroth Hattaavah—"the graves of craving." They buried those who craved other food there. Move on from that place.

Food can be a window to longing for the past. When I speak with visitors from Malaysia, I'm tempted to romanticize Malaysian food and Malaysian life. I think, "Why am I in Australia? If I lived in Malaysia, I could get hawker food for spare change anytime." But then I catch myself. Yes, Malaysian food is wonderful, but that nostalgic memory conveniently forgets that I would be a second-class citizen there. Here I enjoy Malaysian restaurants at Istana, and while more expensive than Malaysia, Australian wages allow for it. Why would I want to return to being a second-class citizen just for cheaper food?

We long for a past that no longer exists—and frankly, we wouldn't actually want to return to it.

**The truth:** The Israelites took for granted what they had in the present while romanticizing a painful past they had actually escaped. Their "strong craving" was for an idealized version of Egypt that never truly existed.

As Christians, when you remember your pre-Christian days, do not think life was better then. You were slaves to sin. You were slaves to the devil. You were on the road to eternal death. Yes, you may have seemed to be enjoying yourself—gaining the world—but you were paying for it with your soul. The hurt, the senselessness, the despair, the lack of hope and purpose were always there.

And then you heard of Jesus, and your heart leapt.

So do not go back. Do not slide back. Do not decrease in your commitment to Christ, your church attendance, your fellowship. Crave God, not the things of the world.
