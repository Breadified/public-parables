# Hebrews 4:14–8:13

**Title:** Jesus is a Better High Priest

**Date:** 18 January 2008

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Can I Trust Him?

When conducting business, we typically ask two fundamental questions. In the Western approach, we ask: Is the contract good? Is it enforceable? This emphasizes the importance of legal systems and lawyers. In the Asian approach, we ask: Can I trust him? This emphasizes the importance of relationships and networks, particularly family connections.

Both are necessary. A contract without trust is fragile; trust without a binding agreement is equally vulnerable. We apply these principles everywhere—in financial dealings, choosing tutors, online marketplaces.

So when it comes to Jesus and doing business with God, we must ask the same two questions: Is the contract good? Can we trust him? Can we truly do business with God?

The writer of Hebrews answers with a resounding yes. In Hebrews 4:14–16, he provides the foundation for our confidence.

> Therefore, since we have a great high priest who has gone through the heavens, Jesus the Son of God, let us hold firmly to the faith we profess. For we do not have a high priest who is unable to sympathize with our weaknesses, but we have one who has been tempted in every way, just as we are—yet was without sin. Let us then approach the throne of grace with confidence, so that we may receive mercy and find grace to help us in our time of need.

---

## The Priesthood of Jesus (4:14–16)

The writer's answer hinges on an unusual concept: Jesus is a high priest. For those familiar with the Old Testament, this is understandable. But for those reading the New Testament for the first time, it's bewildering.

### Why Hebrews?

The concept of Jesus' priesthood appears almost exclusively in Hebrews. While the New Testament contains hints—references to sacrifice, atonement, intercession, Jesus' role as advocate—nowhere else is Jesus explicitly called a priest. This is surprising, given how central the idea becomes in Hebrews.

The letter contains several preparatory mentions before diving into this teaching. In 2:17, we read that Jesus became "a merciful and faithful high priest." In 3:1, he is called "the apostle and high priest whom we confess."

The writer has been building toward this revelation. His argument progresses through several stages: Jesus as the sent one from God (apostle), Jesus as our brother (priest), and a crucial detour about the future rest. All of this preparation points to the priesthood of Jesus—the central claim that we have someone representing us in the heavens.

Now, after these hints, it's time to explain properly. The writer does this by examining two types of priesthood: the priesthood of Levi and the priesthood of Melchizedek.

---

## 1. The Priesthood of Levi

### What Makes a High Priest?

The Old Testament book of Leviticus contains detailed laws about approaching God and the role of priests. The writer summarizes what qualifies someone to be a high priest.

#### Priests Were Representative (5:1–3)

> Every high priest is selected from among men and is appointed to represent them in matters related to God, to offer gifts and sacrifices for sins. He is able to deal gently with those who are ignorant and are going astray, since he himself is subject to weakness.

The first requirement is representation. A high priest must come from among the people—selected from men, appointed to speak to God on their behalf. They must understand human weakness because they share it. This is why a politician who doesn't live in their constituency cannot truly represent it; they are out of touch with the lived experience of those they serve. Similarly, a high priest must be human to represent humans.

However, this strength becomes a fatal flaw:

> This is why he has to offer sacrifices for his own sins, as well as for the sins of the people.

The priest who understands weakness because he shares it is trapped by the same weakness he's trying to cover for others. It's like a blind eye surgeon trying to remove a cataract from a patient's eye—we appreciate the qualification and care, but the fundamental limitation makes the healing impossible.

#### Priests Were Appointed (5:4)

> No one takes this honor upon himself; he must be called by God, just as Aaron was.

The second requirement is divine appointment. A high priest cannot claim the position; he must be called by God.

#### Jesus Fits Both Criteria (5:5–10)—But Not Completely (7:18)

Jesus meets both requirements. First, he was appointed:

> So Christ also did not take upon himself the glory of becoming a high priest. But God said to him, "You are my Son; today I have become your Father." And he says in another place, "You are a priest forever, in the order of Melchizedek."

Second, he was truly representative, fully human:

> During the days of Jesus' life on earth, he offered up prayers and petitions with loud cries and tears to the one who could save him from death, and he was heard because of his reverent submission. Although he was a son, he learned obedience from what he suffered and, once made perfect, he became the source of eternal salvation for all who obey him.

Here, Jesus' prayers and petitions were not merely for himself—not just asking the Father to remove the cup of wrath. They were for us. The fact that he was heard indicates his prayers were answered. And the climax in verse 9—that he became the source of eternal salvation—confirms that Jesus was calling out with loud cries and tears for us, asking God to deal kindly with his people.

Jesus is truly our representative, our brother who can sympathize with our weakness without being bound by sin. He can be one of us without our fatal limitations.

### No More Levitical Priesthood

Yet something is strange. Although Jesus fulfills all the criteria of the Levitical priesthood, the writer says this is not it. Hebrews 7:18 is explicit:

> The former regulation is set aside because it was weak and useless (for the law made nothing perfect), and a better hope is introduced, by which we draw near to God.

The Levitical era has ended. The priesthood was weak and useless—it made nothing perfect. We need something better.

Today, there is no Levitical priesthood. Though 1 Peter and Romans use priestly language about Christians, they are not reestablishing the priesthood. When we speak of the priesthood of all believers, we mean dedication to God and bearing witness to Jesus—not performing sacrifices or mediating between God and people.

Anglican ministers are not priests. The Lord's Supper is not a sacrifice. We do not mediate between you and God. The medieval church thought the church building was a temple, the communion a sacrifice, and the priest a special intermediary. But that thinking stems from other religions—temple, monk, incense, a special person in a special place performing special rituals. This is not Christianity.

The Lord's Supper is simply an ancient tradition to help us remember Jesus and look out for one another, based on his words at his last meal. I am a teacher, not a priest, because the Levitical priesthood is finished.

## A Warning: Hard Teaching Ahead (5:11–6:12)

Yet the writer keeps repeating a strange refrain: God has appointed Jesus as high priest "in the order of Melchizedek." Who is Melchizedek?

This mysterious figure appears suddenly in Genesis 14—a king who blesses Abraham and receives a tenth of his plunder, then vanishes from the pages of Scripture. This is getting too hard.

The writer senses the difficulty and pauses to give his congregation a gentle warning:

> We have much to say about this, but it is hard to explain because you are slow to learn. In fact, though by this time you ought to be teachers, you need someone to teach you the elementary truths of God's word all over again. You need milk, not solid food! Anyone who lives on milk, being still an infant, is not acquainted with the teaching about righteousness. But solid food is for the mature, who by constant use have trained themselves to distinguish good from evil.

He then exhorts them to maturity:

> Therefore let us leave the elementary teachings about Christ and go on to maturity, not laying again the foundation of repentance from acts that lead to death, and of faith in God, instruction about baptisms, the laying on of hands, the resurrection of the dead, and eternal judgment.

The passage that follows (6:4–8) addresses those who turn away from the faith—it will be dealt with in greater detail later. But the writer concludes with encouragement:

> Even though we speak like this, dear friends, we are confident of better things in your case—things that accompany salvation. God is not unjust; he will not forget your work and the love you have shown him as you have helped his people and continue to help them. We want each of you to show this same diligence to the very end, in order to make your hope sure. We do not want you to become lazy, but to imitate those who through faith and patience inherit what has been promised.

### Moving to Maturity

The writer is calling his congregation to grow in maturity. Spiritual growth happens through deeper knowledge of Scripture. This is why we must push beyond surface readings and understand the whole of Hebrews, not just random passages. It's hard work. Shallow learning produces shallow Christians. Sermonettes produce Christianettes. But as a community, we must pursue rigorous knowledge of God's Word.

## 2. The Priesthood of Melchizedek

To understand why Jesus is better, the writer takes us back to Abraham and shows us that there is a priesthood older and greater than Levi's—the order of Melchizedek.

### God Promised to Bless Abraham Through Melchizedek (6:13–14; 7:1–18)

God made a covenant with Abraham:

> When God made his promise to Abraham, since there was no one greater for him to swear by, he swore by himself, saying, "I will surely bless you and give you many descendants." And so after waiting patiently, Abraham received what was promised.

How does God bless Abraham? The answer lies in the Melchizedek account:

> This Melchizedek was king of Salem and priest of God Most High. He met Abraham returning from the defeat of the kings and blessed him, and Abraham gave him a tenth of everything. First, his name means "king of righteousness"; then also, "king of Salem" means "king of peace." Without father or mother, without genealogy, without beginning of days or end of life, like the Son of God he remains a priest forever.

Melchizedek is a great priest. His names—king of righteousness and king of peace—reveal his nature. He is eternal, existing without genealogy or succession. He is greater than Abraham:

> Just think how great he was: Even the patriarch Abraham gave him a tenth of the plunder! Now the law requires the descendants of Levi who become priests to collect a tenth from the people—that is, their brothers—even though their brothers are descended from Abraham. This man, however, did not trace his descent from Levi, yet he collected a tenth from Abraham and blessed him who had the promises. And without doubt the lesser person is blessed by the greater.

Abraham himself gives Melchizedek a tenth. This is extraordinary—the patriarch, blessed by God, acknowledges Melchizedek's superiority. Since the lesser is blessed by the greater, Melchizedek is greater than Abraham. And since Levi descended from Abraham, Melchizedek is even greater than Levi:

> One might even say that Levi, who collects the tenth, paid the tenth through Abraham, because when Melchizedek met Abraham, Levi was still in the body of his ancestor.

This priesthood—Melchizedek's—exists in a different category from Levi's:

> If perfection could have been attained through the Levitical priesthood (for on the basis of it the law was given to the people), why was there still need for another priest to come—one in the order of Melchizedek, not in the order of Aaron? For when there is a change of the priesthood, there must also be a change of the law. He of whom these things are said belonged to a different tribe, and no one from that tribe has ever served at the altar. For it is clear that our Lord descended from Judah, and in regard to that tribe Moses said nothing about priests. And what we have said is even more clear if another priest like Melchizedek appears, one who has become a priest not on the basis of a regulation as to his ancestry but on the basis of the power of an indestructible life.

Jesus is a priest not through legal lineage but through the power of an indestructible life. This is why the Levitical priesthood is superseded:

> The former regulation is set aside because it was weak and useless (for the law made nothing perfect), and a better hope is introduced, by which we draw near to God.

God blessed Abraham through Melchizedek priesthood. God blessed Levi through Melchizedek priesthood. The foundation of this priesthood is not law but indestructible life.

### God Swore an Oath to David (6:16–17; 7:20–22)

God not only made a promise to Abraham; he confirmed his purpose with an oath to David:

> Men swear by someone greater than themselves, and the oath confirms what is said and puts an end to all argument. Because God wanted to make the unchanging nature of his purpose very clear to the heirs of what was promised, he confirmed it with an oath.

This oath is recorded in Psalm 110:4:

> And it was not without an oath! Others became priests without any oath, but he became a priest with an oath when God said to him: "The Lord has sworn and will not change his mind: 'You are a priest forever.'"

And this oath guarantees a better covenant:

> Because of this oath, Jesus has become the guarantee of a better covenant.

### So That We Might Have Hope (6:18–20; 7:23–8:1)

Why does God make both a promise and an oath? So that we might have unshakeable hope:

> God did this so that, by two unchangeable things—the promise and the oath—in which it is impossible for God to lie, we who have fled to take hold of the hope offered to us may be greatly encouraged. We have this hope as an anchor for the soul, firm and secure. It enters the inner sanctuary behind the curtain, where Jesus, who went before us, has entered on our behalf. He has become a high priest forever, in the order of Melchizedek.

If God promises to bless the nations through Melchizedek priesthood, if God swears an oath to put his blessing through Melchizedek priesthood, and if Jesus is the priest of Melchizedek, then we have great hope that it will be accomplished. We can trust him.

The permanence of Jesus' priesthood is what makes salvation complete:

> Now there have been many of those priests, since death prevented them from continuing in office; but because Jesus lives forever, he has a permanent priesthood. Therefore he is able to save completely those who come to God through him, because he always lives to intercede for them.

Such a high priest is exactly what we need:

> Such a high priest meets our need—one who is holy, blameless, pure, set apart from sinners, exalted above the heavens. Unlike the other high priests, he does not need to offer sacrifices day after day, first for his own sins, and then for the sins of the people. He sacrificed for their sins once for all when he offered himself. For the law appoints as high priests men who are weak; but the oath, which came after the law, appointed the Son, who has been made perfect forever.

## The Main Point (8:1–2)

> We do have such a high priest, who sat down at the right hand of the throne of the Majesty in heaven, and who serves in the sanctuary, the true tabernacle set up by the Lord, not by man.

Jesus is our high priest. He serves in the true sanctuary in heaven, not in an earthly copy.

### The Heavenly Sanctuary (8:3–6)

> Every high priest is appointed to offer both gifts and sacrifices, and so it was necessary for this one also to have something to offer. If he were on earth, he would not be a priest, for there are already men who offer the gifts prescribed by the law. They serve at a sanctuary that is a copy and shadow of what is in heaven. This is why Moses was warned when he was about to build the tabernacle: "See to it that you make everything according to the pattern shown you on the mountain." But the ministry Jesus has received is as superior to theirs as the covenant of which he is mediator is superior to the old one, and it is founded on better promises.

The earthly tabernacle and temple were copies and shadows of the heavenly sanctuary. Jesus serves in the true sanctuary in heaven, mediating a superior covenant founded on better promises.

### The New Covenant (8:7–13)

The old covenant revealed a problem:

> For if there had been nothing wrong with that first covenant, no place would have been sought for another. But God found fault with the people and said:

God promises a new covenant:

> "The time is coming, declares the Lord, when I will make a new covenant with the house of Israel and with the house of Judah. It will not be like the covenant I made with their forefathers when I took them by the hand to lead them out of Egypt, because they did not remain faithful to my covenant, and I turned away from them, declares the Lord.

> This is the covenant I will make with the house of Israel after that time, declares the Lord. I will put my laws in their minds and write them on their hearts. I will be their God, and they will be my people. No longer will a man teach his neighbor, or a man his brother, saying, 'Know the Lord,' because they will all know me, from the least of them to the greatest. For I will forgive their wickedness and will remember their sins no more."

> By calling this covenant "new," he has made the first one obsolete; and what is obsolete and aging will soon disappear.

In the new covenant, God writes his law on human hearts. Knowledge of God becomes personal and universal. Forgiveness is complete and permanent. The old system passes away, and Jesus' priesthood brings us into this new reality.

## Why Can We Trust Jesus?

We began with two questions: Is the contract good? Can we trust him?

The answer is yes—both ways.

**The contractual side is covered.** God has made promises and sworn oaths. God does not lie. Two unchangeable things guarantee that what he has promised will be accomplished.

**The relational side is covered.** We have a man inside. We have our own relative on the job—our brother. It's like having a trusted friend as your surgeon or a family member handling your case. You can trust them because they are yours.

Jesus is our high priest forever, representing us in the heavens. He is the one who can bring us to God. No other mediators are needed—not Anglican priests, not Catholic saints, not any intermediary. Jesus alone is sufficient.

We can trust him because he is human like us, yet sinless. Because he was appointed by God. Because he lives forever and always intercedes for us. Because God has sworn an oath about him. Because he mediates a better covenant.

The writer of Hebrews has answered the foundational question: Can I trust him when doing business with God?

Yes. You can do business with God through Jesus. You can trust him completely.
