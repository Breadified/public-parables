# 1 Samuel 9:1-10:27

**Title:** Finding a King

**Date:** 24 July 2022 (ARISE); Draft from 28 May 2017 (SMAC)

## The Search Begins: Israel Looks for Leadership

The nation of Israel set out to find a king. They wanted someone tall, strong, and impressive—someone who could lead them and fight their battles like the other nations around them. What they discovered instead reveals something profound about what makes a true leader.

> Then he presented himself before the Lord by tribes and clans, finally bringing forward Saul son of Kish, who was as handsome a young man as could be found anywhere in Israel, and he was a head taller than anyone else.

Saul appeared to have everything—good looks, impressive stature, a powerful family. But before he could become king, he had to be found. And that's where our story really begins: not with a king seeking power, but with a young man searching for lost donkeys.

## Part 1: Finding Donkeys (9:1-21)

### The Lost Donkeys

Saul's father Kish sent him to find some missing donkeys. It seemed like a simple task, but it became an adventure that would change everything.

> Now the donkeys belonging to Saul's father Kish were lost, and Kish said to his son Saul, "Take one of the servants with you and go and look for the donkeys."

Saul and his servant searched through the hill country of Ephraim, through Shalisha, through Shaalim, and into Benjamin's territory. They didn't find (matza) them. They did not find (matza) them. The repetition emphasizes the fruitlessness of their search. After covering all this ground with nothing to show for it, Saul was ready to give up.

> When they reached the district of Zuph, Saul said to the servant who was with him, "Come, let's go back, or my father will stop thinking about the donkeys and start worrying about us."

### The Servant's Initiative

Here's where we see something important: Saul wanted to quit, but his servant didn't. The servant had less at stake—these weren't his donkeys—yet he showed more perseverance. He took initiative.

> But the servant replied, "Look, in this town there is a man of God; he is highly respected, and everything he says comes true. Let's go there now. Perhaps he will tell us what way to take."

The servant knew of a man of God who was highly respected, whose words always came to pass. This was an opportunity, but Saul complained about having nothing to offer the prophet.

> Saul said to his servant, "If we go, what can we give the man? The food in our sacks is gone. We have no gift to take to the man of God. What do we have?"

Saul's hesitation wasn't based on lack of resources but on fear and excuse-making. The servant, undeterred, offered a solution: he had a quarter shekel of silver.

> The servant answered him again. "Look," he said, "I have a quarter of a shekel of silver. I will give it to the man of God so that he will tell us what way to take."

When faced with an obstacle, Saul complained. When faced with the same obstacle, the servant found a way forward. This pattern reveals something crucial about leadership—it's not about being the biggest or the strongest. It's about initiative, perseverance, and a willingness to serve the goal rather than retreat from it.

### Finding Others

They pressed on toward the town and encountered young women drawing water.

> As they were going up the hill to the town, they met some young women coming out to draw water, and they asked them, "Is the seer here?"

These young women—nobodies, servants drawing water—knew exactly where to find the prophet. They knew he was in town that day, that there was a sacrifice happening, that he would bless it before eating. They even knew the exact timing: he would arrive soon, and Saul would find him.

The irony was sharp: Saul, who would become king, didn't know who Samuel was or where to find him. Meanwhile, simple servant girls knew all about him. The servant who accompanied Saul knew about Samuel. The young women knew about Samuel. Everyone seemed to know except the future king himself.

When Saul finally met Samuel at the town gate and asked where the seer's house was, Samuel replied simply: "I am the seer." After all that searching, Saul didn't even recognize the very person he'd been looking for.

### The Donkeys Are Found

Samuel welcomed Saul and told him something remarkable:

> "As for the donkeys you lost three days ago, do not worry about them; they have been found. And to whom is all the desire of Israel turned, if not to you and your whole family line?"

The donkeys had been found. That was the small matter settled. But Samuel revealed something infinitely larger: Saul was about to discover his real purpose.

Yet even with this stunning announcement, Saul's response was to protest his own unworthiness:

> Saul answered, "But am I not a Benjamite, from the smallest tribe of Israel, and is not my clan the least of all the clans of the tribe of Benjamin? Why do you say such a thing to me?"

Saul saw his limitations. He was from Benjamin, the smallest tribe. His family was the least significant within that tribe. These were facts, but Saul used them as reasons for disqualification. There's a difference between humility and self-defeating doubt. Saul was displaying the latter.

## Part 2: Finding the King's Role (10:1-16)

### The Anointing

Samuel anointed Saul with oil, a ceremonial act that set him apart for a special purpose.

> Then Samuel took a flask of olive oil and poured it on Saul's head and kissed him, saying, "Has not the Lord anointed you ruler over his inheritance?"

Saul was being commissioned as a military leader—a commander to lead Israel and deliver them from the Philistines. This wasn't a symbolic gesture; it was an assignment to war.

Samuel gave Saul three signs to confirm what God would do. The third sign was the most significant:

> After that you will go to Gibeah of God, where there is a Philistine outpost. As you approach the town, you will meet a procession of prophets coming down from the high place with lyres, timbrels, pipes and harps being played before them, and they will be prophesying. The Spirit of the Lord will come powerfully upon you, and you will prophesy with them; and you will be changed into a different person.

The Spirit of the Lord wasn't giving Saul an ecstatic religious experience. It was equipping him for war—physical strength and courage to fight. Throughout the book of Judges, whenever the Spirit of the Lord came upon someone, it empowered them for military victory.

Then came the instruction:

> Once these signs are fulfilled, do whatever your hand finds to do, for God is with you.

This phrase wasn't permission to do whatever he wanted. It was military language—the same phrase used in Judges when describing going to war: "destroy them as much as you are able." Saul was being sent to attack the Philistines.

Moreover, the context made this clear. In 1 Samuel 8, the people had demanded a king specifically to "go out before us and fight our battles." That was the whole reason for wanting a king in the first place. Israel wanted protection from the Philistines, and Saul was anointed to provide it.

### The Missing Moment

Samuel fed Saul the priestly portion—the thigh—a sign of power and strength. It was a specific, meaningful gesture. Then something happened that was left unrecorded: Samuel spoke to Saul on the roof at dawn about his calling.

> After they came down from the high place to the town, Samuel talked with Saul on the roof of his house. They rose about daybreak, and Samuel called to Saul on the roof, "Get ready, and I will send you on your way."

What did Samuel tell him? We aren't told. But something about that conversation didn't take root.

### Saul's Failure to Act

After the three signs were fulfilled—including his prophesying with the prophets—Saul went home. He encountered his uncle and was asked where he'd been. Saul's answer was telling:

> "Looking for the donkeys," he said. "But when we saw they were not to be found, we went to Samuel."

This was technically true, but it was incomplete. Saul was still talking about the donkeys. A third time, we're told the donkeys were found. Saul should have been talking about his commission as king, but instead he kept coming back to the lost livestock.

> Saul replied, "He assured us that the donkeys had been found." But he did not tell his uncle what Samuel had said about the kingship.

Saul refused to acknowledge his calling. Either he was afraid of what it meant, or he knew he'd done the wrong thing. He was supposed to go to war against the Philistines. Instead, he went home. The anointing had happened, the Spirit had equipped him, but Saul turned away from his assignment. He was a coward—hiding when God had called him to fight.

## Part 3: A Leader Cannot Be Found (10:17-27)

### The Second Chance

Since Saul's private anointing didn't work, Samuel arranged a public ceremony. He summoned all Israel to Mizpah and cast lots before the Lord to reveal God's chosen king. The lots fell on the tribe of Benjamin, then on Matri's clan, then on Saul son of Kish.

But when it came time to present him:

> When they looked for him, he was not to be found. So they inquired further of the Lord, "Has the man come here yet?" And the Lord said, "Yes, he has hidden himself among the supplies."

The tallest man in Israel—the one who stood a head taller than anyone else—was hiding among the baggage. It's almost comical if it weren't so tragic. Samuel had to be asked by the people where their king was.

When they brought Saul out, Samuel made a declaration that might have been tinged with irony:

> Samuel said to all the people, "Do you see the man the Lord has chosen? There is no one like him among all the people."

Then the people shouted: "Long live the king!"

### The Scoundrels' Truth

But not everyone was convinced. Some worthless fellows questioned:

> "How can this fellow save us?" They despised him and brought him no gifts. But Saul kept silent.

These scoundrels asked a fair question. And they were right. Saul couldn't save them—at least not in the way that really mattered. He was an anointed king, but he lacked something essential: the courage and commitment to his calling.

Saul's silence in the face of contempt mirrored his earlier silence about his kingship. Both were failures of leadership. A true leader would have addressed the doubt. A true leader wouldn't have hidden.

## What Makes a Leader?

Throughout this passage, we see a striking contrast. The servant who accompanied Saul displayed every quality we'd expect in a leader: initiative, courage, resourcefulness, willingness to serve, knowledge of his people, and the wisdom to seek counsel. Yet he was just a servant.

Saul, by contrast, was chosen by God. He was given every external advantage: height, good looks, family standing, anointing with oil, the Spirit of the Lord. Yet he failed at the most basic requirement of leadership: taking action.

### The Reluctant Leader

The name "Saul" comes from a Hebrew word meaning "to ask." Throughout his story, Saul asks questions. He asks about the donkeys. He asks about the prophet. He asks his uncle questions instead of speaking first. When you're always asking, when you're always questioning whether you should act, you're signaling that you don't want to do it. Saul was passive, distracted, and complaining when he should have been decisive and committed.

### What Leadership Actually Looks Like

True leadership isn't about being the biggest or the best-looking. It isn't about having authority over others. Real leadership is:

- **Seeing what needs to be done and doing it first.** The servant didn't wait to be told to find the prophet—he suggested it. The young women didn't wait to be asked about Samuel—they offered information freely.

- **Perseverance in the face of difficulty.** When Saul wanted to quit, the servant convinced him to continue. When obstacles appeared, the servant found solutions rather than complaints.

- **Being willing to serve, not just rule.** The servant had less at stake but more commitment. He didn't do the minimum required—he offered his own money, his own initiative, his own effort.

- **Taking responsibility for the mission, not for yourself.** A true leader says, "This problem is mine to solve," not "Someone else should fix this."

Jesus taught this same principle when He said:

> Whoever wants to become great among you must be your servant, and whoever wants to be first must be slave of all.

Leadership isn't a title you receive or a position you hold. It's a commitment you make to serve something larger than yourself.

### The Consultant Problem

Too much of what we call "leadership" today is actually consulting. A consultant sees a problem, offers ten solutions, collects a fee, and leaves. The consultant doesn't stay to solve the problem. The consultant doesn't take responsibility.

But a leader says, "This is my problem. I'm going to be part of the solution. I'm going to stay until it's fixed."

If you see something wrong in your workplace, your church, your family, your school—and all you do is complain about it or point out how someone else should fix it, you're not leading. You're criticizing. Real leadership is getting involved, getting your hands dirty, and being willing to fail while trying to make things better.

### Being Equipped for Leadership

There's an important truth here: God will equip those He calls. When Saul was anointed, God provided the Spirit. God didn't leave Saul unprepared. The problem wasn't that Saul lacked ability. The problem was that Saul lacked courage and commitment.

> Matthew 10:19-20: But when they arrest you, do not worry about what to say or how to say it. At that time you will be given what to say, for it will not be you speaking, but the Spirit of your Father speaking through you.

God equips us for the tasks He assigns. But we have to accept the assignment. We have to step into the role. We have to be willing to do the work.

> 1 Peter 4:10-11: Each of you should use whatever gift you have received to serve others, as faithful stewards of God's grace in its various forms. If anyone speaks, they should do so as one who speaks the very words of God. If anyone serves, they should do so with the strength God provides, so that in all things God may be praised through Jesus Christ.

Being a Christian isn't complicated. We're called to recognize the gifts God has given us and use them in service to others. We're equipped. We're sent. The question is: will we go?

## The Greater King

The story of Saul raises a haunting question: if this is the king Israel found, is this the king that can save them?

The scoundrels who questioned Saul were asking the right question, even if they asked it mockingly: "How can this fellow save us?" The answer was: he couldn't. Not really. Saul could fight battles and win victories, but he couldn't save Israel from what they truly needed saving from.

For that, they would need another king.

Thousands of years later, when Jesus came to Jerusalem, He entered the city on a donkey. The crowds recognized Him:

> When he came near the place where the road goes down the Mount of Olives, the whole crowd of disciples began joyfully to praise God in loud voices for all the miracles they had seen: "Blessed is the king who comes in the name of the Lord! Peace in heaven and glory in the highest!"

Jesus found the donkey. And unlike Saul, who hid from his calling, Jesus embraced His. He didn't hide among the supplies. He rode openly into the city, teaching and calling people to repent. He spoke without hesitation. He acted with courage.

And what He came to save us from wasn't just external enemies—it was ourselves. Our sin, our brokenness, our distance from God. That's a salvation only the true King could accomplish.
