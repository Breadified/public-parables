# Luke 18:15-30

**Title:** An Impossible Friend?
**Date:** 19 May 2012
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## What Makes a Friendship Impossible?

We often hear claims about impossible friendships—whether it's the premise that a deep, romantic-free friendship between a man and woman cannot exist, or the reality that some people simply have incompatible personalities. But today, Jesus teaches us about the most paradoxical friendship of all: an impossible friendship with God that becomes possible through Him.

## The Rich Ruler's Question

> A certain ruler asked him, "Good teacher, what must I do to inherit eternal life?" (Luke 18:18)

This ruler is a man of significance and influence—someone important in his community. Yet unlike the arrogant Pharisee we encountered last week, this ruler approaches Jesus with genuine humility. He doesn't assume he already possesses eternal life. Instead, he asks the fundamental question that echoes from all who recognize our mortality and wonder about the world beyond death: *What must I do to inherit eternal life?*

## The Law's Promise

Jesus answers with the law itself:

> "You know the commandments: Do not commit adultery, do not murder, do not steal, do not give false testimony, honor your father and mother." (Luke 18:20)

*Do the law and you will live.* This is the same answer Jesus gives elsewhere—the summary of God's commands that promise life to those who obey them.

## The Ruler's Confidence

The ruler responds with excitement:

> "ALL these I have kept since I was a boy." (Luke 18:21)

Here is a man who, by any external measure, has succeeded. He has kept these commandments since his youth. He has not committed adultery, murder, or theft. He has honored his parents. In the strictest legal sense, he can claim to have done all these things. If the law brings life, then surely he inherits eternal life.

## The One Thing Lacking

Yet Jesus offers a surprising evaluation:

> "You still lack ONE thing." (Luke 18:22)

In contrast to the many commandments the ruler has kept, Jesus identifies a single, critical absence. And what is that one thing?

> "Sell everything you have and give to the poor, and you will have treasure in heaven. Then come, follow me." (Luke 18:22)

There is a wordplay here—kept *all*, lacks *one*, must sell *all*. But more importantly, Jesus has exposed the ruler's true condition. He is missing the foundational commandment: *Love the Lord your God.* The ruler has kept the horizontal laws (relationships with others) but has failed in the vertical relationship—his connection to God Himself.

> When he heard this, he became very sad, because he was a man of great wealth. (Luke 18:23)

This is a deeply sad moment. A man humble enough to ask the right question, yet unwilling to follow through when Jesus answers. He walks away, choosing his wealth over the invitation to follow.

## Why This Test Matters

### He Lacked Love for God

For all the good deeds the ruler has performed, the issue is not his morality—it is that he does not want to follow Jesus. *Follow me!* And he becomes sad.

The first four commandments concern our relationship with God: no gods before Him, no idols, no misuse of His name, and honoring the Sabbath. The ruler has neglected these. What is lacking is an attitude of trust in God, a recognition that we are called to *follow Him*, not merely to be nice to one another.

### The Achilles Heel

This is his one vulnerability—his wealth. Like Achilles, invulnerable everywhere except at his heel, the ruler can succeed in every area of life except this one: his idol, the thing he puts before God, the thing he would sacrifice his Sabbaths and his devotion for.

We each have our Achilles heel. For you, it might be:
- A relationship that means more to you than your walk with God
- The relentless drive for success and advancement
- Studies or career ambitions that consume all your energy
- A professional identity so strong that you compromise your ethics, your family, and your spiritual health to climb higher

God challenges us in these areas not out of nastiness or pettiness, but as an act of mercy. He is trying to inoculate you against that vulnerability. He wants to ensure that one thing will not be a source of stumbling, will not become your master, will not waste your life or leave you with empty regrets.

**What is your one thing—the part of your life you're unwilling to surrender to God?** That is precisely what God will challenge you to release.

## The Problem with Wealth

### An Impossible Friendship

Jesus looks at His disciples and says:

> "How hard it is for the rich to enter the kingdom of God! Indeed, it is easier for a camel to go through the eye of a needle than for a rich man to enter the kingdom of God." (Luke 18:24-25)

This is a vivid picture of the impossible—the smallest opening with the largest animal. It is meant to convey absolute impossibility.

The fundamental problem is this: the rich do not need God. When you have wealth, comfort, and security, there is no desperation that drives you to trust in Him. The rich believe they can manage their own lives. And there is the friendship—an impossible one, because you cannot serve two masters:

> "No servant can serve two masters. Either he will hate the one and love the other, or he will be devoted to the one and despise the other. You cannot serve both God and Money." (Luke 16:13)

We tell ourselves: *I don't love money, just what it buys. I don't love money, I just don't want to let it go.* But friends, we do love money—personally and as a nation. We see it in our national budget, in our aid contributions, in our reluctance to share generously even when we are among the richest nations on earth.

### A Possible Friendship Through a Generous Friend

But then comes the hope:

> Those who heard this asked, "Who then can be saved?" Jesus replied, "What is impossible with men is possible with God." (Luke 18:26-27)

*Possible with God.* This is the gospel. Christ, who gave all. He came to earth already poor, a carpenter without the trappings of a prince. And He gave up everything—His claims, His rights, His dignity, and ultimately His life on the cross. He gave up His love for everyone.

And because of that, Peter can say:

> "We have left all we had to follow you!" (Luke 18:28)

Jesus responds:

> "I tell you the truth, no one who has left home or wife or brothers or parents or children for the sake of the kingdom of God will fail to receive many times as much in this age and, in the age to come, eternal life." (Luke 18:29-30)

The path to eternal life is not through keeping rules or accumulating wealth. It is through seeking His kingdom. When you prioritize His kingdom, He provides:

> "But seek his kingdom, and these things will be given to you as well." (Luke 12:31)

## Living as a Possible Friend

### We Have Left All to Follow You

If you are not yet a Christian, you might worry about the cost of following Jesus. But consider what you gain—treasure in heaven that cannot be stolen or destroyed.

If you are already a Christian, you have already decided to sit loosely with this world and its wealth. You signed up because you knew the real treasure is in heaven. So now, when God asks for financial support—whether for the church, for the poor, for missionaries, or for the gospel—your response should never be reluctance born from unwillingness to part with money. That makes no sense. God has been generous to you; you should be generous in return.

Don't fear Jesus's command to "sell all you have." This doesn't mean you must give away every dollar the moment you earn it. Rather, it's an acknowledgment that periodically, after accumulating, we are willing to give. We do this for holidays, for house investments, for migrations. We can also do it for gospel reasons—to support missionaries, to be generous, to use our wealth for eternal purposes.

Use your wealth strategically:

> Sell everything you have and give to the poor, and you will have treasure in heaven. (Luke 18:22)

Make the exchange: temporal possessions for heavenly treasures. Money is not evil; it can be used for good. But Jesus invites us to *use it*—not to hoard it.

Children receive toys and love them for a moment, but those toys become tomorrow's junk. Yes, enjoy what you have. Yes, thank God for it. But stop investing in future junk. Invest in treasures in heaven—treasures that neither thieves nor moths can destroy, treasures that will last forever.

## The Final Question

The ruler in our story spent his life accumulating wealth and maintaining a facade of righteousness. But he walked away from Jesus because he could not release his grip on what he thought mattered most.

What is stopping you today? What is it that keeps you from becoming a friend of God? Is it an unwillingness to trust Him? Is it a refusal to release control?

> Let the little children come to me, and do not hinder them, for the kingdom of God belongs to such as these. I tell you the truth, anyone who will not receive the kingdom of God like a little child will never enter it. (Luke 18:16-17)

The path forward is simple: give up your grip on what you think you must control, make it secondary, and follow Him. Be a friend of God—not by your own effort, but by receiving the friendship He generously offers.
