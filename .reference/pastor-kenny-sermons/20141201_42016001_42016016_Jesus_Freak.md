## Introduction: The Power of Popularity

The film "The Greatest Showman" tells the story of P.T. Barnum, one of the most famous men of the 19th century. At the height of his fame, hundreds of millions visited his museums and circuses. World leaders—Edward VII, Queen Victoria, Abraham Lincoln—and legendary figures like Charles Dickens, Mark Twain, and Thomas Edison all came to see him. Barnum was the definition of popular, commanding unprecedented influence and admiration.

Jesus, too, was interested in popularity. But He teaches us something startling about how we should pursue it.

## 1. Gain Friends Through Wisdom

> "I tell you, use worldly wealth to gain friends for yourselves, so that when it is gone, you will be welcomed into eternal dwellings." (Luke 16:9)

Jesus tells a parable about a wealthy man's manager who has been accused of wasting his possessions.

> "There was a rich man whose manager was accused of wasting his possessions." (Luke 16:1)

The "manager" here is like a personal assistant or accountant—someone entrusted with the master's resources. The Greek word for "wasting" is the same term used in the Parable of the Prodigal Son, meaning to squander or scatter resources to nowhere. Perhaps he neglected the business, played games, watched dramas all day—essentially scattering his master's wealth without care.

Facing termination, the manager realizes his predicament:

> "The manager said to himself, 'What shall I do now? My master is taking away my job. I'm not strong enough to dig, and I'm ashamed to beg.'" (Luke 16:3)

This is a vivid illustration: he's too refined for manual labor, too proud to accept welfare. He's a white-collar worker with limited options. But desperation breeds ingenuity.

> "I know what I'll do so that, when I lose my job here, people will welcome me into their houses." (Luke 16:4)

He needs favors. He needs patrons. He needs friends—and he needs them fast.

He calls in his master's debtors and grants them extraordinary discounts:

> "So he called in each one of his master's debtors. He asked the first, 'How much do you owe my master?' 'Nine hundred gallons of olive oil,' he replied. The manager told him, 'Take your bill, sit down quickly, and make it four hundred and fifty.'" (Luke 16:5-6)

A 50% discount. Then to another:

> "And how much do you owe? 'A thousand bushels of wheat,' he replied. He told him, 'Take your bill and make it eight hundred.'" (Luke 16:7)

A 20% discount. What's happening here is debated:

- Some suggest creative accounting—he's forgiving debts he had no authority to forgive
- Others see a win-win: he recovers at least some funds for his master while making friends
- Still others believe he's simply taking his commission as a debt collector, which was his legitimate right

But the core strategy is clear: he's granting favors to create future obligations. **It's not what you know, but who you know.** This is the world's wisdom about networking.

I worked in an accounting firm where this pattern was common. When accountants changed jobs, they'd reach out to their former clients because they'd treated them well in the past. Those clients would then send work back to them, creating a mutual benefit cycle. This is shrewd, practical thinking about relationships and reciprocity.

Then comes the surprising response:

> "The master commended the dishonest manager because he had acted shrewdly." (Luke 16:8)

We're puzzled: Why would the master praise a "dishonest" manager? The Greek word *adikios* typically means unjust or unrighteous. But in this context, it may mean something more like "worldly" or "impious"—someone disconnected from God's values. Jesus isn't endorsing dishonesty; rather, He's noting that:

> "The sons of this world are more shrewd in dealing with their own kind than are the people of the light." (Luke 16:8b)

This worldly manager exhibits a quality that Christians often lack: **shrewd wisdom**. Not cunning in a deceptive sense, but astuteness—the ability to perceive a predicament clearly and take decisive action. He doesn't sit paralyzed by his circumstances. He uses his earthly wit to secure his future.

Compare this with the description of a wise manager elsewhere:

> "Who then is the faithful and wise manager, whom the master puts in charge of his servants to give them their food allowance at the proper time?" (Luke 12:42)

The same Greek word for wisdom. The parable isn't about the manager's dishonesty; it's about his **wisdom in making friends**. His street smarts, his discernment, his refusal to be a victim—these are commendable. He recognizes his dire situation and uses every resource at his disposal to address it.

## 2. Making Friends on Earth Costs Something

In childhood, friendship seems free and spontaneous. But as we grow older, friendship requires investment.

**Friendship costs time.** When you're juggling full-time work or exams, spending hours with friends means sacrificing study time, sleep, or career advancement. **Friendship costs opportunity.** Staying in one city to maintain friendships may mean passing up a better job offer elsewhere. **Friendship costs money.** Joining a sports team, going out for coffee, visiting friends in other cities—it all requires resources.

Jesus is saying: **Use your worldly wealth to make friends.**

Consider the wealthy families who send their children to exclusive private schools. On paper, these schools may rank lower academically than certain public schools. From the outside, it seems like a waste of money. But these parents understand something we often miss: they're not primarily buying an education. **They're buying friendships.**

What are the life prospects of a child who isn't just friendly with the local accountant, but who is best friends with the daughter of a Federal Minister, the son of a billionaire, or a member of a royal family? These parents are shrewd. They understand that **in the world, it's not what you know, it's who you know.** They're willing to use their wealth to help their children gain connections that could open doors for a lifetime.

If this makes sense for worldly gain, how much more should it matter for heavenly gain?

The chance of remaining faithful to Christ increases dramatically when you have friends who share your faith and commitment. Yet how much do we actually invest in church friendships? We attend once a week, maybe twice, for a couple of hours. Meanwhile, our closest friends from school spent 35 hours a week with us for six years. Then we wonder why we don't have deep friendships at church.

Jesus invites us: **Invest in friendships with other believers.** Stay a little longer after service. Join a group. Commit to regular attendance. Use your resources—your time, your presence, your energy—to build relationships with people who will strengthen your walk with God. We may not be princes or princesses, but we are sons and daughters of the Most High God.

## 3. Gain Friends in Heaven

Yet Jesus' primary concern isn't friendship on earth. Look again at verse 9:

> "I tell you, use worldly wealth to gain friends for yourselves, so that when it is gone, you will be welcomed into eternal dwellings." (Luke 16:9)

Notice the parallel with verse 4:

> "When I'm removed from management, people will welcome me into their homes." (v.4)

But now we're talking about God's realm. "When it is gone" means when we die. "Eternal dwellings" is the house of God:

> "Look down from heaven, your holy dwelling place, and bless your people." (Deuteronomy 26:15)

> "My Father's house has many rooms; if that were not so, would I have told you that I am going there to prepare a place for you?" (John 14:2)

The question becomes: **Will you be welcomed into God's house?**

This parable isn't about finding friends on earth so you have a couch to crash on when you're homeless. It's about finding friends—making a friendship—who will welcome you into God's eternal home. And Jesus makes this unmistakably clear:

> "No one can serve two masters. Either you will hate the one and love the other, or you will be devoted to the one and despise the other. You cannot serve both God and money." (Luke 16:13)

**Will God welcome you as a friend into His house?**

### The Problem: We've Forgotten the Giver

We are not naturally God's friends. We ignore God because we idolize material things. Rather than acknowledge the Giver, we see only the gifts and treasure them more than the One who gave them.

Consider this: Your parents and grandparents sacrificed enormously for you. They may have immigrated, learned a new language, worked two jobs, and devoted every spare dollar to your education and tutoring. And when you graduate from the best schools, you say, "I did it!" You forget them.

We forget those who came before us—those who established universities, built schools, created systems of law and democracy, constructed the infrastructure of roads and buildings that make our land habitable. We forget God. Behind all of human achievement, God is the Creator. He brought all things into being, breathed life into living creatures, and gave us the intellect and abilities we take for granted.

Who brought you through the stress of exams you thought you'd fail? Who made the final cut when you were on the reserve list? Who healed you when you were sick?

### The Test: Will You Use Everything for God?

By the end of our lives, we should be able to say to God that we used everything. No reserves left. No safety net. We jumped as high and as far as we could, relying only on Him to catch us.

> "Whoever can be trusted with very little can also be trusted with much, and whoever is dishonest with very little will also be dishonest with much. So if you have not been trustworthy in handling worldly wealth, who will trust you with true riches? And if you have not been trustworthy with someone else's property, who will give you property of your own?" (Luke 16:10-12)

We've been blessed with immense wealth and privilege. Will we use all our resources—money, time, energy, our hearts, our minds, our shrewdness—to follow God and expand His kingdom? Will we call others to be God's friends too?

If we're ungrateful to God for what we have on earth, why would He entrust us with anything in the life to come?

Some of you are still searching for God. Use all your resources to search for Him. Reach out via a comment card. I'm willing to meet with you, walk through the evidence, read Scripture together, and discuss difficult questions. But this will cost you something—time and effort. Are you going to say, "I'm too busy; I have to make money and other friends"?

The Pharisees rejected this teaching:

> "The Pharisees, who loved money, heard all this and were sneering at Jesus." (Luke 16:14)

They loved money more than God. And they sneered at Jesus for calling them to something higher. They forgot a fundamental truth: **cash is trash.** Money exists to be used for God.

If you cannot follow God in this life, what makes you think you can follow Him in the next? If you're not friends with God now, you won't be friends with God then. **Now is the time of testing.** Now we use all our resources to follow God.

> "And whoever does not carry their cross and follow me cannot be my disciple." (Luke 14:27)

Don't let ambition stop you from being friends with God. Don't let fear of commitment stop you. Don't even let other friendships stop you from prioritizing your friendship with Him.

As P.T. Barnum said: "No one ever made a difference by being like everyone else." Be willing to be different. Be willing to be a freak for God.

## 4. The Ultimate Friendship

But here's the most remarkable thing: We are not naturally God's friends. We're actually His enemies—rebellious and separated from Him by our sin. Yet God reaches out to us with an offer of friendship:

> "Greater love has no one than this: to lay down one's life for one's friends. You are my friends if you do what I command. I no longer call you servants, because a servant does not know his master's business. Instead, I have called you friends." (John 15:13-15)

Jesus called us friends. And He proved it by laying down His life for us.

This coming Friday is Good Friday. We remember that first Good Friday when Jesus died in the most agonizing way—crucified on a cross—for us. Like Hugh Jackman's character rushing into the raging fire to rescue the helpless, Jesus entered the fires of God's judgment without hesitation to rescue us.

He gave up all His resources. When all Jesus had left was His life, He gave even that. He gave up His popularity and became a freak—despised, mocked, abandoned.

**Because we are His friends.**

The manager made many friends that day through shrewd strategy. But the most important friendship he gained was with his master, who commended him.

Our most important friendship is the one we maintain even when all others abandon us. Even when being a Christian makes us freaks in the eyes of the world.

**Accept His friendship.** Don't delay this decision. Reach out via a comment card and we'll get back to you. But don't hold this off.

In the end, the measure of our lives won't be our popularity or our wealth. It will be whether we used everything we had to pursue friendship with God—and whether we invited others into that same friendship.

**Friends forever.**
