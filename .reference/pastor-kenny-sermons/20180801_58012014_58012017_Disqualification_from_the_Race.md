# Hebrews 12:14-17

**Title:** Disqualification from the Race

**Date:** 26 August 2018

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Pursue Peace and Holiness (Verse 14)

The Christian life is portrayed as a race—not merely about reaching the finish line, but about running correctly. We must make every effort to live at peace with everyone and pursue holiness, for without holiness, no one will see the Lord.

The Greek word translated "make every effort" (diōkō) carries the sense of hot pursuit—to chase after something with energy and determination. It's the language of persecution and pursuit, yet here it describes our pursuit of peace. The persecuted congregation is ironically urged to pursue peace with the same intensity that their enemies pursue them.

### Peace with Everyone

To pursue peace means to actively welcome others and give them the benefit of the doubt. Like Rahab in Joshua 2, who could have been hostile to the Israelite spies but instead received them with peace, we are called to extend peace rather than conflict.

Life is inherently political—we encounter people we connect with and others we don't. Different personalities mean that sometimes we get our way and sometimes we don't. Rather than viewing relationships as a constant win-or-lose battle, we should aim for a little-win/little-win approach. We give people chances. We extend grace.

There is a troubling current in contemporary culture—imported largely from university campuses—of perpetual criticism and assumption of evil intent. Labels like "racist" and "sexist" are shouted without comprehension. Those who disagree are called "deniers." When you call someone Hitler, the logical conclusion is assassination. This is not peace; this is war. We are called to live at peace with each other, and in our brotherhood, we should give each other more rope.

### Pursuit of Holiness

We are to pursue holiness—separation from the common, the sacred. In the Old Testament temple, there was the holy place where only priests could enter, and the Holy of Holies where only the high priest could go. Without holiness, no one can see God.

In Exodus, Moses was told that no one could see God's face and live. God's absolute separation from evil is illustrated by the analogy of an eyeball and dirt—the sacred cannot coexist with the profane without pain or destruction.

Yet in Jesus, God finally accommodates Himself to sinful and mortal humanity. Through His sacrifice on the cross, the price is paid for us to see God. When Jesus died, the veil separating the people from God was torn in two. Now the common person can meet with the sacred. Christians are those who see God through Jesus.

### The Connection Between Peace and Holiness

While it may seem odd to pair "peace" and "holiness" together, we can understand them as pointing toward two relationships: peace toward all people, and holiness toward God. This echoes the greatest commandment: love the Lord your God with all your heart, and love your neighbor as yourself (Matthew 22:37-39).

The Jacob narrative in Genesis illuminates these themes. Jacob pursued peace with his estranged brother Esau through deliberate gifts and reconciliation. Yet Jacob's transformation came through struggle and discipline. He wrestled with God at the Jabbok River, was wounded in the hip, and emerged renamed Israel—"he who struggles with God and with men." Despite his pain and limp, he saw God and lived. Jacob was rewarded for his perseverance: he received blessing and encountered the divine.

The Christian life requires the same active pursuit—of peace with others and holiness toward God—combined with the willingness to endure discipline and pain in that pursuit.

## Warnings Against Falling Away (Verses 15-16)

We must see to it carefully that no one falls short of God's grace. The word "see to it" (skopeō) suggests earnest, careful looking. "Falls short" means to fall away from the race, to stumble, to abandon the pursuit. It is in stark contrast to the pursuit we are called to maintain.

When we fall away from grace, the opposite of peace emerges: a bitter root grows up to cause trouble and defile many. In Deuteronomy 29:18, Moses warns Israel about idolatry—a bitter poison that will ruin the covenant relationship. The root of bitterness produces the fruit of bitterness.

The example given is Esau. He represents the rejection of faith and the pursuit of momentary desires over eternal blessing. Though Esau was the firstborn—holder of the birthright and the father's blessing—he despised these precious inheritances.

When Esau came in hungry from hunting, Jacob offered him lentil stew in exchange for his birthright. Esau agreed. "So Esau despised his birthright." In a moment of hunger and desire, he traded something infinitely precious for something cheap and temporary. The etymology of "sexually immoral" (porneia) relates to "to sell"—Esau sold out, much like prostitution sells something sacred for a pittance.

His godlessness led to this lust. His desires overruled his obedience. He chose the flesh's satisfaction over God's spiritual blessings. Blinded by hunger and desire, he did not want to pursue holiness. He had no regard for the things of God. To him, his birthright was worthless.

Later, we learn that Esau married foreign women who brought their idols into his life (Genesis 28). His true character was revealed in that one act of despising his birthright—he despised God.

When the time came for Isaac to bless his sons, Esau discovered too late what he had lost. He wept with bitter tears, begging for a blessing. But what was done could not be undone. He fell away from grace and was disqualified.

The root of bitterness—whether from unaddressed grievances, unforgiven wrongs, or the pursuit of idols—poisons not just ourselves but many others around us.

## The Reality of Disqualification (Verse 17)

Afterward, when Esau sought to inherit the blessing, he was rejected. Even his tears could not change what he had done. He was disqualified.

Throughout history, athletes have trained, sacrificed, and persevered only to be disqualified for breaking the rules:

- **Marion Jones** won five Olympic medals at Sydney 2000 through years of dedication and discipline. Yet later, she admitted to using performance-enhancing drugs. She was stripped of all her medals and imprisoned. Her disqualification affected not only her individual medals but also the relay teams she was part of—the entire U.S. women's 4x100 and 4x400 relay teams lost their gold medals as well.

- **Rosie Ruiz** appeared to win the 1980 Boston Marathon but had taken the subway for much of the race, running only the final stretch.

- **Julie Miller** was disqualified from the 2015 Ironman Canada race when video evidence showed she had skipped one of the laps.

- **Sally Robbins**, rowing in Australia's women's eight in the 2004 Olympic final, simply stopped rowing with 500 meters remaining. Physically exhausted, she slumped back in her boat. Her crew, leading the race and certain of victory, finished fifth. Her teammates never rowed with her again.

An athlete's disqualification affects everyone depending on them. In a relay race, one person's failure strips the medals from all who carried the baton. This is the reality of falling away: it doesn't just affect you.

### The Warning to Persevere

Yet the warning is not without hope. Hebrews 6:4-12 acknowledges a terrible possibility: those who have tasted God's grace, shared in the Holy Spirit, and experienced God's goodness may fall away. But it also offers encouragement: "Dear friends, we are convinced of better things in your case—the things that have to do with salvation. God is not unjust; he will not forget your work and the love you have shown him."

We are urged to show diligence to the very end and to imitate those who through faith and patience inherit what has been promised. We must not become lazy. We must copy the perseverance of those who endure—like Jacob, who wrestled through the night and emerged transformed.

Jacob represents the persevering, enduring people of faith journeying toward Mount Zion. Esau represents those who reject the life of faith and are ultimately rejected at the mountain of God.

The stakes are eternal. We are not merely running a personal race; we are part of a relay team passing the baton forward. Our faithfulness or falling away affects those who depend on us.

---

## Conclusion

Don't trade the eternal for the momentary. Don't exchange your inheritance in heaven for temporary cash or possessions. Don't sell what is sacred for a bowl of lentil soup. Don't trade the things of God for fleeting satisfaction that lasts only moments.

Pursue peace and holiness with the same hot pursuit that characterized Jacob's journey—with discipline, with pain if necessary, with determination to see God. Run the race in the right way. And endure to the end, knowing that your faithfulness matters not just for you, but for all who follow you.
