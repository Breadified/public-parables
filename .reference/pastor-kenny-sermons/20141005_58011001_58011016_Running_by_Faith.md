## Eric Liddell: A Portrait of Faith in Action

One hundred years ago, at the 1924 Paris Olympics, a Scottish runner named Eric Liddell faced an impossible choice. At just 22 years old, he was the favorite to win the 100-meter sprint—his best event. But then he discovered that the Olympic heats were scheduled for Sunday.

As a committed Christian, Eric believed Sunday belonged to the Lord. Despite all his training, despite being the favorite, despite intense pressure from officials and media who called him a traitor to his nation, he chose to forfeit his best chance at a gold medal. Why? Because he had faith.

## What Does Biblical Faith Actually Mean?

### Faith and Definition (11:1)

The Hebrews were wavering in their commitment to Christ because persecution was mounting. The author reminds them of what faith truly is—not a feeling, but a substantive trust in God's promises.

> Now faith is confidence in what we hope for and assurance about what we do not see. (Hebrews 11:1)

At first glance, this seems straightforward. But the Greek words carry deeper meaning than modern English captures.

**"Faith" (pistis) → "Reality" (hupostasis):** The Greek word *hupostasis* appears five times in the New Testament, three of which are in Hebrews (1:3, 3:14, 11:1). In Hebrews 1:3, it's translated as "substance"—describing how the Son is "the exact representation of his [God's] being." It's not about feelings; it's about objective reality and substance.

**"Assurance" (elegchos) → "Proof" or "Evidence":** The second part means evidence that proves something, like the evidence that convicts a criminal. It's not a subjective feeling but objective demonstration.

**Biblical faith, therefore:** Faith is the reality of what we hope for. Through faith, the unseen is proven and demonstrated.

### The Crucial Distinction: Faith Is Not Subjective

Here's where modern thinking leads us astray. When we say "faith" and "assurance" today, we typically mean feelings. "I feel confident, therefore I have faith. I feel certain, therefore I believe."

But this inverts the relationship. We don't have confidence because we feel confident. We have confidence because there is evidence. We don't have faith because we have feelings. We have faith because we trust in objective reality.

**Faith is not evidence itself—faith is what we do with evidence.**

Think of it this way: The word "believe" is a transitive verb. It requires an object. "I believe *what?*" Just as "enjoy" requires something to be enjoyed, "believe" requires something to be believed in. Faith isn't faith in faith; it's faith in something concrete—God's character and His promises based on evidence.

### A Critical Application

Often people say, "I don't have faith." If what you mean is that the evidence isn't convincing, then the solution is to gather more evidence. Examine the church's testimony. Read what Christians have documented. Study the Bible—the most tested document in history. Investigate Christianity—the most tested belief system. Explore commentaries and apologetics resources. Christianity welcomes scrutiny.

But sometimes people say, "I don't have faith" while simultaneously acknowledging they find the evidence convincing. When this happens, I say gently but directly: that's your problem, not God's problem.

It's like someone showing you evidence that smoking causes cancer, and you respond, "I don't believe it." Or presenting proof that the earth is round, and someone insists it's flat. That's not an evidence problem—that's a bias problem. Something deeper is at work: personal preference, past trauma, or attachment to another agenda.

And that, frankly, is a poor way to make decisions. Yet increasingly, we make our most important choices—marriage, career, finances, everything—based on feelings rather than reason. If you lead with your heart alone, you will fail.

## Faith and the Past (11:2-12)

The rest of chapter 11 looks backward through history to demonstrate what faith actually looks like. Faith is forward-looking trust that God will fulfill His promises, even when that fulfillment is invisible to us.

> For this is what the ancients were commended for. (11:2)

### Creation: Faith in God's Word

By faith, we understand that the universe was formed by God's command, so that what is seen was not made out of what was visible. (11:3)

In the beginning, the cosmos was void, dark, and empty. There was nothing visible. Then God spoke. Light came. He filled it with sun, moon, and stars. The invisible became visible. Through God's word—invisible reality became visible creation.

### Abel: Obedience Through Faith

By faith, Abel brought God a better offering than Cain. By faith he was commended as righteous when God spoke well of his offering. And by faith he still speaks, even though he is dead. (11:4)

Genesis doesn't tell us explicitly why God rejected Cain's offering but accepted Abel's. But Genesis 4:7 hints at it: Cain violated God's word, while Abel—by inference—obeyed it. Abel heard God's approval and listened. His faith meant obedience.

### Enoch: Faith That Transcends Death

By faith, Enoch was taken from this life, so that he did not experience death. He could not be found, because God had taken him away. For before he was taken, he was commended as one who pleased God. (11:5)

Enoch "did not experience death"—he was taken directly to see God. His faith pleased the Lord.

### Noah: Faith When the Future Is Invisible

By faith, Noah, when warned about things not yet seen, in holy fear built an ark to save his family. By his faith he condemned the world and became heir of the righteousness that is in keeping with faith. (11:7)

Noah received a warning about a coming flood. But he had never seen a flood. He didn't know what it would look like—yet he obeyed God in fear and obedience, building the ark despite the mockery of everyone around him. In the middle of the desert, no less. "It won't rain," they scoffed. Yet Noah trusted God's word.

### Abraham: The Ultimate Example of Faith

By faith Abraham, when called to go to a place he would later receive as his inheritance, obeyed and went, even though he did not know where he was going. (11:8)

Abraham received an audible call from God—evidence. So he went, not knowing where. That is faith: believing God's word even when the destination is unseen.

By faith he made his home in the promised land like a stranger in a foreign country; he lived in tents, as did Isaac and Jacob, who were heirs with him of the same promise. For he was looking forward to the city with foundations, whose architect and builder is God. (11:9-10)

By faith, even though Sarah was past childbearing age, she was enabled to bear children because she considered him faithful who had made the promise. (11:11)

And so from this one man, and he as good as dead, came descendants as numerous as the stars in the sky and as countless as the sand on the seashore. (11:12)

Abraham demonstrates what faith looks like across a lifetime. He saw God's promise from a distance. He believed God's character based on evidence of God's faithfulness. The ancients looked back at their descendants and saw God had kept His word.

### Why History Matters: Faith Built on Track Record

We tend to be skeptical of biblical history because it's history, not science or mathematics. But that's a misunderstanding. History is just as valid a way of knowing. It's actually the primary way we know anything about relationships—who to befriend, who to marry, who to trust.

You choose friends and spouses based on history. Has this person been reliable? Loyal? Punctual? Even with doubts initially, over time—through repeated faithfulness—you develop confidence. Conversely, someone unreliable never earns your trust.

Faith builds on this same principle. God is faithful. God has proven faithful throughout history. Therefore, we can trust God's future promises. This isn't magic. It's the logical inference: if God kept His word in the past, He will keep it in the future.

The ancients witnessed God's faithfulness across generations. The Hebrews saw their own existence as the fulfillment of God's ancient promises to Abraham. They had thousands of years of evidence that God keeps His word.

## Faith and the Present (11:13-16, 39-40)

### Seeing from a Distance

But here's the remarkable truth: We are in a better position than the ancients.

All these people were still living by faith when they died. They did not receive the things promised; they only saw them and welcomed them from a distance, admitting that they were foreigners and strangers on earth. (11:13)

The ancients didn't receive what they were promised. But they saw it—from far away. They welcomed the promise with their eyes even as they lived as pilgrims in a foreign land.

This is like immigrant families who leave everything for a country they've never seen. My parents came to Australia decades ago. They had never been here. They'd seen pictures. They had a vision of opportunity, free education, a better life. They sold everything and came. Not based on ignorance—they had relatives here, friends, good reputation, glimpses from television. Enough evidence. They moved forward in faith.

People who speak this way show that they are looking for a country of their own. If they had been thinking of the country they had left, they would have had opportunity to return. Instead, they were longing for a better country—a heavenly one. Therefore God is not ashamed to be called their God, for he has prepared a city for them. (11:14-16)

### We See Even More Clearly

Yet we in the present age have something the ancients didn't: we have seen the fulfillment.

These were all commended for their faith, yet none of them received the things promised, because God had planned something better for us so that only together with us would they be made complete. (11:39-40)

Look at Hebrews 12:2—we are called to fix our eyes on Jesus, the pioneer and perfecter of faith. Historically, we have already seen Jesus.

Consider Matthew 11:11: Among those born of women, none is greater than John the Baptist. Yet the least in the kingdom of heaven is greater than he. Why? Because John the Baptist saw Jesus the promised Messiah, while everyone after him has witnessed Jesus's death and resurrection. We know more than all the ancients combined.

They saw shadows. We see the substance.

- They didn't know His name. We know Jesus.
- They didn't know when He would come. We know—and we celebrate His birth annually.
- They didn't know how He would save the world. We know the stunning answer: God reconciled justice (we deserve punishment for sin) with forgiveness (we need freedom). The solution? Jesus bore our punishment in our place.

### The Advantage We Have Today

Imagine someone immigrating to Australia today. You have friends already living there. You can check reviews online. There are Asian shops so you won't miss home. Flights are affordable. If someone said, "That's too risky," we'd laugh, wouldn't we?

Or imagine you've already seen the HSC exam and know all the answers. Your predecessors took the test blind. They studied without knowing what would be asked. You have the answers. When you know the answers, how can you possibly say the test is harder?

If the ancients were willing to run the race without seeing the answers, how much more should we, who have seen the answers? We have better information. We have the finished work of Christ.

In your struggle against sin, you have not yet resisted to the point of shedding your blood. (Hebrews 12:4)

Most of us haven't faced persecution or martyrdom. The cost of faith for us is minimal compared to what the ancients endured. Yet we struggle. Who told you faith would be hard? Not Scripture. Thousands died to put the Bible in this simple form into your hands.

God has given us everything. He gave His Son. You want more evidence than that? This has all been placed on a plate and handed to us.

Yet we want to think emotionally. We just don't like it. We don't feel it. Don't take it for granted. You will miss it.

Instead, have faith in God.

## The Faith of the Ancients: A Cloud of Witnesses

By faith, the people passed through the Red Sea as on dry land; but when the Egyptians tried to do so, they were drowned. (11:29)

By faith the walls of Jericho fell down after they had been encircled for seven days. (11:30)

By faith the prostitute Rahab, because she welcomed the spies, was not killed with those who were disobedient. (11:31)

And what more shall I say? I do not have time to tell about Gideon, Barak, Samson and Jephthah, about David and Samuel and the prophets, who through faith conquered kingdoms, administered justice, and gained what was promised; who shut the mouths of lions, quenched the fury of the flames, and escaped the edge of the sword; whose weakness was turned to strength; and who became powerful in battle and routed foreign armies. Women received back their dead, raised to life again. (11:32-35)

But many suffered terribly for their faith.

There were others who were tortured, refusing to be released so that they might gain an even better resurrection. Some faced jeers and flogging, and even chains and imprisonment. They were put to death by stoning; they were sawed in two; they were killed by the sword. They went about in sheepskins and goatskins, destitute, persecuted and mistreated—the world was not worthy of them. They wandered in deserts and mountains, living in caves and in holes in the ground. (11:35-38)

Yet whether they experienced triumph or suffering, there's a common thread: they were commended for their faith. Even those who never received the promise. Even those who suffered. This is their testimony to us—faith is not measured by comfort or success. Faith is measured by trust in God's word.

### Jesus: The Perfect Example

Consider Jesus himself. He endured the cross, scorning its shame, and sat down at the right hand of the throne of God. (Hebrews 12:2)

Jesus too was faithful. He had Scripture promising His vindication (Psalm 16:10, Acts 2:31), but He didn't see the future either. In Gethsemane, His prayer was real—"If possible, let this cup be taken from me." Yet His submission was also real—"Yet not my will, but yours be done." He trusted God's character based on the evidence of God's love and God's word. And now, vindicated, He sits at God's right hand.

## The Nature of Faith: Building on Evidence

Faith is belief—and all belief rests on evidence (otherwise it's just superstition). Faith is believing, as all people believe about rain and marriage and every decision—based on sufficient evidence.

Faith cannot stand alone. If faith in faith is itself—faith in the abstract—that's not real. The question is always: how much evidence do we have?

We can never have 100% certainty. That's an impossible standard. Sufficiency means: no remaining objections, adequate time, and reasonable confidence.

We have Scripture. So once we believe, we act 100%. We don't marry someone halfway. We don't board a plane halfway. We commit completely.

But what about the part we cannot see? That's the evidence we're still waiting for. Yes, in the future we'll see it. But by then it's too late to act! You can't wait to see if a woman is a good wife—by the time you find out, she's married to someone else.

Faith is not about perpetually demanding new evidence. Just as with parents or friends, we don't require fresh proof every year simply because we've grown or our standards have changed. We trust based on accumulated history. Has God ever done anything foolish to make us question Him?

You've faced suffering. Didn't God warn you about trials? You've faced persecution. Didn't Jesus teach that the path is narrow? You find the rules restrictive. But we know the important parts. In marriage, if you discover your spouse has an annoying habit, you don't divorce. You rely on God's truth, not the weakness of our emotions.

## The Promise of a Heavenly City

Those who speak in such terms make it clear they are looking for a country of their own. But as it is, they desire a better country—that is, a heavenly one. (11:14-16)

Many of us know what it's like to long for a better place. Whether we ourselves have immigrated, or our parents, or our ancestors—that vision of something better, somewhere better, has shaped our families.

My parents never saw Australia. But they had a vision. They saw opportunity. They saw freedom and education and possibility. They sold everything and came.

This is what faith looks like: seeing the future based on evidence, then acting. Moving forward in faith.

This is what Abraham did his whole life. And this is what Joseph exemplified at the end of his:

By faith Joseph, when his end was near, spoke about the exodus of the Israelites from Egypt and gave instructions about his bones. (11:22)

His bones pointed toward the promised land. Joseph believed God's promise so thoroughly that his final instruction was about burial—a declaration that one day, Israel would leave Egypt and possess the land.

## Conclusion: Running by Faith

Eric Liddell didn't win the 100-meter race. But when he discovered the 400-meter heats fell on a day he could race with a clear conscience, he prepared for a different event. And on July 11, 1924, he ran an Olympic race for the ages. He sprinted the entire distance at full speed—a tactic that seemed reckless for a 400-meter race. But he won, and it remains one of the greatest moments in Olympic history.

After the Olympics, he became a missionary in China, dedicating his life to serving God and the people of Tianjin.

When asked about his tactics in that race, Liddell said: "Well, when the gun goes off, I run as fast as I can. I believe in God and I trust He'll give me strength to complete the second half."

He ran the Christian race the same way—with faith in God.

This is the faith of the ancients. This is the faith that pleases God. Without faith, it is impossible to please God, because anyone who comes to him must believe that he exists and that he rewards those who earnestly seek him. (11:6)

We have better information than they did. We have seen Jesus. We have witnessed His death and resurrection. We have the completed Scripture. We have the cloud of witnesses testifying to God's faithfulness across centuries.

Therefore, run by faith. Not by feeling. Not by emotion. But by trust in God's proven character and God's word. Look to Jesus, the pioneer and perfecter of faith. And remember: you have already been given the answers. All that remains is to act on them.
