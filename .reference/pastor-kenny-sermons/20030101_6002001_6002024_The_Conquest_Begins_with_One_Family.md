# Joshua 2:1-24

**Title:** The Conquest Begins with One Family
**Date:** January 2003
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## A Woman of the Immoral World Finds Faith

Before us stands a remarkable account of divine grace breaking into human darkness. We encounter Rahab—a prostitute, a liar, and a woman living in spiritual darkness—yet through this narrative God demonstrates that there is no sin beyond His redemptive reach, and no person outside the scope of His mercy.

---

## 1. The Immorality of Rahab (Joshua 2:1-7)

### A Life Steeped in Sin

The opening verses drip with irony and tension. Joshua sends two spies from Shittim—a place whose very name carries dark history. The last time Israel camped at Shittim, they fell into sexual immorality with the Moabites (Numbers 25). Now the spies enter a brothel and lodge with Rahab, a prostitute.

> Then Joshua son of Nun secretly sent two spies from Shittim. "Go, look over the land," he said, "especially Jericho." So they went and entered the house of a prostitute named Rahab and stayed there.

The word choice is deliberate—they "stayed there," literally "lay" or "slept" there. Sexual immorality permeates this opening scene.

### A Liar's Deception

But Rahab is not only sexually immoral. She is also a deceiver. When the king learns of the spies' presence, Rahab acts quickly to hide them, then lies to the pursuing soldiers:

> "Yes, the men came to me, but I did not know where they had come from. At dusk, when it was time to close the city gate, they left. I don't know which way they went. Go after them quickly. You may catch up with them."

She then hides the men under stalks of flax on her roof. The ironic result: the soldiers are sent outside the city while the spies remain safe within.

### The Question of Rahab's Sin

We must address the theological problem directly. Did Rahab sin? Evangelical scholars have long debated this:

**The "situation ethics" argument** suggests that love or circumstance can justify falsehood. Joseph Fletcher's approach claims that love "abolishes all law," potentially justifying lying, stealing, even abortion. But Scripture does not support this. Jesus taught that lying itself is the issue, not merely lying to harm someone (John 8:44). Truth-telling is God's standard.

**The "consequentialist" argument** claims that because no one has "a right to the truth that injures others," Rahab's lie was justified. Some have suggested she did not lie "against" her neighbor but "for" him. Yet this reasoning fails under scrutiny. The soldiers were sent on a false chase, and Rahab committed an act of treason against her own city.

Scripture is clear:

> The Lord detests lying lips, but he delights in people who are trustworthy. (Proverbs 12:22)

God wills us to speak truth (Ephesians 4:15). Paul himself refused deception in his ministry (1 Thessalonians 2:5). Augustine, Aquinas, and Calvin all acknowledged this: Rahab sinned. As Calvin wrote, though she acted from a good purpose, "it was not free from fault."

Yet the evangelical consensus remains: **God brought good from her situation, but that does not mean He approved of her lie.** Society itself cannot function without a culture of truth-telling. Contracts, law, business, science—all depend on the premise that people speak truthfully. A civilization built by Christians has thrived precisely because it was built on truth.

## 2. The Faith of Rahab (Joshua 2:8-13)

### From Knowledge to Personal Commitment

Despite her moral failings, Rahab seizes a transformative moment. Before hiding the spies, she goes to the roof and confesses something remarkable:

> I know that the Lord has given you this land and that a great fear of you has fallen on us, so that all who live in this country are melting in fear because of you... When we heard of it, our hearts melted in fear and everyone's courage failed because of you, for the Lord your God is God in heaven above and on the earth below.

She has **heard of God's works**. The drying up of the Red Sea. The destruction of Sihon and Og, the Amorite kings (Numbers 21). Stories that perhaps reached her as a girl—tales of a nation advancing before the true God. Now that judgment is at her door. Fear of the Lord is beginning in her—the beginning of wisdom (Proverbs 9:10).

But knowledge alone is not faith. Watch how Rahab moves from abstract acknowledgment to personal petition:

> Now then, please swear to me by the Lord that you will show kindness to my family, because I have shown kindness to you. Give me a sure sign that you will spare the lives of my father and mother, my brothers and sisters, and all who belong to them—and that you will save us from death.

The pronouns turn personal: "**my** family," "**my** father," "**my** mother." From collective fear ("we heard") to desperate love ("save **us** from death"). This is faith—moving from intellectual assent about God's power to personal trust in His mercy.

### The Sign of the Scarlet Cord

The spies respond with a covenant:

> "This oath you made us swear will not be binding on us unless, when we enter the land, you have tied this scarlet cord in the window through which you let us down... But if you tell what we are doing, we will be released from the oath you made us swear."

The scarlet cord is Rahab's sign—like the blood on the doorposts during Passover. When judgment comes, those inside the marked house are spared. Rahab must gather her family and stay within:

> If any of them go outside your house into the street, their blood will be on their own heads; we will not be responsible. As for those who are in the house with you, their blood will be on our head if a hand is laid on them.

### Rahab Praised for Faith, Not Works

The New Testament celebrates Rahab's transformation:

> By faith the prostitute Rahab, because she welcomed the spies, was not killed with those who were disobedient. (Hebrews 11:31)

> In the same way, was not even Rahab the prostitute considered righteous for what she did when she gave lodging to the spies and sent them off in a different direction? (James 2:25)

Notice what is **not** mentioned: her prostitution, her lying, her past. She is praised for one thing—**faith**. She recognized God's hand, aligned herself with His purposes, and received His mercy.

When the city fell, Joshua himself ensured her deliverance:

> Joshua spared Rahab the prostitute, with her family and all who belonged to her, because she hid the men Joshua had sent as spies to Jericho—and she lives among the Israelites to this day. (Joshua 6:25)

She had become, in the words of one commentator, "grafted into Israel." A Canaanite woman—an outsider, an enemy—received covenant blessing through faith. As Genesis promised, blessing would come to those of other nations through Israel (Genesis 12:3).

## 3. The Faith of Israel (Joshua 2:14-24)

### The Spies Keep Their Word

The spies respond to Rahab with integrity:

> "Our lives for your lives!" the men assured her. "If you don't tell what we are doing, we will treat you kindly and faithfully when the Lord gives us the land."

They speak of treating her "faithfully"—with trustworthiness, not deceit. Despite the dodgy circumstances, the spies model covenant faithfulness.

### A New Generation Passes the Test

When the spies return, they bring news that transforms Israel's resolve. Contrast this with the evil report of the spies forty years earlier (Numbers 13). That generation said, "We cannot enter." Their fear infected the whole nation, and they wandered forty years.

Now a new generation hears different words:

> The Lord has surely given the whole land into our hands; all the people are melting in fear because of us.

Unlike their fathers, these spies speak with conviction and faith. They are strong and courageous. They have seen what Rahab saw—that God's hand is over this conquest. Joshua had commanded them: "Be strong and courageous" (Joshua 1:6-7). Now the spies embody that virtue.

The three-day wait in the hills (Joshua 2:16, 22) echoes Israel's three-day wait at Sinai before receiving God's law. History is about to repeat itself—but this generation will not fail as their fathers did.

## Application: Hope for the Immoral in God's Judgment

### We Are All Rahab

The beauty of this account cuts to the heart: we are like Rahab. Through sin we are guilty of sexual immorality, deceit, and treason against God. We stand condemned. Yet there is no sin Christ cannot forgive.

God is not bloodthirsty or racist. He saved Rahab because she believed. The judgment that fell on Canaan was not genocide but capital punishment—the Canaanites had practiced idolatry, incest, adultery, child sacrifice, and homosexuality for generations. Genesis 15 records God's patience: "The sin of the Amorites has not yet reached its full measure." Four hundred years later, at Joshua's conquest, that measure was full.

But one woman—a prostitute and liar—recognized the true God and was spared.

### The Call to Faith

Will you go to God in prayer and plead for mercy as Rahab did? Will you declare your loyalty to Him? Will you ask Him to save you from coming judgment?

If you have not done so, perhaps today is that day.

Faith in God means welcoming Jesus. It means acknowledging that God judges sin. It means confessing that we are doomed apart from His grace. And it means believing that only through Jesus is there a way out—and that **He will forgive**:

> If we confess our sins, he is faithful and just and will forgive us our sins and purify us from all unrighteousness. (1 John 1:9)

### Do Not Repeat the Past

Learn from Israel's history. The old generation failed through unbelief and disobedience. Paul writes:

> These things occurred as examples to keep us from setting our hearts on evil things as they did... These things happened to them as examples and were written down as warnings for us, on whom the culmination of the ages has come. (1 Corinthians 10:6, 11)

Do not wander outside the house. In a world of immorality, stay within God's boundaries and limits. Stay close to His truth. Soon the walls will crumble—God's conquest is coming. Like Rahab, mark your house with the sign of the covenant and remain faithful.

### God's Faithfulness Demands Ours

Finally, note this: the spies were faithful to Rahab. They kept their word. This reflects the character of our God—faithful, trustworthy, true. We who bear His name must do likewise.

> Do not be conformed to this world... Rather be transformed by the renewing of your mind, that you may prove what is the good and acceptable and perfect will of God. (Romans 12:2)

When we turn to God through faith, He responds in kind with redemption, inclusion, and blessing. There is hope for the immoral, the deceiver, the outcast—anyone who recognizes God's hand and turns to Him in faith.
