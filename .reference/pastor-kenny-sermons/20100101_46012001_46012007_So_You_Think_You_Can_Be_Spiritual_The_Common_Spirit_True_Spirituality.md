# 1 Corinthians 12:1-7

**Title:** So You Think You Can Be Spiritual? The Common Spirit: True Spirituality
**Date:** 10 January 2010
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## What is True Spirituality?

This question troubled the Corinthian church deeply. Corinth was a multicultural harbor city in Greece, strategically positioned at a narrow strip of land connecting north and south. With harbors on both sides—one facing Asia, the other Italy—the city thrived on trade, commerce, and wealth. But alongside prosperity came the inevitable vices: sexual immorality, idolatry, and hedonism. It was a place where almost anything went.

Yet remarkably, Paul had planted a church there. But the Corinthian Christians reflected their city far too closely. Paul himself described them as "worldly—mere infants in Christ" (1 Corinthians 3:1). Their problems read like a catalog of spiritual disaster: sexual immorality, incest, divisions, taking one another to court, idolatry, selfishness, and greed so severe that some members were going hungry.

In reaction, some Corinthians swung to the opposite extreme, teaching that the body and all physical pleasure were evil. Confused about what spirituality actually meant, they wrote to Paul asking for clarity. His answer forms the foundation of these three weeks of study.

## 1. What Are "The Spirituals"?

> Now about the spirituals, brothers, I do not want you to be ignorant. (1 Corinthians 12:1)

The Greek word here is simply "the spirituals"—not "spiritual gifts," as many translations render it. This distinction matters enormously.

### Option A: Spiritual Gifts? (Popular but Problematic)

Many assume Paul is discussing spiritual gifts. But several problems emerge:

- The term "spiritual gifts" does not appear in the New Testament
- "Spiritual gift" (singular) appears only once—Romans 1:11—where Paul refers to the gospel, not a special talent
- Throughout 1 Corinthians, "spirituals" and "gifts" occupy separate territory:
  - Chapter 1:7 describes gifts but mentions no spirit
  - Chapter 7 discusses various gifts (marriage, singleness) with no reference to spirit
  - Chapter 12:27-30 extensively covers gifts without mentioning spirit
  - Chapter 2 emphasizes the Spirit's work with virtually no discussion of gifts

This slim connection is crucial because popular Christian teaching wrongly suggests that gifts come from the Spirit alone—making the Holy Spirit seem like "the Santa Claus of the Trinity," dispensing favors while God the Father appears stern and God the Son plays the hero. But Scripture itself reveals that the entire Godhead gives gifts (12:6, 12:11).

### Option B: Spiritual Truths (The Better Reading)

When Paul addresses "the spirituals," he means **spiritual truths**—specifically, the communication of God's truths to one another. The Corinthian problem centers on how believers speak God's Word. Who should speak? Whose voice should we listen to? How do we resolve disagreement? What order should govern our speech? These questions about spiritual truth-telling drive chapters 12-14.

## 2. Don't Be Ignorant of Spiritual Truths

> You know that when you were pagans, you were somehow led astray to mute idols. (1 Corinthians 12:2)

Paul insists: "I do not want you to be ignorant." True spirituality is not emotionalism, mysticism, empty meditation, or miraculous showmanship. True spirituality rests on **knowledge**—real understanding of the gospel that transforms us.

As pagans, the Corinthians worshiped mute idols: wooden or silver statues that could not speak or move. In stark contrast stands the God of the Spirit—a God who *speaks*, who *reveals himself*, who communicates truth.

Christian spirituality is not:
- Emotionalism or passionate feelings
- Ignorance or anti-intellectual faith
- Tarot cards, horoscopes, or empty meditation
- Miraculous signs or charismatic manipulation

Christian spirituality *is*: knowledge of the gospel that changes how we live.

## 3. The Spiritual Person Confesses Christ's Lordship

> No one who is speaking by the Spirit of God says, "Jesus be cursed," and no one can say, "Jesus is Lord," except by the Holy Spirit. (1 Corinthians 12:3)

If you want a sign of spirituality, don't look for emotion. Look for this: **Does this person declare that Jesus is Lord?**

This is no empty formula or chant. It means a wholehearted commitment to Jesus as the supreme authority of one's life. The gospel proclaims Christ's death and resurrection, establishing him as Lord of all creation. To possess the Spirit means to submit to his Lordship—not perfectly, but genuinely and increasingly.

Some live a divided life: spiritual on Sunday, worldly on Monday; righteous at church, compromised at work. Such people have not truly submitted to Christ's Lordship. Piety and emotional display cannot substitute for obedience.

### A Warning About "Second Baptism"

Ten to fifteen years ago, a harmful heresy spread: the idea of a "second baptism." The first baptism (water baptism) made you Christian. The second (Spirit baptism) made you *powerful*—confident, eloquent, excited about ministry. Only then were you a "real Christian."

This is false. When you first trusted that Jesus died for you and rose again, the Spirit came to you then. That was your true spiritual awakening—not some later emotional experience. When Christians later commit more seriously to their faith, they're not receiving the Spirit for the first time; they're finally responding to a Spirit they've always had.

## 4. The Ultimate Spiritual Truth: The Gospel

> There are different kinds of gifts, but the same Spirit. There are different kinds of service, but the same Lord. There are different kinds of working, but the same God works all of them in all men. (1 Corinthians 12:4-6)

Paul's emphasis is clear: the true mark of spirituality is not diversity of gifts but **unity in the gospel**. Every Christian possesses the same Spirit, serves the same Lord, and works under the same God.

> If we have sown spiritual seed among you, is it too much if we reap a material harvest from you? (1 Corinthians 9:11)

The "spiritual seed" is the gospel—God's Word sown into human hearts.

Some years ago, a prominent American Christian criticized Sydney Anglicans for believing only in "Father, Son, and Holy Bible." The critique was unfair. Yes, we emphasize Scripture, but the Holy Bible is inseparably linked to the Holy Breath of God. Is not God's Word called "the Sword of the Spirit"? The Spirit dwells in the Word.

What that critic really meant was that Sydney Anglicans lack the emotionalism and charismatic enthusiasm he preferred. Yet emotionalism is not the measure of spirituality. The measure is this: Do you know and submit to Jesus as Lord? Do you understand and live out the gospel?

Some today call themselves "Reformed Charismatics"—evangelicals seeking the emotional fervor of Charismatic churches like Hillsong. But true spirituality is not determined by how passionately you feel or how emotionally you worship.

## What True Spirituality Actually Looks Like

Someone might say our church lacks spirituality because we lack formal liturgy, because our music is simple, or because I don't raise my hands or cry from the pulpit. That assessment misses the point entirely.

True spirituality is a place where:
- The Bible is read and proclaimed
- Believers speak spiritual truth to one another
- People submit to Christ's Lordship
- God's Word cuts to the heart, convicting us of sin and our need to repent
- Day by day, members increasingly commit to God's agenda and God's values

I've spoken with some of you about sacrificing time, money, and effort for the sake of others and this church's growth. Those who said yes—who embraced greater suffering, commitment, and sacrifice—*they* demonstrated true spirituality.

To those who haven't yet: Will you commit to Christ's Lordship? Will you submit to him, follow his will, love one another, and care for God's mission? As we do this together, we become a place where true spirituality genuinely flourishes.
