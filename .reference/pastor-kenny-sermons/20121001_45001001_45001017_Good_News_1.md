# Romans 1:1-17

**Title:** Good News

**Date:** 21 October 2012 (SMAC); 19 November 2023 (ARISE)

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Problem: A World Full of Bad News

Every day we're bombarded with stories about war, terrorism, crime, pollution, inequality, and drug abuse. Whether online, in newspapers, on television, or radio, negative news dominates. Studies show that approximately 80% of headlines are negative—and there's a reason: bad news sells.

The media industry has a saying: "If it bleeds, it leads." Research from the journal *Nature Human Behavior* examined over 105,000 headlines and 370 million impressions from online news sources and found that each negative word increased the click-through rate by more than 2 percent. Conversely, positive words in headlines significantly decreased the likelihood of being clicked.

This creates a problem for us. We become fearful, anxious, and polarized. The constant diet of negative news shapes how we see the world. But there's an antidote to this relentless negativity: the good news of the Gospel.

## The Gospel: Jesus Christ Is Lord

The word "gospel" simply means "good news"—from the old English "good spell," or good words, or good message. Paul opens Romans by announcing that he is "set apart for the gospel of God" (Romans 1:1).

What makes this news so good? Paul summarizes it in four words at the end of verse 4:

> **"Jesus Christ is Lord."**

This is the heart of the good news.

### Jesus Christ in the Flesh

Paul explains that this good news concerns Jesus "as to his earthly life...a descendant [seed] of David" (Romans 1:3). To be a descendant of David meant being in line for the throne of Israel—Jesus is the rightful heir to the kingdom promised centuries before.

As the true king of the Jews, Jesus demonstrated His lordship through His works: He fed people, healed them, cast out demons, taught them, guided them, and loved them. And ultimately, He died for them.

### The Substitute: God's Solution to Our Problem

On the cross, Jesus acted as a substitute for our sins. Imagine a kindergarten child who deliberately smashes your brand-new phone. Who pays for it? You broke God's creation. We've sinned. We've hurt what is good and smashed it on the ground.

The problem is that we owe a debt we cannot pay. God, being just, cannot simply ignore our offense—that would be like a police officer ignoring someone being robbed. Justice demands payment.

But here's the deeper problem: we cannot withstand the punishment ourselves. We are weak. To remain under God's judgment would destroy us.

God's solution is the only one that satisfies both His justice and His love: **He punishes Himself.** On the cross, through Jesus, God submitted to punishment. Though innocent, He was declared guilty. Though immortal, He became mortal.

**This is the good news.**

### The Resurrection: Forever King

But Jesus did not stay dead.

> "and who through the Spirit of holiness was appointed the Son of God in power by his resurrection from the dead: Jesus Christ our Lord." (Romans 1:4)

Unlike earthly kings, who inevitably die and fade from power, Jesus is a king forever. His real power lies not in being good or great, but in being good and great **forever**. He is Lord over death, Lord over the grave, Lord over all creation.

This matters immensely. Dead prophets and dead gods cannot save us. Only the living One can. The cross symbolizes both Christ's sacrifice and His resurrection—the only power capable of salvation.

## The Power of Good News

> "For I am not ashamed of the gospel, because it is the power of God that brings salvation to everyone who believes: first to the Jew, then to the Gentile." (Romans 1:16)

The word "salvation" means to be rescued. The good news is a rescue from death, from judgment, and from hell. Hell is not a joke or a Halloween costume party. It is a place of absolute darkness where there is no family, no friends, no God—only eternal suffering and loneliness.

The more you appreciate the reality of hell, the more you appreciate what Christ has done for you. He has rescued us from judgment and given us hope eternal.

## Good News for Everyone

The remarkable thing about the Gospel is that it's not just for some people—it's for everyone.

Paul writes of receiving "grace and apostleship to call all the Gentiles to the obedience that comes from faith" (Romans 1:5). The Gospel is meant for Jews and Gentiles alike, for all people everywhere.

### We Are Set Apart for This Work

Paul was set apart as an apostle ("sent out") for the Gospel's sake. But Paul's calling extends beyond him. Through Ephesians, we learn:

> "For we are God's handiwork, created in Christ Jesus to do good works, which God prepared in advance for us to do." (Ephesians 2:10)

We too are set apart for the work of the Gospel. Not with the same apostolic authority as Paul, but with the same task: to be proclaimers and repeaters of the good news.

> "Speaking the truth in love, we will grow to become in every respect the mature body of him who is the head, that is, Christ." (Ephesians 4:15)

This is why verse 16 urges us: **"Do not be ashamed of the gospel."**

### Overcoming Our Hesitation

We often feel scared to talk about God and Jesus with others. We hesitate for many reasons:

- We doubt the Gospel's power to change anything
- We don't think we're like Paul or have a calling to evangelize
- We fear others will judge us as judgmental or self-righteous
- We're embarrassed to go "spiritual" in conversation
- We don't know how to transition from everyday topics to Jesus
- We worry that admitting our struggles might invite unwanted scrutiny
- We find it culturally offensive in a pluralistic world

But here's what we forget: preaching the Gospel, talking about the things of God—whether to strangers, children, or fellow believers; whether through sharing, correction, or teaching—builds us up. These conversations make us strong. They give us resilience and patience as we wait for Christ's return.

### Share the Good News

The best way to strengthen our faith is to keep meeting together regularly and encouraging one another. This is why gathering for worship, for Bible study, and for fellowship matters so much.

During this Christmas season especially, we have a unique opportunity to share the good news. While we can, without shame, we should speak about Jesus to those around us.

We are each other's reality. We live in the Gospel, shaped by it, and through us, the Gospel brings even more good news to the world.

## A Historical Note: Martin Luther and Romans

Five centuries ago, on Reformation Day (October 31, 1517), a monk named Martin Luther nailed 95 theses to a church door in Wittenberg, Germany, protesting certain practices of the medieval Church. His actions sparked the Protestant Reformation—a movement that changed history.

What inspired Luther's courage? He had read the Book of Romans.

In the Middle Ages, few people had access to Scripture; only educated monks in monasteries could study it. Luther, a trained lawyer, was amazed by what he discovered in Romans. He later wrote in his preface to his commentary on Romans:

> "This letter is truly the most important piece in the New Testament. It is purest Gospel."

> "We find in this letter...the richest possible teaching about what a Christian should know: the meaning of law, Gospel, sin, punishment, grace, faith, justice, Christ, God, good works, love, hope and the cross."

> "It seems that St. Paul wanted to compose a summary of the whole of Christian and evangelical teaching...Therefore each and every Christian should make this letter the habitual and constant object of his study."

The Book of Romans changed Luther's life—and through him, changed the world. Its message remains as powerful today: the Gospel is good news for all people, and it demands to be proclaimed.
