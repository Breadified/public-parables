# Mark 1:1-15

**Title:** The Time Has Come

**Date:** 11 April 2021

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Missing Out

We've all experienced the sting of missing out—losing a race, failing to get into a prestigious school, being outbid at an auction. It's a universal human disappointment.

Prince Philip's life illustrates this poignantly. Despite being a prince of both Greece and Denmark in his own right, with a distinguished naval career where he ranked first in his cadet class, he missed out on kingship. Even after marrying Queen Elizabeth, he never became king. Yet rather than dwelling on what he'd missed, he used his "extra time" remarkably—serving his country faithfully for decades, building a 74-year marriage, and demonstrating genuine greatness through humble service.

Today's passage explores the theme of missing out, but in a far more consequential way.

---

## The Beginning: Preparing for Judgment (1:1-6)

> The beginning of the good news about Jesus the Messiah, the Son of God

Mark opens with a prologue announcing something new. "Gospel" (good news) is not religious jargon—it's genuinely good news. But this version differs from Matthew and Luke: it begins with Jesus as an adult Messiah, not with his birth or early years.

### Understanding "Messiah"

**Messiah** literally means "the anointed one." In ancient Israel, important leaders were anointed with oil—this was how God set them apart, whether prophets (1 Kings 19:16), priests (Exodus 29:7, Leviticus 4:3), or kings (1 Samuel 2:10, Psalm 2:2). It was a title of tremendous significance, not a surname.

The Old Testament prophesied that the Messiah would come to accomplish two things: **to save and to judge**. This is crucial. Isaiah 61 describes the anointed one's mission:

> The Spirit of the Sovereign Lord is on me, because the Lord has anointed me to proclaim good news to the poor. He has sent me to bind up the brokenhearted, to proclaim freedom for the captives and release from darkness for the prisoners, to proclaim the year of the Lord's favor **and the day of vengeance of our God**.

Notice: salvation and judgment go together. This is not comfortable to hear, but it's biblical truth. Salvation only makes sense in the context of judgment. God's punishment for sins accumulates; there's a final accounting coming. When Jesus returns, that's the end.

This raises an unsettling question: *What if I'm not ready?*

### The Messenger Prepares the Way (1:2-3)

Isaiah also gave Israel a warning about when the Messiah might come:

> "I will send my messenger ahead of you, who will prepare your way"... "a voice of one calling in the wilderness, 'Prepare the way for the Lord, make straight paths for him.'"

When royalty travels, a team goes ahead—finalizing schedules, making preparations, announcing their arrival. Similarly, a messenger would precede the Messiah to prepare Israel. Isaiah 40:3-5 describes this preparation vividly:

> A voice of one calling: "In the wilderness prepare the way for the Lord; make straight in the desert a highway for our God." Every valley shall be raised up, every mountain and hill made low; the rough ground shall become level, the rugged places a plain. And the glory of the Lord will be revealed, and all people will see it together.

The word "straight" is significant. It's like a bulldozer leveling terrain—removing obstacles, making the path clear.

But who is this messenger? Continuing in Malachi 4:5:

> "See, I will send the prophet Elijah to you before that great and dreadful day of the Lord comes."

**The messenger is Elijah.** Elijah was so important in Jewish consciousness that he never died—he was taken to heaven in a chariot of fire (2 Kings 2:11). Post-exilic Jews believed he would return before the Messiah arrived. Even today, Jewish families set an empty chair and pour a cup of wine at Passover, hoping Elijah might appear.

Elijah's message was one of repentance. He called the kings of Israel to turn from their rebellion and return to God. Malachi 4:6 echoes this:

> He will turn the hearts of the parents to their children, and the hearts of the children to their parents; or else I will come and strike the land with total destruction.

The message is clear: **Turn. Repent.** This was a last warning before judgment.

## John the Baptist: The Last Warning (1:4-6)

> And so John the Baptist appeared in the wilderness, preaching a baptism of repentance for the forgiveness of sins.

John is the new Elijah. The parallels are unmistakable:

- **He dresses like Elijah:** "John wore clothing made of camel's hair, with a leather belt around his waist, and he ate locusts and wild honey" (Mark 1:6). See 2 Kings 1:8—when people saw this description, they immediately recognized it as Elijah's clothing.

- **He calls Israel to repentance:** Just as Elijah confronted the kings, John confronts the entire nation. "The whole Judean countryside and all the people of Jerusalem went out to him. Confessing their sins, they were baptized by him in the Jordan River" (1:5).

Baptism itself carries profound symbolism. It's immersion in water—death (drowning) and resurrection (emerging). It likely draws on Israel's foundational stories: the cleanings at the temple, Noah's flood, the crossing of the Red Sea, the crossing of the Jordan. All involve water as a boundary between death and new life. Baptism symbolizes dying to your old self and being born to new life—a physical enactment of turning around (repentance).

John offers Israel **the last chance to repent**. It's like a store announcing, "We close in ten minutes." That announcement signals: *This is your final opportunity. Now is the moment to decide.*

---

## Judgment and Baptism with Fire (1:7-13)

> After me comes the one more powerful than I, the straps of whose sandals I am not worthy to stoop down and untie. I baptize you with water, but he will baptize you with the Holy Spirit.

We typically contrast water with the Holy Spirit as external versus internal. But consider another angle. In Matthew 3:11, John adds crucial words: "He will baptize you with the Holy Spirit **and fire.**"

The Spirit and fire appear together throughout Scripture. Isaiah 4:4 speaks of "the spirit of burning." Isaiah 29:5-6 describes God visiting with "windstorm and tempest and consuming flame of fire." Isaiah 66:15 depicts the Lord coming "with fire—His chariots are like a whirlwind—to execute His anger with fury."

**The Holy Spirit here may mean the Holy Wind—the wind of judgment.**

In the Middle East, there are two types of wind: the rainstorm wind and the hot, burning dust storm from the desert—the kind that chokes, makes you hold your breath, causes bushfires, turns the sky red with ash. On judgment day, the sky looks like that.

What's the solution if you're caught in such a fire? You need water. John's message might be: *Come to the water now, because fire is coming!* **Repent now because judgment day is coming.** God is showing up and it won't be pleasant. Be prepared for the fire by being in the water.

Jesus' baptism, however, is enigmatic:

> At that time Jesus came from Nazareth in Galilee and was baptized by John in the Jordan. Just as Jesus was coming up out of the water, he saw heaven being torn open and the Spirit descending on him like a dove. And a voice came from heaven: "You are my Son, whom I love; with you I am well pleased."

Jesus didn't need cleansing, yet he entered the water. This is identification. He aligns himself with sinners to take their place.

The Spirit then sends Jesus into the wilderness for forty days of temptation (1:12-13). Israel spent forty years in the desert and repeatedly failed their test, falling away from God. But Jesus, the true Israel, undergoes forty days of temptation without yielding. He is the perfect human coming to redeem sinful humans.

## A Second Chance: The Time Has Come (1:14-15)

Then something strange happens:

> After John was put in prison, Jesus went into Galilee, proclaiming the good news of God. "The time has come," he said. "The kingdom of God has come near. Repent and believe the good news!"

After all the expectation—the Lord is coming, God is coming to judge, fire and wind are coming—John is imprisoned. Judgment day should arrive. It should be too late.

Yet Jesus proclaims: **There is a reprieve.**

This is paradoxical. "The time has come" and "The kingdom of God has come near" sound final. But instead of judgment, Jesus offers repentance and belief—a second chance.

It's like a basketball game where the score is tied, the clock counts down: 3-2-1. But somehow it doesn't buzz. The clock stays at 1 second. You continue playing. You have another chance to redeem yourself.

God doesn't call time. Jesus grants the world more "seconds" to repent. He will provide a way to be right with God. The rest of Mark details exactly how he does this, but the hints are here:

- **Jesus identifies with sinners** (1:9)—he doesn't need baptism, yet he's baptized to stand in our place.
- **Jesus overcomes temptation** (1:12-13)—where Israel failed for forty years, the true Israel succeeds in forty days.

This is genuinely good news. Judgment is on hold.

---

## What This Means for Us

The message is clear: **Repent and believe the good news!**

**Repent** means turn—turn from a life of rebelling against God to following him. Prepare yourself for God's presence.

**Believe the good news**—the news that despite deserving judgment, Jesus offers salvation. He will take our place, bearing the judgment we deserve, so we can be right with God.

Have you responded to this gospel? It is not too late. But it will be. A mother might make threats and show mercy at the last minute, but how long will she do that? God is not a fool to be laughed at. This last chance is genuinely the last chance. We have a second chance. Don't assume there will be a third.

**Don't miss out.**

---

## The Pattern of Extra Time

Prince Philip might seem to have missed out on many things. He gave up his claims to the thrones of Greece and Denmark to be consort to Elizabeth. Yet with his extra time, he set a remarkable pattern: a 74-year marriage, four children, decades of faithful service to his country—using his second chance well.

The question for each of us is: **What will you do with your extra time?**
