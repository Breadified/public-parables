# Genesis 11:1-9, 12:1-9

**Title:** The Beginning of Nations: From Babel to Abram  
**Date:** 31 January 2021  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Problem: Human Achievement Without God

Humanity possesses remarkable potential. We see this reflected in our technological capabilities and our capacity for unified action. Yet when that power operates outside of God's design, it becomes dangerous.

### The Tower of Babel: Pride in Unity

> 11:1 Now the whole world had one language and a common speech.
>
> 2 As people moved eastward, they found a plain in Shinar and settled there.

After the flood, humanity regrouped in Shinar—a place close to where civilization had begun, with conditions approaching the ideal. They were organized, unified, and spoke one language. This togetherness enabled remarkable technological achievement.

> 11:3 They said to each other, "Come, let's make bricks and bake them thoroughly." They used brick instead of stone, and tar for mortar.

By working together, they pooled their knowledge and created advanced materials. Fired bricks require removing moisture to harden the clay—a significant technological innovation. Tar for mortar was mass-produced binding. Together, they achieved what seemed impossible alone.

> 11:4 Then they said, "Come, let us build ourselves a city, with a tower that reaches to the heavens, so that we may make a name for ourselves; otherwise we will be scattered over the face of the whole earth.

Here lay the fundamental problem. Their unity had shifted from fulfilling God's command to fill the earth into pursuing their own glory. They wanted to make a name for themselves—an act of pride. The tower reaching to the heavens suggests they wanted to become like gods themselves, creating their own temple and marveling at their own handiwork.

Moreover, they directly contradicted God's explicit commands:

> Genesis 1:28 "Be fruitful and increase in number; fill the earth and subdue it."
>
> Genesis 9:1 "Be fruitful and increase in number and fill the earth."

Instead of spreading out to fill the earth, they retreated to their ancestral homeland. They wanted to concentrate power and glory in one place, becoming like children who move back home rather than establishing their own lives.

### God's Recognition of the Danger

> 11:6 The Lord said, "If as one people speaking the same language they have begun to do this, then nothing they plan to do will be impossible for them."

This doesn't mean God feared human intellect surpassing His own. Rather, it reveals a sobering truth: united in rebellion, with advanced technology and shared purpose, humanity becomes capable of unlimited evil. Without moral restraint, technological progress combined with sin creates a society where no wickedness lies beyond reach. A civilization can be technologically sophisticated while morally bankrupt—high-functioning in technology while spiritually diseased.

We see this pattern today. Modern Sydney glitters with technological marvels: skyscrapers, metros, medical advances, infinite information at our fingertips. Yet beneath this shine lies moral decay. We use technology to rationalize the killing of unborn children and call it healthcare. We reshape human bodies and identity through medicine, all while claiming progress. We manipulate masses through information technology and suppress those who disagree. We wage wars that devastate civilizations in days.

When unity operates without God, there is nothing we cannot do—including destroying ourselves.

### God's Solution: Judgment as Mercy

> 11:5 But the Lord came down to see the city and the tower the people were building.
>
> 11:7 Come, let us go down and confuse their language so they will not understand each other.

God's intervention seems severe, yet it was merciful restraint. A unified humanity bent on rebellion posed a threat to itself. By confusing their language, God prevented them from achieving their destructive aims.

> 11:8-9 So the Lord scattered them from there over all the earth, and they stopped building the city. That is why it was called Babel—because there the Lord confused the language of the whole world.

This division wasn't arbitrary punishment—it was preventative medicine. In a sinful world where power corrupts and absolute power corrupts absolutely, decentralization is wise. Just as a diversified investment portfolio protects wealth, distributed power protects humanity from total moral collapse. When one nation or institution gains absolute control, disaster follows.

This principle applies to governments, corporations, denominations, and nations themselves. It is better that power be divided, so that if one center of authority falls into sin, others remain to provide balance. The very diversity that seems like division is actually God's gracious protection.

## The Solution: The Call of Abram

Yet amid God's judgment, He began to work toward restoration. While dividing humanity by language, He also called forth one man through whom He would bless all nations.

> 12:1 The Lord had said to Abram, "Go from your country, your people and your father's household to the land I will show you.
>
> 12:2 I will make you into a great nation,

The word "nation" (ethnos/goy) speaks of a distinct people—a unified community bound by shared identity. But unlike the people at Babel who grasped for unity to exalt themselves, this nation would be built on obedience to God.

> 12:2 and I will bless you;
>
> I will make your name great,

This mirrors the pride of Babel—they wanted to make a name for themselves. God promised to make Abram's name great, but through a completely different means. Why would God do this? 

The answer lies in God's character: all glory belongs to Him. God chose Abram not because he was greatest among nations, but because he was among the fewest. A small, unlikely beginning ensures that God receives all credit for what unfolds.

> 12:2 and you will be a blessing.
>
> 12:3 I will bless those who bless you,
>
> and whoever curses you I will curse;
>
> and all peoples on earth will be blessed through you.

This is not ethnic favoritism but redemptive purpose. Through this one family, all nations would eventually experience blessing. The narrow becomes the channel for universal blessing.

> 12:4 So Abram went, as the Lord had told him; and Lot went with him. Abram was seventy-five years old when he set out from Harran.
>
> 12:5 He took his wife Sarai, his nephew Lot, all the possessions they had accumulated and the people they had acquired in Harran, and they set out for the land of Canaan, and they arrived there.

Abram was elderly with no children of his own. Yet God promised offspring who would inherit a land currently ruled by the Canaanites—a promise audacious in its impossibility.

> 12:6 Abram traveled through the land as far as the site of the great tree of Moreh at Shechem. At that time the Canaanites were in the land.
>
> 12:7-8 The Lord appeared to Abram and said, "To your offspring I will give this land." So he built an altar there to the Lord, who had appeared to him. From there he went on toward the hills east of Bethel and pitched his tent, with Bethel on the west and Ai on the east. There he built an altar to the Lord and called on the name of the Lord.

Abram didn't seize the land immediately. Instead, he planted altars—spiritual deposits marking God's promise. Like a lay-by at a shop or a flag on the moon, these altars testified that this land belonged to his God. It would take over five hundred years before his descendants actually possessed it.

### The Ultimate Fulfillment: Christ

Yet God's promise spoke of a singular offspring, not merely a people:

> Galatians 3:16 The promises were spoken to Abraham and to his seed. Scripture does not say "and to seeds," meaning many people, but "and to your seed," meaning one person, who is Christ.

God Himself would enter human history to solve the problem He identified at Babel. The one who would inherit all things would be Jesus Christ.

> Philippians 2:9 Therefore God exalted him to the highest place and gave him the name that is above every name.

Where the people of Babel sought to make a name for themselves and failed, Jesus—a man from an insignificant people—received the name above every name. Where they wanted to reach heaven through their own efforts, Jesus bridged that gap through His death and resurrection.

Those who bless Christ are blessed. Those who curse Him are cursed. And through His redemptive work, all peoples on earth are blessed. The human problem finds its solution in a Human—God Himself in human form.

## True Unity

True unity doesn't come from everyone being of the same race. Nor does it come from forced diversity. These are secular matters, products of history.

**True unity is in Christ.**

The real question isn't whether we share ethnicity, appearance, or cultural background. It's whether we share the same faith, hope, and love. Do we follow Jesus? Are we united in Him?

You can gather together a beautifully diverse group or a homogeneous one, but if you're not united in Christ, you possess only a unity that destroys. What matters is this: Do we share the same hope, faith, and love rooted in Jesus?

A group united in Christ—whether large or small, diverse or homogeneous—can make a genuine difference in the world. This is the kind of unity God builds, not the kind that collapses into moral chaos when power corrupts. This is humanity's true beginning: not starting over at Babel, but starting again through Christ.
