# 1 Peter

**Title:** An Outline Study of 1 Peter
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Overview: Understanding Aliens in God's Kingdom

Peter's letter addresses believers scattered across Asia Minor, describing them as "aliens and strangers" on earth. This designation carries profound implications for how Christians live, think, and understand their identity. The letter unfolds the reality that followers of Jesus are fundamentally different—not merely in their beliefs, but in their entire way of being in the world.

### What Does It Mean to Be an Alien?

The concept of being an alien reshapes how we approach life's temporal aspects. True aliens think in terms of 1-2 year horizons, not 10-20 year financial plans. They don't invest in permanent fixtures as if they're staying. Their cars, homes, and possessions reflect a transient mindset—because they're going back to their homeland in a couple of years. This radically alters consumer choices, career decisions, and lifestyle priorities.

Yet we often see Christians living indistinguishably from those around them. We pursue the same educational skills, the same entertainments, the same financial strategies. Divorce rates among believers match the broader culture. We carry iPhones and adopt lifestyles as sophisticated as everyone else. This inconsistency reveals something troubling: if we're dishonest about one fundamental reality—that we're aliens—why would we be honest about anything else? True integrity must begin with acknowledging our actual citizenship and allegiance.

## Part 1: Alien Born (1 Peter 1:1-25)

### Election and New Birth (vv. 1-12)

> Peter, an apostle of Jesus Christ, To God's elect, strangers in the world, scattered throughout Pontus, Galatia, Cappadocia, Asia and Bithynia, who have been chosen according to the foreknowledge of God the Father, through the sanctifying work of the Spirit, for obedience to Jesus Christ and sprinkling by his blood: Grace and peace be yours in abundance.

Peter opens by establishing identity before behavior. The readers are not merely scattered refugees—they are God's *chosen* people. This election flows from God's foreknowledge and is accomplished through the Spirit's sanctifying work. Notice the theological order: Father's foreknowledge, Spirit's sanctification, obedience to Christ, and redemption through His blood.

> Praise be to the God and Father of our Lord Jesus Christ! In his great mercy he has given us new birth into a living hope through the resurrection of Jesus Christ from the dead, and into an inheritance that can never perish, spoil or fade—kept in heaven for you, who through faith are shielded by God's power until the coming of the salvation that is ready to be revealed in the last time.

The foundation of Christian existence is new birth into a *living* hope. Not a hope in earthly permanence, but in resurrection reality. This inheritance transcends all temporal decay—it cannot perish, spoil, or fade. It's kept safe in heaven precisely *for you*. Your faith doesn't earn this protection; rather, God's power actively shields you until final salvation is revealed.

> In this you greatly rejoice, though now for a little while you may have had to suffer grief in all kinds of trials. These have come so that your faith—of greater worth than gold, which perishes even though refined by fire—may be proved genuine and may result in praise, glory and honor when Jesus Christ is revealed.

Suffering serves a redemptive purpose: it proves faith authentic. Just as fire refines gold, trials refine faith. And unlike gold, which perishes, genuine faith endures forever and culminates in glory when Christ appears.

> Though you have not seen him, you love him; and even though you do not see him now, you believe in him and are filled with an inexpressible and glorious joy, for you are receiving the goal of your faith, the salvation of your souls.

This describes the paradox of Christian faith: loving one we've never seen, believing without seeing, yet experiencing inexpressible joy. This isn't emotional sentimentality—it's the reality of receiving salvation itself.

### The Prophetic Foundation (vv. 10-12)

> Concerning this salvation, the prophets, who spoke of the grace that was to come to you, searched intently and with the greatest care, trying to find out the time and circumstances to which the Spirit of Christ in them was pointing when he predicted the sufferings of Christ and the glories that would follow. It was revealed to them that they were not serving themselves but you, when they spoke of the things that have now been told you by those who have preached the gospel to you by the Holy Spirit sent from heaven. Even angels long to look into these things.

Old Testament prophets understood they were serving future believers—*you*—not their own contemporaries. They spoke under the Spirit of Christ, pointing toward both His sufferings and subsequent glories. This reveals the unified arc of Scripture: the entire prophetic tradition converges on Christ. Even angels marvel at this mystery of redemption.

### Living as Aliens: Holiness and Hope (vv. 13-21)

> Therefore, prepare your minds for action; be self-controlled; set your hope fully on the grace to be given you when Jesus Christ is revealed. As obedient children, do not conform to the evil desires you had when you lived in ignorance. But just as he who called you is holy, so be holy in all you do; for it is written: "Be holy, because I am holy."

The call to holiness isn't arbitrary rule-keeping—it's conformity to God's character. Having set our hope on future grace, we're equipped to resist present desires that contradict our true identity.

> Since you call on a Father who judges each man's work impartially, live your lives as strangers here in reverent fear. For you know that it was not with perishable things such as silver or gold that you were redeemed from the empty way of life handed down to you from your forefathers, but with the precious blood of Christ, a lamb without blemish or defect.

Our identity as aliens flows from redemption's cost. We were bought at infinite expense—not with corruptible currency, but with Christ's spotless sacrifice. This reframes everything: the "empty way of life" passed down through generations is exposed as worthless compared to our true inheritance.

> He was chosen before the creation of the world, but was revealed in these last times for your sake. Through him you believe in God, who raised him from the dead and glorified him, and so your faith and hope are in God.

Christ's redemptive work was eternally purposed and historically revealed *for your sake*. This establishes the ground of faith: not in ourselves, but in God who raised and glorified Christ.

### New Birth and Enduring Word (vv. 22-25)

> Now that you have purified yourselves by obeying the truth so that you have sincere love for your brothers, love one another deeply, from the heart. For you have been born again, not of perishable seed, but of imperishable, through the living and enduring word of God.

New birth produces transformed affection—sincere, brotherly love rooted in heart reality, not performance. This birth is neither biological nor circumstantial; it flows from God's living, enduring word.

> For, "All men are like grass, and all their glory is like the flowers of the field; the grass withers and the flowers fall, but the word of the Lord stands forever." And this is the word that was preached to you.

Against the backdrop of universal human transience, God's word alone endures. And *that* eternal word is what was preached to you—making you partakers of permanence itself.

## Part 2: Alien Race (1 Peter 2:1-10)

### Putting Off and Craving (vv. 1-3)

> Therefore, rid yourselves of all malice and all deceit, hypocrisy, envy, and slander of every kind. Like newborn babies, crave pure spiritual milk, so that by it you may grow up in your salvation, now that you have tasted that the Lord is good.

Peter identifies sins that damage community: malice, deceit, hypocrisy (the masking of inward evil with outward righteousness), envy (the opposite of gratitude for others' good), and slanderous speech. The solution isn't stern prohibition but natural hunger—like infants instinctively crave milk, believers should crave the word of God.

"Tasted that the Lord is good" (from Psalm 34:8) suggests experiential knowledge. To hear Scripture is to hear the Lord speaking. To drink from His word is to taste repeatedly what He is like, experiencing personal communion with the Lord Himself.

### The Living Stone and Living Stones (vv. 4-10)

> As you come to him, the living Stone—rejected by men but chosen by God and precious to him—you also, like living stones, are being built into a spiritual house to be a holy priesthood, offering spiritual sacrifices acceptable to God through Jesus Christ.

The metaphor pivots on contrast: *dead* stones (like temple architecture) versus *living* stones. The living Stone is Christ Himself—precious, chosen by God, yet rejected by human institutions. As we come to Him, we become living stones, built together into a spiritual house where we function as priests.

> For in Scripture it says: "See, I lay a stone in Zion, a chosen and precious cornerstone, and the one who trusts in him will never be put to shame."

A cornerstone bears weight for the entire structure. Trust in Christ provides security and integrity to all that follows. Those who trust will never be put to shame.

> Now to you who believe, this stone is precious. But to those who do not believe, "The stone the builders rejected has become the capstone," and, "A stone that causes men to stumble and a rock that makes them fall." They stumble because they disobey the message—which is also what they were destined for.

The same stone serves opposite functions: foundation for believers, stumbling block for those who reject. This rejection isn't arbitrary; it's the inevitable consequence of disobeying the message.

> But you are a chosen people, a royal priesthood, a holy nation, a people belonging to God, that you may declare the praises of him who called you out of darkness into his wonderful light. Once you were not a people, but now you are the people of God; once you had not received mercy, but now you have received mercy.

Peter elevates the status of Gentile believers by applying Israel's covenant identity to them. They are chosen, royal, holy, God's possession—with a mission to declare His praises. This represents the centrifugal thrust of God's plan: grace flowing outward from Israel to all peoples. The priesthood is no longer defined by ceremonial law but by renewed minds and godliness.

## Part 3: Living as Aliens—Submission and Integrity (1 Peter 2:11-25)

### Alien Witness (vv. 11-12)

> Dear friends, I urge you, as aliens and strangers in the world, to abstain from sinful desires, which war against your soul. Live such good lives among the pagans that, though they accuse you of doing wrong, they may see your good deeds and glorify God on the day he visits us.

Sinful desires are not mere temptations; they're spiritual warfare against your own soul. Yet the antidote isn't retreat from society—it's excellence of conduct so evident that even accusers must acknowledge the reality of God's grace.

### Submission to Authorities (vv. 13-17)

> Submit yourselves for the Lord's sake to every authority instituted among men: whether to the king, as the supreme authority, or to governors, who are sent by him to punish those who do wrong and to commend those who do right. For it is God's will that by doing good you should silence the ignorant talk of foolish men. Live as free men, but do not use your freedom as a cover-up for evil; live as servants of God. Show proper respect to everyone: Love the brotherhood of believers, fear God, honor the king.

Submission to earthly authorities flows from submission to God—they are ordained by Him. The purpose of this submission is prophetic: to silence false accusations through evident righteousness. Freedom in Christ doesn't license evil; rather, it clarifies that we're servants of God, not of earthly systems. The priorities are clear: love the church, fear God, honor civic authority.

### The Suffering Servant Pattern (vv. 18-25)

> Slaves, submit yourselves to your masters with all respect, not only to those who are good and considerate, but also to those who are harsh. For it is commendable if a man bears up under the pain of unjust suffering because he is conscious of God. But how is it to your credit if you receive a beating for doing wrong and endure it? But if you suffer for doing good and you endure it, this is commendable before God.

Peter addresses the darkest social reality of his era—slavery. Yet even here, the teaching isn't merely accommodation but transformation. Submission to harsh masters, when rooted in God-consciousness, becomes commendable. The distinction is crucial: suffering for wrongdoing proves nothing; suffering unjustly while maintaining integrity witnesses to God's character.

> To this you were called, because Christ suffered for you, leaving you an example, that you should follow in his steps. "He committed no sin, and no deceit was found in his mouth." When they hurled their insults at him, he did not retaliate; when he suffered, he made no threats. Instead, he entrusted himself to him who judges justly.

Christ's passion becomes the template for unjust suffering. He was sinless yet condemned, insulted yet silent, threatened yet committed to the just Judge. This isn't passive victimhood but active trust in divine justice.

> He himself bore our sins in his body on the tree, so that we might die to sins and live for righteousness; by his wounds you have been healed. For you were like sheep going astray, but now you have returned to the Shepherd and Overseer of your souls.

The cross accomplishes substitution: He bore our sins so we might live righteously. His wounds effect healing. The shepherd image restores those who've wandered, establishing Christ as guardian of our very souls.

## Part 4: Living as Aliens in the Family (1 Peter 3:1-7)

> Wives, in the same way be submissive to your husbands so that, if any of them do not believe the word, they may be won over without words by the behavior of their wives, when they see the purity and reverence of your lives.

The "same way" connects to Christ's example of submissive trust in God's justice. For believing wives married to unbelievers, faithful conduct becomes missionary—a silent testimony more powerful than argument.

> Your beauty should not come from outward adornment, such as braided hair and the wearing of gold jewelry and fine clothes. Instead, it should be that of your inner self, the unfading beauty of a gentle and quiet spirit, which is of great worth in God's sight.

Peter contrasts external beauty that fades with inner character that endures—not because external adornment is sinful, but because it's temporary. A gentle, reverent spirit holds eternal value before God.

> For this is the way the holy women of the past who put their hope in God used to make themselves beautiful. They were submissive to their own husbands, like Sarah, who obeyed Abraham and called him her master. You are her daughters if you do what is right and do not give way to fear.

Sarah models submission rooted not in coercion but in hope placed in God. Her descendants inherit this legacy—not through biological descent but through righteous action unmotivated by fear.

> Husbands, in the same way be considerate as you live with your wives, and treat them with respect as the weaker partner and as heirs with you of the gracious gift of life, so that nothing will hinder your prayers.

Husbands' responsibility mirrors the pattern: consideration, respect, recognition of co-heirship in grace. This treatment isn't peripheral—it's essential to spiritual health, affecting even prayer's efficacy.

## Part 5: Living as Aliens in the Church (1 Peter 5:1-14)

### Shepherding the Flock (vv. 1-4)

> To the elders among you, I appeal as a fellow elder, a witness of Christ's sufferings and one who also will share in the glory to be revealed: Be shepherds of God's flock that is under your care, serving as overseers—not because you must, but because you are willing, as God wants you to be; not greedy for money, but eager to serve; not lording it over those entrusted to you, but being examples to the flock. And when the Chief Shepherd appears, you will receive the crown of glory that will never fade away.

Peter addresses church leaders as a peer, grounded in his apostolic experience. Leadership is servant-shepherding, not authoritarian dominion. The motivation matters: willing service, not reluctant obligation; eagerness to serve, not financial ambition; exemplary living, not imperious command. This leadership will be vindicated when Christ, the Chief Shepherd, appears.

### Humility and Anxiety (vv. 5-7)

> Young men, in the same way be submissive to those who are older. All of you, clothe yourselves with humility toward one another, because, "God opposes the proud but gives grace to the humble." Humble yourselves, therefore, under God's mighty hand, that he may lift you up in due time. Cast all your anxiety on him because he cares for you.

The "same way" throughout 1 Peter emphasizes consistent submission to God's ordering. Humility before both God and community is the prerequisite for receiving grace. This isn't humiliation but realistic assessment—casting anxiety onto God because He cares, with the assurance that He will exalt in His time.

### Vigilance and Suffering (vv. 8-11)

> Be self-controlled and alert. Your enemy the devil prowls around like a roaring lion looking for someone to devour. Resist him, standing firm in the faith, because you know that your brothers throughout the world are undergoing the same kind of sufferings. And the God of all grace, who called you to his eternal glory in Christ, after you have suffered a little while, will himself restore you and make you strong, firm and steadfast. To him be the power for ever and ever. Amen.

Spiritual vigilance combats the enemy's predatory designs. Yet resistance doesn't arise from fearfulness but from solidarity—knowing that suffering for Christ's sake unites believers globally. The promised end completes the arc: temporary suffering gives way to eternal restoration, strength, and stability—all rooted in God's grace.

### Final Greetings and Peace (vv. 12-14)

> With the help of Silas, whom I regard as a faithful brother, I have written to you briefly, encouraging you and testifying that this is the true grace of God. Stand fast in it. She who is in Babylon, chosen together with you, sends you her greetings, and so does my son Mark. Greet one another with a kiss of love. Peace to all of you who are in Christ.

Peter's closing emphasizes encouragement and testimony to grace as the foundation for perseverance. The believers are reminded that they stand together—across geography, across generations, across tribulation—united in grace and sealed with the peace of Christ.

---

## Summary Themes

1. **Identity Precedes Behavior**: We are aliens by birth, chosen by God, before we're called to alien conduct.

2. **Living Stones in a Living Structure**: We participate in Christ (the living Stone) and become living stones ourselves, building a spiritual house where all function as priests.

3. **Submission as Trust**: From authorities to family to church leadership, submission echoes Christ's trust in the Father's justice.

4. **Suffering as Witness**: Unjust suffering borne with integrity declares God's character to a watching world.

5. **Temporary Trials, Eternal Glory**: Present suffering is real but brief, culminating in restoration and standing firm in grace forever.
