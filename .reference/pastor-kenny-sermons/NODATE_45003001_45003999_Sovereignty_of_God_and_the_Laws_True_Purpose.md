# Romans 3

**Title:** Sovereignty of God and the Law's True Purpose

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Jewish Position: Boasting in the Law (2:17-3:1)

The Jews of Paul's day held a privileged position. They possessed the law—God's revealed word—and they took great pride in this advantage. Their boast was real: they had been entrusted with God's commandments, His oracles, His truth. This was their identity and their confidence before God.

But Paul asks a penetrating question: *Does this privilege guarantee justification?* What good is the law if it cannot make us right with God?

## The Human Objection (3:3-8)

A natural question arises from Paul's teaching. If the law cannot justify us, then what's the point? Some objected: *"If our unbelief doesn't nullify God's faithfulness, then isn't God unjust to still judge us?"* 

This is the human argument against Paul's doctrine. It seems logical from a fleshly perspective: if God's promises stand regardless of our obedience, why does He condemn sin? The objectors twist Paul's argument into a reason to do evil so that good might come—a perversion he immediately denounces.

## The Conclusion: All Are Under Sin (3:9-18)

Paul draws the inescapable conclusion that spans chapters 1 through 3: **All—Jews and Gentiles alike—are under the power of sin.** There is no distinction. No group has an advantage. No one can boast.

To hammer this home, Paul stitches together Old Testament passages showing the universal reality of human depravity:
- No one seeks God
- All have turned away
- None do good, not even one
- Their throats are open graves
- Their mouths are full of cursing and bitterness
- Their feet are swift to shed blood

This is not pessimism—it's spiritual diagnosis. Paul is preparing the way for the cure.

## The True Purpose of the Law (3:20)

Here is the watershed statement: *"Therefore no one will be declared righteous in his sight by observing the law; rather, through the law we become conscious of sin."*

The law was never meant to justify. That's not its purpose. The law is a mirror. It shows us our sin. It reveals the gap between God's holy standard and our corrupt hearts. When we see ourselves in the law's reflection, we see our need for something—*someone*—beyond ourselves.

This is deeply important. The law accuses. It condemns. It awakens us to our hopelessness. But it is not the solution.

## The Problem and the Solution (3:21-26)

Paul moves from diagnosis to cure. The problem is real: God's wrath is revealed against all unrighteousness (as stated in chapter 1). We deserve judgment.

But now—*a big, beautiful "but now"*—God's righteousness is revealed in a different way. Not through the law, but *apart from the law* and yet *testified to by the law and the prophets*.

**What is this solution?** It comes through faith in Jesus Christ.

**Four pillars hold up this gospel:**

1. **Justified** (dikaiōō)—declared righteous, acquitted in God's courtroom
2. **Redemption** (apolytrōsis)—bought back, ransomed from slavery to sin
3. **Sacrifice of Atonement** (hilastērion)—the mercy seat, the place where God's wrath is satisfied and justice is met
4. **Faith** (pistis)—the instrument by which we receive this gift, trusting in Christ's work

All of this flows from God's grace. We receive what we do not deserve. Christ took what we deserve—condemnation—and gave us what we don't deserve—justification.

## The End of Boasting (3:27-31)

Where, then, is boasting? *It is excluded.* Paul brings the discussion back to where it started (3:1), but with revolutionary clarity.

The law has a new position now. It is not our hope for righteousness. It is not the basis of our standing before God. Instead, it serves as a witness to the righteousness that comes through faith. The law and the prophets testify to Christ. They point to the solution they could never be.

Does this mean the law is abolished? No. Does it mean the law is useless? Not at all. The law still reveals sin. It still calls us to holiness. But it no longer has the burden of justifying us. That work belongs to Christ alone.
