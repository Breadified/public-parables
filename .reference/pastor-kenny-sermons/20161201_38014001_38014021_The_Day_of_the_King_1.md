# Zechariah 14:1-21

**Title:** The Day of the King
**Date:** 25 December 2016
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Anticipating the Day

Christmas and New Year's Day have arrived. For some of us, this catches us by surprise—we've been so busy working that suddenly the day is here. For others, we've been waiting for this day for a very long time. Whether it's the joy of family, presents, celebration, or simply relief from a difficult year, we all anticipate certain days with hope and longing.

The ancient Israelites, two and a half thousand years ago, also longed for a day. In their hopes and disappointments, the prophet Zechariah brings a message from God Himself to the Jewish people—a message about the day when God would finally act on their behalf.

## The Day of Escape (1-8, 12-19)

### Jerusalem Under Siege

Zechariah's message is one of salvation and a day of escape. The chapter opens with an ominous vision:

> A day of the Lord is coming, Jerusalem, when your possessions will be plundered and divided up within your very walls.

The Israelites are backed into a corner. Their own walls—which should have protected them—have failed. The enemies have broken through, and the city is under siege. The people are trapped with nowhere to go. The vision grows darker still:

> Then the Lord will go out and fight against those nations, as he fights on a day of battle.

### Divine Intervention: The Mountain Split

Yet in this moment of hopelessness, Zechariah describes something extraordinary—a geological upheaval that becomes a rescue:

> On that day his feet will stand on the Mount of Olives, east of Jerusalem, and the Mount of Olives will be split in two from east to west, forming a great valley, with half of the mountain moving north and half moving south.

To understand the significance, we must see the geography. Jerusalem sits on a plateau backed against a mountain to the east. The Mount of Olives blocks any escape route to the east, while enemies approach from the flatter west. The people are completely trapped, relying entirely on the city walls for protection.

But God's word is clear: the mountain shall split in two. This is not judgment—this is rescue. The geological barrier that trapped them becomes their pathway to freedom:

> You will flee by my mountain valley, for it will extend to Azel. You will flee as you fled from the earthquake in the days of Uzziah king of Judah. Then the Lord my God will come, and all the holy ones with him.

This echoes Israel's greatest deliverance—the exodus from Egypt. When the Israelites were backed against the Red Sea with Pharaoh's army pursuing them, Moses divided the sea so everyone could walk across. Now, in this future day, God will divide the mountain so His people can escape.

### The Spiritual Escape in Jesus

Five hundred years after Zechariah wrote these words, something remarkable happened. At Jesus' death, we read:

> When Jesus had cried out in a loud voice, he gave up his spirit. At that moment the curtain of the temple was torn in two from top to bottom. The earth shook, the rocks split, and the tombs broke open. The bodies of many holy people who had died were raised to life. When the centurion and those with him saw the earthquake and all that had happened, they were terrified, and exclaimed, "Surely he was the Son of God!"

The earth shook. The rocks split. An earthquake—just as Zechariah prophesied. But this is not a physical valley opening up. The escape is not geographical—it is spiritual.

Here we are, all humanity, backed into a wall of our own making—an impenetrable wall of sin and judgment with no possibility of escape. No matter how much we regret, no matter how wrong we have been, we cannot escape the consequences of our brokenness. We are trapped.

But when there was no hope, suddenly a way out was opened up for us. An escape route. A solution to our deepest problem. A chance to repent, to change our ways, to return to God.

On the cross, Jesus became that solution. When He gave His last breath, the earth shook. A way was opened up for us to return to God. The judgment we deserve fell on Him instead. The barrier between us and our Creator—sin itself—was removed.

## The Day of Peace (9-11, 20-21)

### Universal Peace and Security

With the escape comes restoration and peace:

> The Lord will be king over the whole earth. On that day there will be one Lord, and his name the only name.

And:

> The whole land, from Geba to Rimmon, south of Jerusalem, will become like the Arabah. But Jerusalem will be raised up high from the Benjamin Gate to the site of the First Gate, to the Corner Gate, and from the Tower of Hananel to the royal winepresses, and will remain in its place. It will be inhabited; never again will it be destroyed. Jerusalem will be secure.

No more siege. No more suffering. Instead, prosperity and peace. Jerusalem will be raised high—so secure that it needs no walls because it is beyond the reach of enemies. Universal peace will reign.

### Everything Made Holy

But Zechariah's vision goes even deeper. He describes a holiness that permeates everything:

> On that day "holy to the Lord" will be inscribed on the bells of the horses, and the cooking pots in the Lord's house will be like the sacred bowls in front of the altar. Every pot in Jerusalem and Judah will be holy to the Lord Almighty, and all who come to sacrifice will take some of the pots and cook in them.

Think about this carefully. Holy means set apart for God. God is holy, and those who enter His presence must be holy. But here, everything becomes holy—not just the sacred temple vessels, but the bells on horses, cooking pots, ordinary dishes. Even the most mundane, everyday items are declared holy.

This is the power of Christ's sacrifice. His death is so effective that those who accept it are made holy. We are cleansed by His blood and become vessels of God ourselves. And one day—at the completion of His plan—the entire creation will be cleansed and made holy. There will be no more sin, no more suffering, no more brokenness in the world.

## The Day is Coming (Matthew 17:20, 21:21)

### Faith in God's Power

Jesus echoes Zechariah's message in a surprising way:

> If you have faith as small as a mustard seed, you can say to this mountain, "Move from here to there" and it will move. Nothing will be impossible for you.

This is often misunderstood. It's not about positive thinking or commanding God to give us what we want. It's not about demanding health, wealth, or a Ferrari and assuming God will deliver. God is not Santa Claus. We don't tell God what to do—God tells us what He expects. He is Father, not vending machine. A good father listens to his children's requests but ultimately decides what is best for them.

So what is Jesus really saying? He's talking about believing that Jesus Himself will rescue you. He's standing on the mountain, repeating the words of Zechariah: "Believe, and mountains shall move!"

The point is not about the power of your faith. You don't need much faith at all—just a mustard seed. What you need is Jesus, who can move mountains for you. Jesus is saying:

- Believe in Zechariah's prophecy
- Believe in hope beyond death
- Believe that God is coming to judge
- Believe that Jesus can save you from that judgment

You don't need faith in yourself, or faith in belief, or faith in faith itself. You need faith in God and His ability to judge and to rescue. A tiny amount is enough.

## Conclusion

The day has arrived. For some of us, it catches us by surprise. For others, we've been waiting for this day a very long time.

Zechariah waited five hundred years. The Israelites before him waited thousands of years. And then Jesus came. He split the mountain. He opened the way. He made the holy sacrifice that cleanses us all.

In this new year, amidst the stress and parties and celebrations, we celebrate the anniversary of His birth—in the year of our Lord, as we mark time by His coming.

And Jesus will come again. That day has not yet finished. We await the final conclusion when there will be peace on earth.

Put your trust in Jesus this Christmas. If you ask for salvation, your request will be answered—because that is His deepest will for you. If you ask to return to Him, it will be answered.

Merry Christmas and a Happy New Year.
