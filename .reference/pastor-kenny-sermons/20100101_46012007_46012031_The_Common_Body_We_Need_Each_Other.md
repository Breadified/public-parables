# 1 Corinthians 12:7-31

**Title:** The Common Body: We Need Each Other
**Date:** 17 January 2010
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Who Is the Most Important Person in This Room?

In movies, we know who matters—the star gets top billing, their name appears first and largest. But in a church gathering like this, who is the most important person? Is it the person standing up front? The one with the most money? The oldest member? The most talented? The newest arrival? The question reveals something we need to grapple with together.

Last week we explored how Christians share the same unity, arising from the same Lord and the same Spirit. Today we continue that thought through Paul's teaching on the body of Christ.

## 1. Same Spirit but Different Gifts

### The One Spirit (verse 7)

> Now to each one the manifestation of the Spirit is given for the common good.

Paul teaches that we've all received the same message—that Jesus is Lord—though the Spirit manifests this truth in different ways to each of us. This isn't about water baptism or church membership, but about our spiritual identity. We're identified with Christ, united under His Lordship, sharing the same Spirit of truth.

> All these are the work of one and the same Spirit, and he gives them to each one, just as he determines. (verse 11)

### The Different Gifts (verses 8-10)

Yet within this unity, the Spirit distributes different gifts:

> To one there is given through the Spirit the message of wisdom, to another the message of knowledge by means of the same Spirit, to another faith by the same Spirit, to another gifts of healing by that one Spirit, to another miraculous powers, to another prophecy, to another distinguishing between spirits, to another speaking in different kinds of tongues, and to still another the interpretation of tongues.

**Three Views on These Gifts:**

Christians hold different positions on whether these miraculous gifts continue today. Some are full continuists, believing signs and wonders that accompanied the apostles in Acts continue now. Others are cessationists, arguing these gifts belonged to the apostolic era when the foundation of the gospel was being laid and Scripture was still being written. Most of us fall somewhere in between.

Honestly, we don't know exactly what all these gifts are. Even Christian writers in the fourth century admitted they were unclear. What I believe is this: Paul seems primarily to be describing speaking gifts—prophecy, knowledge, interpretation, discernment. These were still active in Corinth around AD 50. Later letters say far less about signs and wonders, and miraculous events in Scripture were always tied to specific historical and salvation moments, not everyday occurrences.

You may have experienced something supernatural, and I'm not arguing against personal experiences. But individual experiences don't make something normative for all believers. God is a God of order, rationality, and knowledge—not of arbitrary weirdness. Consider the impact of Scripture itself: the gospel is the power of God. Does it change us through learning His word, or through miraculous spectacle?

Here's what matters most: whatever gifts the Spirit distributes—whether spectacular or ordinary—they're given for the common good. They exist to build up the church, to serve each other. To achieve this common good, we need each other.

## 2. Same Body but Different Parts

### The One Body (verses 12-13, 20, 27)

> The body is a unit, though it is made up of many parts; and though all its parts are many, they form one body. So it is with Christ.

A human body functions through cooperation. Hormones, enzymes, blood, organs, and muscle work together. So it is with Christ's body—wherever Christians gather together.

> For we were all baptized by one Spirit into one body—whether Jews or Greeks, slave or free—and we were all given the one Spirit to drink.

This isn't about water baptism (which Paul notes wasn't even his primary calling—1 Corinthians 1:17). This is about spiritual baptism, your identification with Christ as Lord when you believed the gospel. The image of drinking the same Spirit conveys the same reality: we're internally united by the living presence of God.

> Now you are the body of Christ, and each one of you is a part of it.

If we're all Christians, we share a bond greater than anything else. That bond must be reflected in how we gather together.

### The Many Parts That Are Vital for the Body (verses 15-26)

Paul presents two attitudes that might resonate with us.

**I Do Not Belong** (verses 15-19)

> If the foot should say, "Because I am not a hand, I do not belong to the body," it would not for that reason cease to be part of the body. And if the ear should say, "Because I am not an eye, I do not belong to the body," it would not for that reason cease to be part of the body.

Being a hand seems better than being a foot. A hand is functional, multi-purpose, visible. A foot bears weight, gets stinky, and stays hidden in shoes. An eye is glamorous; who ever compliments someone's ears? So the foot might think, "I don't belong," and the ear might feel the same way.

But this is flawed reasoning:

> If the whole body were an eye, where would the sense of hearing be? If the whole body were an ear, where would the sense of smell be? But in fact God has arranged the parts in the body, every one of them, just as he wanted them to be.

Nobody looks at an ugly pinky toe and decides to amputate it. Yet that little toe is essential for balance—you feel its importance immediately when you stub it. Every part belongs. Every part matters.

**Who feels like they don't belong here?**

- The newcomer who finds this church strange, in a school building, surrounded by unfamiliar faces
- The non-Christian who was pressured to attend, who feels fundamentally different
- The person struggling with illness or mental health, afraid of judgment and rejection
- The parent desperate for adult conversation amid the children
- The person of a different ethnicity, standing out visually
- The child who feels invisible among so many adults
- The person battling secret sin, terrified of exposure

We all feel like outsiders sometimes. Even in a minority church, we're all minorities. I once showed our congregation an age distribution chart—it looked like a perfect bell curve with equal numbers in each age group. But if you were the oldest person, you saw young people all around you. If you were young, everyone seemed old. If you were in the middle, nobody was your age. We all feel disenfranchised by our differences.

But look to your unity in Christ, and you'll see that you're valuable to each other:

- Without newcomers, how would we learn to love the stranger as the Good Samaritan did?
- Without people facing sickness, how would we learn to show genuine care?
- God has given us each other to teach us how to be one body.
- The person who frustrates you will make you a better person through your interaction.
- The very old bring wisdom.
- Even non-Christians keep us honest with tough questions, ensuring we're not a cult.

Don't say, "I don't belong." You do. Come to church and our gatherings to be encouraged and to encourage others. Sometimes hardship drives people away—we fear judgment. If that's happened to you, I'm genuinely sorry. That's why I'm committed to making church a safe, caring place. But please don't disappear on us. We need each other.

**I Don't Need You** (verses 21-26)

> The eye cannot say to the hand, "I don't need you!" And the head cannot say to the feet, "I don't need you!"

This attitude often comes from the strong—those doing well in life, flourishing spiritually, gifted in many ways. The temptation is to think, "I can study the Bible myself. I can attend other conferences. I don't need church." Technically you may be right. But Paul says:

> On the contrary, those parts of the body that seem to be weaker are indispensable, and the parts that we think are less honorable we treat with special honor. And the parts that are unpresentable are treated with special modesty, while our presentable parts need no special treatment. But God has combined the members of the body and has given greater honor to the parts that lacked it, so that there should be no division in the body, but that its parts should have equal concern for each other.

You are strong in order to help the weaker parts. Those with less honor deserve special honor. Those parts we'd rather hide need our careful attention. This isn't about covering things up—it's about helping. Helping the weaker. Helping the unpresentable. Helping the less honorable.

We pack our Sundays too full—birthday parties, holidays, social events, work, university meetings, recitals, friends' baptisms and weddings. We've crowded out time for each other. And often it's because we think we don't need each other. Our work is important. Our friends matter. These other things press in. We'll show up in a couple of weeks, maybe. We can't be there every week.

But consider this: while you may not need us, we still need you. We can't find another hand to replace you, another eye, another ear. The whole point of a body is that its parts aren't interchangeable. The foot can't suddenly morph into a hand. The ear can't become an eyeball.

> If one part suffers, every part suffers with it; if one part is honored, every part rejoices with it.

Imagine a congregation where nobody cares about anyone else. Everyone is self-indulgent, speaking whenever they want, doing whatever pleases them, thinking only of themselves. People rush to grab food without regard for others. Newcomers are ignored, left to figure things out alone. Nobody prays for each other—what's the point if they won't even be here next week to hear how it went? Deep conversations don't happen because you're all still strangers. The musicians do their own thing. The sermon feels random. People talk over each other. Why would you ever make yourself vulnerable in such a place? Why would you ever share your struggles?

This is the eye saying, "I don't need you." It's monstrous. It's what the Corinthian church was becoming. Don't let our church become like that.

Now imagine instead: a place where God's word is faithfully taught each week—not just from the pulpit but from each other as you share what you're learning from the Lord, as you confess troubles and temptations and difficulties, as you hear each other and offer help and prayer. As outsiders come in, they hear God's word and see how love actually operates here.

All of us have a role. This requires commitment and regularity. It requires showing up.

> The most important person in this room is the person next to you. The most important person in this room is the other person.

We're all important to each other. We need each other for this body to function properly.

---

**This is what it means to be truly spiritual.**
