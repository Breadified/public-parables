# 2 Peter 3:3-18

**Title:** Happy New Year!
**Date:** January 4, 2016
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Understanding God's Timing

Getting the timing wrong can derail us. Whether it's missing an exam because you misread the date or misunderstanding when God will fulfill His promises, our perception of time shapes how we live. This morning, we're exploring what the Apostle Peter teaches about time—and why getting it right matters for our faith.

## Time Feels Long (v. 3-4)

Peter begins by addressing the scoffers—those skeptical about Jesus's return. They mock believers with a simple question: "Where is this coming he promised?"

> 3 Above all, you must understand that in the last days scoffers will come, scoffing and following their own evil desires. 4 They will say, "Where is this 'coming' he promised? Ever since our ancestors died, everything goes on as it has since the beginning of creation."

Their skepticism rests on observation. Centuries have passed since Jesus's resurrection. Generations have died. Nothing has changed. The same cycles repeat year after year—rain, seasons, weather, the rhythms of nature. To them, time stretches endlessly forward, and Jesus's promise feels like a fairy tale.

This perception runs deep in human experience. Ancient civilizations observed these eternal cycles and developed philosophies around them. Hinduism and Buddhism see time as infinite cycles of death and rebirth, repetition so futile that their followers long for Nirvana—extinction of the self—just to escape the monotony.

We understand this temptation. It's now 2016 AD—nearly 2,000 years since Christ came. We've waited so long that we look foolish announcing judgment. Meanwhile, skeptics gain degrees, buy investment properties, take overseas holidays. They mock: "Where is judgment?"

This waiting exhausts us. It's like standing at the train station waiting for a child who promised to arrive by a certain time. Your emotion shifts from impatience to worry to anger: *Where is he?*

For those suffering, this wait feels unbearable. Chronic illness—not well enough to enjoy life, not sick enough to die quickly. Christians in the Middle East and Africa facing persecution. Injustice that seems endless. We're tempted to think Jesus isn't coming, that time is cyclical, that hope itself is foolish.

Yet in the midst of this frustration, Peter reminds us: though we wait, we do not wait for long.

## Time Is Short (v. 8-12)

Peter now shifts our perspective entirely. Time, from God's vantage point, is actually very brief.

> 8 But do not forget this one thing, dear friends: With the Lord a day is like a thousand years, and a thousand years are like a day.

This is not literal mathematics—it's a metaphor to recalibrate how we perceive duration. Two thousand years to us feels like an eternity. But to the God of eternity, it's merely two days—this weekend. God's timeline encompasses ages we can barely fathom. What seems long to us is a blink to Him.

Think of how your children experience time. A week feels eternal to them. They're bored, restless, convinced summer will never arrive. But to you, the years fly by. Suddenly they're graduating, working, getting married. What they perceive as long, you know is brief.

So too with God. Our waiting, however painful, is brief in His eternal perspective. This time is nothing to the God of eternity.

But there's a second, more urgent sense in which time is short: **we are in a high-risk zone.**

> 10 But the day of the Lord will come like a thief. The heavens will disappear with a roar; the elements will be destroyed by fire, and the earth and everything done in it will be laid bare. 11 Since everything will be destroyed in this way, what kind of people ought you to be? You ought to live holy and godly lives 12 as you look forward to the day of God and speed its coming.

Peter speaks of judgment day—when time itself ends. The heavens will vanish, elements will melt in heat, the earth will be laid bare. This is not speculation. It's certain.

Consider a soldier in wartime. Technically, his life expectancy is eighty years. But he lives in danger, in a high-risk zone. The possibility of death is imminent. So soldiers write their letters as if they're the last ones. They live urgently.

The world was created by God's word—spoken into existence from formless void. That same word once destroyed the world through flood in Noah's time. And that same word will destroy it finally by fire.

> 5-7 But they deliberately forget that long ago by God's word the heavens came into being and the earth was formed out of water and by water. By these waters also the world of that time was deluged and destroyed. By the same word the present heavens and earth are reserved for fire, being kept for the day of judgment and destruction of the ungodly.

The earth's fate is sealed. There is no escape from this destiny. And it could happen at any moment—suddenly, by a snap of God's fingers.

Time moves faster than we realize. Six years ago, there was no Facebook, no Twitter, no iPhone. Now these have reshaped our world. Our children have grown up, started careers, begun families. Life accelerates.

## The Patience of God (v. 9)

Here's the profound mystery: the reason we have more time is not because God forgot His promise or lost power. It's because He is patient.

> 9 The Lord is not slow in keeping his promise, as some understand slowness. Instead he is patient with you, not wanting anyone to perish, but everyone to come to repentance.

God's delay is not negligence—it's mercy. He extends time so that people might repent and be saved. We exist this very moment because of that agenda: *the salvation of souls*.

For those not yet Christians, the message is urgent. You are in great danger. Time is very short. Repent and believe while you still can. God has given you another year. Don't delay salvation.

## Living in Light of Eternity

For Christians, now is the time of salvation. This awareness should transform how we live.

> 11 Since everything will be destroyed in this way, what kind of people ought you to be? You ought to live holy and godly lives as you look forward to the day of God and speed its coming. That day will bring about the destruction of the heavens by fire, and the elements will melt in the heat. But in keeping with his promise we are looking forward to a new heaven and a new earth, where righteousness dwells.

This New Year, let's live differently:

- **Live holy and godly lives.** Not like rich fools who spend without knowing what's coming, but with purpose aligned to eternity.
- **Look forward to the day of the Lord.** Keep your hope fixed on what's coming, not on temporary pleasures.
- **Make telling others about Jesus a goal this year.** God has given us another year—not for selfish enjoyment, but to serve others through family, friends, and church.

As we say "Happy New Year," remember why it's happy: **it's another chance to reconcile with God.**

The year ahead might bring unexpected encounters where God suddenly reveals Himself. You might be going to a movie, going to work, returning from holidays—SNAP—and He returns. Or someone you love comes to faith. Or you grow deeper in your own relationship with Jesus.

Don't put off this urgency. Don't waste another day on what doesn't matter. We're in the final chapter of human history. Make it count.
