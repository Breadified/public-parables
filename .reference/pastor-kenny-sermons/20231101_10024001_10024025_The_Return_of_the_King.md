# 2 Samuel 24:1-25

**Title:** The Return of the King  
**Date:** 5 November 2023  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Trusting in Self (verses 1-10)

### The Census and Its Significance

> 24:1 Again the anger of the Lord burned against Israel, and he incited David against them, saying, "Go and take a census of Israel and Judah."

This opening verse raises important questions about causation and God's role. The account in 1 Chronicles attributes the incitement to Satan, yet both accounts convey the same theological reality—taking this census represents a fundamental failure in trust.

Joab's immediate objection reveals the wrongness of the act:

> 24:2-3 So the king said to Joab and the army commanders with him, "Go throughout the tribes of Israel from Dan to Beersheba and count the people, so that I may know how many there are." But Joab replied to the king, "May the Lord your God multiply the troops a hundred times over, and may the eyes of my lord the king see it. But why does my lord the king want to do such a thing?"

Joab invokes hyperbolic language—"a hundred times over"—to express an infinite abundance. His point is clear: Israel's numbers are meant to be uncountable, like the sand of the sea. There's no need to count them; God's provision is assured.

### The Nature of the Sin

Why is counting the people sinful? The issue goes deeper than mere bureaucracy. God reserves the authority to count infinite things—the stars in the heavens, the grains of sand, the hairs on our heads. When Israel took God's promise of innumerable descendants seriously, they trusted in His provision, not in human statistics.

> Isaiah 40:26 Lift up your eyes and look to the heavens: Who created all these? He who brings out the starry host one by one and calls forth each of them by name. Because of his great power and mighty strength, not one of them is missing.

David's sin was essentially this: he stopped trusting in God's invisible abundance and began trusting in visible numbers. He wanted measurable proof of his strength. In doing so, he assumed a role that belongs only to God.

The census took nine months and twenty days to complete, traveling throughout Israel and the Transjordan. By the time it was finished, even the numbers themselves had become unreliable through elapsed time.

### David's Confession

> 24:10 David was conscience-stricken after he had counted the people, and he said to the Lord, "I have sinned greatly in what I have done. Now, Lord, I beg you, take away the guilt of your servant. I have done a very foolish thing."

Remarkably, David recognizes his sin immediately. Yet this recognition, while important, does not prevent the consequences.

### The Pattern of Misplaced Trust

David had conquered enemies consistently by following God's instructions, not by the size of his army. His victories came through faith, not force. Yet in this moment, he placed his confidence in statistics—in the appearance of strength. This represents a profound shift from his earlier dependence on the Lord.

The application to our own lives is striking. We too are tempted to trust in appearance:

- Campus Christian groups seem thriving
- Year 12 leavers heading to Connect conferences
- Church services sometimes draw large crowds

If we disconnect from news and focus only on our immediate circles, we might believe Christianity is flourishing. But our statistics are momentary, biased, skewed, and late. They capture only a fragment of reality. Meanwhile, the real cultural currents move against us—Islam now outnumbers Presbyterians in Australia; Hinduism exceeds Baptist numbers. The decline of Christian influence is measured not so much in mass apostasy as in generational attrition.

Yet we face the same temptation David did: to trust in the visible while forgetting the invisible God who counts the uncountable.

## Trusting in God (verses 11-15)

### The Choice of Judgment

> 24:11-13 Before David got up the next morning, the word of the Lord had come to Gad the prophet, David's seer: "Go and tell David, 'This is what the Lord says: I am giving you three options. Choose one of them for me to carry out against you.'" So Gad went to David and said to him, "Shall there come on you three years of famine in your land? Or three months of fleeing from your enemies while they pursue you? Or three days of plague in your land? Now then, think it over and decide how I should answer the one who sent me."

What strikes us here is that David is given a choice. Three years of famine means prolonged suffering—drought, crop failure, starvation. Three months of fleeing means constant terror and military defeat. Three days of plague means swift judgment from disease.

This teaches us something profound about suffering in a fallen world. Since all have sinned, all of us experience the consequences of sin in this life. The question is not pain versus painlessness, but what kind of suffering we will bear. As we contemplate our own afflictions, we might ask: given that suffering is inevitable, what forms would be more or less bearable?

- If you have cancer, would you prefer to be in a war zone?
- If you are depressed, would you prefer to be blind?

David, facing this impossible choice, responds by looking beyond his options to a deeper reality.

### The Appropriate Response

> 24:14 David said to Gad, "I am in deep distress. Let us fall into the hands of the Lord, for his mercy is great; but do not let me fall into human hands."

This is the turning point. David chooses the plague—the shortest option—because it forces absolute dependence on God. The first two options involve human agency and control. Famine requires managing relationships with neighboring nations; fleeing requires military strategy. But plague? That is purely God's domain.

David's choice reveals a crucial theological connection to Exodus 30:

> Exodus 30:11-16 Then the Lord said to Moses, "When you take a census of the Israelites to count them, each one must pay the Lord a ransom for his life at the time he is counted. Then no plague will come on them when you number them. Each one who crosses over to those already counted is to give a half shekel, according to the sanctuary shekel... This half shekel is an offering to the Lord... making atonement for your lives."

The same word appears in both passages: census, counting, plague. God had established a way to count His people—through atonement, through ransom, through payment. David bypassed this mechanism entirely, counting without acknowledging God's ownership of the people.

### The Plague and Its Purpose

> 24:15 So the Lord sent a plague on Israel from that morning until the end of the time designated, and seventy thousand of the people from Dan to Beersheba died.

The numbers are striking: 800,000 able-bodied men counted in Israel, 500,000 in Judah. Now, 70,000 dead. Where are your statistics now, David?

Yet even this terrible judgment reveals God's mercy. The plague was appointed for a specific time—three days—and God stopped it. This teaches us that while the consequences of sin are real and severe, God's judgment is neither permanent nor infinite in this life. Relief comes. But that relief is always temporary, because our sins against God are infinite in nature, and thus our judgment is also infinite.

## Mitigation and Rescue (verses 16-25)

### The Angel and the Threshing Floor

> 24:16 When the angel stretched out his hand to destroy Jerusalem, the Lord relented concerning the disaster and said to the angel who was afflicting the people, "Enough! Withdraw your hand." The angel of the Lord was then at the threshing floor of Araunah the Jebusite.

The angel of the Lord—like the destroying angel in Egypt—stands poised to annihilate the city. But God relents. The plague stops. Yet David sees clearly that this temporary relief does not address the fundamental problem.

> 24:17 When David saw the angel who was striking down the people, he said to the Lord, "I have sinned; I, the shepherd, have done wrong. These are but sheep. What have they done? Let your hand fall on me and my family."

David recognizes the tragic injustice: he sinned, but his people suffer. The shepherd failed, and the sheep pay the price. This is the nature of failed leadership, of broken trust, of sin's ripple effects through a community.

### The Altar and the Sacrifice

> 24:18-21 On that day Gad went to David and said to him, "Go up and build an altar to the Lord on the threshing floor of Araunah the Jebusite." So David went up, as the Lord had commanded through Gad. When Araunah looked and saw the king and his officials coming toward him, he went out and bowed down before the king with his face to the ground. Araunah said, "Why has my lord the king come to his servant?" "To buy your threshing floor," David answered, "so I can build an altar to the Lord, that the plague on the people may be stopped."

Araunah, the Jebusite, generously offers everything:

> 24:22-23 Araunah said to David, "Let my lord the king take whatever he wishes and offer it up. Here are oxen for the burnt offering, and here are threshing sledges and ox yokes for the wood. Your Majesty, Araunah gives all this to the king." Araunah also said to him, "May the Lord your God accept you."

But David refuses this gracious gift:

> 24:24 But the king replied to Araunah, "No, I insist on paying you for it. I will not sacrifice to the Lord my God burnt offerings that cost me nothing."

This is the essence of sacrifice. A sacrifice that costs nothing is no sacrifice at all. David pays fifty shekels of silver for the threshing floor and the oxen. He builds the altar. The plague stops.

> 24:25 David built an altar to the Lord there and sacrificed burnt offerings and fellowship offerings. Then the Lord answered his prayer in behalf of the land, and the plague on Israel was stopped.

### The Forward Trajectory

This moment is not the end of the story—it is a beginning. The threshing floor of Araunah becomes sacred ground:

> 2 Chronicles 3:1 Then Solomon began to build the temple of the Lord in Jerusalem on Mount Moriah, where the Lord had appeared to his father David. It was on the threshing floor of Araunah the Jebusite, the place provided by David.

The temple rises on this very spot. Here, bulls and goats are sacrificed continually—temporary mitigations of God's righteous anger, measures that provide relief but never final resolution.

Yet in the temple's shadow stands a prophecy. Jesus uses the temple as a metaphor for His own body. The Good Shepherd lays down His life for His sheep. The blood of bulls and goats, offered again and again, can never truly atone for human sin. Forgiveness is costly—infinitely costly. We cannot pay it. So God Himself, in Christ the Son, pays the price.

## The Pattern of God's Faithfulness

The deeper reason God continued to uphold David lies in His covenant promise, given years earlier:

> 2 Samuel 7:8-16 "Now then, tell my servant David, 'This is what the Lord Almighty says: I took you from the pasture, from tending the flock, and appointed you ruler over my people Israel. I have been with you wherever you have gone, and I have cut off all your enemies from before you. Now I will make your name great, like the names of the greatest men on earth... The Lord declares to you that the Lord himself will establish a house for you: When your days are over and you rest with your ancestors, I will raise up your offspring to succeed you, your own flesh and blood, and I will establish his kingdom. He is the one who will build a house for my Name, and I will establish the throne of his kingdom forever... But my love will never be taken away from him, as I took it away from Saul, whom I removed from before you. Your house and your kingdom will endure forever before me; your throne will be established forever.'"

From a human perspective, David is a mixed figure—sometimes passive, sometimes faithless, yet repeatedly confessing his sin and returning to trust in God. From God's perspective, there is the covenant. God Himself sustains David's line, not because David deserves it, but because God has sworn it.

---

## Conclusion

The narrative of 2 Samuel closes with hope, not despair. The pessimism of previous chapters—Why bother? Why is David even king?—finds its answer in the character of God Himself. God does not abandon His people when they fail. He does not withdraw His covenant when His king sins. Instead, He provides a way forward: repentance, sacrifice, and the promise of an ultimate King who will never fail.

What is required of us is faith—trust that God will care for us even when the statistics are against us, even when our circumstances seem impossible. This is the faith David learned in his downfall. This is the faith we too must learn.
