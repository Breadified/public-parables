# Genesis 3:1-24

**Title:** The Kingdom Perished
**Date:** January 7, 2011
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction

Why do good things end? Why death? Why do we lose the people and creatures we love?

Last week, we explored Genesis 1-2 and saw God's perfect kingdom established—a place where God's people lived in God's place under God's rule. Adam and Eve dwelt in the Garden, in personal relationship with their Creator, with everything they needed.

But very soon, this perfect kingdom perished. Why? Because of sin.

In Genesis 3, we witness exactly what went wrong. There are four distinct steps to how sin enters the world.

## 1. Sin: The Four Steps of Rebellion

### Step 1: God's Word is Doubted

> Now the serpent was more crafty than any of the wild animals the LORD God had made. He said to the woman, "Did God really say, 'You must not eat from any tree in the garden'?" (Genesis 3:1)

The serpent begins with a seemingly innocent question: "Did God really say this? Did He really mean it?" Notice how the serpent distorts God's command. God had said:

> "You are free to eat from any tree in the garden; but you must not eat from the tree of the knowledge of good and evil, for when you eat of it you will surely die." (Genesis 2:16-17)

The command applies to ONE tree. But the serpent asks about ANY tree. By framing it this way, he makes God's word seem unclear or excessive.

### Step 2: The Woman Exaggerates God's Word

> The woman said to the serpent, "We may eat fruit from the trees in the garden, but God did say, 'You must not eat fruit from the tree that is in the middle of the garden, and you must not touch it, or you will die.'" (Genesis 3:2-3)

Eve now adds to God's command—we may not even *touch* the tree. By exaggerating the restriction, it seems unreasonable, making it easier to justify disobedience.

### Step 3: The Serpent Denies God's Word Entirely

> "You will not surely die," the serpent said to the woman. (Genesis 3:4)

Now the serpent directly contradicts God. Not questioning anymore, but outright denial.

### Step 4: The Serpent Denies God's Goodness

> "For God knows that when you eat of it your eyes will be opened, and you will be like God, knowing good and evil." (Genesis 3:5)

Here the serpent casts God as selfish, suggesting He's hiding something good from Eve. There's a half-truth here—her eyes *will* be opened—but half-truths are still lies. The serpent implies God is keeping her from something she deserves.

### The Nature of This Knowledge

What does "knowing good and evil" actually mean? Some assume it means gaining wisdom, but that's not what happens. Others think it means becoming evil, but God Himself is described as knowing good and evil (Genesis 3:22) without being evil.

The best understanding is what we might call "determinative knowledge"—knowledge *because I determine*. When Eve sees the fruit and says "it is good," she's making that determination herself. She's deciding what is good and evil based on her own judgment.

But here's the rebellion at its core: **the determination of good and evil belongs to God alone.** When Adam and Eve claim this authority for themselves, they're committing cosmic treason. It's like an assassination of the King. This sin strikes at the very essence of God's nature and authority.

## 2. The Sinful Action and Its Theology

> When the woman saw that the fruit of the tree was good for food and pleasing to the eye, and also desirable for gaining wisdom, she took some and ate it. She also gave some to her husband, who was with her, and he ate it. (Genesis 3:6)

In direct violation of God's one command, both Adam and Eve rebel completely. They unite in their disobedience.

**Sin is rebellion.** This is the core definition. We often think sin is merely breaking rules—"I haven't murdered, I don't steal, I haven't terrorized anyone, so I haven't sinned." But that misses the point entirely.

Sin is not the mere breaking of rules; **sin is the making of new rules under a new ruler.** Sin is us saying to God: "No, I don't want You to rule my world or my life. I want to run it myself."

Breaking rules is just a symptom. Behind every sin is a heart of rebellion. Consider cancer: the tumor is the symptom, but the real problem is cells working out of control. Similarly, God isn't primarily concerned with our rule-breaking; He's after that attitude of rebellion at the heart level.

## 3. How We Rebel Today

This pattern repeats in our own lives. Let me show you several examples of how the same four steps play out:

**On sexuality:** When the world rejects God's design for sexuality:
1. They doubt God's word: "Isn't that just cultural? Surely God wants to be relevant."
2. They exaggerate it: "God is saying He's against love!"
3. They deny it: "Loving relationships are good, so they're permissible."
4. They deny His goodness: "God must be a bigot if He forbids this."

**On money:** When we struggle with greed:
1. We doubt God's word: "God can't really mean I have to choose between money and Him. It's the *love* of money that's wrong, not money itself."
2. We exaggerate: "Does God want me to be poor and destitute?"
3. We deny it: "God wants me rich first so I can help others."
4. We deny His goodness: "How else will the weak be helped if not by wealthy people?"

**On relationships:** When tempted toward unequal partnerships:
1. We doubt: "God can't really mean don't date non-Christians."
2. We exaggerate: "Does God want me to be lonely forever?"
3. We deny: "God is okay with this as long as there's love and commitment."
4. We deny His goodness: "God won't look out for me, so I have to look out for myself."

The temptation to rebel always starts small—by questioning God's word. And it always leads the same place: to sin, and then to death.

## 4. Judgment: The Fearful Response

After eating, Adam and Eve's first response is fear and shame:

> Then the eyes of both of them were opened, and they realized they were naked; so they sewed fig leaves together and made coverings for themselves. Then the man and his wife heard the sound of the LORD God as he was walking in the garden in the cool of the day, and they hid from the LORD God among the trees of the garden. But the LORD God called to the man, "Where are you?" He answered, "I heard you in the garden, and I was afraid because I was naked; so I hid." (Genesis 3:7-10)

They hide. They're afraid. They're ashamed. This is the human condition after sin—we cover ourselves, we hide from God, we live in fear of judgment.

## 5. The Nature of God's Judgment

God's judgment falls in stages:

**On the serpent:**
> "Cursed are you above all the livestock and all the wild animals! You will crawl on your belly and you will eat dust all the days of your life." (Genesis 3:14)

**On the woman:**
> "I will greatly increase your pains in childbearing; with pain you will give birth to children. Your desire will be for your husband, and he will rule over you." (Genesis 3:16)

**On the man:**
> "Cursed is the ground because of you; through painful toil you will eat of it all the days of your life. It will produce thorns and thistles for you, and you will eat the plants of the field." (Genesis 3:17-18)

Notice how sin reverses the order of creation: the serpent influences the woman, who influences the man. In judgment, God addresses them in reverse order, and their punishments relate to their creation purposes.

**But the ultimate judgment is death:**

> "By the sweat of your brow you will eat your food until you return to the ground, since from it you were taken; for dust you are and to dust you will return." (Genesis 3:19)

The kingdom has perished. People are shamed and dead. The place of blessing is lost. God's rule is now God's judgment.

## 6. The Vicious Cycle

But perhaps you think, "I'm not Adam or Eve. This doesn't apply to me."

Look at what happens in the chapters that follow. Genesis 4-11 shows us that every generation repeats this pattern:

- **Cain** (Genesis 4:1-17): His sin is murder, and his judgment is to wander the earth.
- **Noah's generation** (Genesis 6:9-22): Their sin is wickedness, and their judgment is uncreation—the flood.
- **Babel** (Genesis 11:1-9): Their sin is pride, and their judgment is scattered confusion.

The vicious cycle continues. It doesn't matter who you are—any scenario with humans in it will eventually result in rebellion. We live in a nice part of the world and think we can beat sin based on externalities: "I don't murder or rape, so I'm okay." But this is false confidence. It's arrogance to think we can avoid death or beat its reality by distracting ourselves.

The reality is unavoidable: we all face death. We can be distracted by life's trivialities, drunk on busyness, but one day we must sober up to reality. We are getting older every day. We are facing death. The people around us will die.

## 7. Salvation: There is Hope

But here is the beautiful truth: **God does not leave us to die.** There is hope.

### God's Mercy in the Midst of Judgment

Even after judgment, God shows mercy:

> Adam named his wife Eve, because she would become the mother of all the living. The LORD God made garments of skin for Adam and his wife and clothed them. (Genesis 3:20-21)

God clothes them. God Himself provides covering. He doesn't leave them naked and ashamed.

Then:

> Adam lay with his wife Eve, and she became pregnant and gave birth to Cain. She said, "With the help of the LORD I have brought forth a man." Later she gave birth to his brother Abel. (Genesis 4:1-2)

God continues to give life. Despite death's sentence, life still flows from His hand.

This pattern of mercy continues throughout Genesis 4-11. When Cain is marked for judgment, God protects him from being killed (Genesis 4:15). When Noah's generation faces the flood, God preserves Noah and his family in the ark (Genesis 7:23b).

**God is merciful from the beginning.** This isn't an Old Testament vs. New Testament distinction—it's God's character throughout. He must punish because He is just, but He mitigates that punishment. He disciplines, but with compassion. He ensures survival and continuation even in judgment.

### The Promise of a Savior

Most importantly, there's a hint of the ultimate solution in Genesis 3:15:

> "And I will put enmity between you and the woman, and between your offspring and hers; he will crush your head, and you will strike his heel." (Genesis 3:15)

This offspring of the woman is Jesus. The serpent strikes His heel—killing Him on the cross. But through that very contact, He crushes the head of the serpent. And through His resurrection, Jesus crushes death itself. The vicious cycle is broken.

### God's Heart: He Does Not Delight in Death

At the judge's heart is love. God does not want to punish. Listen to His own words through the prophet Ezekiel:

> "Therefore, O house of Israel, I will judge you, each one according to his ways, declares the Sovereign LORD. Repent! Turn away from all your offenses; then sin will not be your downfall. Rid yourselves of all the offenses you have committed, and get a new heart and a new spirit. Why will you die, O house of Israel? For I take no pleasure in the death of anyone, declares the Sovereign LORD. Repent and live!" (Ezekiel 18:30-32)

> "This is what the Sovereign LORD says: I take no pleasure in the death of the wicked, but rather that they turn from their ways and live. Turn! Turn from your evil ways! Why will you die, O house of Israel?" (Ezekiel 33:11)

God Himself says: I don't delight in your death. I want you to turn and live.

## Conclusion

Death is real. It's the consequence of sin, and we all face it. We can deny it, distract ourselves from it, even try to control it—but we cannot escape it. One day, sooner or later, we must face the reality of mortality.

But for those who know Christ, death is not the end. Our grief over death is real, but it is not final. It is joyful grief, because we know with certainty that the Christian is in a better place, in a better kingdom. We are assured of real hope after death.

But if you are not a Christian, one day when people grieve for you, it will be a day without hope. It will truly be sad.

So deal with this permanence of death today. The answer is not "who cares for you." The answer is not "you die, you die"—you know that's inadequate. The answer is Jesus. Turn from your rebellion. Turn to the One who died to break the vicious cycle of sin and death.
