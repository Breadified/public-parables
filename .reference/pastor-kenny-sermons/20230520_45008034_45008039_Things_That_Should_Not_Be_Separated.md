# Romans 8:34-39

**Title:** Things That Should Not Be Separated
**Date:** May 20, 2023
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Power of Perfect Pairings

There are some things in life that simply belong together. Great chefs understand this instinctively—chocolate and coffee, bread and cheese, bacon and eggs. As Remy says in *Ratatouille*, "Each flavor was totally unique. But, combine one flavor with another, and something new was created!"

Some pairings are so natural they feel inevitable. Consider this couple—both from selective schools in Indonesia, both grew up in the hills, both have brothers. Remarkably, their mothers even worked together at Citibank in Jakarta while pregnant at the same time. These two were made to be together.

Yet in Scripture, there is a pairing far greater than any earthly compatibility: humanity and the love of God.

## Created for Relationship

We were designed to walk with our Creator. In the garden of Eden, God and humanity enjoyed genuine fellowship—compatible, delighting in each other's company. A father and child. A husband and wife. A friend and friend. This is how it was meant to be.

Some find the existence of God self-evident. Others struggle with belief, especially within a purely material, evolutionary worldview. Yet consider the signs around us: the miracle of a child's birth, the mysterious reality of love at a wedding. Are these merely chemicals and selfish genes at work? Or is there something transcendent?

Perhaps we possess a worth that cannot be measured economically or genetically—a worth given because we have a Creator. If this is true, then Scripture's promise follows:

> Neither height nor depth, nor anything else in all creation, will be able to separate us from the love of God that is in Christ Jesus our Lord. (Romans 8:39)

## The Tragedy of Separation

Yet unfortunately, separation exists. Not separation of distance—you cannot fly to Hawaii or dig to the earth's core to escape God. Rather, separation of relationship.

A married couple may lie in the same bed while being as distant as the moon—not because of proximity, but because of broken trust and wrong. Sin creates relational distance. When Adam and Eve ate the forbidden fruit, they rebelled against God's authority. They denied the fundamental compatibility between Creator and creation, claiming independence from their Maker. In that moment, a height of separation appeared. A depth opened between them that no human effort could bridge.

When we cry out "God, where are you?" in our troubles, the problem is not God's existence or absence. The problem is sin—ours and the world's. We ignore God, use His name as a curse, hurt His creation, then wonder why He gives us over to our chosen fate.

### Sin Ruins Compatibility

A perfect pairing, destroyed. A couple meant to be inseparable now finds themselves broken by selfish desires, fighting as individuals instead of partners. The separation is devastating. When you're left with only the left shoe, or a water bottle with no lid, or a burger without fries—something essential is missing. But sin's separation from God is far more tragic. It is eternal, separated by infinite height, separated by infinite depth.

This raises a troubling question: *If I haven't committed infinite sin, why do I deserve infinite judgment?*

The answer lies not in the quantity of sin, but in whom we sin against. To sin against the infinite God is qualitatively different from any other offense. When we betray the one who gave us life, we face an infinite debt. A marriage destroyed by infidelity is a perfect pairing ruined. Now, seemingly incompatible.

## The Solution: Christ Jesus

But God will not allow this unpairing to continue forever. He has given us a solution:

> Who then is the one who condemns? No one. Christ Jesus who died—more than that, who was raised to life—is at the right hand of God and is also interceding for us. (Romans 8:34)

Consider the claims of Jesus. He was a real man, history's wisest teacher, whose ethics and laws still shape civilization. Yet He claimed to be God Himself. Two thousand years ago, He came in person—Christmas, 33 BC.

What He did:

**He died.** This was sacrificial love—God Himself taking upon Himself the punishment we deserved. Forgiveness means absorbing the wrong against you, bearing the hurt yourself rather than demanding judgment.

**He was raised to life.** Because He is the infinite God, He swallowed death's pain and conquered death itself, once and for all.

**He intercedes for us.** Now, through His sacrifice, Jesus pleads with God to forgive us—not just our past sins, but our future ones as well. If we repent and ask, Jesus stands as our advocate, basing His plea on His payment made in full.

As a result, we are brought together with God. We become compatible once again.

## A Symbol of Inseparability

I like to think that this couple were brought together not merely by their matching backgrounds, but by something greater: Jesus, who brought them together as two people forgiven by God. Their mothers' unlikely connection in Jakarta may have been providential, but what truly binds them is their mutual reconciliation with God through Christ.

Likewise, we have been brought together with our Creator—and the result is inseparability.

> Who shall separate us from the love of Christ? Shall trouble or hardship or persecution or famine or nakedness or danger or sword? ... No, in all these things we are more than conquerors through him who loved us. For I am convinced that neither death nor life, neither angels nor demons, neither the present nor the future, nor any powers, neither height nor depth, nor anything else in all creation, will be able to separate us from the love of God that is in Christ Jesus our Lord. (Romans 8:35, 37-39)

Christians worldwide face these trials—many more in Africa, Asia, and the Middle East than in Western nations. Yet nothing can separate us from Christ's love.

## Marriage as God's Picture

Marriage itself is a symbol God has given us—a picture of this inseparability. It can be denigrated and broken, but that is not God's intention. True marriage represents God's will triumphing over human frailty, the bringing together of two people to reflect the inseparability between God and His people, between Christ and His church.

It is the mark of a master chef to combine seemingly disparate elements into something beautiful. Chocolate and chili, kimchi and cheddar, sea salt and caramel, brie and blueberry—strange pairings that somehow work. The kitchen and apron, the chef and towel—all coming together to create something nourishing and whole.

But notice what happens: the dirt from preparation clings to everything—the apron, the towel, the chef's hands. Jesus is that master chef. He bears all of it upon Himself, so we might be presented clean before the Father.

Us and God—a strange pairing by human logic. Yet through God's redemptive work in Christ, we are brought together. And it is that Chef who will keep you together, even when you feel utterly incompatible with one another. His love is unbreakable. His commitment is eternal.

Neither height nor depth can ever separate you from that love.
