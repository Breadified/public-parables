# Matthew 13:1-58

**Title:** The Hidden Kingdom
**Date:** March 15, 2009
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Main Teaching

Jesus speaks in parables as a form of silent, parabolic division—separating those who understand the kingdom from those who remain in spiritual blindness. The parables function not primarily to illuminate but to reveal who truly listens to God's voice.

## The Parable of the Sower (13:1-9)

> That same day Jesus went out of the house and sat by the lake. Such large crowds gathered around him that he got into a boat and sat in it, while all the people stood on the shore. Then he told them many things in parables, saying: "A farmer went out to sow his seed. As he was scattering the seed, some fell along the path, and the birds came and ate it up. Some fell on rocky places, where it did not have much soil. It sprang up quickly, because the soil was shallow. But when the sun came up, the plants were scorched, and they withered because they had no root. Other seed fell among thorns, which grew up and choked the plants. Still other seed fell on good soil, where it produced a crop—a hundred, sixty or thirty times what was sown. He who has ears, let him hear.

This simple, agricultural parable from everyday life introduces a profound spiritual principle. The seed's fate depends entirely on the condition of the soil that receives it.

## Why Parables? The Hidden Kingdom (13:10-17)

The disciples immediately ask the crucial question: "Why do you speak to the people in parables?" This question reveals the paradox that would define Jesus's ministry in Matthew. Are parables meant to clarify or to conceal?

> The knowledge of the secrets of the kingdom of heaven has been given to you, but not to them. Whoever has will be given more, and he will have an abundance. Whoever does not have, even what he has will be taken from him. This is why I speak to them in parables: "Though seeing, they do not see; though hearing, they do not hear or understand.

Jesus quotes Isaiah 6, applying that ancient prophecy to his own ministry:

> In them is fulfilled the prophecy of Isaiah: "You will be ever hearing but never understanding; you will be ever seeing but never perceiving. For this people's heart has become calloused; they hardly hear with their ears, and they have closed their eyes. Otherwise they might see with their eyes, hear with their ears, understand with their hearts and turn, and I would heal them."

### Understanding Isaiah's Prophecy

Isaiah was sent to Israel with a message they would refuse to hear, bringing judgment upon themselves. Jesus applies this directly to his contemporaries. The Israelites' hardened hearts—calloused like a guitarist's fingers that have lost sensitivity—prevent them from perceiving the kingdom. This is not God forcing blindness upon them; rather, they have closed their own eyes and hardened their own hearts.

The apparent harshness of "otherwise I would heal them" reflects not divine cruelty but the tragic reality of judgment: God desires to heal them, but they will not repent. The kingdom remains hidden from those whose hearts have turned away.

### The Revealed Kingdom (13:16-17)

Yet there is a striking contrast:

> But blessed are your eyes because they see, and your ears because they hear. For I tell you the truth, many prophets and righteous men longed to see what you see but did not see it, and to hear what you hear but did not hear it.

The disciples, also Jewish, occupy a fundamentally different position. They listen. They follow. They ask questions seeking understanding. Their privilege exceeds even that of the prophets and righteous ones of old.

## The Secret of the Kingdom (13:18-23)

Jesus explains the parable, revealing the secret that distinguishes the disciples from the crowds:

> Listen then to what the parable of the sower means. When anyone hears the message about the kingdom and does not understand it, the evil one comes and snatches away what was sown in his heart. This is the seed sown along the path. The one who received the seed that fell on rocky places is the man who hears the word and at once receives it with joy. But since he has no root, he lasts only a short time. When trouble or persecution comes because of the word, he quickly falls away. The one who received the seed that fell among the thorns is the man who hears the word, but the worries of this life and the deceitfulness of wealth choke it, making it unfruitful. But the one who received the seed that fell on good soil is the man who hears the word and understands it. He produces a crop, yielding a hundred, sixty or thirty times what was sown.

The secret is remarkably simple: **hear the word and understand it**. This is the essence of discipleship. There is no hidden mysticism here, no Gnostic knowledge reserved for the elite. The "secret" is available to anyone who genuinely listens.

### Four Responses to God's Word

**1. The Path (Hardened Heart):** The word is snatched away before it can take root. The listener never grasps its meaning.

**2. The Rocky Ground (Shallow Joy):** Initial enthusiasm masks the absence of depth. When persecution or difficulty comes, faith withers because there are no spiritual roots.

**3. The Thorns (Choked by Worldliness):** This response is especially relevant to affluent, ambitious people. The worries of this age and the deceitfulness of wealth gradually entangle the heart. The person hears God's word and even grows, but the world's allure grows faster. Worldly priorities—money, power, sex—eventually crowd out spiritual ones. Friendships shift toward those pursuing the same things. Moral standards compromise. Wisdom fades. What remains is a double life, indistinguishable from the world except for a Sunday church attendance. Like fingers calloused from worldly pursuits, the heart becomes insensitive to God.

This slow choking is not harmless unfruitfulness. It is spiritual death, just as devastating as seed snatched away or plants withered in drought.

**4. The Good Soil (Fruitfulness):** This person hears, understands, and produces abundant fruit. Unlike the others, he is not snatched away, not temporary, not choked. He remains rooted, growing, and bearing witness to God's kingdom.

### Application: The Skill of Listening

The key skill Jesus wants to develop in his disciples is listening—true, attentive, understanding listening. This *is* faith in action. The blessing that follows is abundance of fruit and the knowledge of God's kingdom.

For those who gather regularly to hear God's word—whether in church, Bible study, or personal reading—the call is clear: listen and understand. Not to the preacher's words, but to the Scripture itself. Work through the text. Ask why. This is why expository preaching matters. It trains us to hear God's voice directly through his word.

## The Revealing Kingdom: Parables of Growth (13:24-35)

Why does the kingdom seem so small, so hidden, so unsuccessful? The disciples must have wondered this. Matthew 10 showed division within Israel. Matthew 11 showed unrepentant cities rejecting Jesus. Matthew 12 showed Pharisees plotting his death. Where was the kingdom's triumph?

Jesus addresses this discouragement through parables emphasizing that the kingdom operates silently and gradually, revealing itself fully only at the end of the age.

### The Weeds and the Wheat (13:24-30)

> The kingdom of heaven is like a man who sowed good seed in his field. But while everyone was sleeping, his enemy came and sowed weeds among the wheat, and went away. When the wheat sprouted and formed heads, then the weeds also appeared.

The servants ask, "Should we pull up the weeds?" The owner responds:

> No, because while you are pulling the weeds, you may root up the wheat with them. Let both grow together until the harvest. At that time I will tell the harvesters: First collect the weeds and tie them in bundles to be burned; then gather the wheat and bring it into my barn.

This parable teaches patience. The kingdom's victory is assured but postponed. Premature action would damage the good alongside the bad. Judgment belongs to God alone and comes at the appointed time.

### The Mustard Seed (13:31-32)

> The kingdom of heaven is like a mustard seed, which a man took and planted in his field. Though it is the smallest of all your seeds, yet when it grows, it is the largest of garden plants and becomes a tree, so that the birds of the air come and perch in its branches.

From tiny, insignificant beginnings comes surprising growth and shelter for others.

### The Yeast (13:33)

> The kingdom of heaven is like yeast that a woman took and mixed into a large amount of flour until it worked all through the dough.

Kingdom growth is hidden yet pervasive, working quietly through an entire mass until transformation is complete.

### Jesus's Method: The Silent Kingdom (13:34-35)

> Jesus spoke all these things to the crowd in parables; he did not say anything to them without using a parable. So was fulfilled what was spoken through the prophet: "I will open my mouth in parables, I will utter things hidden since the creation of the world."

The Old Testament promised no such hidden, parabolic kingdom. The political kingdoms of Scripture were geographically defined, ethically clear, and openly victorious. Jesus's kingdom operates differently—silently, mysteriously, hidden from hostile eyes yet progressively revealing itself to those with ears to hear.

This is Jesus's deliberate strategy: kingdom advancement through parables rather than political conquest, through quiet influence rather than spectacular power. A submarine's greatest strength lies not in announcing itself but in moving undetected toward its objective.

### Application: Encouragement for the Long Journey

This teaching encourages disciples facing opposition or apparent failure. When people reject you, when nothing seems to happen, when growth feels imperceptible—remember that the kingdom is revealed only at harvest time. This is a lifetime project.

All we can control is our own discipleship. The only thing we can change is our own listening, understanding, and obedience. Leave the final accounting to God.

## The Revealed Kingdom Explained (13:36-50)

When Jesus moves into the house with his disciples, they ask for the interpretation of the weeds parable:

> The one who sowed the good seed is the Son of Man. The field is the world, and the good seed stands for the sons of the kingdom. The weeds are the sons of the evil one, and the enemy who sows them is the devil. The harvest is the end of the age, and the harvesters are angels. As the weeds are pulled up and burned in the fire, so it will be at the end of the age. The Son of Man will send out his angels, and they will weed out of his kingdom everything that causes sin and all who do evil. They will throw them into the fiery furnace, where there will be weeping and gnashing of teeth. Then the righteous will shine like the sun in the kingdom of their Father. He who has ears, let him hear.

The weeds parable culminates in final judgment: separation, burning, and the vindication of the righteous.

### Parables of Priceless Worth (13:44-46)

Jesus offers two more kingdom parables:

> The kingdom of heaven is like treasure hidden in a field. When a man found it, he hid it again, and then in his joy went and sold all he had and bought that field.

> Again, the kingdom of heaven is like a merchant looking for fine pearls. When he found one of great value, he went away and sold everything he had and bought it.

Both convey the same truth: the kingdom's value is so transcendent that any sacrifice to obtain it is worthwhile. Joy accompanies the discovery—this is not joyless duty but gladness at finding what matters most.

### The Dragnet (13:47-50)

> Once again, the kingdom of heaven is like a net that was let down into the lake and caught all kinds of fish. When it was full, the fishermen pulled it up on the shore. Then they sat down and collected the good fish in baskets, but threw the bad away. This is how it will be at the end of the age. The angels will come and separate the wicked from the righteous and throw them into the fiery furnace, where there will be weeping and gnashing of teeth.

Like the weeds and wheat, this parable warns that judgment distinguishes between the genuine and the false, the righteous and the wicked.

## The Final Parable: Understanding as Discipleship (13:51-52)

Jesus checks the disciples' comprehension:

> "Have you understood all these things?" Jesus asked. "Yes," they replied.

Then he offers one final parable—arguably the most important:

> Therefore every teacher of the law who has been instructed about the kingdom of heaven is like the owner of a house who brings out of his storeroom new treasures as well as old.

The disciple, particularly the teacher of Scripture, is a steward who draws from both the old (the Hebrew Scripture and its wisdom) and the new (the revelation of Jesus and his interpretation). The steward integrates, interprets, and applies the full counsel of God across time, honoring both Testament and bringing forth the riches of understanding.

## The Bookends: Discipleship in Matthew (12:46-50; 13:53-58)

The passage opens with a crucial definition:

> While Jesus was still talking to the crowd, his mother and brothers stood outside, wanting to speak to him. Someone told him, "Your mother and brothers are standing outside, wanting to speak to you." He replied to him, "Who is my mother, and who are my brothers?" Pointing to his disciples, he said, "Here are my mother and my brothers. For whoever does the will of my Father in heaven is my brother and sister and mother."

Discipleship redefines family around obedience to God's will.

The passage closes with a sobering note:

> When Jesus had finished these parables, he moved on from there. Coming to his hometown, he began teaching the people in their synagogue, and they were amazed. "Where did this man get this wisdom and these miraculous powers?" they asked. "Isn't this the carpenter's son? Isn't his mother's name Mary, and aren't his brothers James, Joseph, Simon and Judas? Aren't all his sisters with us? Where then did this man get all these things?" And they took offense at him. But Jesus said to them, "Only in his hometown and in his own house is a prophet without honor." And he did not do many miracles there because of their lack of faith.

His own townspeople—those most familiar with him—take offense. Familiarity breeds contempt, not faith. Their lack of faith limits what Jesus can do among them. They hear his wisdom but do not understand. They see his power but do not believe. The kingdom remains hidden from them.

## The Heart of Kingdom Teaching

The parables of Matthew 13 reveal that the kingdom of heaven operates according to principles radically different from earthly kingdoms. It advances through listening and understanding, not through force. It grows quietly, not spectacularly. It distinguishes between genuine faith and mere profession. It promises judgment for those who reject it and vindication for those who embrace it.

The central call remains unchanged: **Listen to Jesus. Understand his word. Bear fruit in obedience.** This is the secret of the kingdom—not hidden knowledge for the few, but open invitation to all who have ears to hear.
