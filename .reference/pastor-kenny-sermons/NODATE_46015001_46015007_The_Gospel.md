# 1 Corinthians 15:1-7, 20-28

**Title:** The Gospel

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## What Is the Gospel?

Many of us have grown up with a familiar answer to this question: Christ died for me. He saved me unconditionally. He loves you. It's a simple, beautiful message—someone loves you enough to die for you. Who wouldn't accept that?

But Paul's understanding of the gospel in 1 Corinthians 15 challenges us to think more deeply about what we believe and proclaim.

## The Gospel of the Resurrection of Christ

Paul begins by establishing the core of his message:

> Now, brothers and sisters, I want to remind you of the gospel I preached to you, which you received and on which you have taken your stand. By this gospel you are saved, if you hold firmly to the word I preached to you. Otherwise, you have believed in vain. (1 Corinthians 15:1-2)

The word "gospel" literally means "good news"—from the Greek *evangelion*. This isn't referring to the biographies of Jesus (Matthew, Mark, Luke, John), but rather Paul's theological summary of what Jesus accomplished. Throughout 1 Corinthians 15, Paul defines the gospel through a series of historical facts:

> That Christ died for our sins according to the Scriptures, that he was buried, that he was raised on the third day according to the Scriptures, and that he appeared to Cephas, and then to the Twelve. After that, he appeared to more than five hundred of the brothers and sisters at the same time, most of whom are still living, though some have fallen asleep. Then he appeared to James, then to all the apostles. (1 Corinthians 15:3-7)

Notice what Paul emphasizes through repetition: *he appeared, he appeared, he appeared*. The resurrection isn't incidental to the gospel—it is central to it. The historical reality of Christ's resurrection is proven by the eyewitnesses who could be questioned, many still alive when Paul wrote these words.

Paul underscores this further:

> And if Christ has not been raised, our preaching is useless and so is your faith. (1 Corinthians 15:14)

Without the resurrection, our faith is futile. In fact, without it, we are "false witnesses about God" because we testify that He raised Christ from the dead—a testimony that means nothing if the resurrection never happened.

## The Atonement and the Resurrection

The death of Christ is absolutely necessary for our salvation. Through what theologians call "penal substitutionary atonement," Jesus paid the debt our sins incurred. We sinned against God and broke our relationship with Him. That breach requires compensation—justice demands a payment. Jesus, the Lamb of God, offered Himself as the sacrificial payment on our behalf.

But here's what's critical to understand: the atonement is incomplete without the resurrection.

> And if Christ has not been raised, your faith is futile; you are still in your sins. (1 Corinthians 15:17)

Paul isn't saying the payment was made but we don't get to enjoy it. He's saying something more profound: without the resurrection, there is no effective payment at all. The cross alone is not the conclusion of the gospel story.

Think of it this way: imagine a friend buys you food at your favorite shop and walks away to deliver it to you. If he never comes back, what do you conclude? Either he ran out of money, lost his way, or the effort wasn't enough. But when he returns with your meal, it proves his promise was genuine and his sacrifice was complete.

The death of Christ proves its effectiveness only through the resurrection. The resurrection is God's declaration: "The debt is paid. The sacrifice is sufficient. Death itself has been defeated."

> But Christ has indeed been raised from the dead, the firstfruits of those who have fallen asleep. For since death came through a man, the resurrection of the dead comes also through a man. For as in Adam all die, so in Christ all will be made alive. (1 Corinthians 15:20-22)

**The cross is the means of salvation; the resurrection is the proof and power of it.**

This is why the resurrection must be part of our gospel proclamation. It reveals that:

- Death has been defeated
- Satan has been conquered
- We will truly be saved—we will live beyond death and judgment
- Christ's love is not merely sentimental but effective and transformative

## The Lordship of Christ

Yet the resurrection carries another crucial implication that makes the gospel challenging in our world today.

> Then the end will come, when he hands over the kingdom to God the Father after he has destroyed all dominion, authority and power. For he must reign until he has put all his enemies under his feet. The last enemy to be destroyed is death. (1 Corinthians 15:24-26)

Believing in the resurrection means acknowledging Christ as King—not just in sentiment, but in actual Lordship over our lives. Where there is a kingdom, there is a king.

This is the demanding part of the Christian faith. Just as we have received salvation in advance of future salvation, we must submit to Christ's Lordship now in anticipation of His future reign. This means asking ourselves honestly: Is Jesus truly Lord of my life?

**Jesus is Lord of my personal life**—my behaviors, my attitudes, what I post on social media, what I watch in private moments.

**Jesus is Lord of my relationships**—how I treat my classmates, my friends, how I conduct myself with the opposite sex. He does not call us to treat others as "friends with benefits" but as image-bearers deserving of dignity and respect.

**Jesus is Lord of my school**—not compromised by culture but standing for what is good and speaking against what is evil.

**Jesus is Lord of my nation and the world**—this is not a private faith confined to Sunday mornings. As you grow and take your place in society as voters, employers, leaders, and citizens, you will help establish institutions under Christ's Lordship.

This gospel affects everything—your science, your mathematics, your geography, your movies, your study groups, your school meetings. It shapes your worldview and ideology.

## The Call to Stand Firm

Paul closes with a powerful reminder:

> Now, brothers and sisters, I want to remind you of the gospel I preached to you, which you received and **on which you have taken your stand**. By this gospel you are saved, **if you hold firmly to the word I preached to you**. (1 Corinthians 15:1-2)

The Christian life is not easy. It requires courage to believe in the supernatural when the culture ridicules it, to submit to Christ's Lordship when the world demands your allegiance to its values. You may face peer pressure, bullying, ridicule for your faith.

But remember: Jesus does love you. He has endured the cross for your sake. Now it is time to respond to His grace through obedience to His will. This is not about earning salvation through works, but about living out what you truly believe—that Christ died, was buried, was raised, and reigns as Lord over all.
