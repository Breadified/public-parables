# Ephesians 2:1-10

**Title:** The Great Escape: Death
**Date:** 22 January 2022
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Reality of Our Condition: Objects of Wrath (1-3)

Paul writes to the Ephesians with a sobering diagnosis of the human condition:

> As for you, you were **dead** in your transgressions and sins, in which you used to live when you **followed the ways of this world** and of the **ruler** of the kingdom of the air, the spirit who is now at work in those who are disobedient. All of us also lived among them at one time, **gratifying the cravings of our flesh and following its desires and thoughts.**

We begin in a state of spiritual death—not merely heading toward death, but already dead. Our death is caused not by disease or age, but by our broken relationship with God, by sin.

### Three Layers of Our Spiritual Death

**First, we follow the ways of this world.** We establish our own laws, reject God's authority, and live by standards we create ourselves.

**Second, we follow the ruler of the kingdom of the air.** This refers to Satan—the personification of evil who, after his defeat in heaven, was given temporary authority over the earth until the time of judgment. The "air" represents the space between earth and heaven where his influence operates. This is the sphere of danger, the realm where spiritual forces work against us.

**Third, we gratify the cravings of our sinful nature.** We pursue pleasure, lust, and materialism. We follow our desires and thoughts as though they were guides to truth. Every Hollywood film, every cultural message tells us to follow what we feel—but if our nature is sinful, those feelings lead us astray.

### The Weight of God's Judgment

It's difficult for us to imagine being "objects of wrath" when we consider ourselves good people. We think of our best days, our kindest moments. But God does not judge us on our good days—He judges us on our worst days.

Consider Hitler: he had a childhood, loved his dog, loved Eva Braun. But we don't judge Hitler by his good days. We judge him by what he did on his worst days. Similarly, we are judged on our worst days—when we are angry, when we are selfish, when we panic and think only of ourselves. When forced to choose between our life and someone else's, who do we choose?

The pandemic revealed our moral weakness. Things we regret. Too easily we are willing to harm our neighbor if we fear for our family. We vote for others to do the dirty work. We are judged by our lusts and greed. If it came down to money or someone else's wellbeing, what would we choose?

We are rightly deserving of God's wrath. We are condemned. Ready for destruction.

### Death Is Inevitable

We cannot escape death. From the moment we're born, we've been dying. Our bodies have built-in mortality. The heart has a shelf life. Our cells eventually stop working as they should, leading to cancer, heart disease, arthritis, and Alzheimer's.

In recent years, there's been a peculiar thinking that we can prevent death forever, that no one ever has to die. But people simply die in different ways. We may delay death, but we cannot prevent it. To keep the very sick and elderly alive while locking them away from family for years—this is a false economy. We have bigger problems.

Lockdowns don't prevent deaths; they only prevent funerals.

### Preparing for Death

The reality is not "if" we die, but "when" we die. There is only one statistic that matters: 100 percent. All of us will die.

If you have dependents, prepare practically. Get insurance so your family will be cared for. Have your affairs in order. But prepare spiritually as well. Ask yourself: Where will my body go after death? Where will my soul spend eternity?

## The Great Reversal: Objects of Mercy (4-9)

But here is where the gospel intervenes with grace:

> But because of his great love for us, God, who is rich in mercy, **made us alive** with Christ even when we were dead in transgressions—it is by **grace you have been saved.**

The turning point is "but"—an alternative to death. Instead of facing judgment, we are made alive. We are given the opposite of what we deserve.

> And God **raised us up** with Christ and seated us with him in the heavenly realms in Christ Jesus, in order that in the coming ages he might show the incomparable riches of his grace, expressed in his kindness to us in Christ Jesus. For it is by grace you have been saved, through faith—and this is not from yourselves, it is the gift of God—not by works, so that no one can boast.

### What God Has Done

"Raised us up" echoes resurrection language. The dead are lying flat; to be raised up is to arise, to be alive again. Just as Christ was resurrected from death, so will we be. "Seated" means we join Him in rest, after the suffering of this earthly life.

All of this comes through grace—God's kindness, His love, His mercy. It is a gift. It is not from ourselves. It cannot be earned by our works, so that no one can boast. We failed to keep God's law; we cannot save ourselves through our own effort.

### The Cross: Our True Escape

God proved this love on the cross. There He swapped His life for ours. This message of the cross is essential for life itself.

### The Church as Essential Service

The church is an essential service—not like a social club, pub, or disco, but like a hospital. An essential service deals with life and death: ambulance, hospital, pharmacy, food. The church deals with eternal life and death.

The church is not designed to keep you safe in this life. An emergency room is not safe—all the sick are gathered there. An ambulance is not safe—it's a gathering place of the injured. The church, similarly, is a gathering place of the spiritually sick, seeking treatment.

The church is designed to save you in the next life. If we truly believe the gospel is a matter of life and death, how can we close the church when people are dying spiritually? Do we close hospitals when there are deaths in the streets? Do paramedics refuse to go because they might get infected? Do firefighters take holiday when buildings are burning?

If your church is truly essential to you—not as a social gathering, but as a place of salvation—then come. The church needs its gospel doctors and nurses, ready to minister to the spiritually sick, to encourage one another to remain faithful until the final judgment, when some will face damnation and others eternal life.

## Our New Purpose: Objects of Good Works (10)

> For we are God's handiwork, created in Christ Jesus to do good works, which God prepared in advance for us to do.

### What This Means

"God's handiwork"—we are the work of His hands. We are His creation, remade and perfected in Christ. Nothing we can boast about. We don't create ourselves; He creates us anew.

But there's more. We are God's handiwork *so that we will work*. We are now to be His hands in the world. Through us, God brings the next generation into eternal life. We are His tools to shape the world to come.

"Good works, which God prepared in advance for us to do"—this is already waiting for us. The path is prepared. The pieces are cut out. All we must do is step into it.

### Battling Sin with the Seriousness We Give to Disease

Imagine if we treated sin the way we treat disease. If someone were engaging in sexual sin with an unmarried partner, we'd say: "That's not safe. Let's maintain social distance." If adultery were discovered in an office, we'd shut the entire building down. If abortion were practiced in a state, we'd lock it all down. If our words weren't edifying, we'd put a mask on and keep silent.

If we've been exposed to sin, we'd confess it to one another and get tested—a rapid assurance test. We'd be desperate for spiritual life, willing to give up parties, relationships, and luxuries to do good.

### Our New Identity and Purpose

Before, we were dead—no purpose, ready to be collected and disposed of. Now we are rescued. We are meant to be workers of good.

God has prepared in advance the good works we will do. Perhaps it's telling a friend worried about disease about Jesus. Perhaps it's inviting someone new to church this year—someone who will like it enough to return. Perhaps it's telling your grandparents about Jesus.

If you are a grandparent, what greater good works could you do than to come to church and set an example for your grandchildren? They have more than enough distractions, the least willpower to resist the world's pull, and have spent perhaps only six months in church over the last two years. Your faithful presence would encourage them immeasurably.

You are an essential worker. Life and death hang in the balance.

---

## The Choice Before Us

Friends, we are all going to die. The question is not *when* we will die, but *how* we will die.

Will we die thinking about ourselves—our needs, our sins, our fears, our escape from the normal life of boredom, fear, and pain?

Or will we die doing the good works prepared in advance for us? And in that, truly escape death.
