# Philippians 1:12-30

**Title:** Joy in Setbacks

**Date:** 13 June 2010

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Progress Through Adversity

We gather today with heavy hearts. Our senior minister Matt recently visited us, and this was the last congregation he attended before entering the hospital for medical treatment. This setback reminds us that suffering comes to those we love and those who have served us faithfully. Yet Paul's letter to the Philippians teaches us something remarkable: even in chains, the gospel advances.

> Now I want you to know, brothers and sisters, that what has happened to me has actually served to advance the gospel. (Philippians 1:12)

Paul's imprisonment in Rome might seem like a defeat, but he insists it's actually progress. To understand this paradox, consider Napoleon's invasion of Russia in 1812. The French general assembled the largest army in European history and won every battle against the disorganized, retreating Russians. Yet Napoleon ultimately lost the war. As the French advanced further from their supply lines, the Russian winter devastated them. The French army that had conquered most of Europe returned home decimated. Years later, Tchaikovsky's 1812 Overture celebrated Russian victory—a triumph born from apparent defeat.

Paul is like the Russians. In chains, stripped of freedom, he appears to be losing. Yet he declares that his suffering results in advance.

## The Gospel Advances in Prison (1:12-13)

Paul's chains have become known throughout the palace guard in Rome. Every official, every servant, every worker who encounters him learns that he suffers for Christ's sake. In that dark, cold prison—hungry, unhygienic, his eyes failing, his body aching—Christ is being preached to guards, servants, administrators, and cleaners. They have all received a revelation of Jesus and his sacrifice.

Nelson Mandela spent 27 years imprisoned but emerged as a rallying figure against apartheid. His jailer, Christo Brand, was so transformed by his contact with Mandela that he smuggled in special treats and even allowed him to hold his infant granddaughter. Another guard, tough Afrikaner cop Rory Steyn, later became Mandela's security chief. Imprisonment did not silence the message—it amplified it.

### Application: Gospel Conversations Everywhere

We may not face Roman chains, yet the principle remains: gospel opportunities exist in the most unlikely places. If Paul could advance the gospel in imprisonment, surely we can in our current liberty.

Consider the people around you: neighbors, shopkeepers, teachers, janitors, administrative staff, bus drivers, canteen workers, nurses, doctors, cleaners. Build regular relationships by visiting the same place at the same time. When you fall ill, those who care for you become your congregation.

Setbacks do not mean lack of progress. The gospel keeps advancing.

## The Gospel Advances in Rome (1:14-18)

Paul's imprisonment has emboldened the believers in Rome. Most of the brothers and sisters have become confident in the Lord and now speak the word without fear. Yet two groups preach Christ in the city.

> It is true that some preach Christ out of envy and rivalry, but others out of goodwill. The latter do so out of love, knowing that I am put here for the defense of the gospel. The former preach Christ out of selfish ambition, not sincerely, supposing that they can stir up trouble for me while I am in chains. But what does it matter? The important thing is that in every way, whether from false motives or true, Christ is preached. And because of this I rejoice. (Philippians 1:15-18)

One group preaches with pure motives, joining Paul in defending the gospel. The other group preaches from envy and rivalry, wanting to stir up trouble for Paul personally while he languishes in chains. Perhaps Paul is the "new kid on the block" in Rome—he didn't found the church there, yet his arrival has excited the believers and stirred jealousy among some rivals. Church history records that this rivalry was so intense that some early Christians believed it contributed to Paul's eventual execution.

Yet Paul dismisses the personal attacks. His self-assured answer is magnificent: Great! More competition! Who cares about their motives toward me? As long as Christ is preached, I rejoice.

This is not indifference to false teaching. Paul elsewhere makes clear that the manner and purity of our proclamation matter deeply. We must never bully people into faith, coerce them, or trick them with false hope. The gospel proclaimed through terrorism and deception is poisoned at its source.

But here, Paul's rivals are not corrupting the message itself—their rivalry is directed at him personally. They want to embarrass Paul, not deceive the hearers. Paul's wisdom is simple: I will not be distracted by personal attacks. If Christ is proclaimed, my joy is complete.

This reveals Paul's formula for joy despite setbacks: he transforms losses into advancement. Physical limitation becomes spiritual gain. Moral pressure becomes opportunity for rejoicing. It is like the martial art of Wing Chun, which teaches you to use your opponent's strength against him. The stronger the enemy strikes, the more you win.

### Paul's Confidence in Vindication (1:19-21)

What gives Paul this perseverance? He thinks long-term.

> I know that through your prayers and God's provision of the Spirit of Jesus Christ what has happened to me will turn out for my deliverance. I eagerly expect and hope that I will in no way be ashamed, but will have sufficient courage so that now as always Christ will be exalted in my body, whether by life or by death. For to me, to live is Christ and to die is gain. (Philippians 1:19-21)

Paul speaks four confident futures: he will be delivered, he will not be ashamed, Christ will be exalted in his body, and he will rejoice. This confidence echoes Job, who lost everything—family, health, wealth—yet refused to curse God. Job trusted that he would be vindicated, and he was.

Paul believes the same: though imprisoned, he will stand firm for the gospel. He will not beg the Roman guards for his release. He will not compromise. God will ensure that everything turns out right—in this life or the next.

This future hope transforms his present attitude: whether he lives or dies makes little difference. To live is to fulfill his purpose in Christ. To die is to depart and be with Christ—which is even better. Yet if he lives, it means fruitful labor remains.

### A Challenging Motto (1:21-22)

"To me, to live is Christ and to die is gain"—what a motto to guide your life. Yet we often reverse it. We think: "To die is Christ, to live is gain." We reason that since heaven awaits us, let's get the most out of this life—wealth, security, influence, fame. We've secured our insurance policy for the afterlife, so now let's invest in this one. This attitude is the opposite of Jesus, who came not to gain but to give.

## The Gospel Advances in Philippi (1:24-30)

Paul faces a genuine tension: he longs to depart and be with Christ, which is far better. Yet he chooses to remain.

> It is more necessary for you that I remain in the body. Convinced of this, I know that I will remain, and I will continue with all of you for your progress and joy in the faith, so that through my being with you again your boasting in Christ Jesus will abound on account of me. (Philippians 1:24-26)

His criteria for discernment is simple: What is beneficial for others? The Philippians need him. His presence will advance their faith and multiply their joy. His return will give them occasion to boast in Christ. Therefore, he will stay.

This principle applies to all of life: our decisions should consider the welfare of others, especially their eternal salvation. We ought to care deeply about people's spiritual futures.

### A Word on Suicide

If you struggle with suicidal thoughts, or if a friend confides such feelings to you, understand that verses 24-25 reveal a profound truth: life is better for others than death. When suicidal despair strikes, the mind lies. It whispers: "I am such a burden. Everyone would be better off if I were gone. I would be doing them a favor."

This is false. Statistics show that the greatest predictor of suicide is knowing someone who has taken their own life. The grief and devastation ripple outward. Whitney Houston's death was followed by her daughter's. Ernest Hemingway's father died by suicide, then Ernest himself, then his sister, his brother, and his granddaughter Margaux. Every suicide becomes a delayed homicide—a vicious cycle of pain.

You do everyone a favor if you live. Your perseverance despite suffering gives others strength. When the Philippians see Paul's faithfulness in chains, they are motivated to endure their own struggles. Your faithfulness will strengthen those who love you.

## Living Worthy of the Gospel (1:27-30)

> Whatever happens, conduct yourselves in a manner worthy of the gospel of Christ. Then, whether I come and see you or only hear about you in my absence, I will know that you stand firm in the one Spirit, striving together as one for the faith of the gospel without being frightened in any way by those who oppose you. This is a sign to them that they will be destroyed, but that you will be saved—and that by God. (Philippians 1:27-28)

To live worthy of the gospel means to stand together, not giving ground to opponents. It means proclaiming Christ without fear—even when threatened by the powerful. Why? Because suffering for Christ is a privilege, not a punishment.

> For it has been granted to you on behalf of Christ not only to believe in him, but also to suffer for him, since you are going through the same struggle you saw I had, and now hear that I still have. (Philippians 1:29-30)

The Christian life involves sacrifice and suffering. This is not masochism or needless risk-taking. Rather, it is the inevitable cost of doing the right thing consistently, of speaking the gospel faithfully, and of refusing to bow to pressure. When harm comes because you stood firm, you have been granted a remarkable privilege: to suffer for Christ's name.

## The Victory Beyond the Battle

We can lose individual battles yet win the war. When Napoleon defeated the Russians in every encounter, everyone assumed French victory. But the Russians understood something deeper: they could lose battles yet ultimately prevail by using the land itself as a weapon. They retreated, burned their own fields so the French had nothing to eat, and waited for winter. The massive French army that had conquered Europe starved and froze in the Russian winter. The greatest military force ever assembled returned home destroyed. The Russians, who lost every battle, won the war.

So it is with the gospel. Sometimes it looks like we're losing. Someone rejects Christ. We're bullied and called names. Our freedoms to preach the gospel seem to shrink. We face suffering and setbacks.

But every loss is ultimately a victory if we stay firm to the gospel. We might be losing battles today, yet we will win the war when Jesus returns with his army.

## Prayer

Heavenly Father, the work of the gospel grows more difficult each day. Help us not to lose sight of the bigger picture—our deliverance when your Son returns. Though we are losers now, we look forward to ultimate victory. Keep us faithful with our unbelieving friends and family, proclaiming Christ even when it costs us. In Jesus' name we pray, Amen.
