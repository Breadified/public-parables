# 1 Corinthians 2:1-17

**Title:** Sins Forgiven  
**Date:** 25 April 2021  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Authority to Teach, Authority to Forgive

> A few days later, when Jesus again entered Capernaum, the people heard that he had come home. They gathered in such large numbers that there was no room left, not even outside the door, and he preached the word to them. (Mark 2:1-2)

Jesus returns to Capernaum and immediately begins teaching—his priority is clear. He came to proclaim the message "repent and believe." The house is packed beyond capacity, which reveals just how hungry people were for his teaching.

### The Paralyzed Man Brought Through the Roof

> Some men came, bringing to him a paralyzed man, carried by four of them. Since they could not get him to Jesus because of the crowd, they made an opening in the roof above Jesus by digging through it and then lowered the mat the man was lying on. (Mark 2:3-4)

The four men demonstrate extraordinary persistence and faith. Houses in first-century Israel were simple structures with flat roofs easily accessible from outside. The fact that this man was described as a "child" or "son" suggests he was young, perhaps injured in an accident. His companions act as makeshift paramedics, treating Jesus as the only place where healing can be found.

Picture the scene: Jesus is speaking, making important theological points. Suddenly, there's noise overhead—shouting, footsteps, banging. Dust begins falling. As the crowd moves out of the way, a massive mat descends slowly through a gaping hole. Everyone falls silent, watching in anticipation. What will Jesus do? Will he heal? Will he perform magic? The tension builds.

### The Surprising Response

> When Jesus saw their faith, he said to the paralyzed man, "Son, your sins are forgiven." (Mark 2:5)

This response seems almost anticlimactic. After such dramatic effort, Jesus doesn't immediately heal. Instead, he offers forgiveness—something invisible, intangible. But this is precisely the point: Jesus recognizes the deeper issue beneath the physical need.

#### The Teachers of the Law Object

> Now some teachers of the law were sitting there, thinking to themselves, "Why does this fellow talk like that? He's blaspheming! Who can forgive sins but God alone?" (Mark 2:6-7)

Their objection is theologically precise. In Jewish understanding, only God can forgive sins. By claiming to forgive, Jesus implicitly claims to be God. The punishment for blasphemy under Levitical law was death by stoning (Leviticus 24:16).

The scribes fail to grasp what's happening. When we sin against another person, we sin ultimately against God, who created both us and that person. All sin is ultimately sin against God, because God has authority over creation. Therefore, only God can truly forgive sin.

#### Which Is Easier?

> Jesus said to them, "Why are you thinking these things? Which is easier: to say to this paralyzed man, 'Your sins are forgiven,' or to say, 'Get up, take your mat and walk'?" (Mark 2:8-9)

Jesus presents a brilliant riddle. From our human perspective, healing seems harder—we can see it, measure it, verify it. Forgiveness is invisible and unverifiable; anyone can claim to forgive without evidence.

But theologically, from God's standpoint, forgiveness is infinitely harder. We forgive relatively easily because we forget, time heals wounds, and we ourselves fail and don't hold grudges. But how can God—immortal and never forgetting, perfect and never failing—forgive? How can the holy God overlook sin?

**For God to forgive, God the Son had to die on the cross.** That is the terrible price of forgiveness. The cross reveals how hard forgiveness truly is. Yet Jesus says, "Your sins are forgiven. By his stripes, we are healed."

We forgive one another not because the other person deserves it, but because Christ forgave us and we pass that gift forward.

#### The Healing as Evidence

> So he said to the man, "I tell you, get up, take your mat and go home." He got up, took his mat and walked out in full view of them all. This amazed everyone and they praised God, saying, "We have never seen anything like this!" (Mark 2:10-12)

Now that Jesus has accomplished the harder thing—securing forgiveness—he does the easy thing. The healing becomes visible proof that forgiveness has occurred. Sin and health are linked in Scripture:

> Praise the Lord, my soul; all my inmost being, praise his holy name. Praise the Lord, my soul, and forget not all his benefits—who forgives all your sins and heals all your diseases. (Psalm 103:1-3)

Our ultimate problem with God isn't physical; it's moral. Physical pain came as a consequence of the first moral failure in Eden. Death entered the world through sin. When God forgives the moral issue, everything else follows. This is why Jesus' priority is repentance and belief—fix the relationship with God, and the symptoms resolve.

**Why are we still sick?** There's a time gap between Jesus' pronouncement of forgiveness and the physical healing. Similarly, we live in that same gap. Full healing remains delayed, just as judgment remains delayed. Everything has been delayed. But understand: the biggest problem we face is not health or sickness. **The biggest problem is our relationship with God.**

## Why Does He Eat With Tax Collectors and Sinners?

> Once again Jesus went out beside the lake. A large crowd came to him, and he began to teach them. As he walked along, he saw Levi son of Alphaeus sitting at the tax collector's booth. "Follow me," Jesus told him, and Levi got up and followed him. (Mark 2:13-14)

Jesus calls Levi with the same simple command he uses with his other disciples: "Follow me." The call is immediate and without hesitation—Levi gets up and follows.

### The Scandalous Dinner

> While Jesus was having dinner at Levi's house, many tax collectors and sinners were eating with him and his disciples, for there were many who followed him. When the teachers of the law who were Pharisees saw him eating with the sinners and tax collectors, they asked his disciples: "Why does he eat with tax collectors and sinners?" (Mark 2:15-16)

Tax collectors were Jews who worked for the Roman occupiers, extracting taxes and often enriching themselves through extortion. They were viewed as traitors and profiteers—the financial criminals and corrupt elites of their day. "Sinners" encompassed prostitutes, robbers, gamblers, and murderers—society's outcasts, treated as lepers.

That Jesus would call such a man to follow him was shocking. That he would sit at table with them was scandalous. **Eating together is an act of fellowship, belonging, and family.** To eat with someone meant inclusion and acceptance. The Pharisees couldn't fathom why the supposed Messiah would associate with such people.

### Jesus' Response

> On hearing this, Jesus said to them, "It is not the healthy who need a doctor, but the sick. I have not come to call the righteous, but sinners." (Mark 2:17)

Jesus' answer is devastating. He again knows what they're thinking and misunderstanding. They believe they grasp his purpose, but they've missed it entirely.

A doctor who avoids the sick, who maintains distance from the diseased, is useless. A doctor's job is precisely to be with those who need healing. Jesus came to seek and save the lost. How then could he stay away from the very people he came to rescue?

The Pharisees believed themselves righteous. They established their righteousness through comparison with others, finding those worse than themselves and concluding they measured well by contrast. But the standard isn't other people—it's God. It's Jesus. When you compare yourself to Jesus, you see how you truly measure up.

> To some who were confident of their own righteousness and looked down on everyone else, Jesus told this parable: "Two men went up to the temple to pray, one a Pharisee and the other a tax collector. The Pharisee stood by himself and prayed: 'God, I thank you that I am not like other people—robbers, evildoers, adulterers—or even like this tax collector. I fast twice a week and give a tenth of all I get.' But the tax collector stood at a distance. He would not even look up to heaven, but beat his breast and said, 'God, have mercy on me, a sinner.' I tell you that this man, rather than the other, went home justified before God. For all those who exalt themselves will be humbled, and those who humble themselves will be exalted." (Luke 18:9-14)

The Pharisees failed a basic theological truth: **everyone, including them, is a sinner.** 

> As it is written: "There is no one righteous, not even one... all have sinned and fall short of the glory of God." (Romans 3:10-23)

When Jesus told the young paralyzed man "your sins are forgiven," he was declaring that even this innocent-looking youth is a sinner. We all are.

### The Greatest Problem We Face

What is the biggest problem in our world? We tend to focus on immediate crises—health, poverty, injustice. During COVID, we saw the spike in India, border closures, hospitals overwhelmed. Those are real crises. Yet sin remains the fundamental problem.

Imagine someone dying of COVID in India. Our first instinct rushes to practical needs: oxygen, hospital care, medical intervention. These matter. But beneath every person's physical need lies a deeper spiritual need. **The biggest problem facing humanity is sin—our separation from God.**

Many years ago, someone came into a summer mission gathering in crisis. What was their immediate need? Legal help, financial assistance from Centrelink, job placement, food bank access—all legitimate practical concerns. But beneath those symptoms was a spiritual need: forgiveness.

This isn't to say we shouldn't help with physical, social, and material needs. James reminds us:

> Suppose a brother or sister is without clothes and daily food. If one of you tells them, "Go in peace; stay warm and well fed," but does not provide for their physical needs, what good is that? (James 2:15-16)

We absolutely must meet genuine physical needs. But we must never lose sight of the primary objective—that people need forgiveness and reconciliation with God through Christ.

### A Call to Action

We live in extraordinary freedom and health compared to much of the world. We're not facing plague or oppression. Our complaints are minor—a day off work we didn't get, minor inconveniences. Yet so many around us face the greatest crisis of all: separation from God.

How much more should we be engaged in proclaiming the gospel—calling others to repent and believe in Jesus? We've been blessed with stability and health. Let's use that freedom and peace to bring friends to church, to invite others to know the One who forgives sins and offers true healing.
