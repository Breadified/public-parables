# 1 Thessalonians 5:12-28

**Title:** A Ready Church  
**Date:** 21 September 2014 (SMAC); 12 June 2023 (ARISE)  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

It's been a challenging few weeks for many of us. We've organized a significant baptism celebration, grieved the loss of Chris Chan, and navigated widespread illness in our community. Yet last week, despite low attendance, you showed up to fellowship and lunch—and we sold exactly the right amount. This is a beautiful picture of faith and community. Thank you for continuing to participate in these shared moments.

As we finish 1 Thessalonians, I want to make sense of these final verses, which Paul delivers in rapid-fire fashion—almost like haiku. The manuscript is running out of space, the thoughts become compressed and cryptic. But there's profound wisdom here about a church that's spiritually ready.

I'm organizing these closing thoughts around verse 23: *"May your whole spirit, soul and body be kept blameless at the coming of our Lord Jesus Christ."* This gives us three dimensions of readiness: Body, Soul, and Spirit.

## Body: Acknowledging Leadership (vv. 12-13)

Paul begins by directing our attention to those who lead:

> Now we ask you, brothers and sisters, to acknowledge those who work hard among you, who care for you in the Lord and who admonish you. Hold them in the highest regard in love because of their work.

The word "acknowledge" (eidenai) means to know and remember. Paul is describing leaders as those who **toil** (kopiontas)—they labor with their bodies. They care for the church (proistamenous—those who stand over you in the Lord), and they admonish with wisdom.

Church leadership isn't about status or the power to boss people around. It's fundamentally about function and responsibility. Just as a police officer's power to stop your car isn't about being "better" than you, but about the burden of keeping streets safe, so Christian leaders are known for their work, not their titles.

This is true of all our church roles:
- **Elders**: the older (in wisdom and experience)
- **Bishops**: those with oversight
- **Deacons**: the serving ones

An egalitarian community isn't one without hierarchy—it's one where hierarchy exists based on function and burden, not status.

### Application: Honor Your Leaders

Acknowledge those who serve, especially those who teach God's Word. As Hebrews 7 reminds us: *"Remember your leaders who spoke the word of God to you. Consider the outcome of their life and imitate their faith."*

Here's the remarkable part: we're told to hold leaders in the *highest* regard **in love** (agape). We're not commanded to love our political leaders or our bosses in that way. But in the church, those who volunteer their time are to be held highest in love. Paul models this himself—he loves the Thessalonians as a father and mother.

One simple way to acknowledge your leaders is to show up and participate. When your mother has slaved for hours to cook dinner, the most loving acknowledgment is to appear, eat, thank her, and help clean up. If you're someone who struggles with regular attendance, remember that your leaders are here consistently, organizing for everyone—including those who don't show up. When we have to send apologies on their behalf, it burdens them further.

The whole teams that led us today—music, setup, children's ministry, hospitality—made church possible. Let's acknowledge them with genuine love and gratitude.

## Soul: Living in Peace Together (vv. 13b-15, 23-24)

If leadership is functional, then we all take turns in some form of leadership role. Therefore, we must treat each other with care:

> Live in peace with each other. And we urge you, brothers and sisters, warn those who are idle and disruptive, encourage the disheartened, help the weak, be patient with everyone.

Paul gives us four pastoral instructions:

**Warn the disruptive.** The word means "unruly"—like a soldier who won't keep rank, who's rebellious. Some are idle (they won't fight for the faith); others are actively disruptive. Both need correction.

**Encourage the disheartened.** The phrase "little soul" (oligopsychos) describes those who are fainthearted, scared, timid. Alongside discipline, the church offers pastoral encouragement to those whose courage has flagged.

**Help the weak.** "Hold fast" to those who are dropping away, whether weak in health or weak in faith. As Romans 15:1 says: *"We who are strong ought to bear with the failings of the weak and not to please ourselves."*

**Be patient with all.** This requires genuine patience.

> Make sure that nobody pays back wrong for wrong, but always strive to do what is good for each other and for everyone else.

Don't keep score. In a church—unlike a company—we're a family of volunteers who step on each other's toes regularly. We all need help at various times. Those who take initiative in helping are exercising leadership in the truest sense.

### Application: Care for One Another

In cold weather, check on those without adequate heating. When people are sick, ask what we can do. The church's soul—its relational health—depends on us seeing each other's needs and responding with patience and love.

## Spirit: Rejoicing in God's Will (vv. 16-22)

Paul concludes with rapid-fire spiritual imperatives:

> Rejoice always, pray continually, give thanks in all circumstances; for this is God's will for you in Christ Jesus.

These echo themes he's woven throughout: he rejoiced over the Thessalonians in 3:9; he prayed unceasingly for them in 1:2; he gave thanks unceasingly in 2:13. Joy, intercession, and gratitude aren't merely feelings—they're the atmosphere of the Spirit-filled life.

Then Paul addresses the Spirit's work:

> Do not quench the spirit. Do not treat prophecies with contempt. But test them all; hold on to what is good, reject every kind of evil.

**Understanding "Quench the Spirit"**

The word "quench" typically refers to extinguishing fire. But what does it mean spiritually? The context is God's will. In 4:8, Paul writes: *"Anyone who rejects this instruction does not reject a human being but God, the very God who gives you his Holy Spirit."* The Spirit isn't quenched by theological disagreement—it's quenched by rejecting God's revealed will.

**Prophecy as Speaking God's Word**

The common interpretation treats prophecy as extra-biblical messages requiring careful testing. But I'd offer another lens: What if prophecy here means speaking God's Word to one another—forth-telling, proclaiming His truth?

Paul himself does this work. In 2:13, he says: *"When you received the word of God, which you heard from us, you accepted it not as a human word, but as it actually is, the word of God, which is indeed at work in you who believe."* The apostle's words carry prophetic authority because they bear God's Word.

In 2 Thessalonians 2:2, Paul mentions "prophecy or word of mouth or letter"—all three as equivalent ways God's Word comes to people.

In 1 Corinthians 14, prophecy is a speaking gift: *"The one who prophesies speaks to people for their strengthening, encouraging and comfort... the one who prophesies edifies the church."* It's about forth-telling God's truth in ways that strengthen the body.

So today, when we speak God's Word to each other—through teaching, counsel, encouragement rooted in Scripture—we're prophesying. Sometimes this includes foretelling as experience, wisdom, and God's Word come together. We won't be perfect; our words can be wrong. But that's okay.

**Test and Keep What's Good**

> Test them all; hold on to what is good, reject every kind of evil.

Test the words and even the person speaking them. God Himself tests our hearts (2:4). We're called to discernment—to weigh what's said against Scripture and the character of the speaker.

## Conclusion: A Church Ready for Christ's Return

Paul closes with an encompassing prayer:

> May your whole spirit, soul and body be kept blameless at the coming of our Lord Jesus Christ. The one who calls you is faithful, and he will do it.

Our readiness—in body (service and leadership), soul (relationships and peace), and spirit (joy and discernment)—isn't something we achieve alone. *The one who calls us is faithful, and he will do it.* Our part is to acknowledge our leaders, care for one another, and remain open to God's Spirit speaking through His Word.

Paul ends by asking the Thessalonians to pray for him and to greet one another with a holy kiss. Then he charges them: *"I charge you before the Lord to have this letter read to all the brothers and sisters."* He's asking them to receive his leadership, even from a distance. Though Paul isn't with them, through their willingness to step into leadership roles and speak God's Word to one another, they become instruments through which His Word bears fruit in each other's lives.

The same is true for us. Christ is not physically present, yet through each of us—as we're willing to serve, encourage, and speak His truth—He ministers to His church. That's what it means to be a ready church.
