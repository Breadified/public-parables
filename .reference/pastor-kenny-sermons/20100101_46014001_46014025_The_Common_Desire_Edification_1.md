# 1 Corinthians 14:1-25

**Title:** The Common Desire: Edification
**Date:** 31 January 2010
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Why Do We Gather?

When asked why people come to Christian gatherings, we hear various answers: reunion, boredom, needing a place to hang out, seeing friends, meeting people for university, or simply responding to a Facebook invitation. But the Bible gives us a clear answer to why Christians gather together.

This passage sits at the culmination of chapters 12-14, where Paul has been building toward this central point: the purpose of Christian assembly is mutual edification through the gospel.

## 1. Desiring Prophecy

> Follow the way of love and eagerly desire spirituals in order that you may prophesy. (1 Corinthians 14:1)

### What Is Prophecy?

The word "prophecy" appears about 25 times in the New Testament, but nearly half those references occur in 1 Corinthians 11-14—the very passage where we're trying to understand its meaning. This creates a puzzle. However, we can work backward from the text to understand what prophecy leads to and thereby discover what it is.

**Two Historical Forms:**

Old Testament prophets operated in two ways:

- **Foretelling**: Prophets like Jeremiah and Isaiah predicted Israel's future exile and judgment. The test of a true prophet was whether these predictions came true.

- **Forthtelling**: Moses, the greatest prophet of the Old Testament, primarily proclaimed what God had already said. Deuteronomy consists of three speeches reminding Israel of God's faithfulness—not predicting the future but calling them to remember.

### The Mark of Prophecy: Edification

> Everyone who prophesies speaks to men for their strengthening, encouragement and comfort. (1 Corinthians 14:3)

> He who prophesies edifies the church. (1 Corinthians 14:4)

> Since you are eager to have spirituals, try to excel in things that build up the church. (1 Corinthians 14:12)

The word "edification" means building—a powerful biblical metaphor for what God does with His people. Jesus said, "I will build my church" (Matthew 16:18). Paul describes the church as God's household "built on the foundation of the apostles and prophets...being built together to become a dwelling in which God lives by his Spirit" (Ephesians 2:19-20).

Remarkably, throughout the New Testament, this building happens through speaking:

- **Ephesians 4:15-16**: "Speaking the truth in love, we will in all things grow up into him...the whole body...grows and builds itself up in love, as each part does its work."

- **Romans 15:20**: Paul's ambition was "to preach the gospel...so that I would not be building on someone else's foundation"—showing that gospel proclamation is how we build.

- **Acts 20:32**: "The word of his grace...can build you up and give you an inheritance among all those who are sanctified."

- **Jeremiah 1:9-10**: God told Jeremiah, "I have put my words in your mouth. See, today I appoint you over nations and kingdoms to uproot and tear down...to build and to plant."

The biblical pattern shows: Spirit → Gospel/Word of Grace/Truth in Love → Edification

**Therefore, prophecy is speaking the gospel intelligibly so others can understand and be built up.**

### What Does This Look Like Today?

When we gather, prophecy takes many forms:

- **Preaching**: Teaching God's Word to remind people of what He has done and promised.

- **Bible study**: Reading God's Word together in fellowship groups builds each other up.

- **Encouragement**: When you tell a struggling friend to keep persevering in Jesus, you're building her up.

- **Loving rebuke**: When you speak truth in love to correct a friend, you're building him up.

- **Prayer and comfort**: When you say, "Let's bring this to God and ask for His help," you're building the other person up.

**The crucial point**: Prophecy is you speaking the words of God to *each other*. You are important to one another because you are the means by which God builds each person up. This is what we should desire!

When you talk together, move beyond small talk about movies, parties, and friends. Ask: "What did you learn today? How have you been changed? How are you going as a Christian? What have you been struggling with before God lately?" 

This doesn't come naturally to everyone, but it's essential. If you join a chess club but never play chess, that's odd. If you go to the gym but never lift weights, that's strange. If you come to a Christian group and never discuss Christian things, that's disappointing.

I'm built up when you share what you've learned, ask genuine questions, challenge me toward godliness, or work through disagreements together, seeking God's truth in love. That makes us all better Christians.

## 2. The Relative Uselessness of Tongues

### What Are Tongues?

> Anyone who speaks in a tongue does not speak to men but to God. Indeed, no one understands him; he utters mysteries with his spirit. (1 Corinthians 14:2)

Some understand "tongues" as foreign languages. At Pentecost, the disciples "began to speak in other tongues as the Spirit enabled them" (Acts 2:4)—and remarkably, foreigners from many nations each heard the good news in their own language (Acts 2:5-11). This was an incredible sign that the gospel transcends all barriers.

However, in Corinth, a cosmopolitan trading city with many languages, tongues became problematic.

### Tongues Do Not Build Up Others

> I would like every one of you to speak in tongues, but I would rather have you prophesy. He who prophesies is greater than one who speaks in tongues, unless he interprets, so that the church may be edified. (1 Corinthians 14:5)

The problem is simple: **unintelligibility**.

> He who speaks in a tongue edifies himself, but he who prophesies edifies the church. (1 Corinthians 14:4)

When no one understands what you're saying, you benefit privately while others gain nothing. Consider Paul's illustration:

> If I come to you and speak in tongues, what good will I be to you, unless I bring you some revelation or knowledge or prophecy or word of instruction? Even lifeless things like a flute or harp—how will anyone know what tune is being played unless there is a distinction in the notes? If the trumpet does not sound a clear call, who will get ready for battle? Unless you speak intelligible words with your tongue, how will anyone know what you are saying? You will just be speaking into the air. (1 Corinthians 14:6-9)

Paul himself speaks in tongues more than anyone, yet he concludes:

> In the church I would rather speak five intelligible words to instruct others than ten thousand words in a tongue. (1 Corinthians 14:18-19)

Five cents for one hundred dollars—practically worthless.

### Don't Do Your Own Thing When Gathered

This principle extends beyond formal tongue-speaking to how we gather:

- If you eat alone while everyone gathers, you serve no one.
- If you pray in a language others don't understand, it bears no fruit.
- If you have conversations in a language others don't know, community dissolves.
- If you talk on your phone while others are present, you don't build anyone up.

Even in worship, when the music moves you, closing your eyes and retreating inward can look like disengagement from the group. **When you're with others, remember that other people matter most.**

The same applies to how we discover our "spiritual gifts." Too often we ask, "What am I good at?" and take questionnaires to identify our strengths. That's doing your own thing. Instead, we should be **outward-focused**, asking, "What needs to be done?" This yields better answers for building up the group—whether that means welcoming strangers, stacking chairs, taking out trash, or wiping tables. These "insignificant" tasks may not appear on a spiritual gifts sheet, but they build the church.

**The core principle**: Don't do your own thing when you're not on your own.

### Tongues Reverses Pentecost

There's a deeper theological issue. Paul quotes Isaiah 28:11:

> Through men of strange tongues and through the lips of foreigners I will speak to this people, but even then they will not listen to me. (1 Corinthians 14:21)

In Isaiah's context, foreign tongues were a sign of **judgment**. When a foreign army invaded with a strange language, it meant God's people were under His judgment—just as at Babel, where language division was a curse.

> Tongues, then, are a sign, not for believers but for unbelievers; prophecy, however, is for believers, not for unbelievers. (1 Corinthians 14:22)

If the whole church speaks in foreign languages and unbelievers walk in, they'll say you're out of your minds and leave under the impression of judgment.

But prophecy (intelligible gospel proclamation) produces the opposite effect:

> If an unbeliever or someone who does not understand comes in while everybody is prophesying, he will be convinced by all that he is a sinner and will be judged by all, and the secrets of his heart will be laid bare. So he will fall down and worship God, exclaiming, "God is really among you!" (1 Corinthians 14:24-25)

**This is true Pentecost**: when people hear the gospel intelligibly, they repent and recognize God's presence.

At Pentecost, the Spirit made the gospel accessible to everyone. If we retreat into unintelligible speech, we reverse that gift—making the gospel exclusive again, confined to an elite few. We must speak the gospel in a way everyone can understand.

## 3. The Importance of Intelligibility

Speaking the gospel intelligently matters:

- **Avoid jargon** that outsiders won't grasp
- **Be careful with in-jokes**—they're good for community, but explain them so newcomers aren't left behind
- **Make your gatherings inclusive**, not exclusive to those already in the know
- **When inviting non-Christian friends, make extra effort** to ensure everything is understandable

The speaking of the gospel is the most important thing we can do. Everyone in the room is important. We build one another by speaking God's words to each other—in pairs, in groups, in conversation.

We are trying to edify the church through the intelligent proclamation of the gospel to each other.
