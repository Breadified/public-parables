# Amos 9:1-15

**Title:** A New Hope
**Date:** 27 December 2015
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## A Terrible Year

This has been a difficult year—not just personally, but for Christians and our country alike. Many of us have faced genuine hardship: sickness, loss of loved ones, unemployment, failure, and the especially painful experience of relationship breakdown. Yet in the midst of this despair, the prophet Amos speaks a word that carries both judgment and hope.

Amos was a prophet from Judah called to proclaim God's message to the northern kingdom of Israel. His words aren't meant to comfort but to confront—he announces not just a difficult year, but years of terrible shaking for God's people.

## The Thresholds Shake (9:1-10)

### The Image of Judgment

> I saw the Lord standing by the altar, and he said: "Strike the tops of the pillars so that the thresholds shake. Bring them down on the heads of all the people; those who are left I will kill with the sword. Not one will get away, none will escape."

This vision uses the language of shaking—which Amos connects to an actual earthquake predicted two years before his prophecy (see Amos 1:1). When you shake something, it moves violently up and down, like a snow globe. The Israelites' lives would be turned completely upside down.

### No Escape—Complete and Utter

The text emphasizes that escape is impossible. No matter where they hide:

> Though they dig down to the depths below, from there my hand will take them. Though they climb up to the heavens above, from there I will bring them down. Though they hide themselves on the top of Carmel, there I will hunt them down and seize them. Though they hide from my eyes at the bottom of the sea, there I will command the serpent to bite them.

This uses the literary technique of *merism*—two extremes (depths and heavens, mountain and sea) to convey "everywhere." God's reach extends to all places. Even exile won't save them:

> Though they are driven into exile by their enemies, there I will command the sword to slay them.

### The Power of God's Sovereignty

The text reveals the cosmic power behind this judgment:

> The Lord, the Lord Almighty, he touches the earth and it melts, and all who live in it mourn; the whole land rises like the Nile, then sinks like the river of Egypt; he builds his lofty palace in the heavens and sets its foundation on the earth; he calls for the waters of the sea and pours them out over the face of the land—the Lord is his name.

God controls everything: the earth, the heavens, water, life itself. The Israelites cannot escape His judgment because judgment comes from the One who sustains all things.

### Eyes Upon Them—But for What Purpose?

There's a profound reversal here. Earlier, in Deuteronomy, God promised the Israelites:

> A land which the Lord your God cares for: the eyes of the Lord your God are always upon it, from the beginning of the year even unto the end of the year.

God's watching eyes were meant to be *security*—a comfort to a fearful people entering a land of giants and danger. But now, in Amos's time, those same watching eyes are turned upon them *for judgment*:

> Surely the eyes of the Sovereign Lord are on the sinful kingdom. I will destroy it from the face of the earth.

You cannot claim God's protection while avoiding His scrutiny. His omniscience cuts both ways: He sees your need, but He also sees your sin. There is no escape from His justice.

## The Threshold Rebuilt (9:11-15)

### A Twist of Hope

Just when despair seems complete, Amos offers an unexpected word of restoration. Some scholars suggest this ending was added later because the rest of the prophecy seemed too bleak—but there's no textual evidence for this. The hope is authentically part of Amos's message.

> In that day I will restore David's fallen shelter. I will repair its broken walls and restore its ruins and will rebuild it as it used to be, so that they may possess the remnant of Edom and all the nations that bear my name, declares the Lord, who will do these things.

The word "David's fallen shelter" (or "booth") echoes the Festival of Sukkot—a temporary shelter built from branches. It also evokes Jacob's Sukkoth, where he built shelters for his livestock. Historically, this shelter represents the extent of Israel's greatest territorial power under David.

### Abundance Beyond Measure

The restoration will bring miraculous abundance:

> The days are coming, declares the Lord, when the reaper will be overtaken by the plowman and the planter by the one treading grapes. New wine will drip from the mountains and flow from all the hills.

In Leviticus, Moses promised continuous fertility with no breaks in the cycle—from planting to harvest to planting again. But Amos transforms this: the cycles overlap. Treading overlaps with planting. Plowing overlaps with reaping. Imagine warehouses so efficient that stock arrives faster than it can be shelved. Imagine shelves emptying as customers buy. So abundant that the supply spills over the mountains like a dam bursting.

### Permanent Restoration

> I will bring my people Israel back from exile. They will rebuild the ruined cities and live in them. They will plant vineyards and drink their wine; they will make gardens and eat their fruit. I will plant Israel in their own land, never again to be uprooted from the land I have given them, says the Lord your God.

The promise is both restoration and permanence. Unlike their previous occupation of the land—where they inherited cities they didn't build and vineyards they didn't plant—this time they will possess it forever.

## How Is Restoration Possible?

How can a people who forfeited their land through sin be restored to it permanently? The answer lies in the New Testament. In Acts 15, James quotes this passage from Amos:

> After this I will return and rebuild David's fallen tent. Its ruins I will rebuild, and I will restore it, that the rest of mankind may seek the Lord, even all the Gentiles who bear my name, says the Lord, who does these things.

This rebuilding happens *through the grace of Jesus Christ*. Jesus, the Son of David, is the One who rebuilds the tent. Through Him, we who deserve judgment can be saved from it.

We have all done injustice—to others, to God. We have withheld the honor and worship He deserves. Yet through Jesus, we receive not just temporary forgiveness, but permanent forgiveness. The promise is heaven—a place of such abundance that we will never be uprooted again.

## A New Hope for a New Year

Perhaps this year has brought changes you didn't want—sickness, loss, unemployment, failure, relationship breakdown. But here is the promise: you stand in God's forgiveness. You have another year of life to share the good news of Jesus with others.

When the clock strikes midnight on New Year's Eve, it becomes 2016 Anno Domini—the 2016th year of the Lord. It is another chance to start fresh, another year to live in the grace of Christ. No matter how difficult this year has been, His mercies are new every morning.

This is the hope we enter the new year with: we will never again be uprooted from the love of God.
