## I. Commanded by God to Teach the Truth (1:1-4, 18-20)

### By the Command of God

> Paul, an apostle of Christ Jesus by the command of God our Savior and of Christ Jesus our hope

Paul has been given a divine command to be an apostle. This command comes from "God our Savior"—the Father himself. It's worth noting that we often think of God the Father as the disciplinarian and Jesus as the savior, but Scripture shows us that the Father is also our Savior. Jesus is the righteous means by which God saves.

### To Timothy, His True Son

> To Timothy my true son in the faith:
>
> Grace, mercy and peace from God the Father and Christ Jesus our Lord.

Timothy was from Asia Minor (present-day Turkey), born to a Jewish mother who had become a believer and a Greek father. Paul met him during his second missionary journey, and Timothy became his protégé and missionary companion. Just as Paul received a command from God to be an apostle, Paul now commands Timothy to continue this work.

### The Command: Stop False Teaching

> As I urged you when I went into Macedonia, stay there in Ephesus so that you may command certain people not to teach false doctrines any longer or to devote themselves to myths and endless genealogies. Such things promote speculations rather than God's work—which is by faith.

The specific command Timothy must enforce involves stopping the teaching of:

- **False doctrines** (v. 3)
- **Myths** (v. 4)  
- **Genealogies**—endless speculation about ancestry (v. 4)
- **Speculations** (v. 4)—the Greek word means "seekings" or inquiries without grounded truth

The issue isn't that these things are controversial, but that they are speculative, unprofitable, and useless:

> Some have departed from these and have turned to meaningless talk. They want to be teachers of the law, but they do not know what they are talking about or what they so confidently affirm.

These false teachers claim knowledge they don't possess. As Paul tells us elsewhere:

> But avoid foolish controversies and genealogies and arguments and quarrels about the law, because these are unprofitable and useless. (Titus 3:9)

Speculation in science can be productive—we test hypotheses and advance knowledge. But speculation cannot replace revealed truth in matters of faith and morality. God declares that murder is wrong; we don't get to experiment with whether mass murder might be useful.

### A Modern Parallel: Theological Liberalism

In our generation, we face similar dangers in the form of theological liberalism—the attempt to unhook ourselves from the truths passed down to us. Beginning in the 18th century, Friedrich Schleiermacher and others sought to reconcile Enlightenment thinking with traditional Christianity by redefining faith as inner experience rather than truth claims.

German source criticism (the J, E, D, P theory) claimed the Bible was merely a collection of disparate human sources, undermining its authority. Through textual, source, form, redaction, and canonical criticism, scholars attempted to prove we cannot trust Scripture.

After 200 years, scholarship has largely returned to acknowledging that yes, biblical authors used sources and edited material—but this doesn't negate their authority. Yet the damage persists: we've lost confidence in the Bible as God's authoritative Word.

This loss of our primary source of authority has consequences. When we unmoor ourselves from Scripture, we find ourselves unmoored from scientific, logical, and historical reality itself. We embrace myths and speculations—theories without evidence or proof. The gospel and the law must work together.

### God's Work: The Law Protects the Gospel

> Such things promote speculations rather than advancing God's work—which is by faith.

"God's work" here refers to God's household law—the Greek word *oikonomia*, from which we get "economy." It's God's business versus idle speculation.

Paul will instruct Timothy in the law so he can teach the Ephesians:

> If I am delayed, you will know how people ought to conduct themselves in God's household, which is the church of the living God, the pillar and foundation of the truth. (1 Timothy 3:15)

## II. Commanded by Love (1:5-19)

### The Goal is Love

> The goal of this command is love, which comes from a pure heart and a good conscience and a sincere faith.

Paul's motivation is not deception or personal gain. His "pure heart," "good conscience," and "sincere faith" demonstrate that his teaching comes from genuine love for God's people—for their good and for the good of their families, communities, and nations.

This is countercultural. Our world defines love as being nice and permissive—allowing others to do whatever they want. We see truth and love as opposites, truth as harsh reality and love as therapeutic feeling.

But Scripture shows us that truth and love are inseparable:

> Open rebuke is better than love carefully concealed. Wounds from a friend are better than kisses from an enemy. (Proverbs 27:6)
>
> Whoever rebukes a person will in the end gain favor rather than one who has a flattering tongue. (Proverbs 28:23)
>
> Speaking the truth in love, we will grow to become in every respect the mature body of him who is the head, that is, Christ. (Ephesians 4:15)

Truth is not the opposite of love; truth *is* love. It's loving even when it hurts, because it awakens us from our make-believe world of myths and lies. It changes us for the better.

### The Law is Good

> We know that the law is good if one uses it properly.

The law's purpose is not to condemn the righteous but to correct and train lawbreakers:

> The law is made not for the righteous but for lawbreakers and rebels, the ungodly and sinful, the unholy and irreligious, for those who kill their fathers or mothers, for murderers, for the sexually immoral, for those practicing homosexuality, for slave traders and liars and perjurers—and for whatever else is contrary to the sound doctrine.

The law transforms sinners into saints. Examples throughout Scripture show this: King David (a murderer and liar), John Newton (a slave trader), and Rosaria Butterfield (who lived in homosexual practice) all experienced the law's redemptive purpose.

### Gospel and Law Together

> That conforms to the gospel concerning the glory of the blessed God, which he entrusted to me.

The order matters: gospel first, then law. We are not saved by works, and we desperately need grace and the gospel. But grace comes *with* law.

If we preach forgiveness without calling believers to obedience, we create a dangerous misunderstanding. We might conclude: "Since I'm forgiven, I don't have to try to reach biblical standards." This inverts the law's purpose entirely.

No. A concession is not permission. Grace gives us unlimited chances to continue pursuing that high standard. It's like a video game with unlimited lives—you still have to finish the game. Grace removes condemnation for our failures but doesn't remove the goal.

### The Law Saves Sinners

> Here is a trustworthy saying that deserves full acceptance: Christ Jesus came into the world to save sinners—of whom I am the worst. But for that very reason I was shown mercy so that in me, the worst of sinners, Christ Jesus might display his immense patience as an example for those who would believe in him and receive eternal life.

We are all lawbreakers and rebels. The law reveals our sin and points us to Christ. Paul himself exemplifies this: once a blasphemer and persecutor, he became a faithful apostle through God's mercy.

The gospel invites us to accept Christ's salvation. But salvation doesn't mean escaping obedience—it means entering Christ's lordship:

> Now to the King eternal, immortal, invisible, the only God, be honor and glory for ever and ever. Amen.

## A Pastoral Appeal

As we begin this series on 1 Timothy, some of you will find the teaching challenging. It may seem like speculation and myth rather than truth. But I ask for an open mind.

Consider the reality: we spend six days a week in the world and a couple hours in church. Even those of us who read Scripture daily invest only a small portion of our time there. This imbalance means the world's ideas have outsized influence over our Christian thinking when Scripture and culture clash.

My only advantage is that you know me, my family, and my heart. You know I'm not your enemy—we're one church family. If I harm you, I harm myself. There's no hidden agenda here. Instead, the agenda is love.
