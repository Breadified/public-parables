# Jonah 4:1-11

**Title:** Living Under the Shade of God's Grace

**Date:** 21 December 2014

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Story Endings Shape Everything

The ending of a story determines whether it succeeds. In Hollywood, filmmakers understand this so well that they'll completely reshape their films if test audiences don't respond positively. *Pretty Woman* was rewritten to give Julia Roberts a happily-ever-after rather than a tragic ending. *Men in Black II* had its final scene changed after 9/11 because showing UFOs destroying the Twin Towers was deemed too raw. *Dodgeball* had an original ending where the underdogs lost—audiences hated it, so the script was rewritten to give them victory.

If the book of Jonah were a Hollywood film, we would have had the perfect ending last week at the conclusion of chapter 3:

> When God saw what they did and how they turned from their evil ways, he relented and did not bring on them the destruction he had threatened.

Jonah had warned Nineveh. The city repented—from the greatest to the least. Destruction averted. Inspirational soundtrack, credits roll, everyone lives happily ever after.

But we don't get that ending. Instead, chapter 4 takes a troubling turn:

> But to Jonah this seemed very wrong, and he became angry.

This is the central mystery of Jonah's final chapter: *Why would God's grace make His servant angry?* The answer reveals three profound truths about Jonah's failure—and potentially, our own.

## 1. God's Character: Grace for All

### Jonah's Knowledge of God's Nature

When Jonah finally prays in chapter 4, he confesses something startling:

> He prayed to the Lord, "Isn't this what I said, Lord, when I was still at home? That is what I tried to forestall by fleeing to Tarshish. I knew that you are a gracious and compassionate God, slow to anger and abounding in love, a God who relents from sending calamity."

Jonah knew God's character perfectly. He fled to Tarshish not out of fear, but because he foresaw the very outcome that occurred: Nineveh's repentance and God's forgiveness.

This phrase—"gracious and compassionate God, slow to anger and abounding in love"—echoes throughout Scripture. It appears first in Exodus, when Moses asked to see God's glory:

> The Lord said, "I will cause all my goodness to pass in front of you, and I will proclaim my name, the LORD, in your presence. I will have mercy on whom I will have mercy, and I will have compassion on whom I will have compassion."

God revealed that His essential nature is mercy and compassion. Later, in Exodus 34, when God passed before Moses again, His character was proclaimed explicitly:

> The Lord, the Lord, the compassionate and gracious God, slow to anger, abounding in love and faithfulness, maintaining love to thousands, and forgiving wickedness, rebellion and sin.

This became the refrain of Israel's faith—repeated in Psalm 103, Numbers 14, and woven throughout the prophets. And then, two thousand years ago at Christmas, God Himself became flesh in Jesus Christ:

> The Word became flesh and made his dwelling among us. We have seen his glory, the glory of the one and only Son, who came from the Father, full of grace and truth.

In Jesus, we see God's compassion toward the weak and lost. We see His relentless desire to forgive sins. God wants to forgive.

### The Problem: Jonah Rejected God's Grace

Yet Jonah actively rejected this very nature of God. He didn't want God to be gracious and compassionate to Nineveh. The true reason for his disobedience wasn't fear or procrastination—it was that he didn't want to see them saved.

Nineveh was no ordinary enemy. It was a dangerous nation, destined to destroy the Northern Kingdom of Israel in the coming centuries. It represented a different culture, different values, a mortal threat. Jonah wanted to see Nineveh destroyed as judgment for its wickedness and the harm it had caused Israel.

Consider the parallels in our own time: Japan and China feuding over islands, haunted by war atrocities. Sunni and Shiite Muslims fighting in Iraq. Israeli-Palestinian conflicts with civilians caught in the crossfire. When nations are mortal enemies, one side's victory becomes the other's devastation. You rejoice at their suffering; you mourn their prosperity.

Jonah wanted Nineveh destroyed. Yet the stunning reality is that God's offer of forgiveness was extended to all:

> When God saw what they did and how they turned from their evil ways, he relented and did not bring on them the destruction he had threatened.

When Israel had sinned with the golden calf in the wilderness, God threatened destruction. But then:

> The Lord relented and did not bring on his people the disaster he had threatened.

**God does not show favoritism.** Salvation is for the Ninevites just as it was for the Jews. His grace and compassion are not reserved for the chosen people but extended to all people.

### Application: The Gospel Is for Everyone

This means the gospel is for all who believe—regardless of background, culture, birth, wealth, or how the world categorizes us. And it means the gospel is for our enemies, the people we can't be bothered about, the annoying or weak or awkward people we'd rather exclude.

Christianity is not a club of insiders. Yes, it stands for something. But it is fundamentally inclusive, open to all who would respond to God's grace.

Consider how you've experienced this grace yourself. God hasn't earned your faith through anything you've done. Jesus paid the price for all sin—freely, completely. As we approach the new year and contemplate our failures, remember: Jesus has already paid. The gift is free to all who will receive it.

## 2. The Shade of God's Grace: A Lesson Through a Plant

### Jonah's Vigil

After delivering his message, Jonah left the city and went to the east to watch:

> Jonah had gone out and sat down at a place east of the city. There he made himself a shelter, sat in its shade and waited to see what would happen to the city.

Picture him settling in to watch the fireworks—positioned on high ground with a lawn chair, waiting for Nineveh's destruction. But God had other plans:

> The Lord God provided a leafy plant and made it grow up over Jonah to give shade for his head to ease his discomfort, and Jonah was very happy about the plant.

Jonah was delighted by this unexpected gift. But God would use this plant to teach Jonah a painful lesson:

> At dawn the next day God provided a worm, which chewed the plant so that it withered. When the sun rose, God provided a scorching east wind, and the sun blazed on Jonah's head so that he grew faint. He wanted to die, and said, "It would be better for me to die than to live."

### The Object Lesson: Comparing Jonah to Nineveh

God then asked the pivotal question:

> Is it right for you to be angry about the plant?

And Jonah, in his distress, answered, "Yes, it is right. I'm so angry I wish I were dead."

God's response cuts to the heart of Jonah's inconsistency:

> You have been concerned about this plant, though you did not tend it or make it grow. It sprang up overnight and died overnight. And should I not have concern for the great city of Nineveh, in which there are more than a hundred and twenty thousand people who cannot tell their right hand from their left—and also many animals?

This is a devastating comparison. Jonah cared passionately for a plant—something he neither created nor sustained, something that appeared and disappeared in a single day. Yet he begrudged God's concern for 120,000 human beings and countless animals, all of whom God created and all of whom He sustained.

### The Hypocrisy Revealed

If Jonah could be happy about the creation of the plant and angry at its destruction, why couldn't he be happy about the creation of Ninevites and saddened by their potential destruction? Both were God's creation. Both were made to grow by His hand, not Jonah's.

Jonah's anger exposed a deeper selfishness: he was willing to receive God's grace but unwilling to extend it to others. He benefited from unmerited divine compassion—his own life had been miraculously preserved in the fish—yet he denied that same compassion to people he viewed as enemies.

### The Deeper Issue: Ingratitude and Self-Centeredness

Jonah's ego-centrism rings through his prayers in verses 2-3. He speaks constantly of "I" and "my." His life is what matters. His preferences are what should be honored. He would rather die than see God's grace extended beyond the boundaries of his own approval.

This is not unique to Jonah. It's a temptation that haunts every believer. We've experienced God's saving grace—either we recognize it or we should. Yet how often do we begrudge that same grace to others? How often do we think, "Yes, God saved me, but *them*? They don't deserve it"?

God's implicit answer is simple: we didn't deserve it either. But He gave it anyway.

## 3. Those Who Cannot Tell Their Right from Their Left: Love as God Loves

### The Final Appeal to Jonah's Compassion

God's closing words emphasize something striking:

> Should I not have concern for the great city of Nineveh, in which there are more than a hundred and twenty thousand people who cannot tell their right hand from their left—and also many animals?

The phrase "cannot tell their right hand from their left" speaks to spiritual ignorance and vulnerability. These are people who don't know what is right. They are "lost"—not in the sense of being physically displaced, but spiritually blind, heading toward judgment without knowledge of God's truth.

We pity animals. We pity plants. We pity endangered ecosystems and climate systems. Yet 120,000 human beings—people without spiritual knowledge, people heading toward eternal judgment—go unconsidered.

Our friends at school, our colleagues at work, our neighbors, our family members, old and young alike—many are "spiritually lost," unknowingly heading toward judgment. We are preoccupied with our own agendas, held back by awkwardness or apathy or the grip of bitterness, and we fail to share the one thing that could save them.

### A Church That Cares

Yet I have witnessed something different in our congregation this year. You have collectively demonstrated the heart of God toward people. You've invited friends, welcomed strangers, opened your homes and your hearts. Many of you have turned to Jesus for the first time. We rejoice at those celebrating their first Christmas with us.

You've made sacrifices out of love for the gospel—cooking dinners for family to create conversations about Jesus. You've opened your houses not just to friends but to strangers, forming genuine friendships. You've opened your wallets to support the church's mission. You've given up time and energy to make our community welcoming. You've knocked on doors in Cherrybrook, inviting neighbors to church. You've committed to regular attendance because you understand that your presence matters—the week you're absent is often the week a visitor arrives.

You've cut short holidays and wedding trips. You've worked on godliness, on biblical knowledge. You've served others and borne one another's burdens. You've done this out of gratitude for what God has done for you.

And this creates a challenge. As our church grows, as we naturally begin to take things for granted, as life gets busier and you enter new seasons—we will face the temptation to turn inward. To focus on ourselves rather than on those still lost, still in darkness, still "unable to tell their right from their left."

## The Question Remains: Was It Right to Be Angry?

At the end of the book of Jonah, the answer is clear: **No, it was not right.**

It was not right for Jonah to believe the gospel was exclusive rather than inclusive. It is open to all.

It was not right for him to be ungrateful. We are loved and saved by Jesus, and we must pass that love to others.

It was not right for him to lack compassion. Our calling is to imitate God's grace and compassion, to seek and save the lost through the proclamation of the gospel.

As we end this year and look toward what's ahead, let's keep doing what you've been doing—but more of it. Let's reach more and more people with the good news that God's grace is for them too.
