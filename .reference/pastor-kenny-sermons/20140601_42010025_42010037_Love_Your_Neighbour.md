# Luke 10:25-37

**Title:** Love Your Neighbour
**Date:** 8 June 2014
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction: Why Neighbours Matter

Everybody needs good neighbours. This Australian television maxim, running since 1985, reflects a deeper truth about human flourishing—we cannot live well in isolation. Good neighbours transform daily life; poor neighbours make it miserable.

When neighbours quarrel over fence lines, tree heights, parked cars, or pets, home becomes a place of anxiety rather than refuge. Yet when neighbours are generous and caring, life is enriched immeasurably. This sermon explores what Jesus teaches us about loving our neighbour and why this command stands at the heart of God's law.

## The Two Great Commandments (Luke 10:25-28)

An expert in the law approaches Jesus with a test: "Teacher, what must I do to inherit eternal life?"

This is no innocent question. The lawyer is attempting to trap Jesus—the same word used to describe Satan's temptation in Luke 4:12. As a professional scholar of God's law, the lawyer likely believed he could expose Jesus's ignorance or force him to prioritize certain commandments arbitrarily.

Rather than recite the entire law, Jesus responds with a question: "What is written in the Law? How do you read it?"

The lawyer answers correctly:

> Love the Lord your God with all your heart and with all your soul and with all your strength and with all your mind; and love your neighbour as yourself.

Jesus affirms: "You have answered correctly. Do this and you will live."

### The Foundation: Deuteronomy 6:4-5

This command originates in Israel's foundational law. God had rescued the Israelites from slavery and given them the promised land. Their response was to be wholehearted love for God—not from obligation, but from gratitude for salvation freely given.

### The Outworking: Leviticus 19:11-18

The command to love one's neighbour is the ethical expression of loving God. It encompasses not stealing, not lying, not defrauding, not holding back wages, protecting the vulnerable, showing fair justice, avoiding slander, and ultimately, refusing hatred.

As Paul writes in Romans 13:8-10:

> Let no debt remain outstanding, except the continuing debt to love one another, for he who loves his fellowman has fulfilled the law. The commandments... are summed up in this one rule: "Love your neighbour as yourself." Love does no harm to its neighbour. Therefore love is the fulfillment of the law.

### What This Standard Requires

The principle "love your neighbour as yourself" sets a clear measure: treat others as you wish to be treated. You would not want adultery, murder, theft, or envy committed against you—therefore, do not inflict these harms on others. You would want generosity and protection—therefore, offer these gifts to your neighbours.

For some of us, this command challenges our tendency toward stinginess. We must recognize our blessings and learn to release our grip on possessions. While wisdom demands we avoid con artists, generosity should remain our disposition. The church offers a concrete way to love—through caring for the elderly who need lawns mowed, the sick who need meals and visitation, the overwhelmed families who need childcare.

For others, this command requires restraint. Not everyone feels strongly about every injustice or social problem. We cannot control history, poverty, disability, or where others are born. We cannot solve every systemic problem, though we can refuse to exploit loopholes within systems. We must be wise, not gullible, and accept that we have limits.

### The Challenge: "Do This"

Yet here lies the crux: Jesus does not merely affirm the law as true doctrine. He commands: "Do this and you will live."

The law moves from theory to action. The question shifts from objective knowledge—"What is written?"—to personal practice: "How do you read it? What will you do?" This is the demand that exposes us. Are we experts in the law or experts in *practicing* the law?

## Who Is My Neighbour? (Luke 10:29-37)

Feeling defensive, the lawyer seeks to justify himself: "And who is my neighbour?"

He wants to lower the bar. Surely he must only love his friends, family, and countrymen? He cannot realistically help everyone. The question itself reveals a desire to define the boundaries of obligation.

### The Parable: A Samaritan's Mercy

Jesus responds not with a definition but with a story. A man travels from Jerusalem to Jericho—from Israel's highest city down to its lowest point, near the Dead Sea. Robbers attack him, strip him of his valuable clothing, beat him, and leave him for dead on the roadside.

A priest happens upon the man. He sees him but passes by on the other side. Under Levitical law, touching a dead or diseased person renders one ceremonially unclean, requiring a journey back to Jerusalem and temple sacrifices. The priest prioritizes convenience and ritual purity over mercy.

A Levite, also on temple rotation, encounters the same dilemma. He too sees and passes by.

Then comes a shocking turn: a Samaritan arrives.

### The Historical Divide: Samaritans and Jews

Samaritans were originally Israelites, but after the Assyrian invasion seven hundred years before Jesus, they intermarried with foreign peoples brought in by the conqueror. Jews viewed them as corrupt, half-bred, and impure. Samaritans despised Jews for their arrogance. They were enemies.

When traveling between Galilee and Jerusalem, Jews would cross the lake, walk the long way around, and cross again rather than pass through Samaritan territory. The priest and Levite would literally walk on the other side—just as the Samaritan now walks toward the beaten man.

### Extravagant Love

What happens next is extraordinary:

> The Samaritan, as he travelled, came where the man was; and when he saw him, he took pity on him. He went to him and bandaged his wounds, pouring on oil and wine. Then he put the man on his own donkey, brought him to an inn, and took care of him. The next day he took out two denarii and gave them to the innkeeper. "Look after him," he said, "and when I return, I will reimburse you for any extra expense you may have."

The Samaritan's compassion overflows all reasonable bounds. He walks while his enemy rides. He spends his own resources. He commits to future expenses without limit. This is not duty—it is extravagant, reckless love for a sworn enemy.

### The Twist in Jesus's Question

Notice the reversal. The lawyer asked: "Who is my neighbour?" implicitly asking, "Whom must I help?"

Jesus asks: "Which of these three was a neighbour to the man who fell into the hands of robbers?" The question has shifted from "Whom must I help?" to "Will you be a neighbour?"

The lawyer, unable even to name the Samaritan, replies: "The one who had mercy on him."

The standard is now inescapably personal. It is not "Who qualifies as my neighbour?" but "Are *you* a neighbour?" The boundary moves from selective legalism to universal obligation. The question moves from external categories to internal character.

### The Impossible Standard

Jesus concludes: "Go and do likewise."

But we recognize the trap we are in. The lawyer began by asking, "What must I do to inherit eternal life?" The law provides the answer: Love God completely. Love your neighbour as yourself. Do this and you will live.

Yet the standard Jesus illustrates is impossible. Who among us would show such reckless, sacrificial love to our enemies? Who could love a sworn adversary with this degree of commitment and cost?

As Paul writes:

> Therefore no one will be declared righteous in his sight by observing the law; rather, through the law we become conscious of sin. (Romans 3:20)

This parable is not primarily a moral example to emulate. To read it as merely a call to "be a good Samaritan" misses its radical nature. We cannot fulfil the law to God's standard. We cannot do our way to eternal life.

## The True Neighbour (Luke 10:25-37 Reinterpreted)

The parable points beyond itself to someone who will do what we cannot.

### Jesus as the Ultimate Samaritan

Jesus is the true neighbour. He loved with infinite capacity—not merely with human effort, but with divine love. Consider the parallels:

- **He took pity on us**: Jesus showed compassion on those whom society had rejected (Luke 7:13).
- **We were the wounded**: Like the man fallen among robbers, we were fallen among our spiritual enemies. Satan fell, and we lay helpless in sin's aftermath (Luke 10:18).
- **He did not pass by the other side**: Where the religious leaders turned away, Jesus stopped and gave everything.
- **He descended**: From heaven's heights to earth's depths—Jericho's low valley becomes a symbol of Jesus's descent into our condition.
- **He bandaged our wounds**: Jesus heals us from sin's damage. We have received his anointing (1 John 2:20, 27; 2 Corinthians 1:21).
- **He carries our burdens**: Jesus lifts our anxieties and sorrows (Philippians 4:6).
- **He provides for all our needs**: "If there's anything else that is good for us, he will provide for us."
- **At tremendous cost**: He gave his own blood, his own life.

### Jesus as the Wounded Man

Yet there is another layer to the parable. The wounded man is the central character throughout—he is present in every scene. The parable might equally be titled "The Parable of the Wounded Man" (as the Parable of the Prodigal Son should perhaps be called "The Parable of the Father").

Jesus identified with the wounded:

- **He fell in with robbers**: Crucified between criminals, he was identified with sinners (Luke 23).
- **He was beaten**: Opposed, vilified, cursed, framed, and betrayed (Luke 23:16).
- **He was stripped naked**: Robbed of dignity and clothing (Luke 23:24).
- **But more than half-dead**: He died completely, bearing the full weight of our judgment.

### Love the Neighbour He Sent

The lawyer's question was "Who is my neighbour?" The question transforms through Jesus's teaching: "Will you be a neighbour?" But ultimately, the deepest question is: "Will you love *the* Neighbour—the one sent for your sake?"

This is not merely a command to love other people better (though that follows). It is a call to recognise and embrace Jesus as the one who fulfilled the law's impossible demands. He is the neighbour who came down to us in our helplessness. He is the one who showed mercy when we were enemies. He is the one who continues to care for us and promises that any cost incurred on our behalf will be redeemed.

Do not pass by on the other side as the religious leaders did. Do not defer responding to him:

> "I'll think about him when exams are over."
> "I'll consider him when I've finished university."
> "I'll listen to him when my work is completed."
> "I'll serve him when I am free."

He has drawn near in his Word. He deserves a hearing. He has earned our attention, obedience, and love through his faithfulness and sacrifice.

## The Nature of Love: Gratitude Over Obligation

Love is an unusual response to God. In the ancient world, gods demanded honour and service—a master-slave relationship. But the God of Israel, having rescued his people from slavery, asks for something different: mutual love. Unconditional love. Love as the response of gratitude for salvation freely given.

This is more like marriage than servitude. In a master-slave relationship, one works endlessly asking, "Have I done enough?" Jesus says you can never do enough through your own effort. He has done it. He has fulfilled the law perfectly. And you can inherit eternal life—not through your achievement, but as a gift.

By definition, an inheritance cannot be earned. It is always received in grace, given by a generous benefactor. Jesus does not call us to earn our way to God. He calls us to receive what he has already accomplished.

## Conclusion

Everybody needs good neighbours. But more urgently, the Neighbour is essential to eternal life.

Let us love God with all our heart, soul, and mind. Let us love the neighbour he has sent for our sakes. And as we work through this series on caring for the poor and serving others, let us remember: all genuine love for our neighbour flows from first loving and trusting the One who loved us first.
