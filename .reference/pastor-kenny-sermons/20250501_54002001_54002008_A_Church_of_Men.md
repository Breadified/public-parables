# 1 Timothy 2:1-8

**Title:** A Church of Men
**Date:** 25 May 2025
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction: The Crisis of Masculinity

We live in a time where masculinity is profoundly misunderstood, ridiculed, and vilified. Men face an impossible cultural bind: earn money and you're exploitative; don't earn and you're a failure. Show chivalry and you're condescending; refuse and you're misogynistic. The result is that men are reduced to two extremes—either aggressive tyrants or passive spectators.

Consider the statistics. Men are four times more likely to die by suicide than women. When you walk into a morgue and see five people who died by suicide, four of them are men. Men are detaching from school, from relationships, from work. Journalist Norah Vincent spent eighteen months living as a man and came away profoundly shaken. She later admitted: *"I really like being a woman. I like it more now because I think it's more of a privilege."* She came to understand that *"Men are suffering. They have different problems than women have but they don't have it better. They need our sympathy, they need our love, and they need each other more than anything else."*

As Jordan Peterson observes: *"Men are suffering because they have no place in the modern world."* Whether you sit on any part of the feminism spectrum, we ought to have empathy. These are our husbands and our sons.

In this letter to Timothy about how the church ought to conduct itself as God's household, Paul addresses men specifically. In a world where men are retreating, God is calling men to step up. Paul outlines three ways:

1. **Step up in Prayer**
2. **Step up for Peace**
3. **Step up to Proclaim God's Provision**

## 1. Step Up in Prayer (2:1-2, 8)

> "I urge, then, first of all, that petitions, prayers, intercession and thanksgiving be made for all people—for kings and all those in authority, that we may live peaceful and quiet lives in all godliness and holiness." (2:1-2)

Paul opens with urgency: *"First of all."* Prayer is not optional—it is the primary posture of the church.

### Four Dimensions of Prayer

Paul employs four distinct Greek words to paint a comprehensive picture of prayer:

- **Supplications** (deēseis): Urgent requests for specific needs, often used when someone cries out to God for help or provision
- **Prayers** (proseuchas): General requests and worship, covering the breadth of petitionary prayer
- **Intercessions** (enteuxeis): Pleading on behalf of others, standing in the gap for their needs
- **Thanksgivings** (eucharistias): Expressions of gratitude, remembering God's faithfulness

This fourfold expression reflects the complete prayer life of the believer—petitions for ourselves, prayers of all kinds, intercession for others, and gratitude for God's provision.

### Men and Prayer

In verses 1-7, Paul uses the Greek word *anthropos* (ἄνθρωπος)—"people" in the generic sense. But in verse 8, he shifts to a different word:

> "Therefore I want the men everywhere to pray, lifting up holy hands without anger or disputing." (2:8)

Here Paul uses *andros* (ἄνδρας)—specifically **men**. He is singling them out because he knows the default temptation for men is passivity in prayer.

There is a widespread cultural perception that prayer is a feminine activity. Research from the Pew Research Center across 84 countries shows that women report praying daily at significantly higher rates than men—an average difference of 8 percentage points. In 43 of these countries, women are more likely than men to engage in daily prayer.

Why? Perhaps women, understanding themselves as the weaker vessel, instinctively turn to God. But perhaps the strong, independent man thinks his independence means independence from God. In his self-reliance, he sees action and problem-solving as his first move, rather than prayer.

Jesus modeled something different. He rose early to pray (Mark 1:35), prayed before choosing his disciples (Luke 6:12), and in Gethsemane submitted entirely to the Father (Luke 22:42). Even though divine, Jesus demonstrated dependence on the Father through constant prayer. He is the perfect example of manhood.

The strong man is not passive toward God. The strong man initiates prayer. He acknowledges his Maker. His strength comes only from the Stronger One. His ability comes from the one who is Able. His wisdom must flow first from the fear of God.

To pray is to acknowledge that true power is found not in human force but in God's gracious hand.

### Application: Who Will Step Up First?

**For fathers:** Do your children hear you pray for them? Have you initiated prayer with your wife after a conflict? It may feel awkward now—but only because you've waited. The longer you delay, the more awkward it becomes. Start now while they're young. Get the ball rolling.

**For young men:** Are you the one initiating prayer in your friend group, Bible study, or shared household? Join the men's group this Saturday. Pray with other men.

**For church leaders:** Is prayer the centerpiece of your ministry? Let it pervade everything—before and after events, before work, after challenges. It doesn't need to be lengthy or ritualistic, but let it remind us constantly that we appeal to a higher power for all we say and do.

## 2. Step Up for Peace (2:2-4)

> "for kings and all those in authority, that we may live peaceful and quiet lives in all godliness and holiness. This is good, and pleases God our Savior, who wants all people to be saved and to come to a knowledge of the truth." (2:2-4)

Paul calls men to pray specifically for those in authority. Why leaders? Because in his time, leadership was predominantly male—and Paul is calling men to pray for other men to do their jobs faithfully.

### The Nature of Peace

The phrase "peaceful and quiet life" carries nuance in the original Greek:

- **Peaceful** (ēremos): External tranquility—a stable, ordered society free from disruption and conflict. The word derives from descriptions of regions at peace, seas without storm, cities without upheaval. It speaks to ORDER.
- **Quiet** (hesychios): Internal calm—a composed, disciplined life marked by integrity and focus.

> "Make it your ambition to lead a quiet life: You should mind your own business and work with your hands, just as we told you, so that your daily life may win the respect of outsiders and so that you will not be dependent on anybody." (1 Thessalonians 4:11-12)

This is not passivity. Peace is not capitulation. Rather, it is the stable, ordered environment necessary for the gospel to flourish.

### The Masculine Call to Order

When Paul instructs men to pray for authorities and to pursue peaceful and quiet lives, he is calling them to something fundamentally masculine: the promotion of order in a world prone to chaos.

Jordan Peterson captures this: *"The masculine spirit is, fundamentally, the spirit of leadership and voluntary confrontation with chaos."*

This resonates with Paul's instruction. We are called not to spiritual passivity but to intentional leadership. Not brute force, but spiritual strength. Not silence, but the voice of intercession and truth.

The work of leadership extends through every level of society—from the king downward to family heads, school boards, corporate governance, local authority, armed forces, and all chains of command. And it goes all the way down to the individual man. It takes immense leadership to sustain a nation.

Even young people, who may feel powerless, can bring order to their own lives: wake up, make your bed, shave or trim your beard, get your prayer life sorted, present yourself to the world ready for work. As Peterson says: *"Make your bed. Get sorted. Present yourself. Then go."*

### Application: Leadership at Every Level

Will you pray for those in authority? Will you then serve and help them bring order? 

There is a whole heap of leadership required to sustain this world—from the king downwards through every tier. It is not enough to critique the government; we must intercede for it. And having interceded, we must act. We must bring order.

Young people: Bring order out of the chaos of your own life first. Pray and go.

## 3. Step Up to Proclaim God's Provision (2:3-6)

> "This is good, and pleases God our Savior, who wants all people to be saved and to come to a knowledge of the truth. For there is one God and one mediator between God and mankind, the man Christ Jesus, who gave himself as a ransom for all people." (2:3-6)

### The Gospel at the Heart

At the heart of this passage is the gospel itself. The prayer for peace and the pursuit of social order are not distractions from our mission—they create the conditions where the gospel can flourish.

Notice the careful structure:

- Prayer for **all people** (2:1)
- for **all in authority** (2:2) to maintain social order
- **So that all people** might be saved (2:4)
- Through **Jesus**, who gave himself a ransom for **all people** (2:6)

The theme of universality—"all people"—threads through the entire passage, culminating in the gospel.

### The Mediator and the Ransom

Christ is presented as the mediator between God and mankind. We are held captive by sin and Satan. A ransom must be paid. Christ steps in as the hostage negotiator, but unlike an ordinary negotiator, he gives himself up to secure our freedom.

This is substitution. This is propitiation. The one who is both fully God and fully Man stands between a holy God and sinful humanity, offering himself as the payment for our release.

### The Purpose of Order

Why does social order matter? So that in peaceful, stable societies, the gospel can be proclaimed clearly. The gospel is not secondary to social concern, nor is social concern secondary to gospel proclamation. They are related.

As Paul says in verses 6-7:

> "This has now been witnessed to at the proper time. And for this purpose I was appointed a herald and an apostle—I am telling the truth, I am not lying—and a true and faithful teacher of the Gentiles." (2:6-7)

### A Necessary Balance

Some may object: *"Isn't our life about proclaiming the gospel?"*

Yes, it is. But social order cannot be relegated to the bottom of our priorities. It may not be as important as gospel proclamation, but it must command our serious attention.

Consider an analogy: You say family is your priority, yet you never fix the roof. Rain leaks everywhere, everything becomes moldy, and your children develop asthma. You lose your job but never find another because you're too busy "spending time with family"—now they're starving. You've confused priorities with abandonment.

Social order is your roof. Social order is your food. The point of maintaining them is *so that* you can do the more important thing: proclaim Christ.

We need both. The balance fluctuates over time, but we neglect neither. Currently, in many evangelical circles, we romanticize ministry work while underinvesting in the structures that sustain society. And it is men who bear the brunt of both callings.

### Application: Men Who Step Up

God is calling for men who will:

- **Pray with urgency and boldness**, acknowledging their dependence on God
- **Promote peace in a culture of conflict**, bringing order and stability
- **Proclaim Christ in a world of confusion**, boldly testifying to his provision

We do not need more passive men. We need godly initiators—men who step up.

As Jordan Peterson writes: *"To stand up straight with your shoulders back is to accept the terrible responsibility of life, with eyes wide open."*

And as John Newton, the former slave trader turned faithful minister, demonstrated through his prayers for William Wilberforce and others in authority—it is the intercession of righteous men that shifts history toward justice and peace.
