# Romans 3:27–4:25

**Title:** The Faithfulness of God
**Date:** 25 November 2012
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## How Do We Get Right with God?

We celebrate the 500th anniversary of the Reformation because on 31 October 1517, Martin Luther—a German monk in Wittenberg—protested against the medieval church's fundamental misunderstanding of how to be reconciled with God. His protest catalyzed a reformation of the church's practices, doctrines, and traditions, sending believers back to Scripture to answer the question at the heart of faith: How does one actually get right with God?

For a thousand years before Luther, the church had accumulated progressive innovations and forgotten its founding document—the Bible. Like any institution that loses sight of its original charter, it had drifted far from Scripture's central message.

## Justification Is Never by Works (Romans 3:27–4:2)

Luther discovered what Scripture had always taught: the Bible has never been about earning your way into heaven. This truth reaches all the way back to Abraham, the patriarch from whom both Judaism and Christianity draw their roots.

> What then shall we say that Abraham, our forefather according to the flesh, discovered in this matter? If, in fact, Abraham was justified by works, he had something to boast about—but not before God. (Romans 4:1–2)

The text implies something crucial: Abraham was *not* justified by works. To be *justified* means to be found righteous in a court of law by the judge—to meet God's standard of righteousness. But how can anyone do this? How can we prove ourselves righteous before the perfectly righteous God? It's like trying to demonstrate you're a great tennis player in front of Federer, or a brilliant pianist in front of Chopin. It's simply impossible.

> Where, then, is boasting? It is excluded. Because of what law? The law that requires works? No, because of the law that requires faith. For we maintain that a person is justified by faith apart from the works of the law. (Romans 3:27–28)

Reconciliation with God has never been by works.

### The Medieval Church's Fatal Error

The medieval church of the 16th century understood something important—they felt the weight of guilt before God. But their solution was tragically mistaken. The practice of *indulgences* developed: if you sinned, you could shorten the penance for that sin by purchasing an indulgence. It was a transaction—pay money, reduce your guilt. As the church built St. Peter's Basilica, indulgences were aggressively marketed. John Tetzel, a notorious salesman, even coined a catchy slogan: "As soon as the coin in the coffer rings, the soul from purgatory springs."

But indulgences weren't just about money. They included walking the church steps, fasting, venerating statues, pilgrimages to holy sites—an endless catalog of works to perform. The assumption was always the same: do enough, and you'll earn your way to heaven.

### We Still Make This Mistake

Today, we haven't entirely escaped this thinking. We trust in our own religious works: temple visits, mosque observances, church attendance, meditation. We might follow the five pillars of Islam or the fourfold path of Buddhism. We get baptized, give to charity, and convince ourselves that being a good person gives us a reasonable chance at heaven. Perhaps we're carbon neutral. Perhaps we recycle. Surely that counts for something?

But there's one insurmountable problem: **you cannot earn your way to heaven.**

Consider the absurdity of it: Can a token gesture, an apology, some flowers, a few dollars, or a couple hours on Sunday really settle the score with God? If someone betrayed you deeply, would their apology instantly restore complete trust? If someone stole from you, would returning the item magically heal the violation? If someone lied to you repeatedly, could you ever be truly carefree around them again?

### The Universal Truth About Sin

We may not emphasize human sinfulness the way medieval Christians did, but we sense it all the same. We lack self-control. We're prone to anger. We scream at our parents in rage. Our thoughts betray us constantly. We cheat. We hate. We lust. We're all born crooked—no one is straight. Only the most arrogant would imagine they're going to heaven based on their moral resume.

Even the religions of the world, though they describe salvation differently, share one universal conviction: *we have done wrong and deserve condemnation.* Even we ourselves set standards and break them. That's why our standards keep decreasing—we're always lowering the bar to meet our own performance.

The hard truth is this: **works can never bring reconciliation, even between mere humans. Works can never, have never, and were never intended to bring reconciliation between God and humanity.**

## Justification Is Only by Faith (Romans 4:3)

Luther's biblical conclusion was revolutionary yet simple: we are made right with God through faith, not works.

> What does Scripture say? Abraham believed God, and it was credited to him as righteousness. (Romans 4:3)

Abraham *believed* God. In English, we use "believe" as a verb and "faith" as a noun, but they're the same word in Greek. What Abraham did was exercise faith—he trusted. And that was all he needed to do.

He didn't perform spiritual acts. He didn't get baptized or do penance or accumulate religious works. He simply believed. He believed even when God made him ridiculous promises and gave him many reasons to doubt. But Abraham realized something essential: *God is bigger than I am, and I don't have the answers.* He trusted. He followed. And God credited this faith to him as righteousness.

### David's Example (Romans 4:6–8)

This principle wasn't unique to Abraham. A thousand years later, King David—one of Abraham's descendants—experienced the same truth:

> David says the same thing when he speaks of the blessedness of the one to whom God credits righteousness apart from works: "Blessed are those whose transgressions are forgiven, whose sins are covered. Blessed is the one whose sin the Lord will never count against them." (Romans 4:6–8)

Paul quotes David from Psalm 32, traditionally written after David committed adultery with Bathsheba and then murdered her husband to hide his sin. What could David possibly do to reverse such atrocities? Could he "unrape" Bathsheba? Could he "unmurder" her husband? Some things are impossible to undo, no matter how sincere the repentance.

Yet in God's mercy, David was found righteous. His sins were forgiven. His debt was wiped clean, never to be counted against him again. How? David believed. He acknowledged his bankruptcy before God:

> I acknowledged my sin to you, and I did not cover my iniquity; I said, "I will confess my transgressions to the LORD," and you forgave the iniquity of my sin. (Psalm 32:5)

David didn't try to earn redemption through works. He didn't rely on the law or search for deeds to perform. He simply acknowledged his guilt, and God forgave him.

### What Faith Actually Is

Many people misunderstand faith as something magical or supernatural—as if believing hard enough makes miracles happen. But faith itself isn't magical at all. We exercise faith constantly in ordinary life: we believe the train will get us to Town Hall; we believe it will rain today; we believe that 1 + 1 = 2. Faith is simply trust.

What matters isn't the *quantity* of your faith, but the *object* of your faith. You can have unwavering belief that money grows on trees, but it won't happen. Or you can have a small, tentative belief that roses are red, and you'll be exactly right.

The critical issue isn't how much faith you have—it's **faith in whom**. Abraham's faith was powerful not because it was extraordinary, but because it was faith *in God*. And our faith today must be faith in what God has accomplished through Jesus Christ.

## The Heart of Forgiveness: Payment (Romans 4:4–5)

> Now to the one who works, wages are not credited as a gift but as an obligation. However, to the one who does not work but trusts God who justifies the ungodly, their faith is credited as righteousness. (Romans 4:4–5)

Here's what people often miss about forgiveness: **there is no magical disappearance of sin.** What actually happens is that *someone else pays the debt*.

Think like an accountant: when you do something wrong, a deficit appears on the ledger. Someone has to settle it. If I borrow your iPhone without permission to play Angry Birds and break it—that's $500 of damage. I owe that debt. It's against me.

But forgiveness works this way: the debt still exists. The phone is still broken. But someone else pays the full price so that it's no longer counted against me.

### The Texas Story

There's a true story from Texas, near Christmas a few years back. A driver named Hayden Carlo was pulled over by a police officer. Carlo explained that his registration was outdated because he simply didn't have the money—it was a choice between feeding his kids or renewing his registration. The officer had to do his job and issue a fine. That was justice.

But then the officer did something extraordinary. He slipped a $100 bill inside the ticket. When Carlo opened it and saw that money, he broke down in his car. That $100 was exactly enough to pay the fine *and* update the registration on both his car and his wife's car.

This is what God has done for us through Christ.

God must punish sin—that's justice. But he provides a way to avoid that punishment. God sends his "hundred dollar bill"—Jesus Christ—which is enough to pay the debts of our sins and give us a fresh start. We receive righteousness. Someone has paid so that we can go free.

### The Unpayable Debt

But notice something profound: this is a payment that no ordinary person can make. Works cannot count toward it, because we cannot restore a broken relationship through our own efforts. This is a payment that no third party can make, because the debt isn't merely material—it's about wrath, about the violation of the broken relationship itself. This payment can only be paid by God himself. The penalty of sin is so costly that it required God himself, incarnated in his Son, to pay the price.

And therefore God now offers us a recount of our life. Your sin is no longer counted against you. You get a chance to start over.

**Faith is simply the vehicle of acceptance.** It's saying yes to what God has done. It's like accepting a lifesaver when you're drowning—you don't earn it, you receive it.

## Faith Alone—Yet Not Alone

When we speak of justification by *faith alone*, we're not describing a dead faith that does nothing. We mean that faith is the *only cause* of justification. Yet the faith that justifies is not isolated. As Martin Luther put it: "It is therefore faith alone which justifies, and yet the faith which justifies is not alone; just as it is the heat alone of the sun which warms the earth, and yet in the sun it is not alone, because it is constantly conjoined with light."

### The Place of the Law (Romans 4:9–12)

> Is this blessing then only for the circumcised, or also for the uncircumcised? For we say that faith was counted to Abraham as righteousness. How then was it counted to him? Was it before or after he had been circumcised? It was not after, but before he was circumcised. (Romans 4:9–10)

This is crucial: Abraham was justified *before* circumcision, not after. God gave the law of circumcision to Abraham and all his descendants—it was the mark that distinguished Israel from every other nation. But Abraham's righteousness preceded the law. He was already right with God through faith.

> He received the sign of circumcision as a seal of the righteousness that he had by faith while he was still uncircumcised. (Romans 4:11)

Circumcision was an *outward sign* of what he already possessed through faith. The law exists for people of faith. Without faith, the law becomes nothing but meaningless works. It's like taking a photo in front of SeaWorld without actually going inside—the picture isn't the reality.

The Jewish people eventually made a tragic mistake: they began to worship the sign rather than pursue the reality it pointed to. They camped around the symbol of faith instead of pursuing faith itself. While that might have been understandable in the beginning, once Jesus came as the true temple and fulfillment of all God's promises, it became foolish to remain fixated on the sign.

### The Role of the Law Today

Some of the law remains prescriptive—"don't steal" is as binding today as it ever was. Some laws have become redundant because they're fulfilled in Christ—circumcision, for example. And some laws contain wisdom designed for a specific time but still teach us principles—like the jubilee's lesson about God's care for the poor and oppressed.

We can discover these distinctions by following Abraham's example and asking: Does this law point me toward faith? Does it lead me toward Christ?

> Do we then overthrow the law by this faith? By no means! On the contrary, we uphold the law. (Romans 3:31)

The law is good because the law has always been about faith. When you follow the law properly, it should lead to the fulfillment of the law—to Christ. The law's true purpose isn't to save you; it's to lead you to Abraham's faith and Christ's redemption.

## The Nature of Abraham's Faith (Romans 4:18–22)

> In hope he believed against hope, that he should become the father of many nations, as he had been told, "So shall your offspring be." He did not weaken in faith when he considered his own body, which was as good as dead (since he was about a hundred years old), or when he considered the barrenness of Sarah's womb. No unbelief made him waver concerning the promise of God, but he grew strong in his faith as he gave glory to God, fully convinced that God was able to do what he had promised. That is why his faith was counted to him as righteousness. (Romans 4:18–22)

Abraham's faith teaches us three essential truths:

1. **Faith believes God's promises**, even when the circumstances make them seem impossible.
2. **Faith can grow**, starting from a mustard seed and becoming mighty.
3. **Faith persists even when it's hard to believe**—Abraham had every natural reason to doubt, yet he grew stronger in his conviction.

## This Promise Is For You (Romans 4:23–25)

> But the words "it was counted to him" were not written for his sake alone, but for ours also. It will be counted to us who believe in him who raised from the dead Jesus our Lord, who was delivered up for our trespasses and raised for our justification. (Romans 4:23–25)

This passage isn't ancient history preserved for curiosity. It's written *for us*. Abraham believed God, and it was credited to him as righteousness. You can believe God too, and it will be credited to you as righteousness. 

Jesus was delivered up for our trespasses—he died bearing the weight of our sin and guilt. And he was raised for our justification—his resurrection proves that the debt is paid and we are now free.

## God of Jews and Gentiles (Romans 3:29–30)

> Or is God the God of Jews only? Is he not the God of Gentiles too? Yes, of Gentiles too, since there is only one God, who will justify the circumcised by faith and the uncircumcised through that same faith. (Romans 3:29–30)

The boundaries we create—circumcised and uncircumcised, Jew and Gentile, insider and outsider—do not limit God's justifying work. God has one method of salvation, and it's available to everyone: faith. Whether you were born into covenant privilege or not, whether you've kept the traditions or ignored them, you can be made right with God in exactly the same way Abraham was—through believing faith in the God who raises the dead.

There is no justification by flesh, by ethnicity, by birthright, or by works. There is only justification by faith in Jesus Christ, who died for your sins and rose again.

---

**Grow strong in your faith.** As you rest in what God has accomplished through Christ, your confidence and trust will deepen. You have been counted as righteous. You are free.
