# John 15:1-15

**Title:** Remain in the Vine
**Date:** March 27, 2016
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Vineyard in Scripture

We're studying the Book of John at church, and John 15 represents the climax of Jesus' teaching to his disciples. In the preceding chapters, Jesus teaches about serving (John 13), the Holy Spirit (John 14 & 16), and now in chapter 15, he presents a central image: the vine.

Jesus begins by saying:

> "I am the true vine, and my Father is the gardener." (John 15:1)

### Israel's Story as a Vineyard

This image would have resonated deeply with Jesus' Jewish audience. Ancient Israel had extensive vineyards fed by an irrigation system from the Jordan River—a life-giving network in an otherwise desert landscape. Jesus uses this familiar horticultural reference to compare and contrast himself with the nation of Israel.

Centuries before Jesus, the psalmist captured God's care for Israel:

> "You transplanted a vine from Egypt; you drove out the nations and planted it. You cleared the ground for it, and it took root and filled the land. The mountains were covered with its shade, the mighty cedars with its branches." (Psalm 80:8-11)

The vineyard was the picture of success—expansive, flourishing, and protective. Like bamboo spreading to create a vast forest, Israel was meant to grow and prosper, its influence extending west to the Mediterranean and east to the Jordan River.

### Israel's Failure: A Spreading Weed

Yet Israel's story took a troubling turn. The prophet Hosea, writing around 700 years before Jesus, captured the contradiction:

> "Israel was a spreading vine; he brought forth fruit for himself. As his fruit increased, he built more altars; as his land prospered, he adorned his sacred stones. Their heart is deceitful, and now they must bear their guilt." (Hosea 10:1-2)

The more successful Israel became materially, the more sinful they became spiritually. They took the blessings God gave them and turned them into idolatry. Rather than being a fruit-bearing vine that nourished the world, they became like a parasitic weed—feeding only themselves, offering thorns instead of shelter to those who sought refuge.

### Jesus as the True Vine

Into this history steps Jesus, declaring: "I am the true vine." He's saying something radical—*I am the Israel that God always intended Israel to be.* I am the one who follows God completely. I am the vine that provides shade to all directions, shelter and protection to all who seek refuge.

## Stay with the Vine (Verses 1, 4-5)

> "Remain in me, as I also remain in you. No branch can bear fruit by itself; it must remain in the vine. Neither can you bear fruit unless you remain in me. I am the vine; you are the branches." (John 15:4-5)

To gain the benefits of being connected to this vine, we must remain attached to it. If a branch is severed—even partially—it receives no nourishment and dies. I once tried to cut down a thick gum tree branch but only managed to saw halfway through. I procrastinated finishing the job, and months later, the branch had fallen by itself. Why? Because even that partial cut severed the flow of nutrients, and without nourishment, it died—eventually falling on my neighbor's fence.

But there's a horticultural practice that illustrates what happens when we remain attached: grafting. Tomatoes, while desirable, are susceptible to disease. So gardeners graft the tomato plant onto stronger, disease-resistant rootstock. The graft takes hold, and the plant grows strong, supplied by and protected by that robust root system.

### Application

If Jesus is the vine of life, then stay with the vine. The things of this world are dying—a decaying earth, a withering planet, a slowly dying sun. Our bodies are slowly perishing. Don't hang onto a dying tree. Instead, be grafted into a life-giving one.

## Stay with the Word (Verses 1-3, 7-8)

> "He cuts off every branch in me that bears no fruit, while every branch that does bear fruit he prunes so that it will be even more fruitful... You are already clean because of the word I have spoken to you." (John 15:2-3)

### Understanding Pruning

Good gardeners know that every plant needs pruning. They prune to direct growth in a particular way, to stimulate the formation of flowers and fruit buds, to remove older stems so the plant can invest energy in new growth, and to prevent the spread of disease.

God prunes for the same reasons—to create growth and improve the health of the branches. The disciples Jesus is speaking to are those branches the gardener has already worked on. He's taken the shears to them and trimmed them clean.

### Discipline vs. Punishment

Christians often confuse discipline with punishment. But as Hebrews reminds us:

> "Endure hardship as discipline; God is treating you as his children. For what children are not disciplined by their father?" (Hebrews 12:7)

For the Christian, suffering isn't punishment from a vicious god—it's discipline from a loving Father. Although painful, it carries a life-giving benefit. The hardship is like cancer being cut away so we can live. It hurts, but the end product is health.

When we're grafted into Christ, we must be transformed to fit the vine. We must get used to the nutrients it provides, change our very nature to align with Christ's nature, and allow ourselves to be bent and shaped to grow as the vine directs. Bits of us must be pruned away—our self-centeredness, our sin, our old ways of thinking.

### The Word as Pruning Shears

The word of God does this pruning work. Scripture is:

> "Living and active. Sharper than any double-edged sword, it penetrates even to dividing soul and spirit, joints and marrow; it judges the thoughts and attitudes of the heart." (Hebrews 4:12)

The Bible cuts more sharply than any scissors or scalpel. It divides our thoughts and attitudes, opens our hearts and minds, and convicts us to change our behavior and understanding.

Many people think the problem lies outside themselves—in their environment or circumstances. They want God to change their situation to suit them. But God doesn't work that way. He doesn't conform to our desires. Instead, he conforms *us* so that we see the world differently, so that we change our behavior, so that we interpret the circumstances of our lives through Scripture and recognize where we need to improve.

This is how the word transforms us. We examine our lives against Scripture, change our attitudes, see God's warnings and promises come true, and gain confidence that God is true. The ultimate goal is to produce in us a wonderful tree full of fruit.

## Stay with Love (Verses 5, 9-13)

> "If you remain in me and I in you, you will bear much fruit; apart from me you can do nothing." (John 15:5)

### The Difference Between Weeds and Cultivated Plants

A weed exists only for itself and bears no fruit. A cultivated plant produces fruit for others—whether leaves, fruit, branches, or flowers. A Christian's life should produce the fruit of love, not remain barren like a weed.

> "My command is this: Love each other as I have loved you. Greater love has no one than this: to lay down one's life for one's friends." (John 15:12-13)

### Love and Obedience

Many think love and commandments are opposites—that love is spontaneous emotion while commands are legalistic rules. But that's only true if you see love as mere feeling. Genuine, purposeful love is directed by God's commands.

> "If you keep my commands, you will remain in my love, just as I have kept my Father's commands and remain in his love. I have told you this so that my joy may be in you and that your joy may be complete." (John 15:10-11)

God shows us what love actually looks like: patient, kind, humble, self-sacrificing. Love is expressed through service to parents and children, kindness to relatives, and faithful friendship with others.

### The Ultimate Expression of Love

Jesus himself is the ultimate example. He became the vine and was hung on a tree. He was pruned and cut, he bled. Though it was a terrible crime, he went to the cross out of love for us. At Calvary, we see Jesus' true love—not as sentiment, but as costly sacrifice.

This is the love we're called to embody. Not a weed that exists only for itself, but a fruit-bearing branch that conforms to the vine and produces love for others.
