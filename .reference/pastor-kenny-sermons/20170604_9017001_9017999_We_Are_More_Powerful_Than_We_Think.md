# 1 Samuel 17

**Title:** We Are More Powerful Than We Think  
**Date:** June 4, 2017  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction

In nature, many creatures have learned an instinctive survival strategy: they make themselves appear larger than they actually are. The frilled-neck lizard extends its collar, a cat fluffs its fur, and a husky's impressive appearance diminishes dramatically once you examine it closely. This principle extends to human conflict as well. Yet as we examine the story of David and Goliath, we discover something profound—appearances deceive us about who is truly powerful.

Malcolm Gladwell's analysis of this biblical account in *David and Goliath* reveals insights worth exploring. The narrative before us contains two essential truths that challenge our assumptions about strength and weakness.

## Part One: David Appears to Be the Underdog

### Goliath's Overwhelming Advantage

Goliath was not merely tall—he was extraordinarily formidable. Scripture describes him:

> His height was six cubits and a span. (1 Samuel 4)

This represents approximately three meters (ten feet). The Septuagint records a more modest four cubits, but either measurement represents a giant of unprecedented size.

His armor alone was devastating. His bronze helmet, coat of scale armor weighing approximately 78 kilograms, bronze greaves, and a spear with an iron point weighing 10 kilograms—this warrior was a walking fortress. The shaft of his spear was like a weaver's rod, and his shield bearer walked ahead of him.

### The Military Stalemate

The geography of Israel's terrain created the perfect conditions for this impasse:

> The Philistines occupied one hill and the Israelites another, with the valley between them.

In ancient warfare, no army wanted to descend into a valley and surrender their height advantage. The armies faced each other across this valley for forty days while Goliath issued his challenge:

> Choose a man and have him come down to me. If he is able to fight and kill me, we will become your subjects; but if I overcome him and kill him, you will become our subjects and serve us.

This was no mere boast—it was a tactical proposal to break the stalemate.

### Israel's Fear and David's Humility

When Saul and the Israelites heard Goliath's taunts:

> They were dismayed and terrified.

Into this moment came David, the youngest son of Jesse, sent from Bethlehem to bring food to his older brothers who served in Saul's army. While his three eldest brothers were seasoned soldiers, David's task was tending his father's sheep. He was young, inexperienced, and apparently unprepared for warfare.

Upon hearing Goliath's challenge, David's oldest brother Eliab reacted with contempt:

> Why have you come down here? I know how conceited you are and how wicked your heart is; you came down only to watch the battle.

Even Saul questioned David's fitness for battle:

> You are not able to go out against this Philistine and fight him; you are only a young man, and he has been a warrior from his youth.

Goliath shared this assessment. When he saw David approaching:

> He looked David over and saw that he was little more than a boy, glowing with health and handsome, and he despised him.

By all outward measures, David was the underdog. The gap between a young, untested shepherd and a battle-hardened giant seemed insurmountable.

### The Application for Us

It is genuinely difficult to be an underdog. Many of us face circumstances that make us feel small and powerless:

- You may be struggling financially
- You may be unable to find work despite your qualifications
- You may have recently failed an important exam
- You may face illness with no cure in sight
- Friends cannot help you; government systems seem indifferent

In such moments, we feel trapped. But there is an answer.

## Part Two: David Is Not the Underdog

What Saul and David's brothers could not see was that God had already transformed David into something far more formidable than his appearance suggested.

### The Hidden Strength of David

When Samuel first anointed David:

> The Lord said, Rise and anoint him; this is the one. So Samuel took the horn of oil and anointed him in the presence of his brothers, and from that day on the Spirit of the Lord came powerfully upon David.

By the time David reached Saul's court, his reputation had already changed. One of Saul's servants described him:

> I have seen a son of Jesse of Bethlehem who knows how to play the lyre. He is a brave man and a warrior. He speaks well and is a fine-looking man. And the Lord is with him.

This is the crucial phrase: "The Lord is with him."

### Evidence of David's True Strength

David himself revealed his qualifications when offering to fight Goliath:

> Your servant has been keeping his father's sheep. When a lion or a bear came and carried off a sheep from the flock, I went after it, struck it and rescued the sheep from its mouth. When it turned on me, I seized it by its hair, struck it and killed it.

David had already proven himself a warrior—a young man capable of wrestling down predators with his bare hands. This was not bravado; it was testimony to genuine martial skill.

Several pieces of evidence suggest David was not a small man at all:

- His oldest brother was physically impressive, suggesting David may have grown to similar size
- He was called "a mighty man of valor, a man of war" before facing Goliath
- He had already killed both a bear and a lion by hand
- King Saul, himself a tall man, offered his armor to David for the battle
- David later proved capable of wielding Goliath's sword

When David first tried on Saul's armor, he removed it not because he was too small, but because he was unaccustomed to it. His choice reveals his true advantage.

### The Weapon of the Skilled

David selected five smooth stones from the stream and approached Goliath with his shepherd's sling. This was not a child's toy—it was the weapon of a trained warrior. In ancient warfare, slingers were elite soldiers, crucial to military success. As a shepherd, David's livelihood depended on his ability to protect his flock from predators using his sling with pinpoint accuracy.

The stones from the Valley of Elah were uniquely suited to David's purpose. These rocks contained barium sulphate, twice the density of normal stone. When hurled from a sling at approximately 45 meters per second, such projectiles possessed stopping power equivalent to a 45mm handgun.

David understood something Goliath missed: the outcome would not be determined by armor or size, but by skill, speed, and faith.

### The Weakness Hidden in Goliath's Strength

Why did Goliath require a shield bearer? Why was he surprised that David approached him? Why did he refer to David carrying "sticks" when David carried only a staff?

Medical history suggests an answer. Extreme height in the ancient world, as today, typically results from gigantism caused by a pituitary gland tumor secreting excess growth hormone. The pituitary gland lies at the base of the brain near where the optic nerves meet. A tumor in this location presses on the optic nerves, causing tunnel vision—a dramatic loss of peripheral sight.

A warrior suffering from such a condition would have profound difficulty dodging projectiles thrown from the side. What appeared to be Goliath's supreme advantage—his size and strength—may have been coupled with a critical vulnerability: compromised vision.

### The Victory

As Goliath moved closer to attack:

> David ran quickly toward the battle line to meet him. Reaching into his bag and taking out a stone, he slung it and struck the Philistine on the forehead. The stone sank into his forehead, and he fell facedown on the ground.

One perfectly placed stone. One moment. The stalemate shattered.

> So David triumphed over the Philistine with a sling and a stone; without a sword in his hand he struck down the Philistine and killed him.

David then took Goliath's own sword and cut off his head. When the Philistine army saw their champion dead, they fled in panic. Israel's victory was complete.

## The Deeper Pattern: Jesus and the Enemy

This narrative echoes a pattern that culminates in Christ:

- The anointed one of God receives the Spirit and faces an enemy presenting himself before God's people
- In the Hebrew text, this enemy wears serpent-like armor—a striking parallel to Satan's form
- Goliath taunted Israel for forty days; Jesus faced forty days of testing in the wilderness
- At the conclusion of both forty-day periods came spiritual battle
- David struck Goliath on the forehead; God promised Eve that the Messiah would strike the serpent's head
- Jesus defeated Satan and cast him down

The pattern is clear: God defeats His enemies not through overwhelming earthly power, but through the perfect weapon wielded by His anointed one.

## Application: Recognizing Our True Power

### The Danger of Spiritual Defeat

Many Christians unconsciously accept a spirit of defeat. We recognize temptation surrounding us. We acknowledge that the world opposes our values. It becomes easy to pity ourselves, to believe we are victims, to assume we lack the resources to resist.

This mentality contradicts the truth of our position. We must reject this propaganda.

### The Resources We Possess

Consider what Scripture assures us:

> What other nation is so great as to have such righteous decrees and laws as this body of laws I am setting before you today? (Deuteronomy 4:8)

We possess moral principles of extraordinary power. We are not left alone:

> Rejoice in the Lord always. I will say it again: Rejoice! Let your gentleness be evident to all. The Lord is near. Do not be anxious about anything, but in every situation, by prayer and petition, with thanksgiving, present your requests to God. And the peace of God, which transcends all understanding, will guard your hearts and your minds in Christ Jesus. (Philippians 4:4-7)

And most powerfully:

> I can do all this through him who gives me strength. (Philippians 4:13)

We are sealed by the Spirit. We are not conquerors merely; we are "more than conquerors" in Christ (Romans 8:37).

### Playing to Our Strengths

Too often we make the strategic error of competing on the world's terms. We attempt to hide our convictions, to play small, to paint a small target hoping we won't be attacked. If we say nothing, we believe we risk nothing.

Yet silence accomplishes nothing good either.

The reality is this: we have been given tremendous advantages—not in the world's estimation, but in God's truth. When seeking a new position or opportunity, we should not merely check off selection criteria. Instead, we should identify the unique strengths we possess that others lack—the specific qualities that solve the problems our opponents face. These strengths may not be immediately obvious, but once unearthed, they become powerful reasons for success.

We must stop pretending to be smaller than we really are.

### The Call to Courage

The world tells us we are insignificant, a minority, on the wrong side of history, that God is dead. Yet we must stubbornly refuse this propaganda.

We are afraid. We want to minimize ourselves to avoid attack. But fear is not the fruit of the Spirit. God has not given us a spirit of timidity, but of power, love, and a sound mind.

Young people especially must not be looked down upon. The story of David demonstrates that God uses the unexpected, the young, the seemingly unprepared to accomplish His purposes.

## Conclusion

God took an ordinary shepherd—a young man with red hair who played the lyre and wrote psalms, a young man who might have started the school chess club rather than joined the sports team—and transformed him into a warrior. God took the qualities that made David appear weak: his eyes, his experience with animals, his careful aim—and turned them into the very weapons that would bring down a giant.

This is what God does. He takes what the world despises and uses it to accomplish His will. He takes what we see as weakness and reveals it as strength.

You are not the underdog you believe yourself to be. The Lord is with you. You are sealed by His Spirit. The victory is not improbable—it is certain, bit by bit, moment by moment, as you trust in Him who gives you strength.

We are more powerful than we think.
