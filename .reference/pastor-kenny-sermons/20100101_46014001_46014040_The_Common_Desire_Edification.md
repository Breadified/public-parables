# 1 Corinthians 14:1-40

**Title:** The Common Desire: Edification
**Date:** 31 January 2010
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Why Gather as Christians?

When we gather as a church, we gather with purpose. Paul's letter to the Corinthians addresses this directly. Throughout chapters 12-14, he's been building toward a crucial point: what should happen when we come together? Chapter 13 teaches us about love, but chapter 14 finally reveals the why behind our gathering.

## 1. The Heart of Prophecy: Desiring the Right Gifts

> **1 Corinthians 14:1** Follow the way of love and eagerly desire spirituals in order that you may prophesy.

When Paul speaks of prophecy, what does he mean? We often think of prophecy as foretelling the future—predicting what will happen tomorrow. But that's only part of the Old Testament picture.

Jeremiah and Isaiah did predict Israel's exile, and the test of a true prophet was whether his predictions came true. But consider Moses, the greatest prophet of the Old Testament. His most important messages weren't about the future at all. They were about remembering—calling Israel back to what God had already done for them in Egypt and the desert. His entire book of Deuteronomy is essentially three long speeches reminding God's people of His faithfulness.

In the New Testament, the word "prophecy" appears about 28 times. Most of those references are scattered throughout 1 Corinthians 11-14, which makes it challenging to determine the exact meaning. But here's what we can do: we can reverse-engineer the meaning by looking at where prophecy leads and what it accomplishes.

### Edification: The Purpose of All Prophecy

The answer is in the text itself. Look at what Paul says prophecy produces:

> **1 Corinthians 14:3-4** Everyone who prophesies speaks to men for their strengthening, encouragement and comfort... he who prophesies edifies the church.

And again in verse 12:

> **1 Corinthians 14:12** Since you are eager to have spiritual gifts, try to excel in gifts that build up the church.

"Strengthening," "encouraging," "edifying," "building up"—these are all translations of the same root word: *building*. Jesus used this metaphor when He said, "I will build my church" (Matthew 16:18). Paul uses it in Ephesians when he describes the church as God's household, "built on the foundation of the apostles and prophets... being built together to become a dwelling in which God lives by his Spirit" (Ephesians 2:19-20).

Throughout Scripture, this building happens through speech. When we speak truth in love, we build each other up (Ephesians 4:15). When the gospel is preached, foundations are laid and churches are built (Romans 15:20). When we receive the word of grace, it can build us up and give us an inheritance (Acts 20:32). Even in the Old Testament, God commissioned Jeremiah to both tear down and build up through the words He placed in his mouth (Jeremiah 1:9-10).

### So Prophecy Must Be Gospel Speech

If prophecy leads to edification, and speaking the gospel in love leads to edification, then prophecy must be something to do with speaking the gospel in love. It is intelligible speech that builds others up. And that's what we should desire.

What does that look like in our gatherings today? I believe it looks a lot like preaching—but not just what happens from the front. When I give a talk, I'm sharing what God has said in Scripture, reminding you of His promises. Yes, I could tell you the future: Jesus is coming back with absolute certainty. This world will end, and a new world without tears will be created. But that's not the heart of what happens when we gather.

The real power of our gatherings comes from all of us speaking the gospel to one another. Yes, there might be talks for an hour up front, but you will talk to each other for hours—before the gathering, over afternoon tea, at dinner afterward, in your Bible study groups, in your conversations with friends.

**When you read God's word to each other in a Bible study group, you are building each other up.**

**When you encourage a friend who's really struggling to keep persevering in Jesus, you are building her up.**

**When you lovingly rebuke a friend with truth, that builds him up.**

**When you hear difficult news and respond by saying, "Let's take this to God in prayer and ask for His help," you are building the other person up.**

You have the spirituals—the breath of God—that will build us all up. Prophecy is you speaking the words of God to each other. You are desperately important to one another because you are the means by which God builds up His people. This is what we should desire.

### Making Conversation Count

When we talk together, our conversation must go beyond small talk. We can't just catch up about movies and parties and what friends are doing, because we know that it's not plain speaking that builds up—it's the speaking of the gospel. At some point in your conversation, we need to ask each other: What did you learn today? How have you been changed by Scripture? How are you doing as a Christian? What have you been struggling with lately?

Some of you will find this harder than others depending on your personality, but this is essential. If I joined a chess club and never played chess, that would be strange. If I went to the gym and never touched the weights, that would be odd. If we gather in a Christian group and never talk about Christian things, I'd be disappointed.

What builds me up is when you tell me something you learned, when you ask thoughtful questions, when you tell me how Scripture has changed you. When you challenge me toward godliness. When we disagree about something, work through it together, and seek to understand what God's will is on a matter. When we humbly search for the truth in the context of genuine love—that makes me a better Christian, and I believe it will make you better ones too.

## 2. The Problem with Tongues: Why Unintelligibility Destroys Community

> **1 Corinthians 14:2** For anyone who speaks in a tongue does not speak to men but to God. Indeed, no one understands him; he utters mysteries with his spirit.

Prophecy is contrasted sharply with tongues. In Acts 2, at Pentecost, something remarkable happened: the Holy Spirit filled the believers, and "all of them began to speak in other tongues as the Spirit enabled them" (Acts 2:4). But what were these "tongues"? Many churches interpret this as a special heavenly language, but look at what actually happened.

> **Acts 2:6-8** A crowd came together in bewilderment, because each one heard them speaking in his own language... "Then how is it that each of us hears them in our own native language?"

The word "tongue" here means *foreign language*. What made Pentecost so miraculous and beautiful was that Parthians, Medes, Elamites, residents of Mesopotamia, Judea, Cappadocia, Pontus, Asia, Phrygia, Pamphylia, Egypt, Libya, Rome, Crete, and Arabia—all heard the wonders of God declared in their own languages. They were overjoyed that the gospel had become intelligible to them.

### The Problem of Unintelligibility

In Corinth, a major trade city with many languages, some people were speaking in foreign languages that nobody understood. But here's the critical problem:

> **1 Corinthians 14:4** He who speaks in a tongue edifies himself, but he who prophesies edifies the church.

If no one understands what's being said, how can anyone be built up except the speaker? Paul makes this clear through a series of illustrations:

> **1 Corinthians 14:7-9** Even in the case of lifeless things that make sounds, such as the flute or harp, how will anyone know what tune is being played unless there is a distinction in the notes? Again, if the trumpet does not sound a clear call, who will get ready for battle? So it is with you. Unless you speak intelligible words with your tongue, how will anyone know what you are saying? You will just be speaking into the air.

Music must be intelligible to be meaningful. Language must be understood to communicate. And what Paul emphasizes throughout is this: tongues without interpretation do not edify the church.

> **1 Corinthians 14:18-19** I thank God that I speak in tongues more than all of you. But in the church I would rather speak five intelligible words to instruct others than ten thousand words in a tongue.

Five words that are understood are infinitely more valuable than ten thousand words that nobody comprehends.

### Don't Do Your Own Thing

Here's the practical application: when you gather together, don't do your own thing.

If you eat alone while everyone is gathered together, you're not serving anyone (1 Corinthians 11). What's the point of gathering if we're just going to do our own thing—eating in a corner, reading a newspaper?

If you pray in a language nobody understands, it won't be fruitful. If you have a conversation in another language while everyone else is present, nobody knows what's happening. This hurts both the community and the outsider.

Don't do your own thing by being on your mobile phone when everyone is here. Even worse is ignoring the group to answer a call. If it's truly urgent, ask for permission. But out of love and basic etiquette, it's better to let it go to voicemail and call back later.

I know that in our church we sing, and when the music is good, it's tempting to think of it as a personal moment with God—to close your eyes and retreat into yourself. That's nice, but you're not by yourself. There are other people to consider. Imagine a Bible study where, right when things get exciting, someone closes their eyes and zones out. They've disengaged and stopped looking out for the group. If you want that personal, eyes-closed experience, do it at home with the music turned up loud.

If you want to express emotion during our gathering, consider something more community-focused: put your arm around someone, give a sidehug, look each other in the eye and acknowledge that we're brothers and sisters in Christ. This is countercultural. We are selfish, and we don't even realize it.

### The Real Problem with Our Spiritual Gifts Conversations

Too often, when we talk about spiritual gifts, we center on ourselves. We start by asking, "What am I good at? What are my gifts?" We fill out questionnaires trying to discover what we're naturally talented at. But that's doing your own thing—serving yourself, not the body.

Instead, we need to be outward-focused. If the most important person in this room is the other person, then when figuring out how to serve, we should ask: **What needs to be done?** This will yield a much better answer about how to serve the group. We might discover that we serve not by using what we think we're good at—which often means being up front or drumming—but by meeting what the group actually needs. Small, insignificant, thankless things like welcoming a stranger, stacking chairs, taking out rubbish, wiping down a dirty table. Those tasks deserve to be on a spiritual gifts list.

## 3. Tongues Reverse the Gospel: A Theological Problem

There's an even bigger theological issue at stake with unintelligible speech in the church. Paul tells the Corinthians to grow up and listen to the Old Testament about what tongues actually mean. He quotes Isaiah 28:11:

> **1 Corinthians 14:21** In the Law it is written: "Through men of strange tongues and through the lips of foreigners I will speak to this people, but even then they will not listen to me," says the Lord.

This prophecy from Isaiah is about God's judgment. When the nation of Israel heard foreign languages, it meant one thing: the enemy had invaded. A foreign tongue was a sign of conquest and judgment—just like the tower of Babel, where God confused languages as a judgment on human rebellion.

Throughout history, languages have marked conquest. If we all spoke Japanese and German today, you'd know we lost World War II. If Russian, we lost the Cold War. Each conqueror brings their own language. When Jerusalem was under siege, the language a stranger spoke determined whether he was friend or enemy. A strange tongue meant you'd lost the war.

### Tongues and Unbelievers

This is why Paul writes:

> **1 Corinthians 14:22** Tongues, then, are a sign, not for believers but for unbelievers; prophecy, however, is for believers, not for unbelievers.

Tongues represent judgment and confusion—the situation of those stuck under judgment who cannot understand God's word. They don't evangelize through a special holy language. Rather, they represent a barrier: the good news is locked away in incomprehensible speech.

Look at what happens when a church gathers and everyone speaks in unintelligible tongues:

> **1 Corinthians 14:23** So if the whole church comes together and everyone speaks in tongues, and some who do not understand or some unbelievers come in, will they not say that you are out of your mind?

They walk out. The gospel is shut to them.

### The Reversal of Pentecost

But Pentecost was meant to be a reversal of judgment. The word of grace came to everyone. Suddenly, everyone could understand the gospel regardless of their language or background. You didn't need to be Jewish or speak Hebrew to hear salvation. The good news was translated into the languages of the world—the gospel became accessible to all.

If we return to unintelligible language, we reverse that beautiful gift. We make the gospel exclusive again, belonging only to an elite few. Those outside remain under judgment.

But when prophecy—intelligible proclamation of the gospel—fills the gathering, something beautiful happens:

> **1 Corinthians 14:24-25** But if an unbeliever or someone who does not understand comes in while everybody is prophesying, he will be convinced by all that he is a sinner and will be judged by all; and the secrets of his heart will be laid bare. So he will fall down and worship God, exclaiming, "God is really among you!"

This is true Pentecost restored. People hear the truth, the gospel, and they repent. They encounter the mind of Christ revealed through intelligible conversation. The authentic Pentecostal experience is when we speak the gospel in a way people can understand.

## 4. Making Our Gatherings Intelligible

Intelligibility matters. When we speak the gospel clearly, people understand it. When we speak in jargon, inside jokes, or exclusive language, we shut people out.

This doesn't mean we should stop our in-jokes—they're good for community. But we need to explain them so outsiders aren't left confused and excluded. At our Connect gathering, we have strong friendship groups from youth camps and certain high schools, which is wonderful. But we need to ensure we're also being inclusive and intelligible for those coming in for the first time.

Next month in December, I'd love for all of us to invite non-Christian friends. We need to make an extra effort to ensure our time together is intelligible. Michael is coming to give the talks—he's very bright and will use his gifts to intelligently explain the gospel to us and our friends. But that won't do much good unless all of us are prepared to speak to each other, to articulate the gospel in clear and accessible ways so that nothing hinders them from saying, "God is really among you!"

It's another chance to practice what we've learned. The speaking of the gospel is the most important thing we can do. Everyone in this room is important. Everyone is the most important person in this room to someone else. We build by speaking the words of God to each other.

We are trying to edify the church through the intelligent proclamation of the gospel to each other, in pairs and in groups. That's the common desire Paul calls us to—to prophesy, to speak the gospel in love, and to build each other up.
