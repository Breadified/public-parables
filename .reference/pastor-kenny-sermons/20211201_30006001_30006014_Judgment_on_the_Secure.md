# Amos 6:1-14

**Title:** Judgment on the Secure

## Be Safe vs. Be Brave: The Tension

We live in an age obsessed with safety. We rate cars by ANCAAP scores, we install security systems and stickers on our windows, we choose neighborhoods based on insurance premiums. Whole industries—occupational health and safety—exist to protect us. Walk into any room and you'll find fire signs, rails, non-slip mats, rubber corners on table edges. We've built a civilization around the principle of "be safe."

But here's the problem: security can become an idol.

Amos challenges this deeply, showing us what happens when we pursue safety above all else. The prophet speaks to a people who have achieved exactly what they wanted—comfort, peace, security. And their blindness will prove catastrophic.

---

## Part 1: Wanting Security (Amos 6:1, 4-6)

### The Problem: Complacency About God

> Woe to you who are complacent in Zion, and to you who feel secure on Mount Samaria, you notable men of the foremost nation, to whom the people of Israel come!

The word "complacent" means ease—a state of careless comfort. These are the upper class, the powerful and secure. Zion (Jerusalem) and Mount Samaria (Israel's capital) represent fortresses, mountains where people trust they are protected.

The historical context matters: In the 8th century BC, Israel and Judah had recently won military victories. Their enemies had been defeated or were occupied with their own problems. International peace treaties existed. Both kingdoms prospered. Their territory had been enlarged. These weren't delusional people—they had real reasons to feel confident.

### The Object of Security: Comfort

But notice what security meant to them. Verse 1 says "complacent"—they wanted comfort, peace of mind.

> You lie on beds adorned with ivory and lounge on your couches. You dine on choice lambs and fattened calves. You strum away on your harps like David and improvise on musical instruments. You drink wine by the bowlful and use the finest lotions.

This paints a picture of daily luxury. Not occasional indulgence—this was their constant reality. They lived like kings with infinite leisure time to pursue music and art. Every meal was feast-quality. Every possession was the finest available.

To relate this to our own time: imagine spending your summers in air-conditioned comfort on leather lounges, sleeping on chiropractor-approved mattresses, dining on expensive seafood and mangoes at year-end dinners, playing guitar while listening to your favorite artists, with access to Airbnb and fine restaurants whenever you wish.

But here's the crucial difference: they lived this way *every single day, at every meal*. This wasn't a vacation—it was their baseline existence.

### The Price: Indifference to Others' Suffering

This comfort had a cost, though they didn't see it:

> But you do not grieve over the ruin of Joseph.

"Joseph" is shorthand for Israel's largest territory (his sons Ephraim and Manasseh). The ruin of Joseph meant the people were being destroyed by the elites in power. And yet the wealthy remained indifferent.

This recalls Genesis 37: Joseph's own brothers didn't just ignore his suffering—they threw him in a pit and ate a meal while the teenage boy screamed and begged for his life, betrayed by those he trusted. The Israelite elites showed this same heartlessness. They had wealth, security, and comfort while their own people suffered oppression.

Their security meant: having bodyguards and security systems to protect themselves from the poor. Gated complexes. Fortresses. All designed to keep out "the scruffy neighbours."

## Part 2: You Cannot Actually Be Secure (Amos 6:2-3)

### Amos's Challenge

But Amos challenges this illusion directly. He asks: How can you think you can be secure?

> Go to Kalneh and look at it; go from there to great Hamath, and then go down to Gath in Philistia. Are they better off than your two kingdoms? Is their land larger than yours?

He's naming three powerful cities—Kalneh (ancient Babylon), Hamath (a prominent Aramean fortress), and Gath (the Philistine stronghold, home of Goliath). These were major powers. Yet they had all been conquered—by Israel herself under King Uzziah.

The point: if these great cities fell, why do you think *you* are safe?

> You put off the day of disaster.

This is the heart of the matter. They've deferred judgment, convinced themselves it won't happen to them.

### A Troubling Question: Has Anywhere Ever Been Safe?

Consider history. There have been times when certain regions seemed more secure than today. Yet the world has never been truly safe. World War II shattered any illusions of permanent security. Cancer and sudden death still come. Stock markets crash. Businesses fail. Houses—we thought they were permanent—sink into the ground (like Jordan Springs). 

We live in a historically unique moment of relative peace and security, thanks largely to American global involvement. But as international dynamics shift, that's changing. We're "putting off the day of disaster," assuming current conditions will last forever.

### Realistic Expectations About Safety

The application is sobering: be realistic about what safety actually means. It exists on a spectrum, never absolute. We've moved up the scale materially, but fragility remains.

The COVID era exposed our anxiety about safety. We shifted from saying "goodbye" to saying "stay safe"—as if safety were something we could guarantee each other. Yet paradoxically, the pursuit of perfect safety created other dangers: increased policing, government overreach, infringement of freedom.

**The truth is:** don't expect to keep your children completely safe, or yourself. The best we can do is genuinely effective: stay married, limit screens, read the Bible to them, instill a work ethic, get them to do something boring every day. That covers about 99 percent. Everything else is often just anxiety disguised as responsibility.

## Part 3: The Result—Terror (Amos 6:3, 7-14)

### A Reign of Terror Arrives

In pursuing security, they actually bring near the opposite:

> And bring near a reign of terror.

The Hebrew word "reign" (literally "throne" or "seat") implies dominion and rule. "Terror" is violence. The Greek word *hamas* appears here—the same word found in Genesis 6:11 when the earth was "filled with violence" before the flood.

They wanted security. Instead, judgment comes as a reign of violence and oppression.

### The Cost of Safety

Here's a difficult truth: there is a cost to safety. When you pursue it absolutely, you sacrifice other things:

- You won't marry (too risky)
- You won't have children (unpredictable)
- You won't serve others (safer to stay isolated)
- You'll skip fellowship (someone might be sick)
- You'll abandon service (better to play it safe)

We've seen this practically during COVID. The pursuit of perfect safety came with a price in human connection, mental health, and spiritual community. Some said goodbye to church attendance for "safety."

But Amos reveals something deeper: their real problem wasn't external threats. It was that they'd become secure *from God himself*. They wanted safety *from his judgment*. But God was coming anyway.

### Judgment Falls

The judgment is described in brutal detail:

> Therefore you will be among the first to go into exile; your feasting and lounging will end.

The very luxuries they cherished—beds of ivory, wine bowls, silk lounges—will be taken. All of it, gone.

> The Sovereign Lord has sworn by himself—the Lord God Almighty declares: "I abhor the pride of Jacob and detest his fortresses; I will deliver up the city and everything in it."

Notice: God specifically detests the fortresses they trusted. Their security becomes their shame.

> If ten people are left in one house, they too will die.

Even the remnant won't survive. The judgment is total. Earlier (Amos 5:3), only a remnant of ten remained. Now even they perish. The same bodies that indulged in complacency now face death.

> And if the relative who comes to carry the bodies out of the house asks anyone hiding there, "Is anyone else with you?" and he says, "No," then he will go on to say, "Hush! We must not mention the name of the Lord."

In their terror, God's very name becomes a curse to avoid. They wanted to keep God out—and he's coming in through judgment.

> For the Lord has given the command, and he will smash the great house into pieces and the small house into bits.

The great mansions they built for security, the furniture they adorned with ivory, all smashed to pieces. Every symbol of their pride is destroyed. This is civilization being unmade.

### The Root Error: Attributing Success to Self

Amos uses strange metaphors:

> Do horses run on the rocky crags? Does one plow the sea with oxen?

These are absurdities—of course they don't. Yet the Israelites' military expansion was equally improbable. A tiny nation, surrounded by enemies, expanding its borders? This couldn't happen by normal means. It could only happen by God's power.

> But you have turned justice into poison and the fruit of righteousness into bitterness. You who rejoice in the conquest of Lo Debar and say, "Did we not take Karnaim by our own strength?"

Lo Debar means "nothing." Karnaim means "power" or "horns." They boasted about conquering insignificant places, taking all the credit themselves. They'd completely misread their own success.

Israel had interpreted prosperity as independence from God. They thought: "We achieved this. Our strength did this. Our military, our wisdom, our power."

But they'd forgotten the gift-giver. God had given them peace, prosperity, wealth, luxuries. They took it for granted, used it only for themselves, denied it to others, and grew complacent.

### The Reversal

God's response is to reverse their fortunes entirely:

> For the Lord God Almighty declares, "I will stir up a nation against you, Israel, that will oppress you all the way from Lebo Hamath to the valley of the Arabah."

The territory Jeroboam II had conquered—from Lebo Hamath to the Dead Sea—will become a corridor of oppression. The enemy will press them from border to border. The great fortresses they trusted will be delivered to the Assyrians. The judgment takes the form of civilizational collapse.

## Part 4: The Parallel Truth—The Gospel Response

### The Rich Fool

Jesus tells a parable that echoes Amos perfectly:

> A certain rich man's ground yielded an abundant harvest. He thought to himself, "What shall I do? I have no place to store my crops." Then he said, "This is what I'll do. I will tear down my barns and build bigger ones, and there I will store my surplus grain. And I'll say to myself, 'You have plenty of grain laid up for many years. Take life easy; eat, drink and be merry.'" But God said to him, "You fool! This very night your life will be demanded from you. Then who will get what you have prepared for yourself?" This is how it will be with whoever stores up things for themselves but is not rich toward God.

The man had exactly what he wanted: security, comfort, endless leisure. And God calls him a fool. Not because he was wealthy, but because he'd made himself the end goal. He'd forgotten God entirely.

### The Radical Generosity of Christ

Here's where the gospel reframes everything: we are those who have the riches of God—both materially and spiritually. We have the gospel, the good news of what Jesus has done for us on the cross.

Jesus possessed infinite divine power and riches. He could have held onto them, secured himself, maximized his comfort. Instead, he lowered himself to become a powerless baby, vulnerable and dependent. He lived a life of service and sacrifice. He died on a cross.

This is the miracle of miracles—the impossible made possible through his sacrifice for us.

## Application: Be Brave, Not Secure

We cannot be complacent. We have the riches of God, and we must steward them differently than ancient Israel did.

Yes, take time to rest. You've worked hard and deserve rest. But don't become complacent about the gospel.

Christmas and summer are seasons when people are more open to church, when they have breathing room from their busy years. Your friends have been working hard and haven't had time to think about spiritual things. But now they're freer. We cannot rest from these opportunities.

These seasons also bring programs and activities. Don't take them for granted. Make the best use of them.

Bring a friend to Christmas Eve. Invite someone to experience the gospel. Store up riches not for yourself but be rich toward God—rich in faith, rich in service, rich in generosity.

The alternative—the complacency of the wealthy Israelites—brought judgment. But grace, lavishly poured out through Christ, calls us to something far more beautiful: to be brave enough to trust God with our security, and generous enough to share his riches with those around us.
