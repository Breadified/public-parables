# 1 Thessalonians 5:12-28

**Title:** God of Peace
**Date:** 21 September 2014
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The End of a Letter

As we come to the end of 1 Thessalonians, we arrive at what might seem like random closing advice. Biblical letters often appear to end with disconnected fragments—a staccato of instructions that sound like last-minute fatherly wisdom. You know the feeling: when you're leaving for camp and your parents suddenly remember a dozen random things to tell you. "Remember your vitamins! Don't forget your phone charger! Bring water!"

But we shouldn't assume Paul is being careless or random. A thoughtful letter—especially from an apostle who had time to compose it—deserves the benefit of the doubt. We should look for purpose. And indeed, these closing verses form a coherent summary of the entire letter's message:

**Love your leaders. Love each other. Love God.**

## 1. Love Your Leaders (vv. 12-13a, 25-28)

> Now we ask you, brothers and sisters, to acknowledge those who work hard among you, who care for you in the Lord and who admonish you. Hold them in the highest regard in love because of their work. (vv. 12-13)

When Paul speaks of those who "work hard," "care," and "admonish," he isn't describing three different types of people. He's painting a portrait of Christian leadership itself—the very kind of leadership he modeled for the Thessalonians.

### Working Hard

Paul himself exemplified this. In 1 Thessalonians 2:9, he reminds them: "Surely you remember, brothers, our toil and hardship; we worked night and day in order not to be a burden to anyone while we preached the gospel of God to you." Leading well requires sacrifice and effort. Our leaders—many serving voluntarily without pay—pour themselves out. They lose sleep ensuring the youth are cared for before they rest. They wake first to prepare breakfast and manage the day's activities. They make difficult decisions about resources and priorities. It's hard, often thankless work.

### Caring for You

To care (Greek: *proistemi*) means to oversee, to be concerned for your wellbeing. Paul describes Christian leadership this way in Romans 12:8 and 1 Timothy 3:12, 5:17. A leader doesn't rule over your entire life, but "in the Lord"—within the church community, they shepherd you toward Christ-likeness.

### Admonishing You

To admonish means to warn, to critique, to call right from wrong. When a leader brings something to your attention—"Hey, what happened there? Why did you do that?"—they're not stalking you or picking a fight. They're caring enough to help you grow toward Christ-likeness.

Hebrews 13:7 captures this beautifully: "Remember your leaders, who spoke the word of God to you. Consider the outcome of their way of life and imitate their faith."

### Our Response: Hold Them in Highest Regard

The phrase "highest regard" carries the sense of "exceedingly abundantly"—the same word Paul uses in Ephesians 3:20 and 1 Thessalonians 3:10. When you hold leaders in highest regard, you're recognizing that they pray for you with that same intensity: "Night and day we pray most earnestly that we may see you again and supply what is lacking in your faith."

To hold them in highest regard means to obey them—and yes, we may resist that word, but there's nothing sinister here. Leaders simply want people who will follow. As Hebrews 13:17 teaches: "Obey your leaders and submit to their authority. They keep watch over you as men who must give an account. Obey them so that their work will be a joy, not a burden, for that would be of no advantage to you."

It's miserable to lead a group that doesn't want to follow. Imagine organizing an event, asking for input via email, receiving silence, then going ahead with your best judgment—only to be met with complaints about the activity itself. When you delegate to the loudest complainers, they accuse you of not leading. When you make a decision, they say you're not listening. It's an impossible position. And when leaders burn out because their service isn't received joyfully, the whole church suffers.

### How to Honor Your Leaders

**Show up.** We don't organize heaps of activities—you're all busy people. But Sunday is our essential weekly gathering. Beyond that, we have the End of Year Dinner and church camp. For those wanting to grow deeper in discipleship and leadership, we have serving groups that meet on weeknights. Commit to at least one level. Leaders organize "all the time" for your benefit; don't add to their burden by chronic non-attendance.

**Encourage them.** Don't wait until burnout has set in. Give leaders the benefit of the doubt. Look at their track record. If they've been faithful, trust them. If you think you could do better, prove it—work your way up. Remember: one mark of a good leader is being a good follower first.

**Pray for them.** (v. 25) "Brothers and sisters, pray for us." Leaders need intercession for the heavy responsibility they carry.

## 2. Love Each Other (vv. 13b-15, 23-24)

> And we urge you, brothers and sisters, warn those who are idle and disruptive, encourage the disheartened, help the weak, be patient with everyone. Make sure that nobody pays back wrong for wrong, but always strive to do what is good for each other and for everyone else. (vv. 14-15)

Notice the progression: we're called to warn each other, just as leaders warn us. The "idle and disruptive" echo Paul's earlier instruction in 4:11 to "lead a quiet life" and "work with your hands." Idleness often breeds restlessness and disruption—as we see in cities with high youth unemployment, which become centers of civil disturbance.

But our care for each other extends beyond correction:

- **Encourage the disheartened.** Those who are overwhelmed, small-souled, crushed by problems.
- **Help the weak.** Hold fast to those who are dropping away, burning out, growing faint.
- **Be patient with everyone.** Never repay wrong with wrong; instead, pursue what is good for each other and everyone else.

### Living in Peace

This is what it truly means to live in peace with each other. Peace isn't the absence of conflict—it's the cessation of war. It's the realization that this kingdom is not ultimately *our* kingdom, so we don't have to fight constantly for our patch. We belong to another kingdom.

If there's unresolved conflict between you and another believer, go directly to that person and seek reconciliation (Matthew 18:15 principle). If that doesn't resolve it, bring a witness. As a last resort, bring it to the church. Don't go around talking to others first; go straight to the source.

### God Is a God of Peace

> May God himself, the God of peace, sanctify you through and through. May your whole spirit, soul and body be kept blameless at the coming of our Lord Jesus Christ. The one who calls you is faithful, and he will do it. (vv. 23-24)

The great war isn't between nations—it's between God and rebellious humanity. We are insurrectionists against the Creator's rule, and the wages of sin is death. But Jesus is the Prince of Peace. On the cross, He purchased peace between God and man with His own blood.

And here's the crucial connection: **peace between God and man produces peace between man and man.** As God has forgiven us through Christ, we forgive others. This isn't mere sentiment—it's the overflow of reconciliation with our Creator.

**Are you still at war with God?** Until that peace is established in your heart, peace with others will remain shallow and conditional.

## 3. Love God (vv. 16-22)

> Rejoice always, pray continually, give thanks in all circumstances; for this is God's will for you in Christ Jesus. (vv. 16-18)

What looks like rapid-fire advice actually sounds like Paul describing himself. Notice how these instructions echo throughout the letter:

- **Rejoice always** (3:9): "How can we thank God enough for you in return for all the joy we have in the presence of our God because of you?"
- **Pray continually** (1:2-3): "We always thank God for all of you, mentioning you in our prayers. We continually remember before our God and Father your work produced by faith, your labor prompted by love, and your endurance inspired by hope."
- **Give thanks in all circumstances** (2:13): "And we also thank God continually because, when you received the word of God...you accepted it not as the word of men, but as it actually is, the word of God."

Paul isn't just issuing orders—he's modeling the life he's calling them toward. This is authentic leadership: *do what I do.*

### The Antidote to Despair

Rejoicing, praying, and giving thanks are the antidote to despondency. Count your blessings. Adjust your attitude toward God. This practice kept Paul alive despite persecution, relentless work, and overwhelming concern for the churches. 

There's a difference between a pessimist and an optimist. Two shoe salesmen were sent to Africa to assess the market. The pessimist called home: "Nobody wears shoes here. I'm coming back." The optimist called: "Nobody wears shoes here—send help!" Same situation, completely different response.

Or consider two boys in a room. One filled with the latest toys breaks down crying: "They'll break!" The other faces a room full of manure, pauses, then dives in enthusiastically: "Where's the pony?!"

**Rejoice always. Pray continually. Give thanks in all circumstances.** This is God's will for you in Christ Jesus.

### Do Not Quench the Spirit

> Do not quench the Spirit. Do not treat prophecies with contempt but test them all; hold on to what is good, reject every kind of evil. (vv. 19-22)

"Quench" means to extinguish. Some debate whether Paul is referring to charismatic phenomena, but the context clarifies his meaning. When God's Spirit is mentioned earlier in 1 Thessalonians:

- **4:8**: "Therefore, he who rejects this instruction does not reject man but God, who gives you his Holy Spirit."
- **1:6**: "You became imitators of us and of the Lord; in spite of severe suffering, you welcomed the message with the joy given by the Holy Spirit."

God's Spirit comes with God's Word. When you believe the Word, you receive the Spirit—the joy of the Spirit. **To not quench the Spirit means: do not quench the Word.** Specifically, Paul's teaching, which calls you to be ready for the day of judgment.

Jesus spoke of this readiness in Matthew 24:42-51 and the parable of the ten virgins (Matthew 25:1-13). The foolish virgins let their lamps go out—they weren't ready. The wise kept oil and were prepared when the bridegroom arrived.

When Paul says "Do not treat prophecies with contempt but test them all," he's referring to God-given messages—particularly his own apostolic writings. In 1 Thessalonians 2:4, Paul claims his apostleship: "On the contrary, we speak as men approved by God to be entrusted with the gospel." The standard for future letters would be those written by those who witnessed the risen Jesus and could testify to that historical reality.

So: **hold on to what is good** (as in 1 Corinthians 15:2, "hold firmly to the word") and **reject every kind of evil**—stay away from sexual immorality, idolatry, and anything that pulls you from God's word.

Don't ignore God's instruction. Don't get distracted from it. The day of judgment is coming. Jesus will return with His holy ones—us. It will not do to be found living carelessly on that day, surprised, our lamps extinguished.

**Be ready.**

---

> Greet all God's people with a holy kiss. I charge you before the Lord to have this letter read to all the brothers and sisters. The grace of our Lord Jesus Christ be with you. (vv. 26-28)

Though this letter ends, it marks the beginning of righteousness for those who receive it. Let us be people of the day—loving our leaders, loving each other, and supremely, loving God.
