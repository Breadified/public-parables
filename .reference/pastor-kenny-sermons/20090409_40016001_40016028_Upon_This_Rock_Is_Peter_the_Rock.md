# Matthew 16:1-28

**Title:** Upon This Rock: Is Peter the Rock?

**Date:** April 9, 2009

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Question

In Matthew 16:18, Jesus makes a remarkable declaration: "You are Peter, and on this rock I will build my church." The challenge lies in understanding what Jesus means by this statement. Is Peter himself the rock upon which the church is built, or does the word-play between "Peter" (petros in Greek) and "rock" (petra) point to something deeper?

This is not merely an academic question. The interpretation we choose has profound theological implications for how we understand church authority, apostolic succession, and the very foundation of Christ's church.

## Four Major Interpretations

### 1. The Roman Catholic Position

The Catholic interpretation argues that petros and petra are essentially identical in meaning. Therefore, Jesus is saying directly: "You are the rock, and upon you I will build my church." This interpretation leads to the conclusion that Peter is the founder of the earthly church and the first pope. The keys given to Peter in Matthew 16:19 are interpreted as keys to the kingdom—authority that passes to his successors in an unbroken line.

As Reformed Christians, we must respectfully disagree with this conclusion. Scripture reveals only one Pope: Jesus Christ himself. More significantly, the New Testament provides no historical evidence of Peter as the head of a Roman church, nor does it suggest any intention of establishing apostolic succession as an ongoing ecclesiastical structure.

### 2. The Protestant Distinction: Peter as Foundation for His Confession

Many Protestant scholars argue for a grammatical distinction between petros (a movable stone or pebble) and petra (an immovable rock, boulder, or cliff). This distinction was particularly clear in classical Attic Greek. If we follow this reasoning, then Peter—whose name literally means "pebble"—cannot be the rock on which the church is built.

Instead, the rock refers to Peter's confession: "You are the Messiah, the Son of the living God" (Matthew 16:16). The church, then, is built upon the confession that Jesus is Christ. Anyone who makes this confession becomes part of Christ's church. As Matthew Henry wrote, "This is that great truth upon which I will build my church." Without this foundational confession, the church ceases to exist. Historical research shows that forty-four out of eighty-five ancient commentators held this interpretation.

### 3. The Rock as Christ Himself

Another significant tradition identifies Jesus as the rock. This interpretation rests on several pillars of evidence:

**Old Testament Foundation:** The Hebrew Scriptures consistently refer to God as Rock. This symbolic language never refers to any human being, but always to God or the Messiah. Consider Deuteronomy 32:4, where God is called "the Rock, his work is perfect"; Psalm 18:2, "The Lord is my rock"; and Isaiah 28:16, where the Messiah is described as a precious cornerstone.

**Jesus' Self-Identification:** In Matthew 21:42, Jesus applies Psalm 118:22 to himself, identifying himself as the rejected stone that becomes the cornerstone—a clear messianic claim.

**Paul's Teaching:** In 1 Corinthians 10:4, Paul explicitly identifies Jesus as the spiritual rock that followed Israel in the wilderness. Furthermore, in Ephesians 2:20, Paul teaches that the church's foundation is "the apostles and prophets"—understanding this to mean their divinely inspired teaching concerning Christ, not their persons or offices.

**Peter's Own Witness:** Most remarkably, Peter himself never claims to be the rock or foundation of the church. Instead, in 1 Peter 2:4-8 and Acts 4:10-11, Peter identifies Christ as the "chief cornerstone" from Psalm 118:22.

### 4. The Recent Scholarly Consensus: Peter as the Rock

However, the latter two interpretations face a structural problem. If the rock in verse 18b does not refer to Peter in verse 18a, why does the text immediately give Peter the keys to the kingdom and the authority to bind and loose? This seems linguistically and contextually awkward. Modern scholars increasingly argue that petros and petra were meant to carry similar meaning.

## The Case for Peter as the Rock

### Semantic Evidence

Several factors support the view that petros and petra were not meaningfully distinct in Koine Greek (the Greek dialect of the New Testament):

- The semantic distinction between petros and petra was primarily a feature of classical Attic Greek, not Koine Greek used in the New Testament.
- Even in Attic Greek, there are instances where petros refers to something larger than a pebble.
- The authoritative lexicons of New Testament Greek continue to note this semantic distinction, suggesting the meaning difference remained relevant to Matthew's audience.
- Most significantly, Jesus almost certainly spoke these words in Aramaic, where there is only one word for stone, pebble, or rock: *Kepha* (or *Cephas*). When Matthew translated this into Greek, he could not use the feminine noun *petra* as a male name, so he employed *petros*, the closest masculine form. This practical necessity rather than theological distinction may explain his word choice.

### Structural Evidence

The structure of Matthew 16:17-19 supports this interpretation. The passage contains three thematic units, each with parallel sub-clauses:

> **Matthew 16:17** – Jesus replied, "Blessed are you, Simon son of Jonah, for this was not revealed to you by man, but by my Father in heaven."
>
> **Matthew 16:18** – And I tell you that you are Peter, and on this rock I will build my church, and the gates of Hades will not overcome it.
>
> **Matthew 16:19** – I will give you the keys of the kingdom of heaven; whatever you bind on earth will be bound in heaven, and whatever you loose on earth will be loosed in heaven.

The logical progression suggests that verse 18b naturally refers back to verse 18a. The consistent structure implies Peter and the rock are meant to be understood as referring to the same person.

### Grammatical Evidence

The Greek demonstrative pronoun *taute* combined with *kai* creates the meaning "this very" or "this same." This grammatical construction points back to the preceding noun, indicating that the rock Jesus refers to in the second part of verse 18 must be the same rock as in the first part. Therefore, if Peter is the first rock, he must also be the second.

## Conclusion: Peter as the Rock—With Important Caveats

Based on grammatical, structural, and semantic evidence, Jesus is indeed calling Peter "the rock" and building his church upon him. Peter, as the first apostle to recognize Jesus as the Christ, receives special responsibility and authority.

This conclusion must be understood correctly, however. It does **not** support the theological claims of Roman Catholicism. History provides no evidence of Peter as a pope, no evidence of apostolic succession, and no biblical support for Roman ecclesiastical supremacy. Even Catholic historians acknowledge that no papal office existed in the first century. As Francis A. Sullivan writes, the threefold structure of church ministry with one bishop does not appear in the New Testament. Peter was never the first pope, nor was he Roman Catholic.

We honor Peter—many of our churches bear his name—but his authority was personal and historically unique. His special commission must not be read as establishing an eternal office that passes to successors. If you read Peter's own letters, you discover that he taught all Christians are royal priests, not subjects of a hierarchical structure.

## Understanding Peter's Special Role

### Peter's Prominence in Scripture

Peter is consistently presented as first among the disciples:

- He is always listed first among the apostles
- He is the leading character in numerous Gospel narratives
- He attempts to imitate Jesus and defend him, even with a sword
- He serves as the spokesperson for the apostolic community
- He receives a special commission from the risen Jesus
- He dominates the first half of Acts, guiding the selection of Matthias, preaching to the Jews, and later bringing the gospel to the Gentiles through Cornelius

All of this is entirely consistent with Jesus' declaration that Peter would be the rock upon which he builds his church.

### A Word of Caution for Our Application

We must be careful not to read this passage as if it speaks directly to our own time. The danger lies in taking a historically unique declaration meant for Peter and attempting to locate a continuing office or authority that extends through the centuries. Peter's role was special, personal, and unrepeatable. There is only one rock, and that particular foundation belongs to that moment in history and that man.

### The Fullness of the Interpretation

It is worth noting that the other interpretations need not be entirely rejected. Peter's identity as the rock can incorporate them:

- Peter receives the keys—the symbol of teaching authority. The apostolic teaching is the key that opens the kingdom. When the Pharisees shut people out of the kingdom, they do so through false teaching.
- Peter's authority to bind and loose reflects the power of apostolic teaching. This authority becomes meaningful only through the proclamation of Jesus as Christ.
- The early church fathers—both Latin and Greek writers such as St. John Chrysostom—understood the "foundation rock" as applying both to Peter personally and symbolically to his confession of faith, as well as to Christ's promise applying more generally to all twelve apostles and the church at large.

## The Keys and Authority

When Jesus gives Peter the keys of the kingdom, he is granting him authority rooted in teaching. The keys represent access and understanding. The apostolic teaching concerning Christ is what opens the kingdom to those who believe. Peter's authority to bind and loose—to declare what is permitted and forbidden—flows directly from his role as a teacher of the gospel.

In Acts, Peter exercises this authority precisely as he preaches Jesus as Christ and calls people to faith. His special responsibility finds its expression in apostolic proclamation, not in ecclesiastical hierarchy.
