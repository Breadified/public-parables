# Matthew 18:15-20:16

**Title:** Proclaiming the Son Through Forgiveness  
**Date:** 22 February 2009  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction: The Weight of Irreversible Things

Forgiveness speaks to something deeply human—the horror of irreversible circumstances we long to undo. We've all experienced moments we wish we could reverse: throwing out something precious without realizing it, the devastation of a first car accident, words spoken in anger that severed a relationship forever, or a selfish act that wounded someone deeply. For some of us, the wound is even greater—parents divorcing, a circumstance beyond our control that shaped our entire world.

Jesus addresses this reality head-on in Matthew 18-20, weaving together three interconnected themes: marriage and faithfulness, the reversal of worldly values, and the radical call to forgive.

## Part One: Unfaithful Israel (Matthew 19:1-29)

### The Question of Divorce

The Pharisees approach Jesus with a test: "Is it lawful for a man to divorce his wife for any and every reason?" They're not genuinely seeking truth—they're testing him, trying to trap him into contradicting Scripture or popular practice.

Jesus cuts through the confusion with clarity rooted in God's original design:

> "Haven't you read that at the beginning the Creator made them male and female, and said, 'For this reason a man will leave his father and mother and be united to his wife, and the two will become one flesh'? So they are no longer two, but one. Therefore what God has joined together, let man not separate."

This isn't new teaching. It echoes throughout Scripture—Genesis 1:27 declares male and female created in God's image; Genesis 2:24 establishes the one-flesh union. The prophet Malachi reinforces it powerfully:

> "I hate divorce," says the Lord God of Israel, "and I hate a man's covering himself with violence as well as with his garment."

God's intention is crystal clear: marriage is for life.

### The Exception That Isn't Really an Exception

But the Pharisees push back: "Why then did Moses command that a man give his wife a certificate of divorce?" 

Jesus explains the heart of the matter:

> "Moses permitted you to divorce your wives because your hearts were hard. But it was not this way from the beginning. I tell you that anyone who divorces his wife, except for marital unfaithfulness, and marries another woman commits adultery."

Here's where careful reading matters. Matthew includes an exception clause—"except for marital unfaithfulness"—but Mark's version omits it entirely. Why would Matthew alone preserve this exception?

The answer lies earlier in Matthew's gospel, at Jesus's birth narrative. When Mary is found to be with child before her union with Joseph is consummated, Matthew describes Joseph as "a righteous man" who "did not want to expose her to public disgrace" and "had in mind to divorce her quietly." Notice the language: Joseph wants to "loosen" his betrothed. Matthew uses the word *porneia* here—a term that can describe illicit sexual conduct outside of marriage, distinct from adultery within a marriage.

Matthew's exception isn't really an exception to the no-divorce rule. It's addressing a specific situation of broken engagement, using specialized language to clarify his meaning without confusing his Jewish audience. The principle stands uncompromised: **marriage is for life.**

### A Serious Word for Believers

This teaching is radical. The disciples themselves react with shock: "If this is the situation between a husband and wife, it is better not to marry."

And Jesus doesn't soften it:

> "Not everyone can accept this word, but only those to whom it has been given. For some are eunuchs because they were born that way; others were made that way by men; and others have renounced marriage because of the kingdom of heaven. The one who can accept this should accept it."

**To those considering marriage: take it seriously.** If you're going out with someone, you're seriously discerning whether you could marry them for life. If you're willing to marry them, you're making a covenant commitment that transcends feelings, circumstances, and even the other person's faithfulness.

We live in an age that treats commitment as optional. I've watched young Christian leaders—conservative, faithful, from solid churches—marry and then divorce within years. One is now dating again; the other has remarried. It's heartbreaking, and it's wrong.

Here's the hard truth: **If the other person becomes unfaithful, you don't have permission to date or remarry.** You may need to separate for safety and sanity, but you remain one flesh. You cannot simply move on as though you had a bad car accident and bought a new vehicle. You've lost your legs—moving on isn't an option.

Your call is to remain committed even when the other person is not. Wait patiently. Pray for their repentance. Endure. Why? Because God remained faithful to us when we were unfaithful. He waited for our repentance. We owe no less to our covenant partner.

If this is too hard—and for some it will be—then don't marry. Singleness is a gift that frees you from this burden. Better to remain unmarried than to marry without the willingness to commit for life.

## Part Two: Israel Coming Last (Matthew 19:30-20:16)

### The Reversal of All Values

After the rich young man walks away, grieving because he "had great wealth," Jesus tells his disciples:

> "I tell you the truth, many who are first will be last, and many who are last will be first."

Then he tells a parable that illustrates God's upside-down kingdom:

A vineyard owner hires workers throughout the day—at dawn, at the third hour, at the sixth hour, at the ninth hour, and finally at the eleventh hour (just one hour before sunset). When payday comes, he pays all of them equally: one denarius each.

The all-day workers are furious. "These men who were hired last worked only one hour, and you have made them equal to us who have borne the burden of the work and the heat of the day!"

The owner's response cuts to the heart of grace:

> "Friend, I am not being unfair to you. Didn't you agree to work for a denarius? Take your pay and go. I want to give the man who was hired last the same as I gave you. Don't I have the right to do what I want with my own money? Or are you envious because I am generous?"

**The principle:** Everyone receives the same reward—not based on effort or seniority, but on the owner's gracious choice. Those who expect privilege based on their status find themselves last. Those who thought themselves forgotten or worthless receive equal honor.

This is a picture of what happens to Israel. For centuries, Israel possessed the covenant, the Law, the prophets. They were "first." But they refused to repent. They refused to receive their King. Meanwhile, the Gentiles—hired at the eleventh hour—would receive the same inheritance, the same salvation, the same standing before God. Israel's refusal to accept Jesus means they come last. The first become last, and the last become first.

### Application to Our Lives

If you're not willing to be "last" in the world's eyes—to lose status, comfort, and recognition for the sake of Christ—then you're not truly following him. The call of discipleship requires releasing what the world calls "first" and embracing what God calls "last."

## Part Three: Forgiveness and Forgiving (Matthew 18:15-35)

### The Process of Reconciliation

Jesus establishes a clear pathway for dealing with sin in the community:

> "If your brother sins against you, go and show him his fault, just between the two of you. If he listens to you, you have won your brother over. But if he will not listen, take one or two others along, so that 'every matter may be established by the testimony of two or three witnesses.' If he refuses to listen to them, tell it to the church; and if he refuses to listen even to the church, treat him as you would a pagan or a tax collector."

Notice what this requires:
- **Courage to address sin directly** rather than gossip or avoid the person
- **Humility to pursue reconciliation** rather than judgment
- **Submission to the church community** in matters of unrepentant sin
- **Clarity on authority:** Whatever the church binds or looses on earth reflects heaven's verdict

This is binding and loosing—not magical power, but the authority to declare God's mind on matters of doctrine and practice. When the church acts in discernment, heaven backs it up.

### Peter's Question and Jesus's Answer

Peter interrupts: "Lord, how many times shall I forgive my brother when he sins against me? Up to seven times?"

Seven was considered generous in Jewish thought. Jesus shatters that arithmetic:

> "I tell you, not seven times, but seventy-seven times."

This isn't a literal number. It means: **forgive without keeping count. Forgive infinitely. Forgive the way God forgives you.**

### The Parable of the Unmerciful Servant

Jesus illustrates the heart of forgiveness through a powerful story:

A king settles accounts with his servants. One servant owes ten thousand talents—an impossibly vast sum, probably equal to decades of wages for thousands of workers. The servant cannot pay. The king orders him, his family, and all his possessions sold to repay the debt.

The servant falls on his knees begging: "Be patient with me, and I will pay back everything."

But he can't. The debt is impossible. Yet the king is moved with compassion and does something extraordinary—he **cancels the entire debt** and releases the servant.

Forgiven. Free. Completely.

But moments later, this same servant encounters a fellow servant who owes him a hundred denarii—a small debt, perhaps a day's wages. He grabs him by the throat and demands payment. When his fellow servant makes the same plea—"Be patient with me, and I will pay you back"—the forgiven servant refuses. He has the man thrown into prison.

When the king hears what happened, he is enraged:

> "You wicked servant! I canceled all that debt of yours because you begged me to. Shouldn't you have had mercy on your fellow servant just as I had on you?"

The king turns him over to the jailers to be tortured until he pays back everything he owed.

Then Jesus applies it directly:

> "This is how my heavenly Father will treat each of you unless you forgive your brother from your heart."

### Why This Matters for Israel—and for Us

Israel received enormous mercy from God. Generation after generation, despite their unfaithfulness, God called them back. He sent prophets. He offered restoration. Yet Israel refused to extend that same mercy to the Gentiles—those they saw as spiritually indebted to them.

The same pattern repeats in every Christian community. We've been forgiven an impossibly vast debt—our sin, our rebellion, our spiritual bankruptcy. Christ paid it all. Yet we hold grudges. We gossip. We refuse reconciliation. We become like the unmerciful servant, torturing others with unforgiveness while enjoying the freedom Christ purchased for us.

## Practical Implications: Proclaiming the Son Through Forgiveness

### For Non-Christians

The reality is stark: people around you are still in chains. They haven't heard the gospel. They're condemned to an eternity of horror separated from God. When was the last time you told someone about Jesus? Once a few years? Once a year? Once a month? Weekly? Daily?

You say you love people, but love that doesn't proclaim Christ's forgiveness and redemption is sentimentality, not love. It's a lost sheep left in the wilderness. Don't let university, new friendships, or distraction rob you of the urgency. These people matter. Eternity matters.

### For Christians

You who follow Christ face different temptations: comfort, pleasure, distraction. University ahead with more friends and more money than you know what to do with. Relationships that draw your focus away from Jesus. The rich young man's struggle is your struggle—will you give God up for the pleasures the world offers?

**Be intentional.** Follow the Son. Listen to the Son. Don't sacrifice your commitment to him on the altar of worldly success.

And **proclaim the Son through forgiveness.** This is radical discipleship:
- Forgive the Christians you love and the Christians you struggle to love
- Practice the reconciliation process Jesus outlined in Matthew 18:15
- Stop the chain mail of gossip and church politics
- Have courage to lead others toward reconciliation rather than becoming a sounding board for complaint
- Declare heaven's verdict: forgiveness is available; chains can be loosen through the gospel

## Closing: Standing Bruised but Still Standing

My prayer for you is this: on that last day, when we all stand before God, we'll be bruised by the world but still standing. Faithful. Forgiven. Forgiving.

Do you forgive? Not just once, but seventy-seven times? Not just those who ask, but even those who don't? Not just those you love, but those you hate?

Will you proclaim the Son? Will you tell people about his forgiveness? Will you live like someone who has been forgiven an impossibly vast debt?

The time is now. The harvest is urgent. And heaven is watching to see if we'll bind and loose as God has bound and loosed for us.
