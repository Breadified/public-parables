# Exodus 32:1-35

**Title:** Australian Idol — Who Do We Want to Be Like?  
**Date:** September 20, 2024  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## The Three Transformations

This passage reveals three dangerous spiritual movements: the Israelites becoming like their idols, Moses becoming like God, and ultimately, God becoming like us through Christ.

---

## Part 1: Becoming Like Our Idols (Verses 1-10, 21-25)

### The Fear Behind Idolatry

Moses has been gone forty days on Mount Sinai—a deliberately long period symbolizing waiting and testing. The people, anxious and vulnerable in the desert, grow impatient. Fear drives them to demand a god they can see and control.

> "Come, make us gods who will go before us. As for this fellow Moses who brought us up out of Egypt, we don't know what has happened to him." (v. 1)

Aaron responds by crafting a golden calf from the people's earrings—a molten idol shaped by human hands. The people declare: "These are your gods, Israel, who brought you up out of Egypt" (v. 4).

### Wrong God, Wrong Way

This sin breaks both the first and second commandments. The calf itself—possibly influenced by Egyptian worship of Apis or symbolizing prosperity and strength—is fundamentally wrong. But more critically, it represents the deeper error: **the god you make is a different god than the God who made you**.

When we craft our own gods, we decide what they look like, what qualities matter, what values count. And inevitably, these are simply our values reflected back at us. We make gods in our image rather than being remade in God's image.

### Natural Revelation Has Limits

We cannot discover God's true nature through observation alone:

- **Creation is beautiful, but creation also contains suffering.** Why assume God is beautiful and not also terrible? Why assume divine design when disease and disability exist?

- **We import worldly definitions into biblical words.** We think "love" means romance and feeling. We imagine "joy" as happiness. "Rest" becomes an exotic vacation. "Family" becomes a nuclear household.

We expect God to be there on our terms, defined by our imagination. This is the danger of relying on natural revelation without God's propositional revelation—His written Word.

### The Inevitable Descent

The people don't just make their idol—they become their idol:

> "Those who make them will be like them, and so will all who trust in them." (Psalm 115:8)

What follows? Indulgence and revelry (v. 6):

> "The people rose early and sacrificed burnt offerings and presented fellowship offerings. Afterward they sat down to eat and drink and got up to indulge in revelry." (v. 6)

Paul captures this: "The people sat down to eat and drink and got up to indulge in revelry... We should not commit sexual immorality" (1 Corinthians 10:7-8).

**A life based on ourselves as the standard becomes a life ruled by base instincts—hunger, fear, lust. Just like animals.** We lose our agency and become enslaved to our own making.

### God's Assessment

God sees the people as "stiff-necked" (v. 9)—rigid and immovable, like a metal idol. They have become what they worship. They lose the ability to choose, to repent, to move toward righteousness. They are out of control, "running wild" (v. 25).

Aaron's excuse—"I threw it into the fire, and out came this calf" (v. 24)—reveals the delusion. We pretend sin just happens. We don't acknowledge our willing participation in rebellion. Sexual immorality isn't accidental; **it comes from an idolatrous heart that refuses God's boundaries**.

### The Application for Today

Look at your non-Christian friends. What are they longing for? What consumes their thoughts? What addictions bind them? Are you following the same idols?

Many of us pursue worldly comfort, worldly success, worldly relationships—all under the guise of "me time" and "balanced living." It's a lifestyle of selfishness dressed in the language of wellness. And as we scroll through Instagram and watch friends succeed, we deep down want it too.

The danger is real: **we shape our idols, and our idols shape us.**

## Part 2: Becoming Like God (Verses 11-20, 26-29)

### Moses' Contrasting Path

While the people create idols in their own image, Moses does something radically different. He intercedes for them, appealing to God's character and promises:

> "Lord, why should your anger burn against your people, whom you brought out of Egypt with great power and a mighty hand?" (v. 11-12)

Moses doesn't imagine what God should be like. He engages God on God's terms—through direct revelation, through God's Word spoken to him.

### The Power of God's Propositional Revelation

After forty days receiving the law directly from God, Moses descends with stone tablets "inscribed on both sides, front and back. The tablets were the work of God; the writing was the writing of God, engraved on the tablets" (v. 15-16).

**We can only know God through God's revelation, not our imagination.** God writes His words; we read and form our conclusions based on His conclusions. We are made in His image—designed to receive His Word and be transformed by it.

The contrast is striking:
- Aaron *engraves* a molten calf from earrings (taken from the ear)
- God *engraves* His commandments in stone (entering the ear as God's Word)

### Moses Inherits Both God's Mercy and Justice

Moses not only intercedes with God's mercy (vv. 11-14); he also executes God's justice.

When he sees the calf and the dancing, "his anger burned" (v. 19)—he has God's anger. He breaks the tablets at the foot of the mountain, destroys the calf, grinds it to powder, and scatters it on water, forcing the people to drink it. This is the judgment of an idol-breaker.

Then he calls: "Whoever is for the Lord, come to me" (v. 26). The Levites rally to him, and he commands: "Go back and forth through the camp from one end to the other, each killing his brother and friend and neighbor" (v. 27). About three thousand people die.

This harsh judgment reveals a fundamental truth: **serving false gods leads to death.** The wages of idolatry are destruction.

### The Call to Immerse in God's Word

Kenny pauses here with a pastoral reflection. Someone had commented that his interpretation seemed like "a stretch." His response is simple but profound: we must add more of God's Word. We sharpen each other through Scripture. We learn. We grow. The more we do this, the less we absorb the world's assumptions and the more we take on a biblical worldview.

**We will either make up our own gods, or we will rely on God. If we lack the discipline to learn and follow His Word—if we ignore it, if we forget it—we are lost. There is no hope. We will become like the animals.**

But if we rely on God's Word, we encounter something far greater.

## Part 3: God Becomes Like Us (Verses 30-35)

### The Foreshadow of True Atonement

The next day, Moses returns to the people and says: "You have committed a great sin. But now I will go up to the Lord; perhaps I can make atonement for your sin" (v. 30).

He ascends and pleads: "Oh, what a great sin these people have committed! They have made themselves gods of gold. But now, please forgive their sin—but if not, then blot me out of the book you have written" (v. 31-32).

Moses offers himself as a substitute. He asks to be blotted out so the people might be spared.

God's response is measured: "Whoever has sinned against me I will blot out of my book" (v. 33). The judgment stands. A plague strikes the people (v. 35).

Moses' atonement is incomplete—touching but insufficient.

### Jesus: The Complete Atonement

This entire pattern points forward to Christ:

> "God made him who had no sin to be sin for us, so that in him we might become the righteousness of God." (2 Corinthians 5:21)

Jesus doesn't just offer to be blotted out temporarily. He is actually blotted out—erased, crucified, dead—so that we might be written into the book of life. His sacrifice is the atonement that actually works. His blood cleanses what Moses' intercession could only foreshadow.

Through Christ, God becomes like us—fully human, fully entering our condition—so that we might become like God.

---

## Conclusion

We are called to follow neither idols—whether statues, celebrities, or worldly desires—nor to rely on our own spiritual effort alone. Instead, we follow our Creator, the Author and Perfecter of our faith, through His revealed Word and ultimately through His Son.

The choice is clear: **become like our idols and lose ourselves to the animals, become like God through His Word and Christ's atonement, or remain captive to our own imagination.**

May we choose God's Word. May we be transformed by His truth. And may we never forget that He became like us so that we could become like Him.
