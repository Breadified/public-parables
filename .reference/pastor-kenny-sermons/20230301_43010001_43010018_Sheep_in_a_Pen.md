# John 10:1-18

**Title:** Sheep in a Pen
**Date:** 12 March 2023 (ARISE) / 8 February 2016 (SMAC)
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction: Why Jesus Uses the Metaphor of Sheep

Jesus uses the image of sheep to describe His followers. This may seem strange—after all, sheep are notably unintelligent animals. If a sheep with a full fleece falls on its back, it cannot right itself. Sheep lack the claws other animals have for defense or escape. Yet there's a powerful incident from eastern Turkey where 1,500 sheep walked off a cliff in a single incident, with 400 dying in the fall. Shepherds had neglected the flock, and when one sheep wandered off the edge, the rest simply followed.

Despite the seeming foolishness of sheep, Jesus chose this metaphor deliberately. Christians are to obey Jesus like sheep follow a shepherd for four essential reasons: His familiar authority, the absence of false authority, His sacrificial authority, and His divine authority.

## 1. Familiar Authority: The Shepherd Knows His Sheep (John 10:1-4)

> Very truly I tell you, Pharisees, anyone who does not enter the sheep pen by the gate, but climbs in by some other way, is a thief and a robber. The one who enters by the gate is the shepherd of the sheep. The gatekeeper opens the gate for him, and the sheep listen to his voice. He calls his own sheep by name and leads them out. When he has brought out all his own, he goes on ahead of them, and his sheep follow him because they know his voice.

The picture Jesus paints is intimate: a shepherd calling his sheep by name. This seems strange to modern ears. In contemporary large-scale shepherding—whether by helicopter in Australia or by mechanical means—individual animals remain anonymous. But in the ancient Middle East, shepherds kept small, precious flocks. The relationship was intensely personal, like caring for beloved pets. The shepherd spent all day and night with the sheep, feeding and protecting them.

This intimacy is captured beautifully in Isaiah:

> He tends his flock like a shepherd: He gathers the lambs in his arms and carries them close to his heart; he gently leads those that have young.

The sheep recognize their master's voice because of this constant presence and care.

### Application: Christians Recognize Jesus' Voice in God's Word

Believers recognize the voice of Jesus in Scripture. This recognition is mysterious—we cannot fully explain why some people respond to God's call while others resist. From a heavenly perspective, the selection of God's flock remains inscrutable. But from an earthly perspective, the answer is clear: stubborn hearts that refuse to listen.

By nature, we are built to know God. We are marked with His image, and He has placed eternity in our hearts (Ecclesiastes 3:11). This natural yearning drives us to seek our origins, our Creator, and the moral foundation of our being. Every child learns morality, which presupposes a moral Being. Everything in creation points to a beginning, which points to a Creator.

It is natural to believe in God. Christians are those who recognize this design, set aside their prejudices, and listen. If that yearning has brought you to church today for the first time, you are in the right place. Hear His voice.

Scripture reinforces this urgency:

> Today, if you hear his voice, do not harden your hearts as you did in the rebellion.

## 2. Not False Authority: Distinguishing the True Shepherd from Thieves (John 10:5-10)

> But they will never follow a stranger; in fact, they will run away from him because they do not recognize a stranger's voice.

It is unnatural not to believe in God. To reject God's voice requires great effort—you must actively work to avoid recognizing His authority.

> Jesus used this figure of speech, but the Pharisees did not know what he was telling them.

The Pharisees exemplified false authority. These religious leaders refused to listen to Jesus and led the sheep of Israel toward destruction. Verse 8 makes this explicit:

> All who have come before me are thieves and robbers, but the sheep have not listened to them.

In biblical times, sheep thieves would enter a pen, slit a sheep's throat to silence it, and toss the body over the wall. These false shepherds were doing the same to people. The blind man Jesus healed in chapter 9 faced threats against his parents and was thrown out of the synagogue. The Pharisees ultimately killed Jesus Himself. They stole from widows' houses and impoverished the people through their exploitation.

### Oppression: The Cost of Resisting God's Voice

False authority depends entirely on oppression. To prevent people from following God requires brainwashing, murder, and violence. This is because resistance to God's voice is fundamentally unnatural.

> The thief comes only to steal and kill and destroy.

Ezekiel condemned the false shepherds of Israel:

> Woe to you shepherds of Israel who only take care of yourselves! Should not shepherds take care of the flock? You eat the curds, clothe yourselves with the wool and slaughter the choice animals, but you do not take care of the flock. You have not strengthened the weak or healed the sick or bound up the injured. You have not brought back the strays or searched for the lost. You have ruled them harshly and brutally. So they were scattered because there was no shepherd, and when they were scattered they became food for all the wild animals.

### Application: False Authority in the Modern World

Today, the same oppression continues both internally and externally.

**Internally:** Some churches have abandoned fundamental Christian truths—the resurrection of Christ, His deity, the authority of Scripture—in pursuit of cultural relevance. We call these "liberal" churches, not in a political sense, but in that they have liberated themselves from traditional Christian belief. Such churches cannot survive by definition: an organization that tells people to be like everyone else cannot attract those who are already like everyone else. These churches have ceased to hear the Shepherd's voice.

False teachers manipulate Scripture, hiding or ignoring inconvenient passages, twisting others to fit their narrative. They survive only through lies and misinformation, stealing from orthodox churches and abandoning the spiritual welfare of their flocks.

**Externally:** The world relentlessly attempts to brainwash us. We are taught that theft is acceptable if it serves a "good cause," that sex is disconnected from biology and morality, and that marriage can be fundamentally redefined. For 1,500 years, societies understood what theft was, what male and female meant, and what marriage entailed. Yet in the last 15 years, those in power claim enlightenment on these obvious matters.

Universities, high schools, media, and social institutions pressure us against Jesus. The so-called "Safe Schools Coalition" amounts to sexual grooming—making children into sexual objects. Even preschools have become propaganda tools.

But our reason rebels against these lies. The natural order within us cannot accept them.

To silence dissent, the system must oppress—removing freedom of religion, criminalizing disagreement as "hate speech." Oppression becomes necessary because truth cannot be hidden. They must terrorize people into self-censorship, forcing them to deny what they plainly see.

Yet freedom of religion is the first freedom because it is foundational. Where freedom exists, religion thrives. That is why the first casualty of tyranny is always religious liberty.

## 3. Sacrificial Authority: The Good Shepherd Lays Down His Life (John 10:11-18)

> I am the good shepherd. The good shepherd lays down his life for the sheep.

Jesus repeats this truth multiple times:

> I am the good shepherd; I know my sheep and my sheep know me—just as the Father knows me and I know the Father—and I lay down my life for the sheep.

And again:

> The reason my Father loves me is that I lay down my life—only to take it up again. No one takes it from me, but I lay it down of my own accord. I have authority to lay it down and authority to take it up again. This command I received from my Father.

To "lay down" means to become vulnerable, like a sheep on its back—defenseless, facing death.

### The Difference Between the Shepherd and the Hired Hand

Compare this to the hired hand:

> The hired hand is not the shepherd and does not own the sheep. So when he sees the wolf coming, he abandons the sheep and runs away. Then the wolf attacks the flock and scatters it. The man runs away because he is a hired hand and cares nothing for the sheep.

The hired hand has no personal investment. He sees the work as just another job, another transaction. When danger comes, he flees. A homeowner would never allow a tradesperson to work on their property the same way a hired hand tends someone else's flock—because it's his home, and he cares deeply.

But a shepherd has invested everything in those sheep. From birth to maturity, through winter nights and dangers, he has nurtured and protected them. He cannot simply walk away. Jesus demonstrates this kind of love. He cares sacrificially, and we follow because He feeds us, sacrifices for us, and loves us.

### We Are Defenseless; He Is Our Protection

> Sheep are dumb and directionless and defenseless.

We are born without claws, completely dependent on a shepherd. Jesus does not compliment us with this description—it is simply realistic. We are sheep who need a shepherd, and the shepherd sees our welfare as his responsibility.

> I am the gate for the sheep. Whoever enters through me will be saved. They will come in and go out, and find pasture. The thief comes only to steal and kill and destroy; I have come that they may have life, and have it to the full.

Jesus is the gate—protection at the entrance, like a shepherd lying at the mouth of a cave. He offers us salvation, pasture, and fullness of life:

> I give them eternal life, and they shall never perish; no one will snatch them out of my hand. My Father, who has given them to me, is greater than all; no one can snatch them out of my Father's hand.

Ezekiel describes what this protection looks like:

> For this is what the Sovereign Lord says: I myself will search for my sheep and look after them. As a shepherd looks after his scattered flock when he is with them, so will I look after my sheep. I will rescue them from all the places where they were scattered on a day of clouds and darkness. I will bring them out from the nations and gather them from the countries, and I will bring them into their own land. I will pasture them on the mountains of Israel, in the ravines and in all the settlements in the land. I will tend them in a good pasture, and the mountain heights of Israel will be their grazing land. There they will lie down in good grazing land, and there they will feed in a rich pasture on the mountains of Israel. I myself will tend my sheep and have them lie down. I will search for the lost and bring back the strays. I will bind up the injured and strengthen the weak, but the sleek and the strong I will destroy. I will shepherd the flock with justice.

### Application: Christ's Sacrifice for You

Christ died for you. He knitted you together in your mother's womb. From the beginning of time, He knew your name. He sees you right now. And at the appointed hour, He dies for you.

Do you think He does not love you? The problem is not His love—it is our disobedience, our refusal to listen to logic and reason. We prefer to be brainwashed by fools rather than follow the One who calls us by name.

## 4. Divine Authority: Belief and the Works of the Father (John 10:31-42)

Jesus' opponents challenged His authority directly:

> How long will you keep us in suspense? If you are the Messiah, tell us plainly.

Jesus responds:

> I did tell you, but you do not believe. The works I do in my Father's name testify about me, but you do not believe because you are not my sheep. My sheep listen to my voice; I know them, and they follow me.

If they will not listen to His familiar authority or respond to His moral sacrifice, Jesus appeals to His divine authority: He is simply God.

They accuse Him of blasphemy:

> We are not stoning you for any good work, but for blasphemy, because you, a mere man, claim to be God.

Jesus defends Himself with two answers. First, even in their own Scripture, people are called "gods":

> Is it not written in your Law, 'I have said you are "gods"'? If he called them 'gods,' to whom the word of God came—and Scripture cannot be set aside—what about the one whom the Father set apart as his very own and sent into the world?

Israel is called "sons of God." By this standard, claiming to be God's Son is not technically blasphemy.

But Jesus' second answer is more direct:

> Do not believe me unless I do the works of my Father. But if I do them, even though you do not believe me, believe the works, that you may know and understand that the Father is in me, and I in the Father.

He appeals to a minor-to-major argument. John the Baptist never performed a sign, yet everything he said about Jesus proved true. Jesus performed countless miracles—how much more credible is His testimony?

> Though John never performed a sign, all that John said about this man was true. And in that place many believed in Jesus.

## Conclusion: The Sheep's Response

As sheep, perhaps Christians are not always as clever as the world, nor as successful or beautiful. But there is one thing Christians excel at beyond anyone: we can recognize our Savior's voice.

He calls each of us by name. He gives us life and life to the full. And we follow.

Perhaps we are not so foolish after all, for we follow the One who cares for us with divine, sacrificial, familiar authority. To say, "The Lord is my shepherd," is to humble ourselves—admitting what is true about our need—and to exalt God, declaring what is true of His love. When you declare that God is your shepherd, you praise and glorify Him, for the God of all creation stoops down to care for poor, lost, not-so-smart sheep like you and me.
