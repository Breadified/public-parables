## The Money Man

Michael Jordan dominated professional basketball. He could slam dunk from the free throw line—earning his title "Air Jordan." He held the highest single-game score of 63 points, led the league in scoring multiple times, and achieved both MVP and defensive honors simultaneously. Beyond statistics, he popularized Nike and the NBA globally. Most importantly, he was the clutch performer—the one you passed the ball to when everything was on the line. Even when sick, even when the pressure was greatest, he was the money man, the go-to guy. Your last shot had to be in his hands.

Paul's letter to the Philippians asks us the same question: When the chips are down in life, who is your go-to guy? Who will you put your confidence in?

---

## Part 1: What NOT to Put Your Confidence In

### Confidence is not found in being a Jew (3:2–3)

> Watch out for those dogs, those men who do evil, those mutilators of the flesh. (Philippians 3:2)

Paul uses strikingly strong language here. In the original Greek, these words alliterate and "watch out" is repeated three times—something like: "Beware of those dogs, beware of those devils, beware of those destroyers."

**Who are these "evil dogs"?** Paul is warning against the Judaizers—a group of Jewish Christians who insisted that Gentile converts must first become Jewish, including circumcision, to truly become Christians. They reasoned: "The early Christians were Jews. Jesus was Jewish. Therefore, you must be Jewish first."

**But Paul overturns this completely:**

> For it is we who are the circumcision, we who worship by the Spirit of God, who glory in Christ Jesus, and who put no confidence in the flesh. (Philippians 3:3)

Paul redefines what circumcision actually means. Circumcision was the Jewish covenant sign—the mark that one belonged to God's people. For centuries, it signified belonging to Israel and participation in God's blessing. But Jesus has changed everything.

Consider Colossians 2:11–12:

> In him you were also circumcised, in the putting off of the sinful nature, not with a circumcision done by the hands of men but with the circumcision done by Christ, having been buried with him in baptism and raised with him through your faith in the power of God, who raised him from the dead.

The true circumcision is now spiritual—the putting off of your sinful nature. It's no longer about cutting skin; it's about cutting sin.

**An illustration:** Which would you rather be—someone with a tattoo reading "Millionaire" on their chest but empty bank accounts, or someone with no tattoo but a million dollars safely in the bank? Obviously, the one with the actual deposit. Physical circumcision is like the tattoo—meaningless without the reality. Gentile Christians have the reality (spiritual transformation) without needing the physical sign.

### Paul's Own Resume (3:4–6)

If confidence in the flesh were possible, Paul would qualify. Listen to his credentials:

> If anyone else thinks he has reasons to put confidence in the flesh, I have more: circumcised on the eighth day, of the people of Israel, of the tribe of Benjamin, a Hebrew of Hebrews; in regard to the law, a Pharisee; as for zeal, persecuting the church; as for legalistic righteousness, faultless. (Philippians 3:5–6)

Paul's pedigree is impeccable:
- Circumcised on the eighth day (indicating devoted parents)
- Genetically descended from Benjamin, one of Israel's founding tribes
- Member of the Pharisee party—the most respected religious organization of his time
- Personally kept the law so thoroughly he was "faultless"
- So zealous he persecuted Christians to prove his devotion

By every measure, Paul was the ideal Jew. If anyone could boast in the flesh, it was him.

To understand the weight of this, imagine the ideal Asian: tutored from infancy, attending selective schools, becoming dux of your primary school, earning AMEB in piano and violin by high school, getting into Sydney University Law School, buying a house before age 25, marrying an Asian, joining social justice clubs, becoming a prefect. This is the cultural ideal—and Paul had the equivalent in his context.

### What Paul Did With It All (3:7–8)

But here's what shocks us:

> But whatever was to my profit I now consider loss for the sake of Christ. What is more, I consider everything a loss compared to the surpassing greatness of knowing Christ Jesus my Lord, for whose sake I have lost all things. I consider them rubbish. (Philippians 3:7–8)

All those achievements? Loss. All that heritage? Rubbish.

When Paul says "rubbish," don't imagine a wastepaper basket. Imagine your red garbage bin—open it, smell it, see the bin juice. That's what he means. He's saying: I'm throwing away my certificates, my reputation, my entire resume. All of it is refuse compared to knowing Christ.

## Why Does Paul Consider His Life Rubbish?

### Because Grace Must Be Alone (3:9)

> Not having a righteousness of my own that comes from the law, but that which is through faith in Christ—the righteousness that comes from God and is by faith. (Philippians 3:9)

Paul's first reason is crystal clear: righteousness comes from Christ alone. Not Christ plus your background. Not Christ plus your heritage. Not Christ plus your achievements. Christ alone.

This exclusivity means nothing can interfere. Your lineage, your credentials, your goodness—all of it must become rubbish by comparison, because grace cannot be mixed with anything else. It must be purely and solely from Christ.

**An illustration:** When you were 14 or 15, you might have gotten a job at McDonald's. They didn't hire you because you were qualified or mature or had excellent customer service skills. They hired you because you were cheap. Your award wage was the lowest. So what happened to your resume full of school competitions, half-yearly reports, sporting achievements, chess club membership? McDonald's didn't care. Those things became rubbish because the one thing that mattered was that you were affordable.

Similarly, compared to gaining Christ, everything else is rubbish.

**Another example:** Jim Elliot was a promising young man from a good family. At school, he was in the newspaper, football team, school plays, and public speaking. At Wheaton College, he was a wrestler, offered a scholarship, majoring in Greek. Yet he felt called to mission work. He and a friend went to Ecuador in 1951, where in 1956 he was killed by the very tribe he was trying to evangelize. His wife Elizabeth and daughter were left behind. His mutilated body was found downstream.

What a waste we might think. But Jim had already made his peace with this. On October 28, 1949, he wrote: "He is no fool who gives what he cannot keep to gain that which he cannot lose."

Jesus made the same point: "What good is it for someone to gain the whole world, yet forfeit their soul?" (Mark 8:36)

## Where Do You Put Your Confidence?

### The Challenge

Consider your own life. Where is your confidence?

**Perhaps in inherited abilities:** Your IQ, musical talent, athletic ability, wealth, beauty, or fashion sense.

**Perhaps in achievements:** Your pay grade, exam results, school ranking, certificates, property, job title.

**Perhaps in dreams of the future:** A healthy, wealthy life with the right university course leading to the right spouse, right house, right income, right health.

But what if your inheritance were lost? What if your earthly achievements became rubbish? What if your dreams turned to refuse?

Imagine your resume folders wet and soggy with bin juice.

**This is what Paul means.** And he's not saying don't pursue education or that you should drop out of school. But in contrast to Christ, these things are like rubbish. They're important, but secondary. The real self-esteem worth having isn't about achievement—it's about belonging to Christ and knowing that God loves you.

### The Right Perspective

The challenge is to have a proper perspective on things. Our achievements and privileges must be prioritized correctly. They are nothing compared to salvation.

**Don't over-invest in rubbish.** If earthly things are rubbish, we must be careful not to sacrifice the eternal for the temporary. If you give up your relationship with Christ to get a good job, a nice salary, and an 8-to-6 office existence in the city—what's the point? When this world ends and you face eternity, what will that have mattered?

**Don't be distracted by rubbish.** It's like going on a bushwalk and seeing only the rubbish on the ground, missing the trees and birds entirely. What's the point?

So here's the question: Are you ready to give them up? What value do you place on this rubbish? Dare we even call them rubbish? Judge yourself. Have you been putting too much effort into things that don't matter? Have you been too distracted by the temporary?

Think about your last few major decisions. Were they for the kingdom and Christ? For others? Or were they for yourself—for self-comfort and self-advancement? Every day brings this choice: to consider your decisions and be able to give them up for the sake of following Christ.

Will you beat the peer pressure? Will you take up your cross and follow Jesus?

## Part 2: Confidence is in Christ

### Knowing Christ Fully (3:10–11)

Paul gives his second reason for treating everything as rubbish: he wants to know Christ in his fullness.

> I want to know Christ and the power of his resurrection and the fellowship of sharing in his sufferings, becoming like him in his death, and so, somehow, to attain to the resurrection from the dead. (Philippians 3:10–11)

What does Paul want to know about Christ? Look at the structure:

- **The power of his resurrection:** Paul is looking forward to future resurrection. He echoes his earlier statement: "to me, to live is Christ and to die is gain" (Philippians 1:21). And later: Christ "will transform our lowly bodies so that they will be like his glorious body" (Philippians 3:21).

- **Fellowship of sharing in his sufferings:** This means suffering for others' sake, not suffering for its own sake. Paul is describing how to follow Jesus, who humbled himself and served others sacrificially. This is what it means to know Christ—to participate in his pattern of loving others at personal cost.

**An illustration:** Suppose you want to really know Jackie Chan, not just as a celebrity. You can read about him, watch his movies with blooper reels, even interview him. But if you want to truly understand him—to get into his head—you have to walk in his shoes. You have to attempt stunts, feel his fear. Break some bones, feel his pain. Keep acting with broken bones, feel his perseverance. Finish a film, feel his joy.

The same is true with knowing Christ. To know Jesus fully, you must understand his life of service and sacrifice. That understanding comes through serving others yourself, especially at cost to yourself.

### The Heart of the Trinity

This reveals God's very nature. We don't worship a solitary God like Islam teaches. We worship the Trinity—Father, Son, and Spirit. From eternity, God exists in relationship. He knows what it means to care for another person, to share, to honor and obey. He is other-person centered.

- The Father looks after the Son
- The Son obeys the Father
- The Spirit is lovingly sent to inform us of the Father's and Son's character

**If you want to know Christ, then serve.** Look out for one another's interests. Bring someone a cup of water and you'll understand. Help someone with their burden and you'll truly know. Sacrifice and suffer for others and you'll get it.

If you want to know Christ, embrace Philippians 2. Love others even when it costs you.

> Not so with you. Instead, whoever wants to become great among you must be your servant, and whoever wants to be first must be slave of all. For even the Son of Man did not come to be served, but to serve, and to give his life as a ransom for many. (Mark 10:43–45)

### Real Christian Experience

In Christian circles today, many say they want to experience God. They want miracles, healings, blessings of all sorts. But we must ask: Do they truly want to experience Christ in his fullness, or is it self-centered concern?

If you genuinely want to experience Christ fully, embrace suffering for others. This is true Christian experience. Paul writes: "In fact, everyone who wants to live a godly life in Christ Jesus will be persecuted" (2 Timothy 3:12).

Some Christians even claim that those without certain spiritual experiences are inferior Christians—not living the victorious life, lacking the Spirit. But that's rubbish again. That's treating experiences like circumcision—as if you need them to be truly Christian. No. We are saved by Christ alone. Everything else is rubbish.

### How Paul Lived This

Look at Paul's own life. Is there any hint he pursued power, miracles, wealth, or health? Does he seek comfort?

No. He:
- Died to himself
- Died to his reputation
- Died to his heritage
- Considered all these things rubbish

His suffering chains would attract people to hear him. His willingness to share in Christ's sufferings demonstrated his genuine confidence in Jesus.

### The Practice of Knowing Christ

Experiencing Christ is serving others as a servant. Not seeking healing but helping. Not seeking visions or voices but visiting the sick and unfortunate. Not seeking power but packing the projector. Not seeking miracles but mopping floors. Not seeking wealth but wiping tables.

**Consider these practical questions:**

At home, school, work, or your group:
- Do we welcome newcomers, or do we just continue chatting with friends as though they don't exist?
- When we eat together, do we feed others before ourselves, or do we take as much as possible?
- When we leave a space, do we leave it clean, or assume someone else will clean it?

**Here's what I'd like to see:**

- Every newcomer is never alone (unless they choose to be). Someone always looks after them, welcomes them, introduces them, shows them how things work, gets them a Bible.

- When we eat together, the first person in line takes a little, lets others go through, then comes back for seconds—not hoarding what's available.

- Every time we gather, we ask ourselves: How can I be serving here? How can I better serve?

We won't do this perfectly. Paul acknowledges this in verse 12.

## Continuing in That Confidence (3:12–16)

> Not that I have already obtained all this, or have already been made perfect, but I press on to take hold of that for which Christ Jesus took hold of me. Brothers, I do not consider myself yet to have taken hold of it. But one thing I do: Forgetting what is behind and straining toward what is ahead, I press on toward the goal to win the prize for which God has called me heavenward in Christ Jesus. (Philippians 3:12–14)

We haven't yet obtained it all. We haven't been made perfect. We haven't been transformed. We don't yet have our bodily resurrection. But Christ has already taken hold of that resurrection and is keeping it safe for us.

So what's left for us now? To know Christ. To suffer on his behalf. To continue pressing forward.

In our sufferings:
- Don't be distracted by circumcision and experiences
- Put your achievements and heritage in the right perspective

**An illustration:** Remember the Empire Strikes Back. The fighter pilots were told: "Stay on target, stay on target." Even as starships exploded around them, they kept their focus. The mission was clear.

You've already got your victory secured in Christ. Stay on target. Don't get distracted by this world, by lineage, by heritage. You're already there. You've lost everything for heaven. You're on the winning team.

## The Joy of Confidence in Christ

You see why Paul begins with a command to rejoice? If you've set your face toward Calvary and carried your cross, then verse 1 makes perfect sense: **Rejoice! Glory in Christ Jesus.**

Rejoice from putting your confidence in Christ. Rejoice even when all is rubbish—because it only means your confidence is in Christ alone. It means you're knowing him through suffering for others. Rejoice in these things!

### Joy Redefined

Philippians is often titled the book of joy because "rejoice" appears so frequently. But the more you read it, the more you read about suffering for others—and that doesn't sound joyful at all. That's because we have a narrow view of joy.

We think joy means happy-happy, everything is well. But Paul uses joy differently. He's talking about security and assurance that comes from confidence in Jesus. It's the deep assurance that as we suffer for others, we're sharing in what Christ already suffered. We're not doing anything new—we're only participating in the pattern he set for us.

He is our fellow friend who carried the same yoke we carry. The yoke of the cross that we complain about, he carried first. 

So we've redefined the acronym: **J.O.Y. = Jesus Our Yokefellow.**

---

## Back to Michael Jordan

At the last seconds of a game, Michael Jordan would outmaneuver his opponent, execute a jump shot, the buzzer would sound, and swish—he wins. Michael Jordan was the one you put your confidence in on the basketball court.

But what about in life?

This game called Earth is finishing. This is the last game ever—lose and you die. The buzzer is about to sound any second. Who will you pass the ball to? Who will you trust? Who will you keep both eyes on?

Don't be a fool and dribble the ball away or pass it to a dummy. There's only one man to pass to in this situation. There's only one you can truly trust.

That man is Jesus, our Yokefellow.

## Final Application

> If anyone would come after me, he must deny himself and take up his cross and follow me. (Mark 8:34)

Some of you are here with boyfriends or girlfriends. Are they worth it if they never encourage you to follow Jesus but rather distract you from it? How will that help you "forget what is behind and strain toward what is ahead"?

Rather, let us persevere. Press on. Strain forward to what is ahead. This is Christian maturity—not letting failures stop us from doing what's right.

Righteousness doesn't come through denominations, church, traditions, intellect, or experience. These can be wonderful things, but they cannot be your foundation for salvation.

I sometimes feel confident that my intellect will stop me from falling away. My logical assessment of truth and sturdy conclusions will keep me from drifting. But that's false confidence. My intellect won't do anything on the day of judgment. Christ will keep me—not my intellect. His righteousness will be displayed—not my intellect.

Know Christ. Follow his example. Take note of those who live according to the pattern he gave us. Many live as enemies of the cross, their god is their stomach, their glory is in their shame, their minds on earthly things. But our citizenship is in heaven. We eagerly await our Savior, the Lord Jesus Christ, who will transform our lowly bodies so that they will be like his glorious body.

**Put your confidence in Christ. He is the Money Man. He is the go-to guy. And He will never let you down.**
