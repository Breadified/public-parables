# Genesis 22:1-19

**Title:** God Will See to It  
**Date:** March 9, 2008  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Story So Far

We arrive at Genesis 22 after reviewing the extensive narrative of Abraham's journey through chapters 12-21. This passage represents a dramatic turn in God's relationship with Abraham—one that tests everything we've learned about faith and divine promise.

Over the past month, we've traced God's progressive revelation to Abraham:

- **Genesis 12**: The initial promise of the Land, Offspring, and Blessing—God's solution to human rebellion at Babel
- **Genesis 15**: The promise reaffirmed as Abraham receives material blessing, military victories, and a covenant relationship with God
- **Genesis 17**: Abraham at 99 years old, questioning where the promised son is. God reiterates His promises, yet Abraham takes matters into his own hands, producing Ishmael—who, God makes clear, is not the promised heir
- **Genesis 18-19**: A transitional section showing Sodom and Gomorrah's unrighteousness contrasted with Abraham's growing righteousness
- **Genesis 20**: Abraham repeats his earlier mistake, calling Sarah his sister while moving among the Philistines. More promises follow
- **Genesis 21**: At last, Isaac is born when Abraham is 100 years old. The other loose threads resolve: Ishmael is sent away, and peace is made with the Philistines

The story has been one of God's repeated promise, Abraham's occasional doubt and self-help efforts, and God's faithful reassurance. Just when we think this cycle might continue indefinitely, Genesis 21 brings resolution. The promised son has arrived. And then we reach Genesis 22.

## 1. A Crazy Idea

> Then God said, "Take your son, your only son, Isaac, whom you love, and go to the region of Moriah. Sacrifice him there as a burnt offering on one of the mountains I will tell you about." (Genesis 22:2)

God tells Abraham to sacrifice Isaac.

At first glance, this makes no sense. After five chapters of buildup—promises, complications, years of waiting—Abraham is now supposed to kill his son and end the entire promise? The emphasis is unmistakable: *your son, your only son*. Ishmael doesn't count. Abraham is 100 years old. Without Isaac, how will God fulfill the promise of land, offspring, and blessing?

It's as if you spent years studying for the HSC, completed all your assignments, achieved your desired score, got into your top university choice, finished your final exams, put your pen down—and then walked out without writing your name on the paper. Everything erased. Gone.

Or imagine if, after 30 years of collecting certificates, degrees, and awards—your most cherished possessions, the things you'd grab in a fire after your family—someone told you to burn them all the night before your most important job interview. Crazy. It makes no sense. It contradicts everything you've been taught.

Yet this is precisely what Abraham is commanded to do—right when things are finally working out, right when God's promises are coming true.

## 2. Faith and Obedience

But here's what happens:

> Early the next morning Abraham got up and saddled his donkey. He took with him two of his servants and his son Isaac. When he had cut enough wood for the burnt offering, he set out for the place God had told him about. (Genesis 22:3)

This echoes Genesis 12:4, where Abraham obeyed God's initial call to leave his country. Now, facing an impossible command, he obeys again.

> When they reached the place God had told him about, Abraham built an altar there and arranged the wood on it. He bound his son Isaac and laid him on the altar, on top of the wood. Then he reached out his hand and took the knife to slay his son. (Genesis 22:9-10)

Abraham has doubted before. He's tried to work things out on his own. But this moment defines him. Genesis doesn't explain *why* he obeys—it simply records that he does. But the author of Hebrews provides the interpretation we need.

Turn with me to Hebrews 11. Here, the Spirit gives us a guided tour of Abraham's faith:

> By faith Abraham, when called to go to a place he would later receive as his inheritance, obeyed and went, even though he did not know where he was going. By faith he made his home in the promised land like a stranger in a foreign country; he lived in tents, as did Isaac and Jacob, who were heirs with him of the same promise. (Hebrews 11:8-10)

> By faith Abraham, even though he was past age—and Sarah herself was barren—was enabled to become a father because he considered him faithful who had made the promise. And so from this one man, and he as good as dead, came descendants as numerous as the stars in the sky and as countless as the sand on the seashore. (Hebrews 11:11-12)

> By faith Abraham, when God tested him, offered Isaac as a sacrifice. He who had received the promises was about to sacrifice his one and only son, even though God had said to him, "It is through Isaac that your offspring will be reckoned." Abraham reasoned that God could raise the dead, and figuratively speaking, he did receive Isaac back from death. (Hebrews 11:17-19)

The author identifies three defining moments of Abraham's faith:
- **Hebrews 11:8**: Obedience in going to a new land (Genesis 12:4)
- **Hebrews 11:11**: Belief that God could give life through Sarah's barren womb (Genesis 15:6)
- **Hebrews 11:17**: Willingness to offer Isaac as a sacrifice (Genesis 22)

Why does Abraham obey? Genesis doesn't tell us, but Hebrews reveals his reasoning: *Abraham reasoned that God could raise the dead.* He had already witnessed God bring life from Sarah's death-like barrenness. Therefore, he trusted God could do it again—could resurrect Isaac if necessary. This is faith: believing God at His word, even when circumstances seem impossible.

The author of Hebrews calls us to this same faith:

> Therefore, since we are surrounded by such a great cloud of witnesses, let us throw off everything that hinders and the sin that so easily entangles, and let us run with perseverance the race marked out for us. Let us fix our eyes on Jesus, the author and perfecter of our faith, who for the joy set before him endured the cross, scorning its shame, and sat down at the right hand of the throne of God. (Hebrews 12:1-2)

Abraham didn't know how the story would end. He didn't understand that it pointed to Christ. Yet he believed anyway. We have the advantage of looking back at Christ; Abraham looked forward in faith. The call is the same for both: don't grow weary, don't lose heart, throw off everything that hinders, and fix your eyes on Jesus—the author and perfecter of faith.

**Abraham is our model of faith: a man who believed God at His word.**

## 3. God Will Provide

But at the crucial moment, something unexpected happens:

> But the angel of the LORD called out to him from heaven, "Abraham! Abraham!" "Here I am," he replied. "Do not lay a hand on the boy," he said. "Do not do anything to him. Now I know that you fear God, because you have not withheld from me your son, your only son." (Genesis 22:11-12)

The test is stopped. An alternative is provided.

> Abraham looked up and there in a thicket he saw a ram caught by its horns. He went over and took the ram and sacrificed it as a burnt offering instead of his son. So Abraham called that place The LORD Will Provide. And to this day it is said, "On the mountain of the LORD it will be provided." (Genesis 22:13-14)

Notice what happened on the mountain. Recall Isaac's question on the journey: *"Father, the fire and wood are here, but where is the lamb for the burnt offering?"* Abraham's answer was prophetic: *"God himself will provide the lamb for the burnt offering, my son."*

God does provide—but it's a ram, not a lamb. This is the first mention of a lamb or sheep in Scripture's narrative of sacrifice. Yet it's significant. In Exodus 12, the Passover lamb becomes central to Israel's redemption. And ultimately, Jesus is revealed as *the Lamb of God* (John 1:29).

Why does the author of Genesis emphasize this detail? Because God's ultimate provision isn't a substitute sacrifice on Mount Moriah. It's the final sacrifice on another mountain—Golgotha, the place of the skull. On Calvary, there was no angel to stop the knife at the last moment. There was no reprieve. God the Father did not withhold His hand. His only Son died—one sacrifice for all people.

God provides the way of salvation, not through our works or our sacrifices, but through Christ.

## The Call to Faith and Obedience

> The angel of the LORD called to Abraham from heaven a second time and said, "I swear by myself, declares the LORD, that because you have done this and have not withheld your son, your only son, I will surely bless you and make your descendants as numerous as the stars in the sky and as the sand on the seashore. Your descendants will take possession of the cities of their enemies, and through your offspring all nations on earth will be blessed, because you have obeyed me." (Genesis 22:15-18)

The promise stands. Abraham's obedience hasn't earned God's blessing—it has revealed Abraham's faith. And that faith is rewarded with the confirmation of everything God promised.

For us, the call is the same: **Believe God. Repent and believe.** (Mark 1:14)

We cannot earn God's provision through our works or our sacrifices. We can only receive it through faith in Christ, who is God's provision for us. When we fix our eyes on Jesus, throw off everything that hinders, and run with perseverance, we join Abraham as children of faith—trusting in the God who provides.

> Then Abraham returned to his servants, and they set off together for Beersheba. And Abraham stayed in Beersheba. (Genesis 22:19)
