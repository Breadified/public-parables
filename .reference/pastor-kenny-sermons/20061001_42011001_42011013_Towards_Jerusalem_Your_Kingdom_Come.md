## The Prayer of Jabez: A Misunderstood Prayer

Years ago, the "Prayer of Jabez" became a bestseller in Christian bookshops. Author Bruce Wilkinson popularized a prayer from 1 Chronicles 4:10, teaching people to pray it repeatedly for 30 days to receive blessings and expand their influence—whether in business, relationships, or personal achievements.

> Jabez cried out to the God of Israel, "Oh, that you would bless me and enlarge my territory! Let your hand be with me, and keep me from harm so that I will be free from pain." And God granted his request. (1 Chronicles 4:9-10)

People began praying for doubled revenue, expanded audiences, increased sales, and material success. Today, this prayer might promise more social media followers, higher exam marks, or a larger property portfolio.

**But is this how prayer really works? Can we ask God for anything and expect a guaranteed answer?**

The Bible teaches something very different. We need to understand what we're actually praying for.

---

## 1. We Ask the Father (Luke 11:2)

The disciples watched Jesus pray and asked Him to teach them. His response begins with the most foundational truth about prayer:

> When you pray, say: "Father, hallowed be your name, your kingdom come. Give us each day our daily bread. Forgive us our sins, for we also forgive everyone who sins against us. And lead us not into temptation." (Luke 11:2-4)

### God Is Transcendent—Yet Accessible

The prayer begins with "Father"—a revolutionary statement. God is not distant or unreachable. He is our Father.

"Hallowed" means holy. God's name is set apart. He is distinct from creation—set apart from sin, from mortality, from weakness. He is transcendent. He has authority and deserves our reverence and respect.

A kingdom requires a king. God is King. He rules over all things.

Yet simultaneously, God is also close. He is a Father—accessible and good. This combination of transcendence and intimacy is crucial.

### The Parable of Persistence

Jesus illustrates this with a story about friendship tested at midnight:

> Suppose you have a friend, and you go to him at midnight and say, "Friend, lend me three loaves of bread; a friend of mine on a journey has come to me, and I have no food to offer him." And suppose the one inside answers, "Don't bother me. The door is already locked, and my children and I are in bed. I can't get up and give you anything." (Luke 11:5-7)

The request is completely inconvenient. It's midnight—dark, dangerous, when everyone sleeps. Bread must be baked fresh in the morning; it's not something lying around. The door is locked, meaning the man would have to wake his whole household.

Yet Jesus concludes:

> I tell you, even though he will not get up and give you the bread because of friendship, yet because of your shameless audacity he will surely get up and give you as much as you need. (Luke 11:8)

The Greek word here means persistence—daring to ask. Not because of friendship alone, but because you refused to accept "no." Because you persisted, you receive.

### Why God Listens: We Are His Children

**Prayer is only possible if God is willing to accept our requests and able to answer them. So why would God listen to us?**

Because we are God's children—not by nature, but by adoption:

> For those who are led by the Spirit of God are the children of God. The Spirit you received does not make you slaves, so that you live in fear again; rather, the Spirit you received brought about your adoption to sonship. And by him we cry, "Abba, Father." (Romans 8:14-15)

We were once under judgment, separated from God by our sin. But when Jesus died and tore down the curtain of separation, we gained access. We can now call God "Abba"—Daddy. This relationship is what makes prayer possible.

### God Is Good

> Which of you fathers, if your son asks for a fish, will give him a snake instead? Or if he asks for an egg, will give him a scorpion? If you then, though you are evil, know how to give good gifts to your children, how much more will your Father in heaven give the Holy Spirit to those who ask him! (Luke 11:11-13)

A father knows how to give good gifts. He will not deceive or trick you. Even imperfect earthly fathers want their children's welfare—so how much more will our perfect Father care for us?

God is not Santa Claus, checking if you've been good enough. He is not a dictator to fear. He is Father—accessible, good, and generous.

### How God Affects Our Prayers

Your view of God shapes how you pray:

- If God is a government official to bribe, you make deals and offer tokens
- If God is a dictator, you use flowery language and act submissively
- If God is an impersonal force, you merely contemplate and don't truly ask
- If God is just a friend, you hesitate to ask for "too much"
- If God is Santa Claus, you make lists and try to manipulate

**But if God is Father:**

- You have access to a transcendent God
- You can admit your problems and weaknesses without fear
- You can trust Him for solutions because He is wise and good, not malicious or foolish

This matters when prayers go unanswered. When your exam scores disappoint despite studying hard. When you don't get the promotion. When someone breaks your heart. When a loved one dies. These are not signs of abandonment—they are invitations to trust that He sees the bigger picture and is guiding you toward what is truly good.

## 2. We Ask for His Kingdom to Come (Luke 11:2-4)

Now we understand to whom we pray—but what do we ask for?

> Ask and it will be given to you; seek and you will find; knock and the door will be opened to you. For everyone who asks receives; the one who seeks finds; and to the one who knocks, the door will be opened. (Luke 11:9-10)

This sounds incredibly generous—almost like a blank check. It tempts us to misread it as: ask for anything you want, and God will give it to you. The Prayer of Jabez mistake.

**But look at verse 3: "Give us each day our daily bread."**

Scholars debate the Greek word *epiousion*. It appears nowhere else in the Bible, making translation difficult. Some argue it means "daily," but that would be redundant with "each day." Others suggest "sufficient" or "for sustenance."

Looking at the word's structure, it may derive from the phrase meaning "the coming day"—so it could read: **"Give us today tomorrow's bread."**

This is prayer for something future. And what is that bread? 

> I am the bread of life. Whoever comes to me will never go hungry. (John 6)

This is the bread of the kingdom—eternal life, the feast in heaven when God's kingdom comes in fullness.

### The Prayer Centers on God's Kingdom

Throughout this prayer, we see a focus on God's agenda, not ours:

- "Hallowed be your name" — God's honor, not our comfort
- "Your kingdom come" — God's rule, not our prosperity
- "Give us tomorrow's bread" — God's future kingdom, not today's wants
- "Forgive us our sins" — pointing to the coming kingdom where sin will be no more
- "Lead us not into temptation" — deliverance in the age to come

When Jesus teaches us to ask for the Holy Spirit, He means the Spirit that leads us into God's kingdom:

> How much more will your Father in heaven give the Holy Spirit to those who ask him! (Luke 11:13)

This is not about power to "name and claim" blessings. This is about asking for forgiveness, for entry into God's kingdom, for salvation itself.

**This is why verse 9 is so positive: such a prayer is guaranteed.**

God wants you in His kingdom. He doesn't require you to be first in your class, to have a prestigious career, or to be wealthy. What He desires, as any good father does, is relationship with you.

If you still have questions about Jesus, pray for answers. Pray for a willing heart to evaluate them. If you are sincere, you will find them.

If you are already a Christian, keep praying for the kingdom—that Jesus will return, that His rule will be established, that people will submit to Him as King.

## 3. Prayer With Eyes Opened

### The Prayer of a Maturing Faith

As children, we pray for puppies and sore fingers. As mature believers, our prayers change.

When we were small, we prayed with eyes closed and hands together—useful in Sunday school to prevent distractions. But adults pray differently: **with eyes opened**.

Eyes open to the dangers around us—like watching a baby near a hot pot. Eyes open to God's agenda—seeing His plan, His desires, His vision for the world.

As we mature and become aware of how desperate and broken this world is, we realize that only one thing can fix it: a complete renewal. A new world. A new kingdom. This should occupy our prayers more and more.

As we become more like Christ, we understand that life is not about us and our needs. It's about loving God and loving our neighbors. And this transforms our prayers.

We pray with eyes open, hands freed and ready for action.

### Jesus Prayed for the Kingdom

On the road to Jerusalem, Jesus was praying. The disciples may have thought: "Great, now we'll learn how to ask God for what we want."

But Jesus wasn't praying for Himself. He was praying for the kingdom. He was aligning Himself with God's agenda. He was praying for tomorrow's bread. He was praying for God's kingdom to come.

---

## Conclusion: The Better Prayer

The Prayer of Jabez is not wrong. But it's a mistake to read it as about us. Jabez prayed for land as one of Abraham's descendants, inheriting God's ancient promise. This doesn't translate to praying for property portfolios.

But the principle works beautifully when applied to God's kingdom: praying for God's territory to expand, His name to be vindicated, His return to be hastened, for people to recognize Jesus as King, for forgiveness and renewal.

**And one day, His rule will be recognized by all.**

I don't need Jabez's prayer for this. I have the prayer of Jesus—the perfect prayer that teaches us to seek first His kingdom, to trust His goodness, to ask boldly, and to pray with eyes wide open to a world in need of redemption.
