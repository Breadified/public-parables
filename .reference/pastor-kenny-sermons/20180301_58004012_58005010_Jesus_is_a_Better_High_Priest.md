# Hebrews 4:12–5:10

**Title:** Jesus is a Better High Priest

**Date:** 4 March 2018

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Jesus the Empathizer

We've been reading through Hebrews to see how Jesus is the better person, the better God to follow. Today we focus on Jesus as the better high priest—and central to understanding His priesthood is recognizing that He is a God who can empathize with us.

The word "empathy" appears directly in our passage:

> For we do not have a high priest who is unable to empathize with our weaknesses, but we have one who has been tempted in every way, just as we are—yet he did not sin. (Hebrews 4:15)

The Greek term here is *sympathy* (sun-pathos)—to suffer with another, to understand another's pain. Jesus is the supreme empathizer because He truly understands the human experience.

### Jesus Is Like Us

Jesus knows what it means to be human because He lived as a human. Consider His experiences:

- **Weariness:** "Jesus, tired as he was from the journey, sat down by the well" (John 4:6)
- **Temptation:** He was tempted by Satan in the desert for forty days (Mark 1:13)
- **Poverty:** "Foxes have holes and birds of the air have nests, but the Son of Man has no place to lay his head" (Matthew 8:20)
- **Tears:** "Jesus wept" (John 11:35)
- **Disappointment:** "O Jerusalem, Jerusalem—how often I have longed to gather your children together, as a hen gathers her chicks, but you were not willing" (Luke 13:34)
- **Rejection:** "From this time many of his disciples turned back and no longer followed him" (John 6:66)
- **Sorrow:** "My soul is overwhelmed with sorrow to the point of death" (Matthew 26:38)
- **Ridicule:** "Again and again they struck him and spit on him. Falling on their knees, they paid mocking homage to him" (Mark 15:19)

Yes, Jesus knows what it's like to be in high school. He understands human weakness because He experienced it Himself. This is precisely what qualifies Him as our great high priest.

> Therefore, since we have a great high priest who has ascended into heaven, Jesus the Son of God, let us hold firmly to the faith we profess. (Hebrews 4:14)

A priest serves as a mediator between God and humanity. And a true mediator must be representative—he must come from among the people he represents:

> Every high priest is selected from among the people and is appointed to represent the people in matters related to God, to offer gifts and sacrifices for sins. He is able to deal gently with those who are ignorant and are going astray, since he himself is subject to weakness. (Hebrews 5:1–2)

When politicians represent their constituents, we rightly expect them to share citizenship, to live in the same electorate, to understand the ordinary needs of the people they serve. A representative who lives far removed from those he represents cannot truly understand their struggles or live with the consequences of his decisions. Jesus, by contrast, became one of us in the fullest sense.

### The Comfort of His Sympathy

When we suffer and ask God "Why?"—sometimes we're not seeking theological answers but wrestling with our specific pain. We don't understand why we must endure what we endure. In those moments, Jesus being one of us offers profound comfort. He is not alien to suffering. Whatever injustice we experience, He endured it Himself at the cross.

> That all cancels out at the cross. Because whatever suffering was unfair, whatever has been done to you, he endured that himself too.

If Jesus went through suffering and emerged on the other side in glory, we too have hope of glory in the future. When facing what seems overwhelming—academic struggles, raising children, any hardship—we can remember that others before us have faced similar trials and survived. Jesus faced the ultimate trial and triumphed. Therefore, let us not give up.

## Jesus Is Not Like Us

Yet there is a crucial way in which Jesus differs from us:

> For we do not have a high priest who is unable to empathize with our weaknesses, but we have one who has been tempted in every way, just as we are—**yet he did not sin.** (Hebrews 4:15)

In His despair, He did not curse God. In His pain, He did not abandon God. In His grief, He did not sin. He was perfectly obedient.

> Son though he was, he learned obedience from what he suffered; and, once made perfect, he became the source of eternal salvation for all who obey him; and was designated by God to be high priest in the order of Melchizedek. (Hebrews 5:8–10)

Jesus "learned obedience" through suffering—He experienced obedience in the ultimate sense. We, by contrast, have never truly experienced obedience. We are sinners.

> For the word of God is alive and active. Sharper than any double-edged sword, it penetrates even to dividing soul and spirit, joints and marrow; it judges the thoughts and attitudes of the heart. Nothing in all creation is hidden from God's sight. Everything is uncovered and laid bare before the eyes of him to whom we must give account. (Hebrews 4:12–13)

We are in trouble. Jesus is not. This is the critical difference.

### The Fatal Flaw of Earthly Priests

The weakness that made ordinary priests relatable also made them inadequate. Yes, they came from among the people and could deal gently with sinners because they themselves were sinners. But this created an impossible problem:

> This is why he has to offer sacrifices for his own sins, as well as for the sins of the people. (Hebrews 5:3)

An earthly high priest cannot simply cleanse the people of their sins. He must first cleanse himself. Can a doctor with a contagious disease truly heal others without infecting them? Can the blind lead the blind? The captain on a sinking ship cannot save his passengers—he too is going down.

If the pool of high priests consists entirely of sinners, how can sin ever truly be atoned for? A priest who shares our weakness cannot save us from that weakness.

**That's where Jesus is different.** He was tempted in every way, yet He did not sin. He alone stands outside the system of sin and can therefore save us from it.

## The Effectiveness of the Perfect Priest

> During the days of Jesus' life on earth, he offered up prayers and petitions with fervent cries and tears to the one who could save him from death, and he was heard because of his reverent submission. (Hebrews 5:7)

Jesus was heard because He is good. His resurrection proves He is sufficient—a high priest unlike us, yet able to help us precisely *because* He is unlike us.

> Let us then approach God's throne of grace with confidence, so that we may receive mercy and find grace to help us in our time of need. (Hebrews 4:16)

## Application: Beyond Empathy

Consider a funeral. Everyone is emotionally devastated. Yet often the most helpful person is the one slightly removed from the family—the one who maintains composure, finishes the speeches, organizes the arrangements. This person has compassion, but they are not overwhelmed by empathy. Their distance is precisely what enables them to help.

Our culture has made much of empathy, but there is a danger in this. We expect to feel everyone's pain, to wear ribbons for every cause, to mourn every tragedy. When someone says, "You can't understand my pain unless you've experienced it yourself," we're told we have no right to speak. "Unless you've struggled with this, you can't offer counsel. Unless you're part of that community, you can't comment."

But this is not how wisdom works. Truth does not depend on feeling the same emotions. You need not have experienced sin to offer righteousness. You need not feel someone's pain to counsel them wisely. In fact, being too emotionally entangled can cloud judgment and prevent genuine help.

Some have criticized the massive outpouring of grief after Princess Diana's death as "fake empathy." Most people grieving had never met her, knew her only through tabloids and television. Yet they felt as though they'd lost a family member. Bloom, in his critique of empathy, points out the danger: emotion can obscure truth and cloud our judgment about both the objects of our empathy and our own capacity to help.

**We can sometimes want fake empathy too.** We ask, "How can God understand me? Has He had an eating disorder? A broken marriage? Lost a child? Can Jesus identify with me when He doesn't know what it's like to wage war against sin?" But Jesus doesn't need those experiences. He has the one that truly matters: perfect obedience to God.

The truth is simple: **Thank God that Jesus is not like us.** If He were merely as good as the best of us, He could not save us. His difference is our salvation. His perfection is our hope. His obedience is our redemption.

---

**Let us approach His throne with confidence, knowing we have a high priest who truly understands our weakness—and who alone possesses the power to save us from it.**
