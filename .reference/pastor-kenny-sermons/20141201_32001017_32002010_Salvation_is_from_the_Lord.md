# Jonah 1:17-2:10

**Title:** Salvation is from the Lord  
**Date:** 14 December 2014  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Sign of Jonah (1:17)

> 17 Now the Lord provided a huge fish to swallow Jonah, and Jonah was in the belly of the fish three days and three nights.

This incredible account raises questions: is it a whale or an ancient sea creature? How could a man survive inside such a creature for three days and three nights? The thought seems biologically impossible—Jonah would need to pass through the fish's mouth and sharp teeth, survive in gastric juices, and withstand the lack of oxygen.

Some point to the alleged case of James Bartley, a whaler who in 1891 supposedly survived inside a whale's stomach. However, this story has been thoroughly discredited. The captain's widow, an eyewitness, denied the incident ever occurred. We still don't have a scientific explanation for how this is possible.

Yet we are called to believe it as true—just as Jesus did when he referenced Jonah in Matthew 12:38-40:

> 38 "Teacher, we want to see a sign from you."
> 39 He answered, "A wicked and adulterous generation asks for a sign! But none will be given it except the sign of the prophet Jonah. 40 For as Jonah was three days and three nights in the belly of a huge fish, so the Son of Man will be three days and three nights in the heart of the earth.

Jesus compared Jonah—a real historical person—to himself. This wasn't metaphor; it was historical correspondence.

### Understanding Miracles and Signs

The real issue isn't the biological feasibility of the fish—it's whether we believe in "the LORD." If we trust that Yahweh is sovereign and controls the natural elements, then a great fish is not a problem.

**The Pharisees' Problem**

The ancient Pharisees wanted to see a sign—proof of God. But when the sign came in the crucified Messiah, they refused to believe. Their problem wasn't lack of evidence; it was *unbelief*.

Today, miracles in Scripture are often dismissed as "fairy tales" that discredit the Bible's reliability. But consider the nature of biblical miracles:

**First:** Miracles serve as *special signs*—they are rare, not everyday occurrences. Counting roughly 123 miracles across 4,000 years means one miracle every 300-400 years, with a concentrated cluster of 37 miracles during Jesus's ministry. This doesn't align with modern expectations of constant miraculous guidance for everyday decisions like university courses, investments, or exam outcomes. Those hopes are probably superstition rather than reality.

**Second:** These miraculous signs mark *key points in salvation history*—moments where God has decisively intervened in human affairs. They were recorded precisely because they were extraordinary: the Flood, the plagues of Egypt, Jesus's miracles. When you pass an exam you thought you'd failed, God might show grace, but that's not a sign being recorded for generations to come.

**Third:** A divine God wanting to give a sign must do so through miraculous means. How else would he stand out from the ordinary? Signs, by definition, must stand out. Imagine a neon sign displaying green words on a green board in darkness—it defeats the purpose. True signs catch our attention precisely because they're extraordinary.

**The Real Issue: A Priori Thinking**

The problem isn't that signs are miraculous. The problem is *a priori* reasoning—deciding in advance whether God exists at all.

Consider: When the U.S. President visited Brisbane, things changed immediately. There were special traffic rules, helicopters, secret service, American accents everywhere. Someone could say, "The president is here," pointing to all this extraordinary activity as evidence. Or, skeptically, someone could dismiss it all: "This is Brisbane—a second-rate city. It's unlikely the most powerful nation's president would visit. Those helicopters are probably normal flight traffic. Those Americans are just tourists. Those suited men in sunglasses don't actually exist."

But aren't all these signs *consistent*? Isn't there strong circumstantial evidence?

Often, the problem isn't miracles themselves. The problem is unbelief. "I don't want to believe the president is in town." Or, "I don't want to believe Jesus is from God." These are positions evidence cannot resolve.

## Going Down (2:1-5)

Thankfully, Jonah is not skeptical. From inside the fish, he finally prays:

> 1 From inside the fish Jonah prayed to the Lord his God.
> 2 He said: "In my distress I called to the Lord, and he answered me. From deep in the realm of the dead I called for help, and you listened to my cry.

Recall from last week that Jonah had numerous opportunities to pray before now. In 1:5, when the sailors prayed to their gods in fear, Jonah "went below deck to sleep." In 1:6, the captain urged him to "call on your god," and he remained silent. In 1:14, the sailors prayed for Jonah—but Jonah did not pray for them.

Now he prays. He has no choice.

**Jonah is Going Down**

Literally, he is drowning:

> 3 You hurled me into the depths, into the very heart of the seas, and the currents swirled about me; all your waves and breakers swept over me.
> 4 I said, 'I have been banished from your sight; yet I will look again toward your holy temple.'
> 5 The engulfing waters threatened me, the deep surrounded me; seaweed was wrapped around my head.

He has been "hurled," the currents "swirl," and waves "swept over" him. We see Jonah immediately after being thrown into the sea—the storm is still raging. He's in the "depths," the "very heart of the seas." Jonah is drowning.

**A Personal Illustration**

I nearly drowned once. In year 7, as a new migrant to Australia, I was confident I could swim—I'd taken lessons in Malaysia's shoulder-high pool, learning breast stroke over ten metres. How hard could full-length swimming be?

At Galston Pool—two metres deep—I jumped in and sank. Drowning isn't what it looks like on TV. You don't have time to raise your hand; you don't have lung capacity to shout for help. You just sink silently. Fortunately, my classmates rescued me. Embarrassed, I learned to swim that summer—the only one in my group to do so.

**Jonah Sinks Into Death**

As the churning sea gives way to silent sea:

> 6 To the roots of the mountains I sank down; the earth beneath barred me in forever. But you, Lord my God, brought my life up from the pit.

The "roots of the mountains" refer to the continental shelf—where the ocean floor drops steeply. Jonah has literally "sank down."

From 1:3, when he disobeyed God and went "down" to Joppa, and 1:5, when he went "down" into the ship—he has been descending. The Hebrew verb *yarád* (to descend) is a key word throughout. Even his "deep sleep" plays on this word. From the moment of his disobedience, Jonah has been going down.

But the picture is not merely of underwater darkness. It's of the *underworld*. Jonah, for all intents and purposes, is dead.

Notice the language: "barred me in forever" (like prison bars), and "brought my life up from the *pit*" (the grave). These aren't ocean metaphors. We're talking about death itself.

See verse 2:

> From deep in the **realm of the dead** I called for help...

*Realm of the dead* = Sheol (Hebrew) or Hades (Greek)—the place of the dead. And verse 4 confirms the spiritual dimension:

> I said, 'I have been **banished** from your sight...

There's a parallel between the "belly of Sheol" and the "belly of the fish" (1:17). The belly of the great fish is like a coffin in the grave.

### The Consequence of Sin

This is the consequence of sin. This is Jonah's price for rebellion against God. He was given free will and chose to run. God's response: Jonah will experience the downward trajectory of disobedience.

**Application: Our Fate**

This fate was meant for us all. We have all sinned and fallen short of God. As Ephesians 2 tells us, we are "dead in our trespasses":

> Ephesians 2:1-3 As for you, you were dead in your transgressions and sins, in which you used to live when you followed the ways of this world and of the ruler of the kingdom of the air, the spirit who is now at work in those who are disobedient. All of us also lived among them at one time, gratifying the cravings of our flesh and following its desires and thoughts. Like the rest, we were by nature deserving of wrath.

By nature, we follow worldly desires and the spirit of disobedience. Consequently, "we were by nature deserving of wrath." We are all going down.

**A Sobering Picture**

By puberty, we reach our maximum height. From then on, we go down. Our spinal and knee cartilage degrades. Our spines bend. We stoop. We move to wheelchairs. We become nearly horizontal in bed. Finally, we're placed flat in our coffins and lowered into our graves. Then we become worm food, and along the food chain, fish food.

Friends, this is our ultimate fate—the consequence of our collective guilt.

But there is hope for us, just as there is hope for Jonah.

## Going Up (2:6-10)

Realizing his desperate predicament—the sea, the belly—something changes. In his going down, God intervenes:

> 6 ...the earth beneath barred me in forever. But you, Lord my God, brought my life **up** from the pit.

Because in verse 7:

> 7 "When my life was ebbing away, I remembered you, Lord, and my prayer **rose** to you, to your **holy temple.**

Jonah finally prayed. He prays toward the "holy temple"—the temple in Jerusalem, the sacred place of the Hebrews.

**Prayer and the Holy Temple**

The temple was meant to be both a place of prayer and the object of prayer itself. Solomon understood this when dedicating the temple (1 Kings 8):

> 27 "But will God really dwell on earth? The heavens, even the highest heaven, cannot contain you. How much less this temple I have built! 28 Yet give attention to your servant's prayer and his plea for mercy, Lord my God... 29 May your eyes be open toward this temple night and day, this place of which you said, 'My Name shall be there,' so that you will hear the prayer your servant prays toward this place. 30 Hear the supplication of your servant and of your people Israel when they pray toward this place. Hear from heaven, your dwelling place, and when you hear, forgive.

God's presence dwells there. As Israel spread across the land, the practice developed of facing toward the temple when praying—like facing Mecca. So Jonah prays UP—the only direction left.

### From Temple to Jesus

**In the New Testament**, the temple is no longer in Jerusalem. The true temple is Jesus.

When Jesus stood in that temple, he said:

> John 2:19 Jesus answered them, "Destroy this temple (meaning his body), and I will raise it again in three days."

Jesus is the one on whom God dwells. He intimately knows the Father because he comes from the Father—arriving on that first Christmas two thousand years ago.

So our prayer is ultimately a prayer to Jesus.

And because Jesus is not physically on earth, there is no particular location to pray toward—we can pray anywhere (bedroom, bathroom, bus). There is no particular time—we can pray continually, even more than five times daily.

We pray anytime, anywhere, in faith. We know he will answer because John 2:19 speaks of destruction and resurrection after three days. Jesus saves us through the *sign of Jonah*.

**The Connection**

Back to Matthew 12:

> 39 "A wicked and adulterous generation asks for a sign! But none will be given it except the sign of the prophet Jonah. 40 For as Jonah was three days and three nights in the belly of a huge fish, so the Son of Man will be three days and three nights in the heart of the earth.

"Three days and three nights"—the mark of descent into death. This is how long Jonah was in the sea and in the fish; it's to be in the place of the dead. Just like Jonah, Jesus descended into death. His body (the temple) was destroyed. He was dead for three days.

He did this *for us*.

And because of his love for us, we can be guaranteed an answer to prayer. Look back at verse 2:

> "In my distress I called to the Lord, and he answered me. From deep in the realm of the dead I called for help, and you listened to my cry.

**Salvation from the Lord**

> 8 "Those who cling to worthless idols turn away from God's love for them. 9 But I, with shouts of grateful praise, will sacrifice to you. What I have vowed I will make good. I will say, 'Salvation comes from the Lord.'"

And God answers:

> 10 And the Lord commanded the fish, and it vomited Jonah onto dry land.

**Application: Our Hope**

Jonah realized something we may have forgotten: there is only one way out of death. That's God. We cry out to him to rescue us. We pray to the true temple—Jesus—to save us.

And God promises to lift us up:

> Ephesians 2:4-7 But because of his great love for us, God, who is rich in mercy, made us alive with Christ even when we were dead in transgressions—it is by grace you have been saved. And God raised us up with Christ and seated us with him in the heavenly realms in Christ Jesus, in order that in the coming ages he might show the incomparable riches of his grace, expressed in his kindness to us in Christ Jesus.

Some of you haven't made that commitment. Perhaps today is your opportunity to do so.

## Conclusion: The Ultimate Miracle

This is the ultimate miracle surpassing all others—the miracle of salvation, the death and resurrection of Christ.

Salvation is from the Lord.

Though we deserve going down, there is a way up. Put your trust in him. Call on him.
