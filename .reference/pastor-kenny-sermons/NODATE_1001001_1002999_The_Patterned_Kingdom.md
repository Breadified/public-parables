# Genesis 1-2

**Title:** The Patterned Kingdom  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## God's Place (Genesis 1:1-2:14)

> In the beginning God created the heavens and the earth. Now the earth was formless and empty, darkness was over the surface of the deep, and the Spirit of God was hovering over the waters. (Genesis 1:1-2)

God begins with patterns and order. Notice the deliberate structure of creation—each day builds upon the last, each element serves a purpose. This is not chaos but design, not accident but intention.

> And God said, "Let there be light," and there was light. God saw that the light was good, and he separated the light from the darkness. (Genesis 1:3-4)

From the very first command, we see God creating through separation and distinction. Light from darkness. Order from void. The pattern continues as God speaks creation into being: waters separated from waters, land from sea, vegetation from barren ground. Each day declares God's sovereignty and careful design.

> Let the water under the sky be gathered to one place, and let dry ground appear. (Genesis 1:9)

The earth takes shape—land and sea, sky and stars, each in its proper place. God sets two great lights to govern day and night, marking seasons and years. He fills the waters with living creatures and the air with birds. The land brings forth animals according to their kinds.

> Then God said, "Let us make man in our image, in our likeness, and let them rule over the fish of the sea and the birds of the air, over the livestock, over all the earth, and over all the creatures that move along the ground." So God created man in his own image, in the image of God he created him; male and female he created them. (Genesis 1:26-27)

Here stands the pinnacle of creation. Humanity alone bears God's image. Not as objects, but as stewards—given dominion over creation, blessed to be fruitful and multiply. God sees all He has made, and it is very good.

> By the seventh day God had finished the work he had been doing; so on the seventh day he rested from all his work. And God blessed the seventh day and made it holy. (Genesis 2:2-3)

Even God's rest is part of the pattern—six days of creative work, one day of sanctified rest. This rhythm reflects God's character and sets the template for human life.

## God's People (Genesis 2:7-25)

> The LORD God formed the man from the dust of the ground and breathed into his nostrils the breath of life, and the man became a living being. (Genesis 2:7)

Adam is created with intimate care—formed from dust, yet animated by God's own breath. He is both creature and image-bearer, both earthbound and divinely inspired.

> Now the LORD God had planted a garden in the east, in Eden; and there he put the man he had formed. And the LORD God made all kinds of trees grow out of the ground—trees that were pleasing to the eye and good for food. In the middle of the garden were the tree of life and the tree of the knowledge of good and evil. (Genesis 2:8-9)

God establishes Eden—a place of abundance, beauty, and purpose. Adam is placed there not to idle but to work and steward God's creation. This teaches us something vital: work is not a curse but a calling. Human flourishing includes meaningful labor.

> A river watering the garden flowed from Eden; from there it was separated into four headwaters. (Genesis 2:10)

Even in paradise, we see the pattern—rivers flowing, boundaries established, order maintained. God's design extends to the very geography of Eden.

> The LORD God took the man and put him in the Garden of Eden to work it and take care of it. And the LORD God commanded the man, "You are free to eat from any tree in the garden; but you must not eat from the tree of the knowledge of good and evil, for when you eat of it you will surely die." (Genesis 2:15-17)

Freedom and boundary work together. Adam receives both privilege and responsibility, both blessing and command. This reveals God's nature: He grants genuine freedom while maintaining moral order. The consequence is not arbitrary—it is the natural fruit of rebellion against the source of life itself.

> The LORD God said, "It is not good for the man to be alone. I will make a helper suitable for him." (Genesis 2:18)

Solitude is the only thing God declares "not good" in creation. Humanity is made for relationship, for partnership, for mutual help and companionship. This is not weakness but design.

> Now the LORD God had formed out of the ground all the beasts of the field and all the birds of the air. He brought them to the man to see what he would name them; and whatever the man called each living creature, that was its name. But for Adam no suitable helper was found. (Genesis 2:19-20)

Adam names the animals—exercising dominion, expressing creativity, recognizing that none of them meet his need for true companionship. The animals are good, but insufficient.

> So the LORD God caused the man to fall into a deep sleep; and while he was sleeping, he took one of the man's ribs and closed up the place with flesh. Then the LORD God made a woman from the rib he had taken out of the man, and he brought her to the man. The man said, "This is now bone of my bones and flesh of my flesh; she shall be called 'woman,' for she was taken out of man." (Genesis 2:21-23)

From Adam's own body, God fashions woman—not from dust like Adam, but from his very self. She is his counterpart, his equal, his complement. Adam's recognition is immediate and joyful. Here is the pattern for human partnership: one flesh, one commitment, one covenant.

> For this reason a man will leave his father and mother and be united to his wife, and they will become one flesh. The man and his wife were both naked, and they felt no shame. (Genesis 2:24-25)

Marriage reflects creation's pattern—two becoming one, nakedness met with trust rather than fear, intimacy without shame. This is the original design: covenant, vulnerability, and mutual belonging.

## God's Rule (Genesis 2:15-17)

The command reveals God's nature as Creator and Lawgiver. He establishes boundaries not to diminish Adam's freedom but to preserve it. 

**God's Prerogative:** As Creator, God alone determines the moral order. He speaks, and reality conforms to His word.

**Human Stewardship:** Adam is appointed as God's vice-regent—one who rules under God's authority, caring for creation on His behalf. This is not tyranny delegated but trust placed.

**The Pattern for All Time:** What we see in Eden becomes the template for all of Scripture and all of human life. Work is sacred. Rest is holy. Relationship is essential. Obedience to God's word is the foundation of life itself. Rebellion carries real consequences, not as punishment but as separation from the Source of all life.

Human identity and purpose flow from this foundational pattern. We are God's image-bearers, called to steward His creation, to love one another, and to live under His wise and loving rule.
