# Genesis 2:18-25

**Title:** The Ultimate Rom-Com
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction: A Familiar Story

We know the opening scene well. Boy meets girl. Eyes lock. He falls for her. The wedding day arrives. They live happily ever after. It reads like every Hollywood rom-com released just in time for Valentine's Day. But I'm not talking about the latest movie. I'm talking about Genesis chapter 2—the ultimate love story, written by God himself.

## The Man: Created for Purpose

### Why Man First?

God created man before the vegetation. You might wonder why. The reason is not very romantic: *there was no man to work the ground* (Genesis 2:5). Work came first in God's design—purposeful work in a place of beauty.

But notice what God does. He doesn't throw man into a harsh wilderness. God plants a garden for him (Genesis 2:8). He provides trees for food and for beauty. He gives man clear instructions: *The Lord God took the man and put him in the Garden of Eden to work it and take care of it* (Genesis 2:15).

### The Vision: Expand the Garden

God's intention wasn't for Adam to maintain a tiny plot. It was to start with an established garden and then expand it—to cultivate and care for creation itself. This is the job: work the ground, grow things, build something. Picture a man fully alive in his purpose—doing meaningful work, coming home satisfied, at peace.

## The Woman: Created as Helper

### The Problem with Alone

Then God says something striking: *The Lord God said, "It is not good for the man to be alone"* (Genesis 2:18). 

Now, the world has misunderstood this verse. Many equate "alone" with loneliness and claim you need another person to complete you. Some say sex and romantic love are the solution to isolation. Others market the lie: "You complete me," or "Find your person and you'll never be lonely again."

But look at what Scripture actually says about loneliness. In Psalm 68:5-6, David writes:

> A father to the fatherless, a defender of widows, is God in his holy dwelling. God sets the lonely in families; he leads forth the prisoners with singing.

Real loneliness is cured by God and by the family of God—by friendship and community. Jesus taught this: *My command is this: Love each other as I have loved you. Greater love has no one than this, that he lay down his life for his friends* (John 15:12-13).

Look at the divorce statistics. Hollywood romantics end in heartbreak. People have sex but feel profoundly insecure and alone. The world's answer—romantic love—doesn't actually solve loneliness. Only friendship with God and with each other does.

### What "Alone" Really Means

So what does "alone" actually mean in Genesis 2:18? Read the full verse: *It is not good for the man to be alone. I will make a helper suitable for him.*

It's not about emotional loneliness. It's about needing help. Imagine you're trying to move a big box by yourself. You need assistance—someone to lift the other end. That's what this is about.

### The Helper's Purpose

What is Adam supposed to do with this helper? Remember his job: *work the ground and take care of the garden* (Genesis 2:15). This isn't about brute strength—another man could do that. But God has given humanity a different command: *Be fruitful and increase in number, fill the earth and subdue it* (Genesis 1:28).

Only one man and one woman together can fulfill this command. They can create new life. They can build a family. This is sacred work—to be "fruitful," to multiply, to fill the earth. If you're too young for marriage, you're too young for this responsibility. If you're still a child yourself, why date? You're not ready for the weight of this calling.

## The Fall: Adam's Response

Adam sees this woman, and he falls—literally falls for her. Look at his words when he sees her:

> Woman was not created from man's head to rule over him,
> Nor from his feet to be trampled by him.
> She was taken from his side to be his equal,
> From beneath his arm to be protected by him,
> From near his heart to be loved by him.

Listen to his joy in Genesis 2:23: *The man said, "This is now bone of my bones and flesh of my flesh; she shall be called 'woman,' for she was taken out of man."* And the text concludes with: *They were both naked, and they felt no shame* (Genesis 2:25).

United. One flesh. A happy ending.

But is it? Turn to Genesis 3, and the story takes a tragic turn. The beauty of God's design is about to be shattered by choice.
