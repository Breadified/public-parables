# Zephaniah 2:4–3:7

**Title:** Judgment Day: Justice  
**Date:** 25 April 2010  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## God Punishes the Wicked

What does justice truly mean? Consider the case of Carl Williams, a Melbourne gangster who operated a drug syndicate and was convicted of four murders during Victoria's gangland wars. At 39 years old, he showed only ruthlessness and no compassion throughout his life. Even at sentencing, he expressed no regret or apology for any of his actions. When he was murdered inside a high-security prison, Victorian Premier John Brumby rejected calls for an investigation, noting that while the prison murder was unacceptable, it would be wasteful to investigate the death of someone who had already cost the state substantial resources in prosecution and incarceration.

This raises a profound question: What is truly fair? The biblical answer comes through the book of Zephaniah, which presents God's judgment on the nations surrounding Judah—a perspective that challenges our modern sensibilities about justice.

## The Judgment on the Nations

Zephaniah has already spoken of the world's destruction. Now he turns to the immediate enemies of Judah, organizing his oracle geographically in expanding circles.

### West: The Philistines (2:4–7)

> Gaza will be abandoned and Ashkelon left in ruins. At midday Ashdod will be emptied and Ekron uprooted. Woe to you who live by the sea, O Kerethite people; the word of the LORD is against you, O Canaan, land of the Philistines. "I will destroy you, and none will be left." (Zephaniah 2:4–5)

Gaza, Ashkelon, Ashdod, and Ekron—the coastal cities of the Philistines and Canaanites, roughly where the modern Gaza Strip lies. These were the very people the Israelites were commanded to drive out during Joshua's conquest, a task left incomplete. Later, King Saul was similarly commanded to destroy them but failed. Here, through Zephaniah, God announces the fulfillment of that ancient promise.

> The land by the sea, where the Kerethites dwell, will be a place for shepherds and sheep pens. It will belong to the remnant of the house of Judah; there they will find pasture. In the evening they will lie down in the houses of Ashkelon. The LORD their God will care for them; he will restore their fortunes. (Zephaniah 2:6–7)

The devastation promised at midday (verse 4) gives way to Judah's blessing by evening. The remnant of Judah will inhabit these coastal lands, finding pasture for their flocks and dwelling in the cities of their former enemies. The Lord will care for them and restore their fortunes.

### East: Moab and Ammon (2:8–11)

> "I have heard the insults of Moab and the taunts of the Ammonites, who insulted my people and made threats against their land." (Zephaniah 2:8)

Moab and Ammon were cousin nations to Israel, descended from Lot, Abraham's nephew. Though they shared kinship, they harbored great pride. When Israel passed through the land during the exodus, God protected these cousins out of loyalty to Lot:

> "Do not harass the Moabites or provoke them to war, for I will not give you any part of their land. I have given Ar to the descendants of Lot as a possession." (Deuteronomy 2:9)

Yet Moab and Ammon repaid this kindness with fear and treachery. Terrified of the migrating Israelites, they hired the prophet Balaam to curse Israel. Though God transformed Balaam's curse into a blessing, this betrayal had lasting consequences:

> No Ammonite or Moabite or any of his descendants may enter the assembly of the LORD, even down to the tenth generation. For they did not come to meet you with bread and water on your way when you came out of Egypt, and they hired Balaam son of Beor to pronounce a curse on you. (Deuteronomy 23:3–4)

Later, during the Judges period, Moab and Ammon even subjugated Israel. Now judgment comes:

> "Therefore, as surely as I live," declares the LORD Almighty, the God of Israel, "surely Moab will become like Sodom, the Ammonites like Gomorrah—a place of weeds and salt pits, a wasteland forever. The remnant of my people will plunder them; the survivors of my nation will inherit their land." (Zephaniah 2:9)

The irony cuts deep: Moab and Ammon will experience the fate of Sodom and Gomorrah—the very cities where Lot once dwelled before his flight. The reference to "weeds and salt pits" echoes Lot's wife, transformed into a pillar of salt. What was left undone in Joshua's conquest—the complete possession of the land—will finally be accomplished.

### South: Cush (2:12)

> "You too, O Cushites, will be slain by my sword." (Zephaniah 2:12)

Cush, the ancient name for Ethiopia and the broader African nations including Egypt, receives a brief but definitive judgment.

### North: Assyria (2:13–3:4)

> He will stretch out his hand against the north and destroy Assyria, leaving Nineveh utterly desolate and dry as the desert. Flocks and herds will lie down there, creatures of every kind. The desert owl and the screech owl will roost on her columns. Their calls will echo through the windows, rubble will be in the doorways, the beams of cedar will be exposed. (Zephaniah 2:13–14)

Nineveh, the "exceeding great city" of Assyria, stands north of Judah—the most distant point in this expanding circle of judgment. This magnificent metropolis, celebrated in the book of Jonah, will become a desolate wasteland where wild animals nest among broken columns and scattered rubble.

> This is the carefree city that lived in safety. She said to herself, "I am, and there is none besides me." What a ruin she has become, a lair for wild beasts! All who pass by her scoff and shake their fists. (Zephaniah 2:15)

Nineveh embodied arrogance—the confidence of a city that believed itself invincible, accountable to none. Yet her ruin will become a byword, a cautionary tale for all who witness her desolation.

## Why Is This Fair?

A natural question arises: Why does God judge these nations? Why not leave them to their own lands? Didn't they exist before Israel? And if God is a loving God, why does He seemingly promote warfare?

### Not Arbitrary Punishment, But Justice

First, these judgments are not arbitrary. They are rooted in sin—real, documented rebellion against God.

- **Moab and Ammon** taunted and backstabbed Israel, despite God's kindness toward them.
- **Assyria** dislocated entire populations and dominated the Middle East through brutal conquest. Nineveh had been warned by the prophet Jonah.
- All these nations were wicked and warring states.

Scripture attests to their guilt:

> "Surely you will fear me and accept correction!' Then her dwelling would not be cut off, nor all my punishments come upon her. But they were still eager to act corruptly in all they did." (Zephaniah 3:7)

### Absolutism: God's Exclusive Claim

Second, God operates on a principle of absolutism. He is the only God and therefore demands absolute obedience and loyalty. This means humanity faces a fundamental choice: follow Yahweh and experience salvation, or follow another god and face destruction. There is no neutral ground.

This may sound oppressive—like an absolute dictator. Yet we understand this principle instinctively in our own families.

Parents establish absolute boundaries for the good of the whole family: children must do their chores, complete their homework, respect their parents, maintain curfews, and abstain from harmful behaviors. If a child persistently violates these boundaries, family life becomes impossible. Eventually, someone must leave home. We accept this as normal and necessary.

God, as the creator of the universe and the source of all order, establishes similar absolute standards. He gives humanity freedom—"eat from anywhere but this one tree"—but also sets limits. To ask, "If you're going to restrict me, why did you conceive me?" misses the point. Authority and boundaries are necessary for flourishing.

This absolutism is not limited to the past. The book of Revelation reveals that it will continue into the future:

> The armies of heaven were following him, riding on white horses and dressed in fine linen, white and clean. Out of his mouth comes a sharp sword with which to strike down the nations. "He will rule them with an iron scepter." He treads the winepress of the fury of the wrath of God Almighty. (Revelation 19:14–15)

Judgment past. Judgment future. Absolutism bookends history.

## But What About God's Patience and Compassion?

You might protest: "But we thought God was patient and tolerant!"

You're right. Between the judgments of the past and the judgments of the future lies the present—our time. In this age, God has emphasized compassion and patience.

### Understanding Tolerance

In modern Christian nations since the 16th century, we've developed a concept of tolerance: citizens can remain loyal to their nation while holding different beliefs than the government. Diversity of opinion is no longer seen as a threat to national unity.

However, tolerance is often confused with relativism—the belief that all views are equally valid. This is a critical error. Relativism, while sounding tolerant, is both intellectually and socially self-defeating.

**Tolerance is not an intellectual position but a social virtue.** It is the willingness to allow, endure, and live alongside people and beliefs with which we disagree—not because all views are equally true, but because we choose to coexist peaceably.

God exhibits something even higher: mercy. He has given us time to choose Him—not through coercion, but through persuasion.

Tolerance is distinctively Christian because we believe God will ultimately judge. Tolerance means holding back our judgment and bearing with those we disagree with, seeking common ground for peaceful coexistence. This does not mean accepting all ways of living as equal or all truths as equivalent.

- **Islam** has not historically embraced tolerance in the present age (violent reactions to perceived insults demonstrate this).
- **Buddhism** confuses tolerance with apathy—"I don't care" is not toleration.
- **Christianity** is unique in granting tolerance as a space for people to discover truth.

God is remarkably kind in tolerating sin now. But make no mistake: His tolerance has limits, and judgment is coming.

## What This Should Move Us to Do

Knowing God's patience is temporary and His judgment is certain should transform how we live:

### 1. Cultivate Compassion

Our methods must be persuasion, not coercion. There is no room for militant Christianity that imposes belief through force or intimidation. Instead, we follow the model of 1 Peter: quiet witness, humble submission, faithful endurance through martyrdom if necessary. Missionaries throughout South Asia, in our schools and families—all bearing witness through transformed lives, not arrogance.

### 2. Live with Gratitude

We live in a time of grace—a grace period, much like a parking ticket you've been forgiven. By the grace of God, we could be in the same position as Judah or the nations now facing judgment. Our existence as believers is a gift of divine patience.

### 3. Proclaim the Son!

The knowledge of coming judgment should compel us to share the gospel, urging others to find their salvation in Christ before time runs out.

## Judah: No Excuse

Yet judgment falls not only on foreign nations. It falls on Judah herself—and this is the most shocking part.

> Woe to the city of oppressors, rebellious and defiled! She obeys no one, she accepts no correction. She does not trust in the LORD, she does not draw near to her God. (Zephaniah 3:1–2)

At first reading, we might think Zephaniah continues condemning Nineveh. But clues reveal otherwise. The mention of prophets, priests, the law, and drawing near to God—these are markers of Jerusalem, not Nineveh.

> Her officials are roaring lions, her rulers are evening wolves, who leave nothing for the morning. Her prophets are arrogant; they are treacherous men. Her priests profane the sanctuary and do violence to the law. (Zephaniah 3:3–4)

Jerusalem's leaders are described as wild animals—worse even than the creatures that will inhabit Nineveh's ruins. Her prophets are arrogant like Moab; her priests profane the very sanctuary they are meant to keep holy.

Jerusalem is a profound disappointment precisely because she should know better.

> The LORD within her is righteous; he does no wrong. Morning by morning he dispenses his justice, and every new day he does not fail, yet the unrighteous know no shame. (Zephaniah 3:5)

Yahweh dwells within Jerusalem. His righteous presence is constantly before them. Every morning, when the sun rises behind the temple, the entire city should awaken to the reality of God's justice and goodness. Yet Jerusalem has become blind to this daily mercy.

Instead of justice in the morning, her leaders devour the spoils through nocturnal prowling—"leaving nothing for the morning." They have perverted their calling.

### A Lesson for Christians Today

As Christians, we should know better. We have:

- The complete revelation of Scripture
- Direct access to God through Christ's blood (Hebrews 10:19–20)
- A great high priest over God's house
- The ability to draw near to God with sincere hearts

> Since we have confidence to enter the Most Holy Place by the blood of Jesus, by a new and living way opened for us through the curtain, that is, his body, and since we have a great priest over the house of God, let us draw near to God with a sincere heart in full assurance of faith, having our hearts sprinkled to cleanse us from a guilty conscience and having our bodies washed with pure water. (Hebrews 10:19–22)

Yet the warning is equally stark:

> If we deliberately keep on sinning after we have received the knowledge of the truth, no sacrifice for sins is left, but only a fearful expectation of judgment and of raging fire that will consume the enemies of God. How much more severely do you think a man deserves to be punished who has trampled the Son of God under foot, who has treated as an unholy thing the blood of the covenant that sanctified him, and who has insulted the Spirit of grace? (Hebrews 10:26–29)

We are called to:

- **Do away with sin.** We have been given new life in Christ.
- **Submit to God.** We know He is Lord.
- **Draw near to Him in fellowship.** We know we can approach because His blood has forgiven us.

## God Is a God of Justice

Justice is not about personality or favoritism. Justice is justice. You cannot commit murder even if your victim was a criminal or a prisoner. Two wrongs do not make a right. God does not show favoritism in judgment; He upholds the integrity of the law itself.

All appropriate steps must be taken because justice concerns not the character of the offender but the character of the law and the order God has established. Justice demands that wrongs be addressed, that accountability be maintained, and that God's righteous standards be upheld.

This is the heart of the message: **God punishes the wicked because He is just.**
