# Amos 1:1-3:2

**Title:** Judgment for All Nations

**Date:** 5 November 2006

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Who Is Amos?

> The words of Amos, one of the shepherds of Tekoa—what he saw concerning Israel two years before the earthquake, when Uzziah was king of Judah and Jeroboam son of Jehoash was king of Israel. (Amos 1:1)

Amos was an ordinary shepherd from Tekoa, not a priest or professional prophet. Yet God called him to deliver a message to Israel, the northern kingdom. To understand his context, remember that Israel and Judah had split into two nations—the larger kingdom in the north and smaller tribes in the south. Amos had to journey north to prophesy to Israel.

At the time of his ministry, Israel was experiencing economic prosperity. Syria, which had previously dominated Israel and demanded tribute, was now occupied with threats from Assyria. This freed Israel to reclaim lost territory and grow wealthier. To the Israelites, everything seemed good—surely God's favor must be upon them. They expected good news from their prophet.

Instead, Amos brought words of judgment.

## The Message: Judgment of Eight Nations

> He said: "The LORD roars from Zion and thunders from Jerusalem; the pastures of the shepherds dry up, and the top of Carmel withers." (Amos 1:2)

The book of Amos opens with a striking pattern of judgment against eight nations. As Amos speaks, the Israelites hear condemnation falling upon their neighbors—the very nations they despised and whom they viewed as rivals. How satisfying! Surely this prophet brings good tidings of their enemies' destruction.

The judgment formula repeats throughout these chapters, following a consistent pattern:

1. **The Formula:** "This is what the Lord says: 'For three sins of [nation], even for four, I will not turn back my wrath.'"
2. **The Crime:** A specific accusation of rebellion against God's moral order
3. **The Punishment:** Exile, destruction, death

### Three Truths in God's Judgment

#### God's Patience

The phrase "three sins, even for four" expresses something profound: God is infinitely patient. He does not punish hastily. Instead, He waits, giving nations repeated opportunities to turn from their wickedness.

God's patience is remarkable but often misunderstood. The nations interpret His restraint as indifference, not recognizing it as an invitation to repentance. They commit sin after sin, thinking their wickedness goes unnoticed. God waits, holds back His judgment, waits again—but instead of repenting, they sin more. Finally, when the cup is full and patience is exhausted, His judgment comes swift and terrible.

It is like a parent's discipline. A child is told repeatedly to do their chores, but ignores the warnings. First, the parent waits. Then waits again. Eventually, patience runs out and discipline falls hard. The child cries, "What's the big deal? I only watched TV!" But it was the tenth time, the hundredth disregard. The punishment is not for one small act but for years of willful disobedience. By the time judgment falls, it is long overdue.

#### Crimes Against Humanity

The nations surrounding Israel are condemned not for attacking Israel specifically, but for crimes against humanity—violations of the basic moral law written into creation.

**Damascus** (Syria) is accused of commercial and military exploitation—using ruthless force to dominate others for profit.

**Gaza** is condemned for slave-trading on a massive scale. God expects humans to respect human dignity. The horror is absolute: "whole communities taken captive" and "the remnant is not left"—total, systematic slavery.

**Tyre** is judged for the same slavery, and worse: they break covenant, violating the sacred bond of brotherhood. God cares deeply about loyalty and fidelity, even among nations who do not acknowledge Him.

**Edom**, descended from Jacob's own brother Esau, is condemned for attacking their relatives with unbridled rage and untempered fury. Betrayal of family adds another layer of wickedness.

**Ammon** pursues territorial conquest through terrorism and atrocities—ripping open pregnant women to terrorize and expand borders. These are war crimes that shock the conscience.

**Moab** commits a final desecration: they burn the bones of Edom's king. In the Old Testament world, cremation was a profound sign of disrespect and contempt for the dead.

**Judah**, God's own covenant people, is judged differently. Unlike the surrounding nations, Judah is not condemned for atrocities against other peoples. Instead, they are judged for rejecting God's law and following false gods—for rejecting the very revelation they possessed.

**Israel** faces the harshest judgment of all. Like the other nations, they commit injustice—but against their own people, those belonging to God. They:

- Sell the righteous into slavery for money and the poor for a pair of sandals
- Trample the heads of the poor as dust
- Deny justice to the oppressed
- Sexually exploit slave girls, defiling God's holy name
- Worship idols and practice evil at their altars

This teaches a vital principle reflected in Paul's letter to Rome: God holds all people accountable to His moral order. The nations without the law are condemned for violating the law written on their hearts—conscience and natural human dignity. Those with the law are judged by that law. Whether revealed or natural, God's moral standard applies to all people, and all are guilty before Him.

> All who sin apart from the law will also perish apart from the law, and all who sin under the law will be judged by the law. For it is not those who hear the law who are righteous in God's sight, but it is those who obey the law who will be declared righteous. (Romans 2:12-13)

#### Punishment: Exile and Annihilation

The punishment that falls is consistent: fire consumes fortresses, cities are destroyed, kings and officials are killed or exiled. The leaders who orchestrated injustice bear the weight of judgment. Those responsible for the nation's wickedness are removed.

The summary: exile and annihilation. Those who refuse to do what is right before God are destroyed through war and death.

## The Shock: Israel Is the Eighth Nation

You can imagine the scene. The Israelite crowd stands listening as Amos names nation after nation—their enemies, their rivals, the people they despise. "Yes! Yes! God will judge them!" They feel vindicated, almost joyful.

And then comes the final judgment. The eighth nation.

It is Israel.

> For three sins of Israel, even for four, I will not turn back my wrath. (Amos 2:6)

The shock is devastating. "That's us!"

## Why Israel? The Privilege That Demands Responsibility

Israel's sin is doubly grave because of what they have been given. While other nations sin in ignorance or rebellion, Israel sins against the light of God's favor.

Consider what God had done for them:
- He brought them out of Egypt with signs and wonders
- He provided for them for forty years in the wilderness
- He gave them the land of the Amorites, conquering enemies far greater and stronger than themselves
- He gave them His law—the Torah
- He raised up prophets to warn them when they strayed
- He set apart Nazirites as signs of His calling

Yet how did Israel respond? They:
- Made the Nazirites drink wine, defiling them
- Commanded the prophets not to prophesy, silencing God's voice

Israel rejected her privileges. She had every advantage, every warning, every opportunity to walk in God's ways. Therefore, her judgment is most severe.

> Now then, I will crush you as a cart crushes when loaded with grain. The swift will not escape, the strong will not muster their strength, and the warrior will not save his life. The archer will not stand his ground, the fleet-footed soldier will not get away, and the horseman will not save his life. Even the bravest warriors will flee naked on that day. (Amos 2:13-16)

## The Final Word: Chosen, Therefore Judged

> Hear this word the LORD has spoken against you, O people of Israel—against the whole family I brought up out of Egypt: "You only have I chosen of all the families of the earth; therefore I will punish you for all your sins." (Amos 3:1-2)

The logic is striking: election brings accountability. Israel is chosen not for exemption from judgment but for stricter judgment. Those loved most are held to the highest standard. Those entrusted with the most are answerable for the most.

## Application Today

As we look at our world—ethnic cleansing, terrorism, exploitation, cruelty—we feel outraged and helpless. How can God allow such things? Amos's answer is clear: God is patient, but His patience is not infinite. He waits for three sins, even four, but there is an end. He will judge.

And here is the stunning truth: every person is accountable to their Maker. These ancient nations did not have Scripture, yet God held them responsible for basic human morality. Why? Because all people are made in the image of God. Life is sacred. To assault one another violently is to rebel against the Creator. God will certainly and thoroughly judge such behavior.

But there is also a word of hope for those who trust in Jesus. Judgment is not only future—it has already come in the cross of Christ. By our sin, we crucified the Holy One sent from God. On the cross, the world was tried, found guilty, and sentenced. Yet for those who are friends with Jesus, forgiven through His sacrifice, judgment's penalty has been paid.

For those outside Christ, however, judgment still looms. A day is coming when all must answer to the Judge. And therefore the question haunts us: Are you ready to meet Jesus? Do your practices today reflect that you believe He is returning? Do you pursue justice, show mercy, and live with compassion as those who have been redeemed?

We who have received salvation dare not treat it complacently. We may have the Bible, attend church, speak Christianly—but do we act righteously? Do we have compassion on our neighbor? If we are worried about judgment, if we sense the weight of our sin and our need of God's forgiveness, there is good news: we can have assurance today.
