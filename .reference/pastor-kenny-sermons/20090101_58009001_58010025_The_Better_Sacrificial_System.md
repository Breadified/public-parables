# Hebrews 9:1-10:25

**Title:** The Better Sacrificial System
**Date:** 25 January 2009
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction: Is Church Obsolete?

Some argue that church itself is obsolete—a vestige of tradition and bigotry. But before we dismiss our gathered worship, we need to ask a deeper question: What has Jesus actually come to do? Rather than clinging to inherited practices, we must evaluate our worship against Christ's intentions for us. The book of Hebrews calls us back to understand this clearly.

## 1. The Levitical System Never Worked

> **Hebrews 8:13** — By calling this covenant "new," he has made the first one obsolete; and what is obsolete and aging will soon disappear.

### The Old Testament Sacrificial System

God established an elaborate system of worship under the first covenant, detailed in Hebrews 9:1-7. This system had three essential components:

**The Temple (Hebrews 9:2-5)**

The tabernacle contained two rooms. The outer room, called the Holy Place, held the lampstand, the table, and the consecrated bread. Behind a curtain lay the Most Holy Place, containing the golden altar of incense and the ark of the covenant. Inside the ark were the gold jar of manna, Aaron's staff that had budded, and the stone tablets. Above it all were the cherubim of the Glory, overshadowing the atonement cover—a room so sacred that humans could scarcely approach.

**The Priest (Hebrews 9:6-7)**

Regular priests entered the outer room to carry out their duties. But only the high priest could enter the Most Holy Place, and only once each year, never without blood to offer for his own sins and the sins committed in ignorance by the people.

**The Offering (Hebrews 9:7)**

A complex sacrificial system developed—the prescribed way to please God. You needed a special place, special religious officials, and special offerings. The entire system pointed to the reality that approaching a holy God required mediation, blood, and atonement.

### Why This System Never Worked

The crucial insight comes in Hebrews 9:8-10:

> **Hebrews 9:8-10** — The Holy Spirit was showing by this that the way into the Most Holy Place had not yet been disclosed as long as the first tabernacle was still standing. This is an illustration for the present time, indicating that the gifts and sacrifices being offered were not able to clear the conscience of the worshiper. They are only a matter of food and drink and various ceremonial washings—external regulations applying until the time of the new order.

The system was designed as an *illustration*, a temporary prototype pointing to something better. It could never actually cleanse the conscience or take away sin. Think of it like a toy car—it looks like the real thing, but it was never designed to be driven on roads or registered for actual use. It can only show a child what driving might be like. To give such a toy to an adult would be an insult, because it fundamentally cannot do what a real car does.

The repeated nature of the sacrifices proves this:

> **Hebrews 10:1-4** — The law is only a shadow of the good things that are coming—not the realities themselves. For this reason it can never, by the same sacrifices repeated endlessly year after year, make perfect those who draw near to worship. If it could, would they not have stopped being offered? For the worshipers would have been cleansed once for all, and would no longer have felt guilty for their sins. But those sacrifices are an annual reminder of sins, because it is impossible for the blood of bulls and goats to take away sins.

If the sacrifices actually worked, they would only need to be offered once. But they had to be repeated year after year—evidence that the system was never designed to accomplish its ultimate end. It was always meant to point forward to a better sacrifice, a better priest, a better temple, and a better way into God's presence.

## 2. The Jesus System Works

> **Hebrews 9:11-15** — When Christ came as high priest of the good things that are already here, he went through the greater and more perfect tabernacle that is not man-made, that is to say, not a part of this creation. He did not enter by means of the blood of goats and calves; but he entered the Most Holy Place once for all by his own blood, having obtained eternal redemption. The blood of goats and bulls and the ashes of a heifer sprinkled on those who are ceremonially unclean sanctify them so that they are outwardly clean. How much more, then, will the blood of Christ, who through the eternal Spirit offered himself unblemished to God, cleanse our consciences from acts that lead to death, so that we may serve the living God! For this reason Christ is the mediator of a new covenant, that those who are called may receive the promised eternal inheritance—now that he has died as a ransom to set them free from the sins committed under the first covenant.

Where the old system failed, Jesus succeeds completely:

- **The Temple**: Jesus himself is the true Most Holy Place. His body is the tabernacle, the meeting place between God and humanity.
- **The Priest**: Jesus is the ultimate high priest, after the order of Melchizedek—not a temporary mediator but the eternal one.
- **The Offering**: His own blood, offered once for all, accomplishes what no animal sacrifice ever could.

Jesus is the ultimate worshipper. He approaches God the Father in the heavenly sanctuary, offers his own unblemished life, and obtains eternal redemption for all who believe.

### The Veil Torn

The connection becomes physical and dramatic at Christ's death:

> **Mark 15:37-39** — With a loud cry, Jesus breathed his last. The curtain of the temple was torn in two from top to bottom. And when the centurion, who stood there in front of Jesus, heard his cry and saw how he died, he said, "Surely this man was the Son of God!"

The curtain that separated the Most Holy Place—that sacred divide which no one could cross except the high priest once yearly—was torn in two from top to bottom. The barrier is gone. Jesus' body was broken so that direct access to God could be opened to all. What a wonderful system!

## 3. We Don't Need to Repeat the Old System

Now comes the critical application that many churches miss. Jesus has fulfilled the Levitical system completely. He is the new, perfect car—the Subaru WRX with the turbo, to use the illustration. There is no need to get another car. There is no need to pretend to get another car. In fact, to attempt to repeat the old system would dishonour Jesus.

Look at how the early church actually met:

> **Acts 2:42** — They devoted themselves to the apostles' teaching and to the fellowship, to the breaking of bread and to prayer.

Notice what they *didn't* do: they didn't go to the temple to worship, bring offerings to priests, or perform sacrifices. Instead, they gathered for something beautifully ordinary—learning Scripture together, sharing life in community, breaking bread over a meal, and praying. No priests, no temple, no offerings. Just believers learning about Jesus, encouraging one another to follow him, and loving one another.

### How the Church Lost This Vision

Unfortunately, many churches have drifted back toward the old system without realizing it. In the Middle Ages, when Christianity became the dominant religion, the church began reintroducing Old Testament worship patterns. Partly this happened because victors often absorb the practices of the vanquished—Christians took over Jewish religious structures. Partly it happened because the church became superstitious and forgot what Jesus had accomplished.

So gradually, the reinterpretation began:

- Churches became *temples*—sacred buildings where God specially dwells
- Altars replaced simple tables
- The Lord's Supper became a *sacrifice*—something offered to God
- Pastors became *priests*—religious officials mediating between God and people
- Donations became *offerings*—sacrifices presented to God
- Singing became solemn *chants*—worship performances rather than joyful expression

The Reformation corrected some of the theology, but the practices lingered. Many churches today look more like temples than fellowship meetings. We still have:

- **Priests** (called pastors or ministers, but functioning as intermediaries)
- **Temples** (sacred church buildings where we come to meet God)
- **Sacrifices** (offerings of money, song, or bodily posture presented to God)

### The Problem with Religious Songs

Listen to how many worship songs still reflect a Levitical mindset:

*"We enter your courts, Lord, with grace and thanksgiving... with hands lifted up..."*

*"I come to You in humble sacrifice... In awe of You I worship..."*

*"Here I am to worship, here I am to bow down..."*

These lyrics—bowing, entering sacred courts, making sacrifices, presenting ourselves—perpetuate the old system's thinking. While pleasant to sing, they can teach an unhelpful theology to those who don't yet understand that Jesus has abolished the need for all this. We're not entering God's courts as supplicants presenting sacrifice. We're approaching boldly as children of God, cleansed by Christ's blood.

### What God Actually Wants

Here's what Scripture reveals God desires instead of sacrifices:

> **Hebrews 10:8-10** — First he said, "Sacrifices and offerings, burnt offerings and sin offerings you did not desire, nor were you pleased with them" (although the law required them to be made). Then he said, "Here I am, I have come to do your will." He sets aside the first to establish the second. And by that will, we have been made holy through the sacrifice of the body of Jesus Christ once for all.

God never wanted sacrifices for their own sake. He wants *obedience*. He wants *godliness*. He wants *fellowship with one another*. He wants *love*.

The collection time in our gatherings is a good example. We should never frame it as an "offering"—as if we're presenting a sacrifice to God. God doesn't need our money any more than he needed little oranges placed on altars or the blood of bulls and goats. Instead, give generously because you want to support the work of ministry, because you want others to hear about Jesus, because you want to be generous. But never give thinking this pleases God directly, makes you closer to him, or will incur his anger if you don't. His anger toward us was already satisfied at the cross. Because of Jesus' blood, God is no longer angry at those who believe.

## 4. Living in the Reality Jesus Opened

The book of Hebrews tells us what our actual practice should be. Notice the four "let us" statements in Hebrews 10:19-25—two directed toward our relationship with God, and two toward our relationships with each other:

### In Relation to God (10:19-23)

> **Hebrews 10:19-23** — Therefore, brothers, since we have confidence to enter the Most Holy Place by the blood of Jesus, by a new and living way opened for us through the curtain, that is, his body, and since we have a great priest over the house of God, let us draw near to God with a sincere heart in full assurance of faith, having our hearts sprinkled to cleanse us from a guilty conscience and having our bodies washed with pure water. Let us hold unswervingly to the hope we profess, for he who promised is faithful.

**Draw near to God with sincerity and faith.** This doesn't mean something mystical. It means living as faithful Christians who genuinely believe in Jesus. It means trusting that no sin is too big for God's forgiveness—you shouldn't sin, but if you do, never believe your sin is beyond grace. It means understanding the finality of Christ's work. He has already paid the price. There is no further sacrifice needed. There is no wrathful Jesus waiting to punish you if you stumble. There is only Christ's finished work and the Father's eternal acceptance of all who are in him.

**Hold unswervingly to hope.** God has promised. He is faithful. In a world of shifting uncertainties, we cling to the one promise that stands: Christ has redeemed us, Christ is returning, and Christ will bring us to our final rest.

### In Relation to Each Other (10:24-25)

> **Hebrews 10:24-25** — And let us consider how we may spur one another on toward love and good deeds. Let us not give up meeting together, as some are in the habit of doing, but let us encourage one another—and all the more as you see the Day approaching.

**Spur one another toward love and good deeds.** This is what we gather for. Not to perform religious rituals, not to present sacrifices, not to show off our piety. We gather to help each other become more loving, more generous, more Christ-like. When the early Christians met, they were doing exactly this—learning from apostolic teaching and encouraging each other to follow Jesus and love one another. Nothing mystical. Nothing weird. Nothing religious. Just believers helping believers become more like Jesus.

**Encourage one another.** The word means to stand alongside, to strengthen, to urge forward. We're preparing one another for the rest that is coming. We're walking with each other through the trials of this age toward the day when Jesus returns and makes all things new.

### The Radical Simplicity of Early Christianity

People who first encountered Christianity thought it was atheism. Why? Because there was no temple, no idols, no sacrifices, no priests. What they saw instead was radical love among believers—people serving each other, sharing with one another, laying down their lives for one another. *That* was the scandal and the beauty of the early church. What counted wasn't what was offered to God directly, but what Christians offered to each other.

When the New Testament uses temple language, it redirects it toward love: "If you want to offer to God, offer to the poor. If you want to sacrifice, sacrifice for others." This is what it means to live in the reality Jesus has opened.

## Conclusion

The Levitical system is obsolete. We are not friends with God through religion and rituals. We are friends with God through Jesus. Believe in him. Trust in his finished work. Draw near to God with confidence. Encourage one another. Love one another. Spur one another on toward good deeds.

This is what we're here for. This is what God wants. As we begin this new year and many groups are starting up, consider joining them—not out of religious obligation, but because learning together and encouraging one another to follow Jesus is what the body of Christ is meant to do.

The old ways are gone. Jesus is the new and living way. All who believe have access to God through him—bold access, confident access, permanent access. Walk in that freedom.
