# 1 Corinthians 4:1-17

**Title:** The Wisdom of Leaders

**Date:** 26 June 2019

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## How Should We Think Wisely About Leaders?

The Corinthian church faced a significant problem: the believers had divided themselves into personality cults, following different leaders—Paul, Apollos, Peter, and others. These apostles were foundational figures in Christianity, wielding apostolic power to heal, founding churches, and shaping the movement that would eventually reach 2.2 billion people worldwide. Their names graced buildings and their legacies inspired devotion. Yet this very prominence created a dangerous temptation toward idolatry. Paul's response to this division offers timeless wisdom about Christian leadership.

### 1. Leaders Are Servants

> This, then, is how you ought to regard us: as **servants of Christ** (4:1a)

The contrast is stark. In the worldly understanding, leaders enjoy privilege—respect, honor, high office. Servants, by contrast, occupy the lowest social positions: underpaid, scolded, embarrassed. Yet Paul insists that Christian leaders must be understood primarily as servants, not as superior beings deserving special honor.

This principle revolutionizes how we think about leadership. Jesus himself modeled this radical reorientation:

> "You know that those who are regarded as rulers of the Gentiles lord it over them, and their high officials exercise authority over them. Not so with you. Instead, whoever wants to become great among you must be your servant, and whoever wants to be first must be slave of all." (Mark 10:42-44)

Jesus demonstrated this servant leadership by becoming "the slave of all"—he saw humanity's deepest need (sin and its consequences) and took action himself rather than waiting for someone else to solve it.

**What does servant leadership look like in practice?** Leaders are the first to identify problems and the first to act on them. Whether it's a need in the community, rubbish on the floor, or an unflushed toilet—a true leader notices and takes initiative to help. This posture of humble service, though unglamorous and often underappreciated, is precisely what makes Christian leadership vital and important.

The key insight: leaders are not special because of their greatness, but important because they do the work. They are replaceable, just as a waiter or dishwasher is replaceable—yet that job is essential. Without someone doing the unglamorous service work, the whole operation fails.

### 2. Leaders Are Entrusted with God's Mystery

> This, then, is how you ought to regard us: as servants of Christ **and as those entrusted with the mysteries God has revealed.** (4:1b)

Paul is not merely a servant—he is an entrusted servant. The mystery he speaks of is not something mysterious or magical, but rather a profound secret that has now been revealed.

What is this mystery? Throughout Scripture, it refers to God's hidden counsel, now disclosed:

> God's wisdom, a **mystery that has been hidden** and that God destined for our glory before time began. (1 Corinthians 2:7)

The mystery encompasses realities that no human being could have discovered or anticipated:

- That a carpenter's adopted son would be the Son of God
- That the cross—a symbol of torture and terror—would become the means of salvation
- That God would simultaneously fulfill both justice and mercy through Christ's death
- That salvation would extend not just to the Jews, but to all nations

With the gospel, these hidden truths are now revealed. Paul received this trust directly from the risen Christ on the Damascus road and was appointed apostle to the Gentiles. From that moment forward, he became personally responsible to God for how he handled this entrusted message.

This trustworthiness carries profound implications:

> Now it is required that those who have been **given a trust must prove faithful.** (4:2)

The messenger matters only insofar as he faithfully carries the message. Paul himself offers a helpful illustration: imagine a beautiful bottle of Fanta or a shiny can of water. We admire the packaging, but we drink the contents. Once empty, we toss away the container. What mattered was never the vessel—only what it contained.

**Paul is accountable to God alone:**

> I **care very little** if I am judged by you or by any human court; indeed, I do not even judge myself. **It is the Lord who judges me.** (4:3-4)

This claim scandalized the Corinthians. How could Paul be so dismissive of their judgment? The answer reveals a deeper principle: because Paul answers to God, he does not need to answer to human critics. He acknowledges his conscience is clear—he is doing his duty faithfully—yet he also admits this doesn't make him innocent. Only God, who sees hidden motives and will bring judgment, can render a true verdict.

> My conscience is clear, but that does not make me innocent. **It is the Lord who judges me.** Therefore judge nothing before the appointed time; **wait until the Lord comes.** He will bring to light what is hidden in darkness and will expose the motives of the heart. At that time each will receive their praise from God. (4:4-5)

**The Conscience of a Leader: Going His Own Way**

This raises a crucial question about leadership authority. When leaders are entrusted with God's word, they must be willing to stand by their convictions, even when it means defying human authority. Consider John Huss, a fourteenth-century priest in the Roman Catholic Church who became convinced that papal authority sometimes contradicted Scripture. When pressured to submit to the Pope's judgment regardless of his own conscience, Huss replied:

> "I cannot offend against God or my conscience by renouncing."

When faced with death for his convictions, Huss demanded: "Show me from God's Word where I am wrong."

By prioritizing his conscience before God's word over institutional authority, Huss helped establish a revolutionary principle: the individual's right to examine Scripture and follow its truth. This foundational commitment—that appeal to God's word supersedes appeal to authority—became the bedrock of the Reformation and modern liberty itself.

**Classic liberalism** emerges from this principle: the state should not compel uniform belief. People should be free to think informed thoughts and work out truth together in community. This freedom is not the freedom to ignore consequences, but the freedom from state coercion. When governments force one vision of truth over another—whether through suppressing speech, controlling belief, or redefining fundamental concepts—they become tyrannies. The solution is not enforced uniformity but a free market of ideas, where truth is tested by Scripture, by community reasoning, by time, and ultimately by reality itself.

Yet this liberty carries responsibility. Leaders must stand by their convictions, rooted in Scripture, willing to endure hardship for truth. As Paul would later emphasize:

> Do not go beyond what is written. (4:6)

Divisions among Christians are only justified when rooted in Scripture itself. We should not divide over preferences—church size, musical style, leadership structure—but only over matters found in God's word. Some divisions are indeed necessary. As Paul warned elsewhere:

> If anyone is preaching to you a gospel other than the one you received, let them be under God's curse! (Galatians 1:8)

But Paul's broader concern is that the Corinthians were dividing over leaders themselves—essentially creating loyalties based on personality rather than truth. Whether Paul planted or Apollos watered, they were building the same building. The foundation is Christ; the builders are servants.

### 3. The Call to Followers: Imitate Leadership

> Even if you had **ten thousand guardians** in Christ, you do not have many fathers, for in Christ Jesus **I became your father** through the gospel. Therefore I urge you **to imitate me.** (4:15-16)

Paul's appeal moves to a personal level. He is not merely an apostle or a servant or a trustee of mysteries—he is their spiritual father, the one who brought them to birth in Christ. This relational foundation gives him the authority to call them to imitation.

> Follow my example, as I follow the example of Christ. (1 Corinthians 11:1)

**What does it mean to imitate leaders at a human level?** It means following their leadership when they faithfully represent their trust. Not because they are perfect—they carry human faults and limitations—but because they have proven themselves faithful. The Corinthians could examine Paul's life: Was he pursuing wealth? Laziness? Deception? No. He was working hard, enduring persecution, and being slandered—all for the gospel.

His actions warranted trust. He had founded their church, was there in its beginning, and his behavior had been consistent throughout. They should listen to his words because his life validated his message.

Paul then paints a vivid portrait of apostolic hardship:

> God has put us apostles on display at the end of the procession, like those condemned to die in the arena. We have been made a **spectacle** to the whole universe, to angels as well as to human beings. We are fools for Christ, but you are so wise in Christ! We are **weak**, but you are strong! You are honored, we are **dishonored**! To this very hour we go **hungry and thirsty,** we are in **rags**, we are brutally **treated**, we are **homeless**. We **work hard with our own hands.** When we **are cursed, we bless; when we are persecuted, we endure it; when we are slandered, we answer kindly.** We have become the **scum** of the earth, the **garbage of the world---right up to this moment.** (4:9-13)

The Corinthians, by contrast, had become complacent:

> Already you **have all you want!** Already **you have become rich**! You have begun to **reign**---and that without us! (4:8)

Paul responds with irony: "How I wish that you really had begun to reign so that we also might reign with you!" The apostles are like jesters in the colosseum—spectacles for entertainment in a brutally pagan world. Yet paradoxically, this suffering contains power. True power in God's kingdom is not measured in comfort, status, or security, but in being willing to endure for truth.

**The Pattern of Leadership: John Huss**

History provides a stunning example. In 1415, John Huss—a thin, sick man fastened to a wooden stake by a rusty chain around his neck—faced execution. Wood and straw were piled to his chin as flames began to consume him. Yet as he burned, he sang hymns and cried out:

> "Christ, son of the living God, have mercy on us."

The authorities killed him because he refused to submit to papal authority when it contradicted Scripture. They dressed him mockingly in priestly robes, then stripped and degraded him. They crowned him with a paper cone labeled "head of heretics." Yet in his final moments, Huss declared:

> "In the truth of the gospel which I have written, taught, and preached, drawing on the sayings and positions of the holy doctors, I am ready to die today."

His ashes were scattered into the Rhine. Yet a century later, his sermons inspired a young monk named Martin Luther. So profoundly did Huss's witness shape Luther that the reformer was later called a "Hussite." Huss's death sparked the Reformation itself. One man's faithfulness under torture became the foundation for religious liberty for millions.

**This is what leaders are:** faithful servants willing to endure hardship for truth, whose conviction becomes a legacy that transforms generations.

**A Call to Wisdom**

The Corinthians were being called to imitate this example:

> You ought to imitate me. For this reason I have sent to you Timothy, my son whom I love, who is faithful in the Lord. (4:16-17)

At a practical level, Paul calls followers to support their leaders. They carry human faults and limitations. Make their leadership easier by listening first—give them the benefit of the doubt. Allow them room to be wrong. Help them lead by providing constructive feedback rather than mere complaint. It is hard to be a servant-leader.

Yet the deeper call is to examine the message itself, not merely the messenger's personality or presentation. When we focus on the personality of leaders rather than the truth they proclaim, we repeat the Corinthians' mistake. Believers can reasonably disagree about secondary matters, yet unity comes through commitment to what is written—Scripture itself.

## The Foundation of All Wisdom

> Do not deceive yourselves. If any of you think you are wise by the standards of this age, you should become "fools" so that you may become wise. For the wisdom of this world is foolishness in God's sight... So then, no more boasting about human leaders! All things are yours, whether Paul or Apollos or Cephas or the world or life or death or the present or the future---all are yours, and you are of Christ, and Christ is of God. (3:18-23)

The final word belongs to Paul's radical reframing. You are not followers of Apollos or Paul—you are inheritors of the gospel itself. You have been entrusted with the profound mystery of life that the world knows nothing about.

Consider what happens when you gather as believers each week: You study Scripture, developing critical faculties and learning to examine a document for meaning. You think together about history, theology, and philosophy. You critique the world and seek solutions. You develop the capacity to disagree with others while remaining civil and reasonable—a thick skin formed by reasoning together rather than merely reacting. And before all this, you pray, cultivating humility to know that understanding comes by God's grace, not by your own cleverness.

**You are leaders of wisdom to the world.**

This is not pride; it is sober recognition of your inheritance. The freedom we enjoy—freedom of conscience, freedom of religion, freedom of speech, freedom to trade and live without coercion—flows from the Christian conviction that appeal to Scripture supersedes appeal to authority. That conviction was purchased through the faithfulness of leaders willing to die for truth.

We desperately need leaders today with such conviction. Leaders willing to endure hardship for truth. Leaders willing to go their own way according to God's word, regardless of cultural pressure. Only when good people die for truth does the kingdom advance here and in the age to come. And in doing so, we build well upon the foundation of Christ.
