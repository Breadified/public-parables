# John 15:1-15

**Title:** Stick with Jesus
**Date:** 27 March 2016
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction

I'm terrible with plants. I once had the ambitious idea to start my own herb and vegetable garden. My mother, an avid gardener, gave me some starter plants. I bought seeds and fertilizer, but nothing worked out. The chillies died. The watering was never quite right—too hot, too cold, too wet. When things did grow, I was too lazy to harvest them. Lettuce came up, but I don't even eat lettuce. Nowadays my mother comes over and tends the garden herself.

Today, we'll learn about gardening from a far better gardener—Jesus himself.

## 1. Stick with the Vine

> "I am the true vine..." (John 15:1)

We're in John 15, which is the climax of Jesus' final teachings to his disciples. In John 13 and 17, Jesus serves his disciples. In John 14 and 16, he speaks about the Holy Spirit. But here in John 15, Jesus reveals himself as the vine—the source of life and nourishment.

### The Vine as Israel

The imagery would have been immediately familiar to Jesus' audience. Ancient Israel had extensive vineyards irrigated by water from the Jordan River. Jesus uses this everyday picture as a metaphor, but he's making a profound theological claim: he is contrasting himself with the ancient nation of Israel.

Traditionally, the vine represented Israel itself—the plant that God had cultivated and nurtured. Look at Psalm 80:

> You transplanted a vine from Egypt; you drove out the nations and planted it. You cleared the ground for it, and it took root and filled the land. The mountains were covered with its shade, the mighty cedars with its branches. Its branches reached as far as the Sea, its shoots as far as the River.

This psalm recalls God's rescue of Israel from Egypt and their settlement in Canaan. God had rescued this vine and planted it in a land flowing with milk and honey. The purpose was for Israel to flourish so greatly that their influence would spread across the land—their leaves covering the mountains and their branches extending east and west. Their population would grow, their territory expand, their influence magnified. Israel was meant to be a mighty tree, nourishing and protecting all who sought its shelter. The vineyard was the very picture of success.

### Israel's Moral Failure

But there was a problem. Though blessed and prosperous, Israel became morally corrupt. The prophet Hosea captures this tragedy:

> Israel was a spreading vine; he brought forth fruit for himself. As his fruit increased, he built more altars; as his land prospered, he adorned his sacred stones. Their heart is deceitful, and now they must bear their guilt. The Lord will demolish their altars and destroy their sacred stones.

Israel sinned against the God who rescued them. They took God's blessings and turned them into idolatry. As their fruit grew, they dedicated it to pagan altars. As they discovered minerals in their land, they crafted sacred stones like the unbelievers did. Israel became less like a "growing vine" and more like a "spreading weed"—parasitic, uncontrolled, failing to nourish the world as intended. Instead of being a giant tree offering protection and shelter, they became a symbol of spiritual corruption.

Yet hope persisted through the ages. Hosea also prophesied:

> People will dwell again in his shade; they will flourish like the grain, they will blossom like the vine. Israel's fame will be like the wine of Lebanon.

### Jesus as True Israel

Fast forward seven hundred years. When Jesus says, "I am the true vine," he embodies Israel's destiny perfectly. He is the true Israel—the Israel that God always meant Israel to be. In that one person, the ideal Israel is realized. Jesus provides the true blessings and security to all who seek his protection. He provides genuine nourishment. True blessing for the entire world.

He provides life as it was meant to be: a life without suffering, without hurt, without disease, without death. **This is the life Jesus promises.**

This Easter season reminds us of the new life Jesus brings. Like chocolate Easter eggs symbolize new life, Jesus didn't stay dead. He rose from the grave on that first Easter. For us, despite our sins and moral failures, Jesus offers a chance to connect with him—to have new life.

### Connection and Nourishment

To gain these benefits, we must be attached to this vine.

> Remain in me, as I also remain in you. No branch can bear fruit by itself; it must remain in the vine. Neither can you bear fruit unless you remain in me. I am the vine; you are the branches. (John 15:4-5)

**Stick with nourishment.** Without the nourishment the vine provides, a branch dies. I once tried to cut down a thick branch of a gum tree but only got halfway through before getting tired. I kept making excuses to finish the job, but eventually, after a couple of months, the branch fell on its own—dead. Being cut halfway was enough to sever the nutrients from the tree. It withered and crashed onto my neighbor's fence. But if you remain attached, you have life.

There's a process in horticulture called grafting. Sometimes you want tomatoes, but tomato plants are susceptible to disease. So you graft the plant onto hardier root stock. Then it grows, supplied by that stronger root, sustained and protected by it.

### Application

Jason, Vivian, Mandy, and all Christians are Christians not because it's a "lifestyle" (though it is a better lifestyle) or a "hobby" (though there's no better hobby) or a "club" (though it's a really good club). We are Christians for our souls—for life now and hereafter, nourished by him. For life to deal with the problem of sin today and the problem of judgment tomorrow. Freedom means being grafted from a dying tree into a life-giving one.

If you haven't already, today is a good opportunity to ask those questions and join Jesus, the true vine.

## 2. Stick with the Word

> He cuts off every branch in me that bears no fruit, while every branch that does bear fruit he prunes so that it will be even more fruitful. (John 15:1-2)

The Gardener is the Father. And every gardener does what gardeners do—they prune.

### The Nature of Pruning

Every plant needs to be pruned. Gardeners prune to:
- Direct the growth of the plant in certain ways
- Stimulate the formation of flowers and fruit buds
- Remove older stems to encourage new growth
- Keep the plant young and prevent the spread of disease

God prunes in order to create growth. The disciples are the pruned branches.

> You are already clean (or pruned) because of the word I have spoken to you. (John 15:3)

The disciples are those branches the gardener has worked on—perhaps even grafted on. The gardener has taken shears, scissors, or a big electrical hedger and snipped them, cleaning them up. Christians are the ones God has already begun working on.

### Pruning by His Word

He pruned us when we first heard the gospel—the good news that he is the Vine. We believed, and his word began its work in us. As Hebrews 4:12 says:

> For the word of God is alive and active. Sharper than any double-edged sword, it penetrates even to dividing soul and spirit, joints and marrow; it judges the thoughts and attitudes of the heart. Nothing in all creation is hidden from God's sight. Everything is uncovered and laid bare before the eyes of him to whom we must give account.

The word is sharper than any scissors, pruning shears, or scalpel. It cuts through our thoughts and the attitudes of our hearts. It opens us up—what's hidden within? But although painful, there is a life-giving benefit. When cancer is cut out, we can live. It hurts, but the end product is spiritual health.

We are being conformed into Christ-likeness. After all, we are grafted into him, not him into us. So parts of us are being pruned away.

### Purposes of Ongoing Pruning

God continues to prune us through his word, shaping us:
- To direct our growth in certain ways and help us learn
- To stimulate growth in our spiritual lives
- To remove sin—that disease that rots us from the inside out

This is how we make sense of trials and suffering. God is shaping and disciplining us in a variety of ways. We interpret the circumstances of our lives through God's word, asking where there's room to improve. This word cuts us with truth, then heals us so we might grow better. We are to trust that in suffering, God has our best interests in mind. In sickness or health, in prosperity or poverty, stick with Jesus' words.

### Application

Some of the baptized are not flashy people. They are not visibly super healthy. But one thing they consistently do—probably better than most of us—is persevere. Their testimonies bear that out, day after day. They stick with Jesus, who gives them nourishment and hope. And we look forward to the end when we can ask why he cut this bit, why this pain—and God will reply: "Let me show you why."

And at the end of the day, there will be a wonderful tree full of fruit.

## 3. Stick with Love

> If you remain in me and I in you, you will bear much fruit; apart from me you can do nothing. (John 15:5)

Jesus is the Vine. God is the Gardener. We are the branches. The promise is that we will bear fruit if we **stick with Jesus**. Pun intended.

Stick with the Vine. Stick with the Word. The result is fruit.

A weed is a plant that bears no fruit. But a vine produces grapes. The fruit is not worldly success or material gain. It is spiritual fruit—**the fruit of love.**

> As the Father has loved me, so have I loved you. Now remain in my love. If you keep my commands, you will remain in my love, just as I have kept my Father's commands and remain in his love. I have told you this so that my joy may be in you and that your joy may be complete. My command is this: Love each other as I have loved you. Greater love has no one than this: to lay down one's life for one's friends. (John 15:9-13)

Remain in Jesus' love by keeping his commands. Sometimes we think that love and commandments are opposite things—love is spontaneous while commandments are legalistic. But that's only if you see love as a mere emotion. Lust, desires—these are meaningless emotions. But love that is purposeful, love that is directed by God's commands, is genuine love.

Love is patient. Love is kind. God shows us what genuine love looks like. This is a love learned from the greatest love of all—**greater love has no one than this: to lay down one's life for one's friends.** Jesus himself set us the greatest example of love.

At Easter, Jesus became the vine hung on a tree—a terrible crime. But from that pruning, from that death, came the most wonderful fruit. At the cross, we see Jesus' true love for us. By the waters of baptism, we are watered. Now it is time to grow.
