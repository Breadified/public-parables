# Titus 2:1-15

**Title:** Personal Godliness  
**Date:** 18 October 2015  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Big Idea

Jesus' appearance calls us to godliness. This passage confronts us with a challenging truth: Christians must be visibly different from the world around them—different in our actions, our words, and our attitudes. Paul writes to Titus about Christians who understand the weight of representing Jesus, and through them, we see how the grace of God transforms us into a people eager to do good.

## God's Grace Teaches Us to Say No to Ungodliness

> For the grace of God has appeared that offers salvation to all people. It teaches us to say "No" to ungodliness and worldly passions, and to live self-controlled, upright and godly lives in this present age, while we wait for the blessed hope—the appearing of the glory of our great God and Savior, Jesus Christ. (Titus 2:11-13)

### Understanding the Appearance

When Paul speaks of "the grace of God," he's pointing us to Jesus himself—the incarnation, God made flesh. This appearance happened in the first century when Jesus walked among us. And this grace "offers salvation to all people" without discrimination or limitation.

But here's where it gets remarkable: this appearance *teaches us*. Not through lectures or commands alone, but through example. Jesus demonstrates before us what a truly godly life looks like.

### Saying No to Ungodliness

The grace of God teaches us to reject ungodliness and worldly passions. Think of it this way: if you genuinely believed in the quality and reliability of something—say, a Toyota car—wouldn't you naturally want to drive one yourself? Belief naturally produces corresponding action. When we truly embrace the truths about God revealed in Jesus, that conviction inevitably shapes how we live.

The passage calls us to three positive virtues:
- **Self-controlled** living—mastering our impulses through wisdom rather than being mastered by desire
- **Upright** living—conducting ourselves with integrity in our relationships and dealings
- **Godly** living—ordering our lives according to God's character and values

## Jesus Transforms Us Into His Own People

> Jesus Christ, who gave himself for us to redeem us from all wickedness and to purify for himself a people that are his very own, eager to do what is good. (Titus 2:14)

### The Transformation

Jesus didn't just teach us—he *redeemed* us. He purchased us from the marketplace of sin, extracting us from all wickedness. More than that, he *purified* us, transforming us into something entirely new.

We've been upgraded. Imagine someone giving you an old, broken-down car and saying, "This is yours now." Then imagine they return and say, "I'm going to give you a luxury vehicle instead—a brand new car, fully restored, completely transformed." Who would want to go back to the old one? The transformation is complete.

### Eager to Do Good

The most beautiful part: we're recreated to be "eager to do what is good." Not reluctant. Not merely obedient. But *eager*—driven by an internal motivation that doesn't need external pressure.

Think of a student who studies with fierce intensity, not because someone forced them, but because they understand what education means for their future. That eagerness comes from within. For us, our motivation springs from what Jesus has done: he picked us up from the dirt, cleaned us up, and made us his own—his team, his family, his brothers and sisters. We're in this together.

This creates an inescapable logic: how could we possibly say to Jesus, "Thanks for redemption, but now I'm going back to my old life"? That would be betrayal at its deepest level. We've been transformed; how could we return to what we were?

## How Christians Live Out Godliness

Paul breaks down his instructions for different members of the community, because godliness isn't abstract—it's lived out in real relationships and real roles.

### Older Men: The Anchor of Stability

> Teach the older men to be temperate, worthy of respect, self-controlled, and sound in faith, in love and in endurance. (Titus 2:2)

When Paul speaks of "older men," he simply means men who have lived longer—though age is somewhat subjective. The question for each of us is: am I aiming toward this standard? If I'm already older, am I living this way? If not, it's time to pray and ask God for help.

Paul calls older men to **maturity** in several dimensions:

- **Temperate/Sober** — Not just abstaining from excessive drinking, but the clear-headed wisdom that comes from disciplined living
- **Worthy of respect** — Dignified, serious, commanding respect through character
- **Self-controlled** — Governed by wisdom, not peer pressure, selfish desires, or ego
- **Sound in faith, love, and steadfastness** — Solid in our trust toward God, generous in love toward others, and persistent when suffering comes

Older men are called to be like anchors—rocklike and steady. We create security. We're the ones younger people can look up to and depend on. Wives want to see this in their husbands. Children want to see it in their fathers. Sisters want to see it in their brothers. The community needs men who bring stable, wise influence rooted in God's character.

### Younger Men: Living the Example

> Similarly, encourage the young men to be self-controlled. In everything set them an example by doing what is good. In your teaching show integrity, seriousness and soundness of speech that cannot be condemned, so that those who oppose you may be ashamed because they have nothing bad to say about us. (Titus 2:6-8)

Young men are called first and foremost to **self-control**—that same governance by wisdom rather than impulse. But there's something more: young men must *model* godliness. Christianity isn't just taught and believed; it must be demonstrated so that people can see it and understand it.

The standard is clear: your speech and conduct should be so sound that your opponents can find nothing to condemn. Not because you're perfect, but because your life consistently reflects the gospel you claim to believe.

### The Pattern Extends to All

This same transformative pattern applies to older women, younger women, and slaves—each group called to live in a way that makes the teaching about God attractive rather than shameful. Godliness isn't reserved for certain people in certain roles; it's the calling of all who have been redeemed and purified by Jesus.

## The Challenge for Us

We must face this squarely: being a Christian means being different. Not different for the sake of being different, but different because we've been transformed by grace. We've encountered Jesus, believed in him, and been remade into a people eager to do good.

The question isn't whether we'll be transformed—Jesus has already done that work. The question is whether we'll live as the transformed people we've become, representing him faithfully in our families, our workplaces, and our communities. That's what godliness looks like.
