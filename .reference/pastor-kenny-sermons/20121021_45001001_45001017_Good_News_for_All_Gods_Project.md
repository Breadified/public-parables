# Romans 1:1-17

**Title:** Good News for All: God's Project

**Date:** October 21, 2012 (revised November 19, 2023)

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## The Gospel Drives Us

Just as every nation has a founding story that shapes its identity and purpose, Christians are driven by a founding story—the gospel, the good news. The book of Romans is one of Scripture's finest expressions of this good news, a book that has transformed lives throughout history. Martin Luther, the great Reformer, found his faith renewed through Romans. Even in my own journey, Romans was one of the first books I studied seriously, fundamentally shaping my understanding of God's message to humanity.

This good news carries a striking implication: by believing in the powerful Gospel, we are conscripted into God's project. We are called not merely to receive salvation, but to participate in God's redemptive work.

**Big Idea: By believing in the powerful Gospel, we are conscripted to God's Project**

---

## 1. Jesus: Set Apart as Lord and Savior (Romans 1:1-4)

### Jesus is Fully Human

Paul begins by establishing a crucial truth: Jesus was "descended from David according to the flesh" (v. 3). He was truly human. This matters profoundly because what is not incarnated is not redeemed. God needed to enter into our form, speak our language, and live our life so that we could understand Him and so that He could identify with us completely.

This incarnation was necessary for another reason: only a human could bear the weight of human sin. Since it is humans who have sinned, atonement requires a human sacrifice. A purely divine being cannot atone for humanity's rebellion—the transaction would be incomplete. Jesus had to be fully human to bear our punishment justly.

### Jesus is Fully God

Yet Jesus cannot be merely human, for "a human is powerless" against sin and death. Paul tells us Jesus was "declared to be the Son of God in power according to the Spirit of holiness by his resurrection from the dead" (v. 4). The resurrection is God's declaration of Jesus's divine power and authority.

Why must Jesus be divine? Because only God has the power to save us. More fundamentally, sin is ultimately an offense against God Himself. Therefore, only God can forgive sin—if anyone else paid the penalty, it would be a third party bearing someone else's debt, and justice would not be satisfied. God Himself had to bear the penalty of sin.

Consider this analogy: imagine a righteous judge whose child commits a crime. The judge comes down from the bench, removes his robes, and takes the punishment meant for his child. This is what God did. He became human to be punished justly, and He remained God so that His punishment would have infinite worth and infinite effect.

This is why systems that deny Jesus's divinity—such as Jehovah's Witnesses—fundamentally miss the gospel's power. A merely good human cannot save anyone. Only God-become-human can accomplish redemption.

> "For God became man that men might become sons of God." — C.S. Lewis

## 2. The Gospel: Promised Long Ago (Romans 1:2)

### God's Consistent Purpose Through History

Paul reminds us that this gospel "he promised beforehand through his prophets in the holy Scriptures" (v. 2). This is not a last-minute rescue plan or an afterthought. The entire Old Testament points toward Jesus. The Bible's narrative arc—from Genesis to Malachi—gradually unfolds God's promise, revealing layer by layer that the hope of all nations would be fulfilled in Jesus Christ.

The Old Testament is not separate from the New; it is the preparation for it. Every sacrifice, every law, every prophet was pointing toward the One who would come. This gospel is God's ancient promise, finally fulfilled.

## 3. Paul: Set Apart for God's Work (Romans 1:1, 5-17)

### Called to Apostleship

Paul introduces himself as "a servant of Christ Jesus, called to be an apostle, set apart for the gospel of God" (v. 1). This was not a position Paul chose; it was a calling he received. As the Lord told Ananias regarding Paul: "Go, for he is a chosen instrument of mine to carry my name before the Gentiles and kings and the children of Israel. For I will show him how much he must suffer for the sake of my name" (Acts 9:15-16).

Through Jesus, Paul "received grace and apostleship to bring about the obedience of faith for the sake of his name among all the nations" (v. 5). His calling was clear: proclaim the gospel so that people would come to believe and obey Jesus Christ.

### A Passion to Preach

Paul's burden for Rome is evident in verses 8-15. He thanks God for the Roman church's faith "because your faith is proclaimed in all the world" (v. 8). Yet his heart yearns to visit them personally, "that I may impart to you some spiritual gift to strengthen you" and that "we may be mutually encouraged by each other's faith" (vv. 11-12).

He confesses: "I am under obligation both to Greeks and to barbarians, both to the wise and to the foolish. So I am eager to preach the gospel to you also who are in Rome" (vv. 14-15). Paul's sense of obligation extends to all people—the educated and the uneducated, the cultured and the uncultured. The gospel is for everyone.

## 4. The Power of the Gospel (Romans 1:16-17)

### No Shame in the Gospel

"I am not ashamed of the gospel, for it is the power of God for salvation to everyone who believes, to the Jew first and also to the Greek" (v. 16).

We are only ashamed of things in which we have no confidence. But Paul proclaims with absolute certainty: this gospel is the power of God. With such infinite power behind it, how can anyone be ashamed of it? How can anyone doubt it?

Consider the contrast: the gospel is not like a run-down, aging vehicle that embarrasses you to be seen in. It is like a sleek convertible—brand new, shiny, filled with unmistakable power. When you are in something genuinely powerful and genuine, you have nothing but confidence. You can raise the windows, turn up the music, put on your sunglasses, and let everyone see you in it. There is no shame.

### Available to All

The gospel's power is "for salvation to everyone who believes"—not just to the Jew first, but also to the Greek. Not only to the educated or the wealthy or the culturally acceptable. It is for all people, everywhere, who believe.

### The Righteousness of God Revealed

"For in it the righteousness of God is revealed from faith for faith, as it is written, 'The righteous shall live by faith'" (v. 17). The gospel unveils God's righteousness—His perfect justice, His commitment to making things right. And this righteousness comes through faith: from faith (our initial belief) to faith (deepening trust throughout our lives). It is the principle stated in Habakkuk: "The righteous shall live by faith."

---

## We Are Also Set Apart (Romans 1:6-7)

Paul's calling was specific to him, but a profound truth applies to all believers. We who read this letter are among "those called to belong to Jesus Christ" (v. 6). Paul addresses us as those "loved by God and called to be saints" (v. 7).

This means we, too, are set apart. We have been called. We have been loved. We have been given a task.

Just as Paul received "grace and apostleship," we have also received grace. And just as Paul was sent to proclaim the gospel among all nations, we too are sent. We have been conscripted into God's project. Our friends, our families, our communities—they need to hear this good news from us. We are to call people to the obedience that comes from faith.

---

## Living God's Project

If every believer brought one person to faith, the church would double in size. Nothing is stopping us. We have a gospel we need not be ashamed of because it is the power of God. We have been loved by God, called to be saints, and set apart for His work.

The question before us is simple: Will we fulfill our calling? Will we participate in God's project? Will we proclaim this good news to our friends, our families, our communities—not with hesitation or shame, but with the confidence that comes from knowing we possess the power of God Himself?

God's project needs you. Your faith, your voice, your willingness to share this good news is essential to His redemptive work in this world.
