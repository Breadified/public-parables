## The Rhythm of Celebration

Throughout the year, we mark time with celebrations: Christmas, New Year, Easter, birthdays, weddings. Each is symbolized by a gathering—a party. Think of the best party you've ever attended: not a dance or gaming event, but one where genuine friendships formed over meaningful conversations and wonderful food. Quality food, generous portions, and people you didn't want to leave.

Perhaps it was a birthday celebration with family. For me, it was my twelfth birthday—my first non-family party—with my mother's noodles, fried chicken, and grass jelly drink, followed by playing with Transformer toys with friends. Or maybe you've experienced a wedding feast: ten courses, celebrating your best friend, dressed up and on top of the world, creating lasting memories. 

Food and people together create a good party.

## A Great Banquet

### The Preparation

> "A certain man was preparing a great banquet and invited many guests. At the time of the banquet he sent his servant to tell those who had been invited, 'Come, for everything is now ready.'" (Luke 14:16-17)

This is a rich man with servants and resources. He prepares a "mega-party"—the word *great* derives from the Greek word *mega*. In those times, banquets were lavish affairs: racks of lamb, fine spices, delicate sauces, excellent bread, aged wine, servants attending to guests reclining at table. Everything polished and prepared to perfection. The cutlery gleamed, the tablecloths were pristine, the pillows for reclining fluffed. Days of preparation by the servants, with the master checking every detail.

When everything was ready, the servant went forth with the message: come now, it is time.

### The Guest List

> "At the time of the banquet he sent his servant to tell those who had been invited, 'Come, for everything is now ready.'" (Luke 14:17)

The guests had already been invited and had already accepted. The custom was simple: when the meal was prepared, the servant would go and call them, often escorting them to the house. It was door-to-door service, like a thoughtful date where the guest is picked up and brought to dinner.

Here we see biblical generosity displayed. The Bible repeatedly commands hospitality:

> "Share with God's people who are in need. Practice hospitality." (Romans 12:13)

> "Above all, love each other deeply, because love covers over a multitude of sins. Offer hospitality to one another without grumbling." (1 Peter 4:9)

> "Keep on loving each other as brothers. Do not forget to entertain strangers, for by so doing some people have entertained angels without knowing it." (Hebrews 13:2)

Hospitality is so important that it is required of church leaders:

> "Now the overseer must be above reproach, the husband of but one wife, temperate, self-controlled, respectable, hospitable, able to teach." (1 Timothy 3:2)

### The Modern Problem

Yet our parties often look nothing like this biblical model. We are stingy with food, fearing the cost. We don't host—instead we ask guests to bring their own meat or pay for themselves and even for us. Weddings that once featured celebratory feasts now offer nothing, while money is spent on photography and exotic honeymoons. The guests go hungry.

What if we returned to simpler celebrations? Perhaps the host provides the meat, guests bring salads and drinks. When invited to someone's birthday dinner, we accept the gift of hospitality. If the expense is too great, skip the elaborate party—just have a simple gathering with a cake. The birthday celebration isn't about the birthday person; it's about their opportunity to serve others.

But generosity extends beyond food—it extends to the guest list. Jesus taught:

> "When you give a luncheon or dinner, do not invite your friends, your brothers or relatives, or your rich neighbors; if you do, they may invite you back and so you will be repaid. But when you give a banquet, invite the poor, the crippled, the lame, the blind, and you will be blessed. Although they cannot repay you, you will be repaid at the resurrection of the righteous." (Luke 14:12-14)

Normally we invite people who have done something for us or might in the future. Jesus invites us to invite the poor, crippled, lame, and blind—those who cannot repay. True hospitality includes those society excludes, giving them the gift of friendship and belonging.

Hospitality means loving strangers—not in the sense of "odd people," but in the sense of those we don't yet know. When you next host a party, make room for someone new. At a wedding, save a table for those you just met. At a twenty-first birthday, reserve spots for future friends—the unpopular people who would never otherwise receive an invitation.

The church should embody this spirit: a generous menu and a generous guest list. To new people: welcome; you've come to the right place. To regulars: make space for the stranger, the newcomer who might become a lifelong friend.

## The Excuses

But something sad happens:

> "But they all alike began to make excuses." (Luke 14:18)

**The first excuse:** "I have just bought a field, and I must go and see it. Please excuse me." (Luke 14:18)

This is a lame excuse. No one buys a field without thoroughly examining it first. Even if true, why not tomorrow? The field won't disappear—it's land. You've already bought it. It's too late to back out anyway. More than lame, it's an insult to the host.

**The second excuse:** "I have just bought five yoke of oxen, and I'm on my way to try them out. Please excuse me." (Luke 14:19)

Again, oxen must be tested to ensure they work properly, especially when yoked together—like a test drive before purchase. But the purchase is already made. It's too late. This too is an insult to the host's hospitality.

**The third excuse:** "I just got married, so I can't come." (Luke 14:20)

This is perhaps the most offensive. In the tightly knit community of the Middle East, a wedding would be public knowledge. The master would likely have been invited to the wedding feast. The claim that this happened unexpectedly strains credibility. More likely, the newlywed simply prefers spending time at home over attending the banquet.

All three excuses are lame, disrespectful, and insulting.

### The Custom Violated

In those times, when issuing a dinner invitation, the host counted the number who accepted. The meal was prepared according to that count. Animals had been killed; food had been prepared. The invited guests had a duty to attend. To refuse was to not simply reject the food—it was to waste it, to disrespect the host's preparation and generosity.

### Application: Honoring Commitments

You know this frustration. You organize a fixed-seating dinner—a wedding or twenty-first birthday. You're charged a small fortune per seat because you're trying to be generous. Then someone last-minute cancels with a lame excuse: "I slept in" (the party is in the evening), "I forgot to do homework" (are you still in primary school?), "My relatives are visiting," "It's too hot," "It's too cold," or my favorite: "I thought it was cancelled."

Some parties are casual—meet after church, come if you can, no RSVP needed. But when you're invited to a party requiring an RSVP, you matter. You're counted on. Your presence affects the meal planning, the seating, the cost. It's not just about food—it's about relationship. When you dump the party, you're not just rejecting the food; you're dumping your friends because something better came along.

Small groups and church gatherings operate on this principle too: food and fellowship with friends. Don't dump your friends.

## The Kingdom of Heaven

But Jesus is talking about something far more important than a birthday party. As someone at the table remarks:

> "Blessed is the man who will eat at the feast in the kingdom of God." (Luke 14:15)

Jesus is speaking of heaven—the kingdom of God—and describing it as a feast. The Bible paints many pictures of heaven: a great city, a place of friendship, the dwelling of God, a gathering of many peoples. But one powerful image is that of a feast.

Five hundred years before Jesus, the prophet Isaiah wrote:

> "On this mountain the LORD Almighty will prepare a feast of rich food for all peoples, a banquet of aged wine—the best of meats and the finest of wines. On this mountain he will destroy the shroud that enfolds all peoples, the sheet that covers all nations; he will swallow up death forever. The Sovereign LORD will wipe away the tears from all faces; he will remove the disgrace of his people from all the earth." (Isaiah 25:6-8)

Isaiah wrote to a people in the midst of war and famine—hungry, thirsty, desperate. The feast he promises is more than food. It is a symbol of peace—fighting is over, enemies sit at the same table. It promises security, an end to hunger, an end to sadness and despair, an end to estrangement.

It predicts particularly the end of war with God.

### The First Meal and the Separation

The separation between humanity and God began at another meal. When Adam and Eve took the fruit they were commanded not to eat, that first disobedience divided us from God. We have all participated in this separation—through our own choices. In our meals, we have failed to be generous. We have been aggressive at the table. We have been rude and unfaithful. Violent words over dinner tables have caused mothers to weep. Bitter words have poisoned relationships. We have lacked love for one another. We have eaten without thankfulness, without acknowledging that God sustains us, believing instead that food itself nourishes us.

### The Second Meal and Reconciliation

But it was another meal that saved us. When Jesus came, he ate not with the powerful and popular, but with sinners—society's outcasts. He forgave them. He accepted them.

And in his last meal before death—the Passover—Jesus took bread and wine and told his disciples: "This is my body, broken for you. This is my blood, poured out for you." He gave his life to save strangers, to save us, to bring us back to God.

### The Future Feast

The promise now is that great banquet in the future—the best party of the universe.

**Generous food.** Isaiah speaks of food that truly satisfies. Not fast food, not junk food. Food that costs nothing yet is eaten with a good conscience. Whatever food seems good to us now, heaven is better.

**Generous guest list.** Not just abundance of food, but abundance of people. Love. No hidden agendas. The best party ever—surrounded by friends, filled with contentment.

**God's presence.** The master himself present—the symbol of peace between us and God. An end to bushfires, to violence, to disasters born from limited good intentions. An end to death itself.

We get to eat with God.

## The Response

### The Master's Anger

The master is angry that his invited guests refused to come:

> "I tell you, not one of those men who were invited will get a taste of my banquet." (Luke 14:24)

He gives them what they want: exclusion from the feast. History shows this pattern—the ancient Jews largely rejected Jesus, missing the invitation to the kingdom.

### For Those Not Yet Christian

If you are not a Christian, what excuses keep you from saying yes?

Are they real obstacles or merely lame excuses? Perhaps the real excuse is: "I'm still trying to find out more." That's valid—but are you truly trying?

To find out more, come to church. Have an honest conversation with a Christian. Explore genuinely. Meanwhile, the excuses we make sound familiar: "I have property to examine," "I have a new car to test," "I have plans with my boyfriend." These are the same three excuses Jesus described two thousand years ago, wearing modern clothes.

The question is simply: Are you serious, or is this just something you say yes to until something better comes along?

### The Master's Generosity

But notice God's response. He does not throw a fit. He does not waste the food or condemn everyone. Instead, he invites more people:

> "The servant came back and reported this to his master. Then the owner of the house became angry and ordered his servant, 'Go out quickly into the streets and alleys of the town and bring in the poor, the crippled, the blind and the lame.' 'Sir,' the servant said, 'what you ordered has been done, but there is still room.' Then the master told his servant, 'Go out to the roads and country lanes and make them come in, so that my house will be full.'" (Luke 14:21-23)

The invited guests refuse to respond to the good news that the feast is ready. What could the host do? He cannot have a feast alone. So he invites the unworthy, the poor, the crippled, the blind, the lame—those normally excluded from banquets. He brings in the undesirables. The poor, who aren't invited to feasts. The crippled, who cannot inspect fields or oxen. The blind and lame, who don't normally marry or conduct business.

Friends, he invites us to heaven. To eat with God.

Being a Christian is an invitation to a party. If you are a Christian, be profoundly joyful that you are going to this banquet. If you are not yet a Christian, we do not want you to miss out. Give it a try. Say yes. If that is something you want to do, don't just go home in silence. Tell the friend who brought you, speak with one of the leaders, or speak with me. We will tell you the next steps.
