# Luke 23:1-25

**Title:** The King on Trial
**Date:** 25 March 2012
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## The Trial Framework

As we gather around the trial of Jesus, we're invited to take our place on the jury. This is not merely ancient history—it's an invitation to render our own verdict on who Jesus is and what that means for our lives.

## 1. King or Rebel? (23:1-12)

The Jewish authorities bring Jesus before Pilate, the Roman Prefect of Judaea. Pilate represents Caesar's authority and Rome's power. According to historical records, he was a ruthless administrator—Philo describes him as "inflexible, a blend of self-will and relentlessness," marked by briberies, insults, and executions without trial.

The accusation against Jesus is threefold:
- **Subverting the nation** (turning people away from true authority)
- **Opposing payment of taxes to Caesar** (a serious matter for any Roman official)
- **Claiming to be Messiah, a king** (directly competing with Caesar's authority)

Jesus responds with measured wisdom: "You have said so." His kingship is real, but not the kind they imagine.

### The Appeal to Herod

When Pilate learns Jesus is from Galilee, he sends him to Herod Antipas, the tetrarch of that region. Herod, who killed John the Baptist and fears Jesus might be John risen from the dead, is curious. He hopes to see Jesus perform a miracle, but Jesus offers no defense, no entertainment. Instead, Herod and his soldiers mock him—dressing him in an elegant robe, the garment of a king worn in mockery.

What's remarkable: **Jesus unites two enemies.** Herod and Pilate become friends through their shared contempt. Yet this unity reveals the true subversion they fear—not political rebellion, but a kingdom that calls people to obedience to God rather than Caesar.

### Application: Whose Kingdom Rules Our Lives?

Jesus is indeed a king, and he demands obedience. Yet we expend far more energy complying with earthly authorities than heavenly ones. We worry about:
- Taxes and fines
- Speed limits
- Trade regulations
- Proper waste disposal

We hold our earthly rulers to meticulous standards, yet treat God's laws—the commands to love, the Ten Commandments—with indifference. We've become scrupulous citizens of earthly kingdoms while neglecting citizenship in heaven.

To be a citizen of God's kingdom requires going beyond mere legalism. It affects our time, our wallets, our priorities. It demands allegiance to the highest King.

## 2. Innocent or Guilty? (23:13-22)

Pilate calls the assembly together with a striking verdict: **"I have examined him in your presence and have found no basis for your charges against him. Neither has Herod, for he sent him back to us; as you can see, he has done nothing to deserve death."**

Not once, but **three times**, Pilate declares Jesus innocent:
- "I find no basis for a charge against this man" (v. 4)
- "He has done nothing to deserve death" (v. 15)
- "What crime has this man committed? I have found in him no grounds for the death penalty" (v. 22)

This is a verdict confirmed at every level of authority—yet it changes nothing.

### Jesus: The Innocent One

Consider what Jesus actually did:
- He healed the sick
- He fed the hungry
- He raised the dead
- He called people to righteousness and repentance
- When seized, he told his disciples to put away their swords

He committed no sin. No deceit was found in his mouth. By every measure of Roman law and human justice, Jesus was innocent.

Yet something darker emerges: the innocent is condemned, while the guilty go free. The people shout: **"Away with this man! Crucify him! Crucify him!"** Their shouts prevail over justice itself.

### Application: A Twisted World

In this scene, we witness a world turned upside down. The most innocent becomes the most guilty. In our own time, this inversion has become comprehensive:

Innocent is called guilty. Guilty is called innocent. Right has become wrong. Wrong has become right. Good masquerades as evil, and evil parades as good.

This is not merely a political or cultural observation—it's spiritual blindness. Our society has grown so twisted, so perverted, so confused that some truths must be spelled out explicitly. When fundamental realities are inverted, silence is complicity.

Yet this inversion begins with a fundamental lie: that Jesus is guilty and Barabbas is innocent. Once we reject God's truth about Christ, we lose our moral compass entirely.

## 3. Death or Released? (23:23-25)

In one final exchange, Pilate presents the crowd with a choice: **Jesus or Barabbas.**

### Who Is Barabbas?

Barabbas means "Son of the Father." His name carries dark irony—he bears a title of relation to God while living in rebellion against him. He was imprisoned for **insurrection and murder**. He was a terrorist, actively rebelling against Roman rule, inciting people to resist taxation and authority, killing those who stood in his way.

By all accounts, Barabbas deserved death. Yet the crowd demands his release and Jesus's execution.

### The Supreme Exchange

Here stands the heart of the gospel:

**The guilty man goes free. The innocent man dies.**

We are Barabbas. We are guilty of rebellion against God. We hate his rule over us. We prefer our own way. We are terrorists against God's kingdom, constantly subverting his authority—a little here, a little there, preparing for the day we might cast him off altogether.

Yet Jesus offers us a swap. The innocent King gives up his life so that we might be released. Those who trust in him have narrowly escaped the death we deserve.

Barabbas the rebel walks free because an innocent man took his place.

---

## The Jury's Verdict

We come to this trial not as spectators but as defendants. While we've been evaluating Jesus, he has been evaluating us—and we are found wanting in God's eyes.

Yet this trial ends not in our condemnation but in our liberation. Jesus, the innocent King, takes upon himself the judgment that should fall on us. His death becomes the ransom for our freedom.

The question before us is no longer whether Jesus is a king or a rebel. It is whether we will acknowledge him as King and surrender our rebellious hearts to his rule.
