# Hebrews 12:5-13

**Title:** Persevere: Because Now is the Time!
**Date:** September 2, 2018 (Fathers Day)
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Power of Perseverance

Over a hundred years ago, Francis Galton studied eminent figures across politics, sport, art, music, and science to understand why some people succeed while others fail. He concluded that high achievement stems from three qualities: ability, zeal, and the capacity for hard labor. When Charles Darwin read Galton's work, he replied that while men differ little in raw intellect (excepting fools), they differ greatly in zeal and hard work—and he considered this "an eminently important difference."

This concept has a modern name: *grit*. Dr. Angela Lee Duckworth, a psychology professor at the University of Pennsylvania, developed the Grit Scale to measure perseverance and passion for long-term goals. Her research across Ivy League undergraduates, West Point cadets, and National Spelling Bee finalists consistently showed that grit—not IQ—was the strongest predictor of success. She concluded that perseverance may be as important, if not more important, than intelligence in determining a person's success.

The actor Will Smith encapsulates this beautifully: "I've never really viewed myself as particularly talented. I've viewed myself as slightly above average in talent. And where I excel is ridiculous, sickening work ethic." He famously said: "You might have more talent than me, you might be smarter than me, but if we get on a treadmill together, there are two things: 1. You are getting off first, OR 2. I am gonna die. It's really that simple."

Hard labor, grit, dying on a treadmill—these are all another name for *discipline*.

## God Disciplines His Children (vv. 4-11)

> In your struggle against sin, you have not yet resisted to the point of shedding your blood. (v. 4)

The Hebrews were struggling to remain faithful. Paul rebukes them for two things: First, they have not truly resisted—they're giving up too easily. Second, and more importantly, they have forgotten an encouraging word about God's loving purposes in their struggle.

> Have you completely forgotten this word of encouragement that addresses you as a father addresses his son? (v. 5)

Paul quotes Proverbs 3:

> My son, do not make light of the Lord's discipline, and do not lose heart when he rebukes you, because the Lord disciplines the one he loves, and he chastens everyone he accepts as his son. (vv. 5-6)

### The Pattern of Earthly Discipline

Life at home is fundamentally a training ground. From childhood, loving parents discipline us:

- Teaching us to use utensils, eat vegetables, wake on time
- Requiring schoolwork, exercise, chores
- Using correction—scolding, temporary consequences, grounded privileges

This discipline doesn't feel pleasant in the moment, but it comes from love. Consider what happens without it: children who are not disciplined become spoiled—ruined, good for nothing. An illegitimate child historically was the neglected child, without a father's guiding hand. The absence of paternal discipline produces dysfunction that ripples across generations.

Paul makes a stark statement:

> If you are not disciplined—and everyone undergoes discipline—then you are not legitimate, not true sons and daughters at all. (v. 8)

*Application for parents:* Train your children before it's too late. When children are young, consistency matters enormously. If you bend on certain standards one night, the next night becomes seven times harder. During your children's formative years, discipline may require you to be present and involved in ways that sacrifice your comfort.

Kenny's own father exemplified this. He was not demonstratively affectionate, but he instilled discipline. When school carnivals were held, Kenny's father never wrote false excuses—he wouldn't claim Kenny was sick when he wasn't. He had traveled 7,000 kilometers for his children's education; missing school was not an option. This discipline taught Kenny honesty and reliability. To this day, Kenny turns up to his commitments whether he feels like it or not. That discipline set him up for success in ways that love expressed through permissiveness never could.

*For those still at home:* You may not appreciate your parents' discipline now, but in time you will thank them for it. As verse 9 promises, you will respect them for it.

### The Superior Discipline of Our Heavenly Father

> We have all had human fathers who disciplined us and we respected them for it. How much more should we submit to the Father of spirits and live! (v. 9)

If we recognize the good in our earthly fathers' discipline, how much more should we perceive our heavenly Father's loving hand in our hardships?

> They disciplined us for a little while as they thought best; but God disciplines us for our good, in order that we may share in his holiness. (v. 10)

There's a crucial contrast here: earthly fathers discipline "as they thought best"—limited by finite wisdom and imperfect judgment. God disciplines us for our actual good, and His purpose is that we share in His holiness.

> No discipline seems pleasant at the time, but painful. Later on, however, it produces a harvest of righteousness and peace for those who have been trained by it. (v. 11)

The contrast continues: all discipline feels sorrowful and painful in the moment. But the actual outcome is eternal—a harvest of righteousness and peace. The temporary pain serves the eternal purpose.

## Reframing Suffering: A Training Perspective

Many struggle with the question of why there is suffering. One answer is that suffering results from sin—both personal and systemic. We live in a fallen world groaning for liberation (Romans 8:18-28). But there is hope. God uses present sufferings as training for Christians.

> No temptation has overtaken you except what is common to mankind. And God is faithful; he will not let you be tempted beyond what you can bear. But when you are tempted, he will also provide a way out so that you can endure it. (1 Corinthians 10:13)

Rather than view life's challenges negatively, remember that God is Father. He acts in love. Under the safety net of heaven, every hardship becomes a chance to grow in holiness and godliness, to understand Christ's love, to appreciate our lives, and to serve others.

**John Piper** illustrates this powerfully. When diagnosed with cancer, instead of despair, he wrote an article titled "Don't Waste Your Cancer." He recognized that cancer was a training opportunity. It deepened his trust in God, deepened his prayer life, helped him take sin seriously, refocused his priorities on Christ, and resolved him to witness to others. He saw the disease not as a disability but as God's hand training him.

**Nick Vujicic** was born in Australia with tetra-amelia syndrome—no arms or legs. At age eight, he struggled with God in anger. But rather than remain bitter, he reconciled with God and became thankful. He submitted himself to God's will and became a living testimony of God's miraculous power. Today, he travels the world sharing his faith, and approximately 200,000 souls have come to Christ through his witness. He says: "I would rather have no arms and no legs temporarily on earth in order to reach someone else for Christ and spend eternity with them."

These stories reveal the truth: hardship, when submitted to God, becomes the very instrument of our holiness and usefulness.

## Be Disciplined: Strengthen Yourself (vv. 12-13)

> Therefore, strengthen your feeble arms and weak knees. Make level paths for your feet, so that the lame may not be disabled, but rather healed. (vv. 12-13)

This is a call to spiritual strengthening. The "feeble arms and weak knees" are metaphors for spiritual condition. Paul urges us to walk a straight path, not to turn aside into false teaching or complacency, but to remain faithful.

### Cultivating Spiritual Discipline

Just as physical training builds muscle, spiritual discipline builds strength:

- Exercise daily and weekly with God's Word
- Cultivate habits of Bible reading and prayer
- Think consistently about things of God
- Attend church regularly

These practices feel like discipline at first, but over time they produce strength. As surely as abdominal crunches build core muscles, these habits keep your spiritual path straight.

### The Crisis of Undiscipline in Our Generation

Hebrews 13 lists specific areas requiring discipline:

- **Loving each other** as brothers and sisters (13:1)
- **Welcoming strangers** with hospitality (13:2)
- **Remembering the suffering** of others (13:3)
- **Honoring marriage** and sexual purity (13:4)
- **Being content** and free from the love of money (13:5-6)

Yet for Generation Y (born 1982-1999), discipline has reached a crisis point:

- Youth unemployment approaches 40% because employers find we don't persevere in jobs
- We resign by SMS instead of facing conversations
- Half of university students don't finish the courses they start
- We exhibit little grit compared to previous generations
- We post our absences on Facebook instead of calling our boss
- We expect jobs to provide satisfaction rather than understanding that work itself is discipline

This flows into all areas of life. Without discipline to show up and keep promises, we cannot genuinely love others. Half of our marriages end in divorce. Our Christian life becomes flaky—God becomes just another optional button on the Facebook of life.

### The Discipline of Church Community

Perhaps this is why our church emphasizes certain habits: turning up regularly, serving consistently, letting others know if we won't be there, keeping our commitments. From the outside, it might look legalistic or burdensome. In reality, we're training in discipline.

Think of it like the State Emergency Service (SES)—volunteers who fight bushfires. They're unpaid. They have other jobs. They sign up freely. But once committed, they attend regular training, respond to callouts across their communities, and even interrupt their normal work for major emergencies. It's discipline. It's not "show up when you feel like it." They've committed to something larger than themselves.

Similarly, church is voluntary—we are saved by grace, and attendance doesn't make you Christian. But for those who have committed to the community, think of yourselves as spiritual first responders. We're not warning of bushfires; we're warning of spiritual danger. If middle-aged volunteers can give time to fight physical fires, surely those convinced of the gospel can commit to serving Christ and reaching others.

Woody Allen said, "80% of success in life is showing up."

There are encouraging signs in the community:

- A core group committed to attending regularly and serving
- Young people cooking lunch for the congregation
- Young people pursuing their licenses and jobs
- A growing culture of reliability

*We do this not merely out of love for those organizing, nor only for those depending on us, nor even for ourselves (though reliability makes us more employable). We do this for our spiritual lives.*

Here's the reality: If we cannot prioritize God's things while we're students with fewer pressures, how will we do so when we're working and a boss is chasing us? If we cannot do it as workers, how will we when we have a spouse to please, children getting sick, and a mortgage to pay? If we cannot cultivate these habits in our formative years, why would you ever change when the kids move out or you retire? You won't suddenly find time for God; you'll find time for golf.

**Now is the time to get discipline.**

For HSC students: prioritize the things of God even now. Train yourselves in these formative years, because good habits last a lifetime. For young families: these are your formative years too. Decide now whether you'll be merely a family or a Christian family with certain values and disciplines.

## The Power of Finishing

Consider the story of Abebe Akhwari, an Olympic marathoner in Mexico City. About 19 kilometers into the 42-kilometer race, he fell badly, wounding his knee and dislocating it. Medical staff urged him to withdraw. But Akhwari continued—walking, limping, running—and finished last among the 57 competitors who completed the race. The winner finished in 2:20:26; Akhwari crossed in 3:25:27, long after sunset, with only a few thousand spectators remaining.

When asked why he didn't quit, Akhwari said: "My country did not send me 5,000 miles just to start the race; they sent me to finish the race."

Of the 75 who started that marathon, 18 others pulled out. No one remembers the gold medal winner's name. But the world remembers Akhwari because he finished despite impossible circumstances.

## The Final Word

You are in the race of your life. Finish it.

Persevere. Not because the race is easy, but because you were sent to finish it. Not because discipline always feels good, but because God disciplines those He loves. Not because your generation excels at commitment, but because *now* is the time to cultivate the habits that will carry you through a lifetime of faithfulness.

As Angela Lee Duckworth says: "If it's important for you to become one of the best people in your field, you are going to have to stick with it when it's hard."

Or as Will Smith says: "I am not afraid to die on that treadmill."

Strengthen your feeble arms. Make level paths for your feet. And finish the race marked out for you.
