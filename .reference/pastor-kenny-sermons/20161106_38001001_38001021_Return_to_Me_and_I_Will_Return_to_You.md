# Zechariah 1:1-21; 5:5-6:8

**Title:** Return to Me and I Will Return to You

**Date:** November 6, 2016 (SMAC); November 2020 (Arise)

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## The Big Idea

In the midst of a destroyed Jerusalem, God promises a rebuilt city and future glory. He declares, "My towns will again overflow with prosperity, and the Lord will again comfort Zion and choose Jerusalem" (1:17). But this restoration requires the people to rebuild the temple so that God can dwell with them once more.

---

## Part 1: Starting from Scratch

### The Challenge of Rebuilding

Have you ever had to start something from scratch? Consider the weight of this question: losing an entire assignment to a power outage and having to rewrite it. Migrating to a new country with nothing but a suitcase. Rebuilding your career after losing everything. The Jews faced this same overwhelming trial when they returned from exile in Babylon.

This is the context of Zechariah's prophecy. The same period as Haggai and Ezra, God spoke to His people through Zechariah's strange dreams and visions—the way He communicated in those days (Hebrews 1:1).

### Vision 1: The Horsemen (1:7-17)

On the twenty-fourth day of the eleventh month, in the second year of King Darius, the word of the Lord came to Zechariah. In a night vision, he saw a man mounted on a red horse standing among myrtle trees in a ravine, with red, brown, and white horses behind him. When Zechariah asked what these meant, the angel explained that these were the Lord's scouts sent throughout the earth to report on its condition.

When the horsemen returned, they brought an unexpected report: "We have gone throughout the earth and found the whole world at rest and in peace" (1:11). Yet this good news troubled the angel. While the nations enjoyed peace, Israel still suffered.

The angel cried out to God: "Lord Almighty, how long will you withhold mercy from Jerusalem and from the towns of Judah, which you have been angry with these seventy years?" (1:12). The world was at peace, but God's people were not.

### God's Response: A Promise of Return

The Lord responded with comfort to His people through the angel:

> "I am very jealous for Jerusalem and Zion, and I am very angry with the nations that feel secure. I was only a little angry, but they went too far with the punishment." (1:14-15)

Then came the radical promise:

> "I will return to Jerusalem with mercy, and there my house will be rebuilt. And the measuring line will be stretched out over Jerusalem." (1:16)

> "My towns will again overflow with prosperity, and the Lord will again comfort Zion and choose Jerusalem." (1:17)

### The Historical Weight of This Promise

Israel's history had been catastrophic. The northern kingdom fell to the Assyrians in 722 BC. In 587 BC, the southern kingdom of Judah was conquered by the Babylonians and exiled to Babylon. Yet miraculously, seventy years later in 537 BC, Babylon fell. The Persians conquered the Babylonians, and King Cyrus decreed that the Jewish exiles could return home to rebuild the temple.

But the journey back was not smooth. The surrounding nations constantly harassed the returning exiles. They filed complaints with the Persian authorities, claiming the Jews were dangerous and untrustworthy. As recorded in Ezra 4:4-5, the peoples around them "set out to discourage the people of Judah and make them afraid to go on building."

When the foundation of the new temple was finally laid, the response was bittersweet. Ezra 3:11-13 describes the moment:

> "All the people gave a great shout of praise to the LORD, because the foundation of the house of the LORD was laid. But many of the older priests and Levites and family heads, who had seen the former temple, wept aloud when they saw the foundation of this temple being laid, while many others shouted for joy."

Some rejoiced at progress, but those who remembered the former temple's glory wept. The new structure was pathetic compared to what had been. It was like redoing a three-thousand-word assignment with less time and fewer resources. You want to cry. How could they ever rebuild to former splendor?

### The Question We Still Ask

Today, we ask the same questions:

- Where is God?
- Where is the glory of the resurrection promised to us?
- Why is sickness still prevalent?
- Why is wickedness celebrated?
- Why do our plans fall apart?

In times when we must start from scratch with seemingly no hope, we must remember God's promises. As Paul wrote, Jesus promised His presence "to the very end of the age" (Matthew 28:20). And Peter reminds us: "The Lord is not slow in keeping his promise, as some understand slowness" (2 Peter 3:9). God has kept His promises throughout Israel's history—it took thousands of years, yet He never failed.

## Part 2: Return to Me

### The Call to Repentance

The opening command is not about waiting for God's return, but about our return to Him. God says in verse 3:

> "Return to me, declares the Lord Almighty, and I will return to you, says the Lord Almighty."

Why must they return? Because their ancestors refused to listen. Verse 4 explains:

> "Do not be like your ancestors, to whom the earlier prophets proclaimed: 'This is what the Lord Almighty says: Turn from your evil ways and your evil practices.' But they would not listen or pay attention to me." (1:4)

Their ancestors faced punishment for their disobedience. Verses 5-6 press the point: "Where are your ancestors now? And the prophets, do they live forever? But did not my words and my decrees, which I commanded my servants the prophets, overtake your ancestors?" (1:5-6)

God calls them to repentance—to turn around completely, to go a different direction. This is what "return" means: a turning back to our Sender, who is God. As Peter preached in Acts 3:19: "Repent, then, and turn to God, so that your sins may be wiped out, that times of refreshing may come from the Lord."

To the credit of the Jews, they did repent. Verse 6 records: "Then they repented and said, 'The Lord Almighty has done to us what our ways and practices deserve, just as he determined to do.'"

So what is stopping God from returning?

### The Missing Piece: Build My House

According to verse 16, there is one crucial expectation: "I will return to Jerusalem with mercy, and there my house will be rebuilt."

God's return is contingent on His house being rebuilt. The prophet Haggai makes this even more explicit. God says through Haggai:

> "These people say, 'The time has not yet come to rebuild the Lord's house.' ... Is it a time for you yourselves to be living in your paneled houses, while this house remains a ruin?" (Haggai 1:2-4)

God's house lies in ruins while the people live in comfort. He commands them: "Go up into the hills, bring down lumber, and build the house, so that I may take pleasure in it and be glorified" (Haggai 1:8).

### Why the Temple Matters

Jerusalem is meant to be the city of God, and its greatest glory is God's presence. This presence is powerfully symbolized by the temple—the place God chose to dwell among His people. An unbuilt temple is not merely a logistical, financial, or administrative problem. It is a theological problem—a breach between God and man. Without the temple, there is no dwelling place for God's presence.

The Jews faced two decades of obstruction from surrounding nations. Work on the temple came to a complete standstill for two years before Haggai and Zechariah began their ministries. But the message is clear: return to the project of building God's temple. Do not get distracted. Finish what you have started.

Everywhere in the Persian Empire, other peoples were rebuilding and prospering. Everywhere except Israel. Why? Because Israel had not yet built God's house.

### Application for Us

The task of building God's house continues today, not with bricks and stones, but with the people of God. Peter writes:

> "As you come to Him, the living stone, rejected by men but chosen and precious in God's sight, you also, like living stones, are being built into a spiritual house to be a holy priesthood, offering spiritual sacrifices acceptable to God through Jesus Christ." (1 Peter 2:4-5)

God is not slow in keeping His promise. He is purposeful in His timing. As Peter explains:

> "The Lord is not slow in keeping his promise, as some understand slowness. Instead he is patient with you, not wanting anyone to perish, but everyone to come to repentance. But the day of the Lord will come like a thief. The heavens will disappear with a roar; the elements will be destroyed by fire, and the earth and everything done in it will be laid bare." (2 Peter 3:9-10)

He delays His final return so that His church might grow. Before fire comes and the earth is laid bare, He wants the maximum number of His elect to come to faith.

### Our Moment to Build

We are starting from scratch. The prohibition on gathering has reset us. For those who have been here longer, it is hard to restart mentally—to motivate ourselves to do what we once did with ease. Some of us have become comfortable at home, where it takes two minutes to prepare for work, making the effort to gather seem like much more work.

But church is not information received passively over a screen. Church is participation, hospitality, welcome, the building of trust with one another. These things cannot happen on the internet. They require us to be present.

To those who are able to gather: if life has returned to normal, let church also return to normal. Commit to your group. Do not use Zoom as a shortcut when you oversleep or forget your laundry. There is no such thing as shortcut fellowship. It takes hundreds of hours of learning together, thinking together, eating together, laughing together, fighting together, and crying together to build God's church. It takes inviting strangers, welcoming them, and proclaiming the gospel.

If you wake up late, get ready quickly and skip breakfast if needed—just show up. It is better to be on time, but it is better to show up than to simply log on. If someone asks you to lunch on Sunday, book them for dinner instead because you will already be having lunch with your church. If you forget your laundry, do it when you get home.

There is no activity more important on Sunday than building God's house. It is our one time each week when we join forces to invite, welcome, and encourage. We need everyone present for this task.

To those who must remain homebound: participate fully. Show up early to train yourself. Interact in the chat. Stay for coffee time. Serve on the online team. But if you are able to gather even once a week, consider prioritizing your churchlet and using that opportunity to join us. Building the church is essential.

## Part 3: The Nations Will Be Judged; Jerusalem Will Be Great

### Vision 2: The Four Horns and Craftsmen (1:18-21)

Zechariah saw four horns—symbols of the power and ferocity of conquerors. These horns had scattered Judah, Israel, and Jerusalem. They represent Assyria and Babylon, who conquered with such force that "no one could raise their head" (1:20).

But then Zechariah saw four craftsmen coming to terrify these horns and throw them down. Historically, this refers to the kingdom of Persia, which would be God's instrument of judgment against the nations who oppressed His people.

This is remarkable: God uses one pagan nation to destroy another. Isaiah 45 reveals that God calls Cyrus the Persian king His "anointed one," chosen to subdue nations and strip kings of their armor. Though Cyrus does not acknowledge God, God strengthens him for this purpose (Isaiah 45:1-5).

### Vision 6 & 7: God Removes Wickedness

Two related visions reinforce that God and sin cannot coexist in the same space.

In Vision 6 (5:1-4), Zechariah sees a giant scroll carrying a curse against thieves and liars. This curse will remove them from the land.

In Vision 7 (5:5-11), Zechariah sees a basket containing the iniquity of all the people throughout the land. A woman (representing Babylon and wickedness) is pushed back into the basket and sealed with a lead cover. Then two women with stork wings lift the basket between heaven and earth and carry it away to Babylon—to build a house for it. The message is clear: wickedness will be judged and removed.

There is a wordplay here: the basket is sent to "another house" (Babylon) because God will have His house in Jerusalem. There is no room for two houses of God—only one.

### Vision 8: God's Spirit at Rest (6:1-8)

The final vision bookends the first. Zechariah sees four chariots coming from between two bronze mountains, each drawn by powerful horses: red, black, white, and dappled. These are the four spirits of heaven going out from standing in the presence of the Lord of the whole world.

The horsemen go to the four winds: black horses to the north (Babylon), white horses to the west, and dappled horses to the south (Egypt). The report comes back: "Those going toward the north country have given my Spirit rest in the land of the north" (6:8).

No longer are the nations "at rest" as in the first vision. Now God's Spirit is at rest because He has achieved His purpose. The nations have been conquered. God will use Persia to judge His enemies and restore His people.

### Vision 3: Jerusalem Will Be Boundless (2:1-13)

In the third vision, a man with a measuring line tries to measure Jerusalem's dimensions. But God interrupts: Jerusalem will be so inhabited with people and livestock that it will overflow like boundaries cannot contain it. "Jerusalem will be a city without walls because of the great number of men and livestock in it" (2:4).

Yet God assures them: "I myself will be a wall of fire around it, and I will be its glory within" (2:5).

### Why Jerusalem Will Be Great

The size of Jerusalem and its protective wall of fire are symptoms of something deeper. Jerusalem is great not because of its dimensions or security systems, but because God dwells there.

Notice how repeatedly God emphasizes His presence:

> "For I am coming, and I will live among you, declares the LORD." (2:10)

> "Many nations will be joined with the LORD in that day and will become my people. I will live among you and you will know that the LORD Almighty has sent me to you." (2:11)

> "The LORD will inherit Judah as his portion in the holy land and will again choose Jerusalem." (2:12)

This is what makes Jerusalem great: God is with them. This is true glory.

### The Call to Return to Zion

Zechariah calls even those who were hesitant to return from Babylon to come home: "Come! Escape to Zion, you who live in Babylon!" The exiles had built comfortable lives in Babylon—established houses and careers. Why leave? But God promises that true prosperity and true security are found in Zion, where He dwells. Come back, and I will come back to you. Jerusalem will be great because the temple will be rebuilt and I will be there.

---

## Conclusion: From Destruction to Resurrection

God uses these eight visions (arranged in a chiasm for memory) to encourage a tired, discouraged people: Do not give up. Finish the temple. The Lord Almighty has conquered your enemies. Your city will be great. I will dwell among you.

Indeed, the Jews persevered. Eighteen years after they first returned from exile, and four years after Zechariah first preached these words, they completed the temple in 516 BC.

Just as God started something new in the face of sin and death—creating a new humanity through Christ, His living temple—He calls us now to return to Him. Let us return so that He might return to us in power and great glory. As Jesus promised four times in Revelation: "I am coming soon!" (Revelation 22:7, 12, 20)

Amen. Come, Lord Jesus.
