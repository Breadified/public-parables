# Mark 8:27-35

**Title:** Who Do You Say I Am?
**Date:** 7 July 2024
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Question Jesus Asked

> 27 Jesus and his disciples went on to the villages around Caesarea Philippi. On the way he asked them, "Who do people say I am?"

This question echoes across two thousand years. Everyone has an opinion about who Jesus is—Buddhists see him as enlightened, Hindus regard him as a powerful guru, Muslims revere him as a prophet, Judaism views him as a false messiah, and even atheists like Richard Dawkins acknowledge him as a great moral teacher. Even ordinary Australians hold their own ideas about Jesus.

## Who People Say Jesus Is

> 28 They replied, "Some say John the Baptist; others say Elijah; and still others, one of the prophets."

The disciples' answers weren't random—they were naming the great prophets of Israel's past, heroes whom God had used as His spokesmen. John the Baptist was called the greatest prophet; King Herod feared him because of his influence. Elijah stood alone against four hundred Baal worshippers and stands as perhaps the greatest Old Testament prophet. These were the heroes of Israel, and in suggesting Jesus might be one of them, the disciples were actually complimenting him.

They weren't entirely wrong. Jesus himself spoke of being a prophet:

> Mark 6:4 Jesus said to them, "A prophet is not without honor except in his own town, among his relatives and in his own home."

But there was something more to understand.

## Who Jesus Actually Is: The Messiah

> 29 "But what about you?" he asked. "Who do you say I am?"
>
> Peter answered, "You are the Messiah."

Peter had made a profound leap forward. The term "Messiah" comes from the Hebrew word meaning "anointed one"—not a crown upon the head, but oil as a sign of kingship, making "Messiah" the Jewish title for King, equivalent to "Caesar" in the Roman world.

Throughout the Old Testament, the Messiah held a specific meaning for the Jewish people. He was the promised King—the one the prophets spoke of who would rule the whole world, bring justice back to the earth, save the oppressed and humble the proud. He was the one who would fight invaders and save Israel. The Messiah was God himself returning to his people.

To the disciples' minds, the Messiah meant a deliverer—someone who would overcome injustice, poverty, and hunger. A shepherd for sheep without a shepherd. A king who would defeat enemies and heal diseases. In Peter's confession, Jesus is recognized as the King whom the people desperately needed.

## The Shocking Truth: The King Must Suffer

And then came the anti-climax, the incomprehensible turn:

> 31 He then began to teach them that the Son of Man must suffer many things and be rejected by the elders, the chief priests and the teachers of the law, and that he must be killed and after three days rise again.

What made this so strange? Kings don't speak of dying. The powerful of this world—presidents, prime ministers, corporate leaders—they don't plan their own deaths. It's like achieving the highest academic ranking and then announcing you'll become a soldier destined to die in war. It's like making the Olympic team only to declare yourself the cleaner. The idea made no sense.

Peter's response was visceral:

> 32 He spoke plainly about this, and Peter took him aside and began to rebuke him.

Peter actually rebuked Jesus, schooling him like a boy, as if to say: "Jesus, obviously you don't understand how the world works. Let me teach you." But Peter's misunderstanding revealed something deeper—he didn't yet grasp what it truly means to be a follower of Christ.

Jesus's response was sharp:

> 33 But when Jesus turned and looked at his disciples, he rebuked Peter. "Get behind me, Satan!" he said. "You do not have in mind the concerns of God, but merely human concerns."

Jesus called Peter "Satan"—not as demon possession, but in the literal sense of "adversary." Peter had become an adversary because he was setting his mind on human concerns rather than God's purposes. Peter's love for his master was real, true enough, but beneath it lay self-interest. If your leader dies, won't you suffer too? Won't your security be threatened?

This is the heart of the problem Jesus identified: self-centeredness. "Get behind me" means to put those human concerns out of the way—to set aside selfish ambition, greed, and self-centeredness. Jesus had to carry his cross because heading to the cross meant that self-centeredness dies. At the cross, love wins, selfish ambition dies, generosity wins, human concerns die, and God's will triumphs.

## The Demand on His Followers

> 34 Then he called the crowd to him along with his disciples and said: "Whoever wants to be my disciple must deny themselves and take up their cross and follow me.

This wasn't merely an echo of what he'd said to Peter—it was an invitation. "Get behind me" becomes "follow me!" To follow Jesus means to deny yourself and carry your cross.

In the first century, taking up one's cross was no metaphor. Condemned prisoners would carry the heavy crossbeam to their execution, literally walking toward death. For us, it's a metaphor for suffering—but one with weight behind it.

Jesus himself exemplified this truth. We are sinners condemned by the judge of all creation to eternal death. But Jesus took that sentence upon himself instead—a swap has taken place. He denied himself and carried his cross so that we need not. If this is true of Jesus, it must also be true of those who claim to follow him.

The Christian road is the road of suffering for others. We are not here to make money, gain possessions, or collect experiences. We are here to serve the world. That's a tough calling:

- When the whole classroom is rude to a teacher, you're the odd one out who refuses to join them, risking bullying.
- When someone is being picked on, you stand up and protect them, putting yourself in the line of fire.
- You move a schoolbag from the hallway so it won't trip others.
- Your mother is working hard in the kitchen; you give up your computer game to help her.
- Your father is mowing in the hot sun; you leave your air-conditioned room to bring him water.
- You go to church or youth group to serve your friends even though your favorite show is on.

Will you serve as Christ served?

## The Journey of Understanding

We are often like Peter—we only half understand. We see the Messiah, the powerful King, but do we truly see what Jesus does and commit to following him? The problem is that we want to write our own destiny, to die slowly on our own comfortable terms.

Having a king means following that king. A king is not a prime minister bound by polls and donors—a king is sovereign. He gives orders to us, not the other way around. His orders become our way of life.

And it's not a bad thing to follow the Master. It is the truest path to life itself.
