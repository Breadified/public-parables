## Introduction: Why Marriage Matters

I want to begin with a provocative claim: we are made for marriage. This is not to diminish those who remain single—Scripture honors singleness as a valid calling. But the design of creation, and the trajectory of Scripture itself, points toward marriage as the ideal for most people.

I say this having only recently been married myself—my wife and I were joined in marriage just over a month ago. We met nearly four years ago at a beach mission, began dating last April, got engaged in December, and were married this past August. It's been quite a journey, and while my experience with marriage is still fresh, I'm grateful for the opportunity to speak to you about these relationships that matter so deeply.

---

## The Two Marriages: Little m and Big M

When we talk about marriage in Scripture, we must understand that there are really two marriages being discussed—and they exist on different planes altogether.

### The Little m Marriage (Earthly)

Paul addresses this directly in Ephesians 5. He outlines the roles of husbands and wives, their responsibilities to one another, and concludes with a profound statement: "For this reason a man will leave his father and mother and be united to his wife, and the two will become one flesh" (Ephesians 5:31).

This is earthly marriage—the union between two people committed to fellowship, intimacy, and building a life together.

### The Big M Marriage (Heavenly)

But then Paul does something remarkable. He pauses and says: "This is a profound mystery—but I am talking about Christ and the church" (Ephesians 5:32).

Marriage on earth is not the ultimate reality. It is a *shadow* of something far greater. The true marriage toward which all Christians are moving is our union with Christ himself. We are the bride; he is the bridegroom.

This becomes even clearer when we look at other passages:

**John the Baptist understood this.** When his disciples grew anxious that people were following Jesus instead of him, John responded with joy: "The bride belongs to the bridegroom. The friend who attends the bridegroom waits and listens for him, and is full of joy when he hears the bridegroom's voice. That joy is mine and it is now complete. He must become greater; I must become less" (John 3:29-30).

John saw himself as the best man at a wedding—necessary for the ceremony, but secondary to the bridegroom himself. His joy was complete in seeing Jesus receive the prominence he deserved.

**At the end of all things, this marriage will be consummated.** Revelation reveals the culmination: "I saw the Holy City, the new Jerusalem, coming down out of heaven from God, prepared as a bride beautifully dressed for her husband" (Revelation 21:2). The church—God's people—will be married to Christ in the ultimate wedding, celebrated with a great supper of the Lamb.

### The Relationship Between Them

Think of earthly marriage as a *trailer* for a movie you haven't yet seen. Before The Phantom Menace was released, people were so excited by the trailers that they'd buy tickets to other films just to watch them. But once the actual movie premiered, the trailers became redundant. No one buys the DVD for the trailers anymore.

Earthly marriage is similar. It points us to the reality of our heavenly marriage to Christ. It's real, it's good, and it matters—but it's not the main feature. Once the ultimate reality arrives, the shadow loses its significance. This is why there is no marriage in heaven: the substance has replaced the symbol.

## Living in Light of the Big M: Holiness Now

So if our ultimate destiny is marriage to Christ, what should we do *now*? How should we live while we wait?

The answer is simple: **we are to live in holiness.**

Paul writes: "Christ loved the church and gave himself up for her to make her holy, cleansing her by the washing with water through the word, and to present her to himself as a radiant church, without stain or wrinkle or any other blemish, but holy and blameless" (Ephesians 5:25-27).

Jesus died to make us clean. The wrath we deserved fell on him instead. The righteous man was condemned while we go free. He endured this trouble to create a people who are holy and blameless.

Our response must be to *become* that holy people he died to make us. We are to be radiant, without stain, wrinkle, or blemish.

### What Does Holiness Mean?

The word *holy* simply means "set apart." When my wife became engaged to me last December, she became set apart. She was no longer available to date others. Though not yet married, she belonged to her future husband. (This is why engagement rings exist—to make the commitment visible!)

In the months before our wedding, my wife prepared herself. She made sure her dress was bought and properly pressed. She had her hair done, her nails done. She ensured she would be presentable and beautiful on the wedding day—without blemish.

We are to do the same in our behavior while we prepare for our Big M Marriage to Christ. We are to be holy—set apart for him, preparing ourselves to meet him, keeping ourselves pure.

## Practical Application: Physical and Emotional Boundaries

This brings us to the most pressing question many of you have: **How far can you go physically in a boy-girl relationship?**

Physical and emotional intimacy is a wonderful thing. The desire to be close to someone, to hold them, to be with them—these are real and human longings. But we must remember a critical truth: if you are not yet married, **you are not yet in a marriage relationship.** You are still brothers and sisters in Christ, and you must treat each other as such.

### No Hint of Immorality

Paul is unambiguous on this point:

> Among you there must not be even a hint of sexual immorality, or of any kind of impurity, or of greed, because these are improper for God's holy people. Nor should there be obscenity, foolish talk or coarse joking, which are out of place, but rather thanksgiving. (Ephesians 5:3-4)

The standard is extraordinarily high: *not even a hint.* It covers everything—physical acts, yes, but also our speech, our humor, our thoughts. It is a blanket prohibition over anything that compromises sexual purity.

You may think I'm making too big a deal of this. But consider what Scripture says about the consequences:

> For of this you can be sure: No immoral, impure or greedy person—such a man is an idolater—has any inheritance in the kingdom of Christ and of God. Let no one deceive you with empty words, for because of such things God's wrath comes on those who are disobedient. (Ephesians 5:5-6)

The stakes are not small. There is no inheritance in Christ's kingdom for those who pursue sexual immorality. God's wrath comes on the disobedient. And you must not be deceived—by television, by friends, by the culture around you—into thinking this doesn't matter.

Be warned: it is far harder to stay pure than you might imagine. The pull toward physical intimacy is powerful, and once you begin crossing boundaries, it becomes exponentially harder to stop.

### Also: Don't Date Non-Christians

There is another critical boundary that flows from this: **Do not date non-Christians.**

Why would you allow someone who doesn't submit to Christ to influence you in such a profound and intimate way? Why would you give your heart to someone who doesn't worship your Lord?

Some of you may be thinking: "But what if they're interested in the gospel? What if they might become a Christian?"

Here's my answer: *Wait and see.* If they become genuinely Christian, that's wonderful. But many people are interested in Christianity—especially if they're interested in *you*. Don't confuse spiritual interest with genuine conversion. And don't mix your agendas. Show them how important your faith is by refusing to date them until they've made that commitment themselves.

If you're already dating a non-Christian, the answer is the same: break up and see if their interest in Christ was genuine. If it was, you can explore a relationship once they've truly come to faith.

The bottom line is this: **Your salvation is at stake.** At best, a non-Christian partner won't join you at the wedding supper of the Lamb. At worst, they'll lead you away from Christ entirely. Don't be deceived by empty words that it doesn't matter. It matters profoundly.

## The Relationship Between Little m and Big M

Here's what we must understand: **We are created for Big M.** Our ultimate marriage is to Christ, and that is the most important union we will ever have.

Whether you marry or remain single on this earth matters far less than whether you will be present at the wedding of the Lamb. Earthly marriage is good and proper—it provides fellowship, it allows for the raising of godly children, it is a beautiful picture of Christ's love for the church. But it remains only a *shadow* of the greater reality to come.

This doesn't diminish the importance of how we live now. Quite the opposite. Because we are engaged to Christ, we must live as engaged people. The imperative is clear: **be holy.** Be godly now—whether you are single or married—as you prepare for your ultimate marriage.

If you are single, be godly. If you are married, be godly. Let every relationship, every choice, every boundary you set be driven by the reality that you belong to Christ and are preparing to meet him.
