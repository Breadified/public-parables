## The Importance of Eating Together

We live in an age where shared meals have become rare. Research shows that the average American eats one in every five meals in her car, one in four Americans eats fast food every single day, and most families eat a single meal together less than five days a week. We come home late, eat on the run, and eat while we work.

This matters more than we realize. Not eating together has measurable negative effects:

- Students who don't regularly eat with their parents are significantly more likely to be truant at school
- Children who don't eat dinner with their parents at least twice a week are 40% more likely to be overweight
- Conversely, children who eat dinner with their parents five or more days a week have less trouble with drugs and alcohol, eat healthier, show better academic performance, and report being closer with their parents

**It's not about the food. It's about the together.**

In many cultures, mealtime is treated as sacred. In France, rushing through a meal invites disapproval. In Mexico and Cambodia, people gather intentionally in shared spaces to eat with loved ones. Dining together actually shifts people's perspectives—it reduces perceptions of inequality and helps people view those of different races, genders, and socioeconomic backgrounds as more equal.

This principle was deeply relevant to the Corinthian church, and it remains relevant to us today.

## Part 1: Eating Incorrectly (1 Corinthians 11:17-22)

### Paul's Harsh Assessment

Paul begins with stark criticism:

> In the following directives I have **no praise** for you, for your meetings **do more harm than good**. In the first place, I hear that when you come together as a church, there are divisions among you, and to some extent I believe it. No doubt there have to be differences among you to show which of you have God's approval.

Paul is not mincing words. He offers no praise. Their gatherings, he says, do more harm than good. There are divisions among them—literal "heresies" or separations.

Then he makes a startling statement:

> So then, when you come together, it is not the Lord's Supper you eat.

This is not about denying the tradition. "Lord's Supper" simply means they are not eating in the manner befitting the Lord. The Greek word *deipna* refers to a substantial evening meal—the kind Jesus shared with his disciples, the kind the early church gathered for daily. But the Corinthians' meals had become miserable occasions.

### What Was Going Wrong

> For when you are eating, some of you go ahead with your own private suppers. As a result, one person remains hungry and another gets drunk. Don't you have homes to eat and drink in? Or do you despise the church of God by humiliating those who have nothing?

The problem was selfishness. Some were eating their own food while others went hungry. Some arrived early and gorged themselves, getting drunk, while latecomers found nothing. It may have been a potluck situation where the rich brought their own food and ate their own portions, while the poor who brought nothing went without.

The result was devastating: they despised the gathered church and humiliated those who had nothing.

### The Application to Us

Share your food. If everyone in your group is hungry and you have a big bag of KFC or McDonald's, you don't eat it alone. There is no "I" in church—only "you" and "you." 

This doesn't mean endless entitlement or always buying someone lunch. Rather, when you gather together, it's on the house. Everyone participates. Rich and poor alike share what they have. When you eat together after church, don't rush to grab the best pieces first. Save seats for those delayed by cleanup. Help wash up afterwards. 

When you gather as a church family, make sure everyone is cared for. This is what it means to receive each other at the table.

## Part 2: Eating Rightly (1 Corinthians 11:23-26)

### Jesus' Example

> For I received from the Lord what I also passed on to you: The Lord Jesus, on the night he was betrayed, took bread, and when he had given thanks, he broke it and said, "This is my body, which is for you; do this in remembrance of me." In the same way, after supper he took the cup, saying, "This cup is the new covenant in my blood; do this, whenever you drink it, in remembrance of me." For whenever you eat this bread and drink this cup, you proclaim the Lord's death until he comes.

Consider what Jesus did at his last meal. He served them. He took bread, broke it, and gave it to the others. He took the cup and gave it to the disciples. He served them.

This is all the more remarkable because it was his last meal on earth. Think of how condemned prisoners often request one final extravagant meal as their last indulgence. They have one final chance to be served, to be treated specially.

But Jesus? His last meal wasn't about indulgence. It wasn't about being served. It was about serving. He could have spent those final hours thinking of himself, resting before his agony. Instead, he poured himself out in service to others.

And the meal itself was merely a glimpse of what would follow. The very next day, his body would be broken—physically bashed, abused, crucified—for us. His blood would be poured out, from the lashings, the thorns, the nails, the piercing spear. All of it for you, for your sins, for my sins. He became the Passover Lamb, the ultimate sacrifice.

Jesus ate in a manner worthy of God. He modeled sacrificial, other-centered love.

### What Was Actually Passed On

Notice what Paul says he received from the Lord: he never actually ate with Jesus. So what was "passed on"?

Not a ritual. Not a special mystical way of eating bread and wine. Rather, Jesus passed on to Paul—and passes on to us—the gospel itself. Jesus calls us to sacrifice for others, just as he does. Paul was told:

> This man is my chosen instrument to proclaim my name to the Gentiles and their kings and to the people of Israel. I will show him how much he must suffer for my name.

What's passed down is not ceremony but the **other-person centredness of the Cross**. Jesus died to save us. He served us by taking on the punishment of our sins.

What's passed on is the proclamation of the Lord's death—and that proclamation is **love. Sacrificial love. Serving love. Other-person centredness. Even unto death.**

This is expressed every day, and all the more when we gather together on Sunday. It's normally expressed in how we hang out with each other and how we eat together. There's a tight relationship between the meeting and the eating. We meet over coffee, lunch, or dinner. At work, we gather by the water cooler. We meet our families typically at the evening meal.

Our eating is important because our meeting is important. And for Christians, our meals hold special spiritual significance.

**Eating is where we practice meeting. Meals are our great opportunity to learn to love and to serve.**

## The Consequences of Eating Unworthily (1 Corinthians 11:27-32)

> Whoever eats the bread or drinks the cup of the Lord in an unworthy manner will be guilty of sinning against the body and blood of the Lord. Everyone ought to examine themselves before they eat of the bread and drink from the cup. For those who eat and drink without discerning the body of Christ eat and drink judgment on themselves.

When Paul speaks of "the body and blood of the Lord," he's not talking about Jesus' literal body and blood (which are safe in heaven). He's referring to the corporate body—the church itself.

> Now you are the body of Christ, and each one of you is a part of it. (1 Corinthians 12:27)

When we eat selfishly, failing to recognize and honor the body of Christ gathered before us, we eat and drink judgment on ourselves.

Paul is explicit about the consequences:

> That is why many among you are weak and sick, and a number of you have fallen asleep. But if we were more discerning with regard to ourselves, we would not come under such judgment. Nevertheless, when we are judged in this way by the Lord, we are being disciplined so that we will not be finally condemned with the world.

The judgment is twofold. First, there is judgment on the individuals who sin through their selfishness. But second, the body of Christ itself suffers. People become weak and sick. Some have even died—"fallen asleep" is a euphemism for death (1 Corinthians 15:18).

In an ancient, subsistent world, if one is always full and another is always hungry, we might naturally expect that the poor and weak would become weaker and sicker, and some might even die from hunger. Your brother or sister in Christ dies because you wouldn't share.

This is serious. This is why Paul says eating unworthily results in judgment. God disciplines his people not to condemn them finally, but to call them back to the heart of the gospel: sacrificial love for one another.

## The Call to Wait for One Another (1 Corinthians 11:33)

> So then, my brothers and sisters, when you gather to eat, **you should all eat together**.

The Greek word here literally means "wait for each other." The old NIV translation captures this better. Paul is calling them to expect each other, to look out for one another, to have regard for each other. It appears elsewhere in Scripture:

> I am **expecting** him along with the brothers. (1 Corinthians 16:11)

> He was **looking forward** to the city with foundations, whose architect and builder is God. (Hebrews 11:10)

The same word conveys anticipation, hope, and care. Paul wants the Corinthians to gather with that attitude—eagerly expecting one another, genuinely looking forward to eating together, caring for those who arrive late or move slowly.

### Practical Application

Don't rush in to the food and take the majority, leaving nothing for others. Invite those who are slow to get their food first. Some go first to get food for those who are immobile or can't feed themselves. Or go first, but grab only a little portion—this ensures there's enough for those at the end of the line. Then go back for seconds. You're making sure everyone has enough.

## Part 3: Eating Together Is About More Than Food

> Anyone who is hungry should eat something at home, so that when you meet together it may not result in judgment. And when I come I will give further directions. (1 Corinthians 11:34)

If all you care about is food, then why are you here? If you want only to care for yourself and eat your own meal and do your own thing, then go home. But when we are together, it must be about each other.

This is a higher calling. Our gatherings have stakes beyond the visible. Meals are not ultimately about nourishment. They are about fellowship. They are about the Lord's Supper—eating in remembrance of Jesus' sacrificial love.

### What to Do While You Eat

**Proclaim in deed.** Do you serve at meals—making sure everyone has a seat, is comfortable, and has their food? When going out for lunch after church, do you save a seat for those delayed by cleanup? Do you take the best piece first, or do you grab it for others? Do you help wash up afterwards?

**Proclaim in words.** Meals are opportunities to talk to one another and encourage. Not endless chatter about random, amusing but irrelevant matters. After church, ask each other: "What did you learn from the Bible today?" There's room for banter and breaking the ice, but don't let your whole life be just breaking ice. Where are we breaking to?

**Pray.** At meals, take the opportunity to pray for each other. "Grace" is not just a token prayer before eating. Better to pray afterwards, once you've heard how everyone is doing. If hungry, eat first, then spend time praying together. This is what grace truly means—giving thanks together and interceding for one another.

## The Evolution of the Lord's Supper

Over time, the full meal shared by the early church became truncated into a symbolic ritual. People got busy. They didn't have time. So the ritual of the Lord's Supper, Holy Communion, or the Eucharist (meaning "giving thanks") came into being.

But we have a more truthful Holy Communion than the symbolic version—it's the actual gathering to eat together, as the early church did.

Consider the early church:

> They devoted themselves to the apostles' teaching and to fellowship, to the breaking of bread and to prayer. (Acts 2:42)

> Every day they continued to meet together in the temple courts. They broke bread in their homes and ate together with glad and sincere hearts. (Acts 2:46)

> On the first day of the week we came together to break bread. Paul spoke to the people and, because he intended to leave the next day, kept on talking until midnight. (Acts 20:7)

> Then he went upstairs again and broke bread and ate. After talking until daylight, he left. (Acts 20:11)

Every day—the Roman Empire had no holidays. In the evening after work, the early church would gather. This meal tied them together. They mixed it with teaching, fellowship, and prayer. They kept talking. There was a certain attitude, a certain spirit, as Christians following Jesus.

Building this culture is not easy. But it's what Jesus wants for us. We are so separated by distance and long work hours. We are isolated. When we do get together, don't take it for granted. Make use of the opportunity. Don't run off from church immediately. Stay a little longer. Chat. Encourage. Be encouraged. Pray.

Many of the most fond memories of church are not memories of the food itself but memories of service, love, and unity shared around the table.

---

## How Do You Eat?

This is the question we must answer. When you gather with others—in your family, in your workplace, in your church—how do you eat? Do you serve? Do you look out for others? Do you make space? Do you share what you have?

Jesus showed us the way. He came not to be served, but to serve. He broke bread with others, gave thanks, and poured out his life in love.

When we eat together, we proclaim his death. We remember his sacrifice. And we practice becoming like him—learning to love sacrificially, to serve generously, and to receive one another as Christ has received us.
