# 1 Peter 5:1-12

**Title:** Living as Aliens: Relationships Across Generations
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Foundation: Aliens in This World

The book of 1 Peter teaches us something essential about Christian identity. According to Scripture, we are all foreigners and strangers in this world—migrants, as some translations put it. We will never quite fit in, and that's by design. This reality shapes how we navigate every relationship: with government and employers, with spouses, with children, and critically, with one another across the generations.

## The Challenge of Youth and Authority

Young people often struggle in their relationships with those older than them. This friction shows up in different ways:

- Conflict with leaders, teachers, and mentors
- Tension at home with parents who seem out of touch
- Responses that range from open rebellion to silent resentment
- In Asian families especially, anger is often internalized—expressed through isolation, music, and anonymous online spaces rather than direct conversation

The root is the same: young people don't understand why elders make the decisions they do, and elders don't always understand the frustrations of the young.

## The Path Forward: Humble Yourselves

Peter addresses this directly in verses 5-10. His instruction is deceptively simple: **Humble yourselves under God's mighty hand.** This isn't weakness—it's alignment with God's order.

The passage continues with practical wisdom:

> Cast all your anxiety on him because he cares for you. Be alert and of sober mind. Your enemy the devil prowls around like a roaring lion looking for someone to devour. Resist him, standing firm in the faith.

Notice what's missing: Peter doesn't say, "Young people, wait until the system changes." He says, "Work within the system. Express your disagreement with respect."

This might sound like: "Dad, I don't think that's right, but I'm listening to you." Or, "Mum, I disagree. Can I explain why?" Or, "Youth group isn't as engaging as it used to be. Can I help us make it better?"

Here's the encouragement: we're all in the same boat. I had parents just like yours. Each of you has parents like the person sitting next to you. We all faced the same pressures—the same obsession with grades, the same pressure to excel academically, the same expectation to master piano or other skills. The struggle is universal across generations.

And Peter's promise matters: things will get better. In time, you will be the elder. The roles will shift.

## For Elders: Leadership as Service

This is the opportunity and the responsibility. When you become an elder, you have the chance to change things—to address what annoyed you when you were younger. But here's the critical distinction: **not for personal gain, but for the good of others.**

Leadership in God's kingdom is fundamentally about service. It's your chance to make a difference, to strengthen the younger generation, to build something that lasts. That's the calling.
