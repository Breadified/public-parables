# Luke 14:25-35

**Title:** Adulting: Courage
**Date:** 19 September 2021
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Following Jesus Requires Risk

> 14:25 Large crowds were traveling with Jesus, and turning to them he said: 26 "If anyone comes to me and does not hate father and mother, wife and children, brothers and sisters—yes, even their own life—such a person cannot be my disciple. 27 And whoever does not carry their cross and follow me cannot be my disciple.

When Jesus speaks of "hating" one's own life, he doesn't mean self-hatred or suicidal thinking. Scripture elsewhere affirms that we care for our bodies as Christ cares for the church (Ephesians 5:29). Rather, this is a term of comparison: following Jesus must supersede all other loyalties—even those we rightly hold dear like family. Everything becomes secondary to our primary allegiance to Christ.

This principle extends to all we possess:

> 33 In the same way, those of you who do not give up everything you have cannot be my disciples.

Jesus calls us to surrender our wealth, careers, dreams, and aspirations—to exchange our priorities for God's priorities. Sometimes this surrender happens gradually through deliberate choices. Sometimes it comes suddenly. But eventually, being Christian will cost us something.

**This is because the disciple of Jesus prioritizes doing the right thing over personal safety.** Often, the right choice is also the safe choice—avoiding crime, drugs, and promiscuity protects both morality and health. But sometimes righteousness demands risk. When push comes to shove, we follow God regardless of consequences. Because if our primary calling is to be Jesus' disciple, all else becomes secondary.

### Examples of Costly Discipleship

**Proclaiming the Gospel** invites rejection and offense. **Speaking truth to power** is never safe. History remembers those who paid dearly for their faith:
- Martin Luther stood against the Holy Roman Empire
- Thomas Cranmer was burned at the stake in Oxford (1556), thrusting his right hand—which had signed his recantation—into the flames first
- Wycliffe died for translating the Bible into English

**Living ethically** means refusing to bully neighbors even when government commands it, accepting unpopularity, and resisting cultural conformity. Dietrich Bonhoeffer stood against Hitler at the cost of his life.

**Surrendering wealth and opportunity** characterizes those who give up fortune for God's purposes. Hudson Taylor traveled to unknown lands. Organizations like Compassion Australia and the Red Cross were born from such sacrifice. Yet in our time, few would be willing to put fortune, houses, and careers on the line.

The epitaph of those Christian warriors reads: "They came, they saw, they conquered." Today's epitaph risks becoming: "They stayed home" or "Meh."

### Our Culture of Safety Anxiety

The COVID pandemic revealed something troubling about our modern preoccupation with safety. The original strain had low severity—many cases were asymptomatic, children were largely unaffected, and deaths concentrated among those over 65. Yet we responded with unprecedented fear, doing things never before attempted in history: shutting down entire economies and quarantining the healthy.

We were personally terrified despite low probabilities and minimal personal exposure. We accepted the unthinkable—letting the dying pass alone, frightened and unable to say goodbye. We spoke of being "safe" as if stepping outside meant facing a zombie apocalypse. This response was disproportionate to the actual danger.

**This is not isolated to the pandemic.** Jonathan Haidt's *The Coddling of the American Mind* documents a shift in thinking happening over the past 10-20 years, particularly among those born after 1995:

- Governments, media, and universities have gone into overdrive protecting people from racism, sexism, and other -isms
- Terms once reserved for the psychologically ill—"safe spaces," "trigger warnings," "microaggressions"—have become mainstream
- Speech has been equated with violence
- We no longer teach the childhood wisdom: "Sticks and stones may break my bones, but your words will never hurt me"

Moreover, young people rarely do risky things anymore. They delay getting driver's licenses, going on dates, and taking other normal coming-of-age steps. Not primarily from moral conviction, but because they remain psychologically children—preferring screens, safety, and someone to protect them from imaginary boogie monsters. **Such people are not those who will carry their cross.**

## Count the Cost Carefully

Does this mean we ignore risk? No. Scripture calls us to prudent calculation:

> Luke 14:28 "Suppose one of you wants to build a tower. Won't you first sit down and estimate the cost to see if you have enough money to complete it? 29 For if you lay the foundation and are not able to finish it, everyone who sees it will ridicule you... 31 Or suppose a king is about to go to war against another king. Won't he first sit down and consider whether he is able with ten thousand men to oppose the one coming against him with twenty thousand?"

We are not gamblers, acting on whim or feeling. We count. Yet we're poor at this because:
- Schools teach calculus rather than probability
- We catastrophize danger (fixating on 2% fatality rates)
- Fear and decision anxiety paralyze us
- Sometimes we plan excessively

> James 4:13 Now listen, you who say, "Today or tomorrow we will go to this or that city, spend a year there, carry on business and make money." 14 Why, you do not even know what will happen tomorrow. What is your life? You are a mist that appears for a little while and then vanishes. 15 Instead, you ought to say, "If it is the Lord's will, we will live and do this or that."

Yes, we cannot know the future, nor do we have capacity to plan it perfectly. We are a mist. But this doesn't paralyze action. Rather, we ask: "Is this the Lord's will?" Then we proceed one step at a time. **Do the next right thing.**

### Choice Requires Risk

Do you want choice? Choice comes with risk—the cost of freedom is the possibility of choosing wrongly. The alternative to risk is fatalism: believing the gods (or government) control everything and our actions don't matter. But we believe otherwise. Even small actions—sharing the gospel, building institutions that outlast us, making a difference in our world—matter.

**If worried, take many quick, small steps.** Like walking in fog, you can't see far ahead, but small steps let you course-correct. This requires tolerance for ambiguity, difficult for those raised in environments demanding perfection—piano, exam marks, memorized essays. Life isn't a Gantt chart. The real world offers no spoon-feeding; the standard is "that'll do," achieved without luxury of time.

**Living with godly intention but less-than-perfect decisions is the path forward.** Perfect timing and decisions are hard. Most of us won't marry our dream person—we marry someone and build love through work. Most won't find the perfect house—we buy within budget and make it perfect. **The best is often the enemy of the good.** Don't wait passively for perfection; make perfection through action.

## Being Safe Also Carries Cost

> Luke 14:34-35 "Salt is good, but if it loses its saltiness, how can it be made salty again? It is fit neither for the soil nor for the manure pile; it is thrown out."

We have one life. Time moves forward. **Being safe sometimes is not the safe choice.** There is a cost to making the safe choice.

If we're always afraid to be salt of the earth, how can we fulfill that calling?

Jonathan Haidt notes something counterintuitive: **being safe can produce the opposite of safety.** Consider peanut allergies. Avoiding peanuts exacerbates the allergy. In trials with young children who had eczema and egg allergies, those who avoided peanuts developed allergies at 14% rates, while those encouraged to eat peanuts developed them at only 2%. Pregnant women who ate peanuts had children less likely to develop allergies. Delayed exposure until adulthood often means it's too late.

**No psychologist recommends avoidance as treatment for phobias.** Keeping someone safe sometimes makes them more vulnerable in the future.

Could COVID follow this pattern? The original strain was mild, yet by avoiding it, did we inadvertently weaken our defenses? Now Delta—genuinely concerning, affecting younger people, more infectious—finds us unprepared. **For some things in life require risk to develop strength:**

- Our immune systems strengthen through exposure
- Our bones strengthen through stress
- Our economy strengthens through challenge
- Our children develop through adversity

> Romans 5:3-5 We rejoice in our sufferings knowing that suffering produces endurance, and endurance produces character and character produces hope.

We prepare children *for* the road, not the road *for* the child. A secure base in childhood exists so children can explore and take risks, not remain perpetually coddled.

### How We're Raising the Next Generation

Playgrounds have changed dramatically:

**Today:** Plastic, low height, soft-fall ground.

**Yesterday:** Hard surfaces, real danger of injury.

The old philosophy: "That must have hurt. But it's okay—it's just a bit of blood. Go play. Try again. You can do it." (Empathy)

The new approach: "That must have hurt terribly. Better go home. Don't climb anymore." (Sympathy without growth)

Children need to break bones to learn caution. They need to cut themselves to avoid sharp objects. They need to fail exams to understand success. They need to fall downstairs to develop balance. They need splinters and small injuries to learn prevention. **If they can get hurt, they learn how not to get hurt.** But helicopter parenting prevents this learning.

Enid Blyton's books, though not intentionally written as parenting guides, show children taking real risks—the path to growing into adults. When we prevent all risk, we prevent growth. If we've done our job well, our children want adulthood. Yet we stop them from reaching it.

If we never take risks, our hearts never break—but we also miss the profound experience of love. We don't want to be candles afraid of the wind. **We want to be bonfires that welcome the wind.**

We cannot avoid cost. We can only choose which cost to bear. We cannot avoid sacrifice. We can only choose our sacrifice. **Choose your cost while you can.**

## The Path Forward

You have already taken the greatest risk: becoming Christian. You trusted a man carrying a cross. Now he asks you to carry the cross also.

The normal stages of adulthood require courage:

**Asking someone out** demands vulnerability. You imagine for months, tread carefully, face rejection—then need time to recover. Move faster.

**Getting married** means committing to another person despite uncertainty. Instead of endless planning—careers, overseas trips for cheaper rings, restaurant reservations months in advance—simply do it. A church, a Zoom call. Done. Leave father and mother.

**Having children** brings the objection: "I don't have enough money." What are your children eating—sashimi daily? When did Christians prioritize money above God's will? I'm unaware of an eleventh commandment: "Thou shalt buy a house before having children." **It's God's will that those who have sex produce children.** We're wealthier than ever, yet we delay. Are we following God's will or our own?

**Starting businesses, changing the world, speaking truth to power, and committing to Christ**—these require stepping into the fog. What stops you from taking the next step?

Life is inherently risky. COVID reminds us that stepping outside carries risk. So does driving, eating certain foods, running in the sun. **But life is also fleeting.** There is equal cost to doing nothing—the opportunity cost of a life unlived, of callings ignored, of love unexpressed before time runs out.

> Ecclesiastes 9:7-10 Go, eat your food with gladness, and drink your wine with a joyful heart, for God has already approved what you do. Always be clothed in white, and always anoint your head with oil. Enjoy life with your wife, whom you love, all the days of this meaningless life that God has given you under the sun—all your meaningless days. For this is your lot in life and in your toilsome labor under the sun. Whatever your hand finds to do, do it with all your might, for in the realm of the dead, where you are going, there is neither working nor planning nor knowledge nor wisdom.

This passage speaks to our moment. Life is short. Food prepared cannot wait—it spoils. Marriage cannot wait indefinitely. We are mortal. The opportunity before you—to begin a family, pursue a calling, speak the truth, follow Christ—**cannot wait until conditions are perfect.**

Some of you, like Brian and Bianca, have decided to seize the day despite uncertainty. You're taking the risk of beginning life together, starting a family, trusting God's purpose. This is courage. This is adulthood.

**May you continue to risk it all for God.**
