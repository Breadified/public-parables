# Hebrews 12:18–29

**Title:** Running Together: From Mount Sinai to Mount Zion

**Date:** 27 July 2024 (ARISE); Originally 9 September 2018 (SMAC)

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Two Mountains: A Contrast in Experience

As we continue through the latter half of Hebrews, we encounter teaching about perseverance in faith despite hardship. One question naturally arises: wouldn't it be easier to maintain our faith if we could experience God physically? This passage answers that question through a striking comparison between two mountains—one that terrified, one that invites.

### Mount Sinai: A Fiery Mountain We Have Not Come To

> You have not come to a mountain that can be touched and that is burning with fire; to darkness, gloom and storm; to a trumpet blast or to such a voice speaking words that those who heard it begged that no further word be spoken to them, because they could not bear what was commanded: "If even an animal touches the mountain, it must be stoned to death." (Hebrews 12:18–20)

The writer describes Mount Sinai—perhaps even a volcano itself. The Arabian peninsula sits on a major tectonic plate with numerous fissures, making volcanic activity plausible in that region. This wasn't merely a mountain; it was the place where God met his people.

**The Historical Context**

Approximately 1,500 years before Jesus, after Israel's escape from Egyptian slavery, God brought the people to Mount Sinai. The pillar of cloud and fire that had guided them became the sign of God's presence at this holy place. Here, on this volcano-like mountain, God would finally speak directly to his people.

The preparation was elaborate and filled with warning:

> "Go to the people and consecrate them today and tomorrow. Have them wash their clothes and be ready by the third day, because on that day the Lord will come down on Mount Sinai in the sight of all the people. Put limits for the people around the mountain and tell them, 'Be careful that you do not approach the mountain or touch the foot of it. Whoever touches the mountain is to be put to death. They are to be stoned or shot with arrows; not a hand is to be laid on them. No person or animal shall be permitted to live. Only when the ram's horn sounds a long blast may they approach the mountain.'" (Exodus 19:10–13)

On the appointed day:

> "On the morning of the third day there was thunder and lightning, with a thick cloud over the mountain, and a very loud trumpet blast. Everyone in the camp trembled. Then Moses led the people out of the camp to meet with God, and they stood at the foot of the mountain. Mount Sinai was covered with smoke, because the Lord descended on it in fire. The smoke billowed up from it like smoke from a furnace, and the whole mountain trembled violently. As the sound of the trumpet grew louder and louder, Moses spoke and the voice of God answered him." (Exodus 19:16–19)

### Why Such Terror? Understanding God and Sin

The overwhelming display of fire, smoke, darkness, and sound reveals something fundamental about God's character: his absolute intolerance of sin.

Consider a simple illustration. If you found even a trace of something repulsive in your food—a speck of contamination—you would recoil in horror. You'd throw it away, scrub your dishes, perhaps fumigate your kitchen. The amount doesn't matter; even the tiniest trace is intolerable because of what it represents.

God's reaction to sin mirrors this perfectly. Sin is repulsive to him. It's not a matter of degree—whether the sin is large or small. It's not even about the sinner versus the sin itself. By association with sin, we are condemned. A cockroach or rat may be clean creatures, but we react with revulsion because of what they carry, what they represent.

> "The sight was so terrifying that Moses said, 'I am trembling with fear.'" (Hebrews 12:21)

Moses—who knew God intimately, who spoke with him face to face—trembled with terror. If such an encounter with God's holiness nearly destroyed even God's closest friend, what hope do the rest of us have?

**The Central Problem**

Here lies the heart of the matter: when God appeared tangibly to humanity, he was unapproachable. To truly experience God as he is would be to face a consuming fire. The experience would be fatal.

### The Modern Search for Experience

We live in an age obsessed with experience. Post-modern culture has moved beyond materialism—we no longer primarily collect possessions. Instead, we collect experiences. We travel for Instagram photos. We purchase moments rather than things. We hope that by documenting our experiences, we can make them last forever. Experience has become our new idol.

Some churches have made their distinctive claim precisely this: that they can bring you into God's presence. They can take you to the mountaintop. They can deliver the experience of God.

But here's the truth: you cannot experience God the way the ancient writers describe, because you would not survive it.

There are entire denominations whose primary selling point is their claim to deliver what Mount Sinai represents—a tangible, visceral encounter with the divine. Yet the Scriptures tell us this is precisely what we cannot have without perishing.

### A Personal Word on Feeling and Faith

I understand the longing for this experience. When I first became a Christian as a teenager, everything converged at once—new friends, romance, academic success, spiritual awakening. My hormones amplified the already transcendent feelings I was experiencing. That peak never returns.

But I cannot equate the fading of those feelings with God becoming unreal. Feelings are merely feelings. They are not facts. Facts don't care about your feelings. The fact that God exists and is terrifyingly holy doesn't depend on whether you feel it to be true. The absence of pleasure or the presence of suffering does not mean you've been abandoned by God.

This is the crisis of our generation. We've been taught to trust our emotions as our guide, yet faith calls us to something deeper—commitment grounded not in feeling but in truth.

## Mount Zion: A Better Mountain We Have Come To

> But you have come to Mount Zion, to the city of the living God, the heavenly Jerusalem. You have come to thousands upon thousands of angels in joyful assembly, to the church of the firstborn, whose names are written in heaven. You have come to God, the Judge of all, to the spirits of the righteous made perfect, to Jesus the mediator of a new covenant, and to the sprinkled blood that speaks a better word than the blood of Abel. (Hebrews 12:22–24)

The contrast is absolute. You have not come to Mount Sinai. You have come to Mount Zion—the heavenly Jerusalem.

What a different picture emerges! Here is not terror but belonging. Not exclusion but inclusion. Not death but life. The angels gather in joyful assembly. Your name is written in heaven. You stand before God not as a condemned criminal but as part of the "church of the firstborn"—redeemed and restored.

### The Superiority of Christ's Word

> "The sprinkled blood that speaks a better word than the blood of Abel."

Abel's blood cried out from the ground for justice—the first murder recorded in Scripture. It demanded vindication and vengeance. But Christ's blood speaks a better word: forgiveness, redemption, reconciliation.

Recall Hebrews 1:1–2:

> "In the past God spoke to our ancestors through the prophets at many times and in various ways, but in these last days he has spoken to us by his Son, whom he appointed heir of all things, and through whom also he made the universe."

God's final, complete revelation came through Jesus. And this word is not external to us; it lives within those who believe. As Hebrews 4:12 tells us:

> "The word of God is alive and active. Sharper than any double-edged sword, it penetrates even to dividing soul and spirit, joints and marrow; it judges the thoughts and attitudes of the heart."

The difference between Sinai and Zion is the difference between external command and internal transformation. At Sinai, the law came from outside, delivered with terror. At Zion, through Christ, God's word cuts into the very marrow of our being, changing us from within.

Zion is not merely spatially superior to Sinai (heavenly versus earthly); it is functionally superior. Christ's priestly ministry from heaven transforms the internal landscape of the believer—our hearts and minds are renewed. The ministry at Sinai remained external, unable to perfect the conscience of the worshiper. But Christ goes deeper.

## The Application: Persevering Through the Shaking

> See to it that you do not refuse him who speaks. If they did not escape when they refused him who warned them on earth, how much less will we, if we turn away from him who warns us from heaven? At that time his voice shook the earth, but now he has promised, "Once more I will shake not only the earth but also the heavens." The words "once more" indicate the removing of what can be shaken—that is, created things—so that what cannot be shaken may remain. Therefore, since we are receiving a kingdom that cannot be shaken, let us be thankful, and so worship God acceptably with reverence and awe; for our "God is a consuming fire." (Hebrews 12:25–29)

The argument moves to its climax: if those who rejected God's warning at Sinai could not escape, how much more serious is our position if we turn away from him who speaks from heaven? The logic is irresistible.

God will shake the earth and the heavens once more. Everything that can be shaken will be removed. Only what is unshakable will endure. This includes the kingdom we have received through Christ.

### Living Without Experience, Yet With Certainty

The practical challenge before us is this: we are called to persevere in faith without the luxury of constant feeling or experience. Chapter 13 addresses the practical disciplines this requires—loving one another, showing hospitality, remembering the suffering, honoring marriage, being content with what we have:

> "Keep your lives free from the love of money and be content with what you have, because God has said, 'Never will I leave you; never will I forsake you.' So we say with confidence, 'The Lord is my helper; I will not be afraid. What can mere mortals do to me?'" (Hebrews 13:5–6)

### The Crisis of Commitment

Our generation struggles with discipline. We are "Gen Y"—born into abundance and constant stimulation. We expect satisfaction in everything. When a job doesn't fulfill us, we resign by text. We post our absence on social media instead of calling. We avoid commitments we've made. Half our university students don't finish the courses they begin. Half our marriages end in divorce. We've lost the grit of previous generations.

This failure of discipline flows into our Christian lives. We treat God as an optional "maybe" button on the Facebook of life. We're flaky. We want the experience without the commitment.

But to be a disciple is to have discipline. To follow Christ requires faithfulness, commitment, grit. Righteousness, holiness, self-control, generosity, compassion, service—all require tremendous will power when confronted by the distractions of the world.

### The Example of Perseverance

There is a story worth remembering. In the 1968 Olympic Marathon in Mexico City, a runner named Akhwari fell badly at the 19-kilometer mark. His knee was apparently dislocated, his leg bleeding. Medical staff urged him to withdraw.

But Akhwari continued—limping, walking, running in agony. Of the 75 who started, 57 finished. But what matters is that Akhwari finished, over an hour after the winner. As he crossed the finish line in the gathering dusk, a small crowd that remained cheered him on.

When asked why he didn't quit, Akhwari replied: "My country did not send me 5,000 miles just to start the race; they sent me to finish the race."

No one remembers the gold medalist that year. But we honor Akhwari for finishing in the most difficult circumstances. So it is with faith. We are sent not merely to begin the Christian life but to finish it.

### Final Exhortation

> "See to it that you do not refuse him who speaks."

Don't seek futile experiences and place your hopes in them. Don't boast that you're not materialistic, only to be hedonistic in collecting meaningless experiences. Faith is not based on our feelings. Suffering does not mean God doesn't love you or isn't present. The lack of pleasure doesn't mean you've been abandoned.

We have come to Mount Zion. We have received a kingdom that cannot be shaken. Let us therefore be thankful, worship with reverence and awe, and persevere—not because we feel like it, but because we know it's true.
