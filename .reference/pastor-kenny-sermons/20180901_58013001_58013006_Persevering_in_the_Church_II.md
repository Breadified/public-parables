# Hebrews 13:1-6

**Title:** Persevering in the Church II
**Date:** 9 September 2018
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Philadelphia: Love of the Brethren

The opening command reflects the meaning of "Philadelphia"—a city name composed of two Greek words: *philo* (love) and *adelphos* (brethren). William Penn, a Quaker who had experienced religious persecution himself, envisioned Philadelphia as a place where all people, regardless of religion, could worship freely and live together.

> Keep on loving one another as brothers and sisters. (13:1)

This is not merely a suggestion but a charge to persist in brotherly love. It calls us to embrace one another as family within the household of faith.

## Welcoming Strangers

> Do not forget to show hospitality to strangers, for by so doing some people have shown hospitality to angels without knowing it. (13:2)

The Greek word for hospitality literally means "love of strangers" (*xenophileos*). In biblical times, there were no inns except those associated with brothels and drunkenness. Travelers depended entirely on the hospitality of homes. The word "hospital" itself derives from the Latin *hospes*, meaning stranger or foreigner. Our modern hospitals trace their origin to the Order of the Hospital (the Knights of Malta), founded in Jerusalem before the First Crusade. These orders cared for pilgrims and those without anyone else to look after them, viewing the sick as "our lords" and serving Christ through their compassionate care.

The command carries particular weight: to "forget" means to be unaware, to lose sight of this obligation. We must not forget status, not forget ego, not forget our own selfish comfort. To welcome the stranger is to identify with Christ Himself, as Matthew 25 makes clear—whatever we do for the least of these, we do for Him.

Interestingly, Christian-influenced countries demonstrate the most multicultural populations globally. Yet while we excel at the macro level in welcoming immigrants, we must examine ourselves locally—in our churches, in our homes. As Leviticus reminds us:

> When a foreigner resides among you in your land, do not mistreat them. Love them as yourself, for you were foreigners in Egypt. (19:33-34)

## Remembering the Suffering

> Continue to remember those in prison as if you were together with them in prison, and those who are mistreated as if you yourselves were suffering. (13:3)

This command calls for empathetic identification. We are to remember those imprisoned unjustly and those suffering persecution—not as distant concerns, but as if we ourselves were there in their place. Christianity remains the most persecuted religion in the world. Believers in Muslim-majority countries face profound vulnerability with little opportunity to hold positions of influence. Behind statistics are heroic stories of Christians refusing to renounce their faith, of moderate Muslims forming human rings around Coptic churches, of believers gathering to worship in hidden basements.

Yet persecution continues. We must not simply offer comfortable prayers from a distance. The call is for genuine solidarity—to bear their burdens as our own.

## Honoring Marriage

> Marriage should be honored by all, and the marriage bed kept pure, for God will judge the adulterer and all the sexually immoral. (13:4)

The word "undefiled" (*amiantos*) appears only four times in the New Testament in this exact form. It means "uncontaminated" or "set apart." Hebrews 7:26 uses this word to describe Jesus Christ our high priest, and James 1:27 describes undefiled religion as that which helps widows and orphans and remains unstained by the world.

Marriage itself is a covenant to be honored. The marriage bed—a metaphor for sexual intimacy reserved for spouses—must remain pure. This extends beyond mere consent; it speaks to the sanctity of the marital bond. No pornography, no third parties, no infidelity defiles what God has given as a sacred gift between husband and wife alone. The sexual union is reserved exclusively for marriage.

But this protection must begin before marriage as well. We must keep the marriage bed pure before marriage too—avoiding quasi-marriages, overnight trips that simulate marital closeness, and the subtle ways we compromise this boundary. Even newly married couples should be intentional about not allowing the world to intrude into this sacred space.

## Freedom from the Love of Money

> Keep your lives free from the love of money and be content with what you have, because God has said, "Never will I leave you; never will I forsake you." (13:5)

The Greek word *aphilargyros* means freedom from the love of silver and gold. The inverse correlation is striking: as the size of a house increases, so often does the hospitality decrease. A small house forces us to squeeze guests in, creating natural hospitality. A larger house becomes easier to hoard, to reserve for ourselves alone.

Money can strangle love. Where the love of money grows, love for others shrinks. Contentment with what we have—not apathy or indulgence, but true satisfaction in God's provision—frees us to use our resources for others. Contentment is not about abandoning ambition in God's kingdom, but rather refusing to be driven by material hunger. We work and earn as we must, but we do not worship at the altar of accumulation.

The promise stands: "Never will I leave you; never will I forsake you." With God's presence and provision assured, we can release our grip on material security.

## Confidence Despite Persecution

> So we say with confidence, "The Lord is my helper; I will not be afraid. What can mere mortals do to me?" (13:6)

This closing affirmation summarizes the entire passage. Persecution may come. Strangers may challenge us. Marriage and purity will be tested. Money will tempt. Yet we have the assurance of God's unfailing presence. We are not to be spoiled children who reject the Father's gifts. We are to persevere despite hardship, to listen to God's instruction, to demonstrate the grit and faithfulness that mark a true disciple.

Discipline—the root of discipleship—requires self-control, generosity, compassion, and service. These virtues demand tremendous willpower when confronted by the world's distractions. Our generation often lacks this grit. We abandon commitments, fail to finish what we start, post our absences on social media rather than honoring our word. We break promises to be present and on time, calling this freedom rather than recognizing it as a failure to love.

Yet Hebrews calls us back to perseverance. The story of Abebe Bikila, the 1968 Olympic marathoner who finished with a bleeding, dislocated knee, reminds us: his country did not send him 5,000 miles to start the race, but to finish it. No one remembers the gold medalist. We honor Bikila because he finished in the most difficult circumstances.

We too are called to finish the race—to persevere in love, to welcome the stranger, to honor marriage, to free ourselves from materialism, and to face whatever comes with the confidence that our God will never leave us nor forsake us.
