# Luke 15:11-24

**Title:** Lost and Found (Easter)

**Date:** 26 February 2017

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## The Setup: A Father's Loss

When we think about what we lose, it ranges from the small—keys left behind—to the deeply painful. But imagine losing a child. That's the situation Jesus sets before us in this parable.

> A man had two sons. The younger said to his father, "Father, give me my share of the estate." (Luke 15:11-12)

What's remarkable about this request isn't just its boldness. In asking for his inheritance while the father still lives, the son is essentially saying: "I wish you were dead." In Jewish culture, inheritances come only upon a parent's death. By demanding it now, he's rejecting his father's authority and his relationship entirely.

Yet the father doesn't respond with the judgment that was legally his right. According to Deuteronomy, a rebellious son could be brought before the elders and even stoned. Instead, the father divides his property.

Here's the deeper tragedy: when the text says he "divided his property," the Greek literally means he "divided his life." This wasn't just money—it was 50 years of work, toil, and livelihood. The father liquidated his life's accumulation in a firesale. Half his business model was gone. His means of supporting his wife, his other son and his family—all diminished.

But most devastating: the son who was his life, whom he'd given life to and invested everything in, was torn away from the family.

**The Application:** This is what we do to God. We are unhappy with the lives He's given us. We're tired of His laws, His boundaries. We tell Him to leave us alone: "Go to Hell, God." As Nietzsche said, "God is dead and we have killed him."

After the Enlightenment, Western society decided to be governed by physical laws and human reason rather than by God. We thought we could be morally upright without Him. But when we distance ourselves from our Creator, we lose everything that sustains us.

---

## The Son's Descent

Not long after receiving his inheritance, the younger son gathers everything and departs for a distant country—far enough that no one knows him, where his past won't follow him, and where he can be completely free from his father's eyes and expectations.

> There he squandered his wealth in wild living. (Luke 15:13)

He lives recklessly and self-indulgently. His older brother later reveals the fuller picture: the son spent his inheritance "with prostitutes" (Luke 15:30).

Then reality strikes:

> After he had spent everything, there was a severe famine in that whole country, and he began to be in need. (Luke 15:14)

No savings. No safety net. And now the economy collapses across the whole region—he cannot escape it.

> So he went and hired himself out to a citizen of that country, who sent him to his fields to feed pigs. He longed to fill his stomach with the pods that the pigs were eating, but no one gave him anything. (Luke 15:15-16)

When you envy what pigs eat, you've hit bottom. For a Jewish boy, this is the ultimate degradation. Pigs were unclean animals. He was starving in the filth, doing the worst job imaginable for the worst wages—and it still wasn't enough.

His trajectory is stunning:
- **Riches to rags**
- **Wealth to filth**
- **Wine to swine**
- **Penthouse to outhouse**

The further he goes from his father, the worse he becomes. Separated from the source of his provision, relationships, and dignity, he spirals into poverty, sickness, and despair.

**The Application:** As our society abandons God, we see this same trajectory. It may not look like poverty in a wealthy country—we have money to insulate ourselves from consequences. But look at the margins of society where people cannot buy their way out of trouble. Look at the mental health crisis in a generation raised without moral foundations. Our fertility rates suggest we won't even sustain a next generation.

The message is clear: when we distance ourselves from God, we become poorer, sicker, lonelier.

---

## Coming Home: The Father's Grace

But then something shifts. In a moment of clarity, the son comes to his senses.

> When he came to his senses, he said, "How many of my father's hired servants have food to spare, and here I am starving to death!" (Luke 15:17)

He realizes something profound: even servants in his father's house live better than he does as a "free" man. He would rather be a servant in his father's employ than continue as he is. As the Psalmist says:

> Better is one day in your courts than a thousand elsewhere; I would rather be a doorkeeper in the house of my God than dwell in the tents of the wicked. (Psalm 84:10)

It's better to suffer under God than have all the success of this world outside of Him.

So he rehearses his confession:

> I will set out and go back to my father and say to him: "Father, I have sinned against heaven and against you. I am no longer worthy to be called your son; make me like one of your hired servants." (Luke 15:18-19)

True humility. True repentance. He doesn't expect mercy—he expects judgment. He's prepared to accept servanthood.

He rises and travels back toward home.

> While he was still a long way off, his father saw him and was filled with compassion for him; he ran to his son, threw his arms around him and kissed him. (Luke 15:20)

The father has been watching. Every day, scanning the horizon. When he sees his son—still distant, still approaching—he doesn't wait. He runs. The father bridges the distance. Instead of raising his hand in anger, he throws his arms around him. Instead of harsh words, he kisses him.

The son begins his confession: "Father, I have sinned against heaven and against you. I am no longer worthy—"

But the father cuts him off. He won't hear the rest. He knows exactly what status his son should have.

> Bring the best robe and put it on him. Put a ring on his finger and sandals on his feet. (Luke 15:22)

These aren't the clothes of a servant. The robe signifies status in the household. The ring is the family signet ring—a symbol of authority and belonging. Sandals are what free members of the household wear; servants go barefoot. The father is saying: "No. I won't receive you back as a servant. You are my son, and you will be received as a son."

> Bring the fattened calf and kill it. Let's have a feast and celebrate. For this son of mine was dead and is alive again; he was lost and is found. (Luke 15:23-24)

The fattened calf is reserved for special occasions—this feast is large enough for the whole village. The father wants everyone to know: my son who was lost has been found. My son who was dead is alive again.

---

## The Deeper Truth

At Easter, we celebrate the death and resurrection of Jesus. God, our Father, lost His own Son for our sakes. On Good Friday, that Son died bearing the sins of the world. He experienced separation from the Father so we wouldn't have to.

But on Easter Sunday, the Son is resurrected. He is made alive.

So we who are dead in our sins can be made alive in Christ. We can come home. We can be received not as servants earning our way back, but as sons and daughters, fully restored to our Father's house.

> Father, I have sinned against heaven and against you. I am no longer worthy to be called your son. But in Jesus' name, make me like one of your hired servants—no, receive me as your son. In Jesus' name, Amen.
