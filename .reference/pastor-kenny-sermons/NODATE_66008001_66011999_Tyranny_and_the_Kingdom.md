# Revelation 8-11
**Title:** Tyranny and the Kingdom
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Understanding Revelation's Structure

### Parallels Between Sections 6-7 and 8-11

The structure of Revelation 8-11 mirrors the pattern established in chapters 6-7. Both sections present judgments followed by interlude passages that provide comfort and context. This parallel structure helps us understand how John is revealing the unfolding of God's purposes through repeating cycles, each time bringing us deeper into the mystery of how God's kingdom advances amid earthly chaos.

## The Prayer That Opens Heaven

### The Significance of the Incense (8:1-5)

The opening vision shows silence in heaven—a pause before the next movement. This silence itself communicates reverence and anticipation. What happens next teaches us about the power of prayer: the prayers of the saints are gathered before God's throne, mixed with incense, and then sent back to earth where they manifest as divine action.

This teaches us that prayer is not a one-way conversation. Our prayers matter eternally. They reach heaven, are treasured by God, and return to accomplish His purposes on earth. When we pray, we are participating in the unfolding of God's kingdom.

## The Trumpets: God's Escalating Call to Repentance

### The First Four Trumpets (8:6-12)

The first four trumpets strike the natural world—earth, sea, fresh water, and heavens. These judgments are linked thematically: each represents a partial destruction (one-third) that affects creation itself. They are not the most severe judgments, and God's mercy is evident in their limitation. A third of creation remains, suggesting opportunity for repentance.

### The Woe (8:13)

A heavenly voice cries out a woe—not to the saints or believers, but to the inhabitants of the earth. This directly echoes language from 5:10, reminding us that God's kingdom encompasses all the earth, and these judgments are meant to awaken earthly dwellers to their need for God.

### The Fifth Trumpet: A New Kind of Horror (9:1-11)

Here the nature of judgment changes. Instead of creation being struck, a being—a fallen star, a key to the abyss—opens demonic forces. The inhabitants of earth are harmed by creatures from the abyss, scorpion-like beings with a king over them named Abaddon (Destruction in Hebrew).

This teaches us that the deepest horrors come not from natural disaster but from spiritual opposition. Yet notice: these demons are limited. They cannot kill—they can only torment for five months. Even in judgment, God's mercy constrains the enemy's power.

### The Heavenly Altar and Prayer (9:13)

Returning to the altar before God's throne—the same location where our prayers were offered in 8:1-5—the sixth trumpet sounds. This connection is crucial: our prayers and God's judgments are inseparably linked. God responds to the intercession of His people.

## God's Mercy in Judgment

Throughout chapters 8 and 9, God's mercy appears in multiple forms:

- **Partial judgment**: One-third is affected; two-thirds remains
- **Limited duration**: The torment lasts only five months
- **Limited scope**: The demons cannot kill the sealed servants of God
- **Escalating warnings**: Each trumpet is an invitation to turn back to God

The judgments are meant not to destroy but to awaken. They are merciful in their purpose—to call people to repentance before final judgment comes.

## The Hardness of Human Hearts (9:20-21)

Yet what's most sobering is what follows: even after these disasters, people still refuse to repent. They cling to idolatry, sorcery, theft, and murder. This raises a difficult question: **What do these people need beyond disasters to move them toward repentance?**

They need spiritual awakening. They need to encounter God's truth. They need witnesses who will proclaim the Gospel with power and authority—which brings us to the interlude of chapter 10.

## The Angel and the Mystery of God (Chapter 10)

An angel descends with a little scroll, proclaiming that the mystery of God will be fulfilled—not delayed further. This echoes Daniel 12:6-9, where Daniel asks how long before these things are fulfilled, and the answer is that the knowledge will be sealed until the time of the end.

John is told to eat the scroll and prophesy again to many peoples, nations, tongues, and kings. The message is this: God's redemptive plan continues. Though judgment comes, God's purposes will not be thwarted, and His word will advance.

## The Two Witnesses (Chapter 11)

### The Holy City and Its Division

The "holy city" mentioned in verse 8 is Jerusalem, but not merely geographical Jerusalem. It is spiritual Jerusalem—the place where God's people dwell. John describes it with two dimensions:

**The outer court**: Given to the nations—representing the world's dominion and earthly opposition
**The inner sanctuary**: Reserved for God's witnesses—representing the spiritual reality that God's kingdom cannot be ultimately conquered

### The Prophets' Ministry

Two prophets minister for 1,260 days—three and a half years, half the final week of Daniel 9. They are clothed in sackcloth, a sign of mourning and repentance. They speak God's word with power: fire proceeds from their mouth, consuming their enemies. They control the weather, withhold rain, turn water to blood.

These prophets recall Elijah and Moses—the Law and the Prophets. Together they represent God's complete revelation, His covenant with His people, and His uncompromising truth.

### Why Sodom and Egypt?

The city is called "Sodom and Egypt" not because of its geography but because of its spiritual character. Sodom represents sexual immorality and rejection of God's messengers. Egypt represents slavery and opposition to God's liberation. The "holy city" has become a place where God's witnesses are killed, where sin reigns.

### The Death and Resurrection of the Witnesses

The beast from the abyss kills the two witnesses. Their bodies lie unburied in the street—ultimate shame and rejection. But then, after three and a half days, God raises them to life, and they are called up into heaven. The earth trembles, and a tenth of the city falls.

This is not defeat—it is vindication. The witnesses' resurrection proves that God's word cannot be killed. Their ascension shows that those who remain faithful will be exalted.

## The Seventh Trumpet and the Final Woe (11:15)

The seventh trumpet sounds, and voices declare: "The kingdom of the world has become the kingdom of our Lord and of His Christ, and He will reign forever and ever."

This is different from 5:10, where the 24 elders sing that we have been made a kingdom and priests. Here, the kingdom is not merely about believers—it's about the cosmos itself being reclaimed by Christ. All dominion, all authority, all rule comes under His sovereignty.

### The Thanks of the Twenty-Four Elders

The 24 elders—representing God's people in both Old and New Covenants—give thanks because judgment is finished and God's reign is established. They recognize that what seemed like chaos and opposition has always been moving toward this moment.

This should comfort us: our God is not surprised by evil. He is not waiting to see what will happen. He has established the end from the beginning. Evil will not have the final word.

## Living in Light of These Truths

We are living between the sixth and seventh trumpets. We are living in the time of God's escalating mercies and calls to repentance. We see disaster, opposition, and spiritual warfare all around us. But we also see—if we have eyes to see—that God is orchestrating history toward His kingdom's full manifestation.

### Like the Two Prophets

We, too, are called to be witnesses. Like the two prophets, we proclaim God's word in an increasingly hostile world. We stand before a "Sodom and Egypt"—a culture increasingly opposed to God's truth. We speak with authority not our own but God's. We face rejection and opposition.

But like the two prophets, we are not ultimately defeated. We eat the scroll—we ingest God's word until it becomes part of us. And we are called to testify to the reality of God's kingdom, knowing that our witness cannot ultimately be silenced.

### A Call to Faithful Living

In light of what we have studied, how should we live? We should live as those who know the end of the story. We should live with urgency—knowing that this age is not infinite. We should live with courage—knowing that our God has already won. We should live as witnesses—speaking God's truth with conviction and compassion.

We should live prayerfully, remembering that our prayers rise before God's throne and return to accomplish His purposes. We should live with expectation—not afraid of what comes before the seventh trumpet, but confident in what the seventh trumpet will bring.
