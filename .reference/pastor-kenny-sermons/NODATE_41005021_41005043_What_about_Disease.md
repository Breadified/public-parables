# Mark 5:21-43

**Title:** What about Disease?  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction

Disease is intrinsically wrong. A doctor once told me about tapeworms—parasites three meters long that live in the bowel, eating the food their victims consume. If the person stops eating, the tapeworm moves around desperately searching for more food. There have even been cases of tapeworms emerging from a person's mouth in their search for nourishment.

But this passage about Jesus healing a woman's disease points to something far deeper than physical healing. It reveals Jesus healing Israel itself of the disease of sin.

## The National Jewish Disease

### The Symbolism of Twelve

The first clue is the repeated use of the number twelve:

> And a woman was there who had been subject to bleeding for twelve years. (Mark 5:25)

Later we discover:

> She was twelve years old. (Mark 5:42)

The number twelve appears twice, which might seem merely to emphasize a long period of suffering. But in Scripture, twelve is the symbol of God's people Israel—the twelve tribes. In Revelation, we see 144,000 (12 × 12) representing God's people. Jesus chose twelve disciples as the new people of God. Mark uses twelve here to symbolize the Jews themselves.

### The Imagery of Daughter

Notice that Jesus calls the woman "Daughter" (Mark 5:34), and we also learn that Jairus's daughter is twelve years old. In the Old Testament, "Daughter" is used as a term for the nation of Israel—the "Daughter of Zion"—appearing throughout Psalms, Isaiah, and Lamentations.

These clues lead me to conclude that both the woman and the girl represent the Jewish nation.

### Israel's Disease: Separation from God

The woman's bleeding disease was spiritually significant. According to Leviticus, any discharge or bleeding rendered a person ceremonially unclean—separated from community, temple, and God. 

For someone bleeding continuously for twelve years, there was no recovery, no cleansing, no access to God. The disease was not merely physical suffering or social stigma. The worst part was complete separation from God—no temple, no atonement, no salvation.

This mirrors Israel's condition during the exile. In Lamentations, the prophet describes the Daughter of Zion:

> All the splendor has departed from the Daughter of Zion... Jerusalem has sinned greatly and so has become unclean. (Lamentations 1:6-8)

Like the woman, the daughter of Zion was afflicted and unclean. Her filthiness came from sin—the sin that separated Israel from her God, bringing His anger and leaving her without comfort.

## The True Doctor

### The Inadequate Cure

When sick, we naturally seek a doctor. But something strange happens in this story:

> She had suffered a great deal under the care of many doctors and had spent all she had, yet instead of getting better she grew worse. (Mark 5:26)

The woman had exhausted her resources seeking help, only to become worse off physically and financially.

### The False Doctors of Israel

If the woman represents Israel, who are the doctors? I believe they symbolize the national religious leaders—the Pharisees, scribes, and priests who offered salvation through the law. Yet they only brought condemnation. 

To combat sin, they multiplied laws and regulations. But this made things worse, because now people broke even more laws. Rules about Sabbath observance, rules about what constituted uncleanness—endless regulations that only increased guilt. Like parasitic worms, these leaders devoured their victims, taking their money at the temple while offering nothing of value in return.

The religious leaders were like treating gangrene by wrapping the blackened toes in bandages while ignoring the real problem—the bad blood supply in the leg. Eventually, every toe falls off. The Pharisees treated only the symptoms, not the disease itself.

### Jesus: The True Physician

But the woman heard of another doctor—one who said:

> It is not the healthy who need a doctor, but the sick. I have not come to call the righteous, but sinners. (Mark 2:17)

This man came for tax collectors, prostitutes, and sinners. This man could heal the sick and lowly. His name was Jesus.

Having nothing to lose, the woman took a risk:

> When she heard about Jesus, she came up behind him in the crowd and touched his cloak, because she thought, "If I just touch his clothes, I will be healed." Immediately her bleeding stopped and she felt in her body that she was freed from her suffering. (Mark 5:27-29)

Notice the language: the word translated "healed" in English is actually the Greek word for "saved." Mark is giving us a double meaning. Jesus does not merely heal her disease—He saves her. Like the paralytic earlier in Mark's Gospel, the stopping of her bleeding means her sins are forgiven.

Jesus confirms this:

> "Daughter, your faith has saved you. Go in peace and be freed from your suffering." (Mark 5:34)

Jesus is the true doctor because He addresses not just symptoms but the root cause. He does not merely restore physical health; He forgives sin. To accomplish this, He would give His life:

> "This is my blood of the covenant, which is poured out for many." (Mark 14:24)

In order to stop the bleeding of the woman, Jesus Himself would bleed.

## Jesus's Search for True Believers

### The Crowd and the One Lost Sheep

Something remarkable happens after the healing:

> At once Jesus realized that power had gone out from him. He turned around in the crowd and asked, "Who touched my clothes?" You see the people crowding against you," his disciples answered, "and yet you can ask, 'Who touched me?'" But Jesus kept looking around to see who had done it. (Mark 5:30-32)

The disciples' response captures the perspective of the age: "Look at all these people! We've gathered crowds. We have a movement. Everyone is touching you!" 

But Jesus is not content with the crowd. He's scanning for one person—not in anger, but in recognition. He's looking for that faithful one. The remnant of Israel. The one who isn't simply following the mob but who genuinely trusts in Him.

Jesus didn't come for crowds. He came to preach repentance and share good news (Mark 1:38). He had been calling for repentance and belief everywhere. And finally, here was someone who actually believed Him.

When power went out from Him and a miracle occurred, it revealed that despite everything—the crowds, the lack of faith, the opposition—there were still Jews who believed. His mission wasn't a complete waste. There was a remnant. 

The woman is the true Daughter of Zion—the faithful one among the unfaithful. She knew she was sick. She admitted it. She knew she needed help and went to the only one who could truly help her.

### A Lesson on Healing and Health

I once refused to address a back injury for a month, thinking it would heal on its own. Only when I admitted I needed help and saw a physiotherapist did I begin to recover. The woman understood what many don't: you must first admit the problem before healing can come. She faced the truth about her condition, and that's when restoration became possible.

## Salvation for Jews, Then Gentiles

This passage represents Jesus's final concentrated attempt to call the Jewish nation before extending His mercy to the Gentiles. Later in Mark (chapters 6-7), Jesus encounters the Syro-Phoenician woman and begins reaching beyond Israel.

But what does this mean for us? We are part of that larger story. Out of the great crowd—Sydney, Australia, the world—Jesus calls each one of us to follow Him. His kingdom is near. Repent and believe!

We are all afflicted with the same disease: rebellion and sin. It's not a national Jewish disease but an international one. No matter what race or nation you're from, you're involved in rebellion against God. The symptoms may look like selfishness, anger, lust, and disobedience. Or you may appear respectable and polite. But the disease is there—you don't need visible spots to be diseased.

We need to do what the woman did: admit the problem, realize that the real issue isn't the toes but the leg, and go to the true Doctor. Beg for mercy. Tell the whole truth to God. And He will save you—whether you're rich or poor, well or sick.

## A Word on Suffering

This passage is not about faith guaranteeing physical health. Nor does it mean that if you're sick, God doesn't care for you. Sickness and faith are most often unrelated. If you're ill, see a doctor. We'll pray for healing and for doctors to do their work well. Continue trusting in Jesus, and take comfort that He loves you, that He Himself underwent suffering, and that there is hope—a day when all suffering will end in heaven.

## Conclusion: The Remnant of Faith

Mark 5 captures Jesus's last attempts at calling the Jewish race before His mission extends to the Gentiles. A woman with twelve years of bleeding. A twelve-year-old girl. The number twelve again appears immediately after, when Jesus sends His twelve disciples into the villages with urgent proclamation: "The kingdom of God is near. Repent!"

They managed to gather five thousand—sheep without shepherds, hungry for bread and life. Jesus fed them both. Soon after, He declared all foods clean, ending the ceremonial law and its separation. And His mission to the Gentiles—another people deemed unacceptable inside the temple—begins.

The Jewish nation was lost in sin, hypocrisy, and legalism. But out of national Israel, there were some saved—the remnant. Some true daughters of Zion. The five thousand hungry Jews. A synagogue ruler. The bleeding lady.

Perhaps you too.
