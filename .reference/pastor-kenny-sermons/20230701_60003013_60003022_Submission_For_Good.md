## Do Not Fear to Suffer for What Is Right

### The Challenge: Daniel Penny's Dilemma

Earlier this year in New York, a man named Daniel Penny—an ex-marine—was on a subway when a homeless man became aggressive and threatening. Acting to protect other passengers, Penny intervened and the man died. For doing good and protecting others, Penny now faces 15 years in prison.

The question Peter addresses is both personal and urgent: If you had the chance to do it again—knowing the consequences—would you? This is the tension of the Christian life.

### Because God's Eyes Are on the Righteous

> Who is going to harm you if you are eager to do good? (1 Peter 3:13)

Peter begins with a paradox. On one level, the answer is simple: doing good should bring reward, not punishment. Yet we live in a broken world where values are inverted. What is good becomes labeled evil. Jesus himself was accused of demonic power for performing miracles.

When suffering comes despite our righteous actions, Peter offers three reasons not to fear:

#### 1. God Sees and Cares

> For the eyes of the Lord are on the righteous and his ears are attentive to their prayer, but the face of the Lord is against those who do evil. (1 Peter 3:12)

God is watching. He knows what you're doing. He hears your prayers. No harm can come to you unless God permits it for your ultimate good. This is not blind faith—it's trust in a God who has demonstrated His love through Christ.

#### 2. Maintain a Clear Conscience

> But even if you should suffer for what is right, you are blessed. Do not fear their threats; do not be frightened. But in your hearts revere Christ as Lord. Always be prepared to give an answer to everyone who asks you to give the reason for the hope that you have. But do this with gentleness and respect, keeping a clear conscience, so that those who speak maliciously against your good behavior in Christ may be ashamed of their slander. (1 Peter 3:14-16)

Peter calls us to three things:
- **Set Christ as Lord in your hearts.** Make Him your highest authority, not the fear of others.
- **Be ready to give a reason for your hope.** Live with such visible faith that people notice and ask about it.
- **Keep a clear conscience.** When you know you've done right, no accusation can shake you.

#### 3. Understand God's Will Includes Suffering for Good

> For it is better, if it is God's will, to suffer for doing good than for doing evil. (1 Peter 3:17)

Here is a profound truth: all suffering falls under God's sovereign will. If we must suffer, better to suffer for righteousness than for wrongdoing. We don't know why some suffering comes—disease, accident, tragedy. But if we must suffer, let it be because we did what was right.

### Jesus Is Our Model

> For Christ also suffered once for sins, the righteous for the unrighteous, to bring you to God. (1 Peter 3:18)

Jesus embodied this teaching perfectly. In the Garden, He prayed that the cup of suffering would pass from Him. Yet He submitted: "Not my will, but yours be done." He suffered—not for His own sins, which He had none—but for ours. The righteous died for the unrighteous.

This wasn't suffering for doing evil. It wasn't random tragedy. **It was suffering for doing good.** He lived in perfect harmony, loved His enemies, showed compassion to the marginalized, remained humble, answered insults with silence, and gave up His rights, His body, and any hope of earthly recognition.

What did He receive? Ridicule, betrayal, torture, death.

Yet by breaking the cycle of hate and revenge through His sacrifice, Jesus demonstrated that there is a way forward. His silent submission to unjust suffering answers the world's violence with redemptive love. This is how He earned the authority to say: "Do not fear."

## Fear Christ, Not the World

### The Shift from Fear of Others to Fear of God

> But in your hearts revere Christ as Lord. (1 Peter 3:15)

The Greek concept of "fear" includes reverence and awe. Throughout 1 Peter, this theme repeats:

- "Live out your time as foreigners here in reverent fear" (1:17)
- "Show proper respect to everyone... fear God" (2:17)
- "Slaves, in reverent fear of God submit yourselves to your masters" (2:18)
- "Wives, submit with the reverence and fear of your lives" (3:2)

Peter connects this to Isaiah's prophecy:

> The Lord Almighty is the one you are to regard as holy, he is the one you are to fear, he is the one you are to dread. (Isaiah 8:13)

Fear God. Not government officials, not angry mobs, not lawsuits, not loss of reputation. When your reverence belongs to Christ alone, you become free from the paralyzing fear of human judgment.

### God Looks After His Own

> He was put to death in the body but made alive in the Spirit. (1 Peter 3:18)

Jesus died, but God didn't abandon Him. He was made alive in the Spirit—resurrection vindication. What seemed like complete defeat became the foundation of our salvation.

#### God Cared for Believers in the Past

Peter then makes a startling claim about verses 19-20, which have puzzled interpreters for centuries. Rather than Jesus preaching a second chance in the afterlife—which contradicts Hebrews 9:27—Peter likely refers to Christ's spirit working through Old Testament preachers to call people to repentance. Isaiah 61:1 describes this explicitly:

> The Spirit of the Lord God is upon me, because the Lord has anointed me to bring good tidings to the afflicted... to proclaim liberty to the captives and the opening of the prison to those who are bound. (Isaiah 61:1)

The point is profound: believers from Noah's time, from the time of exile, from every generation—none of them were abandoned by God. Christ's redemption reaches backward and forward through all history.

> In the days of Noah while the ark was being built... only a few people, eight in all, were saved through water. (1 Peter 3:20)

Noah was alone. He preached righteousness for 120 years while building the ark. Everyone mocked him. Yet he persisted because he feared God more than man's ridicule. And God saved him.

If God cared for Noah, for the exiles, for believers throughout history—**will He not care for us?**

#### Baptism Symbolizes This Salvation

> And this water symbolizes baptism that now saves you also—not the removal of dirt from the body but the pledge of a good conscience toward God. It saves you by the resurrection of Jesus Christ, who has gone into heaven and is at God's right hand—with angels, authorities and powers in submission to him. (1 Peter 3:21-22)

Baptism isn't magic water. It's a pledge—your promise to God and God's promise to you. You're saying: "I belong to Christ. I'll follow Him even when it's hard." And God says: "I've got you. I'm with you. I've already won the victory through My resurrection."

Notice the final image: Christ at God's right hand, with all cosmic authorities in submission to Him. The powers that seem so threatening to us—governments, mobs, opposition—are ultimately under His authority. Our submission to Him places us under His protection.

## Practical Application

### Where We Struggle to Submit to God's Will

#### Fear of the Unknown Future

We don't know what's coming. This paralyzes us from doing good because we can't guarantee safe outcomes.

#### Fear of Government Authority

In many contexts, Christians face restrictions on religious freedom, church gatherings, education of children. The instinct is to resist, to fight back. But Peter says: **Keep doing good.** They may block many doors, but they cannot block them all.

- You control what enters your home. Read Scripture with your children.
- Your church still stands. Invest in it.
- Volunteer to teach Scripture classes. Set an example through godly living.

#### Fear in Marriage

Some wives face rough, difficult marriages. When a husband is harsh or even abusive, the temptation is either to retaliate or to give up on the marriage entirely. Peter's counsel is nuanced:

- **Do not fear submission.** But understand: they cannot force you to do wrong. Keep doing right.
- **Get help.** Seek counsel from trusted friends, counselors, or authorities if abuse is criminal.
- **Gently witness through your life.** Sometimes a husband is won not by words but by seeing his wife's godliness, purity, and gentle strength.

Research actually confirms what Peter teaches: regular religious attendance correlates strongly with lower rates of domestic abuse. The gospel genuinely transforms people.

### Real-World Costs of Doing Good

#### The Fear of Being Harmed

In California, a man known as "Brother John" was shot and killed while delivering food to homeless people. His pregnant wife was injured. His daughter was shot in the arm. He suffered for doing good.

#### The Fear of Legal Consequences

In China, a two-year-old girl was hit by a van and lay bleeding in the street for seven minutes. A dozen people passed by. Motorcycles swerved to avoid her. A truck ran over her a second time. Finally, a woman stopped to help, but the girl died from brain injuries. Why did no one help? Because in China, good Samaritans have been sued and forced to pay hefty fines to those they tried to help. Fear stopped them.

#### The Fear of Ridicule

On a school bus, a young Kenny noticed another student wasn't getting off at his regular stop. Concerned, he alerted the driver. It turned out the boy was skipping school intentionally. Kenny got no thanks, was scolded by the driver, and faced ridicule from classmates. The natural response: "Why do good anymore? It costs and brings no reward."

### The Antidote: A Different Kind of Fear

Nelson Mandela said: "I learned that courage was not the absence of fear. But the triumph over it. The brave man is not he who does not feel afraid, but he who conquers that fear."

And Daniel Penny, facing years in prison for trying to help, was asked if he'd do it again. He said: "I live an authentic and genuine life. If there was a threat and danger in the present, I would do it again."

This is what it means to fear Christ instead of man. You're not fearless. You're afraid—but your fear of God is larger than your fear of consequences. So you do good anyway.

> Do not fear what they fear; do not be frightened. But in your hearts revere Christ as Lord. (1 Peter 3:14-15)

Your submission today isn't to the governments, the mobs, the lawsuits, the ridicule. You have a greater Master. Angels, authorities, and powers are in submission to Him. So are you. And He will look after you, just as He looked after Noah, just as He looked after the exiles, just as He rose Jesus from the dead.

This is the Christian's confidence: not in perfect safety, but in the certainty that God sees, God cares, God wins. And that is enough.
