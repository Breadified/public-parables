## First World Problems and Entitlement

We live in an age of abundance, yet we've become expert at complaining. Years ago, the term "First World Problems" emerged to describe the peculiar complaints of those living in prosperity: forgotten earphones, too many seeds in a watermelon, having to run outside because the treadmill is broken, too much food on one's plate, the absence of decaf soy lattes.

The issue isn't the problems themselves—it's that we take for granted what is luxury for the majority of the world. We feel *entitled* to what we actually have no right to expect. This entitlement blinds us to how genuinely blessed we are.

As we continue our series on "Share the Benefit"—learning to care for our neighbors as Jesus taught through the parable of the Good Samaritan and the story of Zaccheus—we discover that one of the greatest obstacles to generosity is this sense of entitlement. When we believe we deserve what we have, we cannot truly be generous with it.

---

## Part 1: God Does Not Owe Us

### The Parable of the Servant

> **Luke 17:7-10**  
> "Suppose one of you had a servant plowing or looking after the sheep. Would he say to the servant when he comes in from the field, 'Come along now and sit down to eat'? Would he not rather say, 'Prepare my supper, get yourself ready and wait on me while I eat and drink; after that you may eat and drink'? Would he thank the servant because he did what he was told to do? So you also, when you have done everything you were told to do, should say, 'We are unworthy servants; we have only done our duty.'"

Jesus tells of a master and servant. After a brutally hard day's work—plowing fields, turning over soil to bring nutrients to the surface while burying weeds, or shepherding sheep across miles of terrain, defending them, feeding them, occasionally fighting off wolves and lions—the servant comes home exhausted. Sweat-soaked, muscles aching, blisters bleeding, he naturally expects to sit down and rest. He feels *entitled* to his reward.

But the master has other expectations. The servant's work isn't finished. The master hasn't eaten yet. So the servant must prepare the meal and continue serving. Only after this is complete may he finally rest and eat.

The crucial point: this is not extraordinary service deserving thanks. The servant is not doing the master a favor—he's simply doing his duty. There is no gratitude required because this is exactly what a servant is supposed to do.

We struggle to see this in our culture. We thank people for everything, even ordinary transactions. But imagine handing an assignment to your professor and expecting them to thank you profusely for doing what was required. Of course they wouldn't. You simply fulfilled your obligation.

### Our Relationship with God

The comparison invites us to reflect on our relationship with God. We tend to treat God as though He owes us something.

Consider what God has actually given us:

> **Ephesians 2:4-7**  
> "But because of his great love for us, God, who is rich in mercy, made us alive with Christ even when we were dead in transgressions—it is by grace you have been saved. And God raised us up with Christ and seated us with him in the heavenly realms in Christ Jesus, in order that in the coming ages he might show the incomparable riches of his grace, expressed in his kindness to us in Christ Jesus."

We were dead in sin. We could not raise ourselves from spiritual death—just as no corpse can resuscitate itself. Yet Jesus, out of love, mercy, and grace, did what we could never do. He gave us infinite worth because He paid the infinite price of His own life for us.

How, then, can we feel entitled to anything God gives us? We are unworthy servants who have merely received our duty—to respond to this gracious God by living generously toward others.

## Part 2: Rather, We Owe God

Because we have received such mercy, we now owe it to God to be merciful and generous to others. This generosity takes two forms: material and spiritual.

### Material Generosity and the Warning Against Entitlement

> **Luke 16:19-26**  
> "There was a rich man who was dressed in purple and fine linen and lived in luxury every day. At his gate was laid a beggar named Lazarus, covered with sores and longing to eat what fell from the rich man's table. Even the dogs came and licked his sores. The time came when the beggar died and the angels carried him to Abraham's side. The rich man also died and was buried. In hell, where he was in torment, he looked up and saw Abraham far away, with Lazarus by his side. So he called to him, 'Father Abraham, have pity on me and send Lazarus to dip the tip of his finger in water and cool my tongue, because I am in agony in this fire.' But Abraham replied, 'Son, remember that in your lifetime you received your good things, while Lazarus received bad things, but now he is comforted here and you are in agony. And besides all this, between us and you a great chasm has been fixed, so that those who want to go from here to you cannot, nor can anyone cross over from there to us.'"

The rich man's sin was not that he was wealthy. His sin was that he had great material resources yet no generosity. While Lazarus lay at his gate in desperate need, the rich man enjoyed his luxury without sharing. The result was judgment and a complete reversal of fortunes.

We live in a world of immense need. Through television and media, we know of suffering everywhere. Yet often our response is limited to abstract causes—fighting poverty "over there," supporting distant justice movements, signing petitions for distant policy changes. While these have their place, we must ask: what about the people right in front of us?

True generosity begins locally. Start at home by completing your responsibilities and being a genuine friend to those nearby. Be attentive to the lonely person at church. Visit aging relatives. Help your neighbor with yard work. Look within your own community—perhaps there are classmates who need tutoring, nursing homes that need visitors, families struggling with finances who are too embarrassed to ask.

We often avoid local service because it seems boring compared to "big" causes. Yet the irony is striking: we protest government bullying while ignoring the bully at work. We fight to change refugee laws while refusing to invite the unusual classmate to our birthday party. We dream of building toilets in Africa but won't pick up a friend from nearby suburbs. Local, face-to-face generosity demands faithfulness over time—not a one-time donation that absolves us of responsibility.

The core issue with modern welfare systems is that impersonal help can create a sense of entitlement rather than gratitude. When a government or charity gives aid, recipients can forget that someone else's work funded it. Real generosity is person-to-person, face-to-face. It requires accountability and genuine relationship.

### Spiritual Generosity and the Warning Against Withholding the Gospel

But material generosity, while important, is not the greatest need. There is a spiritual generosity we owe to others—the sharing of the gospel itself.

> **Luke 16:27-31**  
> "He answered, 'Then I beg you, father, send Lazarus to my father's house, for I have five brothers. Let him warn them, so that they will not also come to this place of torment.' Abraham replied, 'They have Moses and the Prophets; let them listen to them.' 'No, father Abraham,' he said, 'but if someone from the dead goes to them, they will repent.' He said to him, 'If they do not listen to Moses and the Prophets, they will not be convinced even if someone rises from the dead.'"

The rich man's concern for his brothers' spiritual fate comes too late. The warning they need will not come from someone rising from the dead, but from someone present now—someone who knows Jesus and can testify to the truth of Scripture.

This connects directly to forgiveness and the warning that opens this passage:

> **Luke 17:1-4**  
> "Jesus said to his disciples: 'Things that cause people to sin are bound to come, but woe to that person through whom they come. It would be better for him to be thrown into the sea with a millstone tied around his neck than for him to cause one of these little ones to sin. So watch yourselves. If your brother sins, rebuke him, and if he repents, forgive him. If he sins against you seven times in a day, and seven times comes back to you and says, "I repent," forgive him.'"

To cause one of these "little ones" to sin means to prevent others from accessing forgiveness and salvation. Just as the rich man refused to acknowledge Lazarus at his gate, we can refuse to offer the good news to those right in front of us—in our classrooms, workplaces, neighborhoods, and churches.

We have been forgiven much; therefore we must forgive much. When someone repents, we forgive—not seven times, but infinitely. And we must also be generous in offering the spiritual wealth we possess: the knowledge of Jesus Christ and the forgiveness available through Him.

### The Problem Is Not Lack of Faith

When Jesus calls us to this life of generosity, the disciples respond with anxiety:

> **Luke 17:5-6**  
> "The apostles said to the Lord, 'Increase our faith!' He replied, 'If you have faith as small as a mustard seed, you can say to this mulberry tree, "Be uprooted and planted in the sea," and it will obey you.'"

The disciples think their problem is insufficient faith. Jesus corrects them: the problem isn't faith at all. A mustard seed—one of the tiniest seeds—contains enough power to move mountains if faith is genuine. The issue isn't quantity of faith, but rather what *prevents* us from acting on the faith we have.

## Part 3: Being Thankful—The Real Problem

The real obstacle to generosity is this: **we have forgotten to be thankful**. We take our blessings for granted.

We take for granted the gospel that saved our souls. We take for granted the resources we possess. We take for granted the very grace of God that rescued us from spiritual death.

### The Ten Lepers: A Lesson in Gratitude

> **Luke 17:11-19**  
> "Now on his way to Jerusalem, Jesus traveled along the border between Samaria and Galilee. As he was going into a village, ten men who had leprosy met him. They stood at a distance and called out in a loud voice, 'Jesus, Master, have pity on us!' When he saw them, he said, 'Go, show yourselves to the priests.' And as they went, they were cleansed. One of them, when he saw he was healed, came back, praising God in a loud voice. He threw himself at Jesus' feet and thanked him—and he was a Samaritan. Jesus asked, 'Were not all ten cleansed? Where are the other nine? Was no one found to return and give praise to God except this foreigner?' Then he said to him, 'Rise and go; your faith has made you well.'"

Ten men with leprosy received the same miraculous healing. Yet only one returned to give thanks—and he was a Samaritan, an outsider. Nine lepers never looked back. Nine received the gift of restored health and simply walked away without acknowledgment, without gratitude.

This is our condition. We receive the greatest gift imaginable—salvation, purpose, eternal life—and often we forget to give thanks. We move on to the next thing, assuming we deserve what we've been given.

### Gratefulness as the Motivation for Generosity

Why are Christians generous? Not because we're trying to earn our way to heaven through works—there is no such path. Our generosity flows from **gratitude**. We give because we have been given so much.

This is the opposite of entitlement. When a parent works hard to buy their child a computer, that child doesn't trash it or hoard it selfishly. Instead, they use it wisely and share it because they understand the sacrifice made on their behalf. Likewise, when we truly grasp what God has done for us, we become generous not from obligation but from overflow of thankfulness.

We must remember: not long ago, we were lost. But we were found. Or if we grew up in Christian families, we must remember the odds against which the gospel came to us. We stood on the far side of that great chasm, desperately separated from God, with no way to bridge the gap ourselves. But God mercifully reached across and plucked us out. He gave us purpose. He gave us a reason to live.

To live now is to serve Jesus—the One who did more than His duty, who served us out of love.

## Last World Problems

We are a spoiled generation. We have created elaborate variations of "First World Problems" even as the world faces dire need:

- "I can't be generous because I need to buy another phone since the old one can't play the latest games."
- "I can't commit to a summer mission because I haven't booked my flights to Korea yet."
- "I can't commit to ministry—I need to holiday in France and then ski in Canada."
- "I don't have time; I only get four months off from university."
- "I'd have dinner with that lonely person, but I don't like the food they'd choose."
- "I can't bring my kids to Sunday school this week because they'd miss part of their friend's birthday party."

These are not merely "First World Problems." They are **Last World Problems**—the concerns of people who live as though this world is all there is, who act as though accumulating experiences and avoiding discomfort are the ultimate goods.

But we know better. We have been saved. We have been given grace. We have been called to share the benefit with others.

## The Unfinished Work

There is a danger, particularly for younger Christians, of thinking that we've "done our Christian bit" and can now rest. Some think: "I've packed up the chairs at church—I've done my part." Others believe: "I serve at church, so I don't need to think about evangelism at work." Still others complete one Christian event and assume they deserve a break from all responsibility for the rest of the year.

This thinking extends into later life. We might lead youth group, see it grow, lead Bible studies, and then declare it's "our year off from ministry." That year becomes another year, then another. We dedicate our first 25 years to church and ministry, then shift focus entirely to building careers, investment portfolios, and retirement accounts. We swing between workaholism and hedonism, never finding balance.

And then retirement comes. We envision ourselves playing golf, traveling extensively, finally getting the break we feel we deserve.

But consider John Chapman, a beloved Christian worker in Sydney who died in his eighties. He retired from his official role in Anglican evangelism some 15 years before his death. Yet his retirement was not a retirement at all. He remained an itinerant speaker throughout Australia right up to his final days, with speaking engagements still in his diary. He did not die playing golf. He did not die on holiday. He died serving the Lord—doing his duty until the moment he entered into rest.

This is the calling: to be generous, to serve faithfully, to do our duty—not as an option or an occasional act, but as a way of life. We are unworthy servants, and our privilege is to serve the God who saved us.

Let us have no more talk of entitlements. Let us have no more excuses. We have received much grace. We have a duty. Let us go do it together.
