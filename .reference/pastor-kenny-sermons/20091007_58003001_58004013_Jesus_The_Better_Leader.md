# Hebrews 3:1–4:13

**Title:** Jesus: The Better Leader
**Date:** October 7, 2009
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Introduction: The Promise of Peace

Throughout history, world leaders have promised peace. Neville Chamberlain declared he brought "peace for our time" from Nazi Germany. Barack Obama received the Nobel Peace Prize for his efforts toward nuclear nonproliferation and international diplomacy. Yet these promises often ring hollow.

The author of Hebrews calls us to a different kind of peace—one that comes through Jesus, who is the better leader. Over these chapters, we've seen that Jesus is better at bringing God's message to us and better at being human than we are. Now we discover that Jesus brings true peace through faithful leadership and the rest that only He can provide.

The author's argument unfolds in two movements: **(1) Jesus is more faithful than Moses,** and **(2) Jesus brings rest.**

---

## Part 1: Jesus Is More Faithful Than Moses (3:2–6)

### Moses: The Faithful Servant

> He was faithful to the one who appointed him, just as Moses was faithful in all God's house. (Hebrews 3:2)

Moses holds an extraordinary place in Jewish history. He led Israel out of Egypt—transforming a nomadic tribe of seventy people into a nation of roughly half a million. He received the Law at Sinai and authored the first five books of Scripture. When Aaron and Miriam challenged his leadership, God Himself defended Moses:

> But this is not true of my servant Moses; he is faithful in all my house. With him I speak face to face, clearly and not in riddles; he sees the form of the LORD. (Numbers 12:7–8)

Moses had immediate, intimate access to God's presence. He could enter the tent of meeting and speak with God directly. To rebel against Moses was to rebel against God Himself. In Jewish tradition, Moses was considered the greatest person who ever lived—supreme in his access to God and his faithfulness to God's calling.

The point is important: **The author is not saying Moses was unfaithful.** Moses is even held up as an example of faith later in Hebrews 11. Rather, the comparison is meant to show just how remarkable Jesus' faithfulness truly is.

### Jesus: The Faithful Son

> Jesus has been found worthy of greater honor than Moses, just as the builder of a house has greater honor than the house itself. (Hebrews 3:3)

The image shifts from house to builder. A builder is greater than the house he builds—he can construct multiple houses, improve his craft, and increase in value. The house, by contrast, depreciates over time. The builder's expertise compounds; the house's value declines.

**Jesus is the builder; Moses was part of the house.** This explains why Jesus deserves greater honor.

But there's a deeper difference between them:

> Moses was faithful as a servant in all God's house testifying to what would be said in the future. But Christ is faithful as a son over God's house. (Hebrews 3:5–6)

The contrast is between **servanthood and sonship**—a fundamental difference in relationship and responsibility.

A servant, no matter how faithful, has limited access and limited scope. A servant enters all parts of the house—even the parents' bedroom—but only to clean and serve. A son enters the same spaces for comfort, intimacy, and belonging. On Saturday morning, a son crawls into bed to cuddle with his parents. The servant works; the son dwells.

Similarly, Jesus has **greater access to the Father** than any created being. He is not merely a messenger of God's word but God Himself, the tabernacle of God on earth. All who meet Jesus meet God. And His relationship to the Father is not transactional service but intimate sonship.

Furthermore, Jesus bears **far greater responsibility**. The faithful servant completes his tasks and departs. The son, having grown to maturity, assumes permanent responsibility for the household—24/7 care, midnight emergencies, all the weight of stewardship. **Jesus is faithful over more.**

This faithfulness was tested in the most severe way imaginable. Moses once raised his hand in anger against an Israelite. Jesus raised both arms to be nailed to the cross. Pain could not deter Him from fulfilling His promises. Fear of death did not turn Him away. Loneliness and anguish did not shake His resolve. Jesus proved faithful in a far greater way than Moses—not through different kind of faithfulness, but through faithfulness tested and proved in suffering.

## Part 2: We Are Called to Be Faithful (3:6–14)

### The Call to Persevere

> And we are his house, if we hold on to our courage and the hope of which we boast. (Hebrews 3:6)

> We have come to share in Christ if we hold firmly till the end the confidence we had at first. (Hebrews 3:14)

Jesus' faithfulness creates a calling for us. We are now His household—His people. But this relationship depends on our faithfulness: we must hold on to our courage and hope, and hold firmly to our confidence until the end.

The author illustrates what unfaithfulness looks like through Israel's wilderness wandering.

### The Example of Israel's Failure

After leaving Egypt, Israel traveled toward Canaan. At the edge of the promised land, they sent scouts to survey the territory. But when the scouts returned with reports of powerful inhabitants, the people's faith collapsed:

> So, as the Holy Spirit says: "Today, if you hear his voice, do not harden your hearts as you did in the rebellion, during the time of testing in the desert, where your fathers tested and tried me and for forty years saw what I did." (Hebrews 3:7–9)

Israel's unfaithfulness was not a sudden lapse but the culmination of an entire journey filled with grumbling. From the moment they left Egypt, they complained:

- Not enough bread
- Not enough water
- Not enough meat
- "It was better in Egypt"
- Too many warriors in Canaan
- And finally, at the very threshold of rest: "We can't do this"

Each complaint represented a failure to trust the God who had already defeated Egypt's might.

What was the consequence?

> That is why I was angry with that generation, and I said, "Their hearts are always going astray, and they have not known my ways." So I declared on oath in my anger, "They shall never enter my rest." (Hebrews 3:10–11)

Their hearts hardened. They failed the test. God's judgment was severe: the entire generation that complained would not enter the promised land. Only Joshua and Caleb, who trusted God, would live to see it. The rest wandered in the wilderness for forty years—no rest, no home, no peace. All because of unbelief.

> For we also have had the gospel preached to us, just as they did; but the message they heard was of no value to them, because those who heard did not combine it with faith. (Hebrews 4:2)

The gospel was preached to Israel, but they did not embrace it with faith. The same warning applies to us.

### A Word of Encouragement

Let me be clear: I do not think you will be like the Israelites. God's love is unconditional. Those of us who have trusted in Christ are saved—guaranteed. We are brothers and sisters with Jesus. The conditional "if you hold on" indicates a *potential*, not an actual threat.

Think of it this way: If you jump from an airplane, you will die. That's true. But the solution is not to obsess about how you might jump or whether you'll survive the fall. The solution is simply: *Don't jump.* Enjoy the ride.

Similarly, don't obsess over how you might fall away. Instead, obsess over how you'll stay faithful. The trials we face are testings and discipline, just as they were for Israel. In hindsight, the Israelites should have persevered and trusted God. We must learn from their example and choose a different path.

### Encourage One Another Daily

> See to it, brothers, that none of you has a sinful, unbelieving heart that turns away from the living God. But encourage one another daily, as long as it is called Today, so that none of you may be hardened by sin's deceitfulness. (Hebrews 3:12–13)

Sin is deceiving. Most of us can lie to ourselves—we make decisions based on what we want and then rationalize them perfectly. But it's harder to lie to each other. This is why encouragement is not optional; it's essential to the Christian life.

The church exists, in part, for mutual accountability. We encourage one another through:

- **Praise and positivity** that remind us of God's character
- **Teaching** that shapes our thinking
- **Correction and challenge** that redirect us when we drift

When someone's reasoning doesn't ring true, we ask harder questions: "Are you sure that's a good idea? Have you thought about it this way?" We give courage to choose what's right, even when it's costly.

We also combat grumbling by sharing stories of others' suffering, giving us perspective on our own trials. We combat despair by sharing stories of joy and God's faithfulness. **It is through this mutual encouragement that Christians mature.**

This happens through intentional community:

- **Today:** Don't miss lunch. Use this moment to build up people through truth.
- **Daily:** Find fellow Christians tomorrow and every day this week. Wherever you are, seek out Christian community.
  - Those in Christian homes: Use family meals not just to pray for food, but to pray *for each other*.
  - High schoolers: Join a Christian ISCF group at school. It keeps you accountable where it's easiest to live a double life.
  - University students entering campus: Seek out Christian fellowship immediately.
  - Those at work: Ask coworkers about their faith. Look for City Bible Forums or Christian workplace groups.

Next week, commit to faithful church attendance—not out of tradition or habit, but for the sake of accountability. Those who resist accountability often church-hop, maintaining several memberships so no one community can truly know them. They live double lives, disappearing into the gaps. Don't fall into that trap.

## Part 3: Jesus Brings True Rest (4:1–11)

### The Promise of Rest Still Stands

> Therefore, since the promise of entering his rest still stands, let us be careful that none of you be found to have fallen short of it. (Hebrews 4:1)

This seems strange. Didn't Joshua conquer the promised land? Didn't David rule it? Didn't Israel enter their rest?

In one sense, yes. But in another sense, the promise remains unfulfilled:

> Now we who have believed will certainly enter that rest... It still remains that some will enter that rest. (Hebrews 4:3, 6)

The reason: **the promised land was never ultimately about geography.** It was about rest itself.

> And yet his work has been finished since the creation of the world. For somewhere he has spoken about the seventh day in these words: "And on the seventh day God rested from all his work." (Hebrews 4:3–4)

God's rest at creation points to an eschatological rest—a future, ultimate rest that awaits God's people. The land of Canaan was a *type* of that future rest, but not the thing itself.

### Not Fulfilled in Joshua, David, or the Past

Joshua conquered the land and distributed it to the tribes. Yet God continued to speak through David about "another day":

> Therefore God again set a certain day, calling it Today, when a long time later he spoke through David: "Today, if you hear his voice, do not harden your hearts." (Hebrews 4:6–7)

David himself pointed beyond his own reign to a future day of rest. This means:

> For if Joshua had given them rest, God would not have spoken later about another day. (Hebrews 4:8)

Joshua's conquest was not the final rest. David's reign was not the final rest. The land in the Middle East was never what God was truly talking about.

**The real rest is eschatological—it belongs to the future.** It awaits a better leader to bring us there, and that leader is Jesus alone.

### Make Every Effort to Enter That Rest

> There remains, then, a Sabbath-rest for the people of God; for anyone who enters God's rest also rests from his own work, just as God did from his. Let us, therefore, make every effort to enter that rest, so that no one will fall by following their example of disobedience. (Hebrews 4:9–11)

Being faithful in the present requires active effort. It's not passive waiting; it's intentional striving to remove obstacles to faithfulness:

**In relationships:** If your boyfriend or girlfriend is not helping you stay on course, get out. If they're not a Christian, look for genuine interest in faith before committing. If you're already married to a Christian, remember that marriage is lived daily. Eating together, rituals together, church together—these daily habits will either build you up or drag you away. Choose wisely.

**In studies and career:** Different courses and jobs demand different energies. If a path takes too much time and erodes your ability to stay faithful—not because you waste time on games, but because the demands are genuine—get out. But if attending church and Bible study helps you remain steadfast, make every effort to attend. If smaller informal Christian gatherings strengthen you, schedule them in. Set priorities according to what supports your faithfulness.

**In specific sins:** If you know something is wrong—piracy, dishonesty, sexual compromise—stop it today. Don't negotiate with temptation. Make every effort means decisive action.

Think of the Inuit who hunters who face a pack of hungry wolves. The strongest defense is not to run faster than the wolf, but to know: *don't risk it.* Don't put yourself in the position where you might be caught. Remove the opportunity.

The Christian race is hard. Jesus, who became like us, knows this. He perfected our faith through suffering. But now is not the time to rest or relax our vigilance against sin. **Now is the time to make every effort.**

### The Steven Bradbury Principle

Consider Steven Bradbury, who won gold in the 1,000-meter short track speed skating at the 2002 Winter Olympics. He wasn't the fastest skater. At the quarter-finals, another skater was disqualified, allowing him to advance. At the semi-finals, he was off the pace, but the leaders crashed, so he moved forward. At the finals, he came from the back of the pack while everyone ahead of him fell.

When interviewed, Bradbury said: "Obviously I wasn't the fastest skater. I don't think I'll take the medal as the minute-and-a-half of the race I actually won. I'll take it as the last decade of the hard slog I put in."

His point: *Who finishes counts more than how fast you go.* Bradbury had endured terrible injuries—skate blades slicing through his leg, losing four liters of blood, a broken neck—yet he kept racing. He was tempted to quit: "Why bother? I won't get a medal anyway." But he kept his eyes on the finish line, focused on Jesus, and persevered.

That's the calling before us. Don't worry what others are doing. Don't measure yourself against other runners. **Keep your eyes fixed on Jesus and the future rest He promises.**

## Part 4: God Sees All (4:12–13)

> For the word of God is living and active. Sharper than any double-edged sword, it penetrates even to dividing soul and spirit, joints and marrow; it judges the thoughts and attitudes of the heart. Nothing in all creation is hidden from God's sight. Everything is uncovered and laid bare before the eyes of him to whom we must give account. (Hebrews 4:12–13)

The Christian race is hard, but we do not run in secret. God's word is living and active, piercing to the depths of our hearts. He sees our thoughts and attitudes. Nothing is hidden from His sight. We must give account to Him.

This is both a warning and an encouragement: we cannot hide from God, so we must be sincere in our faithfulness. But we also cannot hide from His grace. He sees us fully and loves us still.

---

## Conclusion: Keep Your Eyes on Jesus

Jesus is the better leader because He is more faithful than Moses and because He brings true rest. His faithfulness summons us to faithfulness. His promised rest calls us to persevere through present trials.

**The call is clear:**

1. **Don't harden your heart** against God's voice
2. **Encourage one another daily** in the faith
3. **Make every effort** to remain faithful until the end
4. **Keep your eyes on Jesus**—not on the speed of other runners, not on your own stumbles, but on Him and the rest He promises

The rest is coming. But for now, stay in the race.
