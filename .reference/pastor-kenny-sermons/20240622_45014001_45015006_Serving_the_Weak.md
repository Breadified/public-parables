# Romans 14:1–15:6

**Title:** Serving the Weak

**Date:** June 22, 2024 (ARISE); March 17, 2013 (SMAC)

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## When Christians Disagree

Throughout church history, believers have disagreed on countless matters—theology, the nature of Christ's blood, the meaning of communion, how to interpret Scripture, which day to gather, what to wear, whether to have music. These controversies have shaped and sometimes fractured the body of Christ.

Yet amid these disagreements, Paul calls us to something deeper: to love one another, to serve the weak, and to submit our freedom to the needs of our brothers and sisters.

---

## 1. Receive the Weak—Don't Despise Them

> Accept the one whose faith is weak, without quarreling over disputable matters. One person's faith allows them to eat anything, but another person, whose faith is weak, eats only vegetables. The one who eats everything must not treat with contempt the one who does not, and the one who does not eat everything must not judge the one who does, for God has accepted them.
>
> — Romans 14:1–3

### Two Types of Believers

Paul identifies two groups in the Roman church: the **weak** and the **strong**.

**The weak in faith:**
- Eat only vegetables (v. 2)
- Regard certain days as more sacred than others (v. 5)
- Believe certain foods are unclean (v. 14)

**The strong in faith:**
- Eat anything without reservation (v. 2)
- Treat all days as equal (v. 5)
- Know that all foods are clean (v. 14)

### Who Are These People?

The backdrop is Rome's early church, where Jewish believers and Gentile believers gathered together. The weak were likely Jewish Christians who maintained Old Testament dietary laws and holy day observances—practices deeply embedded since childhood. For them, pork remained unclean, not because of objective spiritual truth, but because their conscience had been shaped by a lifetime of religious training.

Similarly, the weak included former pagans who struggled with eating meat sacrificed to idols. Their past haunted them; one look at such meat triggered deep spiritual unease.

The strong, by contrast, understood Christian freedom. They knew Jesus had fulfilled the law. They recognized that idols were nothing—meat was just meat. As Paul states:

> I am fully convinced that no food is unclean in itself. If someone regards something as unclean, then for that person it is unclean.
>
> — Romans 14:14

### The Danger of Contempt

Yet when these two groups gathered, tension arose. The strong looked down on the weak. "Don't you understand the gospel? Christ has freed us from these rules. Why do you live in the past?" The contempt was real, the judgment cutting.

Paul's response is clear: **Do not despise the weak.**

> Who are you to judge someone else's servant? To their own master, servants stand or fall. And they will stand, for the Lord is able to make them stand.
>
> — Romans 14:4

The weak are not yours to judge. They belong to God. Your role is not to condemn but to serve.

## 2. Care for the Weak—Don't Cause Them to Stumble

### Understanding "Judgment"

Paul commands, "Do not judge," yet he himself has judged—calling one group "strong" and the other "weak." This seems contradictory, but it hinges on understanding what "judge" means.

In Scripture, "judge" can mean two things:

**1. Discernment** — Making wise distinctions about truth and error. This we must do.
> Jesus said: "Why don't you judge for yourselves what is right?" (Luke 12:57)
>
> Paul wrote: "Brothers and sisters, if someone is caught in a sin, you who live by the Spirit should restore that person gently." (Galatians 6:1)

We must discern between true doctrine and false teaching, between trustworthy people and deceivers. Being a Christian does not mean abandoning your mind in the name of tolerance.

**2. Condemnation** — Despising someone, writing them off, treating them with contempt. This we must never do to a believer.

In Romans 14:10, Paul uses both meanings together:

> Why do you judge your brother or sister? Or why do you treat them with contempt?

The second phrase clarifies the first: judgment here means contempt, dismissal, disdain.

### The Core Command: Don't Cause Stumbling

> Let us stop passing judgment on one another. Instead, make up your mind not to put any stumbling block or obstacle in the way of a brother or sister.
>
> — Romans 14:13

Even knowing the truth—that all foods are clean—Paul says: **Don't exercise your freedom in a way that wounds the weak.**

This requires understanding the power of conscience. When a person believes something is wrong and does it anyway, they sin—not because the thing itself is sinful, but because they've acted against their own conscience. As Paul writes:

> The person who has doubts is condemned if they eat, because their eating is not from faith; and everything that does not come from faith is sin.
>
> — Romans 14:23

**A crucial distinction:** Paul is not asking us to tolerate actual sin in the name of weakness. If someone struggles with pornography, adultery, or theft, we don't say, "Oh, you're weak—I won't judge you." That would be a perversion of this passage. Rather, Paul addresses matters morally indifferent—foods, holy days, practices that are not inherently sinful.

The weak have what might be called an **overly sensitive conscience**—trained by years of religious practice to see danger where none exists. Their sensitivity is harmless, even admirable. It reflects caution. But it is not spiritual strength.

### Why Protect the Conscience?

Why should the strong restrain themselves for the sake of the weak's conscience? Because the conscience is a precious safeguard. It's the last defense against sin. When we train ourselves to ignore our conscience—when we routinely do what we believe is wrong—we corrode that safeguard. We become capable of greater sins.

Consider someone raised to fear cockroaches. Rationally, they're just insects. But years of trained response have made the fear instinctive. If we repeatedly push people to override their instincts, we damage them. Better to let their conscience mature gradually through education and spiritual growth.

Furthermore, setting a bad precedent is dangerous. If you lead someone to act against their conscience and they survive without lightning striking them, they learn that their conscience is unreliable. They begin to override it elsewhere. That path leads away from holiness, not toward it.

### The Principle: Love Supersedes Freedom

> If your brother or sister is distressed because of what you eat, you are no longer acting in love. By eating, do not destroy someone for whom Christ died.
>
> — Romans 14:15

Your freedom to eat is real. Your knowledge is correct. But **love constrains freedom**. The kingdom of God is not about food and drink:

> The kingdom of God is not a matter of eating and drinking, but of righteousness, peace and joy in the Holy Spirit.
>
> — Romans 14:17

### A Practical Application: Alcohol and Gambling

Consider alcohol and gambling. Both are morally neutral—not inherently sinful. Yet both are epidemic sources of family destruction, domestic violence, and addiction in our society.

A Christian may drink wine without sin. Yet when the church gathers, alcohol should not be present. Why? Because we don't know who sits among us—the recovering alcoholic, the adult child of an alcoholic, the person newly freed from gambling addiction. For them, good becomes evil. Your freedom becomes their stumbling block.

This is why wise churches establish clear policies: no alcohol at church events, no gambling-related games. Not out of legalism, but out of love. We don't know their stories. We must assume weakness is present and act accordingly.

> It is better not to eat meat or drink wine or to do anything else that will cause your brother or sister to fall.
>
> — Romans 14:21

## 3. Live for the Lord, Not Yourself

> We do not live for ourselves alone, and we do not die for ourselves alone. If we live, we live for the Lord; and if we die, we die for the Lord. So, whether we live or die, we belong to the Lord.
>
> — Romans 14:7–8

This is the foundation of it all. The strong Christian's freedom is real, but it's never purely personal. You belong to Christ. You live as His servant. Your choices ripple through the body.

> Christ died and rose again for this very reason—so that he might be Lord of both the dead and the living.
>
> — Romans 14:9

## 4. Build Up the Weak—The Strength of Self-Sacrifice

> We who are strong ought to bear with the failings of the weak and not to please ourselves. Each of us should please our neighbors for their good, to build them up.
>
> — Romans 15:1–2

This reverses the natural instinct. The strong don't dominate; they serve. They don't assert their rights; they surrender them.

> Christ did not please himself but, as it is written: "The insults of those who insult you have fallen on me."
>
> — Romans 15:3

This is Christ's pattern. He who had all power chose weakness. He who had all rights surrendered them. The strong Christian mirrors this self-abnegation.

The goal is transformation—not that the weak remain weak forever, but that through patient, loving example, they grow in faith and learn to exercise freedom with wisdom and care. Your sacrifice teaches them. Your restraint models maturity.

> May the God who gives endurance and encouragement give you the same attitude of mind toward each other that Christ Jesus had, so that with one mind and one voice you may glorify the God and Father of our Lord Jesus Christ.
>
> — Romans 15:5–6
