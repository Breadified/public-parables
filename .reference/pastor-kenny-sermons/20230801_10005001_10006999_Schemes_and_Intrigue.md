# 2 Samuel 5-6

**Title:** Schemes and Intrigue
**Date:** 20 August 2023
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Winning

It's a special Father's Day for me and many of the new dads here. My family is doing well—after an eventful afternoon, our newborn arrived and my wife was home by the next evening. Thank you for your prayers and well wishes.

This season has brought a series of wins. In just three weeks, I submitted my thesis, our baby came, I completed my last Hebrew exam, and we finally settled into our own home after four years of moving around. Life is good when everything aligns like this.

David experiences something similar as the new king of Israel. After being anointed, he achieves a series of victories across three areas: he secures a place for his kingdom, establishes peace against his enemies, and brings God's presence into the center of his rule. These three wins demonstrate that God is with him, establishing his kingship.

> **When all the elders of Israel had come to King David at Hebron, the king made a covenant with them at Hebron before the Lord, and they anointed David king over Israel.** (2 Samuel 5:3)

However, 2 Samuel is a challenging book with few direct applications. There's much intrigue and schemes to work through. Let's begin.

## 1. Place (5:1-12)

David immediately turns his attention to securing a city for his kingdom. He sets his sights on Jerusalem, which is occupied by the Jebusites—a Canaanite people group who were part of the inheritance promise to Abraham.

> The king and his men marched to Jerusalem to attack the Jebusites, who lived there. The Jebusites said to David, "You will not get in here; even the blind and the lame can ward you off." They thought, "David cannot get in here." Nevertheless, David captured the fortress of Zion—which is the City of David. (2 Samuel 5:6-7)

### Why Jerusalem?

Jerusalem was strategically significant for several reasons. It sat in the middle of Israel's territory, making it an ideal capital. But more importantly, it had been an unconquered fortress for centuries—even the Benjamites (Saul's own tribe) could never take it. Previous kings had failed where David would succeed.

The Jebusites boasted that their city was so impregnable that even the blind and lame could defend it. But they hadn't counted on David's ingenuity. Recent archaeological discoveries suggest there was a water shaft leading to the Gihon spring inside the fortress. David exploited this weakness, sending Joab through the water duct to open the gates from within.

> On that day David had said, "Anyone who conquers the Jebusites will have to use the water shaft to reach those 'lame and blind' who are David's enemies." That is why they say, "The 'blind and lame' will not enter the palace." (2 Samuel 5:8)

What made Jerusalem special? It had deep historical significance. This was Salem, the place where Melchizedek, the priest-king, blessed Abraham centuries before:

> Then Melchizedek king of Salem brought out bread and wine. He was priest of God Most High, and he blessed Abram, saying, "Blessed be Abram by God Most High, Creator of heaven and earth. And praise be to God Most High, who delivered your enemies into your hand." (Genesis 14:18-20)

Jerusalem was truly an undefeated city—Joshua could not conquer it, neither could the Benjamites. Yet David, the one chosen by God (unlike Saul, the people's choice), captured what previous kings could not.

> David then took up residence in the fortress and called it the City of David. He built up the area around it, from the terraces inward. And he became more and more powerful, because the Lord God Almighty was with him. Now Hiram king of Tyre sent envoys to David, along with cedar logs and carpenters and stonemasons, and they built a palace for David. Then David knew that the Lord had established him as king over Israel and had exalted his kingdom for the sake of his people Israel. (2 Samuel 5:9-12)

Notice two key phrases: "the Lord God Almighty was with him" and "exalted his kingdom." David understood that his victories came not from military strategy alone, but from God's presence.

### A King Needs a Homebase

Just as fathers need their space to recharge, kings need a fortress—a place to defend from and a place to replenish. David established Jerusalem as his headquarters, from which he could both defend his kingdom and launch strategic attacks.

In our spiritual lives, we too need a homebase. While modern Jerusalem has significance in Christian theology (Galatians 4:26 speaks of "the Jerusalem that is above"), our true headquarters is heaven itself. From that heavenly place, we plan our lives and draw our strength.

> But the Jerusalem that is above is free, and she is our mother. (Galatians 4:26)

> For he was looking forward to the city with foundations, whose architect and builder is God. (Hebrews 11:10)

> I saw the Holy City, the new Jerusalem, coming down out of heaven from God, prepared as a bride beautifully dressed for her husband. (Revelation 21:2)

Our significance as believers comes not from political boundaries or earthly territory, but from our connection to God's heavenly kingdom.

### A Troubling Sign

Yet even as David ascends, something ominous appears on the horizon:

> After he left Hebron, David took more concubines and wives in Jerusalem, and more sons and daughters were born to him. These are the names of the children born to him there: Shammua, Shobab, Nathan, Solomon, Ibhar, Elishua, Nepheg, Japhia, Elishama, Eliada and Eliphelet. (2 Samuel 5:13-16)

David's multiplication of wives contradicts the explicit instructions God gave to Israel's kings:

> The king, moreover, must not acquire great numbers of horses for himself or make the people return to Egypt to get more of them, for the Lord has told you, "You are not to go back that way again." He must not take many wives, or his heart will be led astray. (Deuteronomy 17:16-17)

This detail foreshadows troubles ahead, though we'll examine this more closely next week.

## 2. Peace (5:17-25)

David's next victory comes when he defeats his enemies and establishes peace. The Philistines, Israel's ancient rivals, hear that David has been anointed king and immediately march against him in full force.

> When the Philistines heard that David had been anointed king over Israel, they went up in full force to search for him. (2 Samuel 5:17)

It's a pattern: whenever a new leader rises to power, foreign adversaries test their strength. The Philistines were Israel's arch-nemesis—remember, Goliath was a Philistine. But notice what David does:

> David heard about it and went down to the stronghold. Now the Philistines had come and spread out in the Valley of Rephaim; so David inquired of the Lord, "Shall I go and attack the Philistines? Will you deliver them into my hands?" The Lord answered him, "Go, for I will surely deliver the Philistines into your hands." (2 Samuel 5:17-19)

**David inquired of the Lord.** This is the pattern of a godly king—he seeks God's direction before acting.

> So David went to Baal Perazim, and there he defeated them. He said, "As waters break out, the Lord has broken out against my enemies before me." So that place was called Baal Perazim. (2 Samuel 5:20)

When the Philistines attack a second time, David again seeks God's guidance:

> Once more the Philistines came up and spread out in the Valley of Rephaim; so David inquired of the Lord, and he answered, "Do not go straight up, but circle around behind them and attack them in front of the poplar trees. As soon as you hear the sound of marching in the tops of the poplar trees, move quickly, because that will mean the Lord has gone out in front of you to strike the Philistine army." So David did as the Lord commanded him, and he struck down the Philistines all the way from Gibeon to Gezer. (2 Samuel 5:22-25)

God gives David specific tactical instructions, and David obeys. He even waits to hear the sound of God's movement in the poplar trees—emphasizing the importance of listening to God and following His lead, not our own assumptions.

### A Contrast with Saul

This stands in sharp contrast to King Saul. When faced with the Philistines, Saul became paralyzed by fear:

> The Philistines assembled to fight Israel, with three thousand chariots, six thousand charioteers, and soldiers as numerous as the sand on the seashore. When the Israelites saw that their situation was critical and that their army was hard pressed, they hid in caves and thickets, among the rocks, and in pits and cisterns. Some Hebrews even crossed the Jordan to the land of Gad and Gilead. Saul remained at Gilgal, and all the troops with him were quaking with fear. (1 Samuel 13:5-7)

Saul did not conquer the Philistines. But David, who sought God's direction, defeated them decisively.

### Application for Today

Too often we form ideas and rush ahead without consulting God. Or we simply follow society's norms without asking whether they align with His will. David reminds us to stay close to God and seek His direction. We should study His Word to understand His will, and when we know God's direction, we should not fear acting on it.

> The Philistines abandoned their idols there, and David and his men carried them off. (2 Samuel 5:21)

## 3. Presence (6:1-15)

Having secured his place and defeated his enemies, David now pursues something even more important—bringing God's presence to the center of his kingdom.

> David again brought together all the able young men of Israel—thirty thousand. He and all his men went to Baalah in Judah to bring up from there the ark of God, which is called by the Name, the name of the Lord Almighty, who is enthroned between the cherubim on the ark. (2 Samuel 6:1-2)

The Ark of the Covenant was the physical symbol of God's presence. In the ancient tabernacle, it sat in the Holy of Holies, with two golden cherubim (winged creatures) on top, where God was said to dwell. Years earlier, Israel had lost the Ark to the Philistines, who captured it and placed it in their temple. But God vindicated His holiness—when they brought the Ark into their temple, their idol Dagon fell and broke apart. The Philistines realized they couldn't keep the Ark and sent it back to Israel on a cart, where it had remained in Kiriath Jearim for seventy years.

Now David gathers thirty thousand people to bring the Ark to Jerusalem. The mood is celebratory—music fills the air with castanets, harps, lyres, timbrels, sistrums, and cymbals.

> They set the ark of God on a new cart and brought it from the house of Abinadab, which was on the hill. Uzzah and Ahio, sons of Abinadab, were guiding the new cart with the ark of God on it, and Ahio was walking in front of it. David and all Israel were celebrating with all their might before the Lord, with castanets, harps, lyres, timbrels, sistrums and cymbals. (2 Samuel 6:3-5)

But something tragic happens:

> When they came to the threshing floor of Nakon, Uzzah reached out and took hold of the ark of God, because the oxen stumbled. The Lord's anger burned against Uzzah because of his irreverent act; therefore God struck him down, and he died there beside the ark of God. (2 Samuel 6:6-7)

At first glance, this seems harsh. Uzzah was simply trying to prevent the Ark from falling—wasn't he protecting something sacred? But there were specific rules about handling the Ark.

The Levite priests (the Kohathites specifically) were the only ones permitted to touch the Ark, and even they were forbidden from actually touching it. The Ark was to be carried on poles inserted through rings on its sides, with the priests bearing it on their shoulders. No one except the high priest was to touch the Ark directly, and they could only do so on the Day of Atonement.

David's first mistake was using a cart pulled by oxen—a method he had learned from the Philistines when they sent the Ark back. He should have consulted God's instructions in the Law.

> "After Aaron and his sons have finished covering the holy furnishings and all the holy articles, and when the camp is ready to move, only then are the Kohathites to come and do the carrying. But they must not touch the holy things or they will die." (Numbers 4:15)

David's anger and fear were appropriate responses:

> Then David was angry because the Lord's wrath had broken out against Uzzah, and to this day that place is called Perez Uzzah. David was afraid of the Lord that day and said, "How can the ark of the Lord ever come to me?" He was not willing to take the ark of the Lord to be with him in the City of David. Instead, he took it to the house of Obed-Edom the Gittite. (2 Samuel 6:8-10)

David deposited the Ark at the home of Obed-Edom, a Levite. But then something wonderful happened:

> The ark of the Lord remained in the house of Obed-Edom the Gittite for three months, and the Lord blessed him and his entire household. (2 Samuel 6:11)

When David heard that God's blessing had fallen on Obed-Edom's house because of the Ark's presence, he changed his mind and resolved to bring it to Jerusalem—but this time, he did it correctly:

> Now King David was told, "The Lord has blessed the household of Obed-Edom and everything he has, because of the ark of God." So David went to bring up the ark of God from the house of Obed-Edom to the City of David with rejoicing. When those who were carrying the ark of the Lord had taken six steps, he sacrificed a bull and a fattened calf. Wearing a linen ephod, David was dancing before the Lord with all his might, while he and all Israel were bringing up the ark of the Lord with shouts and the sound of trumpets. (2 Samuel 6:12-15)

### David as Priest-King

Notice David is now wearing a linen ephod—a priestly garment. This is significant. The ephod was the ceremonial dress of the high priest, consisting of two linen pieces covering front and back, fastened at the shoulders and waist like an apron.

> But Samuel was ministering before the Lord—a boy wearing a linen ephod. (1 Samuel 2:18)

> These are the garments they are to make: a breastpiece, an ephod, a robe, a woven tunic, a turban and a sash. They are to make these sacred garments for your brother Aaron and his sons, so they may serve me as priests. (Exodus 28:4)

David is functioning as both king and priest, offering sacrifices and conducting the sacred ceremony. While David isn't from the tribe of Levi (he's from Judah), Scripture hints that this was possible. Melchizedek was also both king and priest, and the psalmist declares:

> The Lord says to my lord: "Sit at my right hand until I make your enemies a footstool for your feet." The Lord will extend your mighty scepter from Zion, saying, "Rule in the midst of your enemies!" ... The Lord has sworn and will not change his mind: "You are a priest forever, in the order of Melchizedek." (Psalm 110:1-2, 4)

David, the king of Jerusalem (Salem), takes on the priesthood in the order of Melchizedek. This is a remarkable foreshadowing of Jesus, who is also a Judean king and a priest forever in the order of Melchizedek.

> For it is clear that our Lord descended from Judah, and in regard to that tribe Moses said nothing about priests. And what we have said is even more clear if another priest like Melchizedek appears, one who has become a priest not on the basis of a regulation as to his ancestry but on the basis of the power of an indestructible life. For it is declared: "You are a priest forever, in the order of Melchizedek." (Hebrews 7:14-17)

David brings the Ark properly this time—carried by people, with sacrifices offered at intervals, and with David leading the celebration in his priestly role. This is David at his best: better than Saul, chosen by God, and functioning as both king and priest.

## 4. Progeny (6:16-23)

As the Ark enters the city in jubilation, there's one observer watching from a window with very different feelings:

> As the ark of the Lord was entering the City of David, Michal daughter of Saul watched from a window. And when she saw King David leaping and dancing before the Lord, she despised him in her heart. (2 Samuel 6:16)

Michal is Saul's daughter and David's wife. Her contempt is immediate.

> They brought the ark of the Lord and set it in its place inside the tent that David had pitched for it, and David sacrificed burnt offerings and fellowship offerings before the Lord. After he had finished sacrificing the burnt offerings and fellowship offerings, he blessed the people in the name of the Lord Almighty. Then he gave a loaf of bread, a cake of dates and a cake of raisins to each person in the whole crowd of Israelites, both men and women. And all the people went to their homes. When David returned home to bless his household, Michal daughter of Saul came out to meet him and said, "How the king of Israel has distinguished himself today, going around half-naked in full view of the slave girls of his servants as any vulgar fellow would!" (2 Samuel 6:17-20)

Michal's sarcasm cuts deep. But what's really happening here? Many interpreters assume David was scantily clad, but the emphasis in Scripture is on his priestly garment—the linen ephod. The point isn't that David was naked, but that he was humbling himself before the Lord.

David's response reveals the true source of his joy:

> David said to Michal, "It was before the Lord, who chose me rather than your father or anyone from his house when he appointed me ruler over the Lord's people Israel—I will celebrate before the Lord. I will become even more undignified than this, and I will be humiliated in my own eyes. But by these slave girls you spoke of, I will be held in honor." (2 Samuel 6:21-22)

David's focus is entirely on God's choice, not on public opinion. He reminds Michal that God chose him instead of her father Saul—a painful reminder that Saul's dynasty had ended and David's had begun.

The final detail is stark:

> And Michal daughter of Saul had no children to the day of her death. (2 Samuel 6:23)

Whether through infertility or estrangement, Michal bore no children. With her came the formal end of Saul's lineage. No longer would any of Saul's blood run in the line of Israel's kings. This prepares us for chapter 7, where God makes an extraordinary promise to establish David's dynasty forever.

### A Marriage Gone Wrong

Michal represents unresolved family conflict and wounded pride. A wife disrespects her husband; a husband responds with unnecessary harshness, bringing up her father and comparing her to servant girls. As Proverbs teaches us:

> A gentle answer turns away wrath. (Proverbs 15:1)

Mothers need love and affection. Fathers need respect. Both were absent in this exchange.

## Conclusion

David's victories in 2 Samuel 5-6 reveal a man who understands true kingship: securing his place, establishing peace through obedience to God, and bringing God's presence to the center of his rule. Yet even in his finest moments, hints of future trouble appear—his many wives and the fracture in his marriage.

As we move forward, remember that victory in God's kingdom isn't about military prowess or political achievement. It's about submitting to God's will, seeking His guidance, and celebrating His presence above all else.

> But thanks be to God! He gives us the victory through our Lord Jesus Christ. (1 Corinthians 15:57)

Stick with Jesus.
