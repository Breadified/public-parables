## Why Trust Paul?

Modern critics have questioned Paul's authority, claiming that the "difficult" teachings about sexuality and gender come from him rather than Jesus, and therefore can be discarded. But the book of Acts, written by Luke (not Paul himself) using eyewitness testimony, gives us compelling reasons to trust Paul's authority and teaching.

### Paul Comes in the Line of the Prophets

The resurrection miracle in Acts 20:7-12 echoes the prophetic healings of Elijah in 1 Kings 17:17-24. Young Eutychus falls from a window and dies; Paul throws himself on him and he comes back to life—just as Elijah stretched himself out on the widow's son and he was restored.

The parallels are striking and intentional:
- Both happen in upper rooms
- Both involve dead young men
- Both feature the prophet's physical contact with the body
- Both result in miraculous restoration

This connection tells us that **Paul operates with prophetic authority equal to the great prophets of old**. Luke makes this comparison deliberately, just as he does when he describes Jesus healing the widow's son in Nain (Luke 7:11-17), calling forth reactions that he was "a great prophet" through whom "God has come to help his people."

Paul comes with the same authority as Jesus. His words carry the weight of God's truth.

### Authority Sealed in Blood

But Paul's authority doesn't rest on miracles alone. It's sealed with his willingness to suffer and die for the gospel.

In Acts 19:21, after the failed exorcism by the sons of Sceva, Paul resolves in the Spirit to return to Jerusalem—a decision that parallels Jesus setting his face toward Jerusalem in Luke 9:51, knowing it means death. Just as Jesus sent disciples ahead (Luke 9:52) and faced opposition from Samaritans (Luke 9:53-56), so Paul sends helpers ahead to Macedonia (Acts 19:22) and faces opposition from the Ephesians (Acts 19:23-24).

> **Acts 20:22-25:** "And now, compelled by the Spirit, I am going to Jerusalem, not knowing what will happen to me there. I only know that in every city the Holy Spirit warns me that prison and hardships are facing me. However, I consider my life worth nothing to me; my only aim is to finish the race and complete the task the Lord Jesus has given me—the task of testifying to the good news of God's grace. Now I know that none of you among whom I have gone about preaching the kingdom will ever see me again."

Paul faces this knowledge with clear eyes. He knows suffering awaits, yet he goes willingly. He counts his life as nothing compared to finishing the task Jesus gave him.

This echoes Jesus's own suffering servant role. Paul sees himself as continuing the work of Isaiah 53's suffering servant, completing what Jesus began. In Romans 15:21, Paul quotes Isaiah 52:15—"Those who were not told about him will see, and those who have not heard will understand"—applying it to his own missionary work. He is the vessel through which the gospel reaches the ends of the earth.

Paul's authority is authenticated by his sacrifice. He didn't die for money, position, or false beliefs—he died proclaiming truth. Those who actually knew him wept at his departure (Acts 20:36-38), not because they'd been manipulated, but because they recognized his genuine love and sacrifice.

## The Whole Counsel of God

When Paul says farewell to the Ephesian elders, he emphasizes what made his ministry faithful and what should guide theirs:

> **Acts 20:20-21, 26-27, 31:** "You know that I have not hesitated to preach anything that would be helpful to you but have taught you publicly and from house to house. I have declared to both Jews and Greeks that they must turn to God in repentance and have faith in our Lord Jesus... Therefore, I declare to you today that I am innocent of the blood of any of you. For I have not hesitated to proclaim to you the whole will of God... So be on your guard! Remember that for three years I never stopped warning each of you night and day with tears."

Paul's method was comprehensive:
- **Not hesitating** to preach what was helpful
- Teaching **publicly and house to house**—in every setting and at every scale
- Declaring to **all people, Jews and Greeks alike**
- Proclaiming the **whole will of God**, not just comfortable parts
- **Warning constantly**, with tears, night and day

His message was equally comprehensive: repentance toward God, faith in Jesus Christ, and the full counsel of Scripture. This isn't selective Christianity. It's everything God has revealed through the prophets, the gospels, and Paul's own apostolic letters.

### A Mission for the Next Generation

But Paul's work doesn't end with him. He leaves the elders—and by extension, all of us—with a sacred responsibility:

> **Acts 20:28-30:** "Keep watch over yourselves and all the flock of which the Holy Spirit has made you overseers. Be shepherds of the church of God, which he bought with his own blood. I know that after I leave, savage wolves will come in among you and will not spare the flock. Even from your own number men will arise and distort the truth in order to draw away disciples after them."

This warning resonates through history. The attack on Paul isn't new. In A.D. 140, Marcion rejected Paul's epistles and rewrote Scripture according to his preferences—removing Matthew, Mark, and John, rewriting Luke. Once you start removing books from Scripture because they challenge your preferences, you cannot stop. The result is a false gospel tailored to human desires rather than divine truth.

Today similar voices attack Paul, wanting to remove the difficult passages about sexual ethics, gender roles, and absolute truth claims. But to reject Paul is to lose the gospel of grace itself—salvation by faith alone, forgiveness available to all, justification through Christ's blood shed for sinners (Romans 5:8).

Paul's role in proclaiming salvation isn't incidental to Christianity. It's essential.

### The Suffering Servant as Light to the Gentiles

Paul sees himself fulfilling Israel's ancient calling. In Acts 13:46-47, he claims the words of Isaiah 49:6:

> "I have placed you as a light for the Gentiles, that you may bring salvation to the ends of the earth."

Israel was called to be a light to the nations (Isaiah 49:6). But the nation rejected Jesus. So the role passes to Paul, the suffering servant who brings the gospel to the Gentiles and, ultimately, to Rome itself.

This is his task: to ensure that before this generation passes, the gospel reaches "those who have not heard." And then that generation must pass the torch to the next. This is our inheritance. This is our calling.

## Living the Whole Counsel

The stakes are high, yet we often treat church as a pleasant hobby—a good pattern in our week where we learn a bit, fellowship, and share meals together. All good things. But we forget that church is more than community; it is **a 2,000-year mission**.

In each generation, believers are called to:
- **Guard the gospel** against distortion and compromise
- **Teach the whole counsel of God**—all of it, not just the comfortable parts
- **Pass the torch** to the next generation
- **Sacrifice personally** for the sake of truth and souls

Some of you have been Christians for years. You know Scripture. You've grown up in the faith. But perhaps you've grown weary, distracted, or disappointed. The call is to wake from slumber and remember your role: to share the truth with friends, to serve your church, to be salt and light in the world.

This is not Kenny's teaching alone. This is **the whole counsel of God**—the prophets, the gospels, and Paul's epistles working together as one unified message of repentance, faith, grace, and transformation.

Will you accept this responsibility? Will you personally sacrifice as Paul did? Will you guard what has been entrusted to you and faithfully pass it to the next generation?

The mission continues. The work remains. The gospel still transforms. And you have been given the privilege of carrying it forward.
