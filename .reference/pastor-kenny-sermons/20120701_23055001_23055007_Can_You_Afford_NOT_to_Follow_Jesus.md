# Isaiah 55:1-7

**Title:** Can You Afford NOT to Follow Jesus?  
**Date:** 16 July 2012  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Ultimate Bargain

We all love a good deal. Whether it's a garage sale, finding a free slurpee on 7-Eleven day, or discovering an amazing bargain online—there's something deeply satisfying about getting more than you pay for. The ancient Jewish prophet Isaiah understood this human nature well. Writing around 500 years before Jesus came, Isaiah speaks to us about the greatest bargain ever offered.

## 1. Free Food and the Thirsty Soul

> Come, all you who are thirsty, come to the waters; and you who have no money, come, buy and eat! Come, buy wine and milk without money and without cost. (Isaiah 55:1)

Isaiah addresses those who are thirsty and hungry—people living in an ancient Israel devastated by successive invaders. To an impoverished people, his invitation is extraordinary: come to the waters, drink freely, enjoy the finest goods—wine and milk—without spending a single coin.

In the ancient Middle East, water was precious beyond measure. Unlike our world of swimming pools, ice cubes, and drinking fountains, water was scarce and often unavailable. Most people had no running water in their homes, no bathtubs, no way to bathe regularly. The offer of free water alone was a magnificent gift. But Isaiah promises more—the finest liquids, all free. It's a bargain almost too good to believe.

Yet Isaiah isn't really talking about physical food and drink. His promise reaches deeper:

> Listen, listen to me, and eat what is good, and your soul will delight in the richest of fare. Give ear and come to me; hear me, that your soul may live. (Isaiah 55:2b-3)

The soul—that part of you capable of reflection and meaning-making—needs nourishment just as the body needs food. We can sense this intuitively. Beyond food, safety, love, and self-esteem, we hunger for something more. We want meaning. We want purpose. We want to know we matter.

Yet look at the paradox of our modern age. Australians enjoy unprecedented wealth, health, and education. Yet happiness eludes us. Since the 1950s, the number of people reporting deep unhappiness has risen 20%. Depression rates have skyrocketed tenfold. Suicide rates climb. Family breakdown increases. We've become so accustomed to emotional despair we've created a cultural identity around it—"emo." For all our riches, ranking among the top nations in the world for wealth, we remain profoundly unsatisfied. Not happy, Jan.

### The Cost of Settling for Less

> Why spend money on what is not bread, and your labor on what does not satisfy? (Isaiah 55:2)

This is the rip-off Isaiah exposes. We spend our money and energy on things that don't nourish us. It's like paying full price for a broken item, or ordering an expensive restaurant meal only to receive a tiny portion that leaves you hungry. We labor and pay, yet remain unsatisfied.

The world constantly tempts us with false bargains:

- **Money and status** don't bring lasting satisfaction. Even the wealthiest among us discover that silver can't fill the soul's hunger.
- **University degrees** won't satisfy. Half the students who fight so hard to get in eventually change courses—all that effort for hollow achievement.
- **Travel and experiences** leave us perpetually chasing the next destination. Another temple, another church, another beach—none brings the fulfillment we seek.
- **Possessions** fade in importance overnight. What's essential today becomes kerbside recycling tomorrow.
- **Achievement** doesn't deliver what we hoped. Even a perfect ATAR score brings only temporary validation.

We're being conned by advertising agencies, Hollywood, corporations, and politicians who want us as consumers. They profit from our perpetual dissatisfaction. What tragedy if you spend your entire life chasing bread at twice the price, only to discover at the end that it wasn't bread at all—that all your effort brought nothing.

## 2. The Promise of a Greater King

Into this hunger, God brings a promise through Isaiah—not just food, but spiritual fulfillment:

> I will make an everlasting covenant with you, my faithful love promised to David. See, I have made him a witness to the peoples, a leader and commander of the peoples. Surely you will summon nations you know not, and nations that do not know you will hasten to you, because of the LORD your God, the Holy One of Israel, for he has endowed you with splendor. (Isaiah 55:3b-5)

God speaks of a promise made long ago to King David—that one day another king would come, even greater than David. This new king's influence would extend beyond Israel to all nations. His power would reach peoples he'd never heard of, who would come to him, drawn by his splendor and the character of God working through him.

Fast forward five hundred years. Jesus arrives on the scene around 30 AD, and the Gospel of John shows us how He fulfills this prophecy. When Jesus meets the Samaritan woman at the well, He offers her living water:

> Everyone who drinks this water will be thirsty again, but whoever drinks the water I give him will never thirst. Indeed, the water I give him will become in him a spring of water welling up to eternal life. (John 4:13-14)

Jesus is speaking of something far deeper than physical thirst. He speaks of the thirst of the human soul—our longing for truth, our hunger for certainty and meaning, our need for purpose, our deep curiosity about what lies beyond.

Most profoundly, He addresses our greatest need: forgiveness and love. Deep within us—when all distractions fade and we sit alone with ourselves—we sense a terrible truth. There is a God. This God is holy and just. And we know that one day we must answer to Him. We have done wrong to God, to others, and to ourselves. We carry guilt.

But Jesus offers forgiveness. He offers it freely:

> Let the wicked forsake his way and the evil man his thoughts. Let him turn to the LORD, and he will have mercy on him, and to our God, for he will freely pardon. (Isaiah 55:7b)

The remarkable thing is that this pardon, though free to us, was not free to Jesus. Someone always pays for a bargain. Jesus became that payment. He fed five thousand with five loaves and two fish. Yet years later, on the cross, He Himself thirsted—symbolically experiencing the spiritual thirst that should have been ours to bear. His final words were, "I am thirsty," and then, "It is finished." He paid the debt we owed.

Because of His sacrifice, forgiveness comes to us at no cost. That's the bargain of all bargains.

## 3. The Urgency: Seek While You Can

> Seek the LORD while he may be found; call on him while he is near. (Isaiah 55:6)

Like all true bargains, this offer is time-limited. It's available now. It's near. Don't assume there will be another opportunity tomorrow.

## Can You Afford NOT to Follow Jesus?

We often ask the wrong question. We worry: "Can I afford to follow Jesus? Will it cost me too much? Will I have to give up my lifestyle, my autonomy, my freedom?"

But here's the reality: Following Jesus isn't expensive—it's free. The "cost" people fear is merely the opportunity cost of not pursuing the empty paths the world offers. And those paths, as we've seen, don't satisfy anyway.

Consider what others have chosen and where it's led them. Wealth, education, possessions, achievement—none delivers what the human soul truly needs. We've been fooled into chasing shadows while ignoring the substance.

Even if you think there's a cost to Christianity, it's remarkably small. We casually trust strangers with our lives—we eat food prepared by teenagers, ride in cars driven by people we've never met, take medicines from companies we don't know—simply because "it's probably okay." If you can trust a teenager at a fast-food counter with your health, surely you can trust Jesus with your life based on the testimony of Christians around you who look "okay enough." That's faith the size of a mustard seed—and it's all that's required.

The evidence for Jesus is the strongest you can get from the ancient world: multiple attestation, clear historical origins, internal coherence. It surpasses all other religions and worldviews. And here's what I can tell you from twenty years of Christian faith: the evidence doesn't weaken over time—it strengthens. New manuscripts confirm Scripture's reliability. Science, properly understood, points more and more toward God's design. Quantum physics reveals a universe far stranger and more wonderful than the simplistic materialism taught in high school.

The alternative—the world's arguments against Christianity—they remain exactly the same year after year. But our responses improve. Our understanding deepens.

And what is the real question we should ask?

**How can you afford NOT to follow Jesus?**

This is the best bargain of your life. It's the offer of free spiritual nourishment. It's freedom from the exhausting chase after things that never satisfy. It's the gift of significance—not purchased with money like a tractor, but given freely by God Himself.

> Come, all you who are thirsty, come to the waters; and you who have no money, come, buy and eat! Come, buy wine and milk without money and without cost. (Isaiah 55:1-2)

The food is there. The invitation stands. The cost to you is nothing. The benefit is everything.
