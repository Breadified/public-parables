## Opening Thoughts on Mother's Day

Happy Mother's Day! For some, this day brings sadness and reflection—remembering mothers or children we've lost, or carrying painful memories of absent, abusive, or narcissistic mothers. Yet this message is universal: it's a day to remember that mothers exemplify sacrifice.

What do mothers receive in return for bringing us into this world? Today, they get some flowers and a free breakfast. If they're lucky, maybe a free lunch too! But as Kenny humorously notes, after nearly dying to give birth, flowers and food seem a modest exchange for such a sacrifice.

## Paul's Heart for the Thessalonians (2:17-3:5)

### The Separation

Paul, a follower of Jesus and leader of the church, cared deeply for the Christians in Thessalonica, a city in Greece. In this letter, Paul describes himself as a mother to them (2:8), sharing how he loved them and poured his own life into theirs. But now, they are separated.

> 2:17 But, brothers, when we were torn away from you for a short time (in person, not in thought), we were exceedingly eager in great desire to see your faces. (18) Therefore we wanted to come to you—I, Paul, again and again—but Satan blocked our way.

The backstory explains Paul's separation from them. Due to persecution, Paul had to flee Thessalonica for Berea, then to Athens, then finally to Corinth—all while constantly thinking about the believers he'd left behind. Like a mother separated from her child, Paul experiences separation anxiety. He's not just distracted; he's desperate to return. The phrase "Satan blocked our way" shows Paul's conviction that this separation is not mere circumstance, but spiritual opposition—the supernatural working against his deepest desire to be with them.

### A Mother's Anxiety

This resonates with the Chinese lullaby Kenny shares:

> *In this world, only mothers are good,*  
> *Children with mothers are like treasures,*  
> *Nestled in mother's arms, joy overflows.*  
> *In this world, only mothers are good,*  
> *Children without mothers are like grass,*  
> *Far from mother's arms, where is happiness?*

Separation between mother and child brings distress. From the child's perspective, anxiety is natural and healthy—babies need their mothers constantly. From Paul's perspective, being artificially separated from his spiritual children, he becomes like a desperate mother searching for a way back, fighting to return.

### Timothy as Messenger

Eventually, Paul can bear it no longer and sends a messenger.

> 3:1-2 Therefore, when we could no longer endure it, we thought it best to be left behind at Athens alone, and we sent Timothy, our brother and God's coworker in the gospel of Christ, to establish and exhort you in your faith.

The message Timothy carries is simple: strengthen them in their faith and encourage them in their trials. In the first century, without modern postal systems or email, Paul does the only thing he can—he sends Timothy as a messenger.

> 3:3-4 That no one be unsettled by these afflictions. For you yourselves know that we are destined for this. For when we were with you, we kept telling you beforehand that we were to suffer affliction, just as it has come to pass, and just as you know.

Paul's method is unusual: he doesn't seem to teach anything new. Instead, he reminds them of what they already know—that suffering is part of the Christian calling. Why this emphasis on reinforcement rather than new instruction?

### Why Suffering Strengthens Faith

Paul reminds them because suffering is how Christians grow. As Hebrews 5:8 tells us:

> Though he was a son, he learned obedience through what he suffered.

This refers to Jesus. God the Son, the unchanging God who knows all things, actually learned obedience through suffering on the cross. He experienced pain for our sins, though he lived righteously. If Jesus learned this way, why would we expect to learn obedience without suffering?

We often think suffering is bad, something to avoid at all costs. But suffering is actually how we're shaped and transformed. We're not detached, unfeeling beings, too dignified to be touched by pain. Rather, as Christians, we accept the suffering that comes to us and recognize it as part of our growth in obedience.

Some religions teach that if you care about nothing, pain disappears. But Christianity teaches differently. Paul longed to be with the Thessalonians. He loved them. Separation pained him. Yet he calls them to prepare for whatever suffering might come—persecution, illness, hunger, or the burden of sharing Christ's message with others.

The relationship between Paul and the Thessalonian church was so good that they never took each other for granted.

## Mutual Encouragement and Growth (3:6-13)

### Paul Receives Joy from Their Faith

But here's something remarkable: Paul doesn't just give. He also receives.

> 3:6 But now that Timothy has come to us from you, and has brought us the good news of your faith and love—that you always remember us kindly and long to see us, as we also long to see you.

> 3:7-8 Therefore, brothers, in all our distress and affliction we have been comforted about you through your faith. For now we truly live, if you are standing firm in the Lord!

Paul's language is striking: "we now truly live!" He depends on their faith to live. Before their news arrived, he was anxious and unsettled. But when Timothy returns with word of their steadfastness, Paul experiences joy that goes beyond mere relief—it's a rejuvenation of his own life.

> 3:9 What thanksgiving can we return to God for you, for all the joy that we feel for your sake before our God?

> 2:19-20 For what is our hope or joy or crown of boasting before our Lord Jesus at his coming? Is it not you? For you are our glory and joy.

The Thessalonians are Paul's reward. Their faith, their growth—these are what sustain him through his own suffering. When Paul is discouraged, it's precisely their faithfulness that comes back to encourage him.

### The Principle of Mutual Blessing

This illustrates a principle Jesus himself taught, recorded in Acts 20:35:

> It is more blessed to give than to receive.

We often focus on the blessing that comes to the receiver. But Jesus suggests something profound: the giver is *even more* blessed! Why? Because in giving, we experience a transformation.

This truth is nowhere more evident than in motherhood. We often see mothers as simply giving, giving, giving. Yet something remarkable happens: the act of serving a child changes the mother. As you grow your child, your child also grows you. If you're willing to learn from them, they teach you patience. They inspire gentleness. They push you past your limits of compassion. They challenge you to become a better provider. They redirect your focus from materialism and career ambition toward relationships.

Mothers aren't simply sacrificing for the child's benefit—the child is growing *them*.

We know our mothers as super-wise, healers who know all the answers, meticulous cleaners, makers of delicious meals, comforters, our first supporters. But were they always this way? Look at your sister or cousin when she was younger—immature, self-centered, unable to keep her room clean, always crying and complaining. What happened between that immaturity and the capable woman you know now? *You* happened. She grew because you, her younger sibling, required her to grow.

### Practical Application: Receive While You Serve

When you serve others, adjust yourself to receive the good work that happens in you. Pay attention to your own growth. Learn from those you serve. Recognize that service is not a one-way street.

We are not perfect beings. We are being shaped by Christ, and he uses the people we serve to shape us.

Kenny shares a personal example: preparing this sermon, he was behind schedule and unsure what direction to take. His initial outline was simply about the letters themselves—first and second Thessalonians. But this week, talking with someone from the congregation, clarity emerged. They read Scripture together, asked questions, and worked through the outline. That person is now better. Kenny is now better. Neither of them was expert teaching expert; it was a mutual process of discovery.

When you study Scripture with a young person, it's not you—the perfect scholar—dispensing your vast wisdom to others. It's also you submitting yourself to Scripture and the insights of others. It's an exercise in humility.

### On Burnout and Service

If you're feeling weary about ministry or service work, here's what Kenny would say: the path out of burnout is not to stop serving. We often think, "If I'm tired, I'll just quit serving and everything will get better." But it doesn't work that way. You simply leave your community, and a vicious cycle begins.

Instead, serve—but serve in a healthy, sustainable way, in appropriate seasons. The answer isn't to abandon the child when you're exhausted. It's to serve in a way that's realistic and renewable.

When you do, joy returns. It overflows back to you. Paul's love flowed toward the Thessalonians, and as their faith returned to him, it flowed back to him as well.

## Closing: Mother's Day Blessing

On this Mother's Day, Kenny prays that all the mothers present receive love in return for their sacrifice.

Be preparing souls for Christ. New lives are being shaped. You are preparing them for eternity. This is worth far more than any fancy corporate title. It's worth your body, your beauty, your wealth, your health, your furniture, your possessions. *It is more blessed to give than to receive.*

And the good news? They are preparing you as well. It's worth it.
