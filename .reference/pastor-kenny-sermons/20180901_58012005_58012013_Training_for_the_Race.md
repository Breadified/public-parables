# Hebrews 12:5-13

**Title:** Training for the Race
**Date:** 2 September 2018
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Understanding Pain and Discipline

What is pain? For many years, people understood pain simply as a signal of tissue damage. But modern research reveals this is inadequate. Soldiers in battle can suffer excruciating injuries yet feel no pain. Conversely, patients with healed tissue often experience persistent phantom limb pain. The mind's perception of pain is far more complex than the body's physical damage.

This complexity becomes evident in elite athletes. Olympic gymnasts vault on sprained ankles. Marathon runners cross finish lines bloodied and unaware of their wounds. Why? Because they have trained themselves to think about pain differently—as a tool for achievement rather than a signal to stop.

The original readers of Hebrews faced a different kind of pain: the pain of Christian faith under persecution. Jesus hadn't returned as expected. Times were tough. It was painful to follow Christ. The author writes to strengthen their wavering faith.

## 1. Earthly Pain (Hebrews 12:5-8)

> And have you completely forgotten this word of encouragement that addresses you as a father addresses his son? It says, "My son, do not make light of the Lord's discipline, and do not lose heart when he rebukes you, because the Lord disciplines the one he loves, and he chastens everyone he accepts as his son." (Hebrews 12:5-6)

The word of encouragement ("paraklesis" in Greek) means to build us up. The author quotes from Proverbs 3, establishing a parallel between how earthly fathers discipline their children and how God disciplines His people.

**The Analogy of Fatherhood:**

If you were raised in a normal, loving home, you experienced discipline. The "naughty corner" for not eating vegetables. Scolding for refusing to brush teeth. A smack for incomplete homework. As you grew older: no pocket money for skipped chores. Being grounded for poor school performance. This is what loving parents do.

> "He who spares his rod hates his son, but he who loves him disciplines him promptly." (Proverbs 13:24)

Scripture is clear: discipline is love. A father who fails to discipline is actually failing his child. Why? Because contrary to modern philosophy, we are not born innately good. The prevailing worldview suggests that society corrupts naturally good children. But Scripture teaches the opposite: children naturally tend toward selfishness and rebellion. Discipline trains them toward righteousness.

**The Theology Behind Discipline:**

When parents leave children undisciplined, the results are catastrophic. "Lord of the Flies" illustrates what happens when children are left to their own devices—they imagine, invent, and create evil. An undisciplined child becomes the criminal or brute of adulthood.

> "Endure hardship as discipline; God is treating you as his children. For what children are not disciplined by their father?" (Hebrews 12:7)

Notice verse 7: God treats us as His children precisely through discipline. The opposite—the illegitimate child—is the neglected child. A child born out of wedlock to a mistress, abandoned by the father, growing up without paternal guidance. This is metaphor for fatherlessness.

When fatherlessness becomes epidemic, society fractures. Why is there such poverty in wealthy nations despite government spending? The root cause is simple: these children lack a father. As President Obama acknowledged:

> "Children who grow up without a father are five times more likely to live in poverty and commit crime; nine times more likely to drop out of schools and 20 times more likely to end up in prison."

The father is the source of discipline—typically more direct, less patient, more willing to enforce boundaries. This produces fruit over time. We want fatherlessness to be an exception, not the norm we accept. Yet since 1969, fatherlessness has become increasingly common.

> "If you are not disciplined—and everyone undergoes discipline—then you are not legitimate, not true sons and daughters at all." (Hebrews 12:8)

Without discipline, you lack proof of belonging to God's family.

## 2. Heavenly Comfort (Hebrews 12:9-11)

> "Moreover, we have all had human fathers who disciplined us and we respected them for it. How much more should we submit to the Father of spirits and live!" (Hebrews 12:9)

Here's the contrast: human fathers discipline based on limited knowledge—what "seemed" best to them. But God disciplines with infinite wisdom, always for our actual good.

> "They disciplined us for a little while as they thought best; but God disciplines us for our good, in order that we may share in his holiness. No discipline seems pleasant at the time, but painful. Later on, however, it produces a harvest of righteousness and peace for those who have been trained by it." (Hebrews 12:10-11)

**The Contrast Between Temporary and Eternal:**

Earthly discipline is temporary—lasting for a little while. The pain seems overwhelming in the moment. But God's discipline aims at eternity: that we might "share in his holiness," producing a "harvest of righteousness and peace." The temporary pain yields eternal fruit.

Notice the word "trained" (Greek: "gymmed")—suggesting spiritual conditioning, like athletic training. The discipline produces holiness and righteousness as surely as training produces athletic strength.

**Application to Our Pain:**

Many of us carry deep pain. Some endure chronic suffering for years. Others bear painful memories, grief, failure, loss. Without denying the reality of this pain, we can see God's greater hand at work. God's power supersedes even death and sin. Just as God redeemed the pain of the cross to bring salvation to the world, He works through our suffering toward our good.

Consider John Piper's reflection after his cancer diagnosis: he called it a gift—not as something to desire, but as an opportunity to deepen his faith, trust God more, read Scripture more seriously, and rethink his life's purpose. Cancer became a training opportunity rather than a disability.

Pain is not the same as bad. Easy is not the same as good. Many worthwhile things in life require pain. Elite athletes deliberately endure training pain because they understand it serves a greater purpose. They know that to achieve their goals—Olympic medals, personal records—they must embrace the pain as a means to excellence.

> "Later on, however, it produces a harvest of righteousness and peace for those who have been trained by it." (Hebrews 12:11)

This is the promise: discipline produces righteousness and peace.

## 3. Endure the Pain (Hebrews 12:12-13)

> "Therefore, strengthen your feeble arms and weak knees. Make level paths for your feet, so that the lame may not be disabled, but rather healed." (Hebrews 12:12-13)

**Spiritual Strength:**

The metaphor speaks to spiritual arms and knees. Stand tall. Keep your hands raised in worship. Walk a straight path for God. Don't turn aside from the truth into false teaching or deceptive doctrines.

The Greek word for "disabled" or "turned aside" appears in the Pastoral Epistles to describe spiritual wandering:

> "Some have deviated from these and turned aside to fruitless discussion." (1 Timothy 1:6)
> "Some have already turned away to follow Satan." (1 Timothy 5:15)
> "They will turn away from hearing the truth and will turn aside to myths." (2 Timothy 4:4)

The call is to persevere, to maintain a straight path, not to fall away from faith.

**Training for Endurance:**

Life in this fallen world guarantees ongoing pain. In a corrupt world, there are consequences. In a needy world, there is the pain of costly service. In a tragic world, there is grief. Enduring present pain builds capacity for future trials.

This is how Olympians train. By repeatedly subjecting their brains to pain through training, their brains become sophisticated at sending inhibitory signals down the spinal cord—a phenomenon called "conditioned pain modulation." More pain experienced in training means greater pain tolerance in competition. In essence, pain kills pain.

Similarly, daily spiritual discipline—Bible reading, prayer, consistent church attendance, thoughtful reflection on God's Word—conditions us spiritually. These feel awkward at first, strange even. But over time, they produce spiritual strength. As surely as abdominal crunches build core muscles, these habits keep our spiritual paths straight and our feet firm.

**The Crisis of Discipline in Our Generation:**

I am concerned about our generation's lack of discipline. Young people don't finish what they start. Half of university students don't complete their courses. We lack basic life skills. We exhibit little of the grit our parents' generation possessed. We resign from jobs by text message. We blame authorities rather than take responsibility.

This flows into every area of life. We make promises to others we cannot keep. Half of our marriages end in divorce. And it flows directly into our Christian life: God becomes just another optional button on the Facebook of life.

**Church Discipline as Training:**

Perhaps this is why our church emphasizes certain commitments: showing up regularly, serving consistently, giving proper notice if absent, keeping promises. From the outside, it might look legalistic and painful. But we're attempting to train in discipline—the capacity to do what we've committed to, to keep promises made.

The church is voluntary, yes. You're welcome whether you attend or not. But for those who've committed, think of it like State Emergency Service volunteers. They're unpaid. They have other jobs. They volunteer freely. But they're committed. They attend regular training. They respond to callouts. They have discipline. When there's a fire, they don't say, "It's hot, I'm on holiday." They've signed up to be trusted.

Similarly, if we're convinced that others need to know Christ, shouldn't we be as committed to warning them about spiritual danger as firefighters are to saving communities from physical fire?

**Grit: The Predictor of Success:**

Research by Dr. Angela Lee Duckworth reveals something remarkable: IQ is not the predictor of success. Grit is. She developed the Grit Scale asking whether people finish what they begin, overcome setbacks, and maintain commitment. In testing Ivy League undergraduates, West Point cadets, and spelling bee competitors, grit consistently predicted success—more than raw ability.

Grittier students achieved higher grades. Grittier cadets were more likely to persist. Grittier spellers outranked competitors with greater natural talent. Perseverance may matter as much as intelligence itself.

Charles Darwin, reflecting on Francis Galton's study of eminent figures in history, concluded:

> "I have always maintained that, excepting fools, men did not differ much in intellect, only in zeal and hard work; and I still think this is an eminently important difference."

The difference between success and failure is not intelligence—it's hard work.

**Now Is the Time:**

For students: the formative years are now. Good habits established now will last a lifetime. If you can't prioritize God's Kingdom while studying, how will you when work pressures mount? If you can't maintain spiritual discipline as a young worker, how will you with a family to support, bills to pay, and responsibilities multiplying? If you abandon these habits during the busy years, retirement won't suddenly restore them. You'll spend time on golf, not God.

For young families: these are formative years for your children. What values will define your family? What habits will you model? Will you be merely a family, or a Christian family with distinctive discipline and commitment?

Friends: **now is the time to get disciplined**. Not someday. Not when circumstances are easier. Now.

The present is the time to train for righteousness and holiness. The present is the time to develop self-control and perseverance. These formative years determine the trajectory of your entire life.

**Finishing the Race:**

The 1968 Olympic marathon saw 75 runners start. During the race, Abebe Bikila fell badly about 19 kilometers in. His knee was apparently dislocated, his shoulder bleeding. Medical staff urged him to withdraw. But he continued running—walking, limping, pushing through pain. He finished last, over an hour after the winner.

When interviewed, asked why he ignored medical advice to quit, he said simply:

> "My country did not send me 5,000 miles just to start the race; they sent me to finish the race."

Remarkably, while few remember the name of the gold medal winner that year, the world remembers Bikila. We honor perseverance.

Eric Liddell, the Olympic runner, reflected:

> "It has been a wonderful experience to compete in the Olympic Games and to bring home a gold medal. But since I have been a young lad, I have had my eyes on a different prize. Each one of us is in a greater race than any I have run, and this race ends when God gives out the medals."

We're all in a greater race. The Christian life is a marathon requiring grit, discipline, and perseverance. The call of Hebrews is clear: strengthen your spiritual arms and weak knees. Make level paths. Finish the race.

Persevere. The harvest of righteousness and peace awaits those trained by discipline.
