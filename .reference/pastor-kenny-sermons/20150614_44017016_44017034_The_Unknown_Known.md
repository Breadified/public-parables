# Acts 17:16-34

**Title:** The Unknown Known

**Date:** June 14, 2015

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## How Do We Know?

In a world of conflicting information—about viruses, science, faith, and truth itself—the fundamental question becomes: How do we actually know what is true? This question has shaped Western civilization for millennia, and it remains urgent today.

Throughout history, there have been two primary theories of knowledge. **Empiricism**, championed by Aristotle, teaches that truth comes through observation and experience. We gain real knowledge by observing the world around us, allowing those observations to form universal ideas. As the saying goes, "What you see is what you get."

In contrast, **Rationalism**, developed by Plato, holds that true knowledge comes from within—from reason itself, not external observation. Real truth consists of eternal ideals found deep in the soul. What we observe in the world are merely shadowy reflections of these perfect Ideas. We don't discover truth; we remember it. This is how we can know mathematical truths like 1 billion + 1 billion = 2 billion without counting to a billion—it's something we can figure out through pure reason.

Both approaches originated in Athens and have shaped how we understand knowledge today. One relies on measurement and empirical observation; the other on logical reasoning and imagination about how things fit together.

## Paul in Athens: A City of 30,000 Gods

When Paul arrives in Athens, he encounters both of these philosophical traditions embodied in the people he meets. The city is saturated with idols—so many that one ancient historian remarked it was easier to find a god in Athens than a man.

> 16 While Paul was waiting for them in Athens, he was greatly distressed to see that the city was full of idols.

Yet despite this religious abundance, Paul finds something striking:

> 22 Paul then stood up in the meeting of the Areopagus and said: "People of Athens! I see that in every way you are very religious. 23 For as I walked around and looked carefully at your objects of worship, I even found an altar with this inscription: to an unknown god. So you are ignorant of the very thing you worship—and this is what I am going to proclaim to you."

## Two Worldviews Collide

### The Epicureans: Practical Atheists

The first group Paul encounters are the Epicureans, the empiricists of their day. They were materialists who believed that all you see is all there is. For them, the universe consists merely of atoms—eternal, without origin or end, constantly forming new combinations through chance. They even believed the gods themselves were made of atoms.

This worldview has profound implications. If everything is material, then there are no miracles, no divine intervention, no life after death. This life is all that exists, so the goal becomes pleasure and freedom from pain and fear. "Eat, drink and be merry for tomorrow we die."

When they hear Paul's message about Jesus and resurrection, they dismiss him with contempt:

> 18 Some of them asked, "What is this babbler trying to say?"

They treat him as a mere "scavenger of information," a collector of borrowed fragments, too foolish to be taken seriously. Their materialism leaves no room for what Paul proclaims.

### The Stoics: Religious but Lost

The second group represents a different error—the religious rationalists. The Stoics believed in gods, yes, but their gods are impersonal forces pervading all things, like an energy spread throughout rocks, sky, and trees. These are not relational beings but impersonal powers. The Stoic god is deterministic, ruling everything through fate, indifferent to human concerns.

This leads them to make gods in their own image:

> 29 "Therefore since we are God's offspring, we should not think that the divine being is like gold or silver or stone—an image made by human design and skill."

When people sit alone with their imagination and create a god that mirrors themselves, they end up with a god who shares their values, their ethics, their tolerance of sin. It becomes a subjective god—a god of feelings rather than truth. "If I feel that something is okay, then it must be okay. Surely if two people love one another, it must be acceptable. Surely the Bible is outdated on this issue."

This is the danger of rationalism and imagination replacing revelation. We create gods we can control.

## God's Self-Disclosure

But Paul presents a third way of knowing—through **revelation and history**. God has not left us to figure things out through observation alone or imagination alone. He has revealed himself.

> 24 "The God who made the world and everything in it is the Lord of heaven and earth and does not live in temples built by human hands. 25 And he is not served by human hands, as if he needed anything. Rather, he himself gives everyone life and breath and everything else."

Yes, the world is material. We can study it and do science. But God transcends his creation. He is greater than the material world he made. To think we can't know God because we can't see him is like being an ant focused on the next millimeter of ground, unable to perceive the one who created the ant farm.

But God has left clues:

> 27 God did this so that they would seek him and perhaps reach out for him and find him, though he is not far from any one of us.

Romans 1:20 reinforces this: "For since the creation of the world God's invisible qualities—his eternal power and divine nature—have been clearly seen, being understood from what has been made, so that people are without excuse."

Beyond the material world, there is knowledge we cannot gain through empiricism alone: love, dignity, kindness, compassion, beauty, justice, value, mercy, loyalty, happiness, meaning and purpose. How do you measure friendship? These are metaphysical realities that transcend the five senses. Morality itself—the knowledge that we shouldn't kill each other, that cheating is wrong even if no one sees it—speaks to something beyond pure materialism.

If we are merely atoms and chance, there is no philosophical justification for morality. Yet we know morality exists. This points to something—or Someone—beyond the material.

### God Made Known in History

Most importantly, God has revealed himself not through abstract reasoning or imagination, but through history and person:

> 26 From one man he made all the nations, that they should inhabit the whole earth; and he marked out their appointed times in history and the boundaries of their lands.

> 28 "For in him we live and move and have our being.' As some of your own poets have said, 'We are his offspring."

God is personal. He wants to be known. He reaches out to us:

> 31 For he has set a day when he will judge the world with justice by the man he has appointed. He has given proof of this to everyone by raising him from the dead.

Jesus turned up. In history. Born of the virgin Mary, suffered and died under Pontius Pilate, rose from the dead three days later. This is not something you can prove through science (which requires repeatability) or logic alone (because the resurrection transcends logical categories). But it is something that can be known through historical testimony and evidence.

God has unmasked himself. We can no longer claim ignorance:

> 30 In the past God overlooked such ignorance but now he commands all people everywhere to repent.

## The Response

When confronted with the message of Jesus and resurrection, people respond in two ways:

> 32 When they heard about the resurrection of the dead, some of them sneered,

They close their minds. They refuse to examine the evidence. They prefer their self-made gods to the God who actually exists.

> 32 but others said, "We want to hear you again on this subject."

These are the true empiricists—those willing to consider all available evidence, including historical evidence. They recognize that honest inquiry requires openness to what is actually there, not just what fits our preexisting frameworks.

---

## Application

The question Paul posed to Athens remains urgent for us: Are you building your faith on rationalism (ideas of your own making), empiricism (what you can see and measure), or revelation (what God has actually disclosed)?

You cannot figure your way to God through logic alone. You cannot discover God merely by observing nature. But God has made himself known in history through Jesus Christ. The choice is whether you will open yourself to this knowledge or cling to the gods of your own imagination.
