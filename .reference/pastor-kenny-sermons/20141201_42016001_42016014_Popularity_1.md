# Luke 16:1-14

**Title:** Popularity  
**Date:** 1 December 2014  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Human Desire for Popularity

We all want to be popular. This desire takes many forms:

- **Quality friendships** - connecting with influential people who can open doors and help us advance
- **Quantity of friendships** - accumulating Facebook friends, social media followers, and likes on our posts
- **Investment in appearance** - wearing the right clothes, spending money strategically, keeping up with trends, watching popular movies, listening to popular music, eating at the right places, carrying popular gadgets

We'll invest considerable time, money, and effort to achieve popularity. Yet surprisingly, Jesus himself speaks about the value of making friends—which seems to validate this very human concern.

## 1. Gaining Earthly Friends (16:1-8)

### The Manager's Dilemma

Jesus tells of a wealthy man whose manager has been squandering his possessions—not managing the business well, wasting resources and the master's time. The rich man confronts him:

> "There was a rich man who received an accusation that his manager was squandering his possessions. So he called the manager in and asked, 'What is this I hear about you? Give an account of your management, because you can no longer be my manager.'"

Facing unemployment and unable to do manual labor or accept charity, the manager devises a clever strategy:

> "Then the manager said to himself, 'What should I do, since my master is taking the management away from me? I'm not strong enough to dig; I'm ashamed to beg. I know what I'll do so that when I'm removed from management, people will welcome me into their homes.'"

### The Manager's Strategy

The manager's plan is to generate favors quickly. He calls in the master's debtors and offers them significant discounts:

> "So he summoned each one of his master's debtors. 'How much do you owe my master?' he asked the first one. 'A hundred measures of olive oil,' he said. 'Take your invoice,' he told him, 'sit down quickly, and write 50.' Next he asked another, 'How much do you owe?' 'A hundred measures of wheat,' he said. 'Take your invoice,' he told him, 'and write 80.'"

By offering these discounts, he creates a network of people who owe him personal favors. The principle is simple: who you know matters more than what you know. When accountants lose their jobs, they often approach former clients they've served well, knowing those connections will help them land new positions or referrals.

### The Unexpected Praise

What makes this parable remarkable is that the master actually commends the manager:

> "The master praised the unrighteous manager because he had acted astutely. For the sons of this age are more astute than the sons of light in dealing with their own people."

The praise isn't about the dishonesty—the manager is clearly cheating his master by reducing those debts. Rather, Jesus emphasizes the manager's **astuteness**—his shrewdness, discernment, and ability to perceive his situation clearly and act decisively to solve his problem.

The manager recognized his time was running out. Rather than despair, he used his brains to find a practical solution. He employed every legitimate means available to secure his future.

### Two Critical Lessons

**First:** We must be honest. This passage does not condone dishonesty. Christians are called to be people of truth, for lies belong to the devil—the father of all lies.

**Second:** We must be astute. Use your intellect to solve difficult problems. When you've made mistakes, admit the truth and work to fix them. Be discerning about your circumstances and resourceful in addressing them.

## 2. Friendship with God (16:9-15)

### The True Purpose of Earthly Wealth

The parable points to something deeper than mere earthly networking:

> "And I tell you, make friends for yourselves by means of the unrighteous money so that when it fails, they may welcome you into eternal dwellings."

Notice the parallel to verse 4: just as the manager wanted people to welcome him into their homes, Jesus speaks of being welcomed into eternal dwellings. This isn't about popularity on earth—it's about friendship in heaven, about the life after this one.

Jesus makes this explicit in verse 13:

> "No household slave can be the slave of two masters, since either he will hate one and love the other, or he will be devoted to one and despise the other. You can't be slaves to both God and money."

We cannot serve two masters. There is no middle ground. We must choose either God or money, and that choice determines everything.

### The Problem: We Naturally Ignore God

By nature, we are not friends with God. Instead, we've become consumed with material things—especially money. We see money as the source of security and success, forgetting the One who created both money and all things.

Consider this: Your parents and grandparents have sacrificed tremendously to give you good things. Perhaps they migrated to a new country, learned a new language, worked multiple jobs, and spent every available dollar on your education and tutoring. Yet when you graduate from the best schools, you tell yourself, "I did it," and take all the credit.

We forget those who came before us—the ones who built the universities, established the schools, created democratic governments under the rule of law, and constructed the infrastructure that makes our lives possible. Most importantly, we forget God. Behind all human achievement stands the Creator who brought all things into being, breathed life into creation, and gave us the intellect and abilities we take for granted.

Think back to moments when you thought you might fail—a crucial exam, an important decision. Who brought you through? When you were on a waiting list and made the final cut, who do you think orchestrated that outcome?

### The Solution: Jesus Offers Friendship

Yet despite our natural distance from God, He reaches out to us. Jesus himself defines true friendship:

> "Greater love has no one than this: to lay down one's life for one's friends. You are my friends if you do what I command. I no longer call you servants, because a servant does not know his master's business. Instead, I have called you friends, for everything that I learned from my Father I have made known to you."

Jesus didn't merely help us in our daily lives—He went far further. He died for us in the most agonizing way. When the moment came, He did not hesitate. He went willingly to the cross to save us because we are His friends.

God has sent you a friend request. The question is: will you accept it?

### The Warning: Don't Let Material Things Block Your Friendship with God

Jesus gives us a serious warning:

> "Whoever is faithful in very little is also faithful in much, and whoever is unrighteous in very little is also unrighteous in much. So if you have not been faithful with the unrighteous money, who will trust you with what is genuine? And if you have not been faithful with what belongs to someone else, who will give you what is your own?"

If we are ungrateful to God for what we have on earth, why would He entrust us with eternal riches?

The cost of friendship with God is real. Following Jesus demands sacrifice:

> "Whoever does not carry their cross and follow me cannot be my disciple." (Luke 14:27)

Don't let ambition, fear of commitment, or pressure from others prevent you from becoming God's friend. And once you are friends with God, you will naturally pursue genuine friendships with others.

### The Cost of Christian Community

Being friends with God does cost something materially:

- Joining a church means giving up lucrative Sunday work
- A changed lifestyle toward generosity costs money
- Supporting Bible ministries requires financial investment
- Refusing to be overly ambitious and career-focused costs money
- Attending Christian camps and conferences requires saving and planning
- Simply spending time together—dinners, gatherings—costs money

But this investment yields eternal returns. You gain friends in the eternal dwellings. Because Jesus has called us friends, we pursue friendships with one another as His disciples.

Jesus gave up His own popularity for us. Even when lonely, God is with us. That friendship is worth every cost.
