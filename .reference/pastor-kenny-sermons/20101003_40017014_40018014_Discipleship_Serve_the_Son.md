# Matthew 17:14–18:14

**Title:** Discipleship: Serve the Son
**Date:** October 3, 2010
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## The Greatest in the Kingdom

What does it mean to be great in God's kingdom? Jesus teaches through an unexpected framework: the welcome we extend to the vulnerable and the humble posture we adopt toward others.

The disciples asked, "Who is the greatest in the kingdom of heaven?" Jesus called a child forward and made the child the measure of greatness. This wasn't sentimentality about childhood innocence. In the ancient world, childhood was a position of powerlessness and servitude.

### Be Little: Humility as Kingdom Greatness (18:1–4)

> "Truly I tell you, unless you change and become like little children, you will never enter the kingdom of heaven." (18:3)

In first-century culture, children held no status. Once they could walk and talk, they were thrust into the workforce, assigned the lowest tasks: cleaning, gathering fuel, drawing water, tending flocks. The word for "child" (*pais*) shared meaning with "servant" or "slave." To be a child was to be a servant—vulnerable, powerless, dependent.

Jesus uses the child as an illustration of **humility**:

> "Therefore, whoever takes the lowly position of this child is the greatest in the kingdom of heaven." (18:4)

The criterion for kingdom greatness is not achievement, power, or wisdom. It is the willingness to take a lowly place, to serve without demanding recognition, to accept humiliation as part of following Christ.

**Contrast with Today:** Modern childhood is often the opposite—children are pampered, entitled, exempted from service. The "little emperor" phenomenon reflects how we've inverted the ancient understanding. Parents must teach their children humility through work, through chores, through the experience of serving. This teaches perseverance, punctuality, respect, and the character that prepares them for life.

Are you a "little one"—humble and willing to serve—or a "little emperor"—entitled and demanding to be served?

### Welcome the Little: The Cost of Blocking Others (18:5–9)

> "And whoever welcomes one such child in my name welcomes me." (18:5)

To welcome means to receive, to accept, to include. But Jesus immediately warns of the gravity of failing to welcome:

> "If anyone causes one of these little ones—those who believe in me—to stumble, it would be better for them to have a large millstone hung around their neck and to be drowned in the depths of the sea." (18:6)

The word "stumble" (*skandalon*) means to cause someone to fall away from faith, to block their path toward God's kingdom. The punishment is severe: it would be better to drown than to face the judgment for preventing someone from entering God's kingdom.

Jesus illustrates this danger through the incident of the epileptic boy (17:14–23). The disciples could not heal the boy. Jesus rebuked them as "unbelieving and perverse," saying, "How long shall I stay with you?" Their failure was not merely practical incompetence—it was spiritual blindness rooted in pride and ambition.

The disciples, obsessed with who would be greatest, had become obstacles rather than welcomers. They blocked a child from healing and salvation. Their agenda was power and status, not service.

Jesus continues:

> "If your hand or your foot causes you to stumble, cut it off and throw it away. It is better for you to enter life maimed or crippled than to have two hands or two feet and be thrown into eternal fire." (18:8)

This hyperbolic language drives home the principle: **whatever prevents you from welcoming the vulnerable must be cut away.** Pride, ambition, comfort, status consciousness—these are the hands and feet that cause us to stumble others. The alternative is hellfire.

**The World's Pecking Order:** The world teaches that the young serve the old, the weak serve the strong, the powerless serve the powerful. This is the natural order. School hierarchies, workplace structures, cultural traditions—all reinforce the notion that status flows downward and service flows upward.

Jesus reverses this entirely. In God's kingdom, the strong serve the weak, the old serve the young, the powerful serve the powerless, the wise serve the foolish.

**Practical Welcoming:**

- **Are we looking after those younger than ourselves?** Do we serve our children sacrificially, even when they cramp our style, demand our time, repay us with ingratitude?
- **Are we serving our siblings?** At church, through ministry, through hospitality, through bearing one another's burdens.
- **Three practical principles for church hospitality:**
  1. **You have to be here.** If you don't come consistently, you won't know who is new or missing. You become the one no one knows.
  2. **BELL—arrive early, leave late.** Welcome happens around the edges, in the transitions. Be present before and after.
  3. **Feet, Seat, Meet.** Move across the room, sit next to someone new, have a real conversation. They are often more frightened of you than you are of them.

When you welcome the little, you welcome Christ. When you fail to welcome, you fail Christ.

### The Father's Will: None Should Perish (18:10–14)

> "See that you do not despise one of these little ones. For I tell you that their angels in heaven always see the face of my Father in heaven." (18:10)

Each vulnerable person—each "little one"—has direct access to the Father's attention. Their angels see His face continually. The Father is actively watching over them.

The parable of the lost sheep illustrates the Father's heart:

> "If a man owns a hundred sheep, and one of them wanders away, will he not leave the ninety-nine on the hills and go to look for the one that wandered off? And if he finds it, truly I tell you, he is happier about that one sheep than about the ninety-nine that did not wander off." (18:12–13)

The shepherd leaves the ninety-nine to pursue the one. His joy at recovery is greater than his satisfaction with those who never strayed. This is not the logic of efficiency; it is the logic of love.

> "In the same way your Father in heaven is not willing that any of these little ones should perish." (18:14)

**The will of your Father in heaven is that none of the vulnerable should be lost.** This is not optional sentiment. This is divine will. The question becomes: **Are you willing? How willing are we?**

Once, you were a lost sheep. You did not know the Father. Yet He found you. The glorious Son—the one radiant with divine light, the one greater than Moses and Elijah, the one in whom the Father delighted—denied Himself, carried His cross, and followed the Father's will for your sake.

Will you now deny your own self-interest, carry your cross, and follow the Father's will so that others, like you, are welcomed into the kingdom?

### Even Sons Are Not Exempt from Service (17:24–27)

After teaching about humility and welcome, Jesus demonstrates His own servanthood through a striking act.

> "After Jesus and his disciples arrived in Capernaum, the collectors of the two-drachma tax came to Peter and asked, 'Doesn't your teacher pay the temple tax?'" (17:24)

Peter initially asserts Jesus's freedom: "Yes, he does." Or perhaps, "No, he doesn't"—the wording allows both interpretations. But Jesus clarifies:

> "From whom do the kings of the earth collect duty and taxes—from their own sons or from others?" (17:25–26)

Peter answers correctly: "From others." Jesus replies, "Then the sons are exempt." Kings do not pay taxes to themselves. Sons of kings are exempt from obligation. Jesus, as the Son of God, owes nothing to the temple.

Yet He does something extraordinary:

> "But so that we may not offend them, go to the lake and throw out your line. Take the first fish you catch; open its mouth and you will find a four-drachma coin. Take it and give it to them for my tax and yours." (17:27)

Despite His exemption, Jesus pays the tax. He does not exercise His rights as Son. He humbles Himself as a servant. He serves not because He must, but so that others will not be blocked (*skandalon*) from faith.

This is the pattern for all who follow: We are sons and daughters of the King, exempted from the demands of the world. Yet we voluntarily take the servant's place so that others might encounter Christ through us. Our status as children of God does not free us from service—it calls us deeper into it.

## The Christian Experience

When Apple began opening its stores, they based their model on the Ritz-Carlton Hotel. They wanted not just a store full of products, but a store full of people—employees and customers who felt truly welcomed, truly known, truly valued.

Walking into an Apple store, you sense something: comfort, hospitality, genuine care. The employees have "Apple in their bones." They serve not to make a sale, but because they love the product and want to share its benefits. You are welcomed, not blocked.

**This is how we are called to treat one another in the church.** We are the hosts, the welcomers, the servants. Our experience should be one of genuine love, warmth, and hospitality. When someone enters our community, they should encounter Christ—through the way we welcome them, the way we serve them, the way we treat them as though they are Christ Himself.

> "And whoever welcomes a little child like this in my name welcomes me." (18:5)

**The question is not: "How can I use my gifts to glorify God in spectacular ways?"** The answer is: **If you don't welcome, there is no way you can glorify God.** You don't welcome others, you don't welcome Jesus. If you don't welcome Jesus, you don't welcome God.

Be humble. Be a servant. Welcome the vulnerable. Leave the ninety-nine comfortable sheep to pursue the one who is lost. This is the will of the Father. This is what it means to follow the Son.
