# Hebrews 2:5-3:1

**Title:** Jesus is a Better Man

**Date:** December 5, 2008

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## What is Man?

We live in a culture with competing answers to this question. The movie *The Curious Case of Benjamin Button* suggests a man is simply the sum of his experiences. Others say man is his purpose, or merely a collection of chemicals, or an economic unit driving the machinery of commerce. Still others view humanity as a cosmic accident—the result of blind evolution—or as an impersonal machine fashioned by God for work, or simply as an animal driven by instinct and hormone.

The writer of Hebrews offers a radically different answer.

## The World is for Mankind (Hebrews 2:5-8)

Last week we saw that Jesus is the better messenger because he is God—the exact representation of the Father, God's final word about God. This week we discover a second, equally profound reason: Jesus is a better messenger because he is not only God, but also human.

> It is not to angels that he has subjected the world to come, about which we are speaking. (Hebrews 2:5)

The world was not made for angels. Angels are messengers sent to serve their hearers, much like a postman exists to deliver letters to people and businesses. But the world itself belongs to someone else. The writer quotes Psalm 8:

> What is man that you are mindful of him, the son of man that you care for him? You made him a little lower than the angels; you crowned him with glory and honor and put everything under his feet. In putting everything under him, God left nothing that is not subject to him. (Hebrews 2:6-8)

This is remarkable. Man occupies a paradoxical position in God's creation. In the hierarchy of being, he is low—lower than the angels, mortal and ordinary. Fire and wind are fantastic; man is not. So much so that the psalmist asks in wonder: "What is man that you are mindful of him? Who is he? Nobody much."

Yet simultaneously, man is destined for something infinitely higher. God cares for him. God crowns him with glory and honor—the dignity of kings. God puts everything under his feet. The repetition in verse 8 drives the point home: nothing is excluded from man's dominion.

This is the theology of Genesis 1. The climax of creation is not the heavens or the earth or the vegetation, but man. God did not create man to feed himself; God feeds man. The world exists for humanity's flourishing.

### A Necessary Correction

Some voices in our culture contradict this biblical truth. They argue that mankind is a disease upon the earth—a virus that reproduces uncontrollably and consumes all resources in its path. Popular culture echoes this sentiment: films like *The Day the Earth Stood Still* suggest the world would be better without us. This philosophy has even spawned movements like No Kidding International, which questions whether babies are good news given their environmental cost, suggesting slogans like "Pick Earth over Birth."

Yet this logic is flawed. Yes, we have abused the earth, and yes, humanity contributes to environmental problems. But the solution cannot be the removal of humanity itself—that would be like killing a fly with a nuclear bomb, destroying the very purpose for which creation exists. The answer is not fewer people, but better stewardship. We must recognize our true dignity: you are not a biological accident. Love people. Love children. Love humankind. The earth was made for us.

## Yet We Do Not See It (Hebrews 2:8)

Here is the problem. The psalmist declares that all things are subject to man, but when we open our eyes and look at reality, we do not see this at all:

> Yet at present we do not see everything subject to him. (Hebrews 2:8)

Look at the world around us. The environment is hostile to human flourishing. This is the curse of Genesis 3—the consequence of sin. Consider the wealthiest and most accomplished people we know. Brad Pitt, for all his beauty and success, will die. He is subject to the same limitations and sorrows as everyone else.

Worse still, mankind is subject to sin and death. The premise of F. Scott Fitzgerald's *The Curious Case of Benjamin Button* illustrates our existential predicament: even if we could reverse aging and be born old first, we would still die. We do not see man crowned with glory, sitting at God's right hand.

## But We Do See Jesus (Hebrews 2:9)

Yet there is one man who does fit the description:

> But we see Jesus, who was made a little lower than the angels, now crowned with glory and honor because he suffered death, so that by the grace of God he might taste death for everyone. (Hebrews 2:9)

Jesus is the human who is crowned with glory and honor. He became lower than the angels—became mortal, like us. He tasted death. But unlike us, he did not remain subject to death. Now he sits at the Father's right hand, exalted. He shares in our humanity fully, possessing flesh and blood as we do.

In a sense, the rest of humanity are prototypes of true humanity. Jesus completes what we are meant to be. He is the real human.

### The True Ideal

Who is the ideal person you wish to be like? Not the richest person—not a James Packer or Lachlan Murdoch. Not the smartest—not a Bill Gates. Not the toughest—not a Bruce Willis. Not the most athletic—not a David Beckham. Not the most beautiful—not a Brad Pitt.

The true human, the real ideal, is the one who is kindest and most obedient: Jesus. He is the mortal who did not sin. He is the mortal who rules perfectly. He is other-centered, putting the interests of others before his own. He is willing to die for others. He obeys his Father in heaven.

If you want to become the ultimate human, look to Jesus and be like him.

## Three Life-Changing Implications

### 1. True Brotherhood Through Jesus

Jesus does not leave us to figure this out on our own. He brings us into this new humanity with him:

> In bringing many sons to glory, it was fitting that God, for whom and through whom everything exists, should make the author of their salvation perfect through suffering. (Hebrews 2:10)

Jesus brings many sons to glory—many sons of mankind—to share in his glory. And then:

> Both the one who makes men holy and those who are made holy are of the same family. So Jesus is not ashamed to call them brothers. (Hebrews 2:11)

Through Jesus, we become truly real—truly human. This is an eschatological reality: real love and real brotherhood are found in relationship with Jesus, not in human-made structures. John reminds us that a true friend is one who dies for you. True unity lies in relationship with Jesus.

### 2. He Can Represent and Help Us

Because Jesus became human, he can help us:

> Since the children have flesh and blood, he too shared in their humanity so that by his death he might destroy him who holds the power of death—that is, the devil—and free those who all their lives were held in slavery by their fear of death. For surely it is not angels he helps, but Abraham's descendants. For this reason he had to be made like his brothers in every way, in order that he might become a merciful and faithful high priest in service to God, and that he might make atonement for the sins of the people. (Hebrews 2:14-17)

Notice: he helps not angels, but mankind. The world is not subject to angels; it is subject to man. And so Jesus became like us in every way—so that he might be a merciful and faithful high priest, able to understand our temptations, our struggles, our fear of death.

> He says, "I will declare your name to my brothers; in the presence of the congregation I will sing your praises." (Hebrews 2:12)

Jesus became our brother so he could bring us back to God.

### 3. Fix Your Thoughts on Jesus

The response to all of this is clear:

> Because he himself suffered when he was tempted, he is able to help those who are being tempted. Therefore, holy brothers, who share in the heavenly calling, fix your thoughts on Jesus, the apostle and high priest whom we confess. (Hebrews 2:18 – 3:1)

Because he became man and suffered, he is able to help us in our temptations. He is not a distant God untouched by our struggles. He is the true sent one and the true mediator—both God and man.

This truth stands against an ancient heresy called Docetism, which claimed that Christ's sufferings and human aspects were merely apparent or imaginary. Greek philosophy insisted that if Christ suffered, he could not be divine, and if he was God, he could not suffer. But biblical theology declares something far more glorious: God the Son became incarnate. The climax of the first creation was Adam. The climax of the new creation is Jesus.

---

## Conclusion: What is Man?

So we return to our original question: What is man?

He is not the sum of his experiences. It is okay not to have traveled the world.

He is not a few dollars worth of chemicals.

He is not merely an economic consumer.

He is not a freak of nature or a mere animal driven by instinct.

He is not an impersonal machine.

**Jesus is the man.** The answer to the question of true humanity is found in fixing your eyes on Jesus—the apostle and high priest whom we confess. He is our true ideal, our perfect example, our merciful mediator, and our hope.
