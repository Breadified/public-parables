# Amos 6:1-14

**Title:** Judgment on the Vain
**Date:** December 2015
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Big Idea

God has given Israel its prosperity, but the nation repays His blessing with betrayal—complacent in their wealth, they ignore the suffering of others and forget that their security rests entirely on God's providence.

## 1. The Complacent (Verses 1-6)

> Woe to you who are complacent in Zion,
> and to you who feel secure on Mount Samaria,
> you notable men of the foremost nation,
> to whom the people of Israel come!

The prophet opens with a woe—a dire warning—directed at Israel's elite. By the eighth century BC, Israel had entered a season of unprecedented peace and prosperity. Their armies had conquered neighboring territories. The great powers of the region were preoccupied with their own conflicts, leaving Israel undisturbed. The northern and southern kingdoms enjoyed relative stability, and international peace treaties secured their borders. Israel felt invulnerable.

This confidence is evident in verse 2, where Amos invites the people to examine the fortified cities they had subjugated—Kalneh, Hamath, and Gath in Philistia. Yet despite their military dominance, Israel was not truly stronger than these nations. Their sense of security rested on a false foundation.

Within this cocoon of safety, the wealthy elite indulged themselves relentlessly:

> You lie on beds adorned with ivory and lounge on your couches.
> You dine on choice lambs and fattened calves.
> You strum away on your harps like David
> and improvise on musical instruments.
> You drink wine by the bowlful and use the finest lotions.

What a perfect world they had created. Day after day, they lived in luxury—not on special occasions, but as their ordinary existence. They surrounded themselves with expensive furnishings, the finest foods, and endless entertainment. It paints a picture of endless leisure and self-indulgence.

Yet here lies the spiritual rot: **"But you do not grieve over the ruin of Joseph."**

The name "Joseph" refers to the northern tribes of Ephraim and Manasseh, which formed the heart of Israel's territory. But it also evokes a deeper betrayal. In Genesis 37, Joseph's own brothers—driven by jealousy—threw him into a pit and sold him into slavery. While he screamed for his life, they sat down and ate their meal. They felt no grief, no compassion for the brother they had betrayed.

This is the heartlessness of the complacent rich. While the elite feasted and made music, the ordinary people of Israel were being destroyed by those in power. Just as detailed in chapter 5:

> There are those who hate the one who upholds justice in court and detest the one who tells the truth.
> You levy a straw tax on the poor and impose a tax on their grain...
> There are those who oppress the innocent and take bribes and deprive the poor of justice in the courts.

The powerful had access to the corridors of authority. They could confiscate wealth from the vulnerable, manipulate the legal system, and extort the helpless. And throughout it all, they felt no sorrow for the suffering they caused.

In every age, this pattern repeats. The truly dangerous people in society are often not the obviously violent, but those who wield institutional power without compassion. Whether through government, commerce, or law, the complacent wealthy make decisions that devastate others—yet they never have to face the consequences. They live behind walls and privilege, insulated from the reality of those their choices harm. The poor suffer the fallout of policies made by leaders who will never walk through their neighborhoods or send their children to their schools.

**The crucial question remains: Do those in power grieve for the weak?**

## 2. Judgment on the Complacent (Verses 7-11)

Because of their complacency and heartlessness, God will judge Israel.

> Therefore you will be among the first to go into exile;
> your feasting and lounging will end.

The Israelites believed their military victories meant permanent safety. They thought the defeated buffer zones around them guaranteed their security. They did not understand that destroying these smaller nations simply meant the major powers were now closer, with nothing standing between Israel and destruction.

The judgment comes swiftly and absolutely:

> The Sovereign Lord has sworn by himself—the Lord God Almighty declares:
> "I abhor the pride of Jacob and detest his fortresses;
> I will deliver up the city and everything in it."

Israel's pride is not the healthy pride of a child earning a school prize, but arrogant, complacent, vain pride—the pride of those who have forgotten God. The fortresses they trusted in, the very symbols of their security, God abhors.

The devastation extends to every person and every possession:

> If ten people are left in one house, they too will die.

Even the remnant mentioned in earlier passages—a meager ten survivors—will not escape. Even these few will perish. The judgment is total.

The corpses themselves become so numerous and taboo that the living dare not even speak God's name:

> And if the relative who comes to carry the bodies out of the house asks anyone who might be hiding there, "Is anyone else with you?" he will say, "No," then he will go on to say, "Hush! We must not mention the name of the Lord."

God's name becomes a curse, a jinx to be avoided in whispers. The judgment reaches even into the spiritual realm—God's own name is silenced.

The destruction is comprehensive:

> For the Lord has given the command,
> and he will smash the great house into pieces
> and the small house into bits.

The ivory beds they loved, the fortresses they boasted in, the homes they adorned with luxury—all reduced to rubble. Every element of their pride, gone. This is not the judgment of a moment, but the slow, grinding collapse of an entire civilization. The Assyrians would eventually accomplish this very thing, pressing Israel from north to south, from border to border, until the kingdom fell.

## 3. God Has Been Providential (Verses 12-14)

To understand the weight of this judgment, we must see what God has actually done for Israel:

> Do horses run on the rocky crags?
> Does one plow the sea with oxen?

These rhetorical questions are absurd. Horses do not scramble across jagged rock; oxen cannot be yoked to plow the ocean. Yet this is precisely what the Israelites accomplished—not through their own strength, but through God's miraculous intervention.

In their military campaigns, their "horses" did indeed overrun the impossible terrain. Their "oxen" did plow ground that seemed impassable. But these were no ordinary military victories. They were acts of God. The metaphor recalls the prophecy over Israel in Numbers 24:8:

> God brought them out of Egypt;
> they have the strength of a wild ox.
> They devour hostile nations and break their bones in pieces.

Israel had become a tiny nation surrounded by enemies, yet they survived and thrived. How could this be, except by God's hand? He had given them peace. He had given them prosperity. He had enlarged their territory and blessed them with wealth and luxuries beyond measure.

Yet Israel made a catastrophic mistake. They interpreted their prosperity as independence from God. They looked at their military successes and credited their own prowess:

> You who rejoice in the conquest of Lo Debar
> and say, "Did we not take Karnaim by our own strength?"

Lo Debar means "nothing"—they were proud of conquering insignificant territories. Karnaim means "horns"—symbols of power. But this boasting revealed the deepest spiritual blindness: they attributed their victories to themselves, not to God.

As a result, God will reverse everything:

> For the Lord God Almighty declares,
> "I will stir up a nation against you, Israel,
> that will oppress you all the way
> from Lebo Hamath to the valley of the Arabah."

Just as God had given them victory from border to border, now an enemy will press them from border to border. Everything they built will be undone.

## The Deeper Pattern: Pride and Complacency

This tragedy echoes throughout Scripture. The Rich Fool in Luke 12 made the same mistake:

> Then he said, 'This is what I'll do. I will tear down my barns and build bigger ones, and there I will store my surplus grain. And I'll say to myself, "You have plenty of grain laid up for many years. Take life easy; eat, drink and be merry."'
>
> But God said to him, 'You fool! This very night your life will be demanded from you. Then who will get what you have prepared for yourself?'
>
> This is how it will be with whoever stores up things for themselves but is not rich toward God.

James reminds us:

> God opposes the proud but shows favor to the humble.

The sin of complacency is taking for granted what God has graciously given. It is using His blessings only for ourselves while denying them to others. It is forgetting that every good thing is a gift from His hand, and that we hold it all in trust.

## Application: A Warning for Us

We must not be complacent. We live in a time of material abundance and spiritual privilege. We have been blessed materially with comfort and security. More importantly, we have been blessed spiritually with the Gospel—the good news of what Jesus has accomplished for us on the cross.

Jesus, though divine and could have claimed all power and riches for Himself, did the opposite. He emptied Himself. He became a helpless infant so that one day He might die for us. He took the suffering we deserved upon Himself. This is the miracle of miracles—the impossible made possible through His love.

We dare not be complacent about that grace.

Just as Israel was called to use their prosperity with justice and compassion, we are called to steward our blessings wisely. We are not to hoard for ourselves alone. We are to be "rich toward God"—storing up treasures of faith, generosity, and kingdom work.

At this season—Christmas and summer—we have unique opportunities. Many who are too busy during the year to think about God now have time. They are more open to hearing the Gospel, more willing to attend church. We cannot rest from these opportunities. The programs and activities of this season are not distractions from our faith; they are chances to share the Good News with those who are finally listening.

Let us not take these blessings for granted. Let us seize the moment. Let us ensure that our complacency does not cost others the chance to hear about Jesus.
