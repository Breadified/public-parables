# Psalm 73:1-28

**Title:** God, I Am Missing Out!
**Date:** September 6, 2009
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction: The Experience of Missing Out

We've all felt it—the sting of missing out. A missed bargain. Overlooked praise or reward. The psalmist knew this feeling intimately, but in a far deeper way. He experienced the troubling gap between what God promises and what reality seems to deliver.

This psalm is structured around three moments of certainty, marked by the word "surely" (verses 1, 13, and 18). These turning points guide us through the psalmist's spiritual crisis and eventual resolution.

## 1. The Contradicting Experience (Verses 1-12)

### The Promise

> Surely God is good to Israel,
> to those who are pure in heart. (Psalm 73:1)

This is no new idea—it's the bedrock of Israel's faith. Every Israelite knew it. God had chosen Abraham from obscurity and promised him a nation. Centuries later, He rescued that nation from slavery and led them to Canaan, a land flowing with milk and honey. Like a father to his children, God promised His people blessing, land, offspring, prosperity, and health.

To be pure in heart meant to follow the true God, not idols. So logically, those who follow the one true God should experience His goodness.

### The Painful Reality

But the psalmist's experience contradicts this. Listen to what he confesses:

> But as for me, my feet had almost slipped;
> I had nearly lost my foothold. (Psalm 73:2)

Why? Because he sees something that troubles him deeply:

> For I envied the arrogant
> when I saw the prosperity of the wicked.
> They have no struggles;
> their bodies are healthy and strong.
> They are free from the burdens common to man;
> they are not plagued by human ills. (Psalm 73:3-5)

The wicked are thriving. The arrogant are prospering. Meanwhile, the righteous—those who follow God—struggle. The psalmist feels he has missed out.

### The Wicked Double Down

What makes it worse is that their prosperity doesn't humble them. Instead, they become more arrogant:

> Therefore pride is their necklace;
> they clothe themselves with violence.
> From their callous hearts comes iniquity;
> the evil conceits of their minds know no limits. (Psalm 73:6-7)

They speak with malice and arrogance, threatening oppression:

> They scoff, and speak with malice;
> in their arrogance they threaten oppression.
> Their mouths lay claim to heaven,
> and their tongues take possession of the earth.
> Therefore their people turn to them
> and drink up waters in abundance. (Psalm 73:8-10)

They even mock God Himself:

> They say, "How can God know?
> Does the Most High have knowledge?" (Psalm 73:11)

Yet they remain unaffected. Verse 12 captures the injustice:

> This is what the wicked are like—
> always carefree, they increase in wealth. (Psalm 73:12)

### The Unfair Reversal

To compound the injustice, the good are rewarded with evil while the wicked flourish:

> All day long I have been plagued;
> I have been punished every morning. (Psalm 73:14)

Perhaps the psalmist suffered from literal disease or affliction—waking each day to pain. It felt profoundly unfair.

This is a real problem we see throughout history. Consider Ted Kennedy: a wealthy senator from an influential family, yet involved in the 1969 Chappaquiddick Island incident. Driving with a young political aide, Mary Jo Kopechne, Kennedy's car went off a bridge. He escaped and swam to safety but didn't report the accident until after her body was discovered the next day. The circumstances were troubling—he claimed he didn't knock on nearby houses for help, yet there were at least four, some with lights on. He slept through the night without reporting the accident. Worse still, a diver later revealed Kopechne was found in an air pocket and would have survived if rescued within two hours.

The outcome? Ted Kennedy lived to 77, had a distinguished 46-year Senate career, served nine times, and died a respected figure. Mary Jo Kopechne never married, never had children, never launched the political career she was suited for. She died at 29.

That's unfair. She missed out on everything while he lived fully, largely unaccountable.

The psalmist faces the same spiritual injustice. Good people suffer. Bad people prosper. Where is God's goodness?

### A Contemporary Application

This struggle is as real today as in ancient Israel. As Christians, we envy those in the world:

**Freedom vs. Duty**
They choose their friends freely. We have the obligation to love one another whether we feel like it or not. They don't carry guilt about neglecting relationships. We feel guilty when we miss church or Bible study.

**Money**
When they have money, they spend it as they wish. We're called to stewardship, not selfishness.

**Education and Ambition**
They can devote their entire week to studying for exams and pursuing their goals. We give up Sunday mornings for worship. We sacrifice study time for church service and Bible study nights. We surrender our full potential for others.

**Sexual Freedom**
They're free from Christian moral restrictions. They can have sex whenever and with whoever they wish. In a 2009 *Good Weekend* article, Amanda Hooton critiques chastity as "a denial of life," questioning whether such restrictions can lead to happiness. The freedom to pursue sexual expression without guilt or accountability seems like an obvious advantage.

**Peace of Conscience**
They live without a guilty conscience. No Bible study leaders call to check on them. No community holds them accountable. The world feels lighter.

For non-Christians struggling with this, it's even more acute. A British missionary once told me how she longed to do what her friends did—land great jobs, leave them without explanation, travel the world, have relationships without responsibility, enjoy money without justifying their choices to anyone. Instead, she had to answer to others, justify her decisions, live under spiritual authority.

**God, I am missing out! It's unfair!**

## 2. The Tempting Conclusion (Verses 13-17)

Given this reality, the psalmist arrives at a logical but devastating conclusion:

### The False Conclusion

> Surely in vain have I kept my heart pure;
> in vain have I washed my hands in innocence. (Psalm 73:13)

All his effort to stay pure and follow God's ways—wasted. There's no reward. Not only does God fail to bless him as promised, but he's missing out *while* trying to be faithful. It's a double loss: no future reward and no present joy.

The psalmist is trapped in despair:

> If I had said, "I will speak thus,"
> I would have betrayed your children. (Psalm 73:15)

Should he voice this doubt publicly? No—it would destroy others' faith. Better to stay silent.

But silence is its own torture:

> When I tried to understand all this,
> it was oppressive to me. (Psalm 73:16)

He's living a lie so profound that he can't even speak about it. The contradiction is paralyzing. He feels hopeless.

### The Turning Point

Then something shifts:

> Till I entered the sanctuary of God;
> then I understood their final destiny. (Psalm 73:17)

The sanctuary—the holy place within the temple where God's presence dwelt—becomes the scene of spiritual transformation. The text moves directly from his depression to his newfound understanding without explaining what happened. But something in God's presence changed his perspective. He grasped something crucial: their final destiny.

*A note on the sanctuary:* In the Old Testament, the sanctuary was where God's presence was located. Today, we don't replicate that in church buildings. We don't offer sacrifices. Our gathering isn't "temple activity." This is why referring to the collection time as an "offering" can be misleading, though the language is traditional. What matters is that the psalmist experienced God's presence in a way that reoriented his entire worldview.

## 3. The True Conclusion (Verses 18-28)

### The Wicked Face Judgment

Now the psalmist sees clearly:

> Surely you place them on slippery ground;
> you cast them down to ruin.
> How suddenly are they destroyed,
> completely swept away by terrors!
> As a dream when one awakes,
> so when you arise, O Lord,
> you will despise them as fantasies. (Psalm 73:18-20)

The prosperity he envied? It's a momentary illusion. The wicked stand on slippery ground, moments from ruin. Their apparent triumph is like a dream—vivid while sleeping, but dissolving instantly upon waking. God will expose them as the fantasies they are. Justice is coming.

### The Psalmist's Confession

Reflecting on his entire journey, the psalmist acknowledges his foolishness:

> When my heart was grieved
> and my spirit embittered,
> I was senseless and ignorant;
> I was a brute beast before you. (Psalm 73:21-22)

To doubt God's goodness was brutish. He lacked spiritual understanding. But then comes the resolution:

### The True Treasure

> Yet I am always with you;
> you hold me by my right hand.
> You guide me with your counsel,
> and afterward you will take me into glory. (Psalm 73:23-24)

Here's what he discovered in the sanctuary: God's presence. Not a future promise alone, but *now*—"always with you." God holds him firmly. He's guided by divine counsel. And there's a future: God will lead him into glory.

> Whom have I in heaven but you?
> And earth has nothing I desire besides you.
> My flesh and my heart may fail,
> but God is the strength of my heart
> and my portion forever. (Psalm 73:25-26)

The wicked had fit, strong bodies (verse 4). But the psalmist's strength comes from God Himself, not his physical condition. God is his "portion"—his inheritance, his greatest treasure.

### The Final Word

> Behold! Those who are far from you will perish;
> you destroy all who are unfaithful to you.
> But as for me, it is good to be near God.
> I have made the Sovereign LORD my refuge;
> I will tell of all your deeds. (Psalm 73:27-28)

The tables will turn. The wicked will perish. The faithful will inherit glory. And the psalmist's conclusion? It's good—truly good—to be near God.

## The Answer for Us

The psalmist's crisis mirrors our own. We too wonder: *Am I missing out?* The answer is no. Here's why:

### 1. God Is With You

Historically, God came to us in Jesus, the true temple. He dwelt among us. Today He's with us through His Word and Spirit. Not mystically, but truly—His promises are real, His words are sure. When you read Scripture and understand it, when you work out the gospel's implications in your life, God is genuinely at work in you. It's as real as a beloved's letter—the words on the page are their true presence.

### 2. Judgment Is Coming

Like the psalmist, we don't fully understand suffering now. We're "brute beasts" in our comprehension of God's larger purposes. But judgment is coming. The day of reckoning will make all things clear. The good will receive good. The evil will receive evil. Fairness will reign. This promise sustains us through present confusion.

## For Those Struggling Today

If you're wrestling with this feeling of missing out:

**Don't envy those outside God's family.** Life away from God is empty, however it appears. Judgment awaits them.

**Persevere.** Hold on. Help is coming. Even giants of faith like Martin Luther and others have battled doubt. Struggle is normal. It's not a sign of weak faith.

**Remember what you possess.** You have God's presence. You have His promises. You have the certainty of His justice. These aren't small things. They're everything.

A 37-year-old Christian man once wrote: "I'm a virgin, and yes, I'm romantically clueless. But I'm certain that even I could show affection and love without sex. My singleness doesn't define me. I'd love to experience marriage and intimacy, but I'm not distraught by the possibility I might miss out. Because their absence doesn't mean I can't live a rich and full life."

That's faith. That's the psalmist's discovery. You're not missing out. You're just waiting—for a glory that will far exceed anything the world offers.
