# Matthew 7:7-14

**Title:** The Generous God
**Date:** January 2003
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## What Kind of God Are We Relying On?

When we talk about fully relying on God, the first question we must ask is: what sort of God are we relying on? The answer Jesus gives us is clear—this God is generous.

Think of the most generous person you know. Perhaps someone who hosted a birthday party with great food, entertainment, and sent every guest home with a gift. Or consider Bill Gates, who gave away 30 billion dollars to combat diseases like malaria in the developing world. These are remarkable examples of human generosity.

But God's generosity surpasses all human comparison.

## Ask for Righteousness

> Ask and it will be given to you; seek and you will find; knock and the door will be opened to you. For everyone who asks receives; he who seeks finds; and to him who knocks, the door will be opened. (Matthew 7:7-8)

These three images—asking, seeking, knocking—paint a picture of persistent pursuit, like approaching a headmaster to request better exam marks. But here's the crucial difference: unlike any human authority, what you ask for will actually be given to you.

Yet this passage means far more than simply asking for life's comforts. It's not about marks, iPods, or dogs. This invitation to ask, seek, and knock exists within the context of God's kingdom and righteousness.

### The Kingdom and Righteousness

Just before this passage, Jesus taught:

> Seek first his kingdom and his righteousness, and all these things will be given to you. (Matthew 6:33)

The Kingdom of God is heaven—the place where God reigns, where there is no fear, no violence, no bullying, no tears, no cancer, no death. It's the place of perfect peace and wholeness.

Righteousness means to be right before God. This matters deeply because only the righteous can enter God's kingdom. And here's our problem: we are not righteous.

### The Problem: We Are Not Righteous

We may think of ourselves as good people with good hearts, but when we sit quietly and examine ourselves honestly, we know we fall short. We sin in what we say, what we do, and what we fail to do. Most tellingly, we are not generous—we are stingy.

Look at how we live. We hold back from helping others at school, worried they might outperform us. When friends or strangers ask for help, we say we're too busy, we can't help, we don't want to help. We organize birthday dinners and expect others to pay for themselves. We see neighbors in need but pass by. We know we are not generous.

Even the wealthy among us—like Bill Gates with his 67 billion dollars—keep far more than they could ever use in a lifetime. We see this pattern everywhere: in ourselves, in our families, in our nations. We are not righteous. We are not generous.

### God Is Generous

But here is the good news. There is one man who is truly generous: Jesus.

Jesus left his comfort in heaven and came to earth, lived in poverty, and gave all that he had. He was the one man who did no wrong, who was always generous. On the cross, he gave away his reputation, his dignity, his health. When he had nothing left to give, in the ultimate act of generosity, he gave us his life itself.

Jesus possesses the righteousness that surpasses all understanding. And he gives that righteousness to us—freely, without cost—so that we can bridge the gap between ourselves and God.

### Bridging the Gap

Think about it logically. God is like a holy eye—perfectly righteous, unable to tolerate evil. We are like dirt—selfish, ungracious, unrighteous. When a speck of dirt enters an eye, the eye flinches and spits it out. No matter how small the dirt, the reaction is violent: "Get it out! Get it out!" God and sinful humanity cannot occupy the same space.

But Jesus comes and says: take my righteousness. Now you can stand in the same place with God. That is the great news.

## The Guarantee: No Catch

> Which of you, if his son asks for bread, will give him a stone? Or if he asks for a fish, will give him a snake? If you, then, though you are evil, know how to give good gifts to your children, how much more will your Father in heaven give good gifts to those who ask him! (Matthew 7:9-11)

What's the catch? There is no catch. This is guaranteed.

God is like the good father who feeds his children well. His righteousness is what he desperately wants to give us. If you have younger siblings, you know how toddlers refuse to eat bread or fish—they want dessert and lollies. Parents coax them patiently to eat proper food.

But imagine if your child looked at you and asked for rice, fish, and milk. Would you foolishly give them a snake instead? Would you make them jump through hoops to prove how desperately they want it? Of course not. You would go straight to the rice pot, pile up bowls, get the best fish—everything for them.

And that's just you, a limited human with limitations and selfishness. How much more will your Father in heaven give good gifts to those who ask him?

God is not going to make you jump through hoops to gain the kingdom of heaven through good works, special diets, or rituals. If you think you must earn God's love, you misunderstand who he is. He has been waiting for you to ask. He has been standing here with a spoon in his hand saying, "I just want to give you the best. I've been waiting all your life, worried that you won't gain righteousness and find yourself separated from me. Please, ask."

We can never be righteous on our own—not in the way God requires. The eye cannot be weak, useless, or blind and still be God. What we need is a generous God who gives us his own righteousness. No catch. No hoops. All done for you. That's Jesus.

## Your Choice: The Two Gates

But God can do everything for you—the offering is complete, the door is open. In the end, you have to decide whether to accept it.

> Enter through the narrow gate. For wide is the gate and broad is the road that leads to destruction, and many enter through it. But small is the gate and narrow the road that leads to life, and only a few find it. (Matthew 7:13-14)

Two gates stand before us.

The wide gate is broad, easy, and popular. Many people travel that road because it requires no righteousness, no change, no difficult choice. This is the automatic default—simply carry on as you are. It's comfortable. But it leads to destruction.

The narrow gate is narrow because only a few find it, only a few ask for it. It's unpopular and difficult. Jesus commands: "Enter through the narrow gate." This is the hard choice—the choice to repent, to ask for righteousness, to accept his generosity.

## The Cost of Generosity

Consider our migrant parents who left everything—their support networks, their families, their friends, their schools—to start over in a new country with nothing. They worked day and night so we could have bread, fish, and education. They love us and would die for us, yet they can only provide for this life.

Jesus did even more. He worked harder than any parent, harder than any human could imagine. He placed life—eternal life—before us. It cost him everything. He was generous with us because he paid an awfully lot for that generosity.

Fully rely on this God who gives so generously. He has proven it through history—he gave his life for us.

What will happen after you die? For those who choose the narrow gate, there is a guarantee: heaven. Make that decision today before it's too late.

## A Prayer for Those Entering the Narrow Gate

Dear Father God,

I am guilty of rebelling against you and ignoring you. I deserve your anger, not your mercy. But thank you for sending Jesus to die for me so that I may be forgiven. Thank you that he rose from the dead to give me new life. Please forgive me and change me, so that I may live with Jesus as my ruler for the rest of my life.

In Jesus' name I pray, amen.
