# Hebrews 10:19-39

**Title:** Persevere: Because of the Future
**Date:** 28 September 2014
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Why Do We Give Up?

Psychology reveals a striking pattern: depression, according to researcher Rollo May, is fundamentally "the inability to see or construct a future." When people cannot envision tomorrow, they lose their will to live. Complicated Grief Syndrome demonstrates this same principle—individuals trapped by trauma cannot imagine life beyond their pain and therefore abandon hope.

This insight cuts to the heart of why Christians sometimes fall away. We don't typically abandon Jesus because we've discovered logical flaws in Scripture or intellectual problems with the faith. Rather, we give up because we've lost vision of our future with Him. We get distracted by lifestyle choices—the pursuit of wealth, status, romantic relationships—and forget what we're living for.

Consider the tragic exodus of Iraqi Christians fleeing ISIS. These believers didn't leave because they lacked intellectual reasons to stay. They left because they had lost all vision of a future in their homeland. With neighbors who had betrayed them, living as a persecuted minority in a hostile country, they could see no path forward. Their past held only trauma; their future held only darkness. So they departed, many permanently.

The antidote to giving up is simple: we must recover our vision of the future. As survival researcher Laurence Gonzales discovered, it is "at this point, following what I call the vision, that the survivor's will to live becomes firmly fixed." Athletes like Michael Jordan and Tiger Woods don't endure grueling training by focusing on their current pain. They visualize victory—the winning shot, the green jacket, the cheering crowd. They see themselves at the finish line.

This is why the author of Hebrews calls us back, again and again, to remember our future with Jesus.

## Persevere in Hope

### Confidence in the Future (Hebrews 10:19-23)

> 19 Therefore, brothers and sisters, since we have confidence/boldness (with respects to the holy entrance) to enter the Most Holy Place by the blood of Jesus, 20 by a new and living way opened for us through the curtain, that is, his body, 21 and since we have a great priest over the house of God, 22 let us draw near to God with a sincere (true) heart and with the full assurance (of faith) that faith brings, 23 Let us hold unswervingly to the hope we profess, for he who promised is faithful.

The promise of verse 19 anchors everything that follows: because of Jesus's blood and His completed sacrifice, we have confidence to enter God's holy presence. In the Old Testament temple, only the high priest could enter the inner sanctuary—the Most Holy Place—once a year, and only with blood as atonement. The thick curtain separating the holy space from the people symbolized our separation from God. But when Jesus died, that curtain was torn in two. His body became the way. His sacrifice made the impossible possible.

Notice that verse 19 says we have confidence *to enter*—not that we have already entered. The promise is so certain, so guaranteed, that we can speak of it as if it's already done. Like placing your name on a reserved seat before the ceremony begins, our place in heaven is secured and waiting.

God preserves the converted in perseverance to the end. This is not dependent on our strength or our consistency. Psalm 37:23-24 assures us that the Lord delights in our way and makes our steps firm—though we stumble, we will not fall, for He upholds us. Jesus promised His sheep eternal life, and no one can snatch them from His hand (John 10:28-29). Romans 8:30 traces the unbreakable chain: those God predestined, He called; those He called, He justified; those He justified, He glorified.

The theologian John Calvin summarized Christian doctrine in what became known as TULIP—Total Depravity, Unconditional Election, Limited Atonement, Irresistible Grace, and Perseverance of the Saints. The final point is crucial: believers persevere to the end not through their own resources but through the Father's elective purpose, the Son's intercession, and the Spirit's work within them.

Because of this past guarantee, we can hold unswervingly to our hope. Our future is not uncertain. God's promise is faithful.

### Meet Together (Hebrews 10:24-25)

> 24 And let us consider how we may spur one another on toward love and good deeds, 25 not giving up meeting together, as some are in the habit of doing, but encouraging one another—and all the more as you see the Day approaching.

Why does the author command us not to forsake gathering together? Because we are forgetful people. We easily lose sight of the future when we're isolated. When we gather—whether in staff meetings at work, family dinners, or church—we remind each other of our common purpose. The discipline of meeting together is the discipline of keeping our eyes on the same goal.

Some people struggle with faithfulness in church attendance. Some aren't yet committed—we'd love to persuade you of the importance of knowing Jesus personally. Others know it's important but face difficult circumstances—please let us know how we can help. And yes, some simply lack the motivation to show up—we all need that sometimes.

But notice what happens when we don't gather: we persevere in something else. We drift. We lose the vision. The author calls us to "spur one another on"—a slightly confronting word, because sometimes we need that healthy challenge. We need each other to remember what we're living for.

## Do Not Persevere in Sin

### The Danger of Deliberate Apostasy (Hebrews 10:26-31)

> 26 If we deliberately keep on sinning after we have received the knowledge of the truth, no sacrifice for sins is left, 27 but only a fearful expectation of judgment and of raging fire that will consume the enemies of God. 28 Anyone who rejected the law of Moses died without mercy on the testimony of two or three witnesses. 29 How much more severely do you think someone deserves to be punished who has trampled the Son of God underfoot, who has treated as an unholy thing the blood of the covenant that sanctified them, and who has insulted the Spirit of grace?

This is not about occasional sin. We all sin; 1 John 1:8-10 makes clear that any Christian claiming sinlessness deceives themselves. Rather, this speaks of the sin of apostasy—deliberately abandoning God, deserting the cause you once committed to, giving up on following Jesus altogether.

The logic is sobering: if Christ's complete sacrifice for sin—every drop of blood, every effort made—is not enough for you, then nothing will be. There is nothing more God can do. The author strengthens this warning by comparing it to the Old Testament law: anyone who rejected Moses's law died without mercy on the testimony of two or three witnesses (Deuteronomy 17:2-7). But rejecting Christ is infinitely more serious. It's like stabbing in the back a friend who gave everything for you.

In my years of ministry, I've known a few people who've fallen away from Christ. Their reasons are rarely intellectual. It's extraordinarily rare to hear an ex-Christian say, "I discovered the Bible is false" or "The faith is logically unsustainable." Instead, their departure is almost always rooted in lifestyle—the seductive pull of wealth, the desire to build an empire, the romance of sexual freedom. They want to live carefree and inconsequential lives, forgetting that choosing such a life *is* consequential.

On the day of judgment, their money cannot save them. Their lover cannot save them. Only Christ saves, and if they've rejected Him, they face the living God alone.

## Remember Your Beginning

### Look Back to Move Forward (Hebrews 10:32-34)

> 32 Remember those earlier days after you had received the light, when you endured in a great conflict full of suffering. 33 Sometimes you were publicly exposed to insult and persecution; at other times you stood side by side with those who were so treated. 34 You suffered along with those in prison and joyfully accepted the confiscation of your property, because you knew that you yourselves had better and lasting possessions.

Here's the genius of the author's appeal: when it's hard to imagine the future, look back to the past. Remember when you first became a Christian? Do you recall that day when everything suddenly made sense—when you finally understood that you were sinful and condemned, but that someone else had paid the price for you? Do you remember the relief, the clarity, the comfort of knowing that Jesus loved you so much He gave His life for you?

Do you remember standing up for your faith despite opposition? Perhaps your parents thought you were foolish to become a Christian. Perhaps friends mocked you. Yet you kept insisting on going to church, kept following Jesus, even when it cost you. You endured public insult and persecution. You stood with others who suffered. You joyfully accepted loss—of property, of comfort, of ease—because you knew something better was coming.

Life may have grown complicated since then. Church politics have disappointed you—remember, we follow Christ, not Christians. Believers have let you down. You've grown busy, lost your first love, made foolish decisions. But it's never too late to repent and find forgiveness in Christ.

The author's logic is profound: if you persevered then, you will persevere now. The future is hard to imagine, but the past proves you can endure. Base your confidence not on feelings but on memory.

## Hold Fast to the End

### Do Not Throw Away Your Confidence (Hebrews 10:35-39)

> 35 So do not throw away your confidence; it will be richly rewarded. 36 You need to persevere so that when you have done the will of God, you will receive what he has promised. 37 For, In just a little while, he who is coming will come and will not delay. 38 And, But my righteous one will live by faith. And I take no pleasure in the one who shrinks back. 39 But we do not belong to those who shrink back and are destroyed, but to those who have faith and are saved.

The call is clear: hold fast. Do not throw away your confidence. Why? Because it will be richly rewarded. Perseverance is not punishment—it's the path to inheritance.

Yes, the Christian life is difficult. There's the constant tension between godliness and compromise. There's the missing out on experiences others enjoy. There's the vigilance required against temptation. There's the question, "Why do I always have to be the sensible one?" There are freedoms you won't experience. It would be easier to simply give up.

But consider what you're persevering *for*: a holy entrance into God's presence. God Himself will be there. Sin will be no more. You'll have new bodies that won't be weak and tired. While destruction falls on the world, you'll be safe in the clouds. There will be relief, comfort, joy, and confidence beyond anything this world offers.

True perseverance is not intense effort that burns out. It's stamina—the discipline of showing up, day after day, year after year. As Woody Allen said, "80% of success in life is showing up." It's not the passionate person who runs out of steam; it's the person with ridiculous, sickening work ethic. It's not the sprinter but the marathon runner.

The Christian life is a 50-year training course. We persevere because our fathers persevered. We're part of a chain stretching back through generations, and we're responsible for passing the faith to the next generation. The main game is not intensity but stability—simple disciplines maintained over the long term.

Church, in this sense, is not an optional activity. It's the huddle before we face the world. It's how we remind each other of the future. It's how we train the next generation. It's grit, discipline, and self-control practiced together.

The warning in this passage is real, but it's not for you if you are of the light, if you are loved by God, if you are loved by Jesus. The fate of apostasy belongs to those outside who have been foolish. But you—you are called to remember the earlier days after you had received the light. You belong to those who have faith and are saved.

## A Vision for Perseverance

One final story: Ali Hussein, the sole survivor of an ISIS massacre, was lined up for execution by a firing squad. A bullet grazed his temple and he fell into the mass grave. Lying still among the bodies for hours, he waited for the extremists to leave. Then he fled into the darkness, hiding by the Tigris River, eating plants and worms to survive.

What kept him alive? A vision of the future. He saw his daughter in his mind, heard her voice calling, "Father, father." At that moment, he said, "I had a great will to live." Over the next three weeks, he moved from family to family. When he finally reunited with his daughter three weeks later, she didn't recognize him—he was so thin and bearded. But he had survived because he could see beyond the present horror to a future worth living for.

This is the power of vision. This is why the author of Hebrews calls us to remember the future—to see ourselves entering the holy presence, to see Jesus welcoming us home, to see the reward of our faithfulness. Hold fast to that vision. Do not shrink back. Persevere. For in just a little while, He who is coming will come and will not delay.
