# Ecclesiastes 3:1-22

**Title:** The Limits of Time - Living According to the Times

**Date:** 4 May 2014

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Understanding Time's Rhythm

Timing determines everything in life. Success or failure often comes down to being in the right place at the right time. Think of Bill Gates—he wasn't the only brilliant, ambitious person in his generation, but he was born in 1955, old enough to seize the computer revolution's earliest opportunities yet young enough to take the leap. The same pattern appears across Silicon Valley's founders: Steve Jobs (1955), Paul Allen (1953), Bill Joy and Scott McNealy (1954). Their genius mattered, but so did their timing.

The Teacher in Ecclesiastes understands this profoundly. He wants us to grasp what Solomon discovered about right timing—and it begins with a crucial observation.

## Time Under the Sun Is Cyclical

> There is a time for everything, and a season for every activity under heaven. (Ecclesiastes 3:1)

The Teacher paints life in agricultural rhythms:

> A time to be born and a time to die, a time to plant and a time to uproot, a time to kill and a time to heal, a time to tear down and a time to build, a time to weep and a time to laugh, a time to mourn and a time to dance, a time to scatter stones and a time to gather them, a time to embrace and a time to refrain, a time to search and a time to give up, a time to keep and a time to throw away, a time to tear and a time to mend, a time to be silent and a time to speak, a time to love and a time to hate, a time for war and a time for peace. (Ecclesiastes 3:2-8)

There's a rhythm here—seasons returning, cycles repeating. But what's the Teacher's conclusion?

> What does the worker gain from his toil? I have seen the burden God has laid on humanity. (Ecclesiastes 3:9-10)

It's a burden. An endless cycle.

Consider how life feels: Another school term begins—another cycle of homework, excursions, classes. Then university: more classes, more assignments, more exams. Then work: another day, another client, another house sold. For parents: another nappy, another feeding, another doctor's appointment. Even holidays follow the same pattern. The cycles continue until retirement, and even then—swimming, breakfast, durian shopping, nap, dinner, sleep, repeat.

This is why Eastern religions like Buddhism and Hinduism teach reincarnation—endless cycles of rebirth and death. Their solution? Nirvana—literally "blown out," extinguished, escape into nothingness. Anything would be better than this drudgery. Everything feels meaningless.

But here's where the Christian understanding differs radically:

> So I saw that there is nothing better for a person than to enjoy their work, because that is their lot. (Ecclesiastes 3:22)

## Time Under the Son Is Linear

The Teacher isn't fatalistic like Eastern religion. Look at what he tells us:

> He has made everything beautiful in its time. He has also set eternity in the hearts of men; yet they cannot fathom what God has done from beginning to end. (Ecclesiastes 3:11)

Time isn't a circle with no beginning or end. Time is a line. There is a beginning. There will be an end.

> I know that everything God does will endure forever; nothing can be added to it and nothing taken from it. God does it so that people will fear him. Whatever is has already been, and what will be has been before; and God will call the past to account. (Ecclesiastes 3:14-15)

Even though injustice seems to reign now:

> And I saw something else under the sun: In the place of judgment—wickedness was there, in the place of justice—wickedness was there. (Ecclesiastes 3:16)

God promises:

> God will bring into judgment both the righteous and the wicked, for there will be a time for every activity, a time to judge every deed. (Ecclesiastes 3:17)

This changes everything. The monotony will end. The meaninglessness will end. There will be judgment for the deeds done and undone. Time is purposeful because it's moving toward an end.

## The Error of Wanting Everything

We fail to accept our times. We're discontent with each season:

- When we should be studying, we want to be adults. As adults, we resist independence and cling to childhood (the "failure to launch" phenomenon).
- When too young to date, we dream of marriage. When it's time to marry, we want to stay single. When married, we long to be single again.
- When it's time to establish a career, we want holidays. When it's time for children, we resent missing our careers. When children leave, we miss them while regretting the career that consumed us.
- When time to work, we want family. When with family, we want sleep. When sleeping, we want to work again.

The grass always looks greener. We expect each cycle to be progressively better, more interesting, more meaningful. But this is the illusion. Life doesn't spiral upward indefinitely—it cycles between building and tearing down, gaining and losing. Eventually the exciting new house grows old. The thrilling spouse becomes familiar. New responsibilities replace old accomplishments. We mistake the beginning of each cycle for permanent progress.

## Beautiful in Its Time

Yet the Teacher offers us something profound:

> He has made everything beautiful in its time. I know that there is nothing better for people than to be happy and to do good while they live. That each of them may eat and drink, and find satisfaction in all their toil—this is the gift of God. (Ecclesiastes 3:11-13)

The key isn't to find meaning in each task—some tasks are genuinely mundane. Changing nappies is poo and wee. Another day at school is another cycle. But notice: the beauty isn't in the meaning of the activity. It's in doing the right action at the right time.

A personal illustration: Your toddler wakes at 2 a.m. vomiting. You've just fallen asleep after a late night. You're exhausted, frustrated. Your plans are shattered. You step in the mess on the dark floor floor. Every instinct screams to resent this interruption.

But in that moment, it's your time to be a father. To love your child. To serve him. To clean up after him. To comfort him. To provide him a safe, clean place to sleep. You sit with him while he chatters, wide awake. This is good time—not because vomit is meaningful, but because you're obeying God. You're loving someone who depends on you completely.

This is the radical reorientation the Teacher offers: Don't ask, "Is this meaningful?" Ask instead, "Is this my time to obey God?"

## Living According to the Times

The Teacher returns to this in 2 Peter 3:

> In the last days, scoffers will come, saying "Where is his coming? Everything goes on as it has since the beginning of creation." But they deliberately forget that long ago by God's word the heavens existed and the earth was formed. (2 Peter 3:3-5)

Scoffers look at endless cycles and conclude there is no God, no judgment, no end. But Scripture says there is a beginning and an end. Creation and judgment. Start and finish. Time is linear.

What then should we do now?

**Fear God and keep his commandments. This is the whole duty of humanity.** (Ecclesiastes 12:13)

We can't see the entire picture. We don't know how everything fits together. So we follow God's instructions, much like following directions in the dark without seeing the destination.

### Time to Be a Student

Learn. Be humble. The Scripture teaches:

> A student is not above his teacher, but everyone who is fully trained will be like his teacher. (Luke 6:40)

> Children, obey your parents in the Lord, for this is right. (Ephesians 6:1)

> Obey your leaders and submit to their authority, for they keep watch over you as those who must give an account. (Hebrews 13:17)

Your time as a student won't last forever. Don't waste it. The discipline, the knowledge, the formation of character—these shape your ability to serve God in every season that follows.

### Time to Be a Worker

> If a man will not work, he shall not eat. (2 Thessalonians 3:10)

Work isn't optional. If you don't have employment, your work is finding work. Start at 9 a.m. Search newspapers. Update your resume. Visit workplaces. If the ideal job won't take you, work your way down—gain experience, build skills.

Yes, it feels meaningless sometimes. But this is your time to obey God. We're called to carry our own weight and to help others. You can't do either when dependent on someone else.

### Time to Be a Parent

Parenting is genuinely mundane. Nappies. Food. Cleaning. Mundane. Beautiful. Not because the task is meaningful in itself, but because it's the right action at the right time. Your child will be young only once.

### The Wisdom of Acceptance

The greatest error isn't laziness—it's the relentless pursuit of "having it all." Research shows that even high-achieving professionals eventually face impossible choices. Karen Hughes left her position as counselor to President George W. Bush because she couldn't be the parent and professional she wanted to be. Mary Matalin stepped down from her role as assistant to the President because she missed her daughters' childhood.

The regret heard most often in palliative care, according to Australian blogger Bronnie Ware, wasn't "I wish I'd had more fun." It was "I wish I'd had the courage to live a life true to myself, not the life others expected of me." The second-most-common regret: "I wish I didn't work so hard." This came from nearly every male patient. They missed their children's youth. They missed their partners' companionship.

The lesson? **Time is limited. For you. Not abstract, but for your actual life.** The feminist promise of "having it all"—career, children, the perfect body, the soulmate—assumes infinite time and energy. But time is linear. Judgment ends time.

## The Call to Fear God

Why fear God? Because we're given insight into eternity itself.

> He has also set eternity in the human heart; yet no one can fathom what God has done from beginning to end. (Ecclesiastes 3:11)

We sense there's something beyond this cycle. We know life is temporary. We know we can't control the future. So instead of worrying about the future, instead of trying to "have it all," we do what we can do: **we obey God today.**

The cyclical becomes beautiful when we stop demanding it be meaningful and start asking if we're doing good. Stop demanding that each season be more exciting than the last. Accept your time. Do good in it. See the good in it. That's the gift of God.

> With the Lord a day is like a thousand years, and a thousand years are like a day. The Lord is not slow in keeping his promise. He is patient, not wanting anyone to perish, but everyone to come to repentance. (2 Peter 3:8-9)

God sees the whole timeline. We see only today. So today, fear Him. Obey Him. Do good. See good. Live according to the time He's given you.
