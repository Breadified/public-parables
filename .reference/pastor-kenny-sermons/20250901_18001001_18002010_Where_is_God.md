# Job 1:1–2:10

**Title:** Where is God?  
**Date:** 14 September 2025  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Introduction: A Question Born from Suffering

We live in a world where suffering raises the most profound question any of us can ask: Where is God?

This isn't merely a philosophical abstraction. It's deeply personal. Whether we face sickness, loss, broken relationships, persecution, or grief—suffering makes us wonder if God is really there, and if He cares. The problem of evil and suffering remains the greatest obstacle to belief in God's existence. As the ancient philosopher Epicurus posed it centuries ago: "Is God willing to prevent evil, but not able? Then he is impotent. Is he able, but not willing? Then he is malevolent."

The questions multiply in our pain:
- Why do seemingly good people suffer?
- Is there any reason for this suffering?
- Why does it feel so unfair?
- How long must this continue?

The Book of Job offers us something rare: a glimpse behind the curtain of heaven itself, where we can begin to understand God's perspective on human suffering.

---

## 1. A Great Man (1:1–3)

> In the land of Uz there lived a man whose name was Job. This man was blameless and upright; he feared God and shunned evil. He had seven sons and three daughters, and he owned seven thousand sheep, three thousand camels, five hundred yoke of oxen and five hundred donkeys, and had a large number of servants. He was the greatest man among all the people of the East.

Job was undeniably great—in every dimension of human success.

**His Family:** Seven sons and three daughters—ten children, a number representing completeness. In the ancient Near East, unlike our modern culture, children were understood as assets, not liabilities. The sons would carry on his name and build his legacy. Even his daughters, though married into other families, were included in his feasts and loved—a remarkable picture of family harmony in a world often marked by strife and dysfunction.

**His Wealth:** Seven thousand sheep, three thousand camels, five hundred oxen, five hundred donkeys, plus numerous servants and properties. In today's terms, we might say he owned mansions, businesses, multiple enterprises, and employed hundreds. His wealth was not hidden away; it was actively managed and growing.

**His Character:** What makes Job's greatness even more remarkable is what the text tells us about him personally. He was *blameless*—not sinless, but a man of integrity, whose life was "of one piece." He was *upright*—he acted ethically in his dealings, doing what was right and extending justice and mercy to others. He *feared God*, recognizing that the Lord alone is Creator and that everything—his wealth, his family, his very existence—belongs to God. He *shunned evil*, living a life marked by repentance and faith.

**His Spiritual Leadership:** Job didn't keep his faith to himself. When his children finished their feasts, he would rise early and offer sacrifices for each of them—not because they had sinned, but "lest perhaps my children have sinned and cursed God in their hearts." This was his regular custom, his discipline, not something done haphazardly. He cared about their spiritual wellbeing as much as their physical comfort.

In every way—family, wealth, character, and faith—Job was the greatest man among all the people of the East. He is exactly the kind of man we want to succeed. We naturally root for him.

## 2. A Great Test (1:6–12; 2:1–6)

But then something shocking happens. In a single afternoon, everything changes.

> One day when Job's sons and daughters were feasting and drinking wine at the oldest brother's house, a messenger came to Job and said, "The oxen were plowing and the donkeys were grazing nearby, and the Sabeans attacked and carried them off. They put the servants to the sword, and I am the only one who has escaped to tell you!"
>
> While he was still speaking, another messenger came and said, "The fire of God fell from the sky and burned up the sheep and the servants, and I am the only one who has escaped to tell you!"
>
> While he was still speaking, another messenger came and said, "The Chaldeans formed three raiding parties and swept down on your camels and carried them off. They put the servants to the sword, and I am the only one who has escaped to tell you!"
>
> While he was still speaking, yet another messenger came and said, "Your sons and daughters were feasting and drinking wine at the oldest brother's house, when suddenly a mighty wind swept in from the desert and struck the four corners of the house. It collapsed on them and they are dead, and I am the only one who has escaped to tell you!"

The text arranges these catastrophes in reverse order of the blessings listed earlier: oxen and donkeys gone, then sheep, then camels, then—most devastating—his children, all ten of them, dead in a single moment. From hero to zero in an afternoon.

Job experiences the full spectrum of human suffering:
- Natural disasters (the fire from heaven, the mighty wind)
- Evil and injustice (the raids by the Sabeans and Chaldeans)
- Loss of loved ones (his seven sons and three daughters)
- Loss of property and wealth (complete financial ruin)
- The compounding nature of loss (all at once, with no time to process or recover)

Later, Satan will afflict his body with painful sores from head to foot—so severe that Job scrapes himself with broken pottery as he sits in ashes.

Imagine losing everything. All that represents your life's work, your security, your identity. One moment you're the greatest man in the East. The next, you're sitting in ashes, scraping open wounds with pottery shards, with no explanation and no comfort.

But the text gives us something Job never had: **a glimpse into heaven.**

> One day the angels came to present themselves before the LORD, and Satan also came with them. The LORD said to Satan, "Where have you come from?" Satan answered, "From roaming through the earth and going back and forth in it." Then the LORD said to Satan, "Have you considered my servant Job? There is no one on earth like him; he is blameless and upright, a man who fears God and shuns evil."

God Himself initiates the conversation about Job. He brings Job's faithfulness before the heavenly court. But Satan responds with an accusation:

> Does Job fear God for nothing? Have you not put a hedge around him and his household and everything he has? You have blessed the work of his hands, so that his flocks and herds are spread throughout the land. But stretch out your hand and strike everything he has, and he will surely curse you to your face.

Satan's charge is simple but devastating: Job's faith is transactional. He only loves God because God blesses him. Take away the blessings, and his true heart will be revealed—he'll curse God to His face.

This is Satan's core accusation against any believer: "You only follow God for what you can get from Him."

God permits the test, but with boundaries:

> The LORD said to Satan, "Very well, then, everything he has is in your hands, but on the man himself do not lay a finger."

Then, after Job survives the first test, Satan returns with another accusation:

> Skin for skin! A man will give all he has for his own life. But stretch out your hand and strike his flesh and bones, and he will surely curse you to your face.

God permits this test as well, with one limit:

> Very well, then, he is in your hands; but you must spare his life.

### Why Does God Allow Testing?

At first, this seems cruel. But consider what testing actually reveals. When times are good, anyone can claim to follow God. But what happens when the comfort is stripped away? What happens when following Jesus becomes unpopular, even dangerous? What happens when faith costs something?

We say we love our spouses until temptation comes. We say we're committed to friends until they disappoint us or become unpopular. We claim to be committed to God until exams pile up, or our reputations are threatened, or suffering arrives.

Testing reveals what we truly value.

> Blessed is the man who perseveres under trial, because when he has stood the test, he will receive the crown of life that God has promised to those who love him. (James 1:12)

### God's Absolute Sovereignty

Notice something crucial: Satan doesn't operate independently. He appears before God's throne. He submits to God's authority. God sets the boundaries—what Satan can and cannot do. Satan has no power except what God permits.

Even more striking: the catastrophes that come from Satan's actions are credited to God Himself. When Job loses his children in the wind, the text doesn't say "Satan killed them." It says God did it. This isn't because God has evil intentions—it's because God's sovereignty is so absolute that even evil acts ultimately serve His purposes and remain under His control.

> Our God is in heaven; he does whatever pleases him. (Psalm 115:3)

The world is not dualistic—two equally powerful forces, one good and one evil, locked in combat with humanity as pawns. There is only one God, and He alone gives and takes away.

## 3. The Greatest Response (1:20–22; 2:9–10)

Now comes Job's answer to the test. His response is the reason we study this passage.

> At this, Job got up and tore his robe and shaved his head. Then he fell to the ground in worship and said:
>
> "Naked I came from my mother's womb,
> and naked I will depart.
> The LORD gave and the LORD has taken away;
> may the name of the LORD be praised."

Job's words reveal a profound theological understanding. Notice what he doesn't do: He doesn't curse God. He doesn't blame Satan. Instead, he traces everything back to God's hand. "The LORD gave and the LORD has taken away."

This is the opposite of how we often think. We prefer to say: God gives blessings, and Satan brings suffering. But Scripture teaches something deeper. God is ultimately sovereign over all things, including suffering.

When Job's wife later urges him to curse God and die, his response is telling:

> You are talking like a foolish woman. Shall we accept good from God, and not trouble?

The word translated "trouble" is the same word used for "evil." Job is saying: If we receive good from the hand of God, should we not also receive evil from His hand? Should we not accept it from God?

**In all this, Job did not sin by charging God with wrongdoing.**

This is crucial: It is not sin to acknowledge that God sends suffering. It is not sinful to say that sickness comes from God, that loss comes from God, that hardship comes from God.

What would be sin is to say otherwise—to suggest that God is weak, that Satan can thwart His plans, that God is doing His best but ultimately cannot control the world. That is the real error. That denies God's power and sovereignty.

### The Price of Righteousness

Satan's test reveals a critical question: **What is the price of your righteousness?**

What comfort could Satan offer you to make you abandon God? What blessings would you sacrifice? What pain would make you curse God?

For Job, the answer was: nothing. No amount of loss, no degree of suffering, no personal agony could make him deny God. His righteousness was not for sale.

But Jesus went further still. He was, like Job, blameless and upright—a man who feared God and shunned evil. But unlike Job, Jesus didn't merely endure suffering. He voluntarily sacrificed His blameless, upright life for the sins of others. Not animals, but Himself.

And He did it for love—love for His brothers and sisters, love for a broken world, love for us.

> Two thousand years ago, God made a personal appearance in the man Jesus. The cross was lifted high so that all could see that God is here. And all may reach out to him and find forgiveness, mercy and hope.

---

## Conclusion: God Is Here

In a world that feels random and meaningless, in the midst of suffering that seems to serve no purpose, we have a choice.

We can accept the world as dualistic—a cosmic battleground where good and evil slug it out, and we're merely caught in the crossfire.

Or we can, like Job, trace everything back to God's hand. We can acknowledge that we don't understand His purposes. We can admit that suffering often comes without explanation. But we can also trust that He is sovereign, He is good, and nothing—not even Satan's attacks—can escape His control.

God is here. He hasn't abandoned us. He knows what He is doing. And nothing can affect His sovereignty.

So the call to us is simple: Don't curse God. In good times and bad, remain faithful. Don't let the pain convince you He isn't there. Don't let the silence convince you He doesn't care.

We have been bought by the blood of Jesus. We cannot be purchased by Satan's temporary comforts, nor can we be controlled by fear. Our lives belong to God, and that makes us unshakeable.

May we have the courage to be righteous in hard times, just as Job did, just as Jesus did, just as every believer throughout history has done by God's grace.
