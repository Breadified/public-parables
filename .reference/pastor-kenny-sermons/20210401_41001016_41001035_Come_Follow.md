# Mark 1:16-35

**Title:** Come Follow
**Date:** 11 April 2021
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## New Authority

When Jesus appears on the scene, something remarkable happens. He doesn't begin with displays of power or spectacular acts. Instead, he enters the synagogue at Capernaum and teaches.

> 21 They went to Capernaum, and when the Sabbath came, Jesus went into the synagogue and began to teach.

This seems almost ordinary—a teacher instructing people. Yet the response is extraordinary:

> 22 The people were amazed at his teaching, because he taught them as one who had authority, not as the teachers of the law.

### Authority Through Teaching, Not Coercion

Jesus exercises a fundamentally different kind of authority than the world offers. Consider how authority typically operates:

- **Charisma:** The ability to emotionally manipulate and sway people's feelings—like Hollywood celebrities who can persuade through charm and emotional appeal
- **Force:** The power to compel obedience through resources, strength, or threat of punishment—like governments that enforce laws through coercion

Jesus' authority, by contrast, operates through teaching. This represents something radical: **persuasion rather than coercion.** Teaching attempts to convince others that what you say is true, offering genuine instruction and sound arguments. It respects the listener's capacity to understand and choose.

This distinction carries profound implications. Christianity is fundamentally persuasive, not coercive. Unlike some religions that impose belief through force ("convert or we punish you"), the gospel operates through reasoned argument and invitation. The time of patient teaching is now—a period of grace extended before final judgment arrives.

This explains why Christian societies developed liberal principles: free speech, education, freedom of conscience. We believe in these things because we trust that truth is persuasive when properly taught. We protect the right to critique Christianity, to question Scripture, even to mock Christ—because we believe the gospel stands on its own merits.

### Authority From God Himself

The Greek word for authority is *exousia* (ἐξουσία), composed of *ex* ("out of") and *ousia* ("being"). Jesus teaches not through appeals to other rabbis or traditions, but from his own being—directly from God.

When we humans argue, we appeal to authorities: "This scientist says," "That expert claims," "The pastor teaches." But Jesus speaks with his own authority. He doesn't quote the rabbis to validate his teaching. He simply speaks the words of God himself.

> **Hebrews 1:1-2:** In the past God spoke to our ancestors through the prophets at many times and in various ways, but in these last days he has spoken to us by his Son.

Scripture is accessible to us—we can read God's direct words through Jesus. This is the distinction between secondary sources (appeals to authority) and the primary source (God's own word).

### When Teaching Meets Resistance

Yet Jesus' authority extends beyond mere teaching. When a demon-possessed man cries out in the synagogue, Jesus doesn't reason with the unclean spirit. Instead:

> 25 "Be quiet!" said Jesus sternly. "Come out of him!" 26 The impure spirit shook the man violently and came out of him with a shriek.

This is coercion, not persuasion. The demon is forced out against its will. The people recognize this as a new kind of authority:

> 27 The people were all so amazed that they asked each other, "What is this? A new teaching—and with authority! He even gives orders to impure spirits and they obey him."

This reveals an important truth: God is patient now, offering teaching and persuasion. But this season is not permanent. When final judgment comes, persuasion will give way to coercion. All will bow—either willingly in faith, embracing Jesus as Lord and Savior, or forcibly in condemnation.

> **Philippians 2:10-11:** That at the name of Jesus every knee should bow, in heaven and on earth and under the earth, and every tongue acknowledge that Jesus Christ is Lord.

The choice before us is stark: Will you bend your knee willingly through faith, or will you be forced to bend in judgment? Will you confess Jesus as Lord joyfully, or will that confession be wrung from you in agony?

## Come Follow

Immediately after establishing his authority through teaching and power, Jesus calls his first disciples:

> 16-17 As Jesus walked beside the Sea of Galilee, he saw Simon and his brother Andrew casting a net into the lake, for they were fishermen. "Come, follow me," Jesus said, "and I will send you out to fish for people."

This is an invitation, not a command. Yet the response is immediate and total:

> 18 At once they left their nets and followed him.

They abandon their livelihood—their nets, their business, their livelihood security. Later, James and John do the same, leaving not just their nets but their father Zebedee in the boat.

### The Cost of Following

This sacrifice reveals something about discipleship. When Jesus calls, he calls for primary allegiance. Work cannot compete with following Jesus. Family relationships cannot hold us back. Yet how do we respond when faced with the same call? We offer excuses: "I'm too busy with work. I'm involved in my relationships. Maybe later?"

The issue isn't really time or circumstances. It's a matter of authority. Do we truly acknowledge Jesus as authoritative in our lives, or do we treat him as secondary to other commitments?

Notice that people readily follow other authorities:
- **Celebrities:** We love them, follow their causes, adopt their values because they charm us
- **Government:** We obey because it provides for us and commands our allegiance
- **Charismatic leaders:** We follow because they emotionally persuade us

Yet when Jesus asks us to follow him—to do things that would actually improve our lives and lead us toward eternal salvation—we hesitate and doubt. What is the real barrier?

## Hidden Authority

Word of Jesus spreads rapidly through Galilee. News about the healing of Simon's mother-in-law travels, and soon crowds gather:

> 32-33 That evening after sunset the people brought to Jesus all the sick and demon-possessed. The whole town gathered at the door.

He is going viral. Yet something strange emerges here—what scholars call the "messianic secret":

> 34 Jesus healed many who had various diseases. He also drove out many demons, but he would not let the demons speak because they knew who he was.

Jesus silences the demons even as he casts them out. Then, in the early morning:

> 35-37 Very early in the morning, while it was still dark, Jesus got up, left the house and went off to a solitary place, where he prayed. Simon and his companions went to look for him, and when they found him, they exclaimed: "Everyone is looking for you!"

### Rejecting the Crowd's Attraction

Jesus deliberately avoids the crowds. He could leverage his popularity, build a movement, attract followers through spectacular displays. Instead, he withdraws and prays.

> 38 Jesus replied, "Let us go somewhere else—to the nearby villages so I can preach there also. That is why I have come."

In the ancient world, this is unthinkable. Walking away from a million eager followers—the ancient equivalent of closing your account and starting over. Why would he do this?

**Jesus came to teach and persuade, not to perform miracles or feed crowds.** His mission is to call people to repent and believe. The crowds weren't attracted to his teaching; they came for free healing and help. They wanted his power, not his message.

### The Real Problem With the World

Here lies a crucial insight: **the world's problems cannot be healed by bandages.** You can provide medicine, food, and technology. You can extend someone's life by years or decades. But sin remains. Death claims us all. No amount of social charity, medical advancement, or technological innovation solves the fundamental problem of human fallenness.

If the world could be repaired through human effort, Jesus wouldn't have needed to die. Yet he did die—for sin itself, the irreparable damage at the heart of human existence. We cannot fix our world. We need a new one.

This is why Jesus leaves the crowds. **His priority is proclamation, not provision.** Yet he doesn't abandon compassion entirely.

## Compassion Without Compromise

> 40 A man with leprosy came to him and begged him on his knees, "If you are willing, you can make me clean."
> 41 Jesus was indignant. He reached out his hand and touched the man. "I am willing," he said. "Be clean!"

The word "indignant" appears in the earliest manuscripts—a harder reading that scribes likely softened to "compassion." Jesus is angry. Why? Because his mission is stalled. He wants to save the world, and someone interrupts with a personal crisis.

Yet despite his indignation, Jesus heals. He touches the leper—risking contagion (comparable to embracing someone with a deadly plague)—and restores him.

**Jesus shows compassion even when frustrated by the interruption.** This models the balance we must strike: we should address human suffering when we encounter it, providing minimum care to allow people to hear the gospel. But we must not lose sight of the main mission.

Yet there's a problem. The healed leper doesn't follow Jesus' instructions:

> 43-45 Jesus sent him away at once with a strong warning: "See that you don't tell this to anyone. But go, show yourself to the priest and offer the sacrifices that Moses commanded for your cleansing, as a testimony to them." Instead he went out and began to talk freely, spreading the news. As a result, Jesus could no longer enter a town openly but stayed outside in lonely places.

The leper's testimony, though well-intentioned, undermines Jesus' method. Crowds gather seeking healing rather than redemption.

## Application: Ministry Priorities

As followers of Christ, we face the same tension Jesus experienced. We see poverty, injustice, suffering—and our hearts rightly respond with compassion. Some churches conclude they must abandon evangelism entirely, devoting themselves solely to social care.

But this prioritizes short-term compassion over long-term salvation. **What good is it to help someone survive a few more years on earth while neglecting their eternal destiny?** Both matter, but they matter differently.

The balance requires wisdom: provide basic care—food, shelter, medical attention—as a foundation that allows people to hear the gospel. Don't aim for luxury; provide the minimum necessary so people can listen. But always remember: **proclamation is the primary mission.** Teaching others is the church's first calling.

The world offers charity. Atheists, governments, secular organizations all serve the poor. But only the church proclaims the gospel. So we do both—we feed and clothe as opportunities arise—but we prioritize the Word. We preach in villages and synagogues. We spread the message to those who will listen. We call people to repent and believe in the kingdom of God.

**The time is here. Repent and believe.**
