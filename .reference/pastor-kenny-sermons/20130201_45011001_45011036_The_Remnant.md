# Romans 11:1-36

**Title:** The Remnant

**Date:** 17 February 2013

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## The Context: Israel's Future

Every week, news comes from Palestine—a small region about the size of greater Sydney, yet it dominates international headlines. The conflict between Jews and Palestinians has deep historical roots, particularly stemming from the Balfour Declaration a century ago, which promised the land to Jewish people rather than to those already living there.

Some Christians believed this decision was biblically justified, pointing to Romans 11:26: "And so all Israel will be saved." They thought a mass conversion of Jews would usher in the end times, making a Jewish homeland essential to God's plan. But is this interpretation correct? To understand what Paul actually teaches, we must read Romans 11 in its proper context—following chapters 9-10, where Israel has rejected God. The question becomes: Is that truly the end of the story?

---

## 1. The Remnant (Romans 11:1-11)

### God Has Not Rejected His People

> I ask then: Did God reject his people? By no means! (Romans 11:1)

Despite Israel's rejection of Jesus, God has not abandoned His people entirely. Paul offers himself as the first example—an Israelite, descendant of Abraham, from the tribe of Benjamin, yet a believer in Christ.

### The Elijah Principle

Paul turns to the Old Testament for a deeper illustration:

> God did not reject his people, whom he foreknew. Don't you know what the Scripture says in the passage about Elijah—how he appealed to God against Israel: "Lord, they have killed your prophets and torn down your altars; I am the only one left, and they are trying to kill me"? (Romans 11:2-3)

Elijah felt utterly alone in his faithfulness. But God's response was remarkable:

> And what was God's answer to him? "I have reserved for myself seven thousand who have not bowed the knee to Baal." (Romans 11:4)

God had preserved a faithful remnant all along. This was never about Elijah's solitary faithfulness, but about God's sovereign preservation of His people.

### The Remnant in Paul's Day

> So too, at the present time there is a remnant chosen by grace. And if by grace, then it is no longer by works; if it were, grace would no longer be grace. (Romans 11:5-6)

Salvation has always been God's choice, not human achievement. Just as God preserved seven thousand in Elijah's time, He has preserved a faithful remnant in Paul's day as well—the apostles, disciples, the three thousand baptized at Pentecost, and Jewish converts throughout the Roman world.

### Two Kinds of Israelites

> What then? What Israel sought so earnestly it did not obtain, but the elect did. (Romans 11:7)

This is the crucial point: there are two categories of Israelites. One group sought righteousness but did not find it. The other group—the elect—received what they sought. The distinction is not ethnic but spiritual.

### Application: Your Faithful Position

Being a Christian can feel like an isolated position. Perhaps at work, you're the only believer among atheists. In your extended family, you may be the sole Christian among people of other faiths. The peer pressure can be discouraging, and you may feel like Elijah—as though you're standing alone.

But remember: you are not alone. God has chosen a faithful few, and you are part of that remnant. This is not a burden but a privilege. You are positioned as a light to those around you. Your difference exists for a purpose—to be a good example, to pray for others, to demonstrate Christ's character.

Moreover, because God is a God who chooses, there is hope for even the most hardened friends and family members. As long as someone lives, the story is not finished.

Consider this: when taking an exam, we don't leave early even after finishing. We stay until the final bell, checking and rechecking our work, because every point matters. Similarly, don't give up on anyone. Steve Abbott, our senior minister, prayed and shared the gospel with his father for thirty-five years. His father converted just before he died.

The stubborn atheist, the seemingly hardened unbeliever—don't lose hope. Pray for them. Gently challenge them to think. Work faithfully. God can use you in ways you cannot imagine. Hope comes from grace.

## 2. God the Underdog (Romans 11:11b-24)

### Adversity Serves God's Purpose

It is often in the darkest moments that God accomplishes His greatest work. Paul's situation exemplified this—things could barely have been worse for him. His entire family likely rejected Jesus. His father, mother, siblings, cousins, tribe—all rejected his message. Everything appeared to be falling apart.

Yet God was using this apparent tragedy for something magnificent:

> Rather, because of their transgression, salvation has come to the Gentiles to make Israel envious. (Romans 11:11b)

### God Works Through Human Failure

When the Jewish synagogues rejected Paul, he had nowhere else to turn—so he took the gospel to the Gentiles. When he was expelled from cities in Asia, he went to Europe. God's redemptive purposes transcended human resistance. He bound all people over to disobedience so that He might have mercy on all:

> Just as you who were at one time disobedient to God have now received mercy as a result of their disobedience, so they too have now become disobedient in order that they too may now receive mercy as a result of God's mercy to you. For God has bound all men over to disobedience so that he may have mercy on them all. (Romans 11:30-32)

This is astonishing: rejection of one group opened the door to another's salvation. Other people's disobedience became the means of your salvation. Perhaps you can trace this in your own story—someone unlikely brought you to faith, while the most likely candidate did nothing.

## 3. Do Not Be Arrogant (Romans 11:18-22)

### Remember the Root Sustains You

> Do not boast over those branches. If you do, consider this: You do not support the root, but the root supports you. (Romans 11:18b)

The original branches are the Jews—Abraham, Isaac, Jacob, Elijah, Paul, Barnabas, Peter, and countless faithful Jewish believers throughout history. The tree itself remains. These remnant believers are still part of God's plan, and more can be grafted in.

But hear the warning:

> You will say then, "Branches were broken off so that I could be grafted in." Granted. But they were broken off because of unbelief, and you stand by faith. Do not be arrogant, but be afraid. (Romans 11:19-20)

### The Danger of Taking Grace for Granted

It's thrilling to be part of God's remnant. But beware the cautionary example of the original Israelites. They took God's promises for granted. They forgot the God who loved them. They disobeyed and followed other gods.

> For if God did not spare the natural branches, he will not spare you either. (Romans 11:21)

> Consider therefore the kindness and sternness of God: sternness to those who fell, but kindness to you, provided that you continue in his kindness. Otherwise, you also will be cut off. (Romans 11:22)

What a privilege to be in God's family. Let us not abuse this position through careless sin. Let us fear sin appropriately, having been rescued from darkness and slavery.

God has given us the ability to call Him Father. Do we actually pray? He has revealed Himself through Scripture. Do we read it? He has placed us in a church community where we find nourishment, support, and friendship in a lonely world. Do we attend? Do we serve? These are not small matters—they are expressions of our response to undeserved grace.

## 4. For the Repentance of All (Romans 11:23-32)

### The Mystery Explained

> And if they do not persist in unbelief, they will be grafted in, for God is able to graft them in again. (Romans 11:23)

Now we arrive at the verse that sparked our entire discussion:

> I do not want you to be ignorant of this mystery, brothers, so that you may not be conceited: Israel has experienced a hardening in part until the full number of the Gentiles has come in. And so all Israel will be saved, as it is written. (Romans 11:25-26)

### What Does "All Israel" Mean?

This phrase does not mean every Jewish person will be saved in a mass conversion event. The entire point of Romans 9-11 is that "not all Israel are Israel"—being ethnically Jewish does not automatically secure salvation.

The Balfour Declaration illustrates the danger of this misinterpretation. Well-meaning Christians thought that by giving Jews a homeland and converting them, they could bring heaven to earth. But God's kingdom cannot be forced through political maneuvering.

Rather, "all Israel will be saved" most likely means:

- All the elect remnant of Israel throughout history, or
- All the elect—both Jews and Gentiles—brought to fullness in God's time

It is not an end-times prophecy of instantaneous conversion, but rather the logical unfolding of God's plan: the elect of Israel, hardened for a season, will be graphed back in through envy and repentance.

### God Loves Both Jews and Gentiles

> As far as the gospel is concerned, they are enemies on your account; but as far as election is concerned, they are loved on account of the patriarchs. (Romans 11:28)

God still loves the Jewish people. And God loves the Gentiles. The salvation of all His elect—whether Jewish or Gentile—fulfills His covenantal promises:

> The deliverer will come from Zion; he will turn godlessness away from Jacob. And this is my covenant with them when I take away their sins. (Romans 11:26b-27)

The focus is not on land but on salvation. God's promises to Israel are ultimately spiritual, not territorial.

## 5. The Mystery of God's Wisdom (Romans 11:33-36)

> Oh, the depth of the riches of the wisdom and knowledge of God! How unsearchable his judgments, and his paths beyond tracing out! (Romans 11:33)

> Who has known the mind of the Lord? Or who has been his counselor? (Romans 11:34)

> Who has ever given to God, that God should repay him? (Romans 11:35)

> For from him and through him and to him are all things. To him be the glory forever! Amen. (Romans 11:36)

God's purposes are beyond our comprehension. He works through human resistance and failure. He orchestrates history toward His redemptive ends. We cannot manipulate His kingdom through political maneuvering or human schemes. Instead, we trust that He is working all things together according to His wisdom and love.

---

## Closing Exhortation

Keep hoping. God has saved Himself a remnant—a faithful few in our generation, and a full harvest of the elect throughout all history. Your fidelity matters. Your prayers matter. Your witness matters. Do not grow weary, for God is faithful, and His purposes will be accomplished.
