# Zechariah 7:1-8:23

**Title:** A New People
**Date:** 27 November 2016
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Question: Can We Stop Fasting?

Two years after his initial visions, Zechariah receives a question from the people of Bethel. They send messengers to ask the priests and prophets: "Should we continue mourning and fasting in the fifth month as we have done for so many years?"

For seventy years, during and after the Babylonian exile, the Jewish people had commemorated four tragic dates with fasting:
- The **fifth month** marked the destruction of the temple itself (2 Kings 25:8)
- The **tenth month** commemorated the beginning of Nebuchadnezzar's siege (2 Kings 25:1-2)
- The **fourth month** remembered the fall of Jerusalem's walls (2 Kings 25:3-4)
- The **seventh month** marked the assassination of Gedaliah, the governor (2 Kings 25:25)

These fasts symbolized mourning and repentance. The people kept this tradition for seven decades—now they're back in their land. So their question is simply: "Are we there yet? Can we stop this ritual?"

## Part One: Don't Forget Why You're Fasting

God's answer to the people is indirect but piercing. Through Zechariah, He challenges their sincerity:

> When you fasted and mourned in the fifth and seventh months for the past seventy years, **was it really for me that you fasted?** And when you were eating and drinking, were you not just feasting for yourselves?

God is questioning the motivation behind their fasting. Their sadness was for themselves, not for the sin that caused their exile. Like a criminal crying in self-pity rather than genuine repentance for the harm caused, they went through the motions without addressing the root problem.

Zechariah then reminds them of history. The prophets of old warned Israel when they were prosperous:

> Are these not the words the Lord proclaimed through the earlier prophets when Jerusalem and its surrounding towns were at rest and prosperous?

But the people refused to listen. Their stubbornness came in layers:

- They **turned their backs**
- They **covered their ears**
- They **hardened their hearts as flint**
- They **would not listen to the law** or the words God sent through His Spirit

This resistance had consequences: "When I called, they did not listen; so when they called, I would not listen." The judgment came as promised—they were scattered among the nations like a whirlwind, and the pleasant land became desolate.

**The core truth:** The Israelites were supposed to fast to remember their sin and to stop sinning. Fasting is about humility and mourning—as Moses learned in the wilderness, God humbles us through hunger "to teach you that man does not live on bread alone but on every word that comes from the mouth of the Lord" (Deuteronomy 8:3). Fasting appears in the Day of Atonement as a practice of self-denial for spiritual cleansing (Leviticus 16:29).

### Fasting Today

In our modern context, we don't practice fasting as much in Western Christian circles, yet it remains spiritually valuable:

- **Symbolic mourning:** Expressing humility and contrition before God
- **Thankfulness:** Cultivating gratitude rather than taking provision for granted
- **Discipline:** Gaining control over our physical desires
- **Generosity:** Denying ourselves to give to others

Christian festivals differ from pagan ones. While Roman festivals celebrated excess—food, sensuality, wild parties—Christian observances call us to restraint. Even Christmas, historically a "Mass of Christ," was preceded by fasting in earlier centuries. These sacred times remind us to refrain from sin, remember God, and deny ourselves for the sake of others.

## Part Two: Virtue Signaling Without Justice

However, the Jews had turned their fasts into hollow rituals—what we might call "virtue signaling." They asked about stopping the ritual, but never asked the harder question: "Have we stopped sinning?"

God's challenge shifts to present tense expectations:

> Administer true justice; show mercy and compassion to one another. Do not oppress the widow or the fatherless, the foreigner or the poor. Do not plot evil against each other.

True justice requires both law and mercy. The law says "eye for an eye"—exact consequence. But mercy and compassion make room for genuine cases of hardship. English law reflected this principle with both common courts and courts of equity where exceptions could be made.

The widow, fatherless, foreigner, and poor represent society's most vulnerable—those without legal standing or protection. God consistently demands their protection throughout Scripture.

This pattern repeats itself in the Gospels with the Pharisees, who became experts at ritual while neglecting justice and mercy. Jesus encountered them on the Sabbath and asked directly: "Which is lawful on the Sabbath: to do good or to do evil, to save life or to kill?" When they remained silent, Jesus healed the man—and the Pharisees immediately began plotting to kill Him.

### The Problem with Modern Virtue Signaling

Much of contemporary social activism mirrors this Pharisaic pattern. Governments and institutions mouth the right phrases about justice and love while implementing policies that harm the vulnerable:

- **Climate and social policies** virtue signal compassion but often disproportionately burden the poor
- **Meaningless global charities** pretend to help while leaving systemic injustice untouched
- **Double standards** are rampant: those who create these policies remain unaffected by them, living in comfort while the poor suffer the consequences

As Amos declared: "I hate, I despise your religious festivals; your assemblies are a stench to me."

The painful irony is that we're still in exile spiritually. Hypocrisy still pervades. Sin still dominates. We need these reminders of God's demands for justice and mercy. Yet Zechariah points beyond this to a future when sin will be no more.

## Part Three: Feasting—The Promise of God's Return

In Chapter 8, God makes ten declarations, each beginning with "This is what the Lord Almighty says." After calling the people to justice, He offers a stunning reversal.

### God's Jealousy and Promise

> I am very jealous for Zion; I am burning with jealousy for her.

This is not petty jealousy (the opposite of contentment) but righteous jealousy—the same passion a husband has for his wife. It's the love that says "she is mine, and I will not abandon her." God's jealousy is the opposite of apathy.

God promises to return:

> I will return to Zion and dwell in Jerusalem. Then Jerusalem will be called the Faithful City, and the mountain of the Lord Almighty will be called the Holy Mountain.

God will dwell with His people. The relationship is not about buildings but about people becoming faithful. Yet there was a problem: God was ready to return, but the people weren't ready. The temple remained unfinished. It's as if God was bringing exiles from everywhere only to discover there was no inn prepared.

### From Judgment to Blessing

God had determined to bring disaster on Israel when they angered Him—but now He announces a change of mind:

> Just as I had determined to bring disaster on you and showed no pity when your ancestors angered me, so now I have determined to do good again to Jerusalem and Judah.

This is grace. Law and mercy come together. Jesus embodied this perfectly—He came not only to judge but to save.

### A Complete City Restored

Zechariah paints a vision of Jerusalem renewed:

> Once again men and women of ripe old age will sit in the streets of Jerusalem, each of them with cane in hand because of their age. The city streets will be filled with boys and girls playing there.

The post-exile Jerusalem was demographically incomplete. The elderly couldn't survive the dangerous journey from Babylon. Young children didn't accompany the first wave of returnees. This vision promises a complete community—old and young, all generations thriving together in safety.

God continues His promises:

> I will save my people from the countries of the east and the west. I will bring them back to live in Jerusalem; they will be my people, and I will be faithful and righteous to them as their God.

The seed will grow well, the vine will yield fruit, the ground will produce crops, and the heavens will send rain. From being a curse among the nations, Judah and Israel will become a blessing.

### Even Foreigners Will Seek God

The vision expands beyond Israel:

> Many peoples and the inhabitants of many cities will yet come, and the inhabitants of one city will go to another and say, "Let us go at once to entreat the Lord and seek the Lord Almighty. I myself am going."

Foreigners will come to Jerusalem seeking mercy. People from many cities and nations will desire to follow:

> In those days ten people from all languages and nations will take firm hold of one Jew by the hem of his robe and say, "Let us go with you, because we have heard that God is with you."

This was Israel's original calling—to be a light to the nations, a model that would draw others to God. Yet Israel failed through legalism and hypocrisy. The Pharisees forfeited their place as bringers of the nations to God.

But one Man fulfilled this calling perfectly: Jesus, the true Jew. He acted with right motivations and right deeds. He cared for widows, orphans, foreigners, and the poor. He called rulers and governments to account. He was the righteous Israelite that God promised.

Those who trust in Him—like the bleeding woman who touched the hem of His garment in Mark 5—hold fast to Him seeking to follow. Through Jesus, the gentiles finally come to God.

### Fasts Become Feasts

The promise comes full circle:

> The fasts of the fourth, fifth, seventh and tenth months will become joyful and glad occasions and happy festivals for Judah. Therefore love truth and peace.

The very dates that commemorated disaster will become celebrations of joy. The same days that marked exile will mark restoration. This is the ultimate transformation—mourning turned to dancing, fasting turned to feasting.

> Blessed is the one who will eat at the feast in the kingdom of God. (Luke 14:15)

## Application: Are You Ready?

The fundamental question is not "When is Jesus returning?" but rather "Are you ready for His return?"

God promises His return with absolute certainty, yet it will come unexpectedly:

> The day of the Lord will come like a thief in the night. While people are saying, "Peace and safety," destruction will come on them suddenly.

But this need not surprise those who belong to Christ:

> You are all children of the light and children of the day. We do not belong to the night or to the darkness. So then, let us not be like others, who are asleep, but let us be awake and sober.

As everything will be destroyed and laid bare, the question becomes:

> What kind of people ought you to be? You ought to live holy and godly lives as you look forward to the day of God.

Just as the ancient Jews asked whether they could stop fasting, we must ask a deeper question: Are we ready? Are we living justly? Are we showing mercy? Are we genuinely following Christ, or merely going through religious motions?

The party is coming—the great feast of heaven awaits. But until then, we fast. We discipline ourselves. We deny ourselves. We serve the vulnerable. We seek justice and mercy. And we invite others to take hold of Christ's garment and follow Him into that eternal celebration.
