# Numbers 26:1-23

**Title:** Waiting for the Promised Land  
**Date:** 19 July 2015  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Desperate Search for Land

In Sydney today, we see the same desperation that gripped the ancient Israelites. Newspapers overflow with stories of foreign buyers, first-time homeowners priced out of the market, and families willing to fabricate documents just to secure a place in the right school district. People are willing to do almost anything for a piece of land.

But the Israelites' desperation for land was far deeper than ours. For them, inheriting a portion of the promised land meant inheriting God's covenant blessings. It was never merely about dirt—it was about security, identity, and belonging to God's people.

As we conclude our series on Numbers, we'll examine three movements around this promised land: the daughters claiming their inheritance, the tribes securing their portions, and the leadership transition that would carry Israel into their new home.

## The Daughters of Zelophehad (Numbers 27:1-11)

Zelophehad's five daughters—Mahlah, Noah, Hoglah, Milcah, and Tirzah—approached Moses with a problem. Their father had died in the wilderness, and because he had no sons, his name would vanish from the tribal records. More importantly, without male heirs, they would receive no land.

Their appeal was simple yet profound:

> "Why should our father's name disappear from his clan because he had no son? Give us property among our father's relatives." (Numbers 27:4)

The daughters understood something crucial: land allocation worked through names. As the tribes were divided according to the census, each family's inheritance was determined by male representation in the tribal rolls. By asking for their father's name to be recognized, the daughters were essentially asking to be included in this distribution.

Moses brought their case before the Lord, and God's response was unambiguous: "What Zelophehad's daughters are saying is right. You must certainly give them property as an inheritance" (Numbers 27:7).

From this judgment, God established a law of inheritance that would protect all vulnerable family lines:

> "If a man dies and leaves no son, turn his inheritance over to his daughter. If he has no daughter, give his inheritance to his brothers. If he has no brothers, give his inheritance to his father's brothers. If his father had no brothers, give his inheritance to the nearest relative in his clan." (Numbers 27:8-11)

### Was This Patriarchal Inequality?

Some assume the Bible's inheritance laws were sexist, but this misses the practical wisdom at work. In an agricultural society, land passed to sons because husbands were responsible for farming and providing for their families. Daughters married into other households with their own land. This wasn't oppression—it was a system designed to prevent families from claiming land in multiple tribes simultaneously.

Where daughters had no brothers—a rare circumstance (roughly 3% of inheritance scenarios)—they inherited the land themselves and could farm it. This protected their future and their father's legacy.

The system also provided dowries to daughters as liquid assets, while sons inherited fixed land. Neither gender was forgotten; both were provided for according to different but equally important roles.

Most tellingly, this system emerged at the very beginning of Israel's legal history. Having never owned land before—they were slaves, nomads, foreigners—the Israelites were establishing their first inheritance laws. At its foundation, the patriarchal system was never sexist. It was practical and protective.

## The Tribes Secure Their Portions (Numbers 36:1-13)

But the story didn't end with Zelophehad's daughters. The tribal leaders soon realized a potential problem: if these daughters married outside their tribe, their inherited land would leave with them, diminishing their tribe's overall inheritance.

Their concern was real:

> "When a woman with land marries a man from another tribe, her inheritance becomes part of that tribe's allotment. Part of our inheritance will be taken away." (Numbers 36:3-4)

God's solution was elegant: the daughters could marry whom they wished, but only within their own tribal clan. This ensured the land remained within the tribe in perpetuity.

Why was this so crucial? Because of the Year of Jubilee. Every fifty years, the land returned to its original tribal families. This wasn't just a financial arrangement—it was theological. The promised land was permanently portioned to the twelve tribes of Israel as God's covenant gift. No tribe could be diminished. No family's inheritance could be lost forever.

This principle runs throughout Israel's history. A thousand years later, King Ahab wanted Naboth's vineyard for a vegetable garden. He offered better land or cash. Naboth refused:

> "The LORD forbid that I should give you the inheritance of my fathers." (1 Kings 21:3)

Naboth would not trade his ancestral land—not because he was stubborn, but because his land represented God's promise to his family. His roots went deeper than economics.

### The Theology Behind the Land

The promised land held far more than practical significance. God had sworn it to Abraham five hundred years before. The land promised physical blessings: food, peace, freedom from enemies, rest after desert wandering. But it promised far more than comfort.

The land symbolized being God's people, receiving holiness, finding forgiveness, discovering rest for weary souls, and securing safety. In a sense, inheriting the promised land meant inheriting a relationship with God Himself.

It's like owning land with treasure buried beneath. A wise person doesn't buy for the dirt—they buy for what it represents. When billionaire Clive Palmer controls the mining contracts for Pilbara iron ore, he's not rich because of rock. He's rich because of what that land represents: contracts worth billions in resources and promises.

So it was with Israel. The land was more than real estate. It was the tangible expression of God's faithfulness.

## Your Inheritance: The Better Promise (Revelation 21:25-27)

For the Christian, the parallel is stunning. We too have an inheritance—one far more valuable than any earthly property.

> "Nothing impure will ever enter it, nor will anyone who does what is shameful or deceitful, but only those whose names are written in the Lamb's book of life." (Revelation 21:27)

Through Christ's resurrection, we've already gained a foothold in heaven. We possess the heavenly real estate contract—the off-the-plan penthouse suite of the cosmos. Why? Because our names are written in the Lamb's book of life.

Peter describes this inheritance:

> "In his great mercy he has given us new birth into a living hope through the resurrection of Jesus Christ from the dead, and into an inheritance that can never perish, spoil or fade—kept in heaven for you." (1 Peter 1:3-4)

We inherit mercy. We inherit a living hope. We inherit resurrection—the defeat of death itself and the coming of joy beyond measure. Something that can never perish, spoil, fade, or die. We're longing for immortality and rest for our souls.

## Securing the Inheritance: Joshua and the Priestly Leadership (Numbers 27:12-23)

Before the Israelites could enter the land, Moses himself would not. God commanded him to view Canaan from Mount Abarim, then said:

> "After you have seen it, you too will be gathered to your people, as your brother Aaron was, for when the community rebelled at the waters in the Desert of Zin, both of you disobeyed my command to honor me as holy before their eyes." (Numbers 27:12-14)

Moses had struck the rock in anger instead of speaking to it. In an otherwise flawless leadership spanning four decades, this one act of rebellion cost him entry into the promised land. Like Zelophehad, Moses would die for his sin.

But God's plan didn't end with Moses. He would raise up Joshua, son of Nun—one of only two from the old generation who survived, because he had not rebelled and had always believed they should enter the land.

Moses interceded:

> "May the LORD, the God of the spirits of all mankind, appoint a man over this community to go out and come in before them, one who will lead them out and bring them in, so the LORD's people will not be like sheep without a shepherd." (Numbers 27:15-17)

Joshua would receive authority, but not all of it. Moses was such a towering figure that it took more than one successor. Joshua would stand before the priest Eleazar, who would consult the Urim and Thumim—the priestly means of discerning God's will. At the priest's command, the people would go out and come in, like sheep under a shepherd's care.

Over Israel's history, three roles would remain distinct:

- **The Priest:** Offered sacrifices on behalf of the people
- **The Judge/King:** Governed, led in battle, settled disputes
- **The Prophet:** Carried God's voice to the people

For a thousand years, these remained separate offices. Then, in one man, they converged again. Another Joshua—whose Greek name is Jesus—fulfilled all three roles:

- **Priest in the line of Melchizedek:** Who perfectly sacrificed for our sins
- **Prophet:** Who speaks God's voice and knows God's will perfectly
- **King:** The Shepherd who leads in love, defeats sin, and brings us into new pastures

This is our true inheritance—not Sydney real estate, but Christ the Lord.

## Guarding What Matters (1 Corinthians 10:1-13)

The Israelites' desperate desire for land teaches us something vital. They were so focused on securing God's promise that they fought to protect it fiercely—and we should do the same, though our inheritance is spiritual.

Paul reminds us:

> "For I do not want you to be ignorant of the fact, brothers, that our forefathers were all under the cloud and that they all passed through the sea. They all ate the same spiritual food and drank the same spiritual drink; for they drank from the spiritual rock that accompanied them, and that rock was Christ. Nevertheless, God was not pleased with most of them; their bodies were scattered over the desert." (1 Corinthians 10:1-5)

The first generation had every spiritual advantage—God's presence, provision, and protection. Yet most failed to enter because they gave in to idolatry, sexual immorality, testing God, and grumbling. "These things happened to them as examples and were written down as warnings for us" (1 Corinthians 10:11).

Paul's charge is clear: "So, if you think you are standing firm, be careful that you don't fall! No temptation has seized you except what is common to man. And God is faithful; he will not let you be tempted beyond what you can bear. But when you are tempted, he will also provide a way out so that you can stand up under it" (1 Corinthians 10:12-13).

## A Call to Priorities

In Sydney, people spend all their resources chasing real estate they may never fully own. They spend weekends at property auctions instead of gathering with God's people. They research interest rates and market trends with energy they should invest in studying Scripture and learning God's ways.

But here's the truth: this piece of dirt can never give us what we're looking for. Only the inheritance above can.

For parents especially, this is critical. The elders of Zelophehad's time fought fiercely to preserve the land so that a thousand years later, families like Naboth's could still defend their inheritance. What are we passing to our children? Not dirt and deeds, but faith and devotion. Not property portfolios, but faithfulness to God. Not material security, but commitment to Christ and His church.

There's no point in sacrificing church, faith, and God's Word so our children excel at piano, pass exams, or succeed in sports. These may help secure property in Sydney. They will do nothing to secure their future in heaven.

We are only one step away from glory. Let us not be seduced by what the world offers—appetites, arrogance, anxiety, anger, avarice, arousal. Instead, let us cling to our true inheritance with the same fierce devotion the Israelites showed their land.

Because in Christ, we have something infinitely better: a name written in the Lamb's book of life, and an inheritance that can never perish, spoil, or fade.
