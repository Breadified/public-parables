# John 1:1-18

**Title:** God Revealed Fully
**Date:** 6 March 2005
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## How Does God Reveal Himself?

Imagine someone across the room you'd like to know better. How would you introduce yourself? You might use clever words—a pickup line or witty observation. If separated by distance or time, you might write a letter, send an email, or share a blog. If more personal connection matters, you could send a trusted friend as your representative. Or you could show up in person for coffee or a conversation.

This question becomes profound when we ask it of God: *How does He reveal Himself to us?* If He wants our attention and belief, why doesn't He simply appear today? Why not make Himself undeniably visible?

Today we'll explore how God has chosen to reveal Himself—through His Word, His prophets, and ultimately through Himself in human flesh.

## 1. God Is Word (1:1-4)

> In the beginning was the Word, and the Word was with God, and the Word was God. He was in the beginning with God. (John 1:1-2)

Each Gospel tells Jesus's story differently. Mark begins with Him as an adult. Luke starts with His birth. Matthew opens with His family genealogy. But John takes us further back—to the very beginning of time itself, echoing Genesis 1:1.

### The Word as God's Self-Expression

What is this "Word"? For us English speakers, a word is merely a unit of language—abstract, static, a symbol on a page. But the Greek word *logos* carries far richer meaning.

In Greek philosophy, the Word represented the intelligent, rational principle governing the universe. The Greeks marveled at the world's order rather than chaos and sought to explain it through this cosmic Word—something like the Force in Star Wars. It was largely impersonal, an operating principle.

For Jews, however, the Word of God was deeply personal and dynamic. It was God's active self-expression, His chosen method of communicating and ruling. When the Old Testament speaks of "the word of the Lord," it describes something alive with divine authority, something to be heeded by men and angels alike.

John uses this pregnant concept to reveal who Jesus is: the Word—God's very self-expression made manifest.

### The Word as Creator

> All things were made through him, and without him was not any thing made that was made. (John 1:3)

The Word brings creation into existence. There is nothing—no sun, no tree, nothing at all—that came into being apart from Him. He is the sole agent of creation, needing no assistance. He accomplishes this as One who has always existed, who was present "in the beginning."

Notice the contrast: the Word is eternal; creation came into being through time.

### The Word as Life-Giver

> In him was life, and the life was the light of men. (John 1:4)

In Genesis, God's first act is to create light, separating it from darkness. His final creative act is to breathe life into humanity. Here in John, we find the Word performing these same works—the Word is life itself, the breath that sustains us.

### God's Word Cannot Be Separated from God

If the Word is God's self-expression, we understand why John insists: "the Word was with God, and the Word was God." They are inseparable. You cannot separate God from His Word any more than you can separate a person from their words. We are revealed through what we speak.

Yet there's a crucial difference between God and us. We humans can deceive; our words may not represent who we truly are. We ask "How are you?" without caring. We say we're fine when we're not. Our words can be untrue and powerless.

But God is different. He is "full of grace and truth." He cannot lie. Everything He says is true. His words perfectly reflect His character. When God speaks, it happens—"Let there be light" and light exists. When He makes a promise, His very nature guarantees its fulfillment:

> As the rain and the snow come down from heaven and do not return there, so shall my word be that goes out from my mouth: It shall not return to me empty but it shall accomplish that which I purpose, and shall succeed in the thing for which I sent it. (Isaiah 55:11)

### Application: Understanding God Through His Word

Because God's Word cannot be separated from God Himself, we must understand Him *through* His Word. Jesus Himself came speaking God's words—words that reveal the Father to us with intimacy and authority.

Some today try to separate God from His Word, focusing only on Jesus's actions and downplaying His teaching. But this misses the heart of revelation. The gospel is not merely about what Jesus did; it's about what He *said*. Words matter. God communicates through language because God *is* the Word.

Christianity is therefore a religion of words—of God's spoken truth—not merely of images, statues, or symbols. We evangelize through the same Word that created the universe.

We too must align our character with our words. Our yes must be yes, our no must be no. We are called to truthfulness in all our dealings, to keeping our appointments and commitments—to being people of our word, just as God is.

Be discerning about who you listen to. A teacher's character cannot be forever separated from their words. If someone's life contradicts their teaching, their teaching becomes suspect, however eloquent it may be. Their true character will eventually surface in their words.

## 2. God Reveals His Word (1:5-13)

### Through Prophets and John the Baptist

> There was a man sent from God, whose name was John. He came as a witness, to bear witness about the light, that all might believe through him. He was not the light, but came to bear witness about the light. (John 1:6-8)

This John is John the Baptist, distinct from John the apostle who wrote this Gospel. Like John the Baptist, many prophets throughout Israel's history testified to God's truth. Now John stands in that prophetic line, pointing to "the light"—Jesus Himself.

### Light: Symbol of God's Redemption

When Scripture speaks of light, it means far more than intellectual understanding. Throughout the Old Testament, light symbolizes hope, redemption, and the presence of God returning to His people:

> The people walking in darkness have seen a great light; on those living in the land of the shadow of death a light has dawned. (Isaiah 9:2)

> Who among you fears the LORD and obeys the word of his servant? Let him who walks in the dark, who has no light, trust in the name of the LORD and rely on his God. (Isaiah 50:10)

> I will also make you a light for the Gentiles, that you may bring my salvation to the ends of the earth. (Isaiah 49:6)

Israel had endured centuries of darkness—exile, loss, captivity under Assyria, Babylon, Greece, and Rome. Yet through the prophet Isaiah, God promised light, a new beginning. For 500 years they waited in darkness for this dawn.

Then came John the Baptist, the last in the line of prophets: "Behold, the light!"

### The Shocking Response: Rejection

> He was in the world, and the world was made through him, yet the world did not know him. He came to his own, and his own people did not receive him. (John 1:10-11)

Here is the tragedy. The very people who should have recognized Jesus—who had read Isaiah's prophecies, who awaited the Messiah—rejected Him. The light had come, but darkness chose to refuse it.

> This is the verdict: Light has come into the world, but men loved darkness instead of light because their deeds were evil. (John 3:19)

It's as if Genesis 1:3 were followed by: "But the darkness rejected the light." Something has gone terribly wrong.

**Illustration:** Imagine you're a father coming home from a difficult week. You rush to the door, keys forgotten, but your family is inside with a warm meal waiting. You ring the bell. No answer. You knock. No answer. You peer through the window and see them sitting at the table, eating—without you. You knock again, more urgently. Your own family ignores you. Your own home is closed to you.

This is what happened to the Word. He came to His own people, a Jew among Jews, and was denied entrance. They rejected Him so thoroughly that they eventually killed Him. He came home, and home locked the door against Him.

### The Two Responses: Rejection or Reception

> But to all who did receive him, who believed in his name, he gave the right to become children of God, who were born, not of blood nor of the will of the flesh nor of the will of man, but of God. (John 1:12-13)

There are only two responses: reject the light and remain in darkness, or receive Him and believe in His name. Those who receive Him become children of God—inheritors of God's estate, sharers in His riches.

This is why John wrote his Gospel:

> But these are written so that you may believe that Jesus is the Christ, the Son of God, and that by believing you may have life in his name. (John 20:31)

The Word who created light long ago can create new light in darkness. This is a new creation by the Word, a spiritual rebirth.

Yet this regeneration comes not from our choice or effort, but from God's will alone. It is "not of blood, nor of the will of the flesh nor of the will of man, but of God." We cannot earn it through our decisions or plans. Only God can grant this new birth, accomplished through the One "who became flesh and through blood on the cross."

### Application: Our Own Rejection

We are the children who have locked our Father out of our own house. We pursue our agendas and habits, ignoring the One who rightfully knocks on the door of our lives. We are His creation, yet we reject His rightful rule.

How do we reject light? By closing our eyes and ears to His Word. By refusing to wake up. By perpetual postponement—"I'll get serious about God later," just as first-year university students promise they'll wake up early once they adjust to the new schedule.

The result is that we cease to be His children. We join the world and the Jews of old in failing to grasp the light.

Yet there is comfort: believe in His name. In that name lies forgiveness for our falsehood, our sin, our selfishness.

## 3. God Reveals Himself Fully (1:14-18)

> And the Word became flesh and dwelt among us, and we have seen his glory, glory as of the only Son from the Father, full of grace and truth. (John 1:14)

Here is the climax of revelation: the Word—who is God, who created all things, who gives life—became flesh.

The sequence is striking:
1. The Word was God
2. Creation became (into existence)
3. John became (came into being)
4. Now the Word also becomes—flesh

God limited Himself to humanity. Rather than remaining infinite and unrestricted, He confined Himself to one place, one time: first-century Palestine. He experienced hunger, cold, tears, and all human bodily needs.

This would have scandalized many. Greeks viewed the material body as the worst state of being. Buddhists seek liberation *from* the body. Islam would consider God's integrity compromised by becoming human. God is transcendent, abstract, entirely separate from human messiness.

Yet here He is—the God of the universe, taking on flesh.

### Partial Revelation Becomes Full Revelation

In the past, God revealed Himself partially. Through creation, through the law, through the prophets—but never completely.

Moses asked God to show him His glory. God's response:

> I will cause all my goodness to pass in front of you, and I will proclaim my name, the LORD, in your presence. But you cannot see my face, for no one may see me and live. There is a place near me where you may stand on a rock. When my glory passes by, I will put you in a cleft in the rock and cover you with my hand until I have passed by. Then I will remove my hand and you will see my back; but my face must not be seen. (Exodus 33:19-23)

Moses could see only the afterglow of God's presence. To see God's face directly would be fatal.

Yet now:

> No one has ever seen God; the only God, who is at the Father's side, has made him known. (John 1:18)

God is invisible and dangerous to behold. But the Word who is at the Father's side has revealed Him—not through a letter, email, or blog site, but through Himself. A real person. Present. Touchable. Speaking.

> And from his fullness we have all received, grace upon grace. For the law was given through Moses; grace and truth came through Jesus Christ. (John 1:16-17)

God keeps giving grace upon grace. First through the law and the prophets. Now in these last days, He has revealed Himself fully through Christ. God's self-expression has manifested in humanity.

**Illustration:** When royalty visits a neighboring suburb, people feel the excitement of proximity. How much more thrilling to actually meet them face-to-face! But imagine if the King of the universe—the Creator Himself—came and visited us. Imagine Him saying hello.

### Application: God Has Come

The most common objection we hear is: "Why doesn't God just show Himself now? Then I would believe."

But God has shown Himself. He has come to earth. We cannot say "Why doesn't God reveal Himself?" He has—definitively, historically, undeniably.

Why not again today? God is not a toy to be summoned at our pleasure. Part of His earthly limitation was being in one place at one time. He came once, for all people, for all time. He died once for all mankind. This is sufficient.

We who live 2,000 years later cannot physically witness the events in first-century Palestine. We are bound by time and space as Jesus was during His incarnation. Yet we are not disqualified from belief.

We rely on eyewitnesses for everything—legal proceedings, accident reports, historical facts. We believe these accounts without being present. John the apostle, an eyewitness to these events, wrote this Gospel so that we may believe.

John's repeated purpose is clear: "that you may believe." As you read John's Gospel in the coming weeks, you'll encounter testimony designed to awaken faith in the Word made flesh.

---

## Conclusion

How does God reveal Himself?

- Through His mighty works in creation
- Through the giving of the Law
- Through the prophets He sent to represent Him
- Or He could come and represent Himself

Jesus, the Word who was with God and who is God, has done precisely that. He came in person.

The one who was with God from the beginning is fully qualified to reveal Him. Believe in Him, that you may enter the light and receive life.
