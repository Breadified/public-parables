# John 18:1-40

**Title:** Glorify the Son
**Date:** 14 April 2019
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Betrayal

Throughout Scripture, betrayal stands out as one of the deepest wounds—far more painful than opposition from enemies. Betrayal is unique because it can only come from someone close to you, someone you've trusted. It breaks the bond of trust itself.

Consider the story of Osama bin Laden. For years, the world's greatest military powers couldn't find him, despite searching the mountains of Afghanistan and Pakistan. Yet he was ultimately betrayed by one of his own wives, driven by jealousy. Even the most powerful forces pale against the penetration of trust from within.

Today, we encounter Jesus facing betrayal on multiple fronts—each revealing something profound about how His faithfulness stands in stark contrast.

## Betrayed by Judas (John 18:2-11)

> Now Judas, who betrayed him, knew the place, because Jesus had often met there with his disciples.

The very word "betrayal" appears repeatedly in these verses—he is "the betrayed one" (v.2) and "the traitor" (v.5). This betrayal is emphatic and intimate. Judas knew the place because Jesus had spent countless hours there with his disciples. It's the familiar gathering spot, a place thick with memories—their favorite meeting place, their refuge, their sanctuary. For three years, Jesus walked with these twelve men daily. They were His closest friends.

When Judas leads them there, it's like being broken up with at your favorite restaurant—the place now poisoned by treachery.

> So Judas came to the garden, guiding a detachment of soldiers and some officials from the chief priests and the Pharisees. They were carrying torches, lanterns and weapons.

A Roman cohort—hundreds of trained soldiers, not a small police force. Torches and weapons suggest a military operation, a witch hunt, a war. All this force mobilized against one unarmed rabbi. The sheer overkill reveals their fear and determination.

### A Pattern in Scripture

This moment echoes another betrayal centuries before. In John 18:1, Jesus "crossed the Kidron Valley" to a garden—the same crossing King David made when fleeing from his own son Absalom's coup:

> The whole countryside wept aloud as all the people passed by. The king also crossed the Kidron Valley, and all the people moved on toward the wilderness... David continued up the Mount of Olives, weeping as he went... Now David had been told, "Ahithophel is among the conspirators with Absalom." (2 Samuel 15:23, 30-31)

Like David, Jesus faces betrayal by someone close. And like Ahithophel, Judas meets the same tragic fate—both betray their lords and meet violent ends. This parallel reveals a pattern: betrayal of God's anointed leaders cuts across all of Scripture.

### Christian Betrayal Today

What makes betrayal so devastating is that it can only be perpetrated by those on the inside. An enemy's attack hurts less than a friend's. And sometimes, Christians betray other Christians in ways that wound the body of Christ deeply.

Consider the case of Israel Folau, a rugby player who quoted biblical passages about sin and judgment on social media. Whether politically wise or not, he was quoting Scripture and Christian teaching accumulated over two thousand years. Yet he faced enormous backlash, even from other Christians who joined the "witch hunt" against him—coming with "torches and weapons" like Judas, condemning one of their own for attempting to follow Scripture.

The world's response is understandable. But Christian betrayal troubles me more. We should commend sincere attempts to follow Scripture, even when the timing or context seems wrong. Fellow believers should never be offered up as sacrifices to satisfy the world's demands.

Some Christians operate like Switzerland—always in the middle, sitting in judgment, switching allegiances based on who seems to be winning. But in times of spiritual conflict, that's not neutrality; it's cowardice. We cannot betray our brothers and sisters by piling on when the media does.

If we have concerns about how a Christian is communicating truth, we address it privately (Matthew 18; 1 Corinthians 6:4), in the church family. But we never hand them over to the world to devour. Instead, we should defend them.

The truth is simple: Christians don't betray other Christians for following Scripture. What have they actually done wrong? If someone at Mardi Gras celebrates that lifestyle, we don't care. Why do we care if someone warns about judgment for sin? If you don't believe in hell anyway, it's like complaining that Gollum will be punished in Mordor—why does it offend you?

When the media mob jumps on someone, our instinct should be to withdraw, not join. Because when you betray your brothers to curry favor with the world, you are Judas. And they will come for you next. When that day comes, there will be no one to stand with you, because you abandoned yours.

## Betrayed by the Nation (John 18:12-14, 19-23, 28-40)

Jesus also faces betrayal by those in authority—those entrusted with upholding justice itself.

> Then the detachment of soldiers with its commander and the Jewish officials arrested Jesus. They bound him and brought him first to Annas, who was the father-in-law of Caiaphas, the high priest that year.

"Bound"—like a lamb for slaughter. The imagery is stark and deliberate.

> Caiaphas was the one who had advised the Jewish leaders that it would be good if one man died for the people.

The bitter irony: Caiaphas, unknowingly, speaks a profound theological truth. One man dying for the people is exactly what will happen—but not as he intends. What should be a stumbling block becomes the very means of salvation.

Jesus faces six trials across the Gospels, but John focuses on just two: before Annas and before Pilate. This is intentional—John is emphasizing the legal perversion of justice.

### A Show Trial

> Meanwhile, the high priest questioned Jesus about his disciples and his teaching. "I have spoken openly to the world," Jesus replied. "I always taught in synagogues or at the temple, where all the Jews come together. I said nothing in secret. Why question me? Ask those who heard me. Surely they know what I said." When Jesus said this, one of the officials nearby slapped him in the face. "Is this the way you answer the high priest?" he demanded.

Jesus exposes the trial's fundamental injustice. By rabbinic law, trials must occur in daylight. You cannot arrest someone at night and execute them by morning—that is tyranny, not justice. This is the stuff of totalitarian states, not Jewish law.

Worse, they have no witnesses. Deuteronomy 15:19 demands it: "One witness is not enough to convict anyone... A matter must be established by the testimony of two or three witnesses." Yet they're trying to trap Jesus into incriminating himself through interrogation, hoping he'll say something they can use against him.

The violence—a slap to the face—exposes the pretense. This is theater. And when Jesus points out the illegality of their procedure, they respond with brutality.

### Pilate's Role

The trial continues before Pilate, where the injustice deepens through irony:

> Then the Jewish leaders took Jesus from Caiaphas to the palace of the Roman governor. By now it was early morning, and to avoid ceremonial uncleanness they did not enter the palace, because they wanted to be able to eat the Passover.

Astounding hypocrisy: they obsess over ceremonial purity while breaking their own procedural laws and murdering an innocent man.

> So Pilate came out to them and asked, "What charges are you bringing against this man?" "If he were not a criminal," they replied, "we would not have handed him over to you."

They demand a rubber stamp. When Pilate recognizes this is outside his jurisdiction:

> "Take him yourselves and judge him by your own law." "But we have no right to execute anyone," they objected.

Only Rome can execute—so they've orchestrated this entire theater to force the Roman governor's hand. Even more, Jews and Romans are enemies, yet they collude because both want Jesus dead.

After dialogue that John abbreviates, Pilate reaches his verdict:

> "I find no basis for a charge against him. But it is your custom for me to release to you one prisoner at the time of the Passover. Do you want me to release 'the king of the Jews'?"

Pilate declares Him innocent. But:

> They shouted back, "No, not him! Give us Barabbas!" Now Barabbas had taken part in an uprising.

Barabbas—a murderer, a rebel, a criminal. "Barabbas" means "son of the father." They choose the false son of the father over the true Son of the Father. They choose a murderer over the Messiah. Justice collapses entirely.

### The Betrayal of Justice Itself

Consider a modern parallel: Archbishop George Pell was convicted and sentenced to six years for allegedly assaulting two boys. Yet the case had profound problems:

- Only one witness—the accuser, who never faced cross-examination in court.
- The only other possible witness denied it happened, and has since died.
- Alibi witnesses testified Pell was never alone.
- The first trial voted 11-1 to acquit, but because it wasn't unanimous, they retried.
- The trial was held in secret, unpublished, without witnesses.

Whether guilty or innocent, procedural justice was not served. This is what happens when the system meant to protect the innocent becomes corrupted.

Jesus' trials reveal a hard truth: **Justice can go wrong.** Systems designed to protect can become instruments of injustice. Bad judges exist. The same people who were corrupt lawyers don't suddenly become saints when they wear robes.

This doesn't mean we stop trying for justice or stop obeying the law. But we must not place our ultimate faith in earthly systems. There is a Judge who judges truly, whose verdict is righteous, and whose kingdom is not of this world. That Judge awaits—for Annas, Caiaphas, Pilate, and all who betray the innocent.

## Betrayed by His Disciples (John 18:15-18, 25-27)

Finally, Jesus is betrayed by His closest friend, Peter.

> Simon Peter and another disciple were following Jesus. Because this disciple was known to the high priest, he went with Jesus into the high priest's courtyard, but Peter had to wait outside at the door. The other disciple, who was known to the high priest, came back, spoke to the servant girl on duty there and brought Peter in.

Probably John, the beloved disciple, who had access to the high priest's house.

> "You aren't one of this man's disciples too, are you?" she asked Peter. He replied, "I am not."

A simple servant girl's question triggers Peter's denial. And he's not alone:

> It was cold, and the servants and officials stood around a fire they had made to keep warm. Peter also was standing with them, warming himself.

He blends in, trying to be invisible. But the questions keep coming:

> Meanwhile, Simon Peter was still standing there warming himself. So they asked him, "You aren't one of his disciples too, are you?" He denied it, saying, "I am not."

And a third time:

> One of the high priest's servants, a relative of the man whose ear Peter had cut off, challenged him, "Didn't I see you with him in the garden?" Again Peter denied it, and at that moment a rooster began to crow.

The rooster—that detail carries weight because of what Jesus had said just hours before:

> "Lord, why can't I follow you now? I will lay down my life for you." Then Jesus answered, "Will you really lay down your life for me? Very truly I tell you, before the rooster crows, you will disown me three times!" (John 13:37-38)

**How did Peter fall so far?** From "I will lay down my life for you" to "I am not"—three times. Three denials. Was it shame? Fear? Embarrassment? Loss of faith? Likely a combination.

The process is subtle. Peter doesn't decide overnight to abandon Jesus. Rather, small compromises accumulate. The first assignment comes, then another. Fellowship drops away. Prayer feels less urgent. Bible reading fades. Then a party on Saturday night, a work shift on Sunday, university clubs pulling you in different directions—all for legitimate reasons. But gradually, there's no room for Jesus anymore. You've shifted into a new pattern of living.

Before you realize it, Jesus becomes a distant childhood memory. A friend from church drifts away to study for exams, promising to read the Bible alone. But away from Christian community, it's too easy to drift. A young Christian falls in love with someone who isn't interested in Jesus, so he stops attending church to accommodate her. At first, he thinks he'll eventually share the gospel. But there's always something else to do, and bringing up Jesus feels awkward. A year in, he realizes she'll never come to faith, but by then he's in love and trapped. Jesus moves to the background—maybe someday he can call Him back.

**We betray Jesus not with dramatic gestures, but through gradual withdrawal.** And when we do, does His heart break? Does He look at us with disappointment?

## Jesus' Loyalty

Yet in the face of all this betrayal—by Judas, by the authorities, by His own disciples—Jesus shows unwavering loyalty to God's will.

> Jesus, knowing all that was going to happen to him, went out and asked them, "Who is it you want?" "Jesus of Nazareth," they replied. "I am he," Jesus said.

When Jesus speaks these words—*I am*—they fall back and collapse. The Greek echoes Exodus 3:14, where God reveals His name to Moses: "I AM WHO I AM." Jesus claims the divine name, the very presence and power of God.

In stark contrast stands Peter: *I am not. I am not. I am not.* Three times denying his association with the Son of God.

But Jesus: *I am. I am. I am.* Three affirmations of His identity despite the armed cohort, despite knowing what awaits. When faced with following God's will, Jesus embraces it completely.

> Again he asked them, "Who is it you want?" "Jesus of Nazareth," they said. Jesus answered, "I told you that I am he. If you are looking for me, then let these men go." This happened so that the words he had spoken would be fulfilled: "I have not lost one of those you gave me."

**Jesus made a choice.** He surrenders Himself completely to protect His disciples. He does not betray God's will.

> Then Simon Peter, who had a sword, drew it and struck the high priest's servant, cutting off his right ear. Jesus commanded Peter, "Put your sword away! Shall I not drink the cup the Father has given me?"

The cup Jesus refers to is not merely physical suffering—flogging, crucifixion, the scorn of enemies. It is something deeper and greater: the spiritual agony of bearing the sins of the world, of experiencing the judgment and wrath of God that our sins deserve. It is drinking the cup of God's justice poured out.

He is tempted. The option to turn back exists. But He remains faithful. He does not betray God's will.

> He does this all for us. For the idolators, the fornicators, the homosexual, the thief. He does this for all of us who have sinned against God, out of love.

## Our Choice

So we are left with a question: **Will you be loyal to Him?**

Don't betray Jesus when times get tough. There will be many temptations—the desire to earn more money and overwork, romantic relationships that pull you away, the desire to look cool, the pressure to excel in studies. These aren't inherently evil, but they become betrayal when they replace Jesus at the center of your life.

The pattern is usually gradual. Most people don't wake up one day and decide to abandon faith. Instead, one small compromise leads to another. Without Christian community, without prayer and Scripture, you drift. Activities pile up. Before long, you've built a new pattern of life where Jesus simply doesn't fit anymore.

Will you deny Him or confess Him? Will you choose Barabbas or Jesus? Will you bind yourself to Him and follow Him into glory?

Jesus stands bound before you—innocent, faithful, willing to drink the cup of judgment for your sake. The choice is yours. Stay with Him. Don't let the world's pressure, the busyness of life, or the slow creep of compromise steal your loyalty from the One who was perfectly loyal to you.
