# 1 Samuel 28:1-25

**Title:** The King is Dead

**Date:** September 11, 2022

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Fear of the Philistines (Verses 1-8)

The Philistines gathered their forces against Israel, and Saul's response reveals the depth of his spiritual crisis. When he saw their army assembled at Shunem, he was gripped by terror—the Hebrew emphasizes this through repetition: he was both "afraid" and filled with "terror," literally quaking with fear. This is the same visceral trembling Israel experienced at Mount Sinai when receiving the law.

> In those days the Philistines gathered their forces to fight against Israel... When Saul saw the Philistine army, he was afraid; terror filled his heart. (1 Samuel 28:1, 5)

In his desperation, Saul sought guidance from the Lord, but God's silence was deafening. The Lord did not answer him through dreams, through the Urim (the priestly device), or through prophets. This silence was the consequence of Saul's own choices: he had expelled the mediums and spiritists from the land, he had killed the priests at Nob, and Samuel—God's prophet—was dead.

> He inquired of the Lord, but the Lord did not answer him by dreams or Urim or prophets. (1 Samuel 28:6)

Desperate and disoriented, Saul did the unthinkable. He sought out a medium in Endor—the very practice he had forbidden in his kingdom. He disguised himself and went by night to the woman who practiced divination, asking her to consult a spirit and bring up Samuel from the dead. This was not merely desperate; it was willfully disobedient. The Law was clear: consulting mediums and spiritists was an abomination that would separate one from God's people.

> Saul then said to his attendants, "Find me a woman who is a medium, so I may go and inquire of her." (1 Samuel 28:7)

## No Fear of Disobedience (Verses 9-19)

The woman's initial hesitation reveals something profound. She protested that Saul had cut off all the mediums from the land—why was he now setting a trap for her life? Yet Saul, swearing by the Lord himself, promised she would not be punished. There is tragic irony here: the king invokes God's name while actively breaking God's commands. Worse still, Saul exempts himself from the very law he imposed on others—the arrogance of ruling power.

When the woman saw Samuel materialize, she cried out in surprise. This reaction is telling. If she were a genuine conduit to the spiritual realm, why would she be shocked at achieving what she purportedly does for a living? Her surprise suggests that something unexpected occurred—perhaps God himself, against all occult practice, allowed His servant Samuel to appear to rebuke the king.

> When the woman saw Samuel, she cried out at the top of her voice and said to Saul, "Why have you deceived me? You are Saul!" (1 Samuel 28:12)

Samuel's response to Saul reveals God's ultimate sovereignty. No medium controls the spiritual realm. God alone raises the dead and brings life or judgment as He wills. Samuel's words offered no new information—only what God had already spoken through the prophet years before: the kingdom had been torn from Saul's hands and given to David because of his disobedience against the Amalekites.

> The Lord has done what he predicted through me. The Lord has torn the kingdom out of your hands and given it to one of your neighbors—to David. Because you did not obey the Lord or carry out his fierce wrath against the Amalekites, the Lord has done this to you today. (1 Samuel 28:17-18)

This is a profound lesson for us. We often act as though God has not spoken, seeking answers to questions He has already settled through His Word. We ask Him what His will is for our lives when Scripture is already clear: turn from sin and serve Him. We seek guidance on marriage when the principles are established: marry a believer of marriageable age. We wonder about obedience when God's commands are written. Too often, we ask questions to delay or obscure what we already know we should do.

But Samuel delivers one final, terrible word: death. Saul and his sons would join Samuel that very day. The one who sought guidance through forbidden means received only judgment.

> The Lord will deliver both Israel and you into the hands of the Philistines, and tomorrow you and your sons will be with me. (1 Samuel 28:19)

## Fear of God (Verses 20-25)

When Saul heard Samuel's words, he fell to the ground, filled with fear. His strength was gone—he had eaten nothing all day and night. The woman, seeing him greatly shaken, compassionately urged him to eat. She prepared a meal, butchering a fattened calf and baking unleavened bread.

There is a pattern in Saul's life: he began by seeking lost donkeys and found a kingdom, yet he could not keep it. Now, facing his final hours, he shares a meal. This meal is his last. The next day, he will fall in battle with the Philistines.

> Immediately Saul fell full length on the ground, filled with fear because of Samuel's words. His strength was gone, for he had eaten nothing all that day and all that night. (1 Samuel 28:20)

The silence of God is sometimes more terrifying than His judgment. We live in an age where God does not speak through thunder or visible signs. We have His complete Word in Scripture. The question is not whether God has spoken, but whether we will listen.

There are two paths. The first is Saul's: to experience God's silence as judgment, to feel the cold withdrawal of His presence because of our rebellion. The second is to understand His silence as sufficiency—He has said all He needs to say in His Word, and His grace is complete for those who trust Him.

Ecclesiastes reminds us that God has set eternity in the human heart. We are not meant to find satisfaction in the temporal, the magical, or the forbidden. As Pascal observed, there is a God-shaped vacuum in every human heart that only God can fill.

> He has also set eternity in the human heart; yet no one can fathom what God has done from beginning to end. (Ecclesiastes 3:11)

When we turn away from God's Word, we do not become enlightened—we become desperate. Modern people who reject Christianity do not transcend to something higher; they regress to ancient paganism: ouija boards, tarot cards, crystals, and mysticism. The vacuum remains unfilled, and the human heart continues to hunger for truth that cannot be found in divination or occultism.

But there is an answer. It is found not in seeking guidance through forbidden means, but in submitting to the God who has already spoken. It is found in the One who rose from the dead, Jesus Christ, who is the final and ultimate word of God to His people.
