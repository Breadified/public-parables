# Romans 3:27–4:25

**Title:** The Counting of God
**Date:** 25 November 2012 (SMCC); 17 December 2023 (Arise)

## Introduction: What Counts with God?

We often speak of "counting" in everyday life—accountants balance books, we keep accounts of what we owe and what we're owed. But the deeper question Scripture raises is: *What counts when it comes to God? What doesn't count?*

The answer, throughout Romans, is profoundly simple: **Faith is what counts.**

---

## 1. Abraham's Faith Counts; Works Do Not (3:27–4:5)

### The Meaning of "Credited"

> What does Scripture say? Abraham believed God, and it was credited to him as righteousness. (4:3)

The word "credited" is accounting language—to reckon, to count, to determine by mathematical process. When we say Abraham's faith was "credited as righteousness," we're using the language of a ledger book.

Similarly, "believed" and "faith" are the same word in Greek. In English, we sometimes distinguish between casual "belief" and deeper "faith," but Scripture treats them identically. Abraham *believed* (had faith in) God, and this act of trust was counted as righteousness—that is, as right standing before Him.

### Righteousness Before God

The Greek words "justify" (δικαιόω) and "righteousness" (δικαιοσύνη) share the same root. Imagine standing before a judge: you are either innocent (righteous) or guilty. God declares Abraham righteous—not because of his works, but because of his faith.

> If, in fact, Abraham was justified by works, he had something to boast about—but not before God. (4:2)

We learned in Romans 3:20 that "no one will be declared righteous in God's sight by the works of the law." There is only one way righteousness can be credited to your account: through faith.

### Faith Is Not a Work

> Now to the one who works, wages are not credited as a gift but as an obligation. However, to the one who does not work but trusts God who justifies the ungodly, their faith is credited as righteousness. (4:4–5)

This raises a puzzle: faith *feels* like work. I choose to believe. I am an agent in the process. But this is a trick of language.

Faith, by its very nature, is a *transitive verb*—it requires an object outside itself. You cannot simply "have faith"; you must have "faith *in* something." You cannot merely "believe"; you must "believe *in*" someone. This is the crucial distinction.

**An illustration:** Imagine you're caught in a rip current, thrashing about in panic. A lifesaver calls to you: "Let go! Stop trying to save yourself!" You stop fighting, you become calm and passive, and the lifesaver pulls you to safety. Did you save yourself by choosing to be calm? No. The lifesaver saved you. You simply *trusted* his words and his strength.

In the same way, faith is not my work—it is my trust *in God's work*. Faith looks outward, not inward. It rests on something beyond myself.

### The Good News

All the world's religions teach the same basic message: **Do. Do. Do.**
- Buddhism: Follow the Four Noble Truths
- Islam: Observe the Five Pillars
- Secularism: Asceticism—deny yourself, punish yourself at the gym, count calories, be watched constantly

Even modern secular spirituality has become a religion of deprivation and self-judgment. We've replaced the confession box with Instagram, where we present our "righteous" lives for others to evaluate.

How exhausting. Always being monitored. Always wondering if you're doing enough.

But into this endless striving, Jesus speaks a single word: **Done.**

Because faith in Jesus is counted as righteousness, because His work is complete, we are free from the tyranny of self-justification. We can stop performing. We can rest.

## 2. David's Faith Counts; Sin Is Not Counted (4:6–8)

### The Blessing of Forgiveness

Paul now turns to another Old Testament figure—King David—who teaches the same truth:

> Blessed are those whose transgressions are forgiven, whose sins are covered. Blessed is the one whose sin the Lord will never count against them. (4:7–8; cf. Psalm 32:1–2)

"Blessed" means happy, favored. And why? Because *transgressions are forgiven* and *sins are covered*. Most importantly: *the Lord will not count his sin against him*.

### Faith as Vulnerability

This psalm was traditionally attributed to David after his grievous sins of murder and adultery. How did he find restoration? The answer comes in Psalm 32:5:

> Then I acknowledged my sin to you and did not cover up my iniquity. I said, I will confess my transgressions to the Lord. And you forgave the guilt of my sin.

David's faith consisted of three acts:
- **Acknowledging** his sin
- **Not covering it up**
- **Confessing** it to the Lord

This is an act of trust—radically vulnerable, laying everything bare before God, trusting that He will treat you with mercy rather than judgment. This is faith at its deepest: throwing yourself completely on God's mercy, saying, "It's up to You now."

David did not try to work his way back into God's favor. He did not rely on the law to restore himself. He simply acknowledged his guilt and trusted God to forgive. This has always been the heart of the Jewish experience: *sins shall no longer count against them*.

### The Cost of Forgiveness

Yet we must not romanticize forgiveness. Forgiveness always comes at a cost.

> Where there is a credit, there is also a debit.

In accounting terms, nothing simply vanishes. Someone always pays. The nature of true forgiveness is that the injured party absorbs the debt. There is no magic where sin disappears—it is transferred.

**A true story from Texas:** A driver was pulled over near Christmas with an outdated registration. He explained to the officer that he didn't have the money for renewal—it was either feed his children or pay the registration fee. The officer had to ticket him. Justice demanded it.

But the officer did something extraordinary: he slipped a $100 bill into the ticket envelope.

When the driver opened it and found the cash, he broke down in his car. That $100 was exactly enough to pay the fine *and* update the registration on both his car and his wife's vehicle.

This is what God has done for us. He must punish sin—that is justice. But He has provided a way to satisfy that justice: He sent His "hundred dollar bill"—Jesus Christ—to pay what we owe. Someone has paid so that we can go free.

> God is now offering a recount of your life. Sin no longer counts against you. It is a chance to start over.

## 3. The Uncircumcised's Faith Counts; Circumcision Does Not (4:9–12)

### Circumcision: A Sign, Not a Cause

> Is this blessedness only for the circumcised, or also for the uncircumcised? We have been saying that Abraham's faith was credited to him as righteousness. Under what circumstances was it credited? Was it after he was circumcised, or before? It was not after, but before! (4:9–10)

Circumcision was central to Jewish identity. Every male child was circumcised on the eighth day as a sign of the covenant God made with Abraham (Genesis 17). It was the physical marker that set Israel apart from all other nations.

But here is the surprising truth: Abraham was declared righteous *before* he was circumcised. Genesis 15:6 tells us that Abraham believed God, and it was credited to him as righteousness—while he was still uncircumcised.

Circumcision came later:

> And he received circumcision as a sign, a seal of the righteousness that he had by faith while he was still uncircumcised. (4:11)

A sign points to something beyond itself. A wedding ring is a sign of marriage—but the ring doesn't *make* you married. If you wear a ring without being married, you're not married. If you're married but lose your ring, you're still married.

In the same way, circumcision was an outward marker of the inward reality of faith. It was designed to remind the Jewish people of the faith that Abraham had *before* circumcision. The sign was meant to lead you back to faith.

### Abraham: Father of All Who Believe

> So then, he is the father of all who believe but have not been circumcised, in order that righteousness might be credited to them. And he is then also the father of the circumcised who not only are circumcised but who also follow in the footsteps of the faith that our father Abraham had before he was circumcised. (4:11–12)

Abraham is the father of believers *regardless of ethnicity*. What makes you a child of Abraham is not your ethnic background or your observance of ritual laws, but your *faith*.

### The Law as a Sign Pointing to Faith

The law served a similar function to circumcision. God gave Israel the law—the Ten Commandments, the sacrificial system, the food laws—to set them apart from all other nations. Their identity as God's people became inseparable from their identity as the nation of the law.

But in their zeal to keep the law, the Jewish people sometimes forgot what the law was *for*. They remembered the sign but forgot to visit the attraction.

> The law has always been about faith.

As Romans 3:27 tells us:

> Where, then, is boasting? It is excluded. Because of what law? The law that requires works? No, because of the law that requires faith.

This is not a "law of faith" in the sense of a new set of rules to follow. Rather, the law has always pointed toward faith. When you follow the law rightly, it leads you to recognize your need for grace, your need for God's mercy—which is faith.

This is why Romans 3:31 says:

> Do we, then, nullify the law by this faith? Not at all! Rather, we uphold the law.

Faith does not abolish the law; faith fulfills what the law was always meant to do. As Jesus Himself said:

> Do not think that I have come to abolish the Law or the Prophets; I have not come to abolish them but to fulfill them. (Matthew 5:17)

The law was a tutor, a guardian pointing you toward faith in Christ.

## 4. Too Many to Count: The Promise Extends to All (4:13–25)

### Faith, Not Law, Secures the Promise

> It was not through the law that Abraham and his offspring received the promise that he would be heir of the world, but through the righteousness that comes by faith. (4:13)

Abraham's promise—to be the father of many nations, to inherit the world—came through faith, not through law. If the promise depended on perfect law-keeping, then faith would be meaningless and the promise worthless.

> For if those who depend on the law are heirs, faith means nothing and the promise is worthless, because the law brings wrath. And where there is no law there is no transgression. (4:14–15)

But God gave the promise through faith precisely so that it would be *by grace*—unearned, undeserved, available to all:

> Therefore, the promise comes by faith, so that it may be by grace and may be guaranteed to all Abraham's offspring—not only to those who are of the law but also to those who have the faith of Abraham. He is the father of us all. (4:16)

### The Nature of Abraham's Faith

What, then, is the character of this faith? Romans 4:18–21 paints a portrait:

> Against all hope, Abraham in hope believed and so became the father of many nations, just as it had been said to him, So shall your offspring be. Without weakening in his faith, he faced the fact that his body was as good as dead—since he was about a hundred years old—and that Sarah's womb was also dead. Yet he did not waver through unbelief regarding the promise of God, but was strengthened in his faith and gave glory to God, being fully persuaded that God had power to do what he had promised.

Notice the characteristics of genuine faith:
- **It believes God's promises** even when circumstances contradict them
- **It can grow stronger** over time, even starting as small as a mustard seed
- **It persists even when circumstances are impossible**—when both Abraham's body and Sarah's womb were dead

This faith gave glory to God because it acknowledged God's power over circumstances.

### Written for You

> This is why it was credited to him as righteousness. But the words "it was credited to him" were written not for him alone, but also for us, to whom God will credit righteousness—for us who believe in him who raised Jesus our Lord from the dead, who was delivered over to death for our sins and was raised to life for our justification. (4:22–25)

Abraham's story is not merely ancient history. It is written for *you*. What was credited to Abraham—righteousness through faith—is credited to all who believe.

And the object of our faith is greater than Abraham's faith: we believe in Him who raised Jesus from the dead, who was delivered for our sins and raised for our justification. The same counting that applied to Abraham applies to every believer.

Too many to count.

## 5. One God, One Way of Justification (4:29–30)

### God Is the God of All

> Or is God the God of Jews only? Is he not the God of Gentiles too? Yes, of Gentiles too, since there is only one God, who will justify the circumcised by faith and the uncircumcised through that same faith. (4:29–30)

If God is one, then His way of justification is one. The same faith that justified Abraham (before circumcision), the same faith that justified David (in his confession and vulnerability), justifies both Jews and Gentiles today. There are not two paths to God—one for the law-keepers and one for others. There is only one: faith.

Ethnicity does not determine your standing with God. Neither does your ritual observance. What matters is faith.

> For no one is a Jew who is merely one outwardly, nor is circumcision outward and physical. (Romans 2:28)

---

## Conclusion: A Living Faith for Today

The great theme of Romans 3:27–4:25 is simple but revolutionary: **It has always been about faith.**

Abraham's faith counted. David's confession counted. The circumcision counted as a *sign* of faith, but not as a substitute for it. And through faith, righteousness is credited not to one nation, but to all Abraham's offspring—everyone who believes.

What does this mean for you?

**Confess to God.** Like David, bring your sins, your failures, your shame into the light. Lay them at His feet. Trust His mercy. You may be surprised at His reaction—not condemnation, but forgiveness.

**Grow in your faith.** Let it strengthen over time. Believe God's promises even when circumstances seem impossible. Let your faith give glory to God.

**Remember that the law points to faith.** Continue reading Scripture, but read it Christologically—with the understanding that it all points to Jesus, to His work, to His justification of the ungodly.

**You are counted.** If God counted Abraham as righteous through faith, He counts you as righteous through faith in Christ. Your sins are no longer counted against you. The debt has been paid.

You are no longer accountable. You are free.
