# Philippians 3:12-21

**Title:** Joy: Living for Another World  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Pressing On to Heaven (12-17)

The Christian life is a race, and like any race, it requires perseverance. Paul opens this passage by emphasizing that our journey is not yet complete—we have not arrived at our destination, and we must keep pressing forward.

> Not that I have already obtained all this, or have already attained my goal, but I press on to take hold of that for which Christ Jesus took hold of me. Brothers and sisters, I do not consider myself yet to have taken hold of it. But one thing I do: Forgetting what is behind and straining toward what is ahead, I press on toward the goal to win the prize for which God has called me heavenward in Christ Jesus. (Philippians 3:12-14)

Notice the repeated emphasis: "I press on," "I strain toward," "I pursue the goal." This is an incomplete race. We are a future-oriented people, called to look ahead, not behind.

### Leave Behind the Past (v.13)

In a race, you don't look back. When John Landy was leading Roger Bannister in the famous 1954 Empire Games mile race, Landy made a critical mistake—he glanced back to see how far ahead he was. In that split second, Bannister seized the opportunity and sprinted past him.

The principle applies to our spiritual journey. Our past can strangle us if we let it. We accumulate excuses: "My father didn't teach me." "My mother wasn't there." "I failed that interview." "I'm not smart enough, not strong enough, not rich enough." We convince ourselves we cannot move forward because we are ill-equipped.

But here is the beauty of being a Christian: we can leave the past behind—not because it doesn't exist or hasn't affected us, but because we have been given a new past. We inherit Christ's past. His death is our death. His history is now our history. When we stand before God, our resume has been changed at the top. Our former failures are covered by His sacrifice. Knowing this love, fully assured and confident in what Christ has done, we can press forward toward the prize.

### Keep Your Eyes on Those Who Persevere (v.15-17)

> All of us, then, who are mature should take such a view of things. And if on some point you think differently, that too God will make clear to you. Only let us live up to what we have already attained. Join together in following my example, brothers and sisters, and just as you have us as a model, keep your eyes on those who live as we do. (Philippians 3:15-17)

Paul doesn't call us to be the fastest or the greatest—only to keep running the race. He invites us to look at those around us who persevere despite setbacks. Paul himself was in prison when he wrote this, yet he was still pressing on. Epaphroditus had nearly died, yet he continued. In your own church, there are brothers and sisters committed to Christ, trusting in Him no matter what. They face obstacles and take them one at a time. They serve faithfully, week after week. Follow their example. Not because they are perfect, but because they keep running.

## The Destination: Destruction or Glory (18-21)

This race is not casual or optional. The stakes are eternal.

> For, as I have often told you before and now tell you again even with tears, many live as enemies of the cross of Christ. Their destiny is destruction, their god is their stomach, and their glory is in their shame. Their mind is set on earthly things. (Philippians 3:18-19)

Those who opt out of the race do not arrive at rest or paradise. They arrive at destruction. The image is not a leisurely stroll but an emergency escape. Imagine the collapse of a building—people running down the stairs. Some are exhausted, discouraged, wondering if help is coming. Some choose to stop, to rest, to take their chances. Destruction overtakes them. Do not follow those who abandon the race.

### When the Stomach Becomes God (v.19)

Paul says their god is their stomach. This describes a life ruled by physical and emotional desires rather than truth. The body is tired, the appetite calls, the feelings dictate—and in that moment, objective reality is forgotten. It becomes slavery to lust.

This captures the spirit of our nation. We have stopped running toward heaven and have let destruction consume us. Our god has become our appetite. Consider how food has transformed in our culture. It is no longer fuel for survival but performance art, displayed for Instagram, engineered for political statement. Exercise is not about health but about appearance and toning. We craft our identity around culinary preferences, obsess over the quality of our coffee, make purchasing decisions based on environmental impact. Food has become our theology.

And with it comes the glory in shame. Rather than repent of our desires, we parade them. Rather than acknowledge guilt, we demand that others celebrate what we ought to be embarrassed about. This is the essence of enemies of the cross—not merely falling into sin, but insisting that everyone affirm the sin, celebrate it, and suffer consequences if they refuse.

### Our True Citizenship (v.20-21)

> But our citizenship is in heaven. And we eagerly await a Savior from there, the Lord Jesus Christ, who, by the power that enables him to bring everything under his control, will transform our lowly bodies so that they will be like his glorious body. (Philippians 3:20-21)

Our citizenship belongs to another kingdom. We await a Savior from heaven—not as distant spectators, but as those running the race, knowing He will meet us. And what will He do? He will transform these lowly, tired, aching bodies into glorious bodies like His own.

Until then, we use these bodies for His sake. This body that grows cold, that feels pain, that knows fear—we beat it into submission to serve Christ our King. We have perhaps 90 years in this life, with the best years likely between 13 and 50. Use them well.

## Why We Finish

Sally Robbins, an Olympic rower, had a personal motto: "Never surrender the dream." Yet in the 2004 Olympics, seeing her team drop to fifth place, she gave up. She lay back on her teammate's lap. They finished last.

But the race wasn't about winning a medal. It was about finishing. The team relied on her. The coaches believed in her. Months of training. The sacrifice of her parents. The investment of taxpayers. You finish because others are counting on you.

John Akhwari of Tanzania fell at the 1968 Mexico City Olympics, dislocating his knee. Yet through pain and misery, he finished the race—an hour after everyone else, with half the stadium emptied and darkness falling. When journalists asked why he didn't quit, he answered simply: "My country did not send me 5,000 miles to start. They sent me 5,000 miles to finish."

You have been called to start this journey. You have also been called to finish it.
