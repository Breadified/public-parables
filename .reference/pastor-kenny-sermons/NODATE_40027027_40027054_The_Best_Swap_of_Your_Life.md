# Matthew 27:27-54

**Title:** The Best Swap of Your Life
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## What is the Best Swap in Your Life?

Growing up in Malaysia, I used to swap stamps and marbles with friends. One swap I remember vividly was trading two ordinary marbles for one big, colorful white marble—at the time, I thought that was the best swap ever. Perhaps you collected Coles stickies or Pokémon cards and remember similar trades.

We've all experienced swaps we thought were good. But consider Kyle MacDonald's story. In 2005, he started with a single red paperclip and posted on his website offering to trade it for something "a little more impressive." What followed was extraordinary: the paperclip was swapped for a wooden fish pen, which became a ceramic doorknob, then a camping stove, and eventually equipment and cars. By 2006, through fourteen strategic trades, Kyle had swapped that humble paperclip for a renovated 1920s house on Main Street in a Canadian town.

Today's passage reveals something far greater than any earthly swap—a divine exchange that transforms everything.

## Part 1: Kingship Swapped for Criminality (27:27-38)

We find ourselves at a pivotal moment in Jesus' story. All his friends have abandoned him. The nation rejects him. Yet he is the King of the Jews. In this darkest hour, Jesus makes an unimaginable swap—he exchanges his kingship for the status of a criminal.

> They stripped him and put a scarlet robe on him, then twisted together a crown of thorns and set it on his head. They put a staff in his right hand and knelt in front of him, mocking him. "Hail, king of the Jews!" they said. Then they spit on him, and took the staff and struck him on the head again and again. (Matthew 27:28-30)

The soldiers mock him with parody royalty. A robe replaces his true garments. A crown of thorns replaces glory. A staff becomes a weapon of humiliation. They kneel before him—but in mockery, not worship. He is even given a servant, but only to carry his cross to execution.

> There they offered Jesus wine to drink, mixed with gall; but after tasting it, he refused to drink it. (27:34)

The wine is bitter, the drink of the condemned.

> Above his head they placed the written charge against him: "This is Jesus, the King of the Jews." (27:37)

The title remains, but now it appears as a criminal charge on a cross, not a proclamation of authority.

> Two rebels were crucified with him, one on his right and one on his left. (27:38)

The King sits between criminals. He is fully identified with sinners.

### Why This Swap Matters

When we think of swaps, we naturally think upward—from an old phone to a new model, from high school to university, from an old car to a new one. Kyle went from a paperclip to a house. We pursue upward mobility.

But Jesus does something unimaginable. He swaps downward.

Imagine exchanging a climate-controlled car for one without air conditioning. Imagine leaving a mansion for a granny flat. Imagine trading digital technology for analog, brand new clothes for thrift store finds. Imagine going from a house all the way back to a paperclip.

This is what Jesus did—for us.

He made this swap to identify with humanity. Deep down, we are all guilty as criminals before God. We have broken his laws. We have rebelled and sinned against him. We have harmed our neighbors whom he created. We are criminals in God's eyes. Yet Jesus came to identify himself with us because that was the only way to save us. He had to become one with us—even in our condemnation—to redeem us.

## Part 2: Glory Swapped for Suffering (27:45-48; 45-56)

To understand the weight of this swap, we must remember Jesus' transfiguration just chapters earlier.

> From noon until three in the afternoon darkness came over all the land. About three in the afternoon Jesus cried out in a loud voice, "Eli, Eli, lema sabachthani?" (which means "My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?"). (27:45-46)

The contrast is devastating. In Matthew 17, Jesus was transfigured before his disciples. His face shone like the sun. His clothes became white as light—brilliant and glorious. Moses and Elijah appeared beside him, acknowledging his authority. A voice from heaven declared: "This is my Son, whom I love; with him I am well pleased."

Now, instead of brightness, darkness covers the land. Instead of glorious garments, Jesus hangs stripped and naked. Instead of Elijah appearing to comfort him, Elijah never comes. Instead of God's affirmation, Jesus experiences utter abandonment: "My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?"

Glory has been exchanged for suffering.

### The Meaning of Abandonment

Why did God forsake Jesus? It is because our sin is so terrible that God must turn away in judgment. When Jesus identifies with our sin, God's wrath turns away from us and falls on him instead.

We sometimes think Jesus' death was simply a couple of days of physical pain. But judgment isn't measured by duration or pain level. Judgment is about separation from God. This was hell for Jesus—to be forsaken by the Father. And even three days in that state equals eternity.

What matters is not how much Jesus suffered, but that he suffered at all when he deserved nothing but blessing. This demonstrates how serious sin truly is. The consequences are severe enough that only the Son of God could bear them.

## Part 3: Salvation Swapped (27:39-44)

The crowd taunts him with unwitting truth:

> "He saved others," they said, "but he can't save himself! He's the king of Israel! Let him come down now from the cross, and we will believe in him." (27:42)

Their mockery contains profound reality. Jesus could indeed save others—he had healed the sick, fed five thousand, calmed storms. He possessed the power to save himself. At any moment, he could call upon twelve armies of angels to rescue him.

But he could not do both. The justice of God demands that sin be punished. Jesus faced an impossible choice: either save himself and avoid judgment, or save others and face God's wrath. He could not accomplish both simultaneously.

Yet in staying on the cross, Jesus made his choice—and in doing so, he signals to us that this was the only way to redeem us. He had to die so that we might live. The crowds mocked him without realizing they spoke the deepest truth: "He saved others, and therefore could not save himself."

## Part 4: The Greatest Swap of Your Life (27:50-54)

> When Jesus had cried out again in a loud voice, he gave up his spirit. (27:50)

Jesus gave his life for us. The question now becomes: Will you give your life for his?

> When the centurion and those with him who were guarding Jesus saw the earthquake and all that had happened, they were terrified, and exclaimed, "Surely he was the Son of God!" (27:54)

The guard who had mocked him recognizes the truth. When we admit that Jesus is the Son of God, the greatest swap takes place. We are no longer condemned but enter eternal life. Jesus took the punishment we deserved. He took our place as a criminal. He endured suffering so we would not have to.

### The Seeming Bad Trade That Becomes Everything

Remarkably, in Kyle MacDonald's chain of trades, one swap seemed like the worst possible decision. On his twelfth exchange, Kyle swapped an afternoon with legendary rockstar Alice Cooper for a Kiss snow globe—essentially a cheap decorative item. Online commenters declared it "the worst trade I've ever heard of, bar none."

But something unexpected happened. Corbin Bernsen, a famous actor and passionate snow globe collector with arguably the world's largest collection, saw the globe. He offered Kyle a role in his next film. That role was then traded to a small Canadian town seeking publicity, which offered Kyle a house in return.

What appeared to be the worst trade became the gateway to everything. Out of nowhere came the greatest outcome.

The cross looks pathetic. Everyone abandons Jesus because he appears to be a loser, dying the death of a criminal. Yet on that cross is the greatest trade ever—his life for yours.

Will you now live for him?
