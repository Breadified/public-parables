# Joshua 9-11

**Title:** The Crusades (War, Alliances, and Self-Defense)  
**Date:** 9 July 2016  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction: The Question of War

Why do nations go to war? There are fundamentally three reasons:

1. **Justice** — God uses nations to execute judgment against evil (covered previously)
2. **Alliances** — Nations become entangled in conflicts through political agreements
3. **Self-defense** — Nations fight to protect their people and territory

## Part One: Alliances (Joshua 9:3-20)

### The Gibeonites' Deception

When the Gibeonites heard of Israel's victories at Jericho and Ai, they resorted to a clever ruse. They dressed themselves in worn clothes, loaded their donkeys with tattered supplies, and presented themselves as ambassadors from a distant country, requesting a treaty of peace.

> They went as a delegation whose donkeys were loaded with worn-out sacks and old wineskins, cracked and mended. They put worn and patched sandals on their feet and wore old clothes. All the bread of their food supply was dry and moldy. Then they went to Joshua in the camp at Gilgal and said to him and the Israelites, "We have come from a distant country; make a treaty with us." (Joshua 9:4-6)

The Gibeonites' strategy was to appear pitiable and foreign, appealing to Israel's mercy. Critically, they did not genuinely seek God's favor—they sought only their own survival through deception.

### Israel Falls for the Ruse

Tragically, Joshua and the leaders of Israel failed to inquire of the Lord before making their decision.

> The Israelites sampled their provisions but did not inquire of the Lord. Then Joshua made a treaty of peace with them to let them live, and the leaders of the assembly ratified it by oath. (Joshua 9:14-15)

God had explicitly forbidden Israel from making covenants with the inhabitants of Canaan:

> "Do not make a covenant with them or with their gods. Do not let them live in your land or they will cause you to sin against me, because the worship of their gods will certainly be a snare to you." (Exodus 23:32-33)

### The Sanctity of an Oath

When Israel discovered the deception three days later, the whole assembly grumbled against the leaders. Yet the leaders refused to break their oath, recognizing that God's judgment would fall on them if they violated their sworn word:

> "We have given them our oath by the Lord, the God of Israel, and we cannot touch them now. This is what we will do to them: We will let them live, so that God's wrath will not fall on us for breaking the oath we swore to them." (Joshua 9:19-20)

This principle echoes throughout Scripture. In Judges 11, Jephthah made a rash vow to the Lord and, despite its terrible cost, honored it. Jesus taught us: "Let your yes be yes and your no be no." Our words matter—not just the grand oaths of marriage or covenant, but every promise we make, whether to a loved one or in casual conversation. We are called to be truth-telling, word-honoring people.

### The Consequences: Perpetual Service

The solution was to make the Gibeonites perpetual slaves:

> "You are now under a curse: You will never be released from service as woodcutters and water carriers for the house of my God." (Joshua 9:23)

This arrangement reflects a difficult reality: cultures and peoples with fundamentally different values cannot simply be assimilated. The Israelites had to maintain separation to protect themselves from idolatry's influence. The Gibeonites could not be trusted as full members of the covenant community, yet the oath protected their lives.

### The Trap of Bad Alliances

Israel's alliance with the Gibeonites triggered a cascade of disaster. When the five kings of the Amorites learned that Gibeon had made peace with Israel, they united against this threat. Joshua was now bound by oath to defend an enemy city.

> "Come up and help me attack Gibeon," [Adoni-Zedek] said, "because it has made peace with Joshua and the Israelites." (Joshua 10:4)

The Gibeonites called for help, and Joshua had to march to their defense. The Lord assured him: "Do not be afraid of them; I have given them into your hand" (Joshua 10:8). Yet the war was still triggered by a foolish alliance.

### A Historical Parallel: World War I

Consider how alliances plunged the world into chaos. A Serbian young man assassinated an Austrian-Hungarian prince—a local matter that should have remained local. But because of interconnected alliances, it became a global catastrophe. Austria-Hungary was allied with Germany, who was allied with the Ottoman Empire. Serbia was allied with Russia, who was allied with England and the Commonwealth nations.

Suddenly, the whole world erupted in war. Twenty million died in World War I. It led directly to World War II, which claimed sixty million lives—three percent of the world's population. We are still reaping the consequences today: absent fathers from the trenches created broken families; mothers who worked in factories during wartime fueled the sexual revolution; shell-shocked veterans produced generations of trauma.

All because of bad alliances.

**The lesson is clear: Do not make wrong alliances.** This principle applies spiritually as well:

> "Do not be yoked together with unbelievers. For what do righteousness and wickedness have in common? Or what fellowship can light have with darkness?" (2 Corinthians 6:14)

Whether in business partnerships, marriage, ministry organizations, or political associations, aligning ourselves with those who oppose God's values commits us to positions we cannot ultimately sustain. We inherit their conflicts and become defenders of their causes.

## Part Two: The Problem of Refugees (Joshua 9:21-27)

Once bound by oath to protect the Gibeonites, Israel faced another dilemma: What do you do with enemies in your midst? How can you know they won't betray you?

The solution was perpetual service. The Gibeonites would remain as woodcutters and water carriers for the community and the altar of the Lord. This wasn't cruelty but wisdom—a way to maintain both the oath and the safety of Israel.

The underlying issue reflects a hard truth: **you cannot presume assimilation across deeply different cultures.** Thousands of years of different worldviews, religions, and values cannot be erased by living together. Modern multiculturalism often ignores this reality.

## Part Three: Self-Defense (Joshua 9:1-2; Exodus 22:2-3)

The third reason nations wage war is self-defense. When all the kings west of the Jordan heard of Israel's victories, they united against her:

> "Now when all the kings west of the Jordan heard about these things—the kings in the hill country, in the western foothills, and along the entire coast of the Mediterranean Sea as far as Lebanon... they came together to wage war against Joshua and Israel." (Joshua 9:1-2)

Israel's right to defend herself is grounded in biblical law. When a thief breaks into a home at night and is struck fatally, the homeowner is not guilty of bloodshed. Even in daylight, if the thief attacks, self-defense is justified. God does not condemn those who protect their own lives and loved ones.

### Reconciling Self-Defense with "Turn the Other Cheek"

Yet Jesus commands: "Do not resist an evil person. If anyone slaps you on the right cheek, turn to them the other cheek also" (Matthew 5:39).

The key is understanding what "do not resist" means. The Greek text does not include the word "person"—it may be better translated as "do not resist by evil means." The passage concerns matters of personal honor and insult, not violent assault or attack.

Consider the absurdity of strict pacifism: If someone rapes you, does raping them back bring justice? If a spouse commits adultery, does committing adultery back heal the marriage? If someone kills your child in an accident, will killing their child in return restore justice? No. These examples reveal the limits of retaliatory logic.

The command about the extra mile and the second cheek operates within the context of honor, not abuse. Jesus does not teach that we should tolerate unlimited violence or fail to protect the vulnerable. Even Jesus physically drove moneychangers from the temple.

Consider a practical scenario: You're enjoying Pokémon Go with a friend when someone attacks them. Do you stand by, invoking pacifism? Or do you defend them? If you fail to act, your friend has lost faith in you—not because you believed in non-violence, but because you abandoned them.

### The Legitimacy of Military Defense

Self-defense is legitimate. When evil people and evil nations exist—and they always will, as long as human sinfulness remains—there will always be a need for war by good people to stop them. This is not warmongering; it is the realistic recognition that armies, borders, and weapons are acts of love, not the opposite. They protect the innocent.

Examples abound: D-Day was a just war—allied forces invaded Nazi-occupied Europe to liberate enslaved peoples. The United Nations defended Kuwait from Iraqi invasion. America supported South Korea against communist aggression. These were acts of rescue and protection, motivated by love.

## The Crusades: A Case of Just War and Self-Defense

The historical context of the Crusades has been severely distorted by modern revisionism. Media and academic critics blame the Crusades for Muslim terrorism, claiming Christian aggression provoked Islamic retaliation. This narrative is historically backward.

### The Real History

From the 7th century onward, Muslim armies invaded and conquered Christian lands across the Middle East, North Africa, and beyond. For three centuries, Christians suffered under Islamic rule, experiencing persecution, forced conversions, and violence against pilgrims attempting to visit the Holy Land.

By 1095, the Byzantine Emperor, desperately seeking help from fellow Christians, wrote to the Pope describing gruesome tortures of Christian pilgrims: Christians were being mutilated, churches desecrated, altars defiled, and baptismal fonts corrupted. He warned that if Constantinople fell to the Seljuk Turks, "thousands more Christians will be murdered, tortured, and raped," and "the most holy relics of the Saviour" would be lost forever.

On November 27, 1095, Pope Urban II addressed a multitude and gave a speech that changed history. He detailed the crimes:

> "They destroy the altars and defile them with their uncleanness. They circumcise Christians, and pour the blood of circumcision on the altars or into baptismal fonts. When they torture people to death, they perforate their navels and drag forth the intestines, binding them to a stake, then flogging the victim until the viscera gush forth. What of the abominable rape of women? On whom does the labor of avenging these wrongs fall, if not upon you?"

The Christians responded. They set out the next year and, by God's grace, won and held the Holy Land for a generation.

### Understanding Medieval Warfare

Modern critics impose contemporary notions of warfare on medieval conflicts. Both Christians and Muslims observed different rules of war in that era. To judge medieval warfare by 21st-century standards is anachronistic and unfair.

Furthermore, the claim that Crusaders were motivated by greed is false. The great nobility who led the Crusades gave up fortunes. Robert of Normandy mortgaged his entire duchy to raise money. They came home penniless or dead. The crusader kingdoms they established required immense subsidies from Europe to survive—they were not profitable colonies.

### The Motivation Was Rescue, Not Conquest

The Crusades were wars of rescue and defense, motivated by love. The same love that drove D-Day, that defended Kuwait, that protected South Korea. The same love that motivated Christ to die for us. It was to rescue and protect and defend—out of love.

Be strong and courageous.

### A Final Historical Correction

The notion that Muslims have harbored deep resentment about the Crusades for a millennium is false. Muslim antagonism toward the Crusades did not emerge until about 1900, coinciding with the decline of the Ottoman Empire and the onset of European colonialism in the Middle East. Anti-Crusade sentiment became intense only after the founding of the state of Israel.

The Crusades were a legitimate Christian response to centuries of Islamic aggression, just as Israel's wars against the Canaanites were justified responses to evil that required judgment.

## War in Heaven: The Cross as Victory

The pattern of cosmic conflict reaches its fullest expression at the cross. Revelation 12 uses dramatic imagery to describe what happened there:

> "Then war broke out in heaven. Michael and his angels fought against the dragon, and the dragon and his angels fought back. But he was not strong enough, and they lost their place in heaven. The great dragon was hurled down—that ancient serpent called the devil, or Satan, who leads the whole world astray." (Revelation 12:7-9)

"Michael" means "like God" (Micah = "like Yahweh"). Some dismiss Michael as merely an angelic leader, but Scripture calls him "the great prince that protects his people" (Daniel 12:1)—language that applies to Jesus. Michael is a type or reference to Jesus, the One who defeated Satan.

The death of Christ was not pacifism. Jesus submitted to death, yes, but He was fighting a battle in the spiritual realm against Satan himself. He suffered tremendous injury but achieved victory over death and the enemy. He fought till death—and won.

Jesus was not a pacifist. He conquered through sacrifice, but conquest it was.

## The Conquest Continues (Joshua 10-11)

The military campaigns continued. Joshua pursued the five kings, defeated them, and conquered the southern cities one by one. Then the northern alliance united against Israel under King Jabin of Hazor:

> "They came out with all their troops and a large number of horses and chariots—a huge army, as numerous as the sand on the seashore. All these kings joined forces and made camp together at the Waters of Merom to fight against Israel." (Joshua 11:4-5)

The Lord assured Joshua: "Do not be afraid of them, because by this time tomorrow I will hand all of them, slain, over to Israel. You are to hamstring their horses and burn their chariots" (Joshua 11:6).

Joshua defeated them decisively. The land finally had rest from war:

> "So Joshua took the entire land, just as the Lord had directed Moses, and he gave it as an inheritance to Israel according to their tribal divisions. Then the land had rest from war." (Joshua 11:23)

## The Theology of Judgment: Why Such Violence?

The destruction may trouble modern sensibilities, yet Scripture gives us the framework to understand it. Sin is violent. Payment for sin is violent:

> "Without the shedding of blood, there is no forgiveness of sins." (Hebrews 9:22)

> "For the life of a creature is in the blood, and I have given it to you to make atonement for yourselves on the altar; it is the blood that makes atonement for one's life." (Leviticus 17:11)

The Canaanites faced judgment for their sins, just as Achan was executed for breaking God's command. This was not cruelty but the inevitable consequence of rebellion against the holy God.

It's important to note that the primary combatants were killed—those who fought against Israel. Survivors and foreigners lived among Israel afterward (see Joshua 8:33, 23:7, 12). There was no prisoner-of-war system available in that ancient period; the leadership had to be eliminated to prevent future uprisings.

Moreover, the elimination of the Canaanite leadership was essential to protect Israel from spiritual corruption. Generations of idolatry could not be tolerated in a nation called to worship the one true God.

## Conclusion: A Call to Strength and Courage

Throughout these chapters, God repeatedly commands: "Be strong and courageous." This is not a call to aggression but to faithful obedience in the face of genuine opposition. As long as evil exists, strength and courage will be required to defend the innocent and uphold justice.

The conquest of Canaan reminds us that God takes sin seriously, that He calls His people to faithful action, and that He Himself fights for those who trust in Him.
