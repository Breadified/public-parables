# Galatians 5:13-26

**Title:** The Law and Spirit
**Date:** August 2021
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Freedom and Love

We are called to be free. But what are we free for?

> You, my brothers and sisters, were called to be free. But do not use your freedom to indulge the flesh; rather, serve one another humbly in love. (Galatians 5:13-14)

Freedom is not the liberty to do whatever we want. Rather, true freedom finds its purpose in love—specifically, in serving one another.

> For the entire law is fulfilled in keeping this one command: "Love your neighbor as yourself." (Galatians 5:14)

This is a remarkable statement. Paul reduces the entire law to a single principle: love for others.

### A Question About the Summary of the Law

You may notice that Matthew's account includes two commandments—love God and love neighbor—while Paul here emphasizes only love of neighbor. This seems to omit something crucial. After wrestling with this, I believe the reason lies in Galatians' focus.

Paul addresses the Galatian church on an ecclesiological issue: Who can belong to the community of faith? The false teachers were imposing circumcision and dietary laws as entry requirements. Galatians is fundamentally about church fellowship, not about salvation doctrine (as Romans is). Therefore, Paul's summary addresses the specific problem tearing them apart: how to relate to one another within the church family.

When we look at Romans 13:9, we see a similar summary: "Love your neighbor as yourself" encapsulates the second table of the Ten Commandments—the laws governing how we treat each other. This is the core issue for divided churches.

### Understanding Love in Our Context

In our culture, "love" is often misunderstood. We reduce it to compassion, feelings, or doing nice things for each other. But this shallow understanding becomes a justification for all manner of harm.

Love is weaponized to justify infidelity ("I'm being true to myself"). It excuses materialism ("I'm loving my family"). It violates boundaries ("I'm trying to help you"). Love becomes whatever we want it to be.

Yet love remains a good motivation—even secular voices appeal to it. But we must understand what Paul means.

> Love is patient, love is kind. It does not envy, it does not boast, it is not proud. It does not dishonor others, it is not self-seeking, it is not easily angered, it keeps no record of wrongs. Love does not delight in evil but rejoices with the truth. It always protects, always trusts, always hopes, always perseveres. (1 Corinthians 13:4-7)

Love means serving each other, looking out for the other's wellbeing, not seeking to gratify ourselves.

## Humility in Love

Love requires humility. And here we encounter a challenge facing our congregation.

> Serve one another humbly in love. (Galatians 5:13)

The problem is this: not everyone shares your priorities. Not everyone has had your experiences. To demand that everyone conform to your standard of importance is arrogance.

The Pharisees embodied this error. They set arbitrary standards and condemned everyone who didn't meet them. They stood at the temple, eavesdropping on others' prayers, inserting themselves into matters that weren't their concern, then judging others for failing their self-imposed rules. Legalism is pride. It says, "My standard is the right one, and anyone who differs from me is wrong."

### Legalism and the Role of Conscience

When we remove law as our guide, we might think we've abandoned all moral understanding. But that's not Paul's point. The law cannot be over-specific enough to cover every circumstance—that's precisely why we need the church, community, and the wisdom of the Spirit.

We must leave room for wisdom, humility, conscience, and freedom. Different believers, in good conscience and genuine love, may reach different conclusions about issues the Bible doesn't specifically address. This is especially true of matters where people differ based on their backgrounds, fears, and understanding.

Even on matters where we hold convictions—like the question of how to respond to a pandemic—we must resist spiritual arrogance. Some are motivated by concern for others' health. Some are motivated by love of their families and concern for freedom. Some are motivated by love of creation and distrust of corporations. Some are motivated by love of their ancestors' sacrifices.

When we see each other's positions through the lens of love, we might say: while I believe vaccination is wise and loving, I can recognize my neighbor's desire to protect what they see as natural and pure. While I believe in protecting liberty, I can recognize another's genuine concern for vulnerable people. We need not agree on everything to love one another.

> If you bite and devour each other, watch out or you will be destroyed by each other. (Galatians 5:15)

We are devouring each other. Calling one another names. Creating strawmen of those we disagree with. When fear and desperation take hold, we turn on each other rather than standing together.

But this pandemic is not your brother's or sister's fault. We are in this together. We should not be calculating blame against individual believers when we've made collective decisions that led to this crisis. Instead of attacking one another, let us focus on the real issues and genuine solutions.

The hostage metaphor speaks to our moment: we've lived under restrictions so long, with rules we cannot all perfectly follow, that some have grown desperate. When a few break the rules, the rest turn on them rather than remembering they are all captives. We become cannibals.

Be humble. Your standard may not be everyone's standard. Love your neighbor—including those who disagree with you.

## Living by the Spirit

For Christians, there are two ways to live.

### Walk by the Spirit

> So I say, walk by the Spirit, and you will not gratify the desires of the flesh. For the flesh desires what is contrary to the Spirit, and the Spirit what is contrary to the flesh. They are in conflict with each other, so that you are not to do whatever you want. (Galatians 5:16-17)

The flesh is our sinful nature—the selfish, earthly desires that compete with God's Spirit within us. The Spirit is the new nature we receive in Christ, calling us toward higher spiritual purposes like love.

Walking by the Spirit means aligning our thoughts and decisions with God's Word and His kingdom, not with the world's values and the flesh's impulses.

> But if you are led by the Spirit, you are not under the law. (Galatians 5:18)

This doesn't mean law disappears. It means the law is no longer our master, threatening us with punishment. Instead, the Spirit becomes our guide.

> But he is a Jew who is one inwardly; and circumcision is that which is of the heart, by the Spirit, not by the letter. (Romans 2:29)

Obedience stems from an inward transformation, not from external compliance with rules.

### The Acts of the Flesh Are Obvious

> The acts of the flesh are obvious: sexual immorality, impurity and debauchery; idolatry and witchcraft; hatred, discord, jealousy, fits of rage, selfish ambition, dissensions, factions and envy; drunkenness, orgies, and the like. I warn you, as I did before, that those who live like this will not inherit the kingdom of God. (Galatians 5:19-21)

What strikes me about this list is that many items don't appear in the Old Testament law as specific commandments. Drunkenness, yes, is warned against. But "fits of rage," "dissensions," "factions," "envy"—these represent the inner attitudes and relational behaviors that the law couldn't fully capture.

Paul echoes what Jesus taught in Matthew 5: the law goes beyond external actions to address the heart. We are free from the penalty of the law through Christ, but we are not free from obeying its spirit. The Bible may not specifically mention the coronavirus or the internet, but the Spirit's lordship speaks clearly into our modern circumstances.

### The Fruit of the Spirit

> Those who belong to Christ Jesus have crucified the flesh with its passions and desires. Since we live by the Spirit, let us keep in step with the Spirit. Let us not become conceited, provoking and envying each other. (Galatians 5:24-26)

> But the fruit of the Spirit is love, joy, peace, forbearance, kindness, goodness, faithfulness, gentleness and self-control. Against such things there is no law. (Galatians 5:22-23)

Notice that "fruit" is singular, not plural. These qualities form one unified character shaped by the Spirit. And remarkably, there is no law against them. We won't find specific Old Testament commandments to be joyful or peaceful or patient. Yet it is obvious—when the Spirit dwells within us, these qualities should characterize our lives.

## Conclusion: True Freedom

Martin Luther captured this paradox beautifully:

> A Christian is a perfectly free lord of all, subject to none.
> A Christian is a perfectly dutiful servant of all, subject to all.

We are free—truly free—from the law's condemnation. But that freedom exists so we might serve. The Spirit works within us, not through fear and external rules, but through love that compels us to look beyond ourselves.

This is the freedom we've been called to: not the freedom to do whatever we want, but the freedom to love as Christ loved us.
