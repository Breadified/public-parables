# James 3:13-18

**Title:** Who Is the Wise Man?
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Understanding True Wisdom

When we think of wisdom, we imagine people we admire—parents who navigated life well, accountants who managed finances shrewdly, real estate investors who saw opportunities decades ahead. We recognize wisdom by its fruit: foresight, success, understanding. But what exactly is wisdom? Is it knowledge? Success? The old sage dispensing esoteric truths? Whatever it is, we want it. We desire to be healthy, wealthy, and wise—like Solomon.

But James cuts through our assumptions. He shows us that wisdom isn't about how much you know or how successful you've become. **True wisdom is seen in action.**

> Who is wise and understanding among you? Let him show it by his good life, by deeds done in the humility that comes from wisdom. But if you harbor bitter envy and selfish ambition in your hearts, do not boast about it or deny the truth. Such "wisdom" does not come down from heaven but is earthly, unspiritual, of the devil. For where you have envy and selfish ambition, there you find disorder and every evil practice. (James 3:13-15)

## The Context of James

James writes to scattered Christians—likely Jewish believers—not to explain how one becomes Christian, but how Christians should *live*. Unlike Paul's emphasis on justification by faith, James emphasizes action consistent with belief. He shows us that humility is not the cause of faith, but its fruit. This is a deeply practical book, sometimes called the Proverbs of the New Testament.

The letter reveals problems in the early churches:
- **Favoritism** toward the rich (1:10, 2:1-13)
- **Lack of good deeds**—faith without works (2:14)
- **Quarrels and strife** among believers (3:16, 4:1-3)
- **Proud teachers** seeking status (3:1)
- **Selfish ambition** rather than service

And underlying all these problems: **misuse of the tongue**. The tongue reveals which wisdom we've embraced—whether we're building others up or tearing them down through gossip and malice.

## Two Kinds of Wisdom

There are two wisdoms mentioned in James, and they are utterly opposed to each other.

**Earthly Wisdom:**
- Origin: earthly, unspiritual, demonic
- Character: bitter envy, selfish ambition, jealousy
- Result: disorder and every evil practice

**Heavenly Wisdom:**
- Origin: from above, from God
- Character: pure, peace-loving, considerate, submissive, full of mercy and good fruit, impartial and sincere
- Result: a good life and harvest of righteousness

How do we tell the difference? It's not always obvious where an idea originates. The advice to eat omega-3 fatty acids—is that wisdom from a doctor or a vitamin salesman? Guidelines about parenting change constantly. But we can know them **by their fruit**. We can observe the results.

Consider church leaders and public figures: some have built up the body of Christ through genuine love and faithful teaching. Others have sown discord, enriched themselves, and left destruction in their wake. The results tell the story.

> The wise man shows it by his good life, by deeds done in the humility that comes from wisdom. (James 3:13)

Wisdom isn't just what you claim to know. It's what you *do*. It's not enough to say, "I am smart" (as Homer Simpson sings). The evidence must match the claim.

## Wisdom from Heaven: Christ Is Our Wisdom

If we want true wisdom, where do we get it?

> If anyone lacks wisdom, he should ask God, who gives generously to all without finding fault, and it will be given to him. (James 1:5)

But here's the profound truth: **Christ is our wisdom.**

> Jesus Christ is the wisdom of God (1 Corinthians 1:24) who becomes the ultimate source of all Christian wisdom. (1 Corinthians 1:30)

When Paul writes this, the Corinthian church was tearing itself apart with petty arguments—disputes over food, marriage, and status. Different factions claimed Paul's way was wiser, or Apollos's way. They were measuring wisdom by worldly standards.

Paul's answer shakes everything: Real wisdom isn't cleverness, magical insight, or prophetic power. It's **knowing Jesus Christ**. It's understanding that God has revealed Himself—not through human eloquence or philosophical systems, but through the cross.

The world sees the cross as foolishness. How could God's power be expressed through weakness? How could salvation come through death? But for those with eyes to see, the cross is the wisdom and power of God. In Christ, we are justified—and in Him, we are perfectly wise before God.

This means that when we study Scripture and grow in our understanding of what Christ has accomplished for us, we gain the lens through which to interpret all of reality. 

> In him are hidden all the treasures of wisdom and knowledge. (Colossians 2:3)

Christ is the key that unlocks the universe. He teaches us what everything really means—our relationships, our possessions, our time, our futures. This isn't mystical knowledge. It's seeing reality as it truly is in light of the gospel.

## Applying Wisdom: Making Decisions in Light of the Gospel

### Wisdom in Relation to Eternity

Here's the radical implication: **If you're a Christian, you already have wisdom.** You just might not realize it.

But this wisdom should reshape how we think about everything. We're going to die. We're going to stand before God. Yet we spend enormous energy on things that won't last. We invest in larger houses, expensive clothes, gym memberships, elaborate insurance policies—all to avoid a reality we can't escape. We've become wise about everything except the most important thing.

Every decision we make should be measured against this reality: *"I will face eternity."* That changes things.

### Wisdom in Relation to Now: How Do We Make Decisions?

Asking God for wisdom (James 1:5) isn't asking Him to make you cleverer or more intuitive. It's more like Paul's prayers:

> I pray that the eyes of your heart may be enlightened in order that you may know the hope to which he has called you, the riches of his glorious inheritance in the saints. (Ephesians 1:18)

The mature Christian is one who can look at the whole of reality through Christian eyes—understanding all things in light of what God intends them to be through His redeeming work in Christ. Our task is to work out the implications of the gospel on our actual lives.

The secret has been revealed: **the gospel is the key.** The cross is the most important event in history. If you know that Christ died for your sins, rose from the dead, has been given all authority, and is coming back to judge and restore all things—then you have a framework for understanding everything else.

By contrast, the wisdom of the world says: "Collect as much as you can. Win the game. Accumulate status and possessions." But if you know these things will perish, that there's a judgment coming, that Christ has already won—then this "wisdom" is revealed as profoundly foolish.

## Practical Examples

### From James

The proud teachers in James's churches claimed wisdom, but they sowed quarrels and division. Not consistent with the gospel. The rich thought their wealth proved their blessing, but James reminds them: *"Your wealth has rotted, and moths have eaten your clothes"* (5:2). The quarreling believers forgot there's one Judge, so why are they fighting over who's right?

True wisdom works itself out in:
- **Good deeds and humility** (3:13)
- **Accepting God's word humbly** and getting rid of moral filth (1:21)
- **Being a doer of the word, not just a hearer** (1:25)

As the word is implanted deep within you, becoming part of your psyche, it shapes how you live. The righteous are those who hear and respond.

### In Your Life Today

Wisdom isn't mystical—waiting for doors to open or following gut feelings. It's the hard work of renewing your mind through Scripture and then working out the implications.

**In relationships:** Dating is not condemned in Scripture, but wisdom doesn't operate that way. Perpetually dating without direction isn't wise. Neither is keeping a "best friend" you can't marry—that's a recipe for disaster in your eventual marriage. Wisdom means moving forward thoughtfully, not endlessly cycling through relationships.

**In entertainment:** Computer games aren't inherently evil, but at some point you grow up. Responsibilities increase. A forty-year-old playing games while babies cry in the background isn't wise—he's chosen the wrong priority.

**In finances:** Are you buying things thoughtfully or accumulating out of fear? Spending on expensive clothes because they make you feel attractive? Buying bargains you don't need? Storing up "just in case"? Wisdom says: simplicity, modesty, intentionality. Understand what you really value by examining how you spend money.

**In career:** What work are you pursuing? Why? Is it building your kingdom or God's? Are you making decisions based on fear, status, or genuine calling?

**Reverse engineer your life.** Look at where your time goes, where your money flows, how you invest in relationships. These reveal what you actually believe is important. Then ask: Is that consistent with the gospel? With eternity? With the person God is making me to become?

## The Bottom Line

Wisdom isn't measured by how much you know. It's measured by how you live. 

> Who is wise and understanding among you? Let him show it by his good life, by deeds done in the humility that comes from wisdom. (James 3:13)

The wise man or woman is the one who has encountered Jesus Christ as the wisdom of God, has grasped the reality of the gospel, and is now working out its implications in every decision—from how they treat people, to how they spend money, to how they steward time, to how they face eternity.

You have the key. Christ is your wisdom. Now live like it.
