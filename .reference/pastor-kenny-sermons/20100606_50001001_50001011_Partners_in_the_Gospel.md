# Philippians 1:1-11

**Title:** Partners in the Gospel  
**Date:** June 6, 2010  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Salutations

> Paul and Timothy, servants of Christ Jesus,
>
> To all the saints in Christ Jesus at Philippi, together with the overseers and deacons:
>
> Grace and peace to you from God our Father and the Lord Jesus Christ.
> — Philippians 1:1-2

Paul opens this letter not with titles or authority, but by identifying himself and Timothy as servants—slaves, even—of Christ Jesus. This sets the tone for everything that follows. He writes to the entire church at Philippi: the saints, the overseers, and the deacons. There's an intentional inclusivity here. Grace and peace flow from God our Father and the Lord Jesus Christ together—a unified benediction that frames the letter's central concern: partnership in the gospel.

---

## Paul's Gratitude for the Philippians

> I thank my God every time I remember you. In all my prayers for all of you, I always pray with joy because of your partnership in the gospel from the first day until now.
> — Philippians 1:3-5

Paul's thanksgiving is not perfunctory. Every time he remembers the Philippians, gratitude wells up. And when he prays for them, joy characterizes those prayers. Why? Because of their partnership in the gospel from the very beginning—from Acts 16, when the Philippian church was first planted—until the present moment.

This is the heartbeat of the letter: partnership. We are in this together. Paul is not alone in his apostolic work, and the Philippians are not passive recipients of his ministry. They are active partners, sharing the burden and privilege of advancing the gospel.

> I am confident of this, that he who began a good work in you will carry it on to completion until the day of Christ Jesus.
> — Philippians 1:6

Paul's confidence rests not in human effort but in divine faithfulness. God began a work in the Philippians, and God will complete it. This is not passive assurance; it's an active trust in God's perseverance.

## The Depth of Paul's Affection

> It is right for me to feel this way about all of you, since I have you in my heart; for whether I am in chains or defending and confirming the gospel, you all share in God's grace with me. God can testify how I long for all of you with the affection of Christ Jesus.
> — Philippians 1:7-8

Paul's emotional connection to the Philippians is profound and deliberate. He carries them in his heart. Whether imprisoned or actively defending the faith, they are partners in God's grace. The Greek word here speaks of a deep, visceral longing—the affection of Christ Jesus Himself flowing through Paul toward his beloved congregation.

This is not distant, formal leadership. This is pastoral warmth combined with spiritual intimacy. Paul's feelings are not a weakness but a reflection of Christ's love for His people.

## Paul's Prayer for the Philippians

> And this is my prayer: that your love may abound more and more in knowledge and depth of insight, so that you may be able to discern what is best and may be pure and blameless until the day of Christ, filled with the fruit of righteousness that comes through Jesus Christ—to the glory and praise of God.
> — Philippians 1:9-11

Paul prays for two intertwined realities in the Philippians' spiritual life:

**Knowledge and Discernment:** Paul wants their love to grow not in isolation but in knowledge and depth of insight. Love without discernment can be naive or even harmful. But love shaped by deep understanding of Scripture and spiritual wisdom enables believers to "approve of what is best"—to distinguish between good and better, between what merely looks righteous and what truly honors God.

**Godliness and Fruitfulness:** The natural result is purity and blamelessness. And this comes through the fruit of righteousness—not self-generated morality, but the fruit produced by Jesus Christ working in and through them. The goal of all spiritual growth is always the same: the glory and praise of God.

This prayer reveals what Paul considers most important for believers: not merely correct doctrine, though that matters, but the integration of knowledge with love, discernment with action, and personal holiness with corporate witness—all flowing from Christ and returning glory to God the Father.
