# Luke 23:1-25

**Title:** The King on Trial
**Date:** 13 April 2025
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Great Escape

On 26 March 1944, seventy-six Allied prisoners of war executed one of history's most audacious escapes from Stalag Luft III. What made their plan remarkable wasn't the ambition of a few men, but the sheer scope: they aimed to free 200 prisoners from a camp of 600. Working in the heart of a sand-based camp wired with microphones, they dug three tunnels—Tom, Dick, and Harry—reinforcing them with bed slats and stolen materials. When discovered, the Germans counted the cost: 4,000 bed boards, 90 double bunks, 635 mattresses, and countless other items that had vanished into the tunnels. In the end, 76 men escaped. A remarkable feat of human courage and determination.

But today we consider another great escape—one far more significant. The escape of a man named Barabbas, and what it reveals about Jesus and ourselves.

---

## Part 1: Barabbas (23:18-19)

> Then they all cried out together, "Take this man away! Release Barabbas to us!" He had been thrown into prison for a rebellion that had taken place in the city, and for murder.

We know little of Barabbas beyond these verses. His name—a Greek form of the Aramaic "bar Abbas" (son of the father)—appears in all four gospels, though Matthew calls him "Jesus Barabbas." More importantly, we know his crime: he had been imprisoned for insurrection and murder, likely involvement in a Jewish rebellion against Roman occupation. In our modern terms, he was a terrorist—someone who used covert means and civil unrest to fight against governmental authority.

Yet here stands Barabbas, facing execution for his crimes, when suddenly the crowd demands his release. How is this possible? Luke suggests (as John explicitly states in 18:39) that it was customary at Passover to release a prisoner to the people. This privilege—*privilegium paschale*—offered Barabbas a narrow escape from the death penalty he so rightly deserved.

But the irony deepens when we discover the man in the adjacent cell: another prisoner on trial, one who would become central to all of history.

## Part 2: Jesus on Trial

### The Jewish Sanhedrin (22:63-71)

Jesus had been arrested by the Jewish leaders after being betrayed by his closest followers. Early in the morning, the Sanhedrin—the council of elders, chief priests, and scribes—convened to judge him formally.

> They said, "If you are the Messiah, tell us." But he replied, "If I tell you, you will not believe; and if I ask, you will not answer. But from now on the Son of Man will be seated at the right hand of the power of God."

The echoes of Satan's temptation return here: *If you are the Son of God...* Yet Jesus responds with paradox. His answer—"You say that I am"—seems deliberately ambiguous, answering their question while refusing to provide the clear confession they demand.

Yet here lies a profound legal problem. Under Old Testament law, as stated in Deuteronomy 19:15:

> One witness cannot establish any wrongdoing or sin against a person, whatever that person has done. A fact must be established by the testimony of two or three witnesses.

Jewish tradition, recorded in the Talmud, extended this principle further: a defendant's own confession was inadmissible as evidence. As the Talmud explains, a person might falsely confess to a crime to gain a death they secretly desire. By this standard, the Sanhedrin has violated their own law. Their judgment is legally groundless.

Yet law means nothing when the verdict has been predetermined. As Luke noted in 22:2, the elders, chief priests, and teachers of the law had already decided they wanted Jesus dead. With their religious trial complete, they bring Jesus to Rome's representative in Judea to secure the execution only Rome could authorize.

### The Roman Prefect (23:1-7)

> Then the whole assembly rose and led him off to Pilate.

Pontius Pilate served as the fifth Roman prefect of Judea from AD 26-36. As Rome's representative, he wielded absolute authority—the power to execute. The Roman historian Philo describes him as inflexible, characterized by briberies, insults, robberies, and wanton injuries. He executed men without trial, introduced emperor worship into the temple, and robbed the temple treasury. Yet even such a man becomes the instrument through which Jesus's innocence is declared.

The Jewish leaders bring their charge:

> We found this man subverting our nation. He opposes payment of taxes to Caesar and claims to be Messiah, a king.

Three accusations designed to alarm a Roman governor: rebellion against Rome, tax resistance (a direct economic threat), and claims to kingship (a direct challenge to Caesar's authority). Yet Pilate's investigation yields a surprising result.

> Pilate asked him, "Are you the king of the Jews?" "You have said so," Jesus replied.

Again, Jesus answers with deliberate restraint. And when Pilate questions him further, he finds nothing.

> I find no basis for a charge against this man.

But the crowd insists. Jesus stirs up the people, they claim, teaching throughout all Judea from Galilee to Jerusalem. Pilate, seeking escape from this difficult decision, notices that Jesus is from Galilee—territory under the jurisdiction of Herod Antipas.

### Herod's Court (23:8-12)

> When Herod saw Jesus, he was greatly pleased, because for a long time he had been wanting to see him. From what he had heard about him, he hoped to see him perform a sign of some sort.

Herod Antipas, the Jewish tetrarch, had long desired to witness one of Jesus's miracles. He was curious, fascinated even—a man drawn by spectacle and the promise of divine signs. Yet when confronted with Jesus himself, something unexpected occurs:

> He plied him with many questions, but Jesus gave him no answer. The chief priests and the teachers of the law were standing there, vehemently accusing him. Then Herod and his soldiers ridiculed and mocked him. Dressing him in an elegant robe, they sent him back to Pilate.

Jesus refuses to perform, to satisfy curiosity, to defend himself. His silence speaks volumes. And in his silence, we witness something remarkable: Herod and Pilate, who had been enemies, suddenly become friends. A man's innocence, his refusal to justify himself before power, can create strange unity among his enemies.

## Part 3: Jesus Found Innocent (23:13-16, 22-25)

> Pilate called together the chief priests, the rulers and the people, and said to them, "You brought me this man as one who was inciting the people to rebellion. I have examined him in your presence and have found no basis for your charges against him. Neither has Herod, for he sent him back to us; as you can see, he has done nothing to deserve death."

Three times Pilate declares Jesus innocent. Three times he seeks a way to release him, suggesting merely a flogging as punishment. Yet each time, the crowd's voice grows louder:

> Crucify! Crucify him!

And finally, their voices prevail:

> So Pilate decided to grant their demand. He released the man who had been thrown into prison for insurrection and murder, the one they asked for, and surrendered Jesus to their will.

The judgment is rendered: The guilty man—Barabbas, the terrorist, the murderer—walks free. The innocent man—Jesus, the King—is handed over to crucifixion.

---

## The Trial That Reveals Everything

We must grasp what has truly happened: **It is not Jesus on trial. We are on trial.**

In this courtroom scene, three types of people emerge:

**The Accusers**—Those determined to destroy him, bound by their predetermined verdict, twisting law and testimony to achieve their desired outcome.

**The Deferrers**—Those like Pilate and Herod, who recognize his innocence yet lack the courage to act on it. They pass responsibility to others, wash their hands of the decision, defer him to another authority.

**The Spectators**—Those fascinated by miracle and novelty, drawn to a Jesus who performs signs and speaks broadly acceptable teachings. The crowd that loves him until the moment it turns to hate.

And what is our verdict? Are we among those determined to maintain our own rule? Are we deferrers, knowing what is right yet too fearful to act? Are we drawn merely to the spectacular rather than to surrender?

The answer cuts deeper still: We are Barabbas.

---

## We Are Barabbas

Barabbas stands guilty of insurrection and murder. But his crime reflects a deeper human reality. We too are rebels—not merely against earthly governments, though we struggle even with taxes, but against God himself. Our sin is not primarily the breaking of laws, but the making of laws for ourselves. We are terrorists against the rule of God, subverting his kingdom bit by bit, preparing for the day we might overthrow him altogether.

And Barabbas deserves death. So do we.

Yet something extraordinary occurs. The sentence falls not on the guilty man, but on the innocent one. The swap is made.

> The righteous for the unrighteous (1 Peter 3:18)

Jesus—innocent, obedient to his Father's will unto death—suffers the penalty due to us. Isaiah describes it:

> He was pierced for our transgressions, he was crushed for our iniquities (Isaiah 53:12)

Barabbas walks free. Jesus walks to the cross.

And so do we experience our great escape.

---

## The Narrow Way

In the Great Escape of 1944, 76 men got out. Yet 73 were recaptured; only 3 truly escaped. The cost was high: the men caught, and 500 left behind in prison. But for those three, freedom came at an unimaginable price.

We who follow Christ have escaped. We who place our allegiance and trust in Jesus have narrowly escaped the death we deserve. We must never forget the cost of that escape, nor take it for granted.

Because of Christ's substitution, we are no longer terrorists against God's rule. We are now citizens of heaven—not passive ones, but active participants in the coming of his kingdom. This reality transforms everything:

- **Our time** is no longer our own to waste
- **Our wallets** are not resources for ourselves alone
- **Our priorities** are reordered around the one who freed us

We have exchanged the prison garb of sin for the robes of freedom. We have been set free by the innocent King.
