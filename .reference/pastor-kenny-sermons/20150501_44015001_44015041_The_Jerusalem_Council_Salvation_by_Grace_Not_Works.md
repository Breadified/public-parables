# Acts 15:1-41

**Title:** The Jerusalem Council: Salvation by Grace, Not Works

**Date:** 25 May 2015

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Homecoming Report

When travelers return home, they share reports of their journey—the places they visited, the people they met, and what happened along the way. Paul and Barnabas have just returned from their missionary journey through Asia Minor (modern-day Turkey), and like any good homecoming, they have much to report.

Their reporting, however, gives rise to different reactions in the church. At stake is a fundamental question: **What saves us, and what doesn't?**

---

## 1. "You Cannot Be Saved" (Acts 14:27–15:5)

### The First Report: Antioch

Paul and Barnabas arrive at Antioch on the Levant (note: there are multiple Antiochs, named after Emperor Antiochus, much like the many King George Roads in Sydney). They gather the church and give their first report:

> On arriving there, they gathered the church together and reported all that God had done through them and how he had opened a door of faith to the Gentiles. (14:27)

God had opened the gospel to the Gentiles—the non-Jews, the *ethnos* (from which we get "ethnic"). This is a success story. The gospel reaches beyond Israel's borders to the ends of the earth. It's like an Australian product making it big internationally—imagine Vegemite popular worldwide, or Australia winning Eurovision. This is cause for genuine excitement.

### The Opposition: A Troubling Teaching

As Paul and Barnabas travel toward Jerusalem, they pass through Phoenicia (modern Lebanon) and Samaria, telling how the Gentiles have been converted. In these regions, the believers rejoice at the good news (15:3).

But in Antioch itself, the response is different. Certain people from Judea arrive with a disturbing claim:

> "Unless you are circumcised, according to the custom taught by Moses, **you cannot be saved.**" (15:1)

Their argument seems reasonable from an Old Testament perspective. The Law itself teaches that obedience brings blessing:

> If you follow my decrees and are careful to obey my commands, I will send you rain in its season, and the ground will yield its crops. (Leviticus 26:3-5)

> Keep his decrees and commands, which I am giving you today, so that it may go well with you and your children after you. (Deuteronomy 4:40)

Even the prophets echo this theme:

> Walk in obedience to all I command you, that it may go well with you. (Jeremiah 7:23)

And Jesus himself affirmed the Law:

> Do not think that I have come to abolish the Law or the Prophets; I have not come to abolish them but to fulfill them. (Matthew 5:17)

If the Law is good—and it is, for God makes no mistakes—shouldn't Gentiles who want to join God's people obey it? Shouldn't they undergo circumcision, the sign of the covenant?

This triggers sharp dispute and debate. Paul and Barnabas are appointed to go to Jerusalem with representatives to bring this matter before the apostles and elders.

### A Necessary Application

The Law from the Bible is good. It's from God. God doesn't make bad laws or rush through poorly considered legislation. Read the Old Testament as part of your Bible diet—even Leviticus, difficult as it may be. The Law deserves our high regard.

Yet the answer to the Judaizers' demand is clear: **You can be saved without circumcision.**

## 2. You Can Be Saved Without Circumcision (Acts 15:6–18)

### The Meeting

The apostles and elders meet in Jerusalem to consider this question. What follows is an executive summary of the discussion, containing testimonies from three speakers that all make the same point: **Jews and Gentiles are saved the same way.**

### Peter's Testimony

Peter rises first and points to his own experience "in the early days":

> God made a choice among you that the Gentiles might hear from my lips the message of the gospel and believe. (15:7)

He refers to Acts 10, when God gave him a vision of a sheet containing unclean foods descending from heaven. Peter was commanded to eat—a sign that the gospel is open to Gentiles. He then met Cornelius, a Roman centurion who was uncircumcised, and preached the gospel to him.

> God, who knows the heart, showed that he accepted them by giving the Holy Spirit to them, just as he did to us. (15:8)

The gift of the Holy Spirit—that extraordinary, unrepeatable outpouring at Pentecost for the Jews, then the Samaritans, and now the Gentiles—demonstrated God's acceptance.

> He did not discriminate between us and them, for he purified their hearts by **faith**. (15:9)

Both Jews and Gentiles are cleansed through faith in Jesus. Peter's conclusion is stunning:

> Why do you try to test God by putting on the necks of Gentiles a **yoke** that neither we nor our ancestors have been able to bear? No! We believe it is through the grace of our Lord Jesus that **we are saved, just as they are.** (15:10-11)

Peter calls the Law a "yoke"—not because the Law is bad, but because keeping it perfectly is impossible. Salvation comes through grace alone, not through works of the Law. Both Jew and Gentile receive free, unmerited grace.

### Paul and Barnabas's Testimony

The assembly becomes silent as they listen:

> The whole assembly became silent as they listened to Barnabas and Paul telling about the signs and wonders God had done among the Gentiles through them. (15:12)

The miracles didn't stop with Peter. Paul and Barnabas performed the same miracles among the Gentiles, demonstrating that they had apostolic authority equal to Peter's and that Gentiles have equal access to the gospel. What happened to the Jews happened to the Gentiles.

### James's Conclusion

James, the leader of the Jerusalem church, rises to give his judgment:

> Listen to me. Simon has described to us how God first intervened to choose a people for his name from the Gentiles. (15:13-14)

James argues that what Peter, Paul, and Barnabas experienced is exactly what Scripture predicted. He quotes from Amos:

> After this I will return and rebuild David's fallen tent. Its ruins I will rebuild, and I will restore it, that the rest of mankind may seek the Lord, even all the Gentiles who bear my name, says the Lord, who does these things—things known from long ago. (15:16-18)

To understand the full force of James's argument, we must read the larger context of Amos 9. There, Amos declares:

> Are not you Israelites the same to me as the Cushites? (Amos 9:7)

This is not flattery toward Israel. Rather, Amos reveals that despite all their privileges and the Law, Israel has fallen to the same level of sinfulness as the pagan nations. Jews are equal to Gentiles in their sin.

Therefore, if Jews—who are sinful like Gentiles—can be saved by grace without works of the Law, then salvation for Gentiles must be the same. The law of circumcision, the customs, the rituals—none of these save us.

### The Problem Was Never the Law

The problem with the Law is not the Law itself; it's with people. We cannot keep it perfectly. Jesus fulfilled the entire Law in our place. He obeyed perfectly, then exchanged his righteousness for our sin, bearing our guilt on the cross.

Does this mean the Law no longer applies? No. The Law still applies, but in wisdom and *after* being saved. It becomes part of who we are as God's treasured possession. The circumcision God desires is circumcision of the heart (Romans 2:29)—an inward transformation that produces godly living.

## 3. You Are Saved, So... (Acts 15:19–21, 28–29)

### The Apostolic Decree

James concludes:

> It is my judgment, therefore, that we should not make it difficult for the Gentiles who are turning to God. (15:19)

Instead, they write to the Gentiles with a fourfold prohibition:

> You are to abstain from food sacrificed to idols, from blood, from the meat of strangled animals and from sexual immorality. You will do well to avoid these things. (15:28-29)

This decree appears three times in Acts (15:20; 15:29; 21:25), suggesting its importance.

### Understanding the Four Prohibitions

These four commands appear together in Leviticus 17–18, laws that applied to resident aliens living among Israel:

- **Food sacrificed to idols**: Connected to pagan worship and temple prostitution
- **Sexual immorality**: Ranging from temple prostitution to incest, homosexuality, and adultery
- **Meat of strangled animals**: Improperly slaughtered meat (and thus containing blood that wasn't drained)
- **Blood**: A fundamental prohibition in Leviticus 17:10-12

### Two Interpretations

**One view** (Calvin) suggests that since Gentiles were "in the midst of" the Jewish community at synagogues, where Moses was read every Sabbath, they should follow these particular laws for the sake of Jewish sensibilities and to avoid needless offense. This is wisdom and Christian love.

**Another view** suggests these commands exist for the Gentiles' own good—a warning against pagan practices from which they come. Gentile converts came from a pagan world, surrounded by pagan parents, friends, bosses, and neighbors. The practices forbidden here were their constant temptation.

James gives them a pastoral word: These are the things you'll struggle with in your world. Stay away from them. You will do well if you keep away from sexual immorality.

### Application for Today

Many of us had to make tough decisions when we became Christian. We know the gospel is free—by grace and by faith. But we also know the gospel is transformative. It changes us. It calls us away from sin toward righteousness.

The warning about sexual immorality remains urgent for us today. According to Gallup polling since the early 2000s, American acceptance of behaviors the Bible condemns has reached record highs. Surprisingly, 70% of survey respondents identify as Christian—yet a large percentage consider fornication, homosexual behavior, and other sins "morally acceptable."

**We are drifting.** If your reason for accepting moral changes is "because my friends do it," you know the answer you should give.

### The Reality of Transformation

Where do you sit? The gospel calls us to change—our actions, our minds. If you want to be Christian and stay the same, you're wasting your time. It's like signing up for a free gym membership but refusing to lift a weight, wanting to keep the excess weight. What's the point?

When you become Christian, you commit to give up sin because in heaven there is no sin. Let's start now.

Many of us can testify to the transformation Christ brings:

- People who stopped pornography
- Young men and women who ended relationships with those who didn't share their faith
- Couples who committed to physical purity while dating
- Couples who were living together and quickly married upon becoming Christian

The appropriate response is **rejoice** at these transformations.

---

## 4. Rejoice (Acts 15:23–35)

Paul and Barnabas return to Antioch with representatives from Jerusalem, carrying the letter from the council. This time, the response is entirely different:

> The people read it and were glad for its encouraging message. Judas and Silas, who themselves were prophets, said much to encourage and strengthen the believers. (15:31-32)

This is the homecoming report people longed to hear: changed lives, the grace of God extended to all, and the freedom to follow Jesus without the burden of the Law.

The gospel brings both justification (salvation by grace) and transformation (living righteously). Both are essential. Both are worthy of rejoicing.
