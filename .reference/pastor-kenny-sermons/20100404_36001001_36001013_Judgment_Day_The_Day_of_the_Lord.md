# Zephaniah 1:1-13

**Title:** Judgment Day: The Day of the Lord
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew
**Date:** April 4, 2010

---

## The Coming Judgment

Zephaniah prophesied during the reign of King Josiah (around 640-609 BC), in the final days before Jerusalem's exile. Though Josiah himself was a good king who attempted religious reforms, Judah had become rotten at its core. The northern kingdom had already fallen to Assyria a century earlier, yet Judah learned nothing from their example. Now judgment would come—not to a few, but to the entire nation.

## 1. A Sweeping Judgment (1:2-4)

### The World

> "I will sweep away everything from the face of the earth," declares the LORD.
> 
> "I will sweep away both men and animals; I will sweep away the birds of the air and the fish of the sea. The wicked will have only heaps of rubble when I cut off man from the face of the earth," declares the LORD. (Zephaniah 1:2-3)

God's judgment will be all-encompassing—like a tidal wave wiping the earth clean. Notice the language echoes Noah's flood, when God looked upon humanity's wickedness and grieved that He had made them. This judgment reverses Creation itself. Just as God created the creatures of the sea and birds on Day 5, and land animals on Day 6, so Zephaniah describes their systematic removal.

This is no gentle washing away. God is looking at the wickedness of the world and deciding to hose it down the drain. The slate will be swept completely clean.

### Jerusalem

> "On that day," declares the LORD, "a cry will go up from the Fish Gate, wailing from the New Quarter, and a loud crash from the hills. Wail, you who live in the market district; all your merchants will be wiped out, all who trade with silver will be ruined." (Zephaniah 1:10-11)

Listen—do you hear that? A cry. A wail. A crash. This is the sound of judgment itself.

The Fish Gate lies on Jerusalem's northern wall—the vulnerable point where invaders would enter. The New Quarter, the recent expansions of the city, will feel the assault first. The market district, the economic heart of the city, will collapse. Like water flowing from high ground, the Babylonian invaders will sweep through from north to south, wiping away everything in their path.

### Personal Inspection

> At that time I (God) will search Jerusalem with lamps and punish those who are complacent, who are like wine left on its dregs, who think, "The LORD will do nothing, either good or bad." (Zephaniah 1:12)

But this judgment is not impersonal or mechanical. God Himself will personally visit Jerusalem, searching every corner with lamps, inspecting every cranny and crevice like a mother bleaching the toilet to ensure every bacterium is gone. No one will hide. There will be no safe bubble of air, no ark in the mountains of Nepal.

Those who will face this judgment are the complacent—people like wine left on its dregs, the leftovers who believe God is neutral and detached, neither rewarding good nor punishing evil. They imagine God as an Easter Bunny, handing out chocolates without discernment. They are catastrophically mistaken.

## 2. The Sin of Idolatry (1:4-6)

> "I will stretch out my hand against Judah and against all who live in Jerusalem. I will cut off from this place every remnant of Baal, the names of the pagan and the idolatrous priests—those who bow down on the roofs to worship the starry host, those who bow down and swear by the LORD and who also swear by Molech, those who turn back from following the LORD and neither seek the LORD nor inquire of him." (Zephaniah 1:4-6)

Judah's fundamental sin was idolatry. They worshiped Baal (the god of fertility) and Molech (the god of death, to whom children were sacrificed). It mattered not whether these false gods represented life or death—both were utterly incompatible with the worship of Yahweh.

What's particularly damning is that some tried to have it both ways. They swore by the LORD (Yahweh) and also by Molech. Surely, they reasoned, God would accept a divided loyalty? But as any relationship teaches us, exclusive commitment is non-negotiable. You cannot serve two masters. There is no such thing as a double love.

This problem was not confined to superstitious citizens on the margins. It involved the political leadership—the princes and king's sons—and the religious leadership, those who filled the temple with violence and deceit.

## 3. The Reversal of Promise (1:13)

> Their wealth will be plundered, their houses demolished. They will build houses but not live in them; they will plant vineyards but not drink the wine. (Zephaniah 1:13)

Deuteronomy had promised Israel that if they remained faithful, God would give them houses they didn't build, wells they didn't dig, vineyards and olive groves they didn't plant. This was the covenant blessing. Now it would be completely reversed—they would build but not inhabit, plant but not harvest. Everything promised would be stripped away.

Why? Because Judah forgot. They forgot who brought them out of Egypt. They forgot the God who provided them the land they stood upon. They abandoned the lessons their northern cousins had learned through destruction.

## 4. The Dreadful Expectation (1:7)

> Be silent before the Sovereign LORD, for the day of the LORD is near. The LORD has prepared a sacrifice; he has consecrated those he has invited. (Zephaniah 1:7)

What can you do when God has pronounced His verdict? Be silent. There is nothing left to do but wait in dreadful expectation. The Lord has prepared a sacrifice—Judah and Jerusalem themselves. He has consecrated those He has invited—the Babylonians—to come to dinner, with Jerusalem as the main course.

Even good King Josiah's reforms came too late. The nation was too far gone. A complete sweeping away was necessary.

## Application for Us Today

We stand in a similar position. Peter reminds us:

> In the last days, scoffers will come, saying, "Where is this 'coming' he promised? Ever since our fathers died, everything goes on as it has since the beginning of creation." But they deliberately forget that long ago by God's word the heavens existed and the earth was formed out of water. By these waters the world of that time was deluged and destroyed. By the same word the present heavens and earth are reserved for fire, being kept for the day of judgment. (2 Peter 3:3-7)

Judgment is assured. But do we truly believe it? Or do we, like those in Judah, harbor a double love? With one hand we pledge loyalty to God. With the other, perhaps when no one is looking, we honor another god.

Many of us have modern idols:
- The television, teaching us what to buy and what will make us happy
- The cable modem or streaming service, our altar to self-gratification
- The golf course or gym, where we bow down eighteen times, preoccupied with sports and our appearance
- Hollywood magazines with their stars and astrology
- For parents, the altar of achievement—selective school entrance exams, academic success, endless tutoring and music lessons. Children miss church or youth group, but missing tutoring? Never. I've paid for it.
- The person we cannot give up, even when they tempt us away from Jesus
- The leather god in our pocket or handbag, the wallet we feed with part-time jobs and protect fiercely when generosity calls

Friends, I know you are Christian. You love Jesus and God. But are you, like those who swear by both Yahweh and Molech, harboring a double love? Do you pledge loyalty to God while honoring another god when no one is watching?

Let us give up that god. Let us follow the one true God through Jesus. Everything else must be far, far second.

## Hope for Believers

For us who are in Christ, the Day of Judgment takes on a different meaning. Yahweh has prepared a sacrifice—His Son—so that He might invite us, a different set of people, to join Him.

> Since everything will be destroyed in this way, what kind of people ought you to be? You ought to live holy and godly lives as you look forward to the day of God and speed its coming. That day will bring about the destruction of the heavens by fire, and the elements will melt in the heat. But in keeping with his promise we are looking forward to a new heaven and a new earth, the home of righteousness. (2 Peter 3:11-13)

Judgment is coming. Let us repent and follow the ultimate Judge. The countdown has started since Jesus defeated death and rose as Lord of Judgment. Any time now could be the end.

But for believers, that day will not be dreadful. It will be one of hope—a new heaven and a new earth, the home of righteousness.
