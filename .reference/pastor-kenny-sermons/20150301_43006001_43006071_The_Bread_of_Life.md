# John 6:1-71

**Title:** The Bread of Life  
**Date:** 22 March 2015 (SMAC)  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction: Understanding Bread in Biblical Context

In Hebrew culture, bread wasn't a luxury—it was life itself. The word for "bread" (*lechem*) is synonymous with "food." When people shared a meal, they spoke of "breaking bread." This wasn't merely about sustenance; bread represented security, provision, and survival.

In our modern world of abundance, we've lost this sense of urgency. Yet Jesus chooses to teach about the heavenly bread using the most basic image his listeners could understand. As we study John 6, we'll move from Jesus meeting physical hunger to his invitation to feed on something far more essential: himself.

## Part 1: The Need for Material Food (Verses 1-21)

### The Crisis: A Feast Without Food

The scene opens with Jesus and his disciples on the mountainside near the Sea of Galilee during Passover season. A massive crowd has gathered because they've witnessed his healings and miracles. It's the night of Passover—the holiest meal of the Jewish calendar, commemorating Israel's deliverance from Egypt. Normally, everyone would be in Jerusalem. Instead, they're stuck on a mountainside with Jesus.

The problem is stark: they're hungry, and there's no food.

> "When Jesus looked up and saw a great crowd coming toward him, he said to Philip, 'Where shall we buy bread for these people to eat?'" (v. 5)

This is more than an inconvenience. It's a **logistical nightmare**. There are no bakeries in the wilderness. Five thousand mouths to feed. Even modern shopping centers would struggle. Philip's response reveals the magnitude: "It would take more than half a year's wages to buy enough bread for each one to have a bite!" (v. 7)—roughly equivalent to fifty thousand dollars in today's money.

It's also a **financial crisis**: completely beyond their means.

And it's a **religious crisis**: Passover is mandatory. This meal is essential to Jewish identity and covenant obedience.

### The Solution: Jesus Provides Abundantly

But Jesus is only testing Philip. He already knows what he will do.

A boy appears—mentioned nowhere else in Scripture—with five small barley loaves and two small fish. It's absurdly inadequate. Yet Jesus takes this meager offering, gives thanks, and distributes it. And something extraordinary happens:

> "Jesus then took the loaves, gave thanks, and distributed to those who were seated as much as they wanted. He did the same with the fish." (v. 11)

It's like feeding all of Eastwood with five bowls of rice and two containers of char-sieu. And there are leftovers—twelve baskets full.

In Jesus' hometown culture, a banquet is only considered a true banquet if there are leftovers. This extravagance makes a statement. Jesus doesn't just meet the need; he meets it abundantly, generously, almost scandalously.

### The Spiritual Point: Trust in God's Provision

> "Therefore I tell you, do not worry about your life, what you will eat or drink; or about your body, what you will wear. Is not life more than food, and the body more than clothes? Look at the birds of the air; they do not sow or reap or store away in barns, and yet your heavenly Father feeds them. Are you not much more valuable than they?" (Matthew 6:25-27)

Yet in a land of plenty, we're an anxious people. We worry about eating the right food, measuring kilojoules and sugar levels. We worry about whether our food hurts animals. We worry about what our food eats. We worry about packaging and country of origin and how it's presented on the plate.

Food anxiety has become its own illness.

But Jesus says: don't worry so much. Your Father supplies all your needs. Use common sense, and you'll be fine.

However, Jesus wants to give us something far more important than bread. Something no ordinary food can provide.

## Part 2: Misunderstanding Jesus' Intentions (Verses 14-27)

### The People's Desire: A Political Messiah

After witnessing this miracle, the crowd draws the wrong conclusion:

> "After the people saw the sign Jesus performed, they began to say, 'Surely this is the Prophet who is to come into the world.' Jesus, knowing that they intended to come and make him king by force, withdrew again to a mountain by himself." (v. 14-15)

There's an irony here: they want to make him king *by force*. A true king is powerful; force suggests weakness. What they really want is a king who will do their will. A leader who supplies their desires. A cosmic vending machine.

The next morning, they track him down:

> "Very truly I tell you, you are looking for me, not because you saw the signs I performed but because you ate the loaves and had your fill." (v. 26)

Jesus sees through them. They're not seeking him because they understood his works. They're hungry again. They want breakfast. They'll make him king so he'll feed them perpetually.

### The Application: What We Really Seek

This reveals a painful truth about ourselves. Some of us come to Jesus with a shopping list. We want him to secure our future, get us into selective schools, provide successful careers, find us spouses, make us healthy, give us security.

But listen carefully: **Jesus is not that kind of king**. He's not running for election. He's not here to eliminate sickness or distribute perpetual free food. He's not interested in making heaven on earth—we've tried that experiment before. The Garden of Eden ended in failure. The Kingdom of Israel ended in failure.

His solution is radical: a new heavens and a new earth.

And so Jesus points us away from material obsessions toward something infinitely greater.

## Part 3: Spiritual Food—The Bread of Life (Verses 27-71)

### The Contrast: Earthly vs. Eternal Food

> "Do not work for food that spoils, but for food that endures to eternal life, which the Son of Man will give you." (v. 27)

There are two kinds of food: bread that spoils and bread that endures. One gives life for a day. The other gives life for eternity.

The people still don't understand. When Jesus speaks of working for eternal life, they ask:

> "What must we do to do the works God requires?" (v. 28)

Jesus' answer is simple and revolutionary:

> "The work of God is this: to believe in the one he has sent." (v. 29)

Yet they ask for a sign—even though they've just seen one.

> "What sign then will you give that we may see it and believe you? Our ancestors ate the manna in the wilderness; as it is written: 'He gave them bread from heaven to eat.'" (v. 30-31)

They're comparing Jesus to Moses. They want another miracle, another display of power.

### Jesus Clarifies: God, Not Moses, Provides

> "Very truly I tell you, it is not Moses who has given you the bread from heaven, but it is my Father who gives you the true bread from heaven. For the bread of God is the bread that comes down from heaven and gives life to the world." (v. 32-33)

The people have become so fixated on the law and the lawgiver that they've forgotten the one behind it all: God himself. Jesus redirects their focus from the earthly manna to the true bread—the one that genuinely satisfies.

Their response is telling: "Sir, always give us this bread." (v. 34)

They still think literally. They want a permanent supply of free bread.

### The Declaration: "I Am the Bread of Life"

Then comes one of Scripture's most stunning declarations:

> "I am the bread of life. Whoever comes to me will never go hungry, and whoever believes in me will never be thirsty." (v. 35)

Not bread that sustains for a day. Jesus himself. Eternal satisfaction. Never hungry. Never thirsty again.

The benefits are staggering:

> "All those the Father gives me will come to me, and whoever comes to me I will never drive away. For I have come down from heaven not to do my will but to do the will of him who sent me. And this is the will of him who sent me, that I shall lose none of all those he has given me, but raise them up at the last day. For my Father's will is that everyone who looks to the Son and believes in him shall have eternal life, and I will raise them up at the last day." (v. 37-40)

This isn't about vitamins or fiber. It's about eternal life. On judgment day, when God's wrath is poured out for humanity's sin, those who believe in Jesus will be saved. They will be raised up. They will have eternal life.

This is a matter of life and death.

### The Offense: Belief Requires More Than Miracles

But the people struggle:

> "But as I told you, you have seen me and still you do not believe." (v. 36)

When Jesus says, "I am the bread that came down from heaven," they grumble:

> "Is this not Jesus, the son of Joseph, whose father and mother we know? How can he now say, 'I came down from heaven'?" (v. 41-42)

They know his family. They know his origins. How can he claim to be from heaven?

Jesus replies: "Stop grumbling among yourselves." (v. 43)

### The Metaphor: Eating as Believing

Then Jesus says something that shocks them:

> "I am the living bread that came down from heaven. Whoever eats this bread will live forever. This bread is my flesh, which I will give for the life of the world." (v. 51)

The Jews are horrified: "How can this man give us his flesh to eat?" (v. 52)

But Jesus isn't promoting cannibalism or anything spooky. He's using metaphor—just as he will later call himself the Gate and the Vine. No one thinks he has leaves or is made of wood.

> "Very truly I tell you, unless you eat the flesh of the Son of Man and drink his blood, you have no life in you. Whoever eats my flesh and drinks my blood has eternal life, and I will raise them up at the last day. For my flesh is real food and my blood is real drink." (v. 53-55)

In a discourse about bread and manna, eating is metaphor for believing. It's about identification with Jesus. It's the language of relationship.

Later Jesus clarifies:

> "Whoever eats my flesh and drinks my blood remains in me, and I in them." (v. 56)

To "eat" Jesus is to have him in you, part of you, constituting your very being. It's relationship language.

Listen to how we use these phrases in everyday speech: "you are what you eat," "you're so cute I just want to gobble you up," "you look good enough to eat." And in love songs: "starving me of your love," "I hunger for your touch," "hunger for your love."

Eating Jesus means accepting the flesh he sacrificed for us. It means loving and trusting him as he loves us. It means him remaining intimately inside us.

### The Resolution: His Words Are Life

Jesus brings clarity to this strange language:

> "The Spirit gives life; the flesh counts for nothing. The words I have spoken to you—they are full of the Spirit and life." (v. 63)

Now we can trace the progression: **Eat bread = Eat flesh = Believe Jesus = Believe his words.**

His words are full of the Spirit and life.

We weren't there to witness the feeding of five thousand or the walking on water. But through his recorded words in Scripture, we can see and believe. These words are Spirit-filled and life-giving.

And we will have that same Spirit and life if we accept them, receive them, believe them, **eat them**.

### The Call: Devour Scripture

So eat up his words! Devour the Bible to understand what he's about. Digest his words. Ruminate on his teachings. Drink up his wisdom. Be spoon-fed the truth.

Make sure you bring your Bible and a pen. Let his words form the very fabric of who you are—woven into the fibers of your being. If Jesus were here today, he'd say: "Be genetically modified. Have me in your DNA."

How can we do this more this week? Use existing structures. Print a copy of Scripture and leave it where you'll see it—in your bathroom, by your bed. Attend church. Pre-read before Bible studies. Go to talks in the city and at university. Call Christian friends and discuss. Find what works for you, but the core is simple: **get into Jesus through his words.**

And if we do this and know him, we will live.

### The Cost: His Flesh Was Given

There's one element in this passage that isn't metaphor:

> "This bread is my flesh, which I will give for the life of the world." (v. 51)

His flesh was indeed given for us. Notice the timing: we're at Passover. At Passover, it's not just bread that's eaten. The bread goes with the lamb. Where is the lamb?

God supplied it in Jesus.

In a few years, Jesus will be sacrificed on the cross. His flesh will be torn and killed. His blood will be shed. The Lamb of God will be offered to take away the sin of the world.

### The Rejection: Many Turn Away

But many cannot accept his words. His claim that he is the only way. His assertion that he is from heaven. His insistence that he alone gives life.

> "From this time many of his disciples turned back and no longer followed him." (v. 66)

The majority abandoned him. Those twelve baskets of leftovers from the miraculous feeding—now there's bitter irony. Too many left behind.

But a faithful few remained. Even the inner circle struggles:

> "You do not want to leave too, do you?" Jesus asked the Twelve. Simon Peter answered him, "Lord, to whom shall we go? You have the words of eternal life. We have come to believe and to know that you are the Holy One of God." (v. 67-69)

Peter has grasped it. Jesus has the words of eternal life. Not tricks. Not free food. Not earthly benefits. **Words that give life forever.**

## Conclusion: Bread is Life

Bread is simple: flour, water, yeast, salt. Yet it's basic nutrition for billions across the world. No bread means no life. Bread means life. The breadwinner is the one who brings bread home. Bread is so vital it meant "food" itself in biblical times.

Jesus doesn't just meet physical hunger. He offers himself—the source of eternal life. He died so that we might live. He gave his flesh so that we might have the nutrients we need.

We need Jesus. For life.

Not for comfort alone, though he gives that. Not for ethical standards or instruction, though he offers those. We need him for the deepest nutrient of all: **eternal life**.

This is the bread of life. And he invites us to eat.
