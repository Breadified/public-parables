## The Myth of Neutrality

We live in a world obsessed with neutrality. We're told to be objective, unbiased, independent—the "nice guy" approach. But this is a myth.

Saying there are no values is itself a value statement. To claim neutrality is to make a claim about the world. You cannot escape taking a position; neutrality itself is a position.

Consider what happened when Facebook faced international criticism over removing posts critical of the gay agenda. Mark Zuckerberg quickly met with conservatives to be "seen as neutral." But neutrality is not actually possible. In our pluralistic world, someone's values are always being promoted—the only question is whose.

This plays out everywhere: "Keep God out of politics." "Keep God out of school." "Keep God out of work." "Keep God out of media." These aren't neutral positions—they're specific ideological commitments that exclude the Christian worldview from public life.

We admire Switzerland for avoiding war since 1814, maintaining its "neutrality." But this neutrality often masked something darker: Switzerland sold high-tech weapons to the Nazis, and Swiss bankers profited from stolen goods acquired during the Holocaust. Neutrality became a cover for complicity and cowardice.

Martin Luther grasped this truth profoundly:

> If I profess with the loudest voice and clearest exposition every portion of the truth of God except precisely that little point which the world and the devil are at that moment attacking, I am not confessing Christ, however boldly I may be professing Christ. Where the battle rages, there the loyalty of the soldier is proved, and to be steady on all the battlefield besides is mere flight and disgrace if he flinches at that point.

The question is not whether to take a side—you always do. The question is: which side will you choose?

## Part 1: Not Everything Is Beneficial

Paul challenges our desire for moral comfort with a crucial principle:

> "I have the right to do anything," you say—but **not everything is beneficial**. "I have the right to do anything"—but not everything is constructive. (1 Corinthians 10:23)

Over the previous chapters, the Corinthian church had been told that food offered to idols is morally neutral—it doesn't matter spiritually whether you eat it or not. But Paul pivots here. While we may have the *right* to do something, that doesn't mean we should do it. The real question isn't what is *permitted*, but what is *beneficial*.

### The Heart of Fellowship

Paul's concern isn't actually about the food itself. His concern is about **participation and fellowship**. In a family meal, what makes it truly special—a fancy restaurant or McDonald's—is not the quality of the food. It's the people you're with. It's the participation.

This is why Paul contrasts eating with demons with eating with the Lord:

> Is not the cup of thanksgiving for which we give thanks a participation in the blood of Christ? And is not the bread that we break a participation in the body of Christ? (1 Corinthians 10:16)

The cup and bread aren't mere objects. They represent our fellowship with Christ and with one another. When we participate in the Lord's Supper, we're declaring our unity under one Lord. Similarly, when the Old Testament Israelites ate sacrifices at the altar, they weren't just consuming meat—they were participating in an act of worship and solidarity:

> Do not those who eat the sacrifices participate in the altar? (1 Corinthians 10:18)

The participation was the whole point. You were identifying yourself with that covenant community and that sacrifice. These were not neutral acts.

### You Cannot Serve Two Lords

Paul writes:

> Do not the sacrifices of pagans are offered to demons, not to God, and I do not want you to be participants with demons. You cannot drink the cup of the Lord and the cup of demons too; you cannot have a part in both the Lord's table and the table of demons. (1 Corinthians 10:20-21)

In pluralistic Corinth, every god came with rituals and expectations of loyalty. Eating at an idol's table was an act of allegiance. Therefore, you cannot participate in both the Lord's covenant and another god's covenant simultaneously.

> Are we trying to arouse the Lord's jealousy? Are we stronger than he? (1 Corinthians 10:22)

The issue isn't the food. It's the fellowship. It's the participation. It's what you're declaring about your loyalty through that participation.

## Part 2: Seeking the Good of Others

But what does this look like practically? Paul gives us boundaries:

> Do not cause anyone to stumble, whether Jews, Greeks or the church of God—even as I try to please everyone in every way. For I am not seeking my own good but the good of many, so that they may be saved. (1 Corinthians 10:32-33)

The principle is stunning: **No one should seek their own good, but the good of others** (1 Corinthians 10:24).

### Knowledge Changes Everything

Paul establishes a practical framework:

> Eat anything sold in the meat market without raising questions of conscience, for, "The earth is the Lord's, and everything in it." (1 Corinthians 10:25-26)

In normal circumstances, you can eat freely. You don't investigate where every piece of food comes from. You don't interrogate your host's kitchen. All food belongs to God.

But then:

> If an unbeliever invites you to a meal and you want to go, eat whatever is put before you without raising questions of conscience. But if someone says to you, "This has been offered in sacrifice," then do not eat it, both for the sake of the one who told you and for the sake of conscience. (1 Corinthians 10:27-28)

Knowledge changes everything. Imagine eating at a restaurant, finding the kitchen filthy, then returning to your table. Suddenly, food you would have eaten becomes inedible. The food didn't change—your knowledge did. And knowledge creates moral responsibility.

Similarly, if someone tells you food was offered in sacrifice, you now know something significant: this meal carries spiritual weight for that person. They see it as a special dedication. If you eat it anyway, they will interpret your participation as approval—as agreement with their religious worldview. You become a stumbling block.

### Contemporary Applications

This principle extends to many situations in our world:

**Religious meals and rituals.** When a Buddhist temple offers a meal, or a mosque, the participation means something. It declares identification with that faith community. Similarly, practices like the joss stick or kowtow in family contexts—while family traditions—carry spiritual significance. With this knowledge, we must ask: what am I participating in?

**Celebrations of sin.** What about workplace events supporting causes that contradict Scripture? What about gay "weddings"?

As Christians, we believe the Bible is our instruction for how to live. Marriage is God's institution—a covenantal union between one man and one woman, designed for permanence, fidelity, and the bearing of children. Remove gender from marriage, and you logically remove the requirement for any specific number of participants.

But the question isn't whether you can *technically* attend or eat cake. It's what you're *participating in*. A cake is just food. But your presence and participation declare solidarity with that event. You're telling that person: "I approve. I celebrate this. I'm with you in this."

This is why bakers and photographers in America have refused to participate in certain ceremonies. It's not bigotry—it's a conscientious refusal to participate in something they believe contradicts God's design. And this refusal makes sense through Paul's lens: you're seeking to avoid stumbling someone by giving false approval.

**Participation matters. The food doesn't. The participation does.**

> Participation in such things is not beneficial. It is not constructive. For directly or indirectly, you are condoning sin. There is a limit to accommodation.

## Part 3: Glorifying God Through Every Choice

Paul brings it all together:

> So whether you eat or drink or whatever you do, do it all for the glory of God. (1 Corinthians 10:31)

We often read this and think: "How can I make this activity Christian? I'm eating lunch—let me glorify God at lunch. I'm going to work—let me glorify God at work." This is good. But Paul is suggesting something subtly different.

It's not: "Whatever you happen to do, make it Christian as a second thought."

It's: **"If you are going to eat, if you are going to drink, if you are going to do something, do it for the glory of God."** The question comes first: Should I do this at all? Will this glorify God?

With modern technology and wealth, we can do almost anything. But just because something isn't explicitly sinful doesn't mean it's beneficial. Many things are distractions that pull us from God's purposes. The question isn't "Is there harm in this?" but "What good does this serve? How will it affect someone's salvation?"

The motivation is salvation:

> **For I am not seeking my own good but the good of many, so that they may be saved.** (1 Corinthians 10:33)

This is the heart: People are dying in darkness and sin. They need light. When we realize what's at stake—someone's eternal destiny—our choices shift. We don't ask, "What can I get away with?" We ask, "What will point people toward Christ?"

## The Myth of Neutrality Revisited

There is nothing neutral in God's world. Not meals, not knowledge, not activity, not institutions.

**Knowledge is not neutral.** All information comes with precommitments. Wikipedia is biased. Facebook is biased. News outlets are biased. The "fear of the Lord is the beginning of knowledge" (Proverbs 1:7)—this means there's no knowledge separate from a relationship with God.

**Government is not neutral.** Either a society is ordered on Christian principles or on contrary ones. When Christianity recedes in the public square, other worldviews fill the void.

**Schools are not neutral.** There is either Christian education, or pagan, humanistic, or sexually-ideological education. When Australian schools removed Special Religious Education to be "neutral," they filled that space with Safe Schools sex ideology.

**Activities are not neutral.** Every action carries a statement. Every participation declares allegiance.

The world presents a false choice: either you're actively condemning something, or you're silently accepting it. But silence is a choice. Neutrality is complicity.

## A Model of Faithful Courage

Contrast the Swiss banker-collaborators with Carl Lutz, a Swiss vice consul in Hungary during World War II.

While his nation claimed neutrality, Lutz took sides. He negotiated 8,000 letters of authority to emigrate, then cleverly multiplied them by using families instead of individuals per letter, grouping them into collective passports. Each document placed recipients under Swiss protection.

On one occasion, when Hungarian militiamen fired on Jews near the Danube, Lutz jumped into the river to rescue a bleeding woman. He carried her to his car, declaring her a foreign citizen under Swiss protection, and drove away while the fascists stood stunned.

Why? Because Lutz was a committed Christian. He wouldn't remain neutral while humans were slaughtered. He did what was right, regardless of his nation's official stance.

> I pray that you will pick a side and do what is right too.

## The Battle Rages

Nothing is neutral because everything that matters is contested. The moment someone takes a stand on an issue, neutrality becomes impossible. You must choose.

- Which side will you take on truth and falsehood?
- Which side will you take on sexuality and biblical anthropology?
- Which side will you take on the value of human life?
- Which side will you take on the Lord's Supper and participation with Christ?

> Because sometimes to be neutral is to be immoral.

Where the battle rages most fiercely, there your loyalty must be most clearly proven. Everything else is flight and disgrace.
