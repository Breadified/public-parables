# Isaiah 34:1–35:10

**Title:** The Ultimate Judgment and Joy  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## God's Judgment: The Call to Listen

Isaiah opens with an urgent summons to all creation. The prophet calls the nations to hear God's verdict against them because God's anger at the world's rebellion is just and final.

> **Isaiah 34:1–2**
> Come near, you nations, and listen; pay attention, you peoples! Let the earth hear, and all that is in it, the world, and all that comes out of it! The LORD is angry with all nations; his wrath is upon all their armies. He will totally destroy them, he will give them over to slaughter.

Why is God angry? The previous chapters of Isaiah reveal the nations have rebelled against God's rule. They honor Him with their lips while their hearts remain far from Him. They have rejected His ways, choosing instead the comfort of self-made idols. Their leaders are proud and drunk, making covenants with death itself. Their societies are marked by extortion, bribery, murder, and evil counsel.

## The Result: Complete Destruction

The consequence of such rebellion is absolute. God's judgment brings total annihilation—a destruction so complete it extends beyond the earth itself to the cosmic order.

> **Isaiah 34:3–4**
> Their slain will be thrown out, their dead bodies will send up a stench; the mountains will be soaked with their blood. All the stars of the heavens will be dissolved and the sky rolled up like a scroll; all the starry host will fall like withered leaves from the vine, like shriveled figs from the fig tree.

To grasp the scale of this devastation, consider the Battle of the Somme on July 1, 1916—one of history's most catastrophic events. Twenty thousand soldiers were killed or wounded in the first hour alone. Fifty-three thousand fell on the first day. Over the course of the entire campaign, more than 1.5 million perished. One soldier observed how blood mingled with mud and poppy flowers. Another noted the acrid smell of warm human blood heated by the sun. Yet even this incomprehensible loss represents only a fragment of what Isaiah describes for the entire world.

## Edom as God's Example

Isaiah uses Edom—the blood brother yet bitter rival of Israel—as a specific example of God's judgment. The language shifts to vivid imagery of sacrifice, reflecting the ancient Jewish sacrificial system where animals were slain at the altar, their blood poured out, and their fat burned as an offering to God.

> **Isaiah 34:5–7**
> My sword has drunk its fill in the heavens; see, it descends in judgment on Edom, the people I have totally destroyed. The sword of the LORD is bathed in blood, it is covered with fat—the blood of lambs and goats, fat from the kidneys of rams. For the LORD has a sacrifice in Bozrah and a great slaughter in Edom. And the wild oxe will fall with them, the bull calves and the great bulls. Their land will be drenched with blood, and the dust will be soaked with fat.

This sacrificial imagery carries profound meaning. The ancient sacrificial system served two purposes: to appease God's wrath and to teach Israel that sin carries a violent price. But now the judgment extends beyond animals to people themselves. The nations have become the sacrifice.

## The Land Becomes Desolate

With the people destroyed, the land they inhabited becomes barren and forsaken. Fire consumes it, and wildlife reclaims what human civilization once dominated.

> **Isaiah 34:9–10**
> Edom's streams will be turned into pitch, her dust into burning sulfur; her land will become blazing pitch! It will not be quenched night and day; its smoke will rise forever. From generation to generation it will lie desolate; no one will ever pass through it again.

The cities that once held kingdoms and princes crumble into ruin, consumed by thorns and brambles.

> **Isaiah 34:11–13**
> The desert owl and screech owl will possess it; the great owl and the raven will nest there. God will stretch out over Edom the measuring line of chaos and the plumb line of desolation. Her nobles will have nothing there to be called a kingdom, all her princes will vanish away. Thorns will overrun her citadels, nettles and brambles her strongholds. She will become a haunt for jackals, a home for owls.

The land transforms into a wilderness where wild creatures find refuge and establish their dominion. Yet even in this desolation, God's order remains perfect—not one creature lacks its mate, for God's word has summoned them together.

> **Isaiah 34:16–17**
> Look in the scroll of the LORD and read: None of these will be missing, not one will lack her mate. For it is his mouth that has given the order, and his Spirit will gather them together. He allots their portions; his hand distributes them by measure. They will possess it forever and dwell there from generation to generation.

## Why Judgment Cannot Be Avoided

Judgment is not a comfortable subject. Even God Himself finds it strange, for it contradicts His nature of mercy and grace. Yet it remains inseparable from His character because judgment flows necessarily from His kingship.

A true king must rule, or he ceases to be a king. A true king must enforce his own law, or his authority dissolves into meaninglessness. Therefore, sin and rebellion must ultimately be put down. God's patience is almost unbelievable—He bears with creation's defiance for centuries—but His justice cannot be indefinitely delayed. The world is on notice.

Consider this tension: Why doesn't God simply forgive and move on, as we humans do when we forget an injury? The answer lies in two foundational truths.

**First, forgiveness without justice becomes complicity in evil.** When harm is minor, we forget easily enough. But what of serious crimes? Can a rape victim be told simply to let it go? Can we tell such a person they are petty for seeking justice? Absolutely not. Justice must be done. God cannot pretend rebellion never happened. To do so would require Him to cease being a moral God. A God who says rape is acceptable cannot remain holy.

**Second, while humans forget, God does not.** 

> **Isaiah 34:8**
> For the LORD has a day of vengeance, a year of retribution, to uphold Zion's cause.

God's memory is perfect. He sees all and forgets nothing. Even human societies, with their limited capacity to remember, hold onto the memories of great atrocities. Who forgets the ethnic cleansing of Bosnia? Who forgets the Holocaust? These crimes echo through generations. If human memory is this powerful, God's memory—perfect and infinite—certainly does not fade. Justice delayed is not justice denied; it is justice deferred until the appointed day.

## The Message of Joy: Restoration and Return

Yet Isaiah does not end with judgment and desolation. The prophet turns to paint a radiant picture of restoration. The same God who judges is also the God who saves.

> **Isaiah 35:3–4**
> Strengthen the feeble hands, steady the knees that give way; say to those with fearful hearts, "Be strong, do not fear; your God will come, he will come with vengeance; with divine retribution he will come to save you."

## The Desert Blooms

The barren wilderness transforms into a fruitful garden. The same land that was consumed by fire and abandoned to jackals becomes a place of abundance and beauty.

> **Isaiah 35:1–2**
> The desert and the parched land will be glad; the wilderness will rejoice and blossom. Like the crocus, it will burst into bloom; it will rejoice greatly and shout for joy. The glory of Lebanon will be given to it, the splendor of Carmel and Sharon; they will see the glory of the LORD, the splendor of our God.

> **Isaiah 35:6–7**
> Water will gush forth in the wilderness and streams in the desert. The burning sand will become a pool, the thirsty ground bubbling springs. In the haunts where jackals once lay, grass and reeds and papyrus will grow.

This echoes the recovery of Eden itself—a land restored to fruitfulness by God's creative power, flowing with water and overflowing with life.

## The Disabled Are Healed

The return of God's people is marked by miraculous restoration. Those who were broken are made whole.

> **Isaiah 35:5–6**
> Then will the eyes of the blind be opened and the ears of the deaf unstopped. Then will the lame leap like a deer, and the mute tongue shout for joy.

Why does Isaiah emphasize healing of the disabled? Not merely to display miraculous power, but because these people must walk a holy highway. Only the clean can travel this Way of Holiness.

> **Isaiah 35:8–10**
> And a highway will be there; it will be called the Way of Holiness. The unclean will not journey on it; it will be for those who walk in that Way; wicked fools will not go about on it. No lion will be there, nor will any ferocious beast get up on it; they will not be found there. But only the redeemed will walk there. And the ransomed of the LORD will return. They will enter Zion with singing; everlasting joy will crown their heads. Gladness and joy will overtake them, and sorrow and sighing will flee away.

The redeemed walk in safety, protected from all danger, crowned with eternal joy. Their sorrows and sighs are erased forever.

## Application: We Are the Nations

This passage demands a personal reckoning. We are not distant observers of Isaiah's prophecy—we are the nations of whom he speaks. We are those who have rebelled against God's rule over us.

Some rebellion is loud and obvious: destructive living, violence, immorality, treating others badly, following self-made gods. But rebellion can also be quiet and subtle: living morally and decently on the surface while the heart remains indifferent to God, wishing He would leave us alone, honoring Him with our lips while our hearts are far from Him.

Either form is rebellion. And though we may regard it as insignificant, it is worse than any earthly crime. It is worse than rape. It is worse than ethnic cleansing. It is worse than the Holocaust. Because it is rebellion against the very source of all goodness, all justice, and all life.

## The Seriousness of God's Anger

The vivid imagery of Isaiah's judgment—fire and brimstone, blood-letting, the land as a great slaughterhouse, animals living in volcanic desolation, bodies of the slain—demands we ask: Is this literal description or symbolic language?

Perhaps yes, perhaps no. The point, however, is unmistakable: it is *bad*. God is serious about sin. God's anger toward you is not something to dismiss or minimize. To imagine that hell is cool because you can hang out with your friends is to profoundly misunderstand the nature of God's wrath. It is terrifying. It should motivate us to do everything necessary to escape it—to cut off our hand if it causes us to stumble, to deny ourselves utterly.

Jesus Himself, facing the prospect of bearing God's wrath on the cross, prayed in Gethsemane with such anguish that He sweated drops of blood. If the God-man found the weight of God's anger unbearable, what does that tell us?

## The Solution: Jesus Takes Our Punishment

Yet here is the gospel: Jesus Christ underwent God's full judgment on the cross. He became the sacrifice—not for animals, but for us. He absorbed all the wrath, all the vengeance, all the retribution that we deserve. He bore our judgment so we would not have to bear it ourselves.

Those who believe in Him are now like the exiles returning to the land. They are cleansed. They walk the Way of Holiness. They enter Zion with singing, crowned with everlasting joy.

There is an apt illustration in the prophet's own childhood experience. As a young boy in Malaysia, he had a habit of somersaulting on an armchair despite his father's repeated warnings that it was dangerous. One afternoon, his feet flew over the top of the chair, and he heard the terrible crack of the window breaking beneath the couch. He was in serious trouble. His father had warned him. His father had been proven right. And his father would be home soon to see the damage.

The boy was consumed with fear and wished desperately that the problem would disappear, that he could reverse what he had done. But he couldn't. He couldn't undo it. He couldn't make his father forget about it. He could only wait in terror.

Then his mother told him to go to sleep—perhaps using the incident as an excuse for an afternoon nap. When he awoke, his father was home. But his father said nothing about the broken window. The window was fixed. Everything was normal. The trouble he thought he was in was a non-event. His father had handled it while he slept.

This is what Jesus does for us. We have sinned. We have broken things that matter. The Father is coming home. But before He arrives, there is still time. There is still a way out.

It is not by reversing what we have done—that is impossible. We cannot undo our sin. It is not by forgetting—that is equally impossible. God does not forget, and neither should we pretend the debt doesn't exist. It is not by wishful thinking or passive waiting.

The solution is to believe Jesus. To trust the One who opened the eyes of the blind, made the lame walk, and gave voice to the deaf. To believe that He has borne our judgment, satisfied God's justice, and made us clean.

## Prayer of Commitment

If you are not yet a Christian, or if you are unsure whether you truly belong to Christ, this prayer is for you:

> Father, I am sorry that I have rebelled against you. I know that it hurts you and makes you angry at me. Thank you for Jesus who takes that anger away from me. Help me to continue to trust Jesus to save me. Amen.
