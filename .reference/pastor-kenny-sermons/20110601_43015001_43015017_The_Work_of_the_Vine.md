# John 15:1-17

**Title:** The Work of the Vine
**Date:** 21 June 2011
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction: Growing Fruit

When you plant a garden, you learn quickly that fruit doesn't appear by accident. This year, our family started a veggie patch at home, digging up earth into mounds and tilling the soil. Last autumn brought our first harvest—pumpkins, cucumbers, chilies, broccoli, cherry tomatoes, and some rather dodgy carrots. It's been fun, good for the environment, good for the budget, and a wonderful family activity. (Though I'll confess, there was the time my two-year-old Rebecca ate the manure—which taught us all something about supervision.)

In John 15, Jesus uses gardening to teach us about ministry and leadership. The imagery is deeply rooted in Scripture, and as we investigate this passage, we'll discover what it truly means to bear fruit in God's kingdom.

## 1. The True Vine

Jesus opens with one of His great "I am" declarations: **"I am the true vine."** This title isn't about vineyards or agriculture—it's a theological claim that echoes throughout Scripture.

### Jesus as the True Israel

The vine is a common Old Testament metaphor for Israel as God's chosen nation. In Psalm 80, the psalmist recalls how God brought the nation out of Egypt:

> You brought a vine out of Egypt; you drove out the nations and planted it. You cleared the ground for it, and it took root and filled the land. The mountains were covered with its shade, the mighty cedars with its branches.

But the passage takes a tragic turn. The psalmist laments:

> Why have you broken down its walls so that all who pass by pick its grapes? Boars from the forest ravage it and the creatures of the field feed on it.

The vine—Israel—had been decimated by exile and judgment. Yet the psalmist looks forward to restoration:

> Watch over this vine, the root your right hand has planted, the son you have raised up for yourself... Let your hand rest on the man at your right hand, the son of man you have raised up for yourself.

For centuries after the exile, God's people waited for this son, the one who would restore the vine. There were false messiahs, failed hopes. Then Jesus appeared and announced: **"I am the true vine."** He is that son of man raised by God Himself. He is God not merely looking down from heaven, but coming down from heaven to restore His people and bring salvation.

## 2. The Branches: Cleansed by the Word

In verse 3, Jesus tells His disciples: **"You are already clean because of the word I have spoken to you."**

This is profoundly important. Jesus cleanses His disciples through His teaching. Throughout John's Gospel, Jesus emphasizes that He Himself is the Word—not merely a messenger, but God's complete self-revelation in human form.

> In the beginning was the Word, and the Word was with God, and the Word was God... The Word became flesh and made his dwelling among us.

The "I am" declarations throughout John all point to this reality. Jesus is:
- The true bread (we live by every word from God)
- The true light (revealing truth and calling us to obedience)
- The gate (entrance to abundant life through His teaching)
- The good shepherd (whose voice we recognize)
- The resurrection and the life (whom we trust and obey)
- The way, the truth, and the life
- The true vine

Jesus' teaching encompasses the kingdom of God, God's character, His own identity, judgment, heaven and hell, His death and redemption. When His disciples later asked where else they could go, they concluded: **"You have the words of eternal life."**

### How Does a Word Cleanse?

Through authority. The word of authority that forgives. The word of reconciliation. The word of promise backed by His atoning sacrifice. Like a political prisoner waiting for a word of clemency or pardon from the president, we are cleansed and freed by the authoritative word of our King.

### Remain in the Word

Jesus' command is clear: **"Remain in me, and I will remain in you."** A branch cannot bear fruit by itself—it must remain connected to the vine. So too with us: **"If a man remains in me and I in him, he will bear much fruit; apart from me you can do nothing."**

This has practical implications. We must read God's Word as a personal discipline. We must stay engaged when Scripture is taught, not dismissing it as only for newcomers or non-leaders. I've noticed church leaders sometimes remaining outside during the sermon—preparing tea, organizing Sunday school, having conversations—as if the Word is for others but not for them. This reveals a dangerous assumption. All of us need to remain in the vine.

Throughout the Reformation, the principle of biblical authority was restored: Scripture is our authority—not popes, not bishops, not cultural trends, not merely personal experience. Yet we must be careful. Some, in their enthusiasm, try to add other voices—visions, dreams, inner promptings—to the Word, without the safeguard of Scripture's discernment. This becomes too convenient for manipulation. If God has already spoken supremely through His Word, we should stop trying to find shortcuts around it.

The truth is, **remaining in the Word must be practice, not just theory. We must obey what we read.**

## 3. The Fruit: Loving One Another Through the Word

If Jesus' primary ministry is teaching the Word, then our ministry must be the same. We bear fruit through proclaiming the Word and loving one another.

Throughout this passage, the themes are woven together:
- Remaining in Jesus = bearing fruit = obeying His commands = loving one another
- These are not separate acts; they form one unified ministry

**Jesus' command is simple and supreme: "Love each other as I have loved you."**

But notice what this means. We are to love one another as Jesus has loved us—and His primary ministry was shaping us through His Word. Therefore, our ministry to others must center on God's Word. This is vine work: bringing the Word of God into individuals' lives, growing disciples through the truth.

It may seem strange that if we were converted through the Word, our ministries to others would be different. Yet many modern churches have shifted to emphasizing primarily social justice and welfare systems, as if the Word is secondary. While compassion matters deeply, it cannot replace the central work of proclaiming Scripture.

### The Ministry of Word-Sharing

> Let the word of Christ dwell in you richly as you teach and admonish one another with all wisdom, and as you sing psalms, hymns and spiritual songs with gratitude in your hearts to God. (Colossians 3:16)

> I myself am convinced, my brothers and sisters, that you yourselves are full of goodness, filled with knowledge and competent to instruct one another. (Romans 15:14)

Word ministry happens in both formal and informal settings:

**Formal ministry:**
- Small groups
- Preaching and teaching

**Informal ministry (often the larger work):**
- Welcoming people warmly
- Encouraging one another with what we've learned from Scripture
- Following up with questions and genuine care
- Having real conversations (not mere Facebook friendships)
- Among Christians, holding one another accountable
- Among non-Christians, engaging in wise conversations that lead toward repentance and faith

This is hard work. It's awkward at times. It requires vulnerability and courage. But it is God's design.

> They will neither harm nor destroy on all my holy mountain, for the earth will be full of the knowledge of the LORD as the waters cover the sea. (Isaiah 11:9)

This vision—that knowledge of God fills the earth—comes through faithful people sharing God's Word with one another. **This is the most important work we can do.**

## 4. Don't Confuse Vine Work with Trellis Work

Here's a crucial caveat: many church activities, while appearing necessary, are actually trellis work rather than vine work.

A trellis is support structure—it's important for the plant to grow, but the trellis itself produces no fruit. Churches need trellis: events, rosters, administrative systems, building maintenance, council fees. Some trellis is genuinely necessary. But I suspect much of it distracts us from the actual people work required to grow the vine.

We must guard against four dangers:

1. **Being so preoccupied with organizing that we forget to remain in Jesus**
2. **Being so preoccupied with organizing that we forget the Word**
3. **Being so preoccupied with organizing that we forget people**—their names, their genuine stories, their real spiritual struggles
4. **Being so preoccupied with organizing that we lose sight of the bigger picture**

Leaders should be chosen based on their vine skills, not their trellis skills. They are those who can and do proclaim God's Word—whether in Sunday school, youth group, Bible study, or one-on-one conversations.

### Shifting Priorities

The shift from trellis-dominated ministry to vine-dominated ministry means moving:
- From running programs to **building people**
- From running events to **training people**
- From using people to **growing people**
- From filling gaps to **training new workers**
- From solving problems to **helping people make progress**
- From clinging to ordained ministry to **developing team leadership**
- From focusing on church structure to **forging ministry partnerships**
- From relying on external training institutions to **establishing local training**
- From addressing immediate pressures to **aiming for long-term expansion**—train leaders first, and ministry will naturally follow
- From management to **ministry**

This means recognizing that Sunday school, for instance, should never be a dead end. We should aim to develop people into leaders of leaders, giving them room to make gracious mistakes as they grow. Start small—see that person sitting at the back. Begin training them. Develop them.

Minimize unnecessary trellis by eliminating constant weekly rosters. Go deep rather than wide. Prioritize committed people over flaky volunteers. When rosters are necessary, make them regular and seasonal rather than weekly, getting more effective return on your investment. Delegate without micromanaging. Trust your leaders with macro decisions. Recognize that different types of meetings serve different purposes.

## Conclusion

Jesus is the true vine. We are the branches. Our Father is the gardener, pruning us through His Word so that we bear much fruit. That fruit is love—love for one another expressed supremely through proclaiming God's Word and growing disciples.

The work of the vine—word ministry, disciple-making, genuine relationships rooted in Scripture—is infinitely more important than maintaining trellis. As we remain in Jesus and His Word remains in us, we will bear fruit that lasts, fruit that shows the world we are His disciples.
