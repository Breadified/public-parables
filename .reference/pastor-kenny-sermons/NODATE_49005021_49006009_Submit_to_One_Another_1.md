# Ephesians 5:21-6:9

**Title:** Submit to One Another  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Word Nobody Wants to Hear

There's a word that makes us deeply uncomfortable in our culture: *submit*. Not long ago, the Anglican Church faced significant backlash when they proposed keeping this word in their marriage vows. The media erupted—how could the church possibly maintain such language in the modern age?

And yet, here we are at a wedding where the bride has chosen to promise submission to her groom. It sounds almost incomprehensible, especially when you consider the husband in question! But Vinci and Hanson have asked me to explain why this ancient biblical instruction still makes sense today.

The word "submit" carries terrible baggage in our minds. We think of slavery, exploitation, domestic abuse—people forced into submission against their will. No Christian endorses such things. Yet here is an intelligent, educated woman, choosing to place herself under another's authority for life. Why?

## Three Reasons for Submission

### 1. Because God Commands It

> **Ephesians 5:22-24**
> Wives, submit to your husbands as to the Lord. For the husband is the head of the wife as Christ is the head of the church, his body, of which he is the Savior. Now as the church submits to Christ, so also wives should submit to their husbands in everything.

This is not a light commitment. The comparison is profound: just as the church follows Christ unconditionally, wives are called to submit to their husbands unconditionally. Christians don't pick and choose what to obey when it comes to the Lord Jesus. That same standard applies to wives in relation to their husbands.

### 2. To Allow the Husband to Lead

Notice something striking in this passage: wives receive one sentence of instruction, while husbands receive an entire paragraph. And that paragraph tells husbands to love their wives as Christ loved the church.

What does that mean?

> **Ephesians 5:25-27**
> Husbands, love your wives, just as Christ loved the church and gave himself up for her to make her holy, cleansing her by the washing with water through the word, and to present her to himself as a radiant church, without stain or wrinkle or any other blemish, but holy and blameless.

Christ protected the church. Christ fought for the church. Christ sacrificed His life on the cross to save the church. Christ conquered death and continues to protect the church today. This is what love means—laying down one's life for another.

> **Ephesians 5:28-30**
> In this same way, husbands ought to love their wives as their own bodies. He who loves his wife loves himself. After all, no one ever hated his own body, but he feeds and cares for it, just as Christ does the church—for we are members of his body.

A husband's call is to die for his wife—physically, emotionally, spiritually, and materially. It might mean being the first to stand between danger and his family. It might mean taking the initiative in reconciliation, being the first to say sorry, putting aside his own ambitions for the sake of his family's wellbeing. It might mean guiding his family spiritually, ensuring his wife grows in holiness. It might mean providing materially, taking humble work if necessary rather than letting pride prevent him from caring for those he loves.

The buck stops with him. This is what it means to lead in love.

But here's the critical insight: *Hanson cannot do this without Vinci's support*. He cannot lead without her submission.

Think of any time you've led a team, managed a project, or captained a sports team. It's incredibly difficult when your team won't follow. Yes, you discuss disagreements, but someone must make the final call. That someone bears the responsibility—if the decision is wrong, they face the consequences. But when team members refuse to follow, constantly complain, or drag their feet, they undermine the leader's ability to lead. You cannot be a leader if you're merely following your followers.

By agreeing to submit, Vinci makes room for Hanson to lead. Notice the beautiful symmetry: the bridegroom promises first to love sacrificially. *Then*, knowing his commitment, the bride agrees to follow. The man initiates; she accepts. And so begins a beautiful dance.

### 3. For the Order of Things—When Irreconcilable Differences Arise

You might ask: Why can't we just mutually submit to each other? Why can't couples take turns deciding things, or vote, or involve parents?

Of course you can—and should—in most situations. Part of a husband's love is giving in for the sake of harmony. He lets his wife choose the restaurant because he loves her. She lets him borrow her jacket because she cares for him. There's beautiful give-and-take in marriage.

But this instruction from God was not created for situations where couples can find solutions and compromise. It's given for the time when irreconcilable differences emerge—when one person insists on one decision and the other insists on another, with no amount of discussion resolving it. For example: one wants to live in Brisbane, the other in Melbourne. Both have good reasons. By definition, the difference is irreconcilable.

What happens then? A vote in a democracy of two gets you nowhere. No consultation can break the deadlock.

Under current law, irreconcilable differences mean divorce. You split.

But what if you believe—as Hanson and Vinci just promised—that marriage is for life? What if marriage is something greater than our individual whims, stronger than our disagreements, worth fighting for?

Then you need submission.

Someone, ultimately, must give way. I have children who fight over who sleeps where. Rather than endlessly mediating, I decided in advance that the eldest gets to choose. It's arbitrary, perhaps, but it serves harmony and family unity.

God has done something similar. He's established the order in advance: the husband makes the final call, and the wife submits. He could have reversed it—wives lead, husbands submit—but then husbands would complain about inequality. Either way, someone gives in. God's design is not unjust.

God sees this wedding and sees two people who love each other deeply, who have committed to each other for life. And God says: *I will help you*. I will structure marriage so that when you face irreconcilable differences, you never have to divorce. I will preserve this love. I don't want to see you in Brisbane while your spouse is in Melbourne. I want to see you both together, in Sydney, with us.

## The Deeper Mystery

> **Ephesians 5:32**
> This is a profound mystery—but I am talking about Christ and the church.

If this vision of submission and leadership resonates with you, consider that God offers these same instructions and wisdom for understanding the entire world.

God is calling each of us to submit to Christ, our true husband. This is not submission to an authoritarian tyrant. This is submission to a God who promises to protect us, who loves us—who has already proven His love by dying for us. When we let Him lead, we live healthier, more fulfilled lives now and forever. That makes sense of the true state of the world.
