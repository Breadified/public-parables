# Zephaniah 3:6-20

**Title:** Judgment Day: Future  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew  
**Date:** May 2, 2010

## Main Point

For the people of God, judgment for some means salvation for others. This passage reveals how God's judgment on the nations becomes the occasion for the gathering and restoration of His people.

## 1. Gathering for Judgment (v. 8)

> "Therefore wait for me," declares the LORD, "for the day I will stand up to testify. I have decided to assemble the nations, to gather the kingdoms and to pour out my wrath on them—all my fierce anger. The whole world will be consumed by the fire of my jealous anger." (Zephaniah 3:8)

God uses the metaphor of gathering to describe judgment—like a police sting gathering evidence and witnesses. The nations will be assembled, the kingdoms gathered, for the purpose of pouring out His wrath. This is not happening immediately; God says, "Wait for me." This word came to exiled Judah, reminding them that though Jerusalem had been invaded and the people scattered into captivity, their oppressors would not endure forever. The Day of Judgment was coming, and with it would come restoration and return.

### The Call to Patient Waiting

This message applies profoundly to all who feel unjustly treated, who carry many wounds from enemies, who experience life as unfair. God's word to us is simple: **Wait for me. I am coming soon. I will right the wrongs of this world.**

We often feel the waiting is too long. The suffering seems endless, and patience can breed bitterness. There are no shortcuts in the Christian experience—only perseverance. We are called to be a trusting people of the God who loves us, leaving the timing of His justice in His hands.

The apostle Peter offers comfort for this struggle:

> "But do not forget this one thing, dear friends: With the Lord a day is like a thousand years, and a thousand years are like a day. The Lord is not slow in keeping his promise, as some understand slowness. He is patient with you, not wanting anyone to perish, but everyone to come to repentance." (2 Peter 3:8-9)

**Practical wisdom:** In the midst of suffering, actively look out for others. Suffering is relative—when we count our blessings and serve those around us, we gain perspective. This helps us avoid the trap of becoming bitter or envious. Though waiting is hard, there is something profoundly positive to look forward to.

## 2. Gathering for Salvation

Out of this judgment comes salvation. God's judgment on the nations becomes the means of salvation for both Gentiles and the Jewish remnant.

### a. The Gentiles (vv. 9-10, 20)

> "Then I will purify the lips of the peoples, that all of them may call on the name of the LORD and serve him shoulder to shoulder. From beyond the rivers of Cush (Ethiopia) my worshipers, my scattered people, will bring me offerings." (Zephaniah 3:9-10)

This passage echoes and reverses the curse of Babel. At Babel, humanity united in one language and common purpose, but their intention was to build a name for themselves and avoid scattering. God confused their speech and scattered them across the earth. Now, in God's future gathering, the tables turn:

- **Intention at Babel:** Build a city, make a name for themselves, avoid scattering  
- **Result:** Speech confused, building stopped, no name, scattered

**God's solution (v. 20):**

> "At that time I will gather you; at that time I will bring you home. I will give you name and honor and praise among all the peoples of the earth when I restore your fortunes before your very eyes." (Zephaniah 3:20)

In the future gathering, instead of making their own name, the peoples will call on the name of God. They will be united—"shoulder to shoulder"—but now in worship of the true God. The scattered ones from distant lands like Ethiopia will return, bringing offerings. This echoes the golden age of Solomon, when the Queen of Sheba traveled from distant lands to witness his wisdom and wealth, and she ultimately praised the God of Israel. In that future day, the glory of Jerusalem will be restored, and the nations will come not out of political curiosity, but in worship.

### b. The Remnant Jews (vv. 11-13, 19)

> "On that day you will not be put to shame for all the wrongs you have done to me, because I will remove from this city those who rejoice in their pride. Never again will you be haughty on my holy hill. But I will leave within you the meek and humble, who trust in the name of the LORD. The remnant of Israel will do no wrong; they will speak no lies, nor will deceit be found in their mouths. They will eat and lie down and no one will make them afraid." (Zephaniah 3:11-13)

Jerusalem will be restored, but not as before. God will remove those given to pride and haughtiness. Instead, He will preserve within the city a remnant—the meek, the humble, those who trust in His name. This remnant will be characterized by righteousness: they will speak no lies, no deceit will be found in their mouths (echoing the purified speech of the nations).

The image of eating and lying down without fear evokes peace and security—like sheep and shepherd, or the lion lying down with the lamb. For a people traumatized by exile, this promise of fearlessness is profoundly healing. Historically, this remnant consists of those carried into Babylon; through exile and suffering, they experience purification that transforms them.

As a result of this restoration, the burdens will be lifted:

> "The sorrows for the appointed feasts I will remove from you; they are a burden and a reproach to you." (Zephaniah 3:18)

The appointed feasts—likely the Feast of Tabernacles, with its language of gathering, offerings, and sacred assembly—will no longer be occasions of sorrow and reproach. They will become true celebrations again.

> "At that time I will deal with all who oppressed you; I will rescue the lame and gather those who have been scattered. I will give them praise and honor in every land where they were put to shame." (Zephaniah 3:19)

## 3. What the Gathering Looks Like (vv. 14-18)

The nature of this future gathering is revealed in a passage structured around four parallel ideas:

> "Sing, O Daughter of Zion; shout aloud, O Israel! Be glad and rejoice with all your heart, O Daughter of Jerusalem! The LORD has taken away your punishment; he has turned back your enemy. The LORD, the King of Israel, is with you; never again will you fear any harm. On that day they will say to Jerusalem, 'Do not fear, O Zion; do not let your hands hang limp. The LORD your God is with you, he is mighty to save. He will take great delight in you, he will quiet you with his love, he will rejoice over you with singing.'" (Zephaniah 3:14-17)

**The pattern:**
1. **Rejoice** (vv. 14, 17c)—Singing and great gladness
2. **He has saved us** (vv. 15a, 17b)—Punishment taken away, enemy defeated
3. **He is in our midst** (vv. 15b, 17a)—The Lord is with us, mighty to save
4. **Never fear again** (vv. 15c-16)—Security and fearlessness

### A Community Without Fear

The gathering of God's people should be a place of no fear. Yet for many of us, large gatherings themselves produce fear—fear of getting lost, of being ignored, of encountering danger, of being judged or ridiculed. What would it look like if our Christian community began to exhibit the characteristics of that heavenly gathering even now?

It would be a place where we can speak to one another without fear, where we share our struggles and long sufferings without fear of judgment or ridicule. It would be a place of patient rebuke rather than condemnation, where gossip is absent and compassion is present. When Christ has forgiven us, we offer one another the same grace. We reconcile without anger, we speak without insults or harsh criticism.

This is not automatic. If you have experienced fear in our community, we ask for another chance. Let us all try harder to make this a safe and secure place to be Christians. For many of you, peers at school or non-Christian parents at home make your faith a lonely thing. This community exists so you have space to discuss Christian things, to wrestle with struggles, to find encouragement. Come a little earlier, stay a bit longer. Maximize these opportunities for connection and growth.

When we do this well, our gatherings become occasions for real rejoicing—a taste of that heavenly gathering where we can truly sing.

## The Ultimate Party

Two pictures frame this passage: the party at the beginning (a child's birthday celebration with food, singing, and joy) and the ultimate party at the end.

> "Blessed are those who are invited to the wedding supper of the Lamb!" (Revelation 19:9)

> "Then I looked and heard the voice of many angels, numbering thousands upon thousands, and ten thousand times ten thousand. They sang in a loud voice: 'Worthy is the Lamb, who was slain, to receive power and wealth and wisdom and strength and honor and glory and praise!'" (Revelation 5:11-12)

## Summary: The Theme of Judgment

Over these weeks we have explored judgment from four angles:
- **Judgment: How Near?** It is not far away but coming soon.
- **Judgment: Salvation** God saves His people through His judgment.
- **Judgment: Justice** God judges wickedness and cannot tolerate sin.
- **Judgment: Future** We look forward to the heavenly gathering—the ultimate gathering of God's redeemed people.
