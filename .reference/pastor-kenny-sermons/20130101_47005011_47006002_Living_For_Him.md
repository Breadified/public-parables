# 2 Corinthians 5:11-6:2

**Title:** Living For Him

**Date:** 27 January 2013

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Cost of Redemption

Jean Valjean stands before the court, having made a bargain long ago. His soul belongs to God—a debt paid when hope was gone and strength was needed for the journey. He steps forward with a single declaration: "I am Jean Valjean."

Why would he do this? Because when you belong to God, identity becomes clear. The cost demands response.

Charles T. Studd understood this principle. Born into a wealthy British family in 1862, he became an outstanding cricket player—one of England's finest. He played in the historic 1882 Ashes match against Australia. He had everything the world could offer. Then, in his third year at Cambridge, everything changed. He was converted at an evangelistic meeting and dedicated his life to Christ.

As one account describes: "The world was at his feet, as had been forecast and expected, when suddenly the whole picture changed. He announced he was giving away his personal fortune, leaving the world of sports, and becoming a missionary to China."

For nine years, Studd served with Hudson Taylor's mission. Later, burdened for Africa, he left for the continent in 1910 and established the Worldwide Evangelization Crusade in 1919.

Studd's motivation was strikingly simple: "If Jesus Christ be God and died for me, then no sacrifice that I make can be too great for Him."

This is the heart of the matter. Christ died, and therefore we are willing to sacrifice.

## All Have Died

Over recent weeks, we've considered what Jesus has done for us:

- Jesus came for you
- Jesus cares for you
- Importantly, Jesus has died for you

> 14 For Christ's love compels us, because we are convinced: that **one died for all,** and therefore **all died.**

God in the man Jesus died. He who was innocent and did not deserve to die took our place. This was an act of love. Don't ask God to prove His love by answering your prayers for an iPhone or a girlfriend. God nailed His commitment to you on the cross.

But consider those final words: "therefore all died." What does this mean?

> 15 And he died for all, that those who live should no longer live for themselves but **for him** who died for them and was raised again.

To die means to live not for ourselves but for Him who died for us and was raised. It is death to selfishness. It is death to autonomy. It is death to independence from God. For some, this death feels worse than any physical death—it interferes with their freedom.

Yet this expectation is not unreasonable. Christ has died for all. If He has died for all, then all are indebted to Him.

Consider the concept of debt: A friend does you a favor, and you feel obligated to give something back. We speak of paying our debt to society, owing something to our country, wanting to give something back.

We feel this most acutely with our parents. You are indebted to them. They have brought you up, reared you, provided for you. My own parents left everything to come to Australia for my sister's and my education. We arrived with just our suitcases. That final month in Malaysia—they sold everything: appliances, furniture, ceiling fans, the house. They left their extended families and everything they knew to come to an alien and often unfriendly country for our sake. My mother worked two jobs, desperate to pay off the mortgage. I am indebted to them, and you are indebted to yours. We live for them—to respect them, and when they grow old, to provide for them, making sure they have a room in our homes.

If we understand this debt to mere humans, our mothers and fathers, how much more should we understand our debt to Christ, Lord of all—to the Father in heaven who has given us life, breath, the universe itself, and now has redeemed us from slavery to sin? My parents have not yet died for me, but Jesus has died for all. We are eternally indebted to Him.

That is why all have died. People die for lesser things in life—for gorillas, to protest against governments, to protect whales, for country, for material wealth, even for thrills and experiences. God is not asking us to do trivial things, but to lay down our lives of selfishness, to lay down our lives of arrogance and autonomy—that mindset of always thinking I know best while God knows not. To no longer rebel against His word. It is for our good. If we can give our lives for trivial things, how much more ought we be willing to give them up for God?

Therefore, all died. You have died. Who am I? I have died to myself. No longer I that lives.

## A Different Way of Seeing

As a result, we view the world differently.

> 16 So from now on we **regard no one** from a worldly point of view. Though we once regarded Christ in this way, we do so no longer.

At one time, Paul would have regarded Jesus as a perverter of the law, a charlatan, a blasphemer. No beauty, no majesty. Look at the cross—cursed by God. Any language of judgment applied. The cross declares: cursed by God. Under judgment. Condemned as sinner and criminal. On that basis, Paul persecuted violently.

Then one fateful day, he met the risen Lord Jesus. Everything reevaluated completely.

Jesus is not merely another good man or religious teacher. If He were, His death would be nothing—just another dead person, perhaps leaving some advice but unable to help us now.

Jesus is not just a sacrificial nationalist—someone we remember once a year while carrying on our own lives the rest of the year.

Jesus is not just a fool, dying a foolish death.

Jesus is the Lord who died for all—effective death, able to save all.

This new view of Jesus extends to our view of everybody.

> 17 Therefore, if anyone is in Christ, the new creation has come: The old has gone, the new is here!

Knowing that Christ died for all transforms the way we view people. There was a time when we were impressed by externals—by qualifications, socioeconomic status, age. Instead, we now see anyone in Christ as a new creation. A cosmic change. Saved from death. Saved from futility.

The opposite is also true. Those who are not in Christ—the old has not gone. Death remains. They remain dead in their sins.

We like to label people. No longer Asian or not Asian. No longer selective school or comprehensive school. No longer jock or nerd. No longer parents, brothers, or sisters. For Paul, the only labels that matter are these: in Christ or not in Christ.

We should walk around with this perspective, seeing this as the main distinguishing factor—not out of judgmentalism or discrimination, but because we have been given a task.

## A Ministry of Reconciliation

> 18 All this is from God, who reconciled us to himself through Christ and **gave us the ministry of reconciliation**: 19 that God was **reconciling the world to himself** in Christ, **not counting people's sins against them**. And he has committed to us the message of reconciliation. 20 We are therefore Christ's ambassadors, as though God were making his appeal **through us**.

We have been given the message of reconciliation. We are Christ's ambassadors, as though God were making His appeal through us.

God is not going to appear and tell people the gospel. He did that already two thousand years ago. To expect Him to do that again for each person at their convenience is to expect God to be our slave, doing our bidding. No—God is Lord, and He has chosen a specific time and place to tell us His plans to judge the world and how we can avoid judgment through Jesus. When He appears again, it will be to implement that judgment.

Telling the gospel is not God's responsibility. It is ours. Our job now is to ensure everyone hears it. We cannot do this when we get to heaven.

> 6:1-2 As God's co-workers we urge you not to receive God's grace in vain. For he says, "In the time of my favor I heard you, and in the day of salvation I helped you." I tell you, now is the time of God's favor, now is the day of salvation.

Many of you have tried to do this all summer—explaining to a friend about Jesus, having conversations on Wednesday nights, inviting people to church. Well done! Some of us in this room might be products of these invitations. Thank you for coming. I am sure the person who invited you is genuinely glad you would give church a chance, give the Bible a chance, give me a chance to explain, and ultimately give God a chance to persuade you.

This is the message:

> v.20 We implore *you* on Christ's behalf: **Be reconciled to God**. 21 God made him who had no sin to be sin for us, so that in him we might become the righteousness of God.

Be reconciled. This is a passive act. Not go get reconciled through your own works. Not go do lots of good works to earn it. But accept it. Jesus has died for all. He has died for you. Believe that. Trust that. Live by that.

God made Him who had no sin—no penalty of sin—to be sin, to take the penalty for us. So that we might become the righteousness of God in Him.

You might have been here over recent weeks. We have discussed Christianity thoroughly, haven't we? Lots of conversations here and there. You have raised objections; different people have tried to answer them as best they could. These questions were answered—not perfectly, perhaps, but reasonably. You understand the logic and rationale. And this has happened not just for one question but question after question, discussion after discussion.

Keep asking those questions. But I think for some of you, you have reasonable confidence that the next question you ask will be answered. There is no barrier to faith. Sure, always questions, always doubts. But substantially, things are solid. It is time to be reconciled to God. No one is forcing you. Wouldn't it be great to sort things out with your Creator?

Accept that He has died for you. In His name, find reconciliation.

## A Life Transformed

His death is so major. It demands a response.

We have spent time talking about "for you"—but we cannot leave it there. "For you" must become "for Him." This year, the focus shifts from Froyo Church to For Him Church.

Like Jean Valjean, who received mercy when he deserved judgment, we too have received grace beyond measure. A priest gave him candlesticks when the law demanded his head. Christ gave us His life when we deserved death. That mercy demands everything we have in return.
