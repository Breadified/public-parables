# Jonah 1:1-16

**Title:** The Fear of God
**Date:** 7 December 2014
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Mission Incomplete

We all have jobs—daily responsibilities that sometimes include tasks we genuinely fear. An accountant fears billing. A mother dreads changing nappies. A restaurant worker fears the grease trap. A finance person dreads month-end accounting. We all have job descriptions, yet sometimes we cannot bring ourselves to complete them.

Jonah had a job too.

## Fear of the Mission (1:1-3)

> The word of the LORD came to Jonah son of Amittai: "Go to the great city of Nineveh and preach against it, because its wickedness has come up before me." But Jonah ran away from the Lord and headed for Tarshish. He went down to Joppa, where he found a ship bound for that port. After paying the fare, he went aboard and sailed for Tarshish to flee from the Lord.

Jonah was a prophet—we know this from 2 Kings 14:25, which mentions "Jonah son of Amittai, the prophet from Gath Hepher."

The word that came to him was from Yahweh—God's personal name (translated "Lord" because ancient translators followed the Jewish tradition of not speaking God's name aloud, out of reverence).

God commanded Jonah to go to Nineveh and preach. The word can mean both "against" and "to"—but given Nineveh's wickedness, the force is clear.

**The Wicked City**

Nineveh was the capital of Assyria, and Assyria was known throughout the ancient world as the cruelest civilization ever recorded. The Assyrians had already sent raiding parties into the Northern Kingdom where Jonah lived. Their methods were barbaric beyond modern comprehension—they dismembered victims, impaled people on poles through various body parts, left them to die slowly. When they conquered a city, they would impale survivors on stakes before the town gates and pile the skulls of enemies into pillars at Nineveh's entrance. They treated mass torture as a basic tactic, hosting public torture sessions with 25-100 victims at a time to utterly demoralize their enemies. The entire history of Nineveh is a reign of violence, terror, and bloodshed—what the prophet Nahum would later call a "city of bloodshed."

To understand Jonah's fear, imagine being told today to go to Mosul with ISIS in control and preach there. What would happen? ISIS has a habit of beheading people and broadcasting it to the world.

Yet Jonah does something extraordinary for a prophet. Most prophets complain a little, then obey God. But Jonah ran away—he "fled from the Lord."

As Christians, we've been given a similar task. Jesus commanded us:

> Go and make disciples of all nations, baptizing them in the name of the Father and of the Son and of the Holy Spirit, and teaching them to obey everything I have commanded you. (Matthew 28:19-20)

We are to speak to others about God's goodness, the mercy bought by His Son, and the assurance His Spirit brings. And it terrifies us.

Our fears take many forms:
- Fear of losing our popularity
- Fear of being associated with strange elements of Christianity
- Fear it will cost us time and money—we'd rather rest at lunch alone than talk to coworkers
- Fear of inconvenience—we'd rather mow our lawn than visit neighbors
- Pressure to balance it with everything else
- Fear the effort will be wasted, that nothing will happen

Yet perhaps understanding who Yahweh really is could dispel this fear. And learn what Jonah learns by running away.

## Fear of the Elements (1:4-5)

Jonah rebels and runs, but Yahweh catches up quickly.

> Then the Lord sent a great wind on the sea, and such a violent storm arose that the ship threatened to break up. All the sailors were afraid and each cried out to his own god.

The Lord hurled a great wind—the text emphasizes this divine action with a pause. Stop and consider what God is doing.

The sailors are terrified, and in their fear they turn to their only source of security: their gods. In the ancient world, there were gods for everything—gods of the wind, gods of the water, gods of the boat. It's like being in Bali, where there's a god for every corner, every roundabout, every taxi. Shrines are everywhere, and offerings litter the pavements to appease evil spirits.

> And they threw the cargo into the sea to lighten the ship.

The sailors threw cargo overboard—trying to appease the sea. They offered what they could: banana leaves, joss sticks, fruit, paper money, rice—anything to appease the sea gods and gain peace.

**The Contrast: Pagan Fear vs. True Fear**

Pagans fear countless gods and powers. They believe they can control them through offerings and superstitions:
- Feng shui: orienting your room a certain way brings safety and success
- Number superstitions: buying a house with number 8 and avoiding number 4
- Avoiding ladders and black cats

Some religions even commit terrible acts in this fear—organ harvesting, child sacrifice, widow burning.

Yet these rituals don't work. The storm continues because these gods aren't real. It's like leaving cookies for Santa. People may be sincere, but sincere belief in false things doesn't help. Deep down, people know it too—taxi drivers offering melted chocolate bars in the hot sun, Buddhists offering old oranges. If they truly believed these offerings worked, they'd burn real money instead of fake money.

But there is a real God, and it takes far more than trinkets to appease Him. A few thousand years later, one man would perfectly appease God: Jesus. The perfect man appeased God's wrath by taking it upon Himself. God's justice was satisfied.

**Jonah's Serenity in the Storm**

Yet notice the stunning contrast. While the sailors frantically call to their gods and throw cargo overboard:

> But Jonah had gone below deck, where he lay down and fell into a deep sleep.

Why wasn't Jonah afraid? Why didn't he join the panicking sailors?

## The Fear of God (1:6-16)

The sailors are desperate to know who caused this disaster.

> The captain went to him and said, "How can you sleep? Get up and call on your god! Maybe he will take notice of us so that we will not perish." Then the sailors said to each other, "Come, let us cast lots to find out who is responsible for this calamity." They cast lots and the lot fell on Jonah.

They cast lots—using chance to determine outcomes. In the Bible, casting lots is not generally used for divination or predicting the future (Leviticus 19:26, Deuteronomy 18:10 forbid this). However, it's acceptable when the decision doesn't really matter—which of two acceptable options to choose. For example, the two goats in Leviticus 16, the division of land, or choosing between two apostles who equally fit the requirement. In those cases, either outcome is acceptable, so you can use a lot as a tiebreaker. Casting lots to determine your university course, however, would be unwise. But God controls every lot cast (Proverbs 16:33), and providentially, the lot pointed to Jonah.

The sailors interrogate him with five rapid questions:

> "Tell us, who is responsible for making all this trouble for us? What kind of work do you do? Where do you come from? What is your country? From what people are you?"

Jonah's answer is telling:

> He answered, "I am a Hebrew and I worship the Lord, the God of heaven, who made the sea and the dry land."

**I am a Hebrew.** This identifies his nationality and people.

**I worship the Lord.** The word literally means "I fear Yahweh." This is why Jonah is calm. His fear of Yahweh means he fears nothing else.

**He is the God of heaven, who made the sea and dry land.** This is a merism—naming two extremes to encompass everything in between. Heaven and earth, sea and land—it's all His.

God controls the heavens: the source of winds, stars, moon, and sun. He controls the sea: vast, unexplored, unpredictable. He controls the dry land: everything Jonah tried to run from. In answer to their question—who's responsible?—it's Yahweh, the sovereign God over all the elements.

**The Sailors' Response**

The sailors' reaction is immediate and terrified:

> This terrified them and they asked, "What have you done?" (They knew he was running away from the Lord, because he had already told them so.)

How could you run from the God of heaven, sea, and land? The sea had terrified them, so what must they think of the One greater than the sea?

The sea grows even more violent. The sailors desperately ask:

> "What should we do to you to make the sea calm down for us?"

Jonah's response is striking:

> "Pick me up and throw me into the sea," he replied, "and it will become calm. I know that it is my fault that this great storm has come upon you."

This sounds noble—Jonah accepting responsibility. Yet the sailors cannot comprehend this solution:

> Instead, the men did their best to row back to land. But they could not, for the sea grew even wilder than before.

Only forced to act by the mounting disaster:

> Then they cried out to the Lord, "Please, Lord, do not let us die for taking this man's life. Do not hold us accountable for killing an innocent man, for you, Lord, have done as you pleased."

Notice they call to Yahweh now, not their many gods. They've learned whom to fear.

> Then they took Jonah and threw him overboard, and the raging sea grew calm. At this the men greatly feared the Lord, and they offered a sacrifice to the Lord and made vows to him.

The sailors went from fearing many useless gods to fearing the one true God. And the order matters: they offered sacrifice after God showed mercy. This is a true response to God's power and grace.

## The Sharp Contrast

Here is the striking irony: The pagan sailors who initially feared their little gods have come to a proper fear of Yahweh. They are now trembling in reverence, offering sacrifices and making vows.

But Jonah? Jonah, who claimed to fear Yahweh, ran from Him. He wasn't afraid of the storm—or even death. He had no compassion for the drowning sailors. He simply went to sleep. He showed no fear of Yahweh despite claiming to fear Him.

The sailors cried out for help. Jonah didn't pray.
The sailors showed compassion, trying hard not to throw him overboard. Jonah had compassion for no one.
The sailors came to fear the true God. Jonah feared nothing—and that was his problem.

And notice: when the sailors asked Jonah about his work—"What do you do?"—Jonah didn't answer. He didn't want them to discover his job description. A prophet who won't speak is a prophet failing his calling.

## The Question for Us

Who really fears Yahweh? Jonah claims to, but his actions tell a different story. What is wrong with Jonah that he won't fulfill his job?

And what about us? Do we fear Yahweh? Why don't we fulfill the Great Commission?

Some aspects of a job description are essential to the work itself. You cannot be a dentist and hate bad breath. You cannot be a doctor who faints at the sight of blood. You cannot be a mother and hate babies.

If we truly fear God—if we have encountered the God of heaven, sea, and land—then fear of others becomes small by comparison. Our God is bigger than our embarrassments. Our God is bigger than persecution. Words and sticks and stones cannot hurt what truly matters.

The pagans are like those sailors throwing things overboard—they have nothing but wishful thinking. But we know the true God. We have been commissioned by the sovereign Lord of all creation.

So the real question isn't whether we can do it. The real question is: Do we fear God enough to obey Him?
