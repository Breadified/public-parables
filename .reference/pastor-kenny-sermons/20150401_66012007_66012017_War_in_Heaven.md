# Revelation 12:7-17

**Title:** War in Heaven
**Date:** 24 April 2015
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## A War in Heaven

The book of Revelation presents strange and mysterious imagery—so strange that it has inspired everything from Narnia to Harry Potter. Yet beneath these symbolic descriptions lies a straightforward message about Jesus' death and resurrection. Revelation communicates eternal truths through spiritual symbolism rather than literal description.

John's vision opens with a cosmic conflict:

> 7 Then war broke out in heaven. Michael and his angels fought against the dragon, and the dragon and his angels fought back.

The combatants here are spiritual in nature. The dragon represents Satan—the devil, the ancient serpent who leads the whole world astray. Michael, whose name means "like God" (Micah = like Yahweh), represents Jesus. While some view Michael as merely one angelic leader, passages like Daniel 10:21 and 12:1 describe him as a great prince—titles that apply to Jesus alone. Importantly, Jesus is not an angel but rather the non-angelic leader of an angelic army.

### A Complete Victory

Who wins this heavenly battle?

> 8 But he was not strong enough, and they lost their place in heaven. 9 The great dragon was hurled down—that ancient serpent called the devil, or Satan, who leads the whole world astray. He was hurled to the earth, and his angels with him.

Jesus wins completely. Satan is utterly defeated. The repeated word "hurled" emphasizes his defeat—like pottery smashed on concrete. The symbolic descriptions of heaven and earth matter less than the theological reality: Jesus is all-conquering over evil.

The heavenly proclamation declares:

> 10 Then I heard a loud voice in heaven say: "Now have come the salvation and the power and the kingdom of our God, and the authority of his Messiah. For the accuser of our brothers and sisters, who accuses them before our God day and night, has been hurled down. 11 They triumphed over him by the blood of the Lamb and by the word of their testimony; they did not love their lives so much as to shrink from death. 12 Therefore rejoice, you heavens and you who dwell in them!"

Jesus sits at the right hand of God with all authority and power. His kingdom encompasses the world. During his earthly ministry, he defeated every manifestation of evil:

- He resisted Satan's temptations in the wilderness and won
- He cast out demons and won
- He conquered the powers of sin and won

**This victory is our victory.** Because Jesus triumphed over Satan, we triumph over him. The devil's accusations against us—and he is right that we are sinful, that we have done things we regret and feel ashamed of—are answered by Jesus, who takes those crushing accusations upon himself. We are freed through his sacrifice.

Like the Anzac soldiers who fought for our freedom, Jesus fought for ours. We benefit from his victory without having fought the battle ourselves. Their victory was our freedom. His victory is our salvation.

---

## War on Earth

Yet if the victory is complete, why does the war continue?

> 12 But woe to the earth and the sea, because the devil has gone down to you! He is filled with fury, because he knows that his time is short. 13 When the dragon saw that he had been hurled to the earth, he pursued the woman who had given birth to the male child.

The woman represents God's people. The war in heaven is finished, but its effects persist on earth as a spiritual conflict. We see the evidence in our world:

- Christians are killed for their faith in Africa and the Middle East
- In the West, Christians face increasing social and legal pressure
- Personally, we experience attacks from circumstances, society, friends, and family
- Many feel alone and isolated

It appears Satan is winning. But this appearance is deceptive.

Consider the parallel of Japanese soldiers discovered decades after World War II ended—old men still hiding in jungles with rusted guns, unaware or unwilling to accept that the war was over. They could still inflict harm with their ancient weapons, but they could not win. They had no bullets, no reinforcements, no victory.

Similarly, Satan is allowed to roam for a brief time longer. He is permitted to persecute and mislead people. He can harm us, but he cannot defeat us or separate us from God.

> 14 The woman was given the two wings of a great eagle, so that she might fly to the place prepared for her in the wilderness, where she would be taken care of for a time, times and half a time, out of the serpent's reach.

God's people are protected even as they are attacked. The phrase "time, times and half a time" refers to 3.5 out of seven—we are more than halfway through. The worst is behind us. We are on the downhill stretch.

> 17 Then the dragon was enraged at the woman and went off to wage war against the rest of her offspring—those who keep God's commands and hold fast their testimony about Jesus.

### The Call to Persevere

The war on earth will not last much longer:

- His time is short (v. 11)
- We are past the halfway point (v. 14)
- Victory belongs to those who hold fast (v. 17)

Therefore, remain faithful in this earthly conflict. The war in heaven is won. Jesus has achieved complete victory. What we experience now is only the last desperate rage of a defeated enemy. Continue to persevere in Christian faith.

> 11 They triumphed over him by the blood of the Lamb and by the word of their testimony; they did not love their lives so much as to shrink from death.

This is the pattern of victory: through the blood of Christ and through our testimony—our willingness to stand for Jesus even when it costs us everything.

The Anzac soldiers did not want to fight. They would have preferred to stay home, play cricket, marry, and raise families. But they identified themselves with righteousness, gave up their material desires, set aside their fears, and fought. Young men, barely older than school students today, faced waves of artillery fire on the beaches of Gallipoli and stood fast. They fought for our freedom.

Jesus has purchased our freedom through his blood. He asks only that we preserve his legacy—that we not abandon the freedom he purchased, that we use it for his glory rather than self-indulgence, and that we be prepared to stand firm if our faith is tested.

Some of you will face trials this term. Do not shrink back from persecution or suffering. Be brave. This term, your school may feel like a storm—relentless and overwhelming. Yet after every storm comes sunshine. Some may even struggle to attend church because of rain; what hope do they have of standing firm when real persecution comes? If you cannot endure minor inconveniences for your faith, how will you endure genuine hardship?

The reality is stark: persecution of Christians is intensifying globally. In many countries, believers go to church and read their Bibles knowing these acts might cost them their lives. They convert to Christ knowing their conversion may cost them everything. These men and women are ready to surrender their very existence for what they believe.

What about us? In the West, church attendance declines not because we face martyrdom but because we cannot be bothered to drive a few minutes to worship for an hour on Sunday. We abandon our faith if it threatens our comfort or reputations. We construct a Christianity that permits whatever sins suit our modern lifestyle. Meanwhile, our brothers and sisters across the globe are massacred for believing in the Word we dismiss.

In America, Christians cower. They fear to declare their faith on social media lest they receive mean comments. Middle Eastern Christians will give their lives to keep their souls while we forfeit our souls to protect our reputations and social media reach.

> "I know your deeds, that you are neither cold nor hot. I wish you were either one or the other! So, because you are lukewarm—neither hot nor cold—I am about to spit you out of my mouth."

We are comfortable and insulated, but spiritually we are wretched, pitiful, poor, blind, and naked. We must ask ourselves: Would we still profess Christ if it meant danger? If faith cost us everything, would we still claim it?

**This is what we can do for the martyrs.** We can be inspired by their courage and sacrifice. We can make sacrifices of our own. We can take stands. We can take risks. We can live boldly for Christ rather than lukewarmly for ourselves.

Pick up your cross and follow him up that hill. Be willing to endure for the sake of the gospel. This is what we can do.
