## Introduction

Last week we celebrated water baptisms—Marcus, Veronica, and Sam made public declarations of faith through this ancient practice. Today we're exploring something different and considerably more complex: the Baptism of the Spirit.

I'll be honest—I'm still working through the loose ends of this teaching myself. Your church background will significantly shape how you receive this. Those with deep denominational roots may find it confusing, while those new to church might grasp it more easily. That's because familiar terms can blind us with assumptions we don't realize we're carrying. We'll need to deconstruct those assumptions before we can build something clearer.

---

## What Peter Declared (Acts 2:14-21)

Peter stood before the gathered crowd in Jerusalem and addressed their confusion about the disciples speaking in foreign languages. "These men are not drunk," he insisted. "It's only nine in the morning!"

Instead, Peter explained they were witnessing the fulfillment of Joel's prophecy:

> "In the last days, God says, I will pour out my Spirit on all people. Your sons and daughters will prophesy, your young men will see visions, your old men will dream dreams. Even on my servants, both men and women, I will pour out my Spirit in those days, and they will prophesy."

**Key Observations:**

- **The terminology matters.** Scripture uses several related words—"poured out," "given," "baptized," "come upon," "fall upon." These all describe the dramatic arrival of God's Spirit.
  
- **The scope is universal.** Notice the contrasts: sons and daughters, young and old, servants both male and female. These are "merisms"—paired opposites showing the complete spectrum of humanity. The Spirit will be poured out on everyone.

- **Cosmic signs accompany this event.** Peter references wonders in heaven and signs on earth—blood, fire, billows of smoke, the sun turning to darkness, the moon to blood. These aren't poetic exaggerations but genuine cosmological events marking the arrival of the last days.

- **Salvation is available to all.** "Everyone who calls on the name of the Lord will be saved."

---

## The Three Meanings of Spirit Baptism

### 1. It Marks the End of Judgment

To understand this, we must look back at what Joel said before the pouring out of the Spirit. Before Joel 2:28 (which Peter quotes), there's Joel's prophecy of judgment—locusts, armies, darkness covering the land. The day of the LORD is coming, and it is dreadful.

But notice what comes next: a call to repentance. 

> "Even now," declares the LORD, "return to me with all your heart, with fasting and weeping and mourning." He is gracious and compassionate, slow to anger and abounding in love, and he relents from sending calamity.

After the judgment comes **restoration**. God will drive away the invading army. He will refill the famine. He will restore their pride. "Never again will my people be shamed."

**Peter's Proclamation:** When Peter quotes Joel, he is declaring that **NOW IS THE TIME** for the end of judgment and the beginning of restoration.

**Where is this judgment?** You might ask—I didn't see cosmological events or billows of smoke. The answer is profound: judgment has already occurred. It happened at the cross.

Notice the parallel in Acts 2:22:

> "Jesus of Nazareth was a man accredited by God to you by miracles, wonders and signs, which God did among you through him, as you yourselves know."

Jesus performed the wonders and signs. His life testified to God's presence.

**The darkness at His death:** When Jesus died, darkness covered the land. Luke records:

> "It was now about the sixth hour, and darkness came over the whole land until the ninth hour, for the sun stopped shining."

The sun turning to darkness, the moon to blood—this happened. A grand solar eclipse occurred at the moment Christ died. The blood red of dried blood, appearing black as darkness. The cosmological sign fulfilled.

**The verdict delivered:** On the cross, judgment fell on Christ, not on us. He breathed His last. He died bearing the condemnation we deserve.

**The liberating truth:** The outpouring of the Spirit means judgment is finished. We can go free. Restoration is now available to all who repent.

The cost of this reversal was not ours to pay—it was Christ's. My little daughter learned this recently. At the Kids Club, a helper prepares craft projects, cutting materials into shapes, using them generously. When she made her craft and a few days later broke it, she wept. But the next day, a brand new identical craft appeared on her bed. It cost her nothing, but it cost Grandma something—her time, her resources, her willingness to absorb the loss.

That's salvation. It seems effortless for us on the receiving end. All we do is repent. But Christ was on the giving end. He has already paid.

### 2. It Is a Sign That the Gospel Is for All

The Baptism of the Spirit is not what happens at conversion. Scripture records fifteen conversion accounts in Acts—3,000 at Pentecost, the Samaritan woman, Lydia, the Philippian jailer, and many others. Not a single conversion is described as a Baptism of the Spirit.

Instead, the Spirit-baptism appears only four times in Acts, each marking a pivotal moment in salvation history:

**Acts 2: The Jewish Church Established**
The disciples receive the Holy Spirit. They speak in foreign languages—tongues. The Spirit is poured out.

**Acts 8: The Gospel Reaches Samaritans**
The Samaritans (half-caste, considered unclean by Jewish standards) believe and are baptized in water. Then Peter and John pray for them to receive the Holy Spirit. When the apostles lay hands on them, the Spirit comes.

**Acts 10: The Gospel Reaches Gentiles**
Peter speaks to Cornelius, a Roman—an outsider, forbidden from the Jewish temple. While Peter is still speaking, the Holy Spirit falls on all who hear. They speak in tongues. The circumcised Jewish believers are astonished. Peter declares, "Can anyone keep these people from being baptized with water? They have received the Holy Spirit just as we have."

**Acts 19: The Baptist's Prediction Fulfilled**
Paul finds disciples of John the Baptist at Ephesus who have only received John's baptism. Paul explains: John baptized with water and told people to believe in the one coming after him—Jesus. When they're baptized in Jesus's name and Paul lays hands on them, the Holy Spirit comes upon them, and they speak in tongues and prophesy.

**What Can We Learn?**

- **These are not conversion experiences.** In every instance, people were already believers when the Spirit fell on them. The Samaritans believed before the Spirit came. The Gentiles believed before the Spirit fell. The disciples of John the Baptist were believers before they received the Spirit.

- **There is no necessary order.** Water baptism and Spirit-baptism don't follow a set sequence. In Acts 8, baptism in Jesus's name preceded the Spirit's coming. In Acts 10, the Spirit came before water baptism. In Acts 19, John's baptism came first, then baptism in Jesus's name, then the Spirit. The order varies deliberately—showing this is not a formula but a sovereign divine action.

- **This is not about a "second blessing" or a subsequent stage of Christian life.** The terminology (baptized in the Spirit) appears only twice in Acts (1:5 and 11:16)—a unique phrase reserved for these specific outpourings. It's too particular to generalize into everyday Christian experience.

**What's Actually Happening?**

These Spirit-baptisms fulfill the promise of Acts 1:8:

> "You will receive power when the Holy Spirit comes on you; and you will be my witnesses in Jerusalem, and in all Judea and Samaria, and to the ends of the earth."

Each outpouring demonstrates God's approval at a crucial boundary-crossing moment in the gospel's expansion. Think of them as "booster rockets"—dramatic demonstrations of divine power launching the gospel to the next frontier.

- Jerusalem: *boom*—the church launches
- Judea and Samaria: *boom*—the gospel breaks racial barriers
- The ends of the earth (Gentiles): *boom*—the gospel explodes beyond Israel

The fascinating twist: Joel 3:2 prophesied that God would gather the nations **to destroy them**. But the merciful God has extended His grace to the nations too. He will NOT destroy them but **save them**, just as He does with Israel. This is the great mystery now revealed.

---

### 3. It Signals the Regathering Has Begun

God's mission to regather His scattered people has commenced. Joel spoke of a time when the fortunes of Judah and Jerusalem would be restored—when scattered people would be reunited. The Baptism of the Spirit announces that this gathering has begun.

**The Symbol of Tongues:** The foreign languages are significant. They represent the breaking down of barriers, the gospel going to peoples separated by language and culture. Yet all understand—each in their own tongue—the mighty works of God.

**The Pattern of Approval:** Each stage of this regathering is marked by signs and wonders, signifying that the gospel is breaking through to a new people.

Once the gospel reaches the Gentiles—the ends of the earth—the pattern of dramatic Spirit-baptisms ceases. Why? Because there are no more barriers to cross. You've reached the uttermost parts. The booster rocket has accomplished its purpose. The gospel is now free to expand organically to all nations without needing fresh supernatural attestations of God's approval.

**Application for Us:**

If you are not yet a Christian, understand this: God is ready to regather you. Give Him a chance. Repent and believe.

If you are a Christian, recognize that you are part of this ongoing mission. We don't experience Spirit-baptisms—those were unique moments in salvation history. But we participate in proclaiming what they announced: a time of restoration and reversal, that judgment has been taken away by the blood of Christ, that now is the time to return to God.

## God's Indwelling Presence Is Now Available

Here we must make an important distinction: the Baptism of the Spirit (the dramatic outpouring) is different from the indwelling presence of the Spirit at conversion.

When believers come to Christ, they receive the indwelling Spirit apart from the spectacular baptism. This normal experience is described with a different word—believers are "filled with the Spirit." This is distinct from the Spirit-baptisms Luke records.

The outpourings are emphatic, symbolic demonstrations that the eschatological gift of the Spirit has arrived. They're big movements, divine approval stamped visibly on history.

## The Pentecost Connection

In the Old Testament, Israel celebrated three key festivals, each requiring pilgrimage to Jerusalem:

- **Passover:** Commemorating deliverance from Egypt, the Lamb's blood marking the doorposts
- **Feast of Tabernacles:** Remembering God's wilderness provision, celebrated with booths and offerings
- **Feast of Weeks (Pentecost):** Originally celebrating the wheat harvest; later understood as commemorating the giving of the Law at Mount Sinai

**The Old Law Given:**
When God gave the Law at Mount Sinai, the scene was dramatic—loud trumpet sounds, fire consuming the mountain, God's presence manifest in power.

**The New Law Written:**
Now, at Pentecost, God gives a new covenant. No longer written on stone tablets but on human hearts. Hebrews describes it:

> "This is the covenant I will make with them after that time, says the Lord. I will put my laws in their hearts, and I will write them on their minds. Then he adds: Their sins and lawless acts I will remember no more."

The Law going out from Zion—as prophesied through Isaiah and Micah—begins not with stone and temple sacrifice, but with the Spirit writing God's will on transformed hearts.

**Why This Matters:**
Only the gospel can generate the motivation necessary for true obedience. We're tempted toward legalism, thinking rules will shape us. But the law never works that way. The gospel—the news that we're loved, forgiven, restored—this alone produces the kind of internal transformation that genuine obedience requires.

## The Gospel Proclaimed (Acts 2:23-36)

Peter anchors his proclamation in Jesus's death and resurrection:

**Death:** Jesus was handed over by God's predetermined plan, yet crucified by wicked men's hands.

**Resurrection:** David prophesied this: "You will not abandon me to the grave, nor will you let your Holy One see decay." Jesus rose from the dead. He conquered what had conquered humanity since Eden—death itself.

**Exaltation:** Jesus ascended to the right hand of God and received from the Father the promised Holy Spirit, pouring it out on believers.

**The Verdict:** "Therefore let all Israel be assured of this: God has made this Jesus, whom you crucified, both Lord and Christ."

---

## The Response (Acts 2:37-47)

When the people heard this proclamation, they were "cut to the heart." Their consciences were pierced. They asked the apostles, "Brothers, what shall we do?"

Peter's answer was direct: "Repent and be baptized, every one of you, in the name of Jesus Christ for the forgiveness of your sins. And you will receive the gift of the Holy Spirit."

**The Promise:** "The promise is for you and your children and for all who are far off—for all whom the Lord our God will call."

Approximately 3,000 were baptized that day and added to the church.

**The Life That Followed:**
The young church devoted themselves to apostolic teaching, fellowship, breaking bread together, and prayer. Awe filled everyone as the apostles performed many wonders and signs. All believers lived communally, selling possessions and distributing to anyone in need. Daily they met in the temple and homes, eating together with glad and sincere hearts, praising God and enjoying the favor of all people. And the Lord added to their number daily those being saved.

---

## Closing Thoughts

The signs and wonders authenticated the apostles' message—validating their claim to speak God's very words. A prophet's claim must be tested. God's word through human mouths demands credibility. The miracles provided it.

We live in a different season now. The booster rockets have done their work. The gospel has reached the uttermost parts. Our task is proclamation—calling people to repentance, inviting them into the restoration and reversal that Christ's death and resurrection accomplished.

The Spirit-baptism announced: judgment is finished, restoration has begun, and God's grace now flows to all people. 

This is our message. Live it. Proclaim it. Invite others into it.
