# Philippians 3:12-16

**Title:** Are Christians Perfect?  
**Date:** 19 October 2004  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Question: Are Christians Perfect?

Christians are a new creation. We are resurrected people. God's Spirit—who does no wrong—dwells within us. All of God's blessings are upon us. We even preach perfection as we encourage others toward godliness.

The world certainly expects us to be perfect. The persistent accusations of Christian hypocrisy suggest that people believe we ought to be flawless—otherwise, why would they accuse us of falling short? And doesn't Scripture itself call us to perfection? "Be perfect, because the Father is perfect."

Yet Paul gives us a surprising answer: **We have not obtained perfection.**

## Paul's Honest Confession

Look at Philippians 3:12: "Not that I have already obtained this or am already perfect." Paul explicitly denies having achieved perfection. And in verse 13: "Brothers, I do not consider that I have made it my own."

This is striking. Paul had everything—perfection according to the law. He was "Hebrew of Hebrews, Pharisee of Pharisees," legally perfect (3:5-6). Yet he gave it all up for something greater. In verse 8, he declares: "I have suffered the loss of all things and count them as rubbish, in order that I may gain Christ."

Paul exchanged one perfection for another: the perfection of the resurrection, the perfection of Christ. But just when we think we've found the answer, Paul qualifies himself. He doesn't yet have this perfection either. **Not yet.**

### Illustration: The Inheritance

Imagine discovering you're in a wealthy uncle's will for a unit in a nice neighborhood. You've been commuting forever, so you go knock on his door. "Uncle, can I have the unit now?" He replies, "Not yet—I'm not dead yet. You have it, but not yet."

That's where we are. We have the promise of perfection, but we have not yet obtained it.

## The Competing View: Sinless Perfection Now

Some today teach that we can achieve sinless perfection in this lifetime—if only we try hard enough, pray hard enough, or have enough faith. This belief extends to material perfection as well: the perfect car, the perfect body, the perfect spouse and children, the perfect career and home.

We might think we don't hold this view, but I suspect we differ only in degree. We have our own standards of perfection. We expect a nice career in ministry with job satisfaction, a lovely home in a good part of town, a wonderful wife, healthy children who excel in school and become successful professionals. We might neglect our families occasionally, but only minor lapses—nothing serious. Life should be perfect.

To this expectation, Paul says: **Not yet.**

## The Call: Strain Toward Righteousness

Paul doesn't call us to complacency. Instead, he says in verse 12: "I press on to make it my own." The Greek word *diōkō*—"I press on"—is the same word Paul used to describe himself persecuting the church (3:6). He pursued Christians with zealous tenacity. Now that same fierce drive is redirected toward righteousness.

Verse 13 captures this urgently: "But one thing I do: forgetting what lies behind and straining forward to what lies ahead."

This perfection is not material striving. It's not careerism or material ambition. It's the perfection of holiness—to be holy because the Father is holy. It's **righteousness**.

Paul clarifies in verses 8-9: "I have suffered the loss of all things and count them as rubbish, in order that I may gain Christ—and be found in him, not having a righteousness of my own that comes from the law, but that which comes through faith in Christ, the righteousness from God that depends on faith."

This righteousness is not something we earn. It's something we must **own**.

### Illustration: Owning the Event

When you organize a church event—say, an evangelistic outreach—you do all the work: secure resources, find a great speaker, organize music. Then you ask your youth group to bring friends. You'll get two types of responses.

Some won't engage. It wasn't their idea; they don't understand it; why bother? It's your event, your project—someone else's problem. They don't own it.

Others get excited. They grasp why it matters. They're grateful for your work and want to make it their own. They invite friends. They look for ways to help beyond what's required. They find new areas to contribute that you never imagined. People who own something do far more than the bare minimum.

### Application: Own Your Righteousness

Righteousness comes from God, so we can take it for granted and become complacent. Instead, we're called to own it. To own righteousness means:

- Living justly and doing the right thing
- Living for others
- Showing mercy and exhibiting compassion
- Living lives that please our Father who is righteous

We can do this because it is within our grasp. **Let us own it.**

Yet we must be honest: just because we ought to live righteously doesn't mean we can live perfectly. We will stumble.

## The Christian Race: Not Yet Perfect

Paul uses the metaphor of a race. We might imagine sleek, muscular athletes running effortlessly. But the reality is different. The Christian runner is more like a character in a side-show wearing a bulky, ridiculous costume—overheated, ungainly, straining and gasping toward the finish line.

Ultimately, we will stumble in our pursuit of righteousness. The race is full of hurdles. But Christ has already won the race for us.

Look again at verse 12: "Not that I have already obtained this or am already perfect, but I press on to make it my own, because Christ Jesus has made me his own."

Christians are not perfect. Yet we belong to Christ. In his mercy, he seized us and rescued us from judgment before time even began. Paul hasn't made perfection his own, yet he presses on because Christ Jesus has made him his own.

Christ ran the race on our behalf. The divine one took on flesh, ran to Calvary, and finished victoriously. Therefore, let us keep trusting in his forgiveness and own more of his righteousness day by day through our obedience.

## The Call to the Finish Line

> "Press on toward the goal for the prize of the upward call of God in Christ Jesus. Let those of us who are mature think this way, and if in anything you think otherwise, God will reveal that also to you. Only let us hold true to what we have attained." (3:14-16)
