## Introduction: The "Women and Children First" Principle

Last week's message stirred healthy discussion, reminding us that Scripture often makes us uncomfortable—either because it challenges our culture, or because I've failed to communicate it clearly. Let me ask for your patience and welcome your feedback.

A comment last Sunday raised an important point: the word "slavery" carries too much historical baggage. Perhaps "servitude" better captures the meaning—a normal economic model that can be either righteous or abused. By coincidence, the film *Sound of Freedom* released on July 4th, highlighting modern trafficking as the slavery of our time. This matters deeply.

The phrase "women and children first" became famous through the Titanic's sinking, when Captain ordered evacuations in that order. Today's feminists object: *How dare you suggest women and children are inferior?* 

Yet we'll see that Scripture portrays submission not as inferiority, but as something beautiful—and counterculture to our age.

## Part 1: Submission is Beautiful (1 Peter 3:1-6)

> **1 Peter 3:1** Wives, in the same way live good lives, be submissive to your husbands so that, if any of them do not believe the word, they may be won over without words by the behavior of their wives.

The word "submit" immediately triggers resistance. We imagine Stepford wives—perfectly dressed, perfectly baked, perfectly robotic. Yet this teaching seems absurd in an era where more women attend university than men, where girls' schools outrank boys' schools academically, where we celebrate "Girl Power."

Worse still, submission conjures images of domestic abuse and patriarchal exploitation.

But strangely, the Bible presents submission as *desirable*. Even beautiful.

### The Beauty of Inner Character (3:2-6)

> **3:2-4** When they see the purity and reverence of your lives. Your beauty should not come from outward adornment, such as braided hair and the wearing of gold jewelry and fine clothes. Instead, it should be that of your inner self, the unfading beauty of a gentle and quiet spirit, which is of great worth in God's sight.

> **3:5** For this is the way the holy women of the past who put their hope in God used to make themselves beautiful. They were submissive to their own husbands.

Peter redirects our entire understanding of beauty. Not external—not braided hair, gold jewelry, or fine clothing. These fade. Instead, true beauty is **inner beauty**: a pure life free of resentment and complaint, a reverent life that takes God's Word seriously, a gentle spirit that doesn't nag or manipulate, a quiet spirit that accepts her husband's leadership without trying to usurp it.

### Why We've Lost This Vision

We've lost this understanding of true beauty because of Hollywood, social media, magazines, and television. Young women now aspire to be Instagram influencers. We measure worth by physical appearance and follower counts.

But here's what I wonder: *this collapse of true beauty coincides with the collapse of family and tribe.* In the past, we lived near extended family—aunts, grandparents, great-aunts. We watched people age. We saw them young, middle-aged, old, and dying. We witnessed their character. We naturally learned that **beauty is fleeting, but goodness endures.** A good person wasn't the one who was beautiful; the good person was the one who served others.

Today, our relatives live overseas. Migration has scattered families. Instead, we surround ourselves with same-age friends, all dressed from the same shops, all pursuing the same aesthetic. We reinforce this skewed beauty standard through media that shows only the youngest, skinniest people wearing the same clothes.

**Take advantage of church.** Talk to older people. Learn from women who've lived long lives. Witness real beauty—the kind that doesn't fade.

**Submission is beautiful** because it springs from a heart transformed by God's Word.

### Sarah: The Example (3:6)

> **3:6** Like Sarah, who obeyed Abraham and called him her master.

"Master" is the same word used for Jesus's lordship (1 Peter 3:12, 15). Ephesians 5:22 says: *"Wives, submit to your husbands as to the Lord."*

Sarah was no pushover. She laughed at an angel from God. She took charge and expelled Hagar when she threatened her family. She remained beautiful even in old age—so beautiful the Pharaoh wanted her as his wife. She was not inferior.

**Yet the great Sarah—symbol of Christian freedom in Galatians—submitted her freedom to Abraham her husband.** She called him master. Not because she was weak or because she had no choice, but as a *deliberate choice of her will*.

### Application: Unqualified Submission

> **3:6** You are her daughters if you do what is right and do not give way to fear.

*"Do what is right"* = submission. But understand: **this is not submission to every man.** A wife submits to *her* husband over whatever the issue is—whether big or small, whether she agrees or not.

Here's what submission is *not*:
- Not because he is superior (you know he isn't)
- Not because he's always correct
- Not just for small decisions, but for large ones too
- Not because he's doing the godly thing
- Not because you're in love with him or because he's a good husband

**Submission is unqualified.** No conditions attached. Like obeying Jesus—you don't qualify your obedience to the Lord based on whether you feel like it. The word "master" shows the radical nature of this: *as to the Lord*.

Submission has **no qualifications** because God has ordained the institution of marriage—like He ordains government, employers, and other structures—for our good and our children's welfare.

> **3:6** "Do not give way to fear"

Submission is scary. It requires faith—in your husband, but more importantly, in God. 

> The holy women of the past "put their hope in God."

You trust God even though your husband might have no idea what he's doing. You trust that God knows. Even if your husband is ungodly.

**Submission is risky.** It demands we surrender control and place our confidence in God's sovereignty.

### The Domestic Abuse Question

This is our greatest concern today. Is this an excuse for abuse?

First, understand: **domestic violence is not gendered.** Men and women are equally capable of harm—just in different ways. Men may punch; women may manipulate someone into suicide. Both are abusive. Many married couples hurt each other in unhealthy ways.

I notice a blindside where women lecture men about abuse as though men alone are the problem. But pause for a moment. **Who raised these men?** The majority of children are nurtured by their mothers. If we want better men, we must examine how we're raising our sons.

This doesn't excuse domestic abuse. The Bible has already addressed that. We live under government. Domestic abuse can lead to arrest and jail. If you're abused: **seek help. Contact police. Withdraw from fellowship temporarily. Seek counseling.**

The problem is not the presence of authority—it's the *abuse* of authority. We don't eliminate police because some abuse power; we demand they exercise authority responsibly. Similarly, we don't eliminate marital authority; we call husbands to exercise it in a godly manner.

**The Bible has considered the scenario of an ungodly husband:**

> **1 Peter 3:1** "if any of them do not believe the word, they may be won over without words by the behavior of their wives"

Whether he's ungodly, an unbeliever, or immature in faith—**still submit.** Like other human institutions, we submit to the office, not the person. It's not about merit. This prevents us from masking our own sin. You submit to your boss because he's the boss, not because he's better. Likewise, 1 Peter 2 talks about submission to harsh masters—not just pleasant ones.

In extreme cases: seek help. The husband's authority has limits, just like every citizen is under the government.

### For Those Dating

**If you're dating, ask yourself: Can I submit to this man's authority?** If he's selfish or a jerk, why bother? If he's a non-Christian, how can you trust he'll lead you as Ephesians 5:27 describes—"present her to himself as a radiant church, without stain or wrinkle or any other blemish, but holy and blameless"?

**Girls, you're the one who picks.** There are more boys interested in girls than the reverse. Choose wisely. Look for the sort of person described in verse 7.

## Part 2: Protection and Respect (1 Peter 3:7)

> **3:7** Husbands, in the same way be considerate as you live with your wives, and treat them with respect as the weaker partner and as heirs with you of the gracious gift of life, so that nothing will hinder your prayers.

### The Weaker Partner

"Weaker" (not "weak") refers to physical reality. Men are 15-20% larger on average; we're 40-50% weaker in upper body strength, 30-40% weaker in lower body.

This reality becomes evident in motherhood. Pregnancy depletes calcium; bones become brittle. Breastfeeding accelerates this. Hormones that create joint flexibility also cause mood changes. Pregnancy leaves permanent stretch marks, scars, back pain. Women are *not quite the same afterwards*—unless they have armies of personal trainers and cosmetic surgeons.

Peter chose "weaker" deliberately—we're all weak (we get sick, age, die), but there is a difference.

### The Call to Protection

Throughout history, chivalry emerged from this reality. Crusaders swore "to defend to the uttermost the weak, the orphan, the widow and the oppressed; women should receive especial care."

**The husband's role is to protect.** This means:
- No domestic abuse
- Account for the inequality between you
- Be protective of her vulnerability

Colossians 3:19 commands: *"Husbands, love your wives and do not be harsh with them."*

### Equal in Worth, Different in Role

> **3:7** Treat them with respect, as heirs with you of the gracious gift of life

She is your **equal in worth**—a joint heir of grace. Not inferior. Never inferior.

> "Nothing will hinder your prayers"

The Greek word *ekkopto* means "cut off; to sever." When you mistreat your wife, your prayers are hindered. Your relationship with God is damaged. This is serious.

### What Husbands Should Look For

Not:
- Abs
- A certain status

But:
- A worker who provides
- A godly woman
- Someone willing to die for you
- A self-centered person becoming other-centered

Ephesians 5:25 says: *"Husbands, love your wives, just as Christ loved the church and gave himself up for her."*

### Application for Husbands

**Never leave.** Whether out of boredom, ambition, or feeling inadequate—they are never better off without you. To abandon the battle is cowardice.

**Don't act the hero when you're just doing your job.** You're fulfilling your responsibility to care for, nourish, love, and provide. The buck stops with you. You divide daily tasks, but the ultimate responsibility is yours.

**Be an all-rounder.** Don't just provide materially. Be physically healthy, emotionally present, spiritually engaged. Ensure they receive high-quality inputs in life. TV is a poor substitute.

**Authority and responsibility go together.** Nothing is more frustrating than responsibility without authority. You're assigned a task but given no power. You're police but stripped of arrest power. You're father but denied authority.

**You are meant to present your wife as a spotless bride to Christ (Ephesians 5:27).** You're called to love her as Christ loves the church. You're responsible for raising your children in the Lord. **These are unqualified responsibilities**—not "unless you have a career," not "if she's worth it," not "unless something better comes along."

Therefore, if you want full responsibility, **you need full submission.** Mutual submission is lazy thinking—it assumes everything works out perfectly. But things don't work out perfectly. Peter writes because marriages are broken. Someone needs to submit, or you have a democracy of two, endlessly debating every decision. That's unworkable.

**Practically, submission makes marriage work.** It's like dancing—one person leads, one follows. Not equally, not simultaneously. But beautifully, when done right.

## Conclusion: "Women and Children First"

The Birkenhead sank in 1852, 60 years before Titanic. When the ship tore open on rocks, Lieutenant-Colonel Alexander Seton ordered crew to prioritize women and children. **Every woman and child survived.** The men went down with the ship—including the captain and crew.

This wasn't a social norm. Men had to be reminded. It sprang from Christian history—from the chivalry code. Sieges evacuated women, children, and elderly. Even today, "Air Force One" never carries both the President and Vice President together; one survives.

**The remarkable thing: men died for strangers—women they didn't know, children not their own.** This is submission in crisis.

These commands aren't for peacetime smalltalk. They're for when marriages are on the rocks, when there is hate, when the ship is sinking. **Peter writes for crisis moments, not ideal moments.**

**"Won over without words"** means your character winsovers your words. When submission seems risky, when you're afraid of being hurt or taken advantage of, when you fear losing control—remember the holy women of old. **They put their hope in God.** That's the victory.

### What Submission Actually Means

Submission isn't a set of rules:
- Not "be a doormat"
- Not "never speak your mind"
- Not even just "if you disagree, he decides"

**Submission is an attitude that affects everything: your thoughts, feelings, words, and actions every moment of every day.**

It means rejecting critical, bitter, resentful thoughts about your husband. It means honoring his preferences in small things. It means speaking kindly to him and about him. It means responding with "yes" first, then "but what if..." It means consulting him on finances and schedules. It means graciously receiving his authority. It means influencing him through your prayers, godliness, and reverence—not through nagging, manipulation, or revenge.

Most importantly, it means accepting his care. Letting him lead. Working as a team.

### True Beauty, True Strength

True beauty is submission flowing from a heart transformed by God's Word—a pure and reverent life, a gentle and quiet spirit. This is how the holy women of the past made themselves beautiful.

True strength is a man taking responsibility for his wife's care, protecting the weaker partner, treating her as an equal heir of grace, dying to himself.

**These are the persons worth seeking. These are the marriages that last.**
