# 1 Peter 3:8-18

**Title:** Alien Confidence
**Date:** 11 February 2007
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Problem: Fear Stops Us from Doing Good

Fear is a powerful force that paralyzes us from action. On the TV show Fear Factor, contestants face increasingly terrifying challenges—walking on broken glass, holding breath underwater, ingesting live insects—all for a fifty-thousand-dollar prize. These fears are sensible; they protect us from genuine danger.

But fear doesn't only stop us from doing stupid things. It also stops us from doing *right* things.

### Real Fears That Keep Us from Helping

**Fear of ridicule.** When I was twelve, riding the school bus, I noticed the driver had missed a boy's stop. Worried he'd miss school, I rushed to tell the driver. He stopped reluctantly; I called the boy to get on. He wasn't interested—he was skipping school. Back on the bus, the driver was angry with me, and the other kids mocked me. I'd tried to do good and received nothing but ridicule in return.

**Fear of being hurt.** In July 2011, a man known in his community as "Brother John" was shot in the head while delivering dinner to homeless people with his family. His pregnant wife was grazed by a bullet; their three-year-old daughter was shot in the arm. He died the next day. Who wants to help when this is the cost?

**Fear of being sued.** In 2008, California allowed a paralyzed woman to sue the friend who pulled her from a crashed car, claiming the rescue caused her injury. The rescuer was trying to save her life, fearing the car would explode.

**Fear born from indifference.** In Guangzhou, China, surveillance captured a horrifying scene: a two-year-old girl was run over by a van and left bleeding on the street. For seven minutes, a dozen people walked or drove past her. She was hit by a second truck. Motorcycles swerved around her. No one stopped. Eventually a woman helped her, but the girl died at the hospital. One observer noted that people didn't help because they didn't want the trouble. In China, high-profile lawsuits have ended with Good Samaritans ordered to pay hefty fines to people they tried to help.

These stories make us afraid to do what is right. The world teaches us: protect yourself first.

## God's Call: Do Not Fear

Peter addresses this head-on in 1 Peter 3:14:

> But even if you should suffer for what is right, you are blessed. "Do not fear what they fear; do not be frightened."

This command is radical. The fear of suffering for doing good belongs to those without hope, those with no Father watching over them. But Christians are different. We don't belong to this world, and we don't belong to its fears.

### What Does "Doing Good" Actually Mean?

Before we explore why we shouldn't fear, Peter tells us what he means by doing good. In verses 8-9, he paints a picture of radical community:

> Finally, all of you, live in harmony with one another; be sympathetic, love as brothers, be compassionate and humble. Do not repay evil with evil or insult with insult, but with blessing, because to this you were called so that you may inherit a blessing.

**Living in harmony** means looking out for your neighbor's best interest. This even extends to those outside your faith community—the pagans, as Peter calls them. In chapter 2, he reminds us: "Live such good lives among the pagans that, though they accuse you of doing wrong, they may see your good deeds and glorify God."

**Not repaying evil with evil** is countercultural. The world's way is an eye for an eye, or at minimum, cold indifference toward those who've wronged us. But Peter demands something harder: when someone slanders you, you don't slander back. When someone steals from you, you don't steal back. Instead, you bless them.

The culture knows this principle—shows like *Revenge* and stories like *The Count of Monte Cristo* celebrate the sweet satisfaction of payback. But Peter forbids it. You are to love those who don't deserve it. You are to do good to your enemies.

### The Cost of Doing Good

There's always a cost. Maybe it's the fear of missing out (what Singaporeans call *kiasu*—the fear of being a loser). Maybe it's the sacrifice of your time, your reputation, or your comfort. Maybe it's the pain of being misunderstood or punished for your kindness.

The temptation is to stop. But Peter gives us three powerful reasons why we must continue.

## Three Reasons to Keep Doing Good Despite Suffering

### 1. The Eyes of God Are Upon the Righteous (3:10-12)

> For the eyes of the Lord are on the righteous and his ears are attentive to their prayer, but the face of the Lord is against those who do evil.

God is watching you. But don't misunderstand this as a cosmic surveillance state. This isn't a Big Brother with a big stick, waiting to catch you stumbling so He can punish you.

Yes, the second half of the verse is true: God's face is against those who do evil. But you, as a Christian, are now called righteous—a saint, not a sinner (though you still struggle with sin). God's eyes upon you mean He is *watching out for you*, like a mother keeping an eye on her baby. His oversight is not to punish but to protect and to help. Even when the outcome looks bad in the moment, He will swiftly take care of you.

And notice: not only His eyes, but also His ears. He sees and hears you. He is not distant. He is not indifferent.

### Application: You're Not Alone

Sometimes I try to do good, but my good is imperfect and flawed. People misunderstand my intentions and think I've harmed them. I get punished. It's discouraging. I wonder why I should keep trying.

But imagine if someone who saw everything—someone who knew my heart and my intentions—came to you and said: "I know what's going on. I know you were trying your best to do good. I'm looking out for you." Wouldn't you be even more enthusiastic to do good?

The hardest thing about doing good is doing it quietly, with no one knowing, with no acknowledgment, sometimes with outright punishment. It's exhausting. But God says: *I know*.

When you don't gossip—when people have said terrible things about you, and not only can you not gossip back but you must still look out for their best interest—there's a cost. It costs you marks. It costs you satisfaction. But God is watching. His eyes are on you. His ears are attentive to your prayers. When you feel discouraged, call out. Your Father sees and knows all.

### 2. Suffering Creates a Unique Evangelistic Opportunity (3:15-17)

> But in your hearts set apart Christ as Lord. Always be prepared to give an answer to everyone who asks you to give the reason for the hope that you have. But do this with gentleness and respect, keeping a clear conscience, so that those who speak maliciously against your good behavior in Christ may be ashamed of their slander.

The second reason not to fear is this: your suffering for righteousness is actually a disguised opportunity.

When you continue to do good—when you do good even when unrewarded, even when persecuted—people eventually notice. And when they do, you'll have a unique chance to share why you live this way. You'll be able to tell them about your Father in heaven who hears you and watches over you. And here's the thing: *they will believe you*, because you've lived it. You've embodied this belief. You're different from other Christians who talk more than they act. You actually do good with no expectation that people on earth will reward you or respect you. You do what's right because of God, not because of convenience.

### Application: Living Out Your Faith

Imagine a friend comes to you wanting to gossip about your mutual enemy. You gently refuse: "That's not right. I shouldn't gossip about a person, even my enemy." You respond with sympathy, love, compassion, and humility.

Guess who your friend will trust the next time she needs someone to confide in? Someone who won't gossip. You've just become her safe person. And the next time she has a serious question about life, a deep struggle, a spiritual hunger—you'll be the one she turns to. That's your unique opportunity.

Or imagine you're a non-Christian's child. You tell your parents you're a Christian, that you believe in Jesus, that they should too. They might respond: "You? You're going to teach me how to live? I taught you how to use the toilet!" But then they watch. They see that you've become the obedient child, the one who keeps her word, the one who's responsible. You do good for the family with no prospect of reward. You wash dishes without being told. You look out for others. You make peace in the family. You sacrificially love your siblings. You're humble. You bring harmony to the home. You're sympathetic and a good listening ear.

A unique opportunity will come. And when it does, don't switch modes. Stay gentle and respectful. Don't become an arrogant know-it-all. That would be hypocrisy.

I hope when that opportunity arrives, you'll be prepared to speak. Have something ready—perhaps a verse like John 3:16. Have your testimony about your relationship with Jesus ready. Or be able to share what 1 Peter 3 has meant to you. Your friend will hear it, and by God's grace, will repent and change, just as you did.

If you haven't started doing good, start now. Don't fear. And if you have, begin preparing your answer.

### 3. Jesus' Example: Suffering That Brought Redemption (3:18-22)

> For Christ died for sins once for all, the righteous for the unrighteous, to bring you to God. He was put to death in the body but made alive by the Spirit.

The third and final reason not to fear doing good is Jesus Himself—not only the way He calls us to live, but His example of how suffering for good produces unimaginable fruit.

Jesus lived out everything Peter commands: He lived in harmony. He was sympathetic. He loved others as brothers. He showed compassion. He was humble. He didn't trade insult for insult. He went silently to His death, surrendering His rights, His body, His potential revenge, His reward and recognition—all for the sake of doing good.

What was He repaid with? Ridicule, insults, betrayal, disrespect, torture, death.

Yet He trusted His Father. He sacrificially loved the unrighteous. He gave everything.

And what happened? God saw. He exalted Jesus. Jesus' suffering became the turning point of human history. He broke the cycle of hate, violence, and revenge. His death is His answer to all our fears. This is how He is authorized to say: "Do not fear."

One act, once for all, to show love and bring you to God. Nothing was missed. Nobody was left out.

## The Call to Courage

As you go about your week—in school, at work, in relationships—remember this: You serve a God whose eyes are upon you. You have opportunities ahead to share your faith through your life. And you follow a Savior who already paid the ultimate price for righteousness.

Do not fear to suffer for what is right. Your Father is watching. Your witness matters. And the victory is already won.
