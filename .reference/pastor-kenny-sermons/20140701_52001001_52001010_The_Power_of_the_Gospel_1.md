# 1 Thessalonians 1:1-10
**Title:** The Power of the Gospel
**Date:** 6 July 2014
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## What Is Real Power?

When we think of power, we often imagine impressive displays: large gatherings, influential speakers, chart-topping albums, successful careers, wealth, and health. We see Christians who seem to move through the world with ease—they have the right words, influential positions, functional families, and material success. And then we look at our own lives and wonder: where is the power in our faith? Christianity seems to make no difference. We feel weak and unable to impact the world around us.

But Paul's letter to the Thessalonians reveals something surprising about what true power actually is.

## A Powerful City (1:1-5)

Thessalonica was indeed a city of power and prestige. Its very name meant "Victory of the Thessalians." It served as capital of one of the four Roman districts of Macedonia, and during Jesus's time, it became capital for all the Greek provinces. The Romans built a spacious harbour there due to its commercial importance. Even today, it remains Greece's second-largest city with its largest university.

When Paul, Silas, and Timothy visited this powerful city, something remarkable happened. As Paul writes:

> Our gospel came to you not simply with words, but also with power, with the Holy Spirit and with deep conviction. (1:5)

The gospel came with real power—but not the kind of power the Thessalonians expected.

## The Unexpected Nature of Gospel Power

### They Endured Suffering (1:6)

The Thessalonians received Paul's message gladly. They believed. But they did this despite severe suffering:

> You became imitators of us and of the Lord; in spite of severe suffering, you welcomed the message with the joy given by the Holy Spirit. (1:6)

The persecution was intense. In Acts 17, opposition became so dangerous that Paul had to be smuggled out of the city for his safety—imagine such peril that escape required covert action.

This is strange. We don't usually associate suffering with power. Suffering seems to indicate weakness and vulnerability. Yet Paul says they *have* received the gospel with power *through* their suffering.

**Why is this powerful?** Because it proves they genuinely believed. No one willingly suffers for something they know is false. The Thessalonians didn't merely confess the gospel with their words—they put their bodies on the line. They invested their very lives into the message. This authentic faith gave Paul certainty that God had truly chosen them (1:4).

Paul describes this power in unexpected terms:

> Your work produced by faith, your labor prompted by love, and your endurance inspired by hope in our Lord Jesus Christ. (1:2-3)

The power of the gospel is expressed not in miracles or prosperity, but in *work, labor, and endurance*. They were patient in suffering. They stood up for Jesus even at the worst of times.

### Living as Imitators

The Thessalonians became imitators of Paul, who himself imitated Jesus (1:6). Paul knew his own life intimately—they had spent three weeks together. They watched as Paul lived out what he had learned from Jesus, who carried his cross and suffered on their behalf.

> For it has been granted to you on behalf of Christ not only to believe in him, but also to suffer for him. (Philippians 1:29)

This isn't a call to seek out suffering. As Peter writes:

> But how is it to your credit if you receive a beating for doing wrong and endure it? But if you suffer for doing good and you endure it, this is commendable before God. (1 Peter 2:20)

Yet the reality remains:

> Everyone who wants to live a godly life in Christ Jesus will be persecuted. (2 Timothy 3:12)

The more we set ourselves apart from the world through godliness, the more the world will hate us and what we stand for. This isn't because we go looking for suffering—it's a natural byproduct of faithful living.

### The Contrast with Power Theology

There's a strange phenomenon in modern Christianity: a gospel with no suffering. Sometimes called "power theology" or "the blessed life," it teaches that success is the path to generosity. The economic model says: *be as successful as you can, so that you can be generous to others. The more success, the more generous.*

But the humble Christian model is different: *no matter what circumstance, be generous to others. It is not determined by worldly success.*

Power theology says: "I will bless others when I have been blessed by the world."

The Christian gospel says: "I will bless others because I have been blessed by the gospel."

Are you being godly? Or are you avoiding godliness to escape the suffering that inevitably follows? Compare your life not to others, but to your own walk with Christ.

### They Spread the Gospel (1:8)

What happened next was remarkable:

> The Lord's message rang out from you not only in Macedonia and Achaia—your faith in God has become known everywhere. (1:8)

Like a great bell ringing out across the land, their faith became known. They told their neighbours and friends. They gathered at synagogues to proclaim to the Jews. And they did this despite opposition. Their faith spread from the local region (Macedonia and Achaia in Northern Greece) far beyond.

**Evangelism springs from faithfulness, not from material power.**

Some, especially from older generations and Asian cultures, believe the best way to evangelize is through social status. The thinking goes: when I become a lawyer, doctor, or successful businessman, others will see that Christianity is worthwhile. "Look at that Christian doctor—maybe I should be a Christian too."

But this misses something crucial. Christians are often the ones who *stop* being doctors for the sake of godliness, or become missionaries. Christian lawyers work in unglamorous small offices with lower pay so they have flexibility to serve their church. They are not impressive witnesses by worldly standards.

**But if you understand power as humility, gospel priorities, testimony of patient suffering, and perseverance—then this is truly powerful.**

The key is what you're promoting. Are you selling yourself—saying "Look at me, my life is perfect, come be a Christian"? Or are you pointing to the gospel—saying "I have problems, you have problems, let's be Christian together"?

Be transparent in your walk with unbelievers. Show them you're not perfect, you struggle, you are sinful, and you need forgiveness. *Yet God loves you!* That's the message. You have the solution they don't have.

**Does your faith ring out?** As people get to know you, do they recognize that you are a committed Christian—both in words and in deeds? If you're shy, start simply: attend Christian gatherings at school, university, or work. When people ask where you're going, explain that you have a Christian group and invite them to join. If persecution comes for your faith, remember—this is evidence that the gospel has truly come to you.

Imagine if your extended family talked about you not for academic achievements or professional success, but said: "That boy is so kind—he's a Christian." "That daughter became a Christian and now she's completely different." When the news of the gospel's impact on you spreads—beyond your neighbourhood, beyond your city, all the way to relatives in Hong Kong, Korea, or China—that is real power.

### They Served God (1:9-10)

Paul heard reports of the Thessalonians' transformation:

> They themselves report what kind of reception you gave us. They tell how you turned to God from idols to serve the living and true God, and to wait for his Son from heaven, whom he raised from the dead—Jesus, who rescues us from the coming wrath. (1:9-10)

The Thessalonians turned from the Greek gods—Nike, Zeus, and others—who represented power, strength, and wealth. These gods were mighty, yet flawed: they made mistakes, committed adultery, and waged wars among themselves. But they were powerful, and power was what people sought from them.

The Thessalonians turned from these *dead* idols to the *living* and *true* God. And notice: they didn't passively wait. They actively *served*.

This inverts worldly understanding of power. The pagan gods didn't serve—they were served. But Jesus taught something radically different:

> Those who are regarded as rulers of the Gentiles lord it over them. Not so with you. Whoever wants to become great among you must be your servant, and whoever wants to be first must be slave of all. For even the Son of Man did not come to be served, but to serve, and to give his life as a ransom for many. (Mark 10:42-45)

**Christians serve.** Not expecting anything in return. With humility. Seeking what is good for others, not for ourselves. Imitating the Son of Man.

We are in the same position as the Thessalonians. We face the same call to serve. Turn from the idols that distract us—whether entertainment, status, or comfort—and serve the true and living God.

## The Real Power of the Gospel

In conclusion, the power of the gospel does not lie in flashy displays or impressive numbers. Real power is not measured in export brands or crowd size.

**Real power lies in the changed life.**

This cannot be recorded in statistics, yet we can see it clearly in the lives of one another. Gospel power manifests as:
- Endurance through suffering
- Humbly telling others about the gospel such that the message rings out
- Going about serving others with humility and conviction

The gospel itself is powerful—powerful to change the hearts and minds of wicked men and women. The Thessalonians exemplified this transformation. They became a model to all believers in Macedonia and Achaia.

> You became a model to all the believers in Macedonia and Achaia. (1:7)

This is our calling: to emulate them as they emulated Paul as he emulated Jesus. In doing so, we display the true power of the gospel—not in comfort, success, or ease, but in transformed lives that bear witness to God's faithfulness through suffering, service, and steadfast faith.
