# Jonah 2:10-3:10

**Title:** Turning and Returning
**Date:** 7 August 2015
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Changing Minds

Last year, Sydney experienced a tragic siege when an Islamist gunman took people hostage at the Lindt Chocolate factory in the city. Two people died—the male café boss and a female lawyer. It was a terrifying scene that played out on television screens everywhere.

But one person avoided this tragedy entirely. David Johnson, an employee at the café, was rostered to work that fateful Monday. However, because he had worked so much overtime during the previous weekend, his boss changed his mind and gave him the day off. That simple decision to change their minds potentially saved his life.

Today we hear another story about changing our minds—one that has the power to save our lives.

## Jonah Repents? (2:10-3:4)

We've been reading through the book of Jonah for the past few weeks. Jonah, a prophet of God, was commanded to preach to Nineveh, but he ran away from that task. Now, things seem to be looking up as Jonah finally prays to God for mercy and receives an answer.

> And the Lord commanded the fish, and it vomited Jonah onto dry land.

God repeats his command to preach the message:

> Then the word of the Lord came to Jonah a second time: "Go to the great city of Nineveh and proclaim to it the message I give you." Jonah obeyed the word of the Lord and went to Nineveh. Now Nineveh was a very large city; it took three days to go through it.

**Understanding Nineveh's greatness.** Nineveh was literally "great to the gods." Ancient records testify to an impressive city situated on a high plateau near a natural crossing of the Tigris River. Archaeological evidence from around 645-643 B.C. shows the city surrounded by turreted walls topped with battlements. Upon entering, one would encounter wide streets with stone paving, gardens, parks, canals, palaces, temples, and houses. This was a breathtaking display of human grandeur—one of the wonders of the ancient world.

The phrase "three days to go through it" likely indicates the time it would take Jonah to complete his preaching mission: perhaps three days to speak with people, work his way up to the authorities, and deliver his message. Not three days to walk across the city, which would require an impossible hundred-kilometer journey.

> Jonah began by going a day's journey into the city, proclaiming, "Forty more days and Nineveh will be overthrown."

This sounds good on the surface, but there are problems when we dig deeper:

**First, the message lacks details.** In the original Hebrew, it is literally five words: "Forty Days Nineveh Be Overthrown." There is no message of hope, no mention of God, no introduction like "thus says the Lord," and no solution. How were the Ninevites supposed to repent? The message is minimalistic.

**Second, the timeframe is puzzling.** In Scripture, forty typically represents a long period—forty days of flood, forty days of fasting. An urgent message paired with a forty-day deadline seems contradictory. Forty days gives more time than necessary and a false sense of security.

**Third, there's an inconsistency with the structure.** We're told Jonah has three days of work ahead (verse 3), yet here he only does one day's work (verse 4). Given how the number three appears throughout the book, three days probably represented the actual time for repentance. The Septuagint translation supports this—it says "three days," not forty.

**Something is wrong here. We should be skeptical about Jonah.**

Looking back at his earlier statements, when he spoke to the sailors in 1:9, he evaded their direct questions about who was responsible for the storm. When he finally prayed to God, he merely asked for mercy without admitting his own wrongdoing. These are signs that Jonah has not truly repented.

When we examine his actions more carefully with this understanding, we see something troubling beneath the surface. In 3:1, why does God have to command him a second time? At the end of his prayer in chapter 2, Jonah speaks of "making sacrifices" and "vowing vows"—language that points in only one direction: a pilgrimage to the holy Temple in Jerusalem. Jonah's final words reveal his true intent: once back on dry land, he plans to go to Jerusalem to sacrifice animals and offer his thanks to God. He intends to go to Jerusalem, not Nineveh—which is precisely why God must tell him a second time!

The pattern is clear: **Jonah is the reluctant prophet.** He still doesn't want to do God's work. He is procrastinating and dragging his feet all the way.

**What does procrastination look like?** You have an assignment to finish. But first, you play five minutes of computer. After an hour passes, you walk to the fridge. You eat in front of the TV. You sharpen pencils. Your mother asks if anyone wants to go to the shops—you jump up and say yes, eager to help with anything except studying.

**Application: We procrastinate about telling others about Jesus.** We drag our feet as though it were the worst thing to do. A couple of weeks ago, we talked about fear stopping us—but sometimes procrastination stops us as well. We want to do it, but just not yet.

Perhaps you've thought of friends you'd really like to invite to ISCF, but that was last year. You made a New Year's resolution. You've been busy, and now it's August. You said you'd invite friends when a certain event came along. It came and went. Did you do it? You said you'd invite friends after trials. Well, trials are over—now is the time!

**There is never a good time. We will always be busy.** So just do it.

It takes time to tell other people about Jesus. We have to put in the metaphorical three days of work. Let's not give up when we've only done one. It takes time to cultivate relationships. It takes time to build genuine trust. Inviting our friends to church takes time—they need notice to prepare themselves. And if you find it hard to prioritize church, imagine how they feel when they're just as busy. (And if you are one of those friends here today, thank you for taking time out of your busyness to visit ISCF!)

Will you procrastinate, or will you do the work of an evangelist?

## Nineveh Repented (3:5-9)

But fortunately for the Ninevites, despite Jonah's lack of genuine repentance, God still uses what little Jonah does to bring about his will.

The people of Nineveh repent:

> The Ninevites believed God.

**Repentance means changing your mind.** It means going one way, then turning and going another. From not believing to believing. From not trusting to trusting. The Ninevites changed their minds and believed God.

> A fast was proclaimed, and all of them, from the greatest to the least, put on sackcloth.

**The totality of Nineveh's repentance.** Notice the phrase "from the greatest to the least." This emphasizes that everyone participated in the repentance. Even the greatest—the king himself:

> When Jonah's warning reached the king of Nineveh, he rose from his throne, took off his royal robes, covered himself with sackcloth and sat down in the dust.

The great king of this great city rose from his throne—his place of power and greatness—and recognized that God is king. He sat down in the dust. He took off his royal robes and covered himself with sackcloth—swapping the finest clothing for the worst, silk for burlap.

And even to the least:

> This is the proclamation he issued in Nineveh: "By the decree of the king and his nobles: Do not let people or animals, herds or flocks, taste anything; do not let them eat or drink. But let people and animals be covered with sackcloth. Let everyone call urgently on God. Let them give up their evil ways and their violence. Who knows? God may yet relent and with compassion turn from his fierce anger so that we will not perish."

Even the animals, herds and flocks were included—signifying the comprehensive nature of their repentance. While animals cannot truly repent, this detail shows that Nineveh's transformation touched everything in their society.

**In ancient times, fasting, wearing sackcloth, and putting dust on oneself were the outward signs of genuine repentance.** The Ninevites sincerely repented. Their apologies were not mere words but actions rooted in changed behavior.

Real repentance means a changed direction sustained over years of perseverance. It is not an emotional roller coaster but a steady hand in the process of ridding oneself of sin.

As a result, we see the possibility of an entire city changing—and this has happened throughout history. Whether in Nineveh, Rome, London, New England, or cities across Africa, kings and cities have moved from child sacrifice, debauchery, and pagan worship to follow the true and living God. And it can happen again.

Yet while an entire city repenting is amazing, it is not the biggest miracle here.

## God Repented (3:10)

The biggest miracle is that God repented:

> When God saw what they did and how they turned from their evil ways, he relented and did not bring on them the destruction he had threatened.

**Relent means the same as repent**—to turn from one action to another.

God did not have to do this. When a king meets a peasant on the road, who gives way? Yes, God is sovereign. There is no change in his mind in an ultimate sense—all is predestined, and God does all the work, not Jonah. Yet from our human perspective, God changes his mind through the actions of fallible men and women.

Our repentance would be useless if God did not relent. We must not take his forgiveness for granted. This is not an obvious outcome. As verse 9 says, "Who knows?" If we've done something wrong to another person and we say sorry, it depends on that person whether they forgive us. We cannot demand forgiveness.

**Yet God, who does not have to forgive, amazingly does forgive.** His very character is to love and to show mercy.

Perhaps you've done something really bad. Based on what you did, no one can forgive you. Based on what you did, you can't even forgive yourself. And yet God is willing to forgive you.

> **God promises to change his mind if you will but change your mind.**

He is able to do this—not because he is fickle or arbitrary, but because his Son, his one and only Son, came into the world two thousand years ago and died for your sins. That unforgivable sin that you committed, God has paid for it so you can now go free. So repent and turn from your evil ways.

> **Acts 3:18-20:** But this is how God fulfilled what he had foretold through all the prophets, saying that his Messiah would suffer. Repent, then, and turn to God, so that your sins may be wiped out, that times of refreshing may come from the Lord, and that he may send the Messiah, who has been appointed for you—even Jesus.

Like the manager in the Sydney siege who gave up his life so that others could go free, Jesus gave his life for you. But you must take the opportunity.

## Returning

In the siege, hostages noticed a locked sliding door with an emergency button on the side. On an unsupervised walk to the toilet, some of them pressed that button and ran for their lives. They knew they would die if they stayed—the gunman had threatened something would happen the next day, and he had a backpack that looked like explosives. They took the opportunity to escape, turned, and returned to safety.

Friends, we have all walked far away from God and gotten ourselves into a mess. But we can change our mind and return to God. He promises to change his mind and welcome us back.

If you haven't returned to God, perhaps today is a good time to do so. Life is fleeting. Make peace with your maker before it is too late.
