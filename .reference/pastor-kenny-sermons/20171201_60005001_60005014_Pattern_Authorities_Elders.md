**Title:** Pattern Authorities: Elders

## The Challenge of Leadership in Every Generation

We live in a time of stark demographic contrasts. The CIA Factbook tells us that Germany and Japan have a median age of 46—nations growing old, losing workers, facing the prospect of decline. Meanwhile, middle Africa has a median age of just 16. In our world today, there are 1.2 billion young people between the ages of 15 and 24, and this "youth bulge" often brings civil unrest rather than stability.

Peter's letter addresses both young and old, the youngest and the oldest among us. Over these past weeks, we've been learning about the right pattern of authorities—how we are called to honour and submit to those who lead over us. Three weeks ago we looked at government; two weeks ago, employers; last week, husbands and fathers. Today we turn to another order in society that God has established: the church itself, with its own hierarchy and offices described in the language of older and younger.

## 1. Leaders of the Flock (1 Peter 5:1-4)

> To the elders among you, I appeal as a fellow elder, a witness of Christ's sufferings and one who also will share in the glory to be revealed: Be shepherds of God's flock that is under your care, serving as overseers.

### The Shepherd Metaphor

In Scripture, the shepherd becomes the primary ancient Israelite metaphor for leadership. A shepherd is the leader, guide, protector, and feeder of sheep. When the prophet Ezekiel confronted the false shepherds of Israel, he revealed their fatal flaw:

> The shepherds did not search for my flock but cared for themselves rather than for my flock. Therefore I am against the shepherds and will hold them accountable for my flock.

God promised to rescue His flock from the mouths of those who exploited them. And in John 10, Jesus announced: "I am the good shepherd. The good shepherd lays down his life for the sheep." This is the model of all church leadership—leaders who willingly sacrifice themselves to protect those entrusted to their care.

Today we don't often use the word "shepherd" for church leaders, but we do use a related term: **pastor**, which comes from the Latin for pasture or pastoral land. The pastor is the leader who guides the flock to good pasture. While this includes some physical and pastoral care, the primary task of the church leader is theological. The church is the depository, sustainer, and school for truth and wisdom. The pastor is the teacher; the heretic is the thief who would steal that truth.

### Three Titles, One Calling

Scripture uses different titles for church leaders:

- **Shepherds**: The protectors and feeders of the flock
- **Overseers** (*episkopos* in Greek—from which we get "scope," and by transliteration, "bishop"): Those who have oversight, who see and care for the whole
- **Elders**: Literally the older people, who are typically wiser, more experienced, and learned

Having older people in leadership is the natural order. The wise pass wisdom to the next generation, and when they are frail or gone, the younger takes their place. This creates the natural succession of leadership.

Some churches make much of precise distinctions between these titles, but they all essentially describe leaders in the church. The specific structure varies by tradition—from concentrated authority in one person (Catholic and Anglican), to authority shared among a few (Presbyterian), to authority dispersed through the whole congregation (Baptist). The particular structure matters less than that we avoid anarchy.

### Three Requirements for Leaders

Rather than focusing on titles, Scripture emphasizes three requirements for those who lead:

**1. Leaders are willing, not coerced**

> Not because you must, but because you are willing, as God wants you to be.

Leadership must be voluntary, never forced. We don't want people serving out of guilt or compulsion, because they will eventually burn out, become bitter, and leave the church hating it. You are called to be a slave to God, but you volunteer to serve the church.

**2. Leaders serve, not for personal gain**

> Not greedy for money, but eager to serve.

Technically, there are no employees in the church. Those who lead receive a stipend—not a wage—to cover living expenses. Originally, churches had land that could be cultivated or leased to provide this support. The reasoning was profound: truth is bigger than money and cannot be corrupted by financial incentives. This principle also applies to academia and government, both modeled on the church.

But greed isn't only about money. In the original Greek, this covers all forms of greed—for recognition, titles, grandiosity, or popularity. The leader who seeks these things has already compromised.

**3. Leaders are worthy examples**

> Not lording it over those entrusted to you, but being examples to the flock.

True leadership isn't about power or titles. It's about character. A leader shows the flock what servanthood and sacrifice look like. Leadership in the church is not a position to be displayed but a character to be embodied.

### A Noble Task

> Here is a trustworthy saying: Whoever aspires to be an overseer desires a noble task. (1 Timothy 3:1)

To all who lead formally—those who shepherd the church, the youth, Sunday school, campus groups, and those who serve behind the scenes—thank you. Yours is a noble decision. The task is not easy. You must prioritize among many good things, choosing the best over the good. The flock has been entrusted to you, and people are counting on you.

### Every Elder Becomes a Teacher

But eldership doesn't apply only to those in formal leadership roles. Paul writes to Titus:

> Older men are to be sober-minded, dignified, self-controlled, sound in faith, in love, and in steadfastness. Older women likewise are to be reverent in behavior, not slanderers or slaves to much wine. They are to teach what is good, and so train the young women to love their husbands and children.

The truth is this: **all of us, except perhaps the very youngest, are elders to someone.** The parents here informally lead young adults who are watching your example and listening to your words. Young workers, university students are watching how you spend your money and manage your time. University students, high school students are following your example in how you balance study, volunteering, and enjoyment.

But let us ask honestly: Are you an example to the young? What do they see?

- Do they observe crass joking and rudeness, or genuine kindness?
- What movies are you watching? They watch what you watch.
- How do you dress? How do you spend your time?

The young and impressionable follow you. Some of you might think, "I'm just a kid. I've just graduated. I want to have fun." But you are not kids anymore. Just as adults once cleaned up after you, it's now your time to clean up after the younger ones. It's time to grow up and take your place in adulthood.

> By this time you ought to be teachers, but you need someone to teach you the elementary truths of God's word all over again. You need milk, not solid food! (Hebrews 5:12)

Some of you should be moving on in life—getting married, starting families. Stop delaying adulthood with overseas holidays, second careers, additional degrees, endless job changes. You are older now. You are an elder, and much more is expected of you. The carefree days should be shortening, not lengthening.

### The Reward of Faithful Leadership

> When the Chief Shepherd appears, you will receive the crown of glory that will never fade away. (1 Peter 5:4)

What is this crown of glory? It's not a physical crown. It's the people you have invested your time in—not socially, but spiritually. Those you have read the Bible with, kept accountable, encouraged, and cared for with tears. This is the fruit of your labour and your inheritance.

Consider a simple illustration: Do your job well in raising children, and you inherit a god-fearing, parent-honouring son or daughter who will eventually become one of your best friends. Do a poor job, and you'll be stuck with a man-child or a whinging daughter for the rest of your life. You live with the result.

Likewise, invest in your youth group, and you'll have a nice youth group. Invest in your church, and you'll have a nice church. This is the glory that awaits us. This is the reward for the elders.

## 2. Submission of the Young (1 Peter 5:5-9)

> Young men, in the same way be submissive to those who are older.

Just as submission applies to other institutions—government, employers, husbands—so it applies in the church. Submit to your leaders.

> Have confidence in your leaders and submit to their authority, because they keep watch over you as those who must give an account. Do this so that their work will be a joy, not a burden, for that would be of no benefit to you. (Hebrews 13:17)

Now, leaders are regular sinners too. They can do the wrong thing sometimes and might not be very nice. There are ways to address this: talk to them directly. But in the meantime, respect and submit to them. If they ask for help with something that isn't unconscionable, assist them. Don't make an already tough job harder. Overseers must delegate, and they need reliable people to delegate to.

This Christmas, consider thanking your leaders. Don't thank me—I receive a stipend; I'm simply doing my job. But your other leaders do this voluntarily, giving up their time in addition to long weeks of work or study. That's something that's genuinely difficult for the young to do, because it requires humility.

### The Paradox of Youth and Humility

> All of you, clothe yourselves with humility toward one another, because God opposes the proud but gives grace to the humble. Humble yourselves therefore under God's mighty hand, that he may lift you up in due time. Cast all your anxiety on him because he cares for you. (1 Peter 5:5-7)

Humility suggests weakness—submitting, casting anxiety, needing help. But we don't associate the young with these qualities. We associate the young with strength, vigour, and vitality. Look at most of you:

- You are the tallest you have ever been
- You are the strongest and fittest you have ever been
- You are the most beautiful you have ever been
- Your hair is most luxurious and plentiful
- You are the best physical manifestation of yourself that you have ever been

From birth, you've been on a chart of exponential growth in strength. Who wouldn't want to invest in you? Why would you need to be humble?

> The glory of young men is their strength, but the splendour of old men is their gray hair. (Proverbs 20:29)

But here lies the danger. When you don't need anyone, submission isn't a priority.

### The Warning: The Roaring Lion

> Be self-controlled and alert. Your enemy the devil prowls around like a roaring lion looking for someone to devour. Resist him, standing firm in the faith, because you know that your brothers throughout the world are undergoing the same kind of sufferings. (1 Peter 5:8-9)

The young are especially vulnerable to pride and arrogance. You see a chart of your past experience—five inches of growth per year, increasing strength year after year—and you think it will continue forever. You extrapolate the graph infinitely upward. But that's not how the graph works in real life. You might never be taller than you are right now. You have reached the height of your strength; a plateau approaches, and then decline. The young don't feel this decline yet, which is why Scripture says:

> Folly is bound up in the heart of a child, but the rod of discipline will drive it far away. (Proverbs 22:15)

The young, by definition, lack real-world experience. Not education or theory or qualifications—but experience. They can only focus on their immediate past and future, ignoring the greater arc of time.

### The Example of Driving

Consider the licensing system for young drivers. Years ago, you could get your learner's permit at 15 and a provisional license at 18, automatically graduating to a full license after a year. But that system had a serious problem: high levels of car accidents and mortality, concentrated among the under-25 age group. Despite more education and more tests, the problem persisted.

Why? **Not because of a lack of education, but because of a lack of maturity, experience, and foresight.** The young think they are invincible. They cannot think long-term; they think only of immediate gratification. What they gain in strength, they lack in foresight. The licensing authorities made you wait not because of the test, but because of the hours—the time needed to experience millions of different scenarios so that your reactions become trained and automatic.

This is even biological. Research shows that the frontal lobe—the part of the brain where long-term consequential thinking develops—doesn't mature until around age 25. No coincidence, then, that car insurance premiums decrease at that age.

The young should submit and learn from the older.

### A Culture That Rejects Wisdom

But our culture does not value gray hair and age. We dye away gray hair as an embarrassment. We promote euthanasia and physician-assisted suicide to rid ourselves of the elderly. They are slow, cranky, a burden on healthcare. Our Western society loves the young. But **the young ought not to be leaders.**

There are exceptions. Scripture acknowledges that:

> A poor but wise youth is better than an old but foolish king who no longer knows how to heed a warning. (Ecclesiastes 4:13)

And young believers can be examples:

> Let no one despise you for your youth, but set the believers an example in speech, in conduct, in love, in faith, in purity. (1 Timothy 4:12)

But generally, Scripture warns against young leadership. When King Rehoboam rejected the counsel of the elders and listened instead to young men who had grown up with him, he split the kingdom (1 Kings 12). And Isaiah warns:

> I will make mere youths their officials; children will rule over them. People will oppress each other—man against man, neighbour against neighbour. The young will rise up against the old. (Isaiah 3:4-5)

When the young rule, it brings judgment.

### The Problem in Our Time

Increasingly, we applaud when the young become our leaders—Kevin Rudd, Barack Obama, Justin Trudeau. Where are the elders? Either eldership has been eliminated, or it exists but manipulates the young to do its bidding (as in Mao's Cultural Revolution). It serves the purposes of evil to have young leadership, because the young are easier to manipulate. They value the immediate over the future. They act on feelings rather than on wisdom. They have no experience of how the real world works. The younger the voters, the more left-leaning. The younger the politicians elected, the more inexperienced and utopian the policies.

When the young rule, it means one of two things: either **the eldership has failed**, or **the eldership is manipulating them.**

## 3. The Path to Mature Leadership (1 Peter 5:10-14)

> And the God of all grace, who called you to his eternal glory in Christ, after you have suffered a little while, will himself restore you and make you strong, firm and steadfast. To him be the power for ever and ever. Amen. (1 Peter 5:10-11)

**Friend, be elders.** You may be young now, but you are already transitioning to become elders. It's time to start taking up the reins of responsibility. It's time to gain the experience of leadership so that you can become old and mature, ready for whatever comes next. But this won't work if you won't learn, if you are arrogant, or if you lack humility.

God doesn't ask the young for physical strength. He wants you to be strong in a different way: to be **firm and steadfast**. To be faithful people. To delay gratification. To suffer for the greater, longer-term good rather than shirk responsibility. To learn now so that you can be mature leaders of the future.

### Practical Humility for the Young

How do we cultivate this humility in practice?

**Ask questions humbly.** When I was young, I viewed question time as an opportunity to show how smart I was and how wrong the speaker was. Now, from up here, I can tell you that's not very gracious. Ask questions genuinely seeking understanding, not seeking to display your cleverness.

**Fill out comment cards.** Many of you already do this—it's voluntary, and we genuinely want your feedback and critique. But more importantly, I would love to read more about how the word of God affects you personally. Tell us about the sin you've acknowledged and how you plan to change. Each week, ask for prayer. Humility expressed in prayer is powerful. Don't worry about me—I'll practice humility too. I have other ways: the rector and my wife! (Laughter)

### Real Change Requires Patience and Humility

I participate in some political groups. They're nice people and passionate, but they act like 25-year-olds. They think that if we protest or sit outside a politician's office, everything will change. We'll reverse the bad laws. Why should they listen? "Because we demand it! Loudly!" That's the strategy of the young and foolish. That's not how the real world works.

**Real change requires humility. Real change takes time.**

Before you think about changing the whole body, help a teacher in class. Join the student representative council. Organize a school formal. Volunteer for campus ministry leadership.

Before you take on a politician, join a party branch. Help out at an election. Understand the policies.

Before you protest the principal, take an interest in school affairs. Volunteer at the fete or working bee. Join the parent council. Get elected president of the P&C.

Before you complain to the building superintendent, attend strata meetings. Do work for them. Be a faithful servant. Get elected to the strata committee. Get elected as chairman.

Before you become a church leader, **serve diligently. Be willing, wanting, and worthy. Learn and gain experience.** Before we can govern, we must learn to govern.

Don't defer maturity—accelerate it. Get married. Learn leadership. It takes time and experience.

As you can see from the prominence of young leaders in our governments, companies, and even churches, we need more leaders. **You are it.**

> Listen to your father who begot you. Do not despise your mother when she is old. (Proverbs 23:22)
