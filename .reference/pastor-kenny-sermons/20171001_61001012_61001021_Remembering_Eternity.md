# 2 Peter 1:12-21

**Title:** Remembering Eternity  
**Date:** October 2017  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Importance of Memory

Peter opens this letter with an urgent concern: reminding his readers of essential truths. Though they already know these things and are firmly established in the faith, Peter insists on refreshing their memory while he still can.

> So I will always remind you of these things, even though you know them and are firmly established in the truth you now have. (1:12)

Peter is an aging apostle aware that his death is imminent. Christ has made clear to him that his time is short. His final wish before departure is simple but profound: ensure that people remember.

> I think it is right to refresh your memory as long as I live in the tent of this body, because I know that I will soon put it aside, as our Lord Jesus Christ has made clear to me. And I will make every effort to see that after my departure you will always be able to remember these things. (1:13-15)

This theme echoes throughout 2 Peter. In chapter 3, Peter returns to it again and again—reminding, stimulating, recalling, urging not to forget. Memory is the beating heart of his entire letter.

### Why Memory Matters

A world without memory would be catastrophic. We would:
- Have no accounting, no history, no books
- Repeat the same day endlessly, never learning from mistakes
- Lose all gratitude and accountability
- Be vulnerable to manipulation—those in power could rewrite history to suit their agenda
- Lose the wisdom of our elders

Like the Israelites in the wilderness, we are a forgetful people. God had performed the most astounding miracles—Egypt, the most powerful nation on earth, was crippled. Yet within months in the desert, the people forgot. They craved the "good old days" and even wanted to return to slavery.

> The rabble with them began to crave other food, and again the Israelites started wailing and said, "If only we had meat to eat! We remember the fish we ate in Egypt at no cost—also the cucumbers, melons, leeks, onions and garlic. But now we have lost our appetite; we never see anything but this manna!" (Numbers 11:4-6)

The pattern repeats in Hebrews. We too are prone to complain, to harden our hearts, to turn away from God by forgetting what He has done.

### The Role of the Church

When we gather as a church—on Sundays, at Bible studies, in fellowship—we gather to remind each other. We rehearse the same truths week after week. Some might call it boring. But consider: we eat dinner nearly the same way every night—vegetables, meat, rice. It's repetitive, yet it nourishes us. We grow bit by bit.

So it is with the Word of God. Remembering, by definition, means returning to the same truths. And through this faithful repetition, we grow.

## The Content of Memory: What We're Called to Remember

Peter is not asking us to remember his own innovations or insights. He directs us to two sources of authority: the apostles and the prophets.

> Dear friends, this is now my second letter to you. I have written both of them as reminders to stimulate you to wholesome thinking. I want you to recall the words spoken in the past by the holy prophets and the command given by our Lord and Savior through your apostles. (3:1-2)

### The Apostles' Memory

The apostles' testimony rests on eyewitness accounts—not cleverly devised stories, but firsthand experience.

> For we did not follow cleverly devised stories when we told you about the coming of our Lord Jesus Christ in power, but we were eyewitnesses of his majesty. He received honor and glory from God the Father when the voice came to him from the Majestic Glory, saying, "This is my Son, whom I love; with him I am well pleased." We ourselves heard this voice that came from heaven when we were with him on the sacred mountain. (1:16-18)

Peter witnessed the Transfiguration. He stood on that mountain and heard the Father's voice affirming Jesus as His beloved Son. This was not imagination or philosophical speculation—it was history. It happened.

The apostles' memory carries weight precisely because it is historical, not mythological. You wouldn't fabricate what they reported. Their willingness to face martyrdom and suffering testifies to their sincerity. Their testimony is trustworthy because it bears the marks of authenticity—different enough to be genuine, consistent enough to be reliable.

### The Prophets' Memory

The Old Testament prophets also speak with authority.

> We also have the prophetic message as something completely reliable, and you will do well to pay attention to it, as to a light shining in a dark place, until the day dawns and the morning star rises in your hearts. Above all, you must understand that no prophecy of Scripture came about by the prophets' own interpretation of things. For prophecy never had its origin in the human will, but prophets, though human, spoke from God as they were carried along by the Holy Spirit. (1:19-21)

The prophets did not speak from their own understanding. The Holy Spirit carried them along as they spoke God's Word. When we read the Old Testament prophets in light of Jesus's arrival, we find their testimony confirmed. The promise is not an outmoded relic but a light shining in darkness until the morning star—Jesus himself—rises in our hearts.

## The Assurance This Brings

### Equal Access to Revelation

We often think that those who lived in the first century had an advantage—they saw Jesus, walked with the apostles, heard the prophets directly. But through written Scripture, we possess something remarkable: equal standing with the first generation of believers.

> you have received a faith as precious as ours (1:1)

The quality of revelation available to us is not diminished. We don't receive a degraded version passed through layers of interpretation. When we open the Bible, we encounter the apostles' memories directly. You can test any teacher—including me—against what Scripture itself says. There are no second-class Christians in terms of revelation. We all have access to the same authoritative Word.

### The Word Speaks to Us Now

Peter doesn't merely preserve the past for historical interest. His message is that what God has spoken continues to transform us in the present.

> The Bible is not outmoded. It is not irrelevant. It is a present reality that changes you now.

This isn't magic. It's the natural power of God's Word working through history and truth. When we encounter Scripture, it does something to us. Words carry the power to reshape our thinking, redirect our desires, and realign our hearts with God's purposes.

## The Cost of Remembering

Memory is not free. There is a real cost to remembering, and we must be willing to pay it.

### The Effort Required

Remembering requires intentional effort. It costs us to:
- Come to church and engage with Scripture and community
- Invest time in knowing God's Word
- Preserve truth for ourselves and pass it on to others
- Recognize that once a truth is forgotten, it cannot easily be rediscovered (unlike a scientific principle that can be researched again)

If we treat Christianity with the same casual commitment we might give to electives in school, we will lose it. The gospel can be lost in a generation if we fail to teach and proclaim it with seriousness.

### The Resistance of Boredom

Sometimes worship feels repetitive. We worry we're "not learning anything new." Our culture chases novelty—fashion, trends, the latest thing. But remembering by its very nature involves repetition.

> You don't memorize your times tables once and retain them forever. You don't write an essay once and never need to review it. Recalling requires reinforcement.

Yet this is precisely what we need. We are tempted to chase whatever is new and exciting, but our calling is to hold fast to what Christ has accomplished for us on the cross. This requires the discipline of remembrance.

### The Spiritual Stakes

If we don't remember, we fall away.

> Anyone who lacks these qualities is so nearsighted and blind that they have forgotten that they have been cleansed from their past sins. (1:9)

Spiritual forgetfulness leads to moral blindness. We lose sight of what we've been freed from and what we've been called to.

---

## Our Responsibility to Pass It On

We bear a solemn responsibility to transmit the faith to the next generation. The Scripture's preservation throughout history testifies to this: even if every copy of the Bible were destroyed, there would be enough collective memory among believers to reconstruct it.

But a written Bible lying on a shelf is worthless if no one remains to tell people about it. The transmission of faith requires living witnesses—people who remember, people who teach, people who proclaim.

---

## The Ultimate Question: Will You Remember Eternity?

Here is the hard truth: many will not remember. They will live for today, conforming to present culture, chasing contemporary approval. The truths that matter most—the resurrection of Christ, the reality of sin and judgment, the virgin birth, biblical sexuality and marriage—these become unpopular. And when they're unpopular, we tend to forget them.

But one day, when you are old, you will face this question: Will you remember Christ? Will you have invested in the memory of eternal truth? Or will you reach the end of life having chased a thousand temporary things?

Peter's urgent plea echoes across the centuries to us: Remember. Remember what God has done. Remember what the apostles witnessed. Remember what the prophets declared. Remember that eternity is real. Remember that Christ is Lord.

For in the end, remembering is not a luxury—it is our lifeline to truth.
