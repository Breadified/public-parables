# Jonah 4:1-11

**Title:** Living Under the Shade of God's Grace  
**Date:** December 21, 2014  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Power of Endings

The ending of a story makes or breaks it. If Jonah were a film, we'd have had a perfect Hollywood conclusion last week at the end of chapter three:

> When God saw what they did and how they turned from their evil ways, he relented and did not bring on them the destruction he had threatened.

After storms, sea monsters, near-death experiences, and dramatic twists, Jonah finally preached to Nineveh. The city repented—everyone from the greatest to the least. Destruction was averted. Cue the inspirational music. Everyone lives happily ever after.

But then we read chapter 4:

> But to Jonah this seemed very wrong, and he became angry.

The story takes an unexpected turn. Jonah is angry. But why?

## Jonah's Anger: The Real Reason (4:1-4)

Jonah's anger reveals his true motive for fleeing God's original call. In his prayer, he confesses:

> Isn't this what I said, Lord, when I was still at home? That is what I tried to forestall by fleeing to Tarshish. I knew that you are a gracious and compassionate God, slow to anger and abounding in love, a God who relents from sending calamity.

The root of Jonah's disobedience wasn't fear or procrastination—it was something far darker. He simply didn't want to see the Ninevites saved. This was no abstract theological disagreement. Nineveh was Israel's mortal enemy. A dangerous, godless nation that would eventually destroy the Northern Kingdom. They represented everything Israel despised: a different culture, different values, a genuine threat.

Jonah wanted Nineveh destroyed for its wickedness and the harm it had caused Israel. But God offered them grace instead.

Consider the intensity of Jonah's response. The Hebrew describes God's salvation as "evil" to Jonah—"evil with a great evil." His anger was so consuming that he concluded:

> Now, Lord, take away my life, for it is better for me to die than to live.

Death seemed preferable to witnessing God's grace extended to his enemies. This reveals how anger distorts our moral compass. What is objectively good—God's mercy—Jonah perceives as evil. 

God's response cuts through this confusion:

> But the Lord replied, "Is it right for you to be angry?"

## God's Concern for All People (4:5-11)

After his one day of preaching, Jonah positions himself east of the city, waiting to see destruction. Picture him: settling into a comfortable spot, making a shelter, sitting in its shade, watching like someone at New Year's Eve waiting for fireworks. But the fireworks he wanted were the annihilation of 120,000 people.

Instead of letting Jonah's bitterness fester, God teaches through a living parable:

> Then the Lord God provided a leafy plant and made it grow up over Jonah to give shade for his head to ease his discomfort, and Jonah was very happy about the plant. But at dawn the next day God provided a worm, which chewed the plant so that it withered. When the sun rose, God provided a scorching east wind, and the sun blazed on Jonah's head so that he grew faint. He wanted to die, and said, "It would be better for me to die than to live."

The comparison is unmistakable. Jonah rapidly cycles through joy and devastation over a plant that appeared overnight and died overnight. God asks:

> Is it right for you to be angry about the plant?

Jonah insists it is—his anger is justified.

Then comes God's final, penetrating question:

> You have been concerned about this plant, though you did not tend it or make it grow. It sprang up overnight and died overnight. And should I not have concern for the great city of Nineveh, in which there are more than a hundred and twenty thousand people who cannot tell their right hand from their left—and also many animals?

The logic is devastating. If Jonah can grieve a plant he never cultivated, how can he deny that God should care for 120,000 human lives plus animals—all of God's creation? Both the plant and Nineveh were God's work, not Jonah's. God alone tended them and made them grow.

There's even a deeper layer. The name of the plant—*kikoyon*—contains the letters of Jonah's name. In a sense, God's gracious plant was for Jonah. Just as God gave Jonah unmerited shade from the scorching sun, God gave Nineveh unmerited grace from destruction. Both rescued from evil through divine mercy.

The profound question emerges: If Jonah was happy to receive unmerited grace, why does he begrudge that same grace to the Ninevites? His anger exposes a fundamental hypocrisy.

## The Heart of the Matter

Jonah's struggle reflects a universal human tendency: we rejoice in God's grace toward ourselves while resenting it toward others. Kenny shared a personal illustration: As a schoolboy in Malaysia, he asked his principal for a prefect position and received it. He was thrilled—until his best friend used the same approach and succeeded. Instead of sharing in his friend's joy, Kenny resented him. He had benefited from bold initiative, yet he begrudged his friend the same opportunity.

Why? Because we're often too self-centered. We think only of our own experience, forgetting others. We want good things for ourselves but resent when others receive them. We foolishly fear that another's blessing somehow diminishes our own.

But Scripture calls us to something radically different:

> Love your enemies, do good to those who hate you. (Luke 6:27)

## The Gospel for Everyone

Can we not all rejoice together in God's free, unmerited grace? Can we not both be grateful? We have an obligation to share this great news—not to selfishly hold it to ourselves and our friends, but for everyone.

If God's grace is good enough for us, it's good enough for our enemies. And yes, even our enemies deserve the gospel. Not just obvious enemies, but those people we've written off: the annoying, the weak, the awkward, the uninteresting. Those we look down on. Those in our own communities, our schools, our families.

The question reverberates: Are there people or groups we deeply, secretly wish would not be saved? Whose salvation would actually upset us?

That's the real test of whether we truly understand grace.
