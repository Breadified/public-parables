# Amos 3:1-15

**Title:** Judgement on the Wise
**Date:** 22 November 2015
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## God Knows Israel (1-2, 2:9-12)

Israel's story began like that of the American Pilgrims—a people escaping persecution. Enslaved in Egypt, God miraculously freed them and brought them into the promised land. Out of all the families on earth, God chose Israel as His own.

> "You only have I chosen of all the families of the earth; therefore I will punish you for all your sins."

The word "chosen" here translates the Hebrew *yada*—to know. But this isn't mere intellectual knowledge. It's intimate knowledge, like when God told Jeremiah, "Before I formed you in the womb I knew you." God didn't just know about Israel; He knew them deeply and personally.

Over a thousand years, God's promise to Abraham became reality. From one couple to a family, from a tribe to a nation—people, land, and blessing. By Solomon's time, Israel had become the wisest nation on earth. Visitors came from distant lands to witness their majesty and wealth.

### The Blessing of God's Knowledge

When we believe in a God who knows what He's doing, wisdom naturally follows. Knowing God brings earthly benefits:

- Reading Scripture leads to education (universities were originally founded as Christian institutions)
- Belief in God's law brings less corruption and more efficient societies
- Valuing human life creates better human capital
- Avoiding vice saves money and promotes health
- Belief in order produces science rather than superstition

These aren't guarantees in a fallen world, but the overall package lends itself to success. It's worth noting that nations touched by the gospel—England, America, Scandinavia, and modern Israel—have flourished. The Bible makes us wiser through its practical wisdom about our nature, our place in the world, and our role in God's creation.

## Israel Does Not Know God (1, 3-10)

Yet there's a shocking conclusion: despite all this knowledge and blessing, Israel will be judged.

> "Hear this word, people of Israel, the word the Lord has spoken against you—against the whole family I brought up out of Egypt."

The shock isn't just God's judgment—it's that Israel judged itself through its own actions. Look at the cause-and-effect they understood:

> "Do two walk together unless they have agreed to do so?"

Israel had covenanted with God at Shechem to serve Him. They knew the agreement. And when judgment comes, it comes because the cause precedes it:

> "Does a lion roar in the thicket when it has no prey? Does it growl in its den when it has caught nothing? Does a bird swoop down to a trap on the ground when no bait is there?"

These aren't random catastrophes. God doesn't judge without reason. When disaster strikes a city, the Lord has caused it—because Israel broke the covenant they willingly made.

> "Surely the Sovereign Lord does nothing without revealing his plan to his servants the prophets."

God warned them repeatedly through His prophets. When the trumpet sounds in the city, people tremble because judgment is coming. Israel knew this cause and effect. Yet remarkably, even the pagan nations see the pattern:

> "Proclaim to the fortresses of Ashdod and to the fortresses of Egypt: 'Assemble yourselves on the mountains of Samaria; see the great unrest within her and the oppression among her people.'"

The verdict is devastating:

> "They do not know how to do right," declares the Lord, "who store up in their fortresses what they have plundered and looted."

Here's the tragedy: these are God's people. They weren't ignorant or left in darkness. They were the ones God knew intimately, the ones invited to His family celebrations. They saw His blessings everywhere. Yet in their success, they took that knowledge for granted. They bit the hand that fed them. They became ungrateful.

### Application: Knowing and Doing

We face the same danger. If we know God, we must *show* our gratitude by actually knowing Him—reading Scripture, meditating on His word, especially during breaks when life slows down. Knowledge demands action. As Paul wrote:

> "If I have the gift of prophecy and can fathom all mysteries and all knowledge, and if I have a faith that can move mountains, but do not have love, I am nothing."

Love isn't emotion—it's serving sacrificially. If you know what is right, do it. Stop being hypocrites. You've been taught well at youth group, at church, at university. You have access to excellent resources. Use them! Share that blessing so others might know God too.

## The Consequences of Forgetting God (11-15)

Because Israel refused to use their knowledge rightly, they lost everything.

> "An enemy will overrun your land, pull down your strongholds and plunder your fortresses."

Within forty years, the Assyrian lion—symbolized by the massive winged lions that guarded Assyrian temples—would devour the northern kingdom. By 722 BC, Israel was destroyed.

But even in this devastation, there's a glimmer of hope:

> "As a shepherd rescues from the lion's mouth only two leg bones or a piece of an ear, so will the Israelites living in Samaria be rescued, with only the head of a bed and a piece of fabric from a couch."

God would preserve a remnant. The Assyrians, following their policy of forced assimilation, would interbreed the population, destroying their national identity. Yet not all would be lost—a few bones, an ear's worth of genetic heritage would survive.

All the symbols of their inherited blessing would be destroyed:

> "I will destroy the altars of Bethel; the horns of the altar will be cut off and fall to the ground. I will tear down the winter house along with the summer house; the houses adorned with ivory will be destroyed and the mansions will be demolished."

Everything they accumulated through God's wisdom—their double-storey homes, their marble floors, their ivory decorations—gone. They forgot that knowing God came before possessing things.

### The Remnant's Gratitude

Centuries later, in Luke 17, Jesus encountered ten lepers on the border of Samaria. These were descendants of that remnant—people with just an ear's worth of genetic heritage from ancient Israel. Jesus healed all ten. But only one came back to thank Him.

> "One of them, when he saw he was healed, came back, praising God in a loud voice. He threw himself at Jesus' feet and thanked him—and he was a Samaritan."

How different from the old Samaria that took God's knowledge for granted! This Samaritan learned the lesson his ancestors failed to learn: gratitude.

Christians are grateful people. The attitude of thanksgiving is the right response of the wise.

This is what the Pilgrims understood when they celebrated their first harvest in 1621. Despite losing half their number to a brutal winter, they recognized God's provision through an unlikely helper—Squanto, a Patuxet who miraculously spoke English and knew how to survive on the very land where they'd settled. When Squanto died, Governor Bradford declared he had been "a special instrument sent of God."

The Pilgrims didn't take their blessings for granted. Neither should we.

**What are we taking for granted that we should be thankful to God for?**
