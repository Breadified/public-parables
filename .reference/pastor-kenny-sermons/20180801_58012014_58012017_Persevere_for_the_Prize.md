# Hebrews 12:14-17

**Title:** Persevere for the Prize
**Date:** 26 August 2018
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Introduction: The Cost of Trading

In life, we constantly make trades and exchanges. We swap one thing for another—sometimes wisely, sometimes foolishly. The Christian life itself is a great swap: we exchange our old way of living for a new set of behaviors centered on Christ. As we study Hebrews 12:14-17, we'll see what happens when we trade away the precious gift of God's grace for temporary satisfaction.

---

## 1. Peace with Everyone

> Make every effort to live in peace with everyone. (12:14)

The Greek word *dioko* means to pursue with intensity and determination. Last week we heard about the Christian race as a metaphor for spiritual endurance. Here, we're called to actively *pursue* peace.

### Living at Peace in a Divided World

Life is inherently political. We encounter people we naturally align with and others we clash with. Different personalities, different perspectives—sometimes we get our way, sometimes we don't. But Christ calls us to make every effort to live at peace. Rather than viewing the world in terms of win-lose scenarios, we're invited to seek win-win solutions.

**Making every effort means:**
- Giving people chances to be heard
- Offering the benefit of the doubt
- Resisting the impulse to assume the worst

### The Problem of Suspicion

Our culture has embraced what we might call a "hermeneutic of suspicion"—a tendency to distrust everyone's motives. Every disagreement gets reduced to prejudice. A woman who chooses to stay home with her children is labeled patriarchal and misogynist, when perhaps she simply prefers caring for her own children over leaving them with strangers. Someone skeptical about catastrophic climate change is labeled a "denier" and grouped with racists and bigots.

When we assume that everyone who disagrees with us is either stupid, evil, or both, we've already declared war. We cannot even hear their case because we've condemned them without a trial. This is not peacemaking—this is troublemaking.

### The Example of Compromise Rightly Understood

Consider Scott Morrison: a conservative Christian who held deep convictions on traditional marriage, border security, energy policy, and religious freedom. Yet he was willing to work alongside Malcolm Turnbull, whose views on these issues were diametrically opposed. Morrison served as number two, waiting for his moment while compromising on implementation—pushing back on emissions targets, delaying same-sex marriage votes, trying to be a team player.

The result? When Morrison finally led, he was able to contain many of the excesses of the previous government that his critics advocated. He made every effort to work with everyone, even those he fundamentally disagreed with.

## 2. Be Holy—Don't Compromise on Convictions

> and to be holy; without holiness no one will see the Lord. (12:14)

This is the tension the Christian must live in: pursue peace *and* be holy. Be a peacemaker *and* be different from the world.

### What Does Holiness Mean?

Holiness means separation from what is polluted and common. In the Old Testament, the holy place was separated from the ordinary, and the Holy of Holies represented extreme separation—set apart for God alone.

As Romans 12:18 reminds us: "If it is possible, as far as it depends on you, live at peace with everyone." But notice the condition: *if it is possible*. We cannot pursue peace at the expense of truth. We must not be brainwashed. We must draw lines.

### The Danger of Doctrinal Drift

Here's what troubles me deeply: Christians' views on fundamental issues have shifted not just once, but repeatedly—and in opposite directions:

- **Divorce:** We once believed it was wrong. Then we said it was merely "not preferred." Now we say it's sometimes preferable (in cases of abuse, for example).
- **Homosexuality:** We once taught it was sin. Then we said it was "just not a preferred lifestyle." Now many Christians encourage people to embrace this identity.

Think about what this means for the authority of Scripture. If God's Word can be interpreted in fundamentally opposite ways on the same issue, how can anyone trust it? How can unbelievers trust Christianity when they watch us completely reverse our convictions every generation?

The problem is not that Scripture has changed—it hasn't. Culture has changed, and weak-willed Christians have simply followed along. We're always one generation behind the world. This is not holiness; this is capitulation. We've been changed by the world rather than changing the world.

### The Challenge: Guard Your Convictions

Look at Peter Dutton as an example of someone who refused to compromise. He had convictions and stood by them, even when backbenchers were unhappy. It took courage—the kind of courage Turnbull didn't believe anyone possessed.

The question for each of us: What are you compromising on? Notice where you've drawn your lines. We have traded lives with Jesus—lives of holiness. Don't trade that away bit by bit.

## 3. Three Warnings: "Lest Any"

> See to it that no one falls short of the grace of God and that no bitter root grows up to cause trouble and defile many. (12:15-16)

Notice the threefold repetition: "lest any" or "no one." These are serious warnings.

### First Warning: Don't Fall Short of Grace

> See to it that no one falls short of the grace of God. (12:15)

The Greek *skopos* means to look earnestly, to keep watch. The race metaphor returns: those who run may fall away from the grace of God. This is about both life and doctrine. Watch yourself carefully. Don't take God's grace for granted.

### Second Warning: The Bitter Root

> That no bitter root grows up to cause trouble and defile many. (12:15)

This echoes Deuteronomy 29:18:

> Make sure there is no man or woman, clan or tribe among you today whose heart turns away from the Lord our God to go and worship the gods of those nations; make sure there is no root among you that produces such bitter poison.

A bitter root produces bitter fruit—and it entangles many. The person who harbors it takes God's grace for granted. According to Deuteronomy 29:19, this root is a person with a false view of eternal security—someone who thinks, "I shall be safe, though I walk in the stubbornness of my heart." They misunderstand God's covenant, assuming that belonging to God's people guarantees protection from His judgment.

### Third Warning: Don't Despise Your Inheritance

> See that no one is sexually immoral, or is godless like Esau, who for a single meal sold his inheritance. (12:16)

The word "sexually immoral" (*porneia*) etymologically means "to sell"—a reference to prostitution, selling something precious for a cheap price. This directly parallels what Esau did: he sold his birthright.

#### The Story of Esau

From Genesis 25:

Esau was a skilled hunter while Jacob preferred staying in tents. Isaac loved Esau for his wild game, but Rebekah favored Jacob. One day, when Esau came in from hunting, famished, Jacob was cooking stew. Esau asked for some, saying he was about to die of hunger. Jacob replied, "First sell me your birthright."

Esau, desperate and short-sighted, agreed. Jacob made him swear an oath, then gave him bread and lentil stew. Esau ate, drank, and left—and "despised his birthright."

Later, when Isaac was blessing Jacob under deceptive circumstances, Esau returned to claim his blessing only to discover it was already given. He wept bitterly, asking if his father had no blessing left for him. Instead, Isaac gave Esau a curse:

> Your dwelling will be away from the earth's richness, away from the dew of heaven above. You will live by the sword and you will serve your brother. But when you grow restless, you will throw his yoke from off your neck.

#### The Point: What Are You Trading Away?

Esau's hunger blinded him. Lentil soup became more important than God's blessing. He despised his birthright—and in despising it, he despised God Himself.

Think about what Christians trade away:

- **Money and wealth:** People sacrifice heavenly inheritance for earthly cash. Do you think there's no wealth in heaven?
- **Possessions:** We trade eternal treasures for temporary things. What could earth possibly offer compared to eternity?
- **Sexual pleasure:** A moment of satisfaction versus a lifetime of holiness and God's blessing.
- **Entertainment and worldly enjoyment:** A few years of earthly pleasure versus forever in God's presence.

One act revealed Esau's true character. He took his inheritance for granted, as if it were nothing.

## 4. The Regret of Esau

> Afterward, when he wanted to inherit this blessing, he was rejected. Even though he sought the blessing with tears, he could not change what he had done. (12:17)

Esau sought the blessing *with tears*—a place of genuine repentance. But repentance could not undo what he had done. The consequences were permanent.

### A Modern Illustration: The Tragedy of Wasted Leadership

Consider Malcolm Turnbull. He backstabbed Tony Abbott to take the leadership, setting a precedent for instability. When Abbott fought from opposition, Turnbull promised not to touch energy policy—yet he broke that promise. Eight years later, he repeated his mistake, attempting to work with the opposition on the National Energy Guarantee, even releasing draft legislation to opposition members before consulting his own party.

The backbench revolted. Turnbull realized the danger too late. He consulted MPs, promised to defer emissions targets, but it was over. The MPs had seen his pattern: a leopard never changes its spots. There would be no third chance.

He blamed the insurgents, even calling 50% of his own party insurgents. But the truth was unavoidable—he had rejected the gift of leadership by betraying the principles that made him leader. He will be remembered not for achievements, but for how he obtained office and how he lost it.

This is the gnashing of teeth Jesus speaks of in Luke 13:28—the weeping and regret of those who stand outside the kingdom, watching others feast while they are cast out.

---

## Conclusion: What Will You Do?

We've all been dealt certain cards in life. But the greatest card any Christian holds is the gift of grace. The question is: what will you do with it?

Will you trade it away for lentil soup—temporary pleasure, worldly gain, selfish ambition? Will you live in eternal regret?

Or will you keep it, cherish it, guard it as holy? Will you live in constant gratitude, never taking God's grace for granted?

Make the right decision. Pursue peace with everyone. Be holy. Guard your inheritance. Don't fall short of the grace that cost Christ everything.
