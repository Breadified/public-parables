# 1 Samuel 7:2-17

**Title:** Breaking the Cycle
**Date:** 14 May 2017
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction: The Vicious Cycle

Being a parent brings both joy and challenge. One of the most difficult challenges is becoming trapped in irreversible cycles—patterns of relating that seem impossible to escape. Picture the exhausting loop: parents become busy and tired, they scream at their children, the emotional toll leaves them more tired, the children become upset with nightmares and bedwetting, parents grow angry over disrupted sleep, mornings become rushed, evenings bring more fatigue, and the cycle repeats endlessly. No solution in sight. How can such an intractable pattern ever be broken?

Israel faced a similar trap, and their story shows us the way out.

## Part 1: Repentance (Verses 2-6)

### The Twenty Years of Silence

The Israelites had just committed a devastating sin. Rather than seeking God's help, they attempted to use the Ark of God—the wooden container of God's presence—as a magical talisman, believing they could control God and guarantee victory in battle. Their presumption led to catastrophic defeat. The Philistines captured the Ark, though God ultimately proved He could not be contained and arranged for its return.

When verse 2 begins, we encounter a startling fact: "The ark remained at Kiriath Jearim a long time—twenty years in all."

The question haunts us: Did Israel simply wait in passivity for twenty years, doing nothing, only then repenting? The answer is both more complex and more hopeful. While the Ark sat at Kiriath Jearim for those two decades, the people cried out to God ceaselessly. For twenty years they lamented. And for twenty years, God met them with silence.

This silence should not surprise us. After all, when Samuel came of age and the word of God came to Israel, they immediately ignored it—perhaps even before Samuel could properly speak, the people had already made up their minds to trust in themselves rather than God. The silence continued as God withheld the voice of the prophet.

But silence is not abandonment. At the end of twenty years, Samuel finally spoke.

### Putting Away Idols

Samuel's message was direct and demanding: "If you are returning to the Lord with all your hearts, then rid yourselves of the foreign gods and the Ashtoreths and commit yourselves to the Lord and serve him only, and he will deliver you out of the hand of the Philistines."

The Israelites obeyed. They put away their Baals and Ashtoreths.

These ancient idols speak volumes about human nature—and about our own times. **Baal** (meaning "master") was the god of nature, agriculture, and rain. **Asthoreth** represented fertility, prosperity, and sexual gratification. They were the idols of every age.

Today we worship the same gods under new names. We have our own Baals in the form of "Mother Nature," the spirituality of "oneness with nature," yoga, and New Age philosophies—all while forgetting that nature itself includes mosquitoes, poisonous mushrooms, venomous snakes, parasites, disease, and death. We have our modern Ashtoreths in the cult of sexuality that saturates every sitcom, advertisement, and increasingly, our school curricula—treated as dogma that cannot be questioned.

When we worship these idols, we give our allegiance to the false gods we have imported from the nations around us—the Philistines of our own day. We may also worship the gods of Mammon (money) and Molech (power), but the pattern remains the same: we bow to what creation offers rather than to the Creator.

### Genuine Repentance Bears Fruit

Notice something crucial in verse 6: "On that day they fasted and there they confessed, 'We have sinned against the Lord.'"

Their practical repentance came *before* their confessed repentance. They put away idols, then confessed sin—not the reverse. This ordering reveals a profound truth: repentance without fruit is empty words.

Throughout the book of Judges, Israel repeatedly *talked* about repentance. But their words changed nothing. The cycle ground on endlessly. It was only when Israel combined confession with concrete action that God moved to their aid.

This time, God had waited twenty years to ensure genuine transformation, not mere lip service.

Consider a child who apologizes for leaving a towel on the floor—but leaves it there daily. Is that repentance? Or consider saying sorry to your mother without changing the behavior—if you repeat the same apology every day, something is profoundly wrong.

When we come before God, our repentance must be both confessional *and* practical. As Paul writes in 1 Thessalonians 1:9, true repentance means: "you turned to God from idols to serve the living and true God." That is practical repentance.

Jesus commanded: "Produce fruit in keeping with repentance" (Matthew 3:8). Real repentance means giving up our little gods—the gods that whisper to us that the earth controls our destiny, not God; that happiness springs from sexual gratification rather than from communion with our Creator. It means turning from these false sources of life and returning wholly to the true God.

## Part 2: Routed—The Test of Faith (Verses 7-11)

### The Philistine Attack

As soon as the Israelites gathered at Mizpah, news reached the Philistine rulers. Whether they saw a unified assembly as a threat or as an opportunity to strike a concentrated target, they made their move: "When the Philistines heard that Israel had assembled at Mizpah, the rulers of the Philistines came up to attack them."

Fear gripped the Israelites: "When the Israelites heard of it, they were afraid because of the Philistines."

But this moment revealed something new in Israel's character. Rather than marching out in military force as they might have before, they called upon Samuel: "Do not stop crying out to the Lord our God for us, that he may rescue us from the hand of the Philistines."

They asked Samuel to be their intercessor—literally, to not be silent before God on their behalf.

### God's Dramatic Intervention

Samuel took a young lamb and offered it as a whole burnt offering—a sacrifice of complete dedication and ascension. As he prayed on Israel's behalf, the Philistines drew near to engage in battle. But in that moment, something extraordinary happened:

"The Lord thundered with loud thunder against the Philistines and threw them into such a panic that they were routed before the Israelites."

The true God of nature—the one the Philistines had mocked by worshipping false gods of nature—rose up against them. Thunder itself became a weapon. The Philistines scattered in terror, and Israel pursued them, slaughtering them along the way. The rout was complete.

### Breaking the Judges Cycle

Throughout the book of Judges, Israel followed a predictable pattern: sin, judgment, cry out to God, receive a warrior-judge, temporary peace, then sin again. The cycle never broke. The judges fought, they won, but the fundamental problem remained unsolved.

But now something different emerged. The solution was not a mighty warrior-judge like Samson or Gideon. The breakthrough came through intercession—through Samuel crying out on behalf of the people, accompanied by sacrificial offering. The judge's role had fundamentally changed.

This shift points forward to a future fulfillment. Samuel interceding for Israel, bringing a sacrifice on their behalf, winning the case before God—these are shadows of something greater to come. As Paul writes in Romans 8:34: "Christ Jesus who died—more than that, who was raised to life—is at the right hand of God and is also interceding for us."

Jesus is our ultimate Judge, but He is a Judge who intercedes rather than condemns. He is also the ultimate Sacrifice: "God presented Christ as a sacrifice of atonement, through the shedding of his blood—to be received by faith" (Romans 3:25).

When Jesus brings our case before God, He does not present our credentials—we are guilty. Instead, He brings His own credentials: His perfect righteousness, His work on the cross, and the rights He has won for us to live. And Jesus wins that case absolutely.

Because of His intercession and sacrifice, we need not wait twenty years for forgiveness. When we confess our sin and put our faith in Christ, we are saved immediately. The cycle is broken not through our own effort but through His work. And the fruit of repentance begins at once—not as payment for salvation, but as the natural outpouring of a transformed life.

## Part 3: Remember—The Monument (Verses 12-17)

### Ebenezer: "Thus Far the Lord Has Helped Us"

After the victory, Samuel took a stone and set it up between Mizpah and Shen as a permanent monument. He called it Ebenezer, saying: "Thus far the Lord has helped us."

The name Ebenezer has unfortunate associations today—we think of Ebenezer Scrooge and miserliness. But in Scripture, this name means something profound: remembrance. The stone was placed on a main highway where people passing to and from the Ark would see it regularly. Every journey would prompt the question: "What is this stone?" And the answer would come: "Here God delivered us. Here the cycle was broken."

Interestingly, Ebenezer echoes another place from earlier in Samuel's story—chapter 4, verse 1, where Israel faced the very opposite: defeat and the loss of the Ark. But now, through repentance, God helps them. The same location bears opposite histories: once marked by despair, now marked by deliverance.

### A Life of Remembrance and Service

The victory brought lasting change. "The Philistines were subdued and they stopped invading Israel's territory. Throughout Samuel's lifetime, the hand of the Lord was against the Philistines." Towns that had been captured were restored. Peace came between Israel and her neighboring peoples.

Samuel himself established a pattern: "From year to year he went on a circuit from Bethel to Gilgal to Mizpah, judging Israel in all those places. But he always went back to Ramah, where his home was, and there he also held court for Israel. And he built an altar there to the Lord."

Samuel did not remain in one place. He traveled, judging, teaching, reminding the people of God's faithfulness. But he always returned home to Ramah, where he built an altar to the Lord. His life became a living memorial—a circuit of remembrance.

### Why We Remember

We set up stones and monuments because remembrance is essential to the human soul. It combats the amnesia that allows us to return to old patterns and old sins. This is why we observe Mothers Day—not merely as a sentimental holiday, but as a deliberate act of remembrance. The modern tradition began in America in 1905 when Anna Jarvis created it to remember her own mother's sacrifices. Yet the idea reaches back further still to Mothering Sunday in the United Kingdom and Europe—the fourth Sunday in Lent—when the faithful would return to their mother church, the main church of their hometown, to worship and remember.

On this Mothers Day, we do both: we remember the sacrifices our mothers have made for us, and we remember the sacrifice of Jesus, who gave everything for our redemption.

Jesus is our Ebenezer stone. He is the permanent monument to God's help. Through His work, the cycle of our dysfunction is decisively broken. We need not remain trapped in endless patterns of sin and failure. We need not wait twenty years for deliverance.

Jesus offers us a break—a true rest, like Mothering Sunday offers a return to one's home church. This break in the cycle allows us to begin again, restored and whole, ready to serve Him with renewed hearts.
