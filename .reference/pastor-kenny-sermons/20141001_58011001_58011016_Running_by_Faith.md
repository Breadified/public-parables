## Faith and the Future: Understanding What Faith Really Is

### What Is Biblical Faith?

The letter to Hebrews was written to Jewish Christians facing persecution and temptation to abandon their faith. The author reminds them:

> But my righteous one will live by faith. And I take no pleasure in the one who shrinks back. But we do not belong to those who shrink back and are destroyed, but to those who have faith and are saved. (Hebrews 10:38-39)

So what does it mean to live by faith? The passage opens with a definition that has often been misunderstood:

> Now faith is the reality of things hoped for, the assurance about what we do not see. (Hebrews 11:1)

Many modern readers interpret this as confidence or a feeling—a subjective inner conviction. But this misses the mark. The Greek word *hupostasis* (reality/substance) appears five times in the New Testament, three times in Hebrews. In Hebrews 1:3, the Son is described as "the exact representation of his **being**"—meaning the actual reality and nature of God, not a feeling. Similarly, Hebrews 3:14 uses *hupostasis* to mean the conviction or reality we hold firm. The second part of verse 1 uses *elenchos* (proof or demonstration), not mere subjective conviction.

A better translation captures this: **Faith is the reality of things hoped for and proof of things we do not see.**

### Faith Isn't Subjective—It's Evidence-Based

When we make faith about confidence and assurance—words that feel subjective in modern English—we get it backwards. We think: "I feel confident, therefore I have faith" or "I feel assured, therefore I believe." This makes faith dependent on our emotions.

But that's backwards reasoning. **We don't have faith because we feel confident. We have confidence because we have faith. We don't have proof because we feel assured. We have assurance because we have proof.**

The critical insight: **Faith is not evidence itself. Faith is what you do with evidence.**

Think of it this way. The word "believe" (Greek *pistis*) is a transitive verb—it always needs an object. When we say "I believe," we're incomplete. Believe in what? The same applies to faith. Faith must have an object. We cannot have faith in faith—that's circular. Faith must be faith *in something real*.

**Faith is trusting in objective reality.** It's hope based on something concrete, not on feelings or wishes. When we say someone lacks faith, we might mean the evidence isn't convincing. The solution then is to gather more evidence. But often when people say "I don't have faith," they're not saying the evidence is bad. They're saying "I don't feel it" or "I don't have the chemistry." That's not an evidence problem—that's a personal problem.

## Faith and the Past: Learning from History

### The Testimony of the Ancients

Now the author turns to history to demonstrate what faith actually looks like in practice. **By faith we understand that the universe was formed at God's command, so that what is seen was not made out of what was visible.** (Hebrews 11:3)

The universe began void and dark. Then God spoke, and what was unseen became seen. Light, sun, moon, and stars filled the cosmos. God's word transformed the invisible into the visible.

### Abel: Faith in Obedience

> By faith Abel brought God a better offering than Cain did. By faith he was commended as righteous when God spoke well of his offerings. And by faith Abel still speaks, even though he is dead. (Hebrews 11:4)

Genesis doesn't explicitly say why God rejected Cain but accepted Abel, but Genesis 4:7 provides a clue: Cain disobeyed God's word. Abel, by inference, obeyed. Abel heard God and, in reverent fear and obedience, offered what was right. His faith proved itself in action.

### Enoch: Faith in Transformation

> By faith Enoch was taken from this life, so that he did not experience death: "He could not be found, because God had taken him away." For before he was taken, he was commended as one who pleased God. (Hebrews 11:5)

Enoch walked with God and was taken directly to see Him, never experiencing death. His life was marked by faith that pleased God.

### Noah: Faith Despite Ridicule

> By faith Noah, when warned about things not yet seen, in holy fear built an ark to save his family. By his faith he condemned the world and became heir of the righteousness that is in keeping with faith. (Hebrews 11:7)

Noah received a warning of something "not yet seen"—a flood. He had no idea what it would be like. Yet in reverent fear and obedience, he built the ark while everyone mocked him. In the middle of the desert, it's not going to rain. Yet he followed God's word.

### Abraham: Faith Without Knowing

> By faith Abraham, when called to go to a place he would later receive as his inheritance, obeyed and went, even though he did not know where he was going. By faith he made his home in the promised land like a stranger in a foreign country; he lived in tents, as did Isaac and Jacob, who were heirs with him of the same promise. For he was looking forward to the city with foundations, whose architect and builder is God. (Hebrews 11:8-10)

Abraham received a clear call—an audible voice. That was his evidence. And so he went, not knowing where he was going. **That's what faith is.** Not subjective feeling, but trust in God's word.

And by faith even Sarah, who was past childbearing age, was enabled to bear children because she considered him faithful who had made the promise. (Hebrews 11:11)

And so from this one man, and he as good as dead, came descendants as numerous as the stars in the sky and as countless as the sand on the seashore. (Hebrews 11:12)

### Why History Matters

We're often skeptical about the Bible because it's history, not science or mathematics where we feel more certainty. But history is actually the primary source of knowledge in the area of relationships—the most important area of life.

How do you know who to be friends with, who to marry, who to work for? You rely on history—on a person's track record. With trustworthy people, skepticism fades when they repeatedly come through. With unreliable people, you lose trust. This is how relationships work.

**The same principle applies to our relationship with God.** Faith is based on the faithfulness of another. God has been faithful throughout history. The faith in God is not futile. We can trust in future outcomes because of past patterns.

**Faith is not magic.** It's an extrapolation of the past into the future. And you can trust the God of the Bible because He is faithful to all His promises. The ancients trusted His word, and their faith came true. The method of transmission doesn't matter—whether a voice from heaven, oral transmission, or words on a page. We have faith in the same way they did: **trust in God's word.**

This faith pleases God:

> And without faith it is impossible to please God, because anyone who comes to him must believe that he exists and that he rewards those who earnestly seek him. (Hebrews 11:6)

## Faith and the Present: Our Advantage

### They Saw from a Distance; We See Now

But here's the remarkable thing: we are in a better position than the ancients.

> All these people were still living by faith when they died. They did not receive the things promised; they only saw them and welcomed them from a distance, admitting that they were foreigners and strangers on earth. (Hebrews 11:13)

The ancients didn't receive what they hoped for. Yet they saw it from a distance and welcomed it. They were like immigrant families who leave everything for a better country they've never seen. My parents came to Australia decades ago with incomplete knowledge—thinking kangaroos roamed backyards, packing groceries from home, risking everything without the guarantee of return. Life was hard. Interest rates were 18%. Yet they persevered because they had a vision.

The ancient believers were like that. They saw the promises from a distance, but they acted on them anyway.

> People who say such things show that they are looking for a country of their own. If they had been thinking of the country they had left, they would have had opportunity to return. Instead, they were longing for a better country—a heavenly one. Therefore God is not ashamed to be called their God, for he has prepared a city for them. (Hebrews 11:14-16)

### We Have Better Information

But we have a massive advantage:

> These were all commended for their faith, yet none of them received what had been promised, since God had planned something better for us so that only together with us would they be made perfect. (Hebrews 11:39-40)

**We now see the fulfillment.** We have seen Jesus. Consider Matthew 11:11:

> Truly I tell you, among those born of women there has not risen anyone greater than John the Baptist; yet whoever is least in the kingdom of heaven is greater than he.

Why? Because John the Baptist got to see Jesus, making him greater than all the Old Testament prophets. And everyone after John is greater than him because we see Jesus's death and resurrection—the actual fulfillment of all the promises.

We know more than the ancients:

- They didn't know His name, but we know Jesus.
- They didn't know when He would come, but we know and celebrate His birth every year.
- They didn't know how He would save the world, but we do. We see the incredible solution: God's justice required punishment for our sin, but His mercy offers forgiveness. The answer? **Jesus takes the punishment for us.**

They saw the exam questions. We have seen the answer key. How can we say it's harder when we've been given the answers?

### The Example of Eric Liddell

Some of you may know the story of Eric Liddell. One hundred years ago at the 1924 Paris Olympics, this 22-year-old Scotsman was the favorite to win the 100-meter race. But then came disaster: the Olympic heats were scheduled for Sunday.

Eric was a Christian who believed Sunday belonged to the Lord. Despite intense pressure—officials, media, his country calling him a traitor—he forfeited his best chance of winning gold. Why? He had faith.

He didn't win the 100m, but he switched to the 400-meter race, which had a Sunday-friendly schedule. On July 11, 1924, Eric Liddell won gold in the 400m in one of the greatest Olympic moments in history. After the Olympics, he became a missionary in China, dedicating his life to service.

When asked about his racing strategy, Liddell said:

> When the gun goes, I go as fast as I can, and I trust to God that I'll have the strength to do the second half.

He ran the Christian race the same way—with faith in God.

### Abraham's Example Continued

The passage continues with more examples throughout biblical history:

> By faith Abraham, when God tested him, offered Isaac as a sacrifice. He who had embraced the promises was about to sacrifice his one and only son, even though God had said to him, "It is through Isaac that your offspring will be reckoned." Abraham reasoned that God could even raise the dead, and so in a manner of speaking he did receive Isaac back from death. (Hebrews 11:17-19)

By faith Isaac blessed Jacob and Esau in regard to their future. By faith Jacob, when he was dying, blessed each of Joseph's sons. By faith Joseph, when his end was near, spoke about the exodus of the Israelites from Egypt and gave instructions concerning the burial of his bones. (Hebrews 11:20-22)

Joseph gave instructions to take his bones to the promised land—a land he would never see in his lifetime, yet he believed God's promise enough to provide for its fulfillment.

### The Path of Obedience

Whether experiencing victory or suffering, all these believers shared one thing:

> By faith the people passed through the Red Sea as on dry land; but when the Egyptians tried to do so, they were drowned. By faith the walls of Jericho fell, after the army had marched around them for seven days. By faith the prostitute Rahab, because she welcomed the spies, was not killed with those who were disobedient. (Hebrews 11:29-31)

And many suffered:

> There were others who were tortured, refusing to be released so that they might gain an even better resurrection. Some faced jeers and flogging, and even chains and imprisonment. They were put to death by stoning; they were sawed in two; they were killed by the sword. They went about in sheepskins and goatskins, destitute, persecuted and mistreated—the world was not worthy of them. They wandered in deserts and mountains, living in caves and in holes in the ground. (Hebrews 11:35-38)

Whether triumphant or suffering, they were commended for their faith. Even though they did not receive what had been promised, they trusted God's word.

## The Call to Faith Today

### We Cannot Wait for Complete Certainty

Faith requires action without 100% certainty. The future remains unseen. But waiting for absolute proof means waiting forever. You don't half-marry someone because you only know half her life. You don't commit 50% to a relationship due to lingering worries. When sufficient evidence is present, you commit fully.

The same principle applies to getting on a plane. You cannot half board. Once you have enough evidence to believe, you act completely.

Consider a mustard seed of belief—yet act like a great tree.

### The Evidence We Have Is Sufficient

We have the Bible—the most tested document in history. Christianity is the most tested belief system. Books and websites scrutinize it. Free speech allows the church to be criticized first. Yet the evidence accumulates and holds firm.

After two thousand years of scrutiny, God cannot prove His love more than He has. He gave His Son. What more could He offer?

### When You Don't "Feel" It

Sometimes people say "I don't have faith" even when evidence mounts. Often they mean "I don't feel it" or "I don't have the chemistry." That's not an evidence problem. That might be a heart problem:

- Unfaith or disbelief
- Rebellion against God
- Anger, fear, or anxiety clouding judgment
- Emotional resistance rather than logical objection
- Past hurt from a Christian who failed you (remember, we follow Jesus, not Christians)
- Family conflict leading to rejection of their faith
- Love of sin—preferring the short-term freedom it offers over accountability to God
- Subjective bias, loyalty to prior beliefs, or unwillingness to admit being wrong
- Unresolved trauma

Making decisions based on feelings rather than evidence is dangerous. It leads to poor choices in marriage, jobs, money, and investments. Trusting your heart over your head invites failure.

### The Challenge to Abraham's Heirs

So if the ancients were willing to trust God without knowing the outcome, how much more should we? We have better information, clearer evidence, and fulfilled promises before us.

The Hebrew Christians facing persecution needed to hear this: You have not yet resisted to the point of shedding your blood. (Hebrews 12:4) 

Is faith hard? Yes. But the ancients endured greater hardship with less evidence. They didn't have the answer key. You do. They didn't know Jesus's name. You do. They didn't see His resurrection. You do.

The standard is not perfection or emotional certainty. The standard is: Do you have sufficient evidence? If yes, then act in faith, trusting the God who has proven faithful throughout history.

**Don't take this for granted. You will miss out. Have faith in God instead.**
