# Romans 3:1-26

**Title:** The Faithfulness of God
**Date:** November 18, 2012
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Family Precedence and God's Order

In families, older siblings often set precedence for younger ones. If the elder can't drive until nineteen, neither can the younger. It's about consistency and fairness, but also about protection—younger siblings can't complain they're being treated unfairly if the older sibling received the same restrictions.

This same pattern appears throughout God's salvation history. A phrase repeats constantly in Romans' early chapters: "First the Jew, then the Gentile."

> For I am not ashamed of the gospel, for it is the power of God for salvation to everyone who believes, to the Jew first and also to the Greek. (Romans 1:16)

> There will be tribulation and distress for every human being who does evil, the Jew first and also the Greek, but glory and honor and peace for everyone who does good, the Jew first and also the Greek. (Romans 2:9-10)

For nearly two thousand years before Jesus came, God chose the Jews first. He called Abraham, made promises to his nation, and chose Israel among all the great nations. Israel is the "older sibling." But God also promised that through this chosen nation, all other nations—the Gentiles—would be blessed.

Yet we face a problem. By the end of Romans 2, we've discovered there were no truly faithful Jews. They didn't follow God, so they didn't receive the blessing. And if they didn't receive it, how can the Gentiles? The older sister refused her responsibility, leaving the younger sister in limbo.

## The Problem: Jews Forfeited Their Advantage

> What if some were unfaithful? Does their faithlessness nullify the faithfulness of God? (Romans 3:3)

Paul first establishes what advantage the Jews actually had:

> Then what advantage has the Jew? Or what is the value of circumcision? Much in every way. To begin with, the Jews were entrusted with the oracles of God. (Romans 3:1-2)

> He declares his word to Jacob, his statutes and rules to Israel. He has not dealt thus with any other nation; they do not know his rules. (Psalm 147:19-20)

The Jews possessed a genuine advantage—they received God's words first. But they lost this privilege because:

**They failed to teach the law.** God's words are universal because God himself is universal—not a local deity but Creator and Lord of all creation. The law was meant to be shared with the Gentiles, yet the Jews blasphemed God's name instead.

**They disobeyed what they knew.** Although they possessed the law, they didn't practice it. Rather than spreading this treasure, they desecrated it. The Gentiles saw Jewish hypocrisy and wanted nothing to do with God's law.

Being first is good—you have the advantage of knowledge. But this advantage wasn't meant to be hoarded. It was meant to be shared, just as the good news spreads from Jew to Gentile.

### Application for Today

Some of us stand in a similar position regarding the gospel. Perhaps you come from a non-Christian family and you're the first to know the truth—your siblings' "older brother" spiritually. Are you using your advantage to share the gospel with them? The gospel comes to you not just for your benefit, but for those around you.

Ask yourself: Does my life show evidence that God is transforming me into Christ's likeness? Or am I bashing up the gospel with my sin? Don't follow the ancient Jews' example of unfaithfulness.

## God's Faithfulness Remains Unshaken

Despite Jewish failure, Paul makes a stunning declaration:

> By no means! Let God be true though every one were a liar, as it is written, "That you may be justified in your words, and prevail when you are judged." (Romans 3:4)

God is absolute truthfulness. While humans have relative standards for truth—white lies, omissions, flattery—God's faithfulness is complete and unwavering. He doesn't give up when humans fail, as we do. He remains faithful to all his promises.

His faithfulness is inseparable from his righteous judgment. God has no double standards, no arbitrariness. He judges righteously precisely because he is faithful to his word. King David understood this when confessing his adultery:

> Against you, you only, have I sinned and done what is evil in your sight, so that you may be justified in your words and blameless in your judgment. (Psalm 51:4)

## The Dangerous Question: Can We Sin So Grace Abounds?

Some might think: If God is faithful no matter what, and our unfaithfulness doesn't nullify his faithfulness, then surely we can do whatever we want. God will work good from it anyway—an open blank check.

Paul anticipates three forms of this objection:

- If our unrighteousness demonstrates God's righteousness, isn't it unfair for God to judge us?
- If my lie showcases God's truth to his glory, why am I still condemned as a sinner?
- Why shouldn't we do evil so good may come?

His answer is unmistakable:

> By no means! For then how could God judge the world? (Romans 3:6)

Just because God is faithful doesn't mean our actions don't matter. God judges not by end results alone, but by the process. Yes, God can take sin and transform it to gold. But yes, he will still judge.

It's like a parent promising their child a great reward for tutoring a younger sibling who eventually gets top marks—yet the older child was lazy, never actually tutored, and constantly distracted the younger one. The top mark doesn't validate the poor tutoring. The end result doesn't excuse the negligence.

## Everyone Is Under Sin

With this settled, Paul delivers the verdict:

> Are we Jews any better off? No, not at all. For we have already charged that all, both Jews and Greeks, are under sin. (Romans 3:9)

To prove this universality, he strings together Old Testament passages:

> None is righteous, no, not one; no one understands; no one seeks for God. All have turned aside; together they have become worthless; no one does good, not even one. (Romans 3:10-12)

The passages continue, describing sin's corruption through every part of our bodies—throat, tongue, lips, feet, eyes. All of us, every part of us, participate in sin.

What's striking is that Paul uses passages originally describing pagan atheists to characterize the Jews. The fool says in his heart, "There is no God," and the Lord looks down to see if anyone seeks him—yet Paul applies these to Israel. The older sibling has descended to the level of the younger. Both are now under the law's judgment.

> Now we know that whatever the law says it speaks to those who are under the law, so that every mouth may be stopped, and the whole world may be held accountable to God. For by works of the law no human being will be justified in his sight, since through the law comes knowledge of sin. (Romans 3:19-20)

### Application: We Are Forgiven, Not Sinless

Christians often misunderstand what following Jesus means. We must be clear: Christians are forgiven, not sinless. We've received mercy that others haven't—yet. The hope isn't that we become perfect, but that God's judgment is not upon us. We get another day when we won't sin because Christ has already borne that judgment.

## God's Solution: The True Jew

Yet despite Jewish failure, God remains faithful to his promises. The Gentiles are not abandoned. Here's how God resolves it:

> But now the righteousness of God has been manifested apart from the law, although the Law and the Prophets bear witness to it—the righteousness of God through faith in Jesus Christ for all who believe. (Romans 3:21-22)

Focus on verse 22: "the righteousness of God through **faith of** Jesus Christ." This can be read as the faithfulness of Jesus—that it is the faithful Jesus who saves us.

In the absence of a righteous Jew to lead the way, God provides the true Jew. This true Jew taught the true way. This true Jew did not blaspheme God's name before the Gentiles. Jesus was the faithful Jew who obeyed God in both deed and heart:

> A Jew is one inwardly, and circumcision is a matter of the heart, by the Spirit, not by the letter. His praise is not from man but from God. (Romans 2:29)

Notice the wordplay: "Judah" (Yehudah) connects to "praise" (Yahweh and yada). Judah means "praise of Yahweh." Jesus—the true Jew—is this praise, this one whose praise comes from God, not man.

So when Paul says the righteousness of God is now "manifested," it's the same word used in Romans 1:19—God's nature revealed so clearly that no one has excuse. Just as Adam and Israel knew God's law, now all know God through the revelation of Christ. And this manifestation is "apart from the law."

This doesn't mean going above and beyond the law, adding extra works. It means someone else altogether replaces the law. Jesus takes the law's place.

## The Sacrifice and Our Response

What did Jesus accomplish?

> For there is no distinction: for all have sinned and fall short of the glory of God, and are justified by his grace as a gift, through the redemption that is in Christ Jesus, whom God put forward as a mercy seat by his blood, to be received by faith. This was to show God's righteousness, because in his divine forbearance he had passed over former sins. It was to show his righteousness at the present time, so that he might be just and the justifier of the one who has faith in Jesus. (Romans 3:22-26)

The "mercy seat" recalls the Old Testament—the temple's holy of holies, the ark of the covenant, the place where God dwelt. There, priests would sacrifice and sprinkle blood on the ark. For centuries this ritual continued, yet sin remained unchanged, even worsened.

Finally came Jesus—the ultimate sacrifice, the true mercy seat. His blood redeems us. God's justice is satisfied, and he can now justify those who believe.

### What Is Your Response?

Friends, if you believe that we are equally sinful before God, that we are liars compared to God's absolute truth, that we cannot go above and beyond the law to earn righteousness—if you believe this, I want you to act.

If you are not a Christian, today is the day to go to God and plead for his mercy. God will answer you because Jesus has died for you.

Our solution to sin is more laws, more legislation, more regulations, another election. But when God fixes the world, he didn't send more laws. He sent a person—Jesus. He became the sacrifice for our sins and provides righteousness apart from the law.

Let us follow our older brother, Jesus.
