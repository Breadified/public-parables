# 2 Peter 3:3-13

**Title:** God's Perspective on Time  
**Date:** September 16, 2011  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Our Perspective: Time Feels Long

We often misjudge how much time has passed. Here's a humorous Irish exchange that captures this perfectly:

> Mrs. Murphy: "I hear your brother-in-law, Pat, is pretty bad off."
>
> Mrs. Casey: "Nah! He's good for a long time—he's got years."
>
> Mrs. Murphy: "As long as that?"
>
> Mrs. Casey: "Yes; he's had four different doctors, and each one of them have given him three months to live."

The point is simple: time can feel remarkably long to us. This perception especially affects how we think about Jesus's promised return.

### The Scoffers' Argument (verse 4)

> They will say, "Where is this 'coming' he promised? Ever since our fathers died, everything goes on as it has since the beginning of creation."

The scoffers in Peter's day were mocking Christians for believing in Christ's return. Their argument was straightforward: centuries have passed, our ancestors have died, yet nothing has changed. The world continues on as it always has. This is historically true—two thousand years have elapsed since Peter wrote these words, and the world still stands.

This perception of time's length is amplified in modern life. Australians enjoy a life expectancy of about 81 years (compared to roughly 40 years in parts of Africa). This extended lifespan allows us to pack multiple careers, international travels, and varied experiences into a single lifetime. No wonder time feels so long to us. We can fit so much into it.

## God's Perspective: Time Is Short

Yet Scripture presents a radically different understanding of time.

### Time in God's Eyes (verse 8)

> But do not forget this one thing, dear friends: With the Lord a day is like a thousand years, and a thousand years are like a day.

In God's eternal perspective, a thousand years is simply a brief moment—like a day to us. The passage uses this comparison not as literal mathematics but as a way to recalibrate our thinking about time. From God's vantage point, the entire period since Christ's ascension—all two thousand years—is nothing. It's merely a few seconds in eternity.

### The Pattern God Has Established (verses 5-7)

> But they deliberately forget that long ago by God's word the heavens existed and the earth was formed out of water and by water. By these waters also the world of that time was deluged and destroyed. By the same word the present heavens and earth are reserved for fire, being kept for the day of judgment and destruction of ungodly men.

God created the world by a word. Just as easily, by a word alone, He can unmake it and return it to the formless chaos that existed before creation—that deep darkness with the Spirit of God hovering over the waters (Genesis 1:2).

God has already done this once. In Noah's day, there were plenty of scoffers who mocked the coming judgment. But the flood came. The same power that destroyed the ancient world by water now reserves the present heavens and earth for fire. This destiny is sealed, reserved, and inescapable because of human sin.

### The Sudden Nature of Christ's Return (verse 10)

> But the day of the Lord will come like a thief. The heavens will disappear with a roar; the elements will be destroyed by fire, and the earth and everything in it will be laid bare.

Though we may live longer than previous generations, we live in a high-risk zone. Natural disasters like the Maui fires remind us that no life insurance policy can protect against sudden judgment. When the day of the Lord comes, it will arrive without warning—in an instant, as if God merely snapped His fingers. In that sense, time is terrifyingly short. We exist on borrowed time in a world that runs on God's clockwork, not our own.

## The Gift: Time for Repentance (verse 9)

> The Lord is not slow in keeping his promise, as some understand slowness. He is patient with you, not wanting anyone to perish, but everyone to come to repentance.

While judgment is certain and complete and final, God has given us extra time. He is deliberately delaying His judgment out of patience and mercy.

Two thousand years ago, Jesus came to rescue His people. He took upon Himself all our wickedness and sin, and He paid for it through His death on the cross. For the last two millennia, God has spent His time convincing us to follow Jesus. He is patient, waiting for just one more, then a few more—waiting for that final elect person whom He has chosen to walk through the door of faith.

This is the remarkable position we're in. Some of us are grateful for relatives who have been given time to repent and believe. But here's the crucial truth: you too have been given that time. It's not too late.

## Living in Light of God's Timing (verses 11-12)

> Since everything will be destroyed in this way, what kind of people ought you to be? You ought to live holy and godly lives as you look forward to the day of God and speed its coming.

The brevity of time in God's eyes and the certainty of coming judgment should transform how we live now. We are called to holy and godly living—not out of fear, but out of longing for that day when Christ returns and all things are made new.
