# Luke 11:1-13

**Title:** Go Ask!!  
**Date:** October 29, 2006  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction

Jesus teaches His disciples how to pray in this passage, and several important questions emerge when we first encounter it:

- How is it that the disciples didn't know how to pray? Didn't people pray before Jesus? What new understanding does He bring?
- What should we actually be praying for?
- Does Jesus really encourage us to nag God?
- And does He truly promise that we can ask for anything and receive it?

To navigate these questions, we'll explore three foundational truths: we ask the Father, we ask for the kingdom to come, and we simply ask.

## 1. We Ask the Father

Rather than using the formal term "prayer," it's helpful to think of it simply as asking. Prayer, at its heart, is just asking someone for something. And the first question we must answer is: *who* are we asking?

> **Luke 11:1-2** One day Jesus was praying in a certain place. When he finished, one of his disciples said to him, "Lord, teach us to pray, just as John taught his disciples." He said to them, "When you pray, say: 'Father...'"

Jesus emphasizes that we ask **the Father**. This point is reinforced later:

> **Luke 11:11-13** "Which of you fathers, if your son asks for a fish, will give him a snake instead? Or if he asks for an egg, will give him a scorpion? If you then, though you are evil, know how to give good gifts to your children, how much more will your Father in heaven give the Holy Spirit to those who ask him!"

### Understanding God as Father

How we view God determines how we pray. Consider these different conceptions:

- **A god to be bribed:** We make deals and try to buy favor through ritual offerings
- **A distant dictator:** We approach in fear with elaborate, flattering language
- **An impersonal force:** We meditate or contemplate rather than ask
- **A weak god:** We believe we must help Him accomplish His will
- **An elderly grandparent:** Distant and slow to respond, requiring constant nagging

By contrast, Jesus teaches us that God is **Father**—and this changes everything.

### The Character of Our Father

The Father in heaven is:

- **Good** (11:11-13): He is not malicious. He won't deceive us or give us something harmful when we ask for something good
- **Wise and knowing**: He understands what His children truly need. He cannot be fooled or manipulated
- **Not coerced or blackmailed**: His gifts flow from His nature, not from our pressure or performance
- **Close like a friend** (11:5): We have access to Him at any time, even in the middle of the night

### Access Through Jesus

But here's the crucial question: Why would God listen to our prayers at all? What gives us access to the Father?

The answer lies in what Paul describes in Romans 8:

> **Romans 8:14-17** "Those who are led by the Spirit of God are sons of God. For you did not receive a spirit that makes you a slave again to fear, but you received the Spirit of sonship. And by him we cry, 'Abba, Father.' The Spirit himself testifies with our spirit that we are God's children. Now if we are children, then we are heirs—heirs of God and co-heirs with Christ..."

We have access because God has justified us through Jesus. We were once under judgment, condemned and guilty. But because Christ paid the penalty for our sins, we are now free from condemnation. We are no longer aliens before God—we are His children. Before Christ, God could not hear our prayers because of our status. Since Christ, we have direct access to the Father.

### Practical Application: Who Do You Pray To?

Two questions for reflection:

**1. To whom do you address your prayers?** The New Testament shows that while we can pray to Jesus or the Spirit, the majority of prayers are directed to the Father. As a practice, when you pray, address God as Father. This is a reminder of your true relationship with Him. You don't need to use flowery language, make deals, or perform elaborate rituals. He hears you clearly because of Jesus.

**2. What is your view of God?** Do you see Him as a loving Father who knows what is best for you, or as a cruel tyrant bent on destruction? When prayers go unanswered—when you don't receive the exam mark you wanted or the relationship you hoped for—do you blame God, or can you trust that He knows best and pray, "Blessed be the Lord"?

Our God is Father, and we are His children.

## 2. Ask for the Kingdom to Come

Now that we understand *who* we're asking, the next question is: *what* should we ask for?

The passage mentions many things we might pray for: food, forgiveness, deliverance from temptation, and in verse 9 Jesus seems to promise that we can ask for anything:

> **Luke 11:9-10** "So I say to you: Ask and it will be given to you; seek and you will find; knock and the door will be opened to you. For everyone who asks receives; he who seeks finds; and to him who knocks, the door will be opened."

### The Prosperity Gospel Misinterpretation

Some people take these verses to mean that God promises to grant every request. They teach that if you want the latest luxury item or a high-end car, you simply need to ask sincerely enough. If you don't have it, either you haven't asked properly or you lack enough faith. "Name it and claim it," they say, because God wants to make you happy.

But this reading makes God sound like an indulgent parent, and it misses the context entirely. **The context of Jesus' promise is the kingdom of God.** We are asking for the kingdom.

### Understanding God's Kingdom

Jesus begins the prayer itself with these words:

> **Luke 11:2** "When you pray, say: 'Father, hallowed be your name, your kingdom come.'"

The kingdom is central to everything Jesus teaches. Let's trace its biblical history:

- **Abraham:** God promises Abraham a land
- **Exodus:** God delivers Israel to the promised land, but they need a king to govern it
- **Monarchy:** When a king rules over a land, that is a kingdom. God promises David an everlasting kingdom
- **Exile:** Israel loses the land; the king is carried away; the kingdom seems finished
- **The Prophets:** God sends prophets to announce that though the old kingdom has fallen, a new kingdom will come
- **Waiting:** The Jewish people wait through the centuries. By the first century, they're still waiting—not a kingdom, but Roman rule under Caesar
- **Jesus Arrives:** The angel Gabriel announces to Mary who will bear her son:

> **Luke 1:32-33** "He will be great and will be called the Son of the Most High. The Lord God will give him the throne of his father David, and he will reign over the house of Jacob forever; his kingdom will never end."

Jesus Himself declares that the kingdom is near (Luke 10:9). He comes to bring the kingdom of God.

### The Whole Prayer Is About the Kingdom

This understanding transforms how we read the entire prayer. When Jesus teaches us to pray for "daily bread" (Luke 11:3), the original language suggests something deeper: "Give us today tomorrow's bread." This points not merely to today's meal but to the bread of eternal life—the bread of the kingdom to come (John 6).

Similarly, when we pray for forgiveness (Luke 11:4), we're praying for what the kingdom brings: the end of sin and its consequences through God's forgiveness. When the kingdom comes, there is no more sickness because there is no more sin.

Even the promise about the Holy Spirit takes on new meaning:

> **Luke 11:13** "If you then, though you are evil, know how to give good gifts to your children, how much more will your Father in heaven give the Holy Spirit to those who ask him!"

Some misinterpret this as asking for miraculous power to "claim" blessings. But in this context, the Spirit is the Spirit of the age of forgiveness and restoration—the Spirit who brings people into right relationship with God. To ask for the Holy Spirit is to ask to become a Christian, to enter the kingdom.

### Two Dimensions of the Kingdom's Coming

The kingdom comes in two ways:

1. **The Final Coming:** Jesus will return, heaven and earth will be destroyed and renewed, and everything will be transformed
2. **The Present Coming:** Through His promise, we believe and obey Him as King in our lives now

### Prayer Application: What Do You Actually Pray For?

Consider your own prayer life honestly:

- Do you pray for exams, assignments, health, and peace in the world? Yes, these are legitimate prayers for today's bread
- But do you pray for tomorrow's bread? Do you pray that the kingdom will come?
- Do you ask the Father for your friends, family, and even strangers to submit to Christ as King?
- Do you long for the new earth, or does this old earth seem too comfortable?
- What occupies the center of your prayers? What reveals what you truly value?

As we mature in Christ, our prayer life should mature with us. Our requests should reflect a reorienting of our priorities. Instead of being preoccupied with the things of this world, we become increasingly concerned with the things of the kingdom to come.

## 3. Ask

This brings us to our third point: simply **ask**. Jesus wants us to pray, to ask, to seek, to knock—even to persist like someone urgently appealing to a friend at midnight.

> **Luke 11:5-10** "Suppose one of you has a friend, and he goes to him at midnight and says, 'Friend, lend me three loaves of bread, because a friend of mine on a journey has come to me, and I have nothing to set before him.' Then the one inside answers, 'Don't bother me. The door is already locked, and my children are with me in bed. I can't get up and give you anything.' I tell you, though he will not get up and give him the bread because he is his friend, yet because of the man's boldness he will get up and give him as much as he needs. So I say to you: Ask and it will be given to you; seek and you will find; knock and the door will be opened to you. For everyone who asks receives; he who seeks finds; and to him who knocks, the door will be opened."

### The Cultural Context

In a Mediterranean village, sharing food was a matter of honor and hospitality. Bread was baked fresh daily, not stored in shops. For the householder, being asked to help a friend—even at an inconvenient hour—was a matter of community responsibility. The friend's boldness in asking, his persistence despite the inconvenience, is what moves the householder to respond.

### A Warning About Misapplied Prayer

There's a popular teaching called "The Prayer of Jabez" that has circulated for decades. It encourages people to pray repeatedly for God to bless them, enlarge their territory, and make their businesses prosper—repeating these requests daily until God answers. Some books and songs promote this approach.

But this prayer misunderstands Scripture on several counts:

**1. It's not about us:** Jabez's prayer (1 Chronicles 4:9) was about inheriting the land promised to Abraham. But we cannot simply take an Old Testament prayer about territorial inheritance and apply it to ourselves out of context.

**2. It points to Jesus:** All of God's promises to Israel in the Old Testament find their fulfillment in Christ. The blessings given to Israel are shadows of the greater blessings to come through Jesus.

**3. Blessings are spiritual, not material:** God's primary blessing in this age is not material prosperity but forgiveness and right relationship with Him.

**4. Chanting mantras is superstition:** Repeating a prayer formula daily as though the repetition itself compels God to act treats prayer like magic or superstition.

### What We Should Actually Be Asking For

Rather, Jesus teaches us to ask for the kingdom:

- Ask for God's name to be honored and vindicated in the world
- Ask for Jesus to return
- Ask for people to submit to Christ as their King
- Ask for forgiveness and restoration
- Ask for the Holy Spirit to work in hearts

For these prayers, you can *never* ask enough. You can always "nag" God about the kingdom. It's like asking your parents if you can study—of course! Asking if you can serve and help—of course!

### Why We Don't Ask

And yet, many of us rarely ask for the kingdom to come. Why?

**We're not desperate enough:** We don't see much suffering in our own lives. We might not witness the spiritual perishing of our non-Christian friends—perhaps because we don't spend time with them, or perhaps because they seem to be doing fine materially. We can become apathetic, thinking we can't change things anyway, so why bother?

**We're confused about God's will:** We ask God for guidance about which course to take, which job to accept, which relationship to pursue. But His will is clear: **His kingdom to come.** This is God's unambiguous will. When someone asks what God's will is, the answer is always: for His kingdom to come. And you can align yourself with that will through your prayers.

### The Certainty of the Answer

But here's what should drive us to pray this prayer: **You know you will receive a positive answer.** Jesus will definitely return. The kingdom will definitely come.

If you are a Christian, keep praying for the kingdom. Pray for God to return. Pray for His will to be done on earth as it is in heaven.

If you are not yet a Christian, this is what Jesus offers you:

> **Luke 11:9-10** "Ask and it will be given to you; seek and you will find; knock and the door will be opened to you. For everyone who asks receives; he who seeks finds; and to him who knocks, the door will be opened."

Seek the Holy Spirit. Ask to enter the kingdom.

### A Vision for Our Prayer Life

What if, over the months and years ahead, as we grow in love for one another, we also grew in longing for the kingdom to come? What if we began to see the desperate condition of this world and recognized that what it truly needs is a complete transformation—that this world must be destroyed so that a new world can arise?

What if our prayers increasingly focused on Jesus returning and on bringing as many people as possible into His kingdom while we wait?

I hope that as we break into prayer groups, you'll begin practicing this more intentionally. Let's pray together now, using Luke 11 as our guide:

> **Luke 11:1-4** "One day Jesus was praying in a certain place. When he finished, one of his disciples said to him, 'Lord, teach us to pray, just as John taught his disciples.' He said to them, 'When you pray, say: Father, hallowed be your name, your kingdom come. Give us each day our daily bread. Forgive us our sins, for we also forgive everyone who sins against us. And lead us not into temptation.'"
