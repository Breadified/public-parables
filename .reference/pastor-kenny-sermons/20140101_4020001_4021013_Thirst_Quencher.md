# Numbers 20:1–21:13

**Title:** Thirst Quencher
**Date:** 2014
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## A Strange Situation (20:1-10)

The Israelites face another crisis of thirst in the desert. But this moment marks a dramatic turning point in their spiritual journey—and in how God deals with His people.

> Now there was no water for the community, and the people gathered in opposition to Moses and Aaron. They quarreled with Moses and said, "If only we had died when our brothers fell dead before the LORD! Why did you bring the LORD's community into this desert, that we and our livestock should die here? Why did you bring us up out of Egypt to this terrible place? It has no grain or figs, grapevines or pomegranates. And there is no water to drink!" (Numbers 20:2-5)

This grumbling echoes a familiar pattern throughout their wilderness journey. From the beginning, the Israelites have complained repeatedly—a pattern so consistent it led to judgment. Their lack of faith at Kadesh forty years earlier resulted in a death sentence: the entire generation would wander in the desert until they died, and only their children would enter the promised land.

Now, at the close of that forty-year period, with a new generation ready to cross into Canaan, the same thirst returns. Following the established pattern, we would expect judgment. Instead, God responds with surprising mercy.

> Moses and Aaron went from the assembly to the entrance to the Tent of Meeting and fell facedown, and the glory of the LORD appeared to them. The LORD said to Moses, "Take the staff, and you and your brother Aaron gather the assembly together. Speak to that rock before their eyes and it will pour out its water." (Numbers 20:6-8)

## A New Leader Needed (20:9-12)

Moses takes the staff and gathers the people, but something goes wrong:

> He and Aaron gathered the assembly together in front of the rock and Moses said to them, "Listen, you rebels, must we bring you water out of this rock?" Then Moses raised his arm and struck the rock twice with his staff. Water gushed out, and the community and their livestock drank. (Numbers 20:10-11)

God's instruction was clear: speak to the rock. Instead, Moses spoke to the people. Why does this matter?

The rock symbolizes God Himself—He is our refuge and strength. When God commands Moses to speak to the rock, He's inviting an act of humility, a petition directed toward God rather than a rebuke directed at the people. Instead, Moses strikes the rock in anger, his words sharp with judgment: "Listen, you rebels."

This reveals Moses's true transgression:

> But the LORD said to Moses and Aaron, "Because you did not trust in me enough to honor me as holy in the sight of the Israelites, you will not bring this community into the land I give them." (Numbers 20:12)

Moses lacked faith. He approached the situation with his own frustration rather than trusting God's method. When he raised his arm to strike, he acted with defiance—a "high hand" against God. The Hebrew phrase describes rebellious action, the kind of uprising against legitimate authority. Moses treated the people with unnecessary judgment when God was dealing with them differently, with mercy for a new generation.

The consequences are severe. Along with Aaron, Moses forfeits the privilege of leading Israel into the promised land. Both leaders—who have sustained the people through their wilderness trials—must pass the mantle to a new generation.

> Miriam died and was buried. (Numbers 20:1)

Later, Aaron ascends Mount Hor and dies there, his priestly garments passing to his son Eleazar. The old leadership exits the stage, making way for what comes next.

## A New Start (20:1, 33:36-39)

This passage marks the fortieth year. We're back at Kadesh, where the judgment was pronounced forty years earlier. The entire generation that refused to enter Canaan has now passed away. What God promised has come to pass.

The new generation faces the same thirst their parents knew—but God treats them differently. Instead of judgment following their complaint, God provides. It's as though God breaks the cycle of sin and punishment and offers a new way forward.

Think of returning to your parents' homeland after many years. They take you to the places they knew as children, showing you where they played, where they lived, what shaped them. They want you to walk in their shoes so you understand their experience. But there's a crucial difference—they also protect you from the suffering they endured. Your father experienced bullying at his old school, so he shows you the school, but he doesn't expect you to be bullied.

God does something similar here. The new generation shares the experience of thirst that tested their parents' faith. But God's response is not judgment—it's provision.

## A New Forgiveness (21:4-9)

The promise of a new way is tested almost immediately:

> They traveled from Mount Hor along the route to the Red Sea, to go around Edom. But the people grew impatient on the way; they spoke against God and against Moses, and said, "Why have you brought us up out of Egypt to die in the desert? There is no bread! There is no water! And we detest this miserable food!" (Numbers 21:4-5)

The same grumbling resurfaces. But again, God's response differs from the past. Where judgment once came swiftly, now God offers a way of salvation:

> Then the LORD sent venomous snakes among them; they bit the people and many Israelites died. The people came to Moses and said, "We sinned when we spoke against the LORD and against you. Pray that the LORD will take the snakes away from us." So Moses prayed for the people. The LORD said to Moses, "Make a snake and put it up on a pole; anyone who is bitten can look at it and live." So Moses made a bronze snake and put it up on a pole. Then when anyone was bitten by a snake and looked at the bronze snake, he lived. (Numbers 21:6-9)

Notice that this solution doesn't come through Moses's intercession alone. Instead, God provides a symbol—the bronze serpent on a pole—that offers healing to anyone who looks upon it. It's a way of salvation that doesn't depend on the intercessor being perfect.

Why a snake? The serpent recalls Eden, where sin entered humanity. A dead snake—defeated and lifted up—symbolizes sin conquered. But this image points forward to something greater.

> Just as Moses lifted up the snake in the desert, so the Son of Man must be lifted up, that everyone who believes in him may have eternal life. (John 3:14-15)

Centuries later, Jesus identifies Himself with this image. He would be lifted up on a cross—not as a threat, but as salvation. Just as the bitten Israelites looked upon the bronze serpent to live, we look to the cross and find eternal life.

This is the pattern breaking through the Old Testament toward the New: a new way of dealing with God's people. Not judgment for the failures of leaders, but salvation through faith in what God provides.

## A New Beginning—Restored Victory (21:1-3, 20:14-21)

The change extends beyond spiritual mercy to military success. The new generation experiences what the old generation could not:

> When the Canaanite king of Arad, who lived in the Negev, heard that Israel was coming along the road to Atharim, he attacked the Israelites and captured some of them. Then Israel made this vow to the LORD: "If you will deliver these people into our hands, we will totally destroy their cities." The LORD listened to Israel's plea and gave the Canaanites over to them. They completely destroyed them and their towns; so the place was named Hormah. (Numbers 21:1-3)

Compare this to the earlier generation's attempt to enter Canaan:

> Then the Amalekites and Canaanites who lived in that hill country came down and attacked them and beat them down all the way to Hormah. (Numbers 14:45)

The old generation was defeated. The new generation is victorious. The trajectory shifts unmistakably toward the promised land. Where once Israel experienced failure and exile, now they experience success and forward movement.

Even when Edom refuses them passage—a rejection that echoes ancient family wounds—God provides an alternative route. The narrative moves them steadily closer to Jordan's banks, where Jericho comes into view.

## The Deeper Pattern: Recapitulation

What's remarkable is that God doesn't simply move on from the old generation's failures. Instead, He brings the new generation through similar trials—thirst, grumbling, testing. They're meant to walk in their parents' shoes, to understand what was tested in that generation.

But here's what makes it "new": the outcomes differ. The new generation receives water where their parents received judgment. They receive salvation through the bronze serpent where previous judgment came swiftly. They experience military victory where their parents knew only defeat.

This pattern—walking through similar circumstances but with different outcomes—reflects something the New Testament calls recapitulation. Paul writes:

> Consequently, just as the result of one trespass was condemnation for all men, so also the result of one act of righteousness was justification that brings life for all men. For just as through the disobedience of the one man the many were made sinners, so also through the obedience of the one man the many will be made righteous. (Romans 5:18-19)

Adam sinned, and humanity inherited condemnation. Jesus came as a new Adam, living the life we should have lived. He was tested in the wilderness but did not grumble. He faced suffering but maintained perfect faith. He died—but His death, unlike ours, had power to save.

Jesus had to be fully human to fully recapitulate the human experience. He was born, grew through childhood and adolescence, learned to read and write, matured into adulthood, faced betrayal and suffering, and died. But He did all of this perfectly, without sin. Because He lived righteously where we failed, His life became the basis for our salvation. He walked the path we couldn't walk and now offers us the benefit of His perfect obedience.

## Application

**For those without Christ:** Look to the cross. Just as the Israelites looked to the bronze serpent for healing, we look to Jesus lifted up for eternal life. The snake that was lifted saved those who were dying from the bite of sin. The cross that lifted Jesus saves all who believe.

**For believers:** God deals with us differently than we deserve. Like the new generation of Israel, we inherit a new way of relating to God—not through fear of judgment, but through the grace of salvation. Our old selves, with their patterns of rebellion and self-reliance, have been crucified with Christ. We're freed to live in a new way, walking in righteousness not through our own strength, but through faith in what God has provided.

The wilderness thirst never returns because the water flows from the rock. The generational curse is broken. The old leadership—imperfect, prone to anger, unable to fully trust—gives way to new leaders. And ultimately, all of this points to the one Leader who never fails, whose faithfulness is perfect, and through whom God offers mercy to all who believe.
