# 1 Corinthians 9:19–10:13

**Title:** A Church of Athletes
**Date:** 15 May 2016
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Principle: Becoming All Things to Win Some

> Though I am free and belong to no one, I have made myself a slave to everyone, to win as many as possible. (9:19)

Paul begins with a paradox. He possesses genuine freedom—Roman citizenship with its attendant rights and privileges—yet he willingly surrenders this freedom to become a servant. This is not weakness but strategic choice. Just as elite athletes like Lance Armstrong calculate every gram of food consumed, accounting for season, intensity, and recovery needs, Paul calculates every relational decision with a singular aim: to win people to Christ.

The athlete's mentality is instructive. Those competing in the Tour de France restrict carbohydrates in winter, increase them during racing season, modify meals during competition, and carefully time nutrient intake within the critical "glycogen window" following exertion. Everything serves the goal of winning.

Similarly, Paul becomes "all things to all people":

- To the Jews, he lives like a Jew
- To those under the law, he submits to the law  
- To the Gentiles, he lives without the law's restraints
- To the weak, he becomes weak

> I have become all things to all people so that by all possible means I might save some. (9:22)

His motivation transcends personal preference or comfort. As Martin Luther would later write, "A Christian man is the most free lord of all, and subject to none; a Christian man is the most dutiful servant of all, and subject to every one."

The goal is salvation—not mere friendship, not cultural relevance, but the eternal rescue of souls through the gospel.

## The Practice: Accommodation in Preferences

### Food as a Test Case

In our daily lives, we face the same principle. With roughly 21 meals per week, are we willing to sacrifice a few to serve others? Food becomes the test case because it so easily reveals our willingness—or refusal—to accommodate.

Consider how particular we become:
- Specific coffee preparations, teas, sweeteners
- Health concerns or cost considerations
- Cultural or class associations
- Reluctance to eat with certain people due to pickiness

Yet Paul asks: Is this *your* preference or your friend's? If you're trying to reach someone with the gospel, will you let food stand in the way? 

The practical path forward:
- If you must eat specific cuisine, discipline yourself with other meals so you can adapt when it matters
- If concerned about health, exercise and eat well during other meals to afford flexibility when serving others
- If cost is the issue, be disciplined elsewhere to afford meaningful time together
- At minimum, buy a drink and cherish the company

Food can separate us or unite us. Which will it be?

### The Broader Pattern

This principle extends far beyond meals—to movies, sports, music, conversation topics. Our willingness to accommodate reveals whether we're truly committed to others' spiritual welfare or merely demanding they meet us on our terms.

The irony: Christians are the most flexible people on earth. We have no dietary laws like other religions, no prescribed language for Scripture, no required political system. Christian societies have become the most multicultural by choice, intentionally welcoming people with different preferences. When critics call Christians intolerant, they reveal their ignorance. True tolerance is a Christian virtue born from genuine care—we accommodate because one life matters, and only the gospel can save it.

## The Limit: Standing Firm on Truth

Yet accommodation has boundaries. Paul issues a stern warning through Israel's history.

> These things happened to them as examples and were written down as warnings for us. (10:11)

The Israelites participated in God's covenant community—they ate the same spiritual food and drank the same spiritual drink. Yet they disqualified themselves through idolatry, sexual immorality, testing God, and grumbling. Their shared meal fellowship meant nothing because they abandoned the truth that sustained them.

### Modern Missional Confusion

Some contemporary churches misapply Paul's principle. They argue:
- Pastors should swear to speak the culture's language
- Youth groups should watch questionable films to be "relevant"  
- Churches should silence biblical teaching on sexuality to avoid offense

This is catastrophic misreading. Paul never said to compromise the gospel itself—only the preferences that don't affect salvation. He would say "do not eat" the wrong food.

Consider the desert illustration: You're driving through a wasteland filled with luggage—clothes, makeup, gaming systems, furniture, toys. A dying person needs rescue. You move nonessential items aside to make room. But you don't discard water, fuel, the engine, or the spare tire. These sustain life itself.

You cannot surrender the very truth that saves in order to seem relevant. That isn't winning anyone—it's futile. Diluting Scripture to suit prevailing culture doesn't lead to salvation; it leads to spiritual death.

### The Distinction

A loving church compromises on preferences—food, entertainment, cultural expressions, even timing and methods of engagement.

A loving church stands uncompromising on truth—the reality of sin and judgment, Christ's atoning death, the exclusivity of salvation through him, biblical sexual ethics.

When political pressure mounts to affirm what Scripture condemns, accommodation becomes apostasy. This is particularly urgent as churches face pressure regarding sexuality, marriage, and politics. We can challenge one another on time use, finances, marriage choices—but increasingly, secular society forbids challenging how people vote or live sexually. Yet these are precisely where biblical truth must speak clearly.

## The Discipline: Running to Win

> Do you not know that in a race all the runners run, but only one gets the prize? Run in such a way as to get the prize. (9:24)

Athletes pursue perishable crowns through rigorous training. Christians pursue an imperishable crown. This demands discipline—strategic, purposeful living rather than aimless activity.

> I strike a blow to my body and make it my slave so that after I have preached to others, I myself will not be disqualified for the prize. (9:27)

Paul's fear is not losing salvation but disqualification—the possibility of preaching the gospel effectively while his own life contradicts it. The discipline isn't punitive but formative, training the body like an athlete trains for competition.

## The Promise: God's Faithfulness in Testing

> No temptation has overtaken you except what is common to mankind. And God is faithful; he will not let you be tempted beyond what you can bear. But when you are tempted, he will also provide a way out so that you can endure it. (10:13)

The final word is reassurance. Every temptation we face is common—we're not uniquely weak or peculiarly tested. More importantly, God is faithful. He sets the ceiling on temptation itself and provides an escape route.

This is how Christians navigate the tension: We accommodate freely in matters of preference, empowering us to reach those far from God. We stand firm absolutely on matters of truth, refusing to betray the gospel for cultural approval. And we trust that God himself sustains us through the inevitable conflict this creates.

The measure of a healthy church is whether it can hold both convictions simultaneously—radical hospitality toward people combined with radical fidelity toward truth. This is the athlete's discipline applied to the spiritual realm: every choice calculated, every preference surrendered or defended based on whether it serves the gospel and guards the souls entrusted to our care.
