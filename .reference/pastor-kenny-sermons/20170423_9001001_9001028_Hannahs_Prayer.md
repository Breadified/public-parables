# 1 Samuel 1:1-28

**Title:** Hannah's Prayer

**Date:** June 2022 (Arise); April 23, 2017 (SMAC)

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Underdog Story

We love underdog stories. They captivate us in ways that victories of the already-powerful never do. The movie Dodgeball perfectly illustrates this—test audiences hated the original ending where the underdog team lost, forcing filmmakers to reshoot so the underdogs would win. 

Research confirms this instinct. When surveyed about a sports matchup between a heavily favored Team A and underdog Team B, 81% of respondents chose to root for Team B. But when Team B unexpectedly took a 3-0 series lead, half the respondents switched their allegiance—suddenly the formerly-dominant Team A became the underdog worth supporting. We're drawn to those fighting against the odds.

Today, we begin First Samuel with a story about an underdog named Hannah. And her story functions as a preview to both the book of First Samuel and ultimately to the Gospel itself.

## Hannah's Desperate Circumstances

Hannah faces multiple hardships simultaneously:

### She is barren and infertile

> 1:1-2 There was a certain man from Ramathaim, a Zuphite from the hill country of Ephraim, whose name was Elkanah son of Jeroham, the son of Elihu, the son of Tohu, the son of Zuph, an Ephraimite. He had two wives; one was called Hannah and the other Peninnah. Peninnah had children, but Hannah had none.

Barrenness was more than personal disappointment—it was understood as a curse. God had promised Israel that none among them would be childless (Deuteronomy 7:14). Hannah's infertility wasn't simply missing an experience; it meant the fundamental purpose of marriage remained unfulfilled. Like playing soccer but never scoring a goal, her role in God's covenant community seemed blocked.

This grief disproportionately affects women, who face biological time constraints men do not. Many today delay the decision about children, waiting for degrees, promotions, houses—until the window closes. For the first time in history, 50% of women aged 30 are childless, and 25% will never have children. At life's end, few wish they'd sacrificed more for their employers; most want family nearby. When your parents die, who sits at their deathbed? Usually their children—not employers or best friends.

### Her distress is intensified by Peninnah's cruelty

> 1:6-7 Because the Lord had closed Hannah's womb, her rival kept provoking her in order to irritate her. This went on year after year. Whenever Hannah went up to the house of the Lord, her rival provoked her till she wept and would not eat.

While grieving her own loss, Hannah faces constant taunting from her co-wife. Polygamy creates a toxic dynamic—wives competing for one husband's attention, escalating their claims on his affections. Hannah's personal suffering is weaponized against her.

### Her husband offers insensitive comfort

> 1:8 Her husband Elkanah would say to her, "Hannah, why are you weeping? Why don't you eat? Why are you downhearted? Don't I mean more to you than ten sons?"

Elkanah appears kind—he gives Hannah a double portion of the sacrifice—yet he fundamentally misunderstands her grief. He questions why she weeps, instructs her to eat, then turns the conversation to himself. This is not comfort; it's dismissal. Even her husband cannot truly support her.

### The priest publicly shames her

> 1:9-14 Once when they had finished eating and drinking in Shiloh, Hannah stood up. Now Eli the priest was sitting on his chair by the doorpost of the Lord's house. In her deep anguish Hannah prayed to the Lord, weeping bitterly. And she made a vow, saying, "Lord Almighty, if you will only look on your servant's misery and remember me, and not forget your servant but give her a son, then I will give him to the Lord for all the days of his life, and no razor will ever be used on his head."

Hannah makes a desperate vow. By "no razor," she means to dedicate her son as a Nazirite—a living offering set apart for God's purposes. She's not just asking for a child; she's offering to give that child back.

> 1:12-14 As she kept on praying to the Lord, Eli observed her mouth. Hannah was praying in her heart, and her lips were moving but her voice was not heard. Eli thought she was drunk and said to her, "How long are you going to stay drunk? Put away your wine."

In her moment of deepest dependence on God, the priest—God's own representative—accuses her of being drunk and wicked.

> 1:15-16 "Not so, my lord," Hannah replied, "I am a woman who is deeply troubled. I have not been drinking wine or beer; I was pouring out my soul to the Lord. Do not take your servant for a wicked woman; I have been praying here out of my great anguish and grief."

**At this point, Hannah has nothing.** No child. No support from her rival's constant mockery. No real comfort from her husband. No validation from God's priest. She stands alone, empty-handed, accused of wickedness while attempting the ultimate act of dependence—prayer.

## The Reversal

Yet everything changes:

> 1:17-18 Eli answered, "Go in peace, and may the God of Israel grant you what you have asked of him." She said, "May your servant find favor in your eyes." Then she went her way and ate something, and her face was no longer downcast.

After prayer, Hannah's demeanor transforms. Peace replaces despair.

> 1:19-20 Early the next morning they arose and worshiped before the Lord and then went back to their home at Ramah. Elkanah made love to his wife Hannah, and the Lord remembered her. So in the course of time Hannah became pregnant and gave birth to a son. She named him Samuel, saying, "Because I asked the Lord for him."

The name Samuel means "God has heard." The Lord remembered her. Her desperate prayer was answered.

Hannah then composes a prayer of exultation that reveals God's character and previews the entire book:

> 2:6-8 "The Lord brings death and makes alive; he brings down to the grave and raises up. The Lord sends poverty and wealth; he humbles and he exalts. He raises the poor from the dust and lifts the needy from the ash heap; he seats them with princes and has them inherit a throne of honor."

## The Character of God Revealed

Hannah's song reveals a God who is fundamentally on the side of the humble, the lowly, the weak. Notice the contrasts:

> 2:4-5 "The bows of the warriors are broken, but those who stumbled are armed with strength. Those who were full hire themselves out for food, but those who were hungry are hungry no more. She who was barren has borne seven children, but she who has had many sons pines away."

The powerful are no threat to God. Kings with their armies, their food supplies, their many sons to fill their ranks—none of this impresses Him. He breaks the strong and lifts the weak.

> 2:1-3 "My heart rejoices in the Lord; in the Lord my horn is lifted high. My mouth boasts over my enemies, for I delight in your deliverance. There is no one holy like the Lord; there is no one besides you; there is no Rock like our God. Do not keep talking so proudly or let your mouth speak such arrogance, for the Lord is a God who knows, and by him deeds are weighed."

This theme saturates First Samuel. The book is essentially written as a check on royal power—instruction for Israel's kings to stop placing confidence in military strength and material possessions, and instead to trust in the Lord who raises up and takes down as He wills.

## A Pattern That Echoes Through Scripture

Hannah's story parallels Elizabeth's in Luke 1. Both are barren. Both pray for a child. Both are answered by God. Both dedicate their sons for a divine purpose—Samuel to prepare the way for Israel's king, John to prepare the way for Jesus Himself.

Both births represent the same theological truth: God intervenes for the humble. He lifts up the weak. The mighty fall, and the meek inherit the earth.

> Matthew 5:5 Blessed are the meek, for they will inherit the earth.

This is not culturally universal. In many Eastern cultures, people cheer for strength and winners, not underdogs. But in the Western world, shaped profoundly by Judeo-Christian influence, we're drawn to stories where the powerless are vindicated. What other religion has a crucified man as its central figure? What other faith uses an instrument of execution as its symbol? 

Christianity itself is the ultimate underdog story—God becoming vulnerable, suffering, dying, and rising again to redeem the weak and broken.

## Application: For Those Who Feel Low

Have you ever faced a worse day, worse year, worse season than you can bear? Do you feel like the perpetual underdog, the perpetual loser—especially at being a Christian?

Hundreds of millions of Christians face persecution globally. They're harassed, kicked from their countries, imprisoned, and martyred. Yet they cling to the promise that God sides with the humble.

Even in more privileged contexts, many feel like failures. But hear Hannah's song: the Lord brings down the high and exalts the low. He remembers those He seems to have forgotten. He answers the prayers of the desperate.

The message that runs through Scripture and echoes through our culture is this: giants can be toppled. With faith in God, we too can overcome.

## Setting for First Samuel

Hannah fulfilled her vow. After Samuel was weaned—likely around age 3-5 based on historical weaning practices—she brought him to the temple at Shiloh to serve before the Lord:

> 1:24-28 After he was weaned, she took the boy with her, young as he was, along with a three-year-old bull, an ephah of flour and a skin of wine, and brought him to the house of the Lord at Shiloh. When the bull had been sacrificed, they brought the boy to Eli, and she said to him, "Pardon me, my lord. As surely as you live, I am the woman who stood here beside you praying to the Lord. I prayed for this child, and the Lord has granted me what I asked of him. So now I give him to the Lord. For his whole life he will be given over to the Lord." And he worshiped the Lord there.

Samuel grew up in the temple, ministering before the Lord while his mother continued to receive blessings—God graciously gave her three sons and two daughters as well.

## The Book's Greater Purpose

First Samuel functions as a preview to the entire narrative arc of kingship in Israel. Through Samuel's life and ministry, we'll see how God raises up humble leaders, humbles proud ones, and ultimately points toward the true King—Jesus Christ, who came not with earthly power but with self-giving love.

We love underdogs because our God is the God of underdogs.
