# 1 Peter 3:1-7

**Title:** Alien Submission
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Understanding the Challenge with Submission

Submission is undoubtedly a difficult word in our contemporary culture. It carries connotations of oppression, inequality, and injustice—particularly in societies where women's educational achievement has surpassed men's in many measurable ways. The very word conjures images of subservient "Stepford wives" or tragic figures trapped in abusive relationships.

The reality is stark: men fear being labeled misogynists for even mentioning the word, church ministers rush to qualify it with disclaimers, and women who embrace it are accused of betraying their own cause. Yet this is precisely the teaching Peter sets before us, and we must engage with it seriously.

## The Foundation: Biblical Submission Flows from the Heart

> Wives, in the same way [live good lives] be submissive to your husbands so that, if any of them do not believe the word, they may be won over without words by the behavior of their wives. (1 Peter 3:1)

The key to understanding Peter's instruction is recognizing that biblical submission begins as a matter of the heart, not mere external compliance. Peter elaborates on this in verses 2-4:

> When they see the purity and reverence of your lives. Your beauty should not come from outward adornment, such as braided hair and the wearing of gold jewelry and fine clothes. Instead, it should be that of your inner self, the unfading beauty of a gentle and quiet spirit, which is of great worth in God's sight.

Submission, then, is fundamentally about:

- **A pure life**: One that doesn't store up resentment, disdain, or bitterness
- **A reverent life**: One that takes God's Word seriously and seeks to live it out
- **A gentle spirit**: Not nagging, manipulating, argumentative, or impatient
- **A quiet spirit**: One that happily accepts the husband's leadership without attempting to usurp it

### True Beauty Reconsidered

Peter's challenge to women stands in stark contrast to the world's definition of beauty. In an era of carefully curated appearances—elaborate hairstyles, gold accessories, designer clothes—Peter points to something far more enduring and precious.

The holy women of the past understood this. They made themselves beautiful through submission and godliness, not through outward adornment. And their beauty lasted. When we consider the arc of a human life—childhood, adulthood, middle age, old age, death—we begin to grasp what Peter is saying. Physical beauty inevitably fades. What remains is character.

Our culture obscures this truth. We surround ourselves with people our own age, all dressed alike from the same stores, all reinforced by media images of the youngest and skinniest. We attend weddings where brides have invested small fortunes in their appearance. We compare ourselves endlessly with our friends. Meanwhile, the extended family—grandparents, aunties, grandaunties—are scattered overseas, and we rarely witness the natural aging process or attend funerals that might remind us of life's brevity and true beauty's nature.

**What this means practically:**
- **For young women**: God cares far more about your inner character than your appearance. You don't need expensive clothes or perfect hair to be beautiful in God's eyes. Be modest and unpretentious.
- **For young men**: Stop pursuing the wrong kind of women. When you chase only physical attractiveness, you make it harder for your sisters to develop genuine godliness. Look for the modest and godly.
- **For all of us**: Take advantage of the church's diversity of ages. Attend funerals, not just weddings. Let the reality of mortality reshape your values.

## Submission is Unqualified

> Like Sarah, who obeyed Abraham and called him her master. You are her daughters if you do what is right and do not give way to fear. (1 Peter 3:6)

The second critical point: biblical submission has no conditions attached to it. This is where Peter's teaching becomes truly radical.

### Sarah: A Powerful Example

Sarah was no pushover. She was brave enough to laugh at an angel sent by God himself (Genesis 18:10-12). She had the authority to demand that Abraham send away Hagar when she became a threat. Yet this woman of strength, this foundational figure in Israel's story, deliberately chose to call Abraham "master" and submit to his leadership.

Consider the irony: in Galatians, Paul uses Sarah as a symbol of Christian freedom itself. Yet she understood that freedom includes the freedom to submit. As Peter writes, "You are her daughters if you do what is right and do not give way to fear."

### What "Unqualified" Means

Submission is not:
- Conditional on whether you agree with your husband's decisions
- Restricted to only major decisions
- Restricted to only spiritual matters
- Dependent on whether your husband is godly, loving, or even competent
- Something you do because you're in love or get along well

Submission is choosing to place yourself under your husband's authority **because you are ultimately under Christ's authority**. It's obedience to the Lord Jesus expressed through your relationship with your husband.

## Addressing the Objections

### What About Domestic Abuse?

Domestic abuse is a grave sin and there is no excuse for it. That said, several things must be said clearly:

**First:** All couples hurt each other in unhealthy ways. Husbands may wound with violent words and angry outbursts. Wives can wound with sharp, stinging words and the relentless dripping criticism that erodes a man's confidence until he becomes a shell of himself. Both do damage. We notice one more readily because it's visible and dramatic; we miss the other because it's quiet and cumulative. But neither excuses abuse.

**Second:** There already exists a solution to extreme abuse. In our legal system, domestic violence can lead to arrest and imprisonment. The authority structure doesn't need to be dismantled; it needs to be invoked. If you're being abused, contact the police, seek counseling, and withdraw from fellowship temporarily if needed.

**Third:** We must not throw out the entire structure of marital authority because it can be abused. That would be like abolishing police forces because some police abuse their power. The solution isn't to eliminate authority; it's to call for its proper use.

**Fourth:** Husbands actually *need* authority to fulfill their God-given responsibility. Imagine being told you're responsible for something but given no power to accomplish it. This is untenable. According to Ephesians 5:25-27, husbands are called to present their wives to Christ as radiant, spotless, and blameless. Fathers are responsible for raising children in the Lord. These are unqualified responsibilities, which means the authority required to fulfill them must be similarly unqualified.

### But Doesn't Galatians 3:28 Teach Mutual Submission?

> There is neither Jew nor Greek, slave nor free, male nor female, for you are all one in Christ Jesus. (Galatians 3:28)

And Peter does write:

> Submit to one another out of reverence for Christ. (Ephesians 5:21)

However, language of mutuality can be asymmetrical. Galatians 3:28 refers to spiritual equality before God—not the elimination of gender, ethnicity, or social structures. When Galatians 6:2 calls us to "bear one another's burdens," it doesn't mean everyone exchanges burdens equally; it means those who are stronger help those who are weaker. Not symmetrical.

Similarly, when Ephesians 5:21 speaks of submitting to one another, the context that follows is explicitly about asymmetrical relationships—wives to husbands, children to parents, servants to masters. Peter never says husbands should submit to wives in the same way wives submit to husbands. The command is one-directional.

In reality, submission exists throughout all human institutions. In the workforce, in the military, in sports teams—someone must have authority or nothing functions. A democracy of two is impossible. You cannot hold a household vote on every decision. Someone must lead.

### What If My Husband Isn't a Christian?

Peter's original context includes exactly this situation—wives married to unbelieving husbands. Yet he commands submission anyway:

> Wives, in the same way [live good lives] be submissive to your husbands so that, if any of them do not believe the word, they may be won over without words by the behavior of their wives.

The mechanism is not argument but lived example. A wife's godly, reverent, pure conduct may be what God uses to draw her husband to faith.

The principle is this: we submit to the office, not the person. Just as employees submit to bosses not because the boss is necessarily wiser or better, but because the boss holds the position, wives submit to husbands because of the marital structure God has ordained—regardless of the husband's spiritual maturity.

In fact, one could argue God's sense of irony is at work here. Research suggests women's minds remain sharper longer than men's—widows often thrive while widowers decline. Perhaps God deliberately gave the submissive role to the mentally stronger sex, knowing it would take genuine strength and confidence to submit. It's not a role for the weak; it's a role for the strong-hearted.

### What If My Husband Won't Lead?

This is a common problem. Since Genesis 3:16, many wives naturally want to take over, and many husbands lazily abdicate. If this is your situation, you may need to:

- Repent of your desire to control
- Ask for his input and advice
- Gently encourage him toward greater leadership
- Seek counsel from a godly pastor if the pattern persists

The problem isn't that he makes mistakes; the problem is that he abandons his responsibilities. Encouraging him to lead is actually a gift most husbands would welcome.

## For Those Preparing for Marriage

Before you commit, think carefully about merit. Here are the right questions to ask:

**For women:**
- Can I genuinely submit to this man's authority?
- Is he a Christian? (How can he present me spotless to Christ if he doesn't know Christ?)
- Is he maturing in godliness? Is he reading Scripture, working on his character, growing spiritually?
- Can he control his temper? Is his faith genuine and deepening?
- If he's a Christian but immature, will you genuinely respect his leadership as he grows? Or will you always be waiting for him to become someone different?

Don't marry hoping someone will change. If he's not yet the man you need, don't date him. It's not fair to either of you.

**For men:**
- Can you present this woman as a radiant bride to Christ?
- Will she listen to you? Can you genuinely lead her?
- Is she a Christian? How can you lead someone spiritually when you don't share the same ultimate allegiance?
- Are you mature enough in your own faith to be her spiritual head?

## The Challenge to Husbands: Leadership and Love

> Husbands, in the same way [live good lives] be considerate as you live with your wives, and treat them with respect as the weaker partner and as heirs with you of the gracious gift of life, so that nothing will hinder your prayers. (1 Peter 3:7)

Peter's instruction to husbands parallels his instruction to wives: live good lives that demonstrate the gospel. But there's a specific word here: "weaker partner." This refers to physical difference—men are on average 15-20% larger than women. More significantly, it refers to the physiological reality of motherhood: pregnancy depletes calcium, breastfeeding weakens bones through hormonal changes, pregnancy leaves permanent marks on a woman's body. Motherhood is physically costly in ways fatherhood is not.

Because of this difference, husbands are called to be considerate and respectful. More than that, they bear the primary responsibility for the family. Nothing is more demoralizing than being held responsible for something over which you have no authority. Husbands need leadership authority to fulfill their calling.

### What Husbands Must Do

Husbands, love your wives as you love your own body. This means:

- **Never abandon them.** Don't leave out of boredom, ambition, or inadequacy. To cut and run when the battle is hardest is cowardice.
- **Don't abrogate your responsibility.** You are responsible for the family's spiritual, emotional, physical, and material wellbeing. Divide the daily tasks, certainly, but the ultimate accountability rests with you.
- **Be an all-rounder.** Provide materially, yes, but also ensure high-quality physical, emotional, and spiritual input into your family's life. Your presence and engagement matter more than the entertainment options available.
- **Love sacrificially.** Your model is Christ, who gave himself up for the church. Your love should be willing to cost you something.

## A Final Word on Our Cultural Moment

For the past two centuries, following the Enlightenment, we've celebrated personal freedom and individual rights. We've rightly rejected slavery and tyranny. Yet in the process, we've lost all sense of proper authority and order. Teachers no longer have authority. Parents are friends with money. Police are hesitant to act. Governments facilitate your goals but won't tell you what to do.

This loss of authority is affecting our marriages and our children. The decisions you make about your spouse in the next ten years will shape the next fifty years of your life. Choose wisely. Think about whether this person can lead you or be led by you with genuine respect. Think about whether you're ready for the weight of leadership or the grace of submission.

Meanwhile, let us all live such good lives that our conduct opens doors for the gospel to be declared.
