# Ecclesiastes 9:7-10

**Title:** Enjoy Your Wife
**Date:** April 8, 2023
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## A Counterintuitive Call to Celebration

The book of Ecclesiastes captures the wisdom of Solomon, the wisest man to ever live, as he reflects on life's meaning in his later years. The passage we're examining begins somewhat soberly:

> Enjoy life with your wife, whom you love, all the days of this meaningless life that God has given you under the sun—all your meaningless days. For this is your lot in life and in your toilsome labor under the sun.

The language of "lot in life" and "toilsome labour" doesn't immediately inspire confidence about marriage. It suggests a life of difficulty—suggesting we accept the hard reality as our portion. The word "toil" refers to labor, and Solomon emphasizes it twice to underscore the weight of human work, like a laborer sweating under the sun with an axe and spade.

Yet this apparent pessimism masks a deeper truth.

## Understanding "Meaningless": Temporary, Not Void

The original Hebrew word translated as "meaningless" is better understood as "temporary" or "fleeting." The phrase "under the sun" reinforces this sense of temporariness—we use this expression in everyday speech to describe momentary attention ("our day under the sun"). It suggests something brief, not something without purpose.

Life is necessarily temporary. We are mortal, vulnerable, and destined to face our Maker. But temporariness does not equal meaninglessness.

This reality confronts us starkly: if we are all going to die, what is the point? Why take risks? In a video game, if you've lost all your health points and still have levels to complete, why continue playing? In modern China, young people face the phenomenon of "lying flat"—a response to a "996" future (9am to 9pm, six days a week). Knowing they may never afford a house or achieve their dreams, they ask: Why bother? Why risk humiliation, injury, or worse when safety seems preferable?

Yet life is inherently risky. We've survived a pandemic that could have claimed us simply for stepping outside. We risk accident driving a car, skin cancer from the sun, disease from our choices, even harm from a careless social media post. And specifically for Daniel and Jessica on their wedding day: Why risk marriage? Your spouse could prove difficult. Children may disobey. Illness may strike. Divorce could split your assets. You've known each other only briefly—who truly knows what you're getting into?

**Why take the risk?**

## Solomon's Bold Answer: Embrace Life

Despite acknowledging life's temporariness and dangers, Solomon's counsel is clear and countercultural:

> Go, eat your food with gladness, and drink your wine with a joyful heart, for God has already approved what you do.

He calls us to celebrate: to eat with gladness, to drink with joy, knowing that God has already sanctioned these pursuits. White garments and anointed heads—symbols of celebration and care for oneself—mark the occasion.

Most importantly:

> Enjoy life with your wife, whom you love.

Get married. Live for life itself, not from fear. This is countercultural in every generation. Korea has seen marriage rates decline 40% in the last decade. Yet Solomon's word stands: commit to marriage, to a shared life.

Verse 10 deepens this vision:

> Whatever your hand finds to do, do it with all your might, for in the realm of the dead, where you are going, there is neither working nor planning nor knowledge nor wisdom.

The logic is striking: precisely because death awaits us, where no work, planning, or wisdom can proceed, we must engage fully now. "Whatever your hand finds to do" encompasses the marriage and labor mentioned in verse 9—the opportunities for good that come our way. Do them with all your might. Lead a productive life. Don't lie flat. Take the risk. Know God. Don't demand perfection.

## The Paradox of Temporary Life

The temporariness of life on earth, the real dangers we face—these truths do not require us to retreat into lockdown mode or refuse meaningful engagement. During the height of COVID, many couples took the calculated risk to gather in permitted groups, to walk together, to pursue connection. Daniel even got a dog, which Kenny notes as a sign of choosing fullness of life despite uncertainty.

## What We're Called to Fill This Temporary Life With

Marriage itself is one answer. Fill your fleeting years with:

- **Children** and the next generation
- **Diapers, chores, and sleepless nights**
- **Arguments, but also reconciliation and forgiveness**
- **Short-term pain that yields long-term gain**

There may be tough seasons. More sickness than health. More poverty than wealth. This is honest, biblical realism.

**But take a risk. Have faith.**

As Christians, this is what faith means: trusting God despite unknowns and going ahead anyway. What might happen in the future does not rob us of present joy. We refuse to let anxieties rule over us.

## Life Is Not Meaningless

Yes, life is fleeting and temporary. But it is not meaningless. Because life is short, we will make this short life count. We can do tremendous good in the brief time we have before death parts us. And then we meet our Maker and give account of what we did with the life we were given.
