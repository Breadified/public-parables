# 1 Corinthians 13:1-13
**Title:** Reinventing The Church: The Common Ministry
**Date:** 3 May 2020
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Gifts Without Love Are Useless

Paul opens this passage with a profound principle: our spiritual gifts, no matter how impressive, become worthless without love. Consider this equation:

**(GIFTS) × (LOVE) = PROFIT**

> If I speak in the tongues of men and of angels, but have not love, I am only a resounding gong or a clanging cymbal. If I have the gift of prophecy and can fathom all mysteries and all knowledge, and if I have a faith that can move mountains, but have not love, I am nothing. If I give all I possess to the poor and surrender my body to the flames, but have not love, I gain nothing. (1 Corinthians 13:1-3)

Think of it this way: a company might work diligently for a year, yet if they fail to turn a profit, they've failed as a business. Similarly, your talents and time are good and great, but if exercised without love, they become profitless—useless for God's kingdom. Without love, spiritual gifts produce only noise, like an orchestra with no conductor. Love is the essential ingredient that makes everything else meaningful.

## What Is This Love?

> Love is patient, love is kind. It does not envy, it does not boast, it is not proud. It is not rude, it is not self-seeking, it is not easily angered, it keeps no record of wrongs. Love does not delight in evil but rejoices with the truth. It always protects, always trusts, always hopes, always perseveres. (1 Corinthians 13:4-7)

This is the kind of love we must bring to our service of one another. Love provides the proper focus for our gifts and talents. It provides the motivation by which we judge our activity:

- **Patience and kindness** address the difficulties of not getting along with each other
- **Freedom from envy, boasting, and pride** counter the temptation toward individualism
- **Courtesy and humility** oppose rudeness and self-centeredness
- **Forgiveness and emotional control** mean we don't hold grudges or nurse anger
- **Commitment to truth** means we don't delight in evil
- **Steadfast care** means we always protect, trust, hope, and persevere

And we do this not because we have discovered love on our own, but because Jesus has loved us first. When you read through this list again, you see Jesus perfectly embodied in every single characteristic.

## Love as Our Eternal Concern

> When the Son of Man comes in his glory, and all the angels with him, he will sit on his throne in heavenly glory. All the nations will be gathered before him, and he will separate the people one from another as a shepherd separates the sheep from the goats... Then the King will say to those on his right, "Come, you who are blessed by my Father; take your inheritance, the kingdom prepared for you since the creation of the world. For I was hungry and you gave me something to eat, I was thirsty and you gave me something to drink, I was a stranger and you invited me in, I needed clothes and you clothed me, I was sick and you looked after me, I was in prison and you came to visit me."... The King will reply, "I tell you the truth, whatever you did for one of the least of these brothers of mine, you did for me." (Matthew 25:31-40)

This is Christ's ultimate measure of our lives. We must show concern for others—not because we are naturally loving, but because we recognize that every person matters to Jesus. The test is always: Does this exhibit thoughtful love, or is it merely the unbridled exercise of our gifts?

God has given us gifts for the **common good**, not just for ourselves. You must think of loving the whole body of Christ, not just yourself or your friends. No ministry operates in isolation. The body is more important than the exercise of any individual gift.

## Love and Our Freedom

Paul addresses specific situations in Corinth where this principle of love must govern our decisions:

### Chapter 8: The Stumbling Block

> Knowledge puffs up, but love builds up... Be careful, however, that the exercise of your freedom does not become a stumbling block to the weak. (1 Corinthians 8:1, 9)

The Corinthians had knowledge—they understood that idols are nothing but wood and stone. This knowledge is good and true. But knowledge alone makes us arrogant. Love edifies. Not everyone has this understanding. Some believers still struggle with the associations of idolatry, and their conscience is weak. If you exercise your freedom without regard for them, you cause them to stumble.

> If what I eat causes my brother to fall into sin, I will never eat meat again, so that I will not cause him to fall. (1 Corinthians 13:13)

Paul's response demonstrates patience and kindness—he willingly restricts his own freedom out of concern for his weaker brother.

### Chapter 11: The Common Meal

At the Lord's Supper, the community was not waiting for one another. Some were eating while others went hungry, creating judgment and division. Love demands that we wait for each other, that we consider how our behavior affects those gathered with us.

## Making Love the Standard for All Life

Every activity of our life—every judgment we make and skill we exercise—is ultimately judged by the standard of love:

- What course of study should you pursue?
- When and what should you eat?
- What clothes should you wear?
- What should you say?
- What beverages should serve at your gatherings?
- What games should you play?

In the free use of our gifts, love must provide the direction. **Love supersedes our gifts and restrains our liberty when necessary.**

## The Motivation for Service

In the past, we've used the acronym **FAT** (Faithful, Available, Teachable) as the primary criterion for service. But I made a mistake. If FAT is primary and love is secondary, people might develop skills yet never learn to love. The result? No matter how faithful, available, and teachable—it equals zero. Useless.

The healthier standard—though it sounds unhealthy—is **LOVEFAT**. Love must be primary; the other qualities follow.

Why do you serve? Consider what should **not** motivate you:

- Not to gain a new experience or have fun
- Not to pad your resume for secular employers
- Not to be with your friends or hang around the cool leaders
- Not to boost your self-esteem or feel better about yourself
- Not even for your own spiritual training and growth

**However**, none of these outcomes are necessarily bad. Serving does bring joy. If you volunteer at church, employers should know what you're doing with your time. You will make friends serving alongside others. You will feel good about helping. You will develop skills, especially in relating to people.

**But these cannot be your primary reason for serving—not even close.**

You must do it, as Bryan Adams sings, "for love." Because all those other reasons fade away. Careers change. Friends scatter. Self-esteem fluctuates. Only love endures.

## When Gifts Seem Required

Some argue: "Why else would God give me this gift? Shouldn't I use it? Surely if God gave it to me, I should exercise it?"

This logic misses something important. A high test score doesn't automatically point you toward medicine or law. It's simply a tool. Your score could direct you toward an arts degree to demonstrate that you don't need a flashy career. Or toward teaching, because we need excellent teachers. Or toward politics, because wisdom is needed there. Or away from traditional careers entirely to pursue your own studies. The tool itself is neutral—your goal determines how you use it.

**Gifts are like tools.** What do you want to accomplish? If you love, your gifts will never be wasted—whether you exercise them in a prominent role or in service that no one notices. A brilliant person doing a simple course, if that course serves the common good, has not wasted their ability.

## Everyone Has Gifts for Love

"But I have no other gifts to exercise," some say. There will always be a need to love. Don't know where to serve? First ask yourself: What are your gifts? Then look at needs—not just traditional official roles, but **love needs**.

Look around you. What can you do?

- **Who needs care?** Who needs to hear the gospel? Who needs encouragement? Who needs someone to talk to?
- If you're not mute, you have the gift of greeting
- **Mouth:** You can speak God's words to each other. Real, vulnerable conversation—not just surface talk about the latest purchase or movie
- **Legs:** You can walk to someone who's hurting
- **Memory:** You can remember others' needs and pray for them during the week
- **Eyes:** You can see who's sitting alone or rushing out because they fear loneliness
- **Arms:** You can lift a chair, pull a garbage bin, give someone a hug, offer comfort
- **Hands:** You can write a note or text someone with encouragement to keep following Jesus
- **Driver's license:** You can give people rides
- **Internet access:** You can email about youth group or check on someone who hasn't been at church
- **Wallet:** You can give generously (if you work)
- **Home:** You can host a dinner
- **Phone:** You can pray with someone

All of these have been given by God. Don't say you have no gifts. Open your mind to the opportunities. They all involve love.

## Shift From Gifts to Opportunities

This is why I'm moving away from the language of "gifts" to the language of "opportunities." Life presents us with opportunities—whether single or married, able-bodied or limited, wealthy or poor. Approach life not by asking "What gifts do I have?" but by asking "What needs exist? How can I fill them?" That is how gifts actually serve the common good and actually profit the kingdom.

The problem is our focus on the glamorous, upfront roles—which perhaps should comprise only 1% of church activity. How can everyone do the 1%? We can't all be singers or speakers or musicians. Formal roles are limited.

Yet many seminars and conferences begin at the wrong place: "What are you good at? What looks impressive?" This invariably leads to the obligatory categories—leading, singing, playing instruments—as if God only created a few dozen upfront gifts. But I challenge any of these conferences to come up with "the gift of taking out the garbage." One of our brothers here has discovered it. Whatever the service, it's useless without love. We need a love seminar. The gifts will work themselves out.

**Make the decision to love first. The gifts will follow.**

## Love Never Fails

> Love never fails. But where there are prophecies, they will cease; where there are tongues, they will be stilled; where there is knowledge, it will pass away. (1 Corinthians 13:8)

> When I was a child, I talked like a child, I thought like a child, I reasoned like a child. When I became a man, I put childish ways behind me. Now we see but a poor reflection as in a mirror; then we shall see face to face. Now I know in part; then I shall know fully, even as I am fully known. And now these three remain: faith, hope and love. But the greatest of these is love. (1 Corinthians 13:11-13)

### Why Love Lasts When Gifts Fade

One day it will get boring. The musicians will tire of playing. Bible leaders face the temptation to chase popularity. Next Gen leaders feel pressure to stay trendy rather than follow the narrow way. Those who serve morning tea, those who pack up chairs, those who usher and welcome—all face weariness.

And when those initial reasons fade—the fun, the resume boost, the self-esteem, the cool factor—will you still serve? Out of love?

Consider missionaries who sacrifice comfort and familiarity. Consider those who will quit glamorous jobs or take less prestigious roles for the sake of love. Consider the low self-esteem that comes from taking flak for trying to live godly. Consider being alone, packing up chairs while others have gone home.

Will you serve then? Out of love?

Here's a danger for young people in church: it's very cool to join a group; young people move in packs. But your loyalty must be to the Lord Jesus first—not to your peer group. As a result, your loyalty is to serving the body of Christ, not to peer pressure. You will still care for individuals when the group abandons them.

**If you are burning out in a ministry, or if you're no longer doing it out of love, or if you're only doing it because someone pressured you—please don't do it. Come and talk to me or the other leaders.**

### The Fruit That Lasts

All the gifts—prophecy, tongues, knowledge—they will pass away. But what will remain?

> These three remain: faith, hope and love. But the greatest of these is love. (1 Corinthians 13:13)

One day SMAC will disappear. You will forget what you learned. People won't remember your deeds. But love will count—if you did things out of genuine concern for others, if you weren't self-seeking, if you loved.

In the end, who cares how talented we are or how wonderful our ministries grow? Only if they count toward **love**. True spirituality is love. Love is true spirituality. God knows your motivation, whether the ministry succeeds or fails.

**A gift is not a gift when it is not given in love.**

## The Good vs. The Best

We face the tyranny of choice—so many good options, and we're all capable of doing multiple things well. A friend once told me: "The good is the enemy of the best."

Do the best. Love.

We need to make choices and limit our options. Don't pursue just any good thing—pursue the best thing. And the best thing is love. Don't trivialize your opportunities. Make them count through love.
