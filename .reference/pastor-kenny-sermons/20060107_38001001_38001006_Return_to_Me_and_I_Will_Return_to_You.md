# Zechariah 1:1-6

**Title:** Return to Me and I Will Return to You  
**Date:** January 7, 2006  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction: Starting From Scratch

What does it mean to start from scratch? The question carries weight when you've invested everything into something and lost it all.

Consider the 2004 Indian Ocean tsunami that devastated Indonesian towns like Aceh. Homes, businesses, years of labor—gone in moments. Survivors faced not just the physical work of rebuilding, but the psychological burden of doing again what they'd already accomplished once. Starting over requires both physical energy and mental resilience to push past the discouragement of repetition.

Or think of migrants arriving in a new country with only suitcases, searching for jobs and housing in tiny apartments. Or a student who loses a completed 3,000-word assignment when the power cuts out. The mental hurdle is as real as the physical one.

The Jews returning from Babylonian exile faced exactly this trial. They had to start from scratch in their own land.

## The Path to Exile and Return

**587 BC:** The armies of Babylon laid siege to Jerusalem, conquered it, and burned it to the ground. The southern kingdom of Judah was taken into exile in Babylon—a foreign land far to the north. Over time, the exiles settled, built families, and established some normalcy. But they were not in their promised land.

**519 BC:** Seventy years later, a new world power emerged. The Persians conquered Babylon, and King Cyrus issued an edict allowing the exiles to return home and rebuild the temple of God.

**537 BC:** The Jews returned to Jerusalem under the leadership of Joshua the high priest and Zerubbabel the governor. Each family returned to their own towns.

**535-517 BC:** In the second year of their return, they began laying the temple foundations. The rebuilding would take twenty years, with several obstacles along the way.

## The Deeper Problem: God's Absence

On the surface, the return seemed successful—the people had come home and temple rebuilding had begun. Yet several signs suggested God had not truly returned to them:

### First: Jerusalem Remained Desolate

Only half the exiled population returned. Many had died; others remained in Babylon. The city was razed. The walls were torn down. Nothing remained. Unlike their ancestors who entered a land flowing with milk and honey, where houses and fields awaited them, these returnees found emptiness. Where was God in such desolation?

### Second: The Temple Rebuilding Was Weak

The temple being rebuilt bore no comparison to the original. When the foundation was laid, those who remembered Solomon's magnificent temple wept openly:

> "But many of the older priests and Levites and family heads, who had seen the former temple, wept aloud when they saw the foundation of this temple being laid, while many others shouted for joy. No one could distinguish the sound of the shouts of joy from the sound of weeping, because the people made so much noise." (Ezra 3:12-13)

The new temple lacked materials, resources, and glory. The grand vision of a temple five hundred cubits by five hundred cubits had shrunk to a modest structure. How could God's presence dwell in such a diminished place?

### Third: The Nations Opposed Them

The surrounding peoples actively hindered the rebuilding work:

> "Then the peoples around them set out to discourage the people of Judah and make them afraid to go on building. They hired counselors to work against them and frustrate their plans during the entire reign of Cyrus king of Persia and down to the reign of Darius." (Ezra 4:4-5)

This opposition persisted for two decades and even halted the work completely for two years before Haggai and Zechariah began their prophetic ministries.

## The Theological Crisis

These problems pointed to something far deeper than logistics, finances, or hostile neighbors. They revealed a theological crisis:

Jerusalem's identity rested entirely on the presence of God. The city was nothing without the temple, and the temple's significance lay not in its physical structure but in God's decision to dwell there among His people. The temple was where heaven met earth, where humanity encountered the divine.

Though God cannot be contained—heaven itself cannot contain the infinite Creator—He had chosen to reveal Himself through the temple's presence. This was His covenant way of relating to Israel. Without a functioning temple, there was no dwelling place for God. Without God's presence, there was no Jerusalem.

## Zechariah's Message: Eight Visions of Hope

Into this despair came Zechariah with a message from God: *Keep building. Do not give up. God will return in glory.*

Zechariah delivered this encouragement through a series of eight visions. Two themes emerge clearly from these visions:

### Theme 1: The Nations Will Be Subdued

**The First Vision (1:7-17):** Zechariah sees a horseman on a red horse with other horses behind him, sent to patrol the earth and report what is happening.

Their report: The whole world is at rest and at peace (1:11).

This sounds promising—world peace! But for the Jews, it meant injustice. The nations that had oppressed Judah for seventy years faced no consequences. They remained secure and arrogant, unpunished and unchallenged.

God's response is clear:

> "I am very jealous for Jerusalem and Zion, but I am very angry with the nations that feel secure. I was only a little angry, but they added to the calamity." (1:14-15)

Despite appearances, God promises:

> "Therefore, this is what the LORD says: 'I will return to Jerusalem with mercy, and there my house will be rebuilt. And the measuring line will be stretched out over Jerusalem.' ... 'My towns will again overflow with prosperity, and the LORD will again comfort Zion and choose Jerusalem.'" (1:16-17)

**The Eighth Vision (6:1-8):** Four chariots—superior to horses—patrol the earth toward the four winds of heaven. Their report: God's Spirit has come to rest in the north country. The nations have been conquered. God's anger has been satisfied.

The visions bookend the series with the same message: *The nations will fall. God will prevail.*

### Theme 2: Jerusalem Will Be Great Because God Dwells There

**The Third Vision (2:1-13):** A man with a measuring line attempts to measure Jerusalem's dimensions. But God interrupts him:

> "Jerusalem will be a city without walls because of the great number of men and livestock in it." (2:4)

The city will expand beyond any walls that could contain it—a sign of extraordinary prosperity and population.

Yet some might fear that a wall-less city is defenseless. God reassures:

> "And I myself will be a wall of fire around it, and I will be its glory within." (2:5)

But the greatest security is not military—it is God's presence itself. Notice how repeatedly God promises:

> "For I am coming, and I will live among you, declares the LORD." (2:10)

> "I will live among you and you will know that the LORD Almighty has sent me to you." (2:11)

> "The LORD will inherit Judah as his portion in the holy land and will again choose Jerusalem." (2:12)

What makes Jerusalem truly great is not its size, not its walls of fire, not its prosperity—but the presence of God. This is the glory. This is the hope.

Zechariah's message to the discouraged returnees: *Return to Me, and I will return to you. Keep building. God will come. Jerusalem will be great because I will be there.*

## Application for Us Today

A common mistake is to assume that since the temple was central to Jewish worship, church buildings must hold similar significance for Christians. But Jesus Himself reinterprets the entire symbolism:

> "Jesus answered them, 'Destroy this temple, and I will raise it again in three days.' ... But the temple he had spoken of was his body." (John 2:19, 21)

Jesus is the true temple. He is the place where God dwells. When you meet Jesus, you meet God Himself.

The old temple was static—bound to one location in Jerusalem. But Jesus, through His Spirit, is everywhere and anywhere, present in His Word. When you encounter Scripture, you encounter Jesus. You meet the temple. You meet God.

This means the actual building where we gather matters far less than the presence of God's Word. We can meet Jesus in a church building, a school hall, a house, a car, or anywhere else. Where God's Word is proclaimed and believed, there Jesus is. There the temple is.

If you want to know Jesus, immerse yourself in His Word. If you want to meet God, get into the Bible. There is no other way.

## Conclusion: Hope in Jesus

What does it mean to start from scratch? God Himself started from scratch. In the face of human sin and death, He created a new way to relate to us—through His temple, Jesus Christ.

Jesus is the one who truly rebuilds the temple. He was demolished on the cross and raised it up again in three days, defeating death itself and securing eternal life for all who trust in Him.

Facing disappointments and setbacks in life, we are called—like the Jews returning from exile—to place our hope not in our own reconstruction efforts, but in Jesus. This hope ultimately points us forward to the last day, to heaven where disappointments cease, where justice prevails, and where evil will not endure.

Jesus's final message to His disciples is an encouragement: Keep trusting God's Word. The temple is already built. The enemies you face—people, politics, injustice, evil, disasters—will not prevail. God will return. Heaven awaits, a place without walls where you will meet your God face to face.

Keep building your faith. Keep returning to God's Word. And He will return to you.
