# James 3:13-18

**Title:** Who is Wise? The One Who Shows It by His Deeds

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Understanding Wisdom

When we think of wise people, we imagine parents, accountants, bankers—people who seem to know things or succeed in life. But what actually makes someone wise? Is it knowledge? Success? Some esoteric insight? The truth is, we all want wisdom. It's valuable, worth pursuing. As Solomon knew, wisdom is one of life's greatest treasures.

James addresses this very question in his letter to scattered Christians. Unlike Paul, who explains the gospel and justification by faith, James focuses on something more practical: *What should a Christian actually look like?* His answer centers on Christian living—not the belief that produces action, but the actions that flow from genuine faith. That's why James is sometimes called the Proverbs of the New Testament.

## The Problem: Wisdom Without Works

But James saw real problems in his readers' lives. Their behavior didn't match their faith. They showed favoritism to the rich (2:1-13) while oppressing the poor (1:10, 5:1-2). They lacked good deeds (2:14)—they claimed faith but didn't feed the hungry. They quarreled among themselves (3:16, 4:1-3), with proud teachers competing over who was wisest. Their tongues revealed the problem: the same mouth that praised God also cursed people (3:9-12).

The core issue was simple: their actions contradicted the gospel message they claimed to believe.

## Two Kinds of Wisdom

James presents a stark contrast. There are two wisdoms in the world, and they're utterly opposed.

**The "wisdom" of the world** (what we call worldly wisdom):
- **Origin:** Earthly, unspiritual, demonic
- **Character:** Bitter envy, selfish ambition, jealousy
- **Result:** Disorder and every kind of evil

**True wisdom from above:**
- **Origin:** Heavenly, from God
- **Character:** Pure, peace-loving, considerate, submissive, full of mercy and good fruit, impartial and sincere
- **Result:** A harvest of righteousness, a good life

How do we tell the difference? We can't always know the origin, but we can always see the results. Like things that look similar at first but reveal themselves by their fruit—the character of leaders like some prominent figures in church history shows us this principle. True wisdom bears the fruit of humility, mercy, and righteousness. False wisdom produces strife, ambition, and corruption.

## Who Is Wise?

The answer in James 3:13 cuts to the heart: *Who is wise and understanding among you? Let them show it by their good life, by deeds done in the humility that comes from wisdom.*

The wise person shows it by his deeds. Not by claiming it. Not by sounding intelligent. The criterion is simple: good life, humble deeds done in wisdom.

In the Old Testament, the wise men—those who wrote Proverbs, Job, Song of Songs, and parts of Psalms—analyzed life's complexities and gave practical advice rooted in their knowledge of God. But they lived it out. Their wisdom affected everything: whom they ate with, how they raised children, their financial decisions. It was a whole lifestyle.

Wisdom is profoundly practical. It's not mystical or abstract. It's the natural outworking of faith—the ability to look at any situation and act correctly in light of what we know about God.

## How Do We Get Wisdom?

Verse 17 is clear: wisdom is from above, from heaven. It doesn't come from human reasoning or worldly philosophy.

James 1:5 tells us the way: *If any of you lacks wisdom, you should ask God, who gives generously to all without finding fault, and it will be given to you.* 

Wisdom is a gift. James 1:17 reminds us that *every good and perfect gift is from above, coming down from the Father of the heavenly lights.*

But what does this heavenly wisdom actually mean? Does it make us suddenly cleverer or more insightful? No.

## Christ Is Our Wisdom

The answer emerges in 1 Corinthians 1-2. Paul addressed a similar situation in Corinth—Christians quarreling over who was wiser, fighting about food laws and marriage, divided into camps behind different teachers. He was attacking what we might call "worldly wisdom" (*sophia*—the root of "philosophy"), which is humanity thinking its way to God.

Real wisdom is different. It's God revealing himself to us. The world looks at reality as if God doesn't exist, as if he hasn't revealed himself. But true wisdom recognizes that God alone tells us what the universe really means.

And the central revelation? Christ. Jesus Christ is the wisdom and power of God (1 Corinthians 1:24). The cross—foolishness to the world—is actually where God's greatest wisdom is revealed. Death and resurrection. The world cannot understand the cross, yet it's the key to everything.

Paul writes: *If we are in Christ, then we are justified, and therefore in Christ we are perfectly wise, for he is our wisdom before God* (1 Corinthians 1:30). This isn't about becoming cleverer. It's about being restored to right relationship with God through Christ.

## Wisdom and God's Word

Christ's wisdom is connected to God's Word. If we're Christian, we're already wise—in the sense that we know the basis for all our decisions. We know God's will through his Word. The ongoing transformation happens through the renewal of our minds (Romans 12:2).

As we study the Bible, we become more aware of what Christ has done for us. We appreciate his work with grateful and humble hearts. This transforms how we make decisions. We're not waiting for mystical guidance—doors opening or feelings of inclination. Rather, we're objectively working out the implications of the gospel for our lives.

Colossians 1:25-27 and 2:3 remind us that in Christ *are hidden all the treasures of wisdom and knowledge.* The mature Christian (Colossians 1:28) is one who can look at the whole of reality through Christian eyes, understanding all things in light of what God has accomplished through redemption.

## Wisdom in Practice

What does this look like in James? The problems were concrete:

- The rich oppressed the poor—but they'll fade away (1:10). There's one Judge (4:12).
- Believers quarreled among themselves—inconsistent with knowing the gospel.
- Their deeds didn't match their faith—but the gospel calls for obedience, a lifestyle of good deeds and humility.

James 1:21-25 is key: *Get rid of all moral filth and the evil that is so prevalent, and humbly accept the word planted in you, which can save you... Do not merely listen to the word... Do what it says.*

The word must be buried deep inside, part of our very nature. The righteous are those who hear and respond.

## Guidance for Today

This raises a practical question for us: How do we make decisions? The responsibility is ours to make choices that conform to the reality revealed in the gospel—not through mystical impressions, but through understanding God's Word.

Whether it's choosing a movie, a career path, or how to spend your time: ask yourself, what does the gospel reveal about this? Renew your mind through studying the Bible. Take advantage of university, Bible conferences, Bible fellowships that prioritize Scripture study. Pick your community based on where the Word is studied seriously.

Every decision we make should be in light of the gospel of the cross. When you understand that Christ died for your sins, rose from the dead, was given all power and authority, and is coming back to judge and restore all things—that changes everything. In that light, what seems important to the world becomes foolish. The game of making money, collecting possessions—it's all passing away.

## The Wise Life

Who is wise? The one who shows it by his deeds. The one whose life reflects the transformation of the gospel. This is not perfection or instantaneous knowledge, but growth—as we study Scripture, as we understand more deeply what Christ has done, we make decisions more appropriately. Our lives become the outworking of the gospel, lived in humility and good works.

That's the wisdom from above.
