# Amos 5:18-27

**Title:** The Day of Judgment—Christmas Message
**Date:** December 20, 2019 (SMAC); December 19, 2021 (ARISE)
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Big Idea

The first Christmas was meant to be a day of judgment but turns out to be a day of hope.

## Introduction: The Stress of Christmas

It's looking a lot like Christmas again—and this season before the holiday is often the worst time of the year. We face stress from Christmas shopping, the anxiety of getting the right presents and decorations, and the depression that can come with forced gaiety and financial debt. Many miss loved ones during the holidays, and we accumulate mountains of waste. Yet within days, we can echo the words of Amos, speaking of his own "Christ-Amos"—his experience of the day of judgment.

## 1. Great Expectations (Amos 5:18-19)

> Woe to you who long for the day of the Lord! Why do you long for the day of the Lord? That day will be darkness, not light.

Throughout the Old Testament, God's people looked forward to the Day of the Lord with great expectation. From creation itself, the seventh day was set aside as a day of rest and peace—a day when God's creation would be in perfect order. But sin made that perfect day elusive. The promise, however, was that one day God would bring this day: a day of blessing, freedom from oppressors, rest from enemies, and freedom from death and disease—an end to war with sin itself.

King David, who lived 200 years before Amos, captured this hope in the Psalms:

> The Lord is my strength and my defense; he has become my salvation. Shouts of joy and victory resound in the tents of the righteous: "The Lord's right hand has done mighty things!" I will not die but live, and will proclaim what the Lord has done. This is the gate of the Lord through which the righteous may enter.

Yet Amos speaks of the day quite differently. Instead of the glorious day expected, he describes something shocking:

> It will be as though a man fled from a lion only to meet a bear, as though he entered his house and rested his hand on the wall only to have a snake bite him. Will not the day of the Lord be darkness, not light—pitch-dark, without a ray of brightness?

Amos uses a vivid metaphor from the wildlife of the ancient Middle East: escape the lion, only to encounter a bear in the forest; run to the safety of your house, only to be bitten by a snake on the wall. It's like a horror film—the villain you escape appears in the room you thought was safe. The Israelites expected to escape the death and corruption of this world on the day of judgment, but instead would find judgment waiting for them.

This is like expecting Christmas to be fun, only to have presents turn into snakes, candy canes into poison, feasts into vomit. The Israelites were expecting a glorious day but would instead face destruction.

## 2. Great Judgment (Amos 5:20-27)

Why would God judge His own people? Because their hearts were far from Him.

> "I hate, I despise your religious festivals; your assemblies are a stench to me. Even though you bring me burnt offerings and grain offerings, I will not accept them. Though you bring choice fellowship offerings, I will have no regard for them. Away with the noise of your songs! I will not listen to the music of your harps. But let justice roll on like a river, righteousness like a never-failing stream!"

God had commanded Israel to observe festivals and prescribed rituals and sacrifices as a means of dealing with sin and maintaining a personal, loving relationship with Him. But Israel had reduced this sacred system to mere ritual and form, even merging it with pagan practices. What was meant to help them remember God's blessings and mourn their sins became empty ceremony.

The temple became a place not of contemplation but of commerce—where elites took bribes, did their networking, and turned worship into an opportunity for profit. Their festivals had become vehicles for idolatry.

> "Did you bring me sacrifices and offerings forty years in the wilderness, people of Israel? You have lifted up the shrine of your king, the pedestal of your idols, the star of your god—which you made for yourselves."

The people went through the motions of worship while abandoning the heart of worship itself. They celebrated but forgot the meaning, leaving God out of the picture entirely.

This bears a striking similarity to Christmas today. The festival was meant to remember the birth of Christ—a time for thanksgiving, a reminder that God came to earth, an occasion for reconciliation and recognizing that the helpless are as important as the rich. But modern Christmas has become about selfishness, greed, anger, and profiteering. We celebrate the commercialized aspects while forgetting the meaning. We've lost the heart.

Society won't help us protect the true meaning of Christmas. Shopping centers and schools once played Christian hymns and nativity scenes; not anymore. We must be proactive. The heart has been cut out, just as the Israelites cut their hearts away from serving God.

> "Therefore I will send you into exile beyond Damascus," says the Lord, whose name is God Almighty.

Like their forty years in the wilderness, Israel would face exile—judgment for their hollow worship.

## 3. Great Expectations Redeemed (Luke 2:8-14)

But something surprising happens. Fast forward 500 years from Amos. The first Christmas turns out to be completely different from expectations—again.

> And there were shepherds living out in the fields nearby, keeping watch over their flocks at night. An angel of the Lord appeared to them, and the glory of the Lord shone around them, and they were terrified.

The shepherds were frightened. An angel and divine light appeared in the middle of nowhere. More than that, they felt the fear that comes when God draws near—the fear of judgment. Finally, God was in their midst.

But then the angel said:

> "Do not be afraid. I bring you good news that will cause great joy for all the people. Today in the town of David a Savior has been born to you; he is the Messiah, the Lord. This will be a sign to you: You will find a baby wrapped in cloths and lying in a manger."

Instead of God appearing with great chariots and an army of swords, instead of noise and overwhelming power—He appears as a baby in the quiet town of Bethlehem. The Messiah, the Lord, God incarnate appears as the most non-threatening person of all: a helpless, naked infant, wrapped only for protection from the cold.

> Suddenly a great company of the heavenly host appeared with the angel, praising God and saying, "Glory to God in the highest heaven, and on earth peace to those on whom his favor rests."

This is why we celebrate Christmas. Peace is here. The Prince of Peace. Emmanuel—God in the midst of us.

How do we reconcile these two visions: the expectation of judgment versus the day of celebration? Where did the judgment go?

God, in His mercy, delayed judgment. He sent us a baby—a gift. Judgment is deferred, but it is not canceled. It's like the tornado warning Kenny received before a storm hit Kurnell and Cronulla. The delay gave people time to protect themselves. God's delay in judgment gives us time to repent, to turn to Him.

But the delay wasn't cheap. The baby grew to be a man—Jesus—who died on the cross. He was struck by the full force of God's judgment so that the storm would lose its power by the time it reaches us. In contrast to the Israelites' failure to sacrifice, God brought the sacrifice Himself.

## Conclusion: Joy to the World

Given all this, let's enjoy Christmas! It's a day of repentance and a day of joy. Don't be a sourpuss.

Remember that God is giving us another day and another year to repent, extending His mercy one more time. Celebrate that act of patience. Within reason, eat and be merry, give gifts—but remember the reason for the season.
