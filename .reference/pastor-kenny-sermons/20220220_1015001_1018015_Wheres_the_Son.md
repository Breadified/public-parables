# Genesis 15:1–18:15

**Title:** Where's the Son?
**Date:** February 20, 2022
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## The Promise and Abraham's Doubt (15:1–6)

Abraham has waited a long time for the child God promised. He's accumulated wealth and blessings—from Egypt to his military victories—yet the one thing he desires most remains out of reach. The pain of childlessness weighs heavily, and he begins to doubt.

> **"Sovereign Lord, what can you give me since I remain childless and the one who will inherit my estate is Eliezer of Damascus?"** (Genesis 15:2)

God had just promised to be Abraham's great reward, yet Abraham's response reveals his struggle: "What can you give me?" When we're young, we don't think about legacies. But as we age, thoughts of mortality and inheritance naturally surface. Abraham's doubt is understandable—he lashes out at God because, frankly, who else can he blame? The act of creating life remains ultimately in God's hands, no matter our medical advances.

God's response is clear and unambiguous:

> **"This man will not be your heir, but a son who is your own flesh and blood will be your heir."** (Genesis 15:4)

When God says "son," He means *son*—not a nephew, not an adopted heir, not some creative interpretation. There are no loopholes with God.

Then comes one of Scripture's most profound moments. God takes Abraham outside and shows him the stars:

> **"Look up at the sky and count the stars—if indeed you can count them. So shall your offspring be."** (Genesis 15:5)

And Abraham's response marks a turning point:

> **"Abram believed the Lord, and he credited it to him as righteousness."** (Genesis 15:6)

This is justification by faith—but not in the way we often think. Faith has a peculiar quality: it's never about the subject (the one believing), but always about the object of faith. When Abraham believes God, he's not demonstrating his skill or ability. Rather, he's affirming that *God is trustworthy*. Faith says nothing about Abraham—it says everything about God's righteousness.

Yet Abraham's journey reveals the constant struggle of faith. Despite this moment of belief, he continues to wrestle:

> **"Sovereign Lord, how can I know that I will gain possession of it?"** (Genesis 15:8)

## The Covenant Signed in Blood (15:9–17)

God responds to Abraham's request with something extraordinary—a covenant ceremony. He instructs Abraham to bring specific animals: a heifer, a goat, a ram (all three years old), a dove, and a young pigeon. These weren't random choices; they're the animals that would later be used in sacrificial worship.

Abraham prepares the animals for an ancient Near Eastern covenant ritual. He cuts the larger animals in half and arranges the pieces opposite each other, leaving the birds whole. This wasn't simply a religious custom—it was a binding legal practice.

> **"Strange ritual but in the ancient near east, when people made promises to each other they would cut animals in half and lay the sundered parts across from one another to make a passageway in between. Then the parties would pass through the sundered parts."** This agreement would form a covenant or contract. In Hebrew, covenants are described as "cut"—to "cut a covenant." The blood signifies that if any of the parties fail to meet their commitments, the punishment would be similar: they would be "cut."

The Hebrew practice later echoes in Jeremiah:

> **"Those who have violated my covenant and have not fulfilled the terms of the covenant they made before me, I will treat like the calf they cut in two and then walked between its pieces."** (Jeremiah 34:18–19)

But here's what makes this moment crucial: Abraham falls asleep.

> **"As the sun was setting, Abram fell into a deep sleep, and a thick and dreadful darkness came over him."** (Genesis 15:12)

When Abraham wakes:

> **"When the sun had set and darkness had fallen, a smoking firepot with a blazing torch appeared and passed between the pieces."** (Genesis 15:17)

Throughout Scripture, God's presence is marked by fire—the burning bush to Moses, the lightning and smoke on Mount Sinai, the pillar of cloud and fire in the wilderness. Here, God Himself passes between the severed animals.

This is the stunning truth: Abraham prepared everything for a mutual covenant ceremony, but when it came time to walk between the pieces—to bind himself by blood—only God passed through. Only God would face the penalty if the covenant were broken. This is a *unilateral* covenant. God is saying, "If I fail to keep my promise, may I be cut as these animals are cut."

## The Promise Extended (15:13–16)

God then reveals the scope of His promise—not immediate, but cosmic:

> **"Know for certain that for four hundred years your descendants will be strangers in a country not their own and that they will be enslaved and mistreated there. But I will punish the nation they serve as slaves, and afterward they will come out with great possessions."** (Genesis 15:13–14)

Abraham's son is not merely a personal fulfillment—he's a cog in God's redemptive plan spanning centuries. The promise to Abraham is not "I want you to have the experience of parenthood" but rather "Your descendants will become a nation, and through them, all nations will be blessed."

God is constantly pushing Abraham to see beyond himself and his petty wishes. This is the lesson of the unilateral covenant: we cannot manipulate God's will. We cannot turn selfish desires into righteousness through clever schemes.

## The Attempt to Help God (16:1–16)

Ten years pass, and patience wears thin—not for Abraham, but for Sarai. She takes matters into her own hands.

> **"The Lord has kept me from having children. Go, sleep with my slave; perhaps I can build a family through her."** (Genesis 16:2)

Sarai had brought Hagar, her Egyptian servant, from Pharaoh's court years earlier. Now she offers a surrogacy arrangement: Abraham will sleep with Hagar, the child will be born, and Sarai will adopt the child. This way, they can manipulate God's will into existence.

Abraham agrees—perhaps too easily. We might wonder if Sarai expected him to refuse, to say, "No, Sarai. This is not your fault. We are part of God's plan, and we must be patient. I love you. You complete me. We must trust in God."

But he doesn't. Instead, Abraham compounds the problem by testing his wife's commitment, tempting her toward sin. The pattern repeats: when we tempt someone to break God's will, they do.

Hagar becomes pregnant, and immediately the household fractures:

> **"When she knew she was pregnant, she began to despise her mistress. Then Sarai said to Abram, 'You are responsible for the wrong I am suffering. I put my slave in your arms, and now that she knows she is pregnant, she despises me.'"** (Genesis 16:4–5)

This echoes Adam's blame-shifting in Eden: "The woman you put here." Sarai blames Abraham. Abraham, trying to reset the situation, tells Sarai to do with Hagar as she sees fit. Sarai mistreats her, and Hagar flees.

This is why polygamy doesn't work. You cannot have two wives and expect harmony. The attempt to help God fulfill His promise by taking matters into their own hands has created a child—Ishmael—who, while blessed by God, is not the promised heir. Abraham must wait longer still.

## The Covenant Renewed: Isaac Arrives (17:1–27)

Many years pass. Abraham is now ninety-nine years old when God appears again:

> **"I am God Almighty; walk before me faithfully and be blameless."** (Genesis 17:1)

Yet Abraham still doubts:

> **"Will a son be born to a man a hundred years old? Will Sarah bear a child at the age of ninety?"** (Genesis 17:17)

He asks, "If only Ishmael might live under your blessing!" Abraham and Sarah cling to the son they made, hoping perhaps Ishmael is God's plan. Maybe Eliezer? Maybe Lot? Surely there's no way they can bear a child now.

Then God introduces circumcision—a sign of the covenant:

> **"Every male among you shall be circumcised. You are to undergo circumcision, and it will be the sign of the covenant between me and you."** (Genesis 17:10–11)

**"Any uncircumcised male, who has not been circumcised in the flesh, will be cut off from his people; he has broken my covenant."** (Genesis 17:14)

This seems strange. If God's covenant is unilateral—if God will fulfill it regardless—why require circumcision? And why circumcision specifically?

The answer lies in understanding what circumcision represents. It comes *after* God's unilateral promise, not as a condition for it. Rather than creating a bilateral agreement, circumcision is a physical reminder—a repeated "cutting" of the covenant in the flesh itself. Every time a man goes to the toilet, he remembers: "God knows best. I don't." It's like a tattoo on the forehead, a teaching tool for generations.

Most remarkably, it directly addresses Abraham's struggle. His search for a son led him astray. Through Hagar and Ishmael, he tried to fulfill God's promise through his own efforts. Circumcision becomes a constant, intimate reminder to trust God's plan, not his own.

Yet God does not abandon Ishmael:

> **"Yes, but your wife Sarah will bear you a son, and you will call him Isaac. I will establish my covenant with him as an everlasting covenant for his descendants after him."** (Genesis 17:19)

The name itself carries meaning—Isaac means "laughter," a reminder that both Abraham and Sarah laughed in disbelief. Yet the promise is certain: "by this time next year" (Genesis 17:21). Even Ishmael will be blessed and become a great nation, but the covenant promise flows through Isaac.

## Patience and Trust

The pattern repeats throughout Scripture. Abraham's struggle mirrors our own. We see our immediate circumstances—today's worries, tomorrow's deadlines, the balance between competing demands. We doubt. We worry. We try to control outcomes and turn our selfish desires into righteousness.

But God continually calls us to see the big picture—the heavenly promised land kept for us. Jesus promised, "I have prepared a place for you" (John 14:2). The concerns of today, as important as they seem, pale into insignificance when compared to the ultimate goal.

The lesson is not to micromanage our lives, trying to shave percentage points of risk and control every variable. Instead, we must align our values with God and trust Him with the outcomes. Do we really think we can do a better job managing our lives than God?

Abraham learned, slowly and painfully, to leave the little things to God—to trust in His unilateral covenant, signed in blood, that cannot fail. His descendants became as numerous as the stars. Through them came the Messiah.

We too are called to patience. The second coming of Christ is taking longer than we hoped, but we must continue to trust. Just as God kept His promise to Abraham—naming Isaac, giving him a son born of Sarah when she was ninety—we can be confident that God will keep His promises to us.

Continue to walk before Him faithfully, blameless, trusting in the One who has already cut the covenant in blood.
