## Opening Prayer for the Grieving

Our church has been hit by a wave of grief. In the last fortnight, we've lost several family members of our congregation—grandfathers, grandmothers, mothers-in-law, sisters-in-law, and uncles. We pause to pray for those who are grieving, and through them, we share in their sorrow.

Our church is not isolated. We are part of a network of families, and our grief reminds us of our interconnectedness in Christ.

## The Mystery of the Gospel: Gentiles as Heirs

Paul's central ambition was clear:

> "It has always been my ambition to preach the gospel where Christ was not known."

This mission had three dimensions:

1. **Gentiles Called to Glorify God** (vv. 1-14)
2. **Paul's Apostolic Authority to Lead the Gentiles** (vv. 15-23)
3. **The Service of the Gentiles** (vv. 24-33)

### The Gospel for All Nations

In the Old Testament, the Gentiles held an ambiguous place in God's plan. They were sometimes portrayed as second-class citizens—think of the Court of the Gentiles in the Temple, where they could worship but without the same privileges as Jews. Many expected that if Gentiles were ever included in God's covenant, they would need to become Jewish first, undergoing circumcision.

But the gospel changed everything. God's new revelation astonished the early church: the gospel is not just for Jews, but for Gentiles—the nations—as full heirs of God's promise, without requiring conversion to Judaism first.

> Romans 3:29 "Or is God the God of Jews only? Is he not the God of Gentiles too? Yes, of Gentiles too."

This is the mystery Paul keeps emphasizing:

> Romans 16:25-27 "Now to him who is able to establish you in accordance with my gospel, the message I proclaim about Jesus Christ, in keeping with the revelation of the mystery hidden for long ages past, but now revealed... so that all the Gentiles might come to the obedience that comes from faith."

> Ephesians 3:6 "This mystery is that through the gospel the Gentiles are heirs together with Israel, members together of one body, and sharers together in the promise in Christ Jesus."

> Galatians 3:28 "There is neither Jew nor Gentile, neither slave nor free, nor is there male and female, for you are all one in Christ Jesus."

This doesn't mean sameness in every way, but rather an equality of standing before God.

### An Illustration: The Roman Household

Imagine a Roman household. Who belongs? Not just the landowner's family, but also servants, maids, and laborers—all living together in one house serving the master.

The Jewish people may have imagined God's family this way: Jews as the inner family in the temple, Gentiles as servants and maids outside in the temple court—all together glorifying God, but in different roles, with different privileges.

But the mystery of the gospel reveals something revolutionary: Gentiles are not second-class servants. They are children of God in their own right.

### A Modern Application: On Racism and Equality

The Jew-Gentile distinction is ancient, yet it speaks powerfully to our modern discussions about racism and justice.

Not long ago, Australians voted on whether to enshrine a special Voice for Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander peoples in our Constitution. While I understand the reasoning—seeking to address historical injustices—I believe a race-based law troubles the gospel's vision.

If we want to help the marginalized, let us help all the marginalized—the poor Anglos, the African poor, the voiceless immigrants. If we want to establish welfare for those in need, why create a box labeled "Aboriginal/Torres Strait Islander" rather than "those who need help"?

The solution to racism is not reverse racism toward other races. As we drift from Christian foundations, we lose the Christian understanding of equality before God.

The mystery of Christ has been revealed. Yet we act as if we want to hide it again.

## Paul's Apostolic Authority: Leading the Gentiles

### Paul's Ministry as Priestly Service

Paul describes his role in remarkable language:

> Romans 15:15-16 "I have written you quite boldly on some points to remind you of them again, because of the grace God gave me to be a minister of Christ Jesus to the Gentiles. He gave me the priestly duty of proclaiming the gospel of God, so that the Gentiles might become an offering acceptable to God, sanctified by the Holy Spirit."

Notice the priestly language. Paul sees himself as:
- A **minister** (leiurgeo—not deacon) of Christ Jesus to the Gentiles
- A **priest** performing the priestly duty of proclaiming God's gospel
- Someone who presents the Gentiles as an offering to God

This authority came from Jesus himself:

> Acts 9:15 "Go! This man is my chosen instrument to proclaim my name to the Gentiles and their kings and to the people of Israel."

And it was acknowledged throughout the early church. Paul was seen as fulfilling Isaiah's vision of the suffering servant, continuing Jesus's work:

> Acts 13:46-47 "We had to speak the word of God to you first. Since you reject it and do not consider yourselves worthy of eternal life, we now turn to the Gentiles. For this is what the Lord has commanded us: 'I have made you a light for the Gentiles, that you may bring salvation to the ends of the earth.'"

This quotes Isaiah 49:6: a servant called to be a light to the nations.

### Paul's Missionary Strategy

Paul explains his unique mission:

> Romans 15:17-20 "I glory in Christ Jesus in my service to God. I will not venture to speak of anything except what Christ has accomplished through me in leading the Gentiles to obey God by what I have said and done—by the power of signs and wonders, through the power of the Spirit of God. From Jerusalem all the way around to Illyricum, I have fully proclaimed the gospel of Christ. It has always been my ambition to preach the gospel where Christ was not known, so that I would not be building on someone else's foundation."

Signs and wonders accompanied the apostolic ministry, but these are not repeatable today. The apostolic age passed; their writings became the New Testament we possess.

Paul's ambition was to pioneer new territory, reaching people who had never heard Christ's name. He had moved from Jerusalem all the way to Illyricum (modern-day Albania)—a vast geographical sweep.

> Romans 15:23-24 "But now that there is no more place for me in these regions, and since I have been longing for many years to visit you, I plan to do so when I go to Spain. I hope to see you while passing through and to have you assist me on my journey there."

Scholars debate what Paul meant by "no more place." Some suggest he was respecting the work of other apostles and missionaries, wanting to avoid building on their foundations. Others argue he was pursuing a missionary strategy of reaching the unreached.

But the language "unreached" or "less reached" can be misleading. Whether a billion people or a thousand remain unbelieving, the real work happens locally—person to person. Gospel proclamation is labor-intensive: it's youth leaders, Sunday school teachers, Scripture teachers, neighbors talking to neighbors about Jesus. The statistics matter less than the reality of whether my neighbor knows Christ.

> Romans 15:21 "As it is written: 'Those who were not told about him will see, and those who have not heard will understand.'"

Paul's role was unique. He was laying apostolic foundations in new territories, fulfilling Old Testament prophecies about light coming to the nations. But Rome itself presented a puzzle: why would Paul go to Rome if he didn't want to build on someone else's foundation? Perhaps because Rome was no longer the beginning point—it had become the center from which the gospel would spread to the ends of the earth.

### An Illustration: The Partner and the Associate

Think of a law firm: a senior partner comes in for a day or an hour, does the high-level deal, charges $2,000 an hour, then leaves the junior associate to "dot the I's and cross the T's" at $200 an hour.

Jesus arrived with three years of extraordinary ministry—healing, teaching, and ultimately dying and rising again. Paul spent the next thirty years executing that work, bringing the gospel to the ends of the known world. By the time Paul reached Rome, you could say he had completed most of the project.

Tradition suggests he may have reached Spain as well. Some sources indicate he was released from his first imprisonment and made a fourth missionary journey to Spain, then was arrested again under Nero and executed. But regardless, the Book of Acts closes in Rome.

From Rome—the center of the civilized world—the gospel spread via Roman roads to the ends of the earth. From Roman Britain, the gospel would travel to England's colonies, including eventually to Australia and to you.

The gospel reaching the world has been theologically completed. But Paul's work reminds us: he had a job for the Gentiles to do as well.

## The Service of the Gentiles: The Collection for Jerusalem

### The Symbolic Pilgrimage

Before heading to Spain, Paul had one more crucial task. He was traveling back to Jerusalem to deliver a collection from the Gentile churches:

> Romans 15:25-29 "Now, however, I am on my way to Jerusalem in the service of the Lord's people there. For Macedonia and Achaia were pleased to make a contribution for the poor among the Lord's people in Jerusalem. They were pleased to do it, and indeed they owe it to them. For if the Gentiles have shared in the Jews' spiritual blessings, they owe it to the Jews to share with them their material blessings. So after I have completed this task and have made sure that they have received this contribution, I will go to Spain and visit you on the way. I know that when I come to you, I will come in the full measure of the blessing of Christ."

This sounds like travel plans—an itinerary. But why did Paul risk his life by personally delivering the collection when someone else could have brought it? And why did he travel with a large delegation of church representatives?

### The Pilgrimage of the Nations

Something deeper is happening here. Paul uses priestly language, and there's a prophetic undercurrent running through the Old Testament about the end times.

In the ancient prophecies, the nations would flow to Zion with gifts:

> Isaiah 18:7 "At that time gifts will be brought to the Lord Almighty from a people tall and smooth-skinned, from a people feared far and wide... the gifts will be brought to Mount Zion, the place of the Name of the Lord Almighty."

> Isaiah 60:14 "The children of your oppressors will come bowing before you; all who despise you will bow down at your feet."

The Old Testament envisioned a day when Gentiles would submit to Israel, bringing tribute like the procession of a victorious Roman general entering the city with captured treasures and spoils.

Now the Jews have largely rejected the gospel. But Paul is undeterred. He is bringing a pilgrimage of Gentiles—a procession—to Jerusalem with their gifts. He is the priest leading this procession, not of servants, but of those who have become God's slaves by their own faith.

Through this symbolic act, Paul hopes to signal to the Jews that the end times have come. The Gentiles are here, bringing their offerings to Mount Zion. Perhaps this will provoke the Jewish people to jealousy, making them realize what they're missing—that the Messiah has come, that God has accepted the Gentiles as His people.

This connects to Paul's strategy mentioned in Romans 9-11: provoking Israel to jealousy through the salvation of the Gentiles.

### The Call to Prayer

> Romans 15:30-33 "I urge you, brothers and sisters, by our Lord Jesus Christ and by the love of the Spirit, to join me in my struggle by praying to God for me. Pray that I may be kept safe from the unbelievers in Judea and that the contribution I take to Jerusalem may be favorably received by the Lord's people there, so that I may come to you with joy, by God's will, and in your company be refreshed. The God of peace be with you all. Amen."

Paul asks for prayer—for his safety and for the acceptance of this gift. He senses danger ahead. History tells us he faced significant opposition, though Acts curiously doesn't record the collection's delivery. Paul would eventually reach Rome as a prisoner awaiting trial.

## Application: Our Ambition in Light of God's Mercy

### We Are the Priestly Offering

> Romans 12:1 "Therefore, I urge you, brothers and sisters, in view of God's mercy, to offer your bodies as a living sacrifice, holy and pleasing to God—this is your true and proper worship."

We are the continuation of Paul's priestly work. The Gentiles Paul gathered are now us. We are the living offering presented to God.

### What Is Your Ambition?

In light of God's mercy, what drives you?

Paul's ambition was clear: preach where Christ was not known. Not career advancement. Not accumulating possessions. Not checking off bucket lists or keeping up with neighbors' materialism.

**The end times are here. Death surrounds us. We are playing for keeps.**

Our choices to follow God—or not—are permanent, unable to be reversed for eternity.

This demands action:

- **Proclaim the gospel.** With your words, your life, your witness.
- **Build infrastructure.** Support the institutions and leaders who continue teaching the gospel.
- **Have children.** Raise the next generation of gospel proclaimers.
- **Serve anywhere.** Here, in your neighborhood, across the world.

Put away earthly ambitions. Your aspiration is not to visit foreign countries or match your neighbors' experiences.

**Your ambition is heavenly.**

We desire God to return. We work for all people to be prepared for His coming. Until then, we serve as priests, presenting ourselves and our communities as an offering to the Lord.
