# Luke 19:28-48

**Title:** Welcoming the King
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## The Expected King (28-35)

Jesus approached the end of his journey through Luke's Gospel and arrived at the Mount of Olives, overlooking Jerusalem. Here he gave his disciples strange instructions: go into the village ahead, find a young donkey that no one had ever ridden, untie it, and bring it to him. If anyone questioned them, they should simply say, "The Lord needs it."

This was an unusual request—the kind that wouldn't normally work in any era. Yet when the disciples went, they found everything exactly as Jesus had said. Even the owners, when they asked why the disciples were taking their donkey, received the simple reply, "The Lord needs it," and they released it.

What was Jesus doing? The answer lies in the Old Testament. Five hundred years before Christ, the prophet Zechariah predicted that Israel's king would come riding on a donkey:

> Rejoice greatly, Daughter Zion!  
> Shout, Daughter Jerusalem!  
> See, your king comes to you,  
> righteous and victorious,  
> lowly and riding on a donkey,  
> on a colt, the foal of a donkey.

To see the donkey was to see the king. Jesus was signaling that he was the expected king.

### Why a Donkey?

A donkey seems an odd choice to our modern ears. But in Hebrew culture, it made perfect sense. The Israelites were forbidden from importing horses from Egypt, so donkeys were the natural choice for their mountainous terrain. Throughout Israel's history, the donkey was actually the mount of the elite—a sign of wealth and rulership. Judges 10:4 and 12:14 describe judges who rode donkeys as signs of their authority and success.

Beyond Zechariah, other Old Testament passages linked the donkey with the king. In Genesis 49, the dying Jacob blessed his son Judah (Jesus's ancestor) saying, "He will tether his donkey to a vine, his colt to the choicest branch"—a symbol of kingly prosperity.

Most significantly, the donkey symbolized peace. Zechariah 9:10 continues with the vision of the king who brings not conquest but peace:

> I will take away the chariots from Ephraim  
> and the warhorses from Jerusalem,  
> and the battle bow will be broken.  
> He will proclaim peace to the nations.

In every way, the donkey announced: here comes the king who will bring peace.

## The Expected Reception (36-38)

Jesus received an extraordinary welcome. As he rode down from the Mount of Olives, the crowd spread their cloaks on the road before him—an act that symbolically declared him king. There was precedent for this: in 2 Kings 9, when one of Israel's kings was anointed, his followers spread their cloaks beneath him and blew trumpets, shouting, "He is king!"

The disciples began to praise God with loud voices, singing words from Psalm 118:

> Blessed is he who comes in the name of the Lord.  
> Peace in heaven and glory in the highest!

This was more than a casual greeting. It was a royal procession—the kind that followed a king's victory and conquest. In the ancient world, such processions included tributes, gifts, and all the pageantry of a triumphant return. The people were welcoming Jesus as the long-awaited king with the honor he deserved.

## The Unexpected Reception (39, 47-48)

But not everyone welcomed him.

Some of the Pharisees in the crowd told Jesus to rebuke his disciples and make them be quiet. The religious leaders did not want attention drawn to him. They did not want the crowds proclaiming him as king.

This tension escalated into something darker. Later, we learn that the chief priests, the teachers of the law, and the leaders of the people were actively looking for a way to destroy Jesus. They sought his death. This was shocking—these were the very people who knew the Old Testament, who were familiar with Zechariah 9 and Psalm 118, who should have recognized the king when he arrived. Yet they rejected him.

## The Unexpected King (40-46)

When the Pharisees demanded that Jesus silence his disciples, he responded: "I tell you, if they were to keep silent, the stones would cry out!"

This was a profound statement. Jesus was not merely a king for human audiences. He was king over all creation. His kingship was so evident, so undeniable, that even inanimate stones would burst into praise. The fact that dead stones would cry out while living people refused to acknowledge him stood as a judgment on the religious leaders—their silence was a greater sin than the stones' praise.

But there was more. Jesus had not come to establish an earthly military kingdom. He came as the unexpected king—unexpected because he came not with military power but with a message of forgiveness and peace. Yet the religious leaders were troubled not by his claims of divinity but by his moral authority. He condemned their hypocrisy:

> Beware of the teachers of the law. They like to walk around in flowing robes and love to be greeted with respect in the marketplaces and have the most important seats in the synagogues and the places of honor at banquets. They devour widows' houses and for a show make lengthy prayers.

As Jesus drew near to Jerusalem and saw the city, he wept over it. His response was not anger but sorrow. He said:

> If you knew this day what would bring you peace—but now it is hidden from your eyes. For the days will come on you when your enemies will build an embankment against you, surround you, and hem you in on every side. They will crush you and your children within you to the ground, and they will not leave one stone on another in you, because you did not recognize the time of your visitation.

The city had missed its moment. God had come to them, and they did not recognize him. This visitation—this direct appearance of God himself—would never come again in the same way. The consequence would be catastrophic.

When Jesus entered the temple, he began to drive out those who were selling, saying, "It is written, 'My house will be a house of prayer,' but you have made it a den of robbers." The temple, meant to be a place of prayer, had become a marketplace. The religious leaders had corrupted even this sacred space.

## How Do We Welcome the King?

Here is the critical question: How do you receive Jesus in his first coming?

Jesus appeared to humanity two thousand years ago through the reliable historical records we have—the Gospel accounts. Some claim they would believe if God would appear to them personally today. But consider this: God did appear. When Jesus walked the earth, most people rejected him and sought his death. Even a direct, personal appearance offers no guarantee of faith.

Moreover, God does not expect us to rely on miraculous appearances. Luke 16:27-31 suggests that God calls us to trust in his word—the testimony of Moses and the prophets—rather than in signs and wonders. We have the Scripture, and that is sufficient.

Two thousand years have not diminished the reality of Christ's first coming or changed what he offers. The word of God in your own language, in words you can read and study, is God speaking to you: recognize the time of your visitation.

Yet there is another visitation coming—the return of Christ. For those who reject him now, that return will bring not welcome but judgment. He will come in his full power and glory, and those who have not repented will face his wrath. The question is not whether he will come, but whether you will be ready.

If you would welcome Jesus now—if you would spread your garments before him in submission and acknowledge him as king—then his second coming will bring an entirely different reception:

- Your sins are forgiven
- Your reward is great
- Your faith has made you well
- You will eat your fill
- Rejoice, for yours is the kingdom
- Here is the crown of life
- Well done, good and faithful servant

Will you welcome him?
