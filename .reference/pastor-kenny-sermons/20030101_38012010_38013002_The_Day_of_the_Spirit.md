# Zechariah 12:10-13:2

**Title:** The Day of the Spirit
**Date:** January 2003
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## A Day of Both Mourning and Rejoicing

Life often contains bittersweet moments—two opposing ideas held together. Think of leaving school but looking forward to university, or getting to college while having to leave friends behind. There's exhilaration but also sadness. Baptism carries this same quality: a time of mourning and a time of rejoicing.

> 12:10 "And I will pour out on the house of David and the inhabitants of Jerusalem a spirit of grace and supplication. They will look on me, the one they have pierced, and they will mourn for him as one mourns for an only child, and grieve bitterly for him as one grieves for a firstborn son."

This verse summarizes the passage beautifully—it contains both dimensions: time of rejoicing and time of mourning.

## Part One: A Day of Mourning (12:11-14)

### Historical Echoes of Grief

> 12:11 "On that day the weeping in Jerusalem will be as great as the weeping of Hadad Rimmon in the plain of Megiddo."

This reference points to two significant mourning events in Israel's history. The first involves Hadad of Aram, who killed King Ahab of Israel in battle:

> "But someone drew his bow at random and hit the king of Israel between the sections of his armor... As the sun was setting, a cry spread through the army: 'Every man to his town. Every man to his land!' So the king died and was brought to Samaria, and they buried him there." (1 Kings 22:34-37)

The second refers to King Josiah's death at Megiddo:

> "While Josiah was king, Pharaoh Necho king of Egypt went up to the Euphrates River to help the king of Assyria. King Josiah marched out to meet him in battle, but Necho faced him and killed him at Megiddo." (2 Kings 23:29)

> "Archers shot King Josiah... He was buried in the tombs of his ancestors, and all Judah and Jerusalem mourned for him. Jeremiah composed laments for Josiah, and to this day all the male and female singers commemorate Josiah in the laments." (2 Chronicles 35:23-25)

Both kings were pierced by arrows. Both deaths brought national mourning.

### An End-Times Mourning

The reference to Megiddo carries eschatological weight. Megiddo is the Hill of Megiddo—Armageddon in Greek—the place of the final battle on earth. Zechariah is describing an end-of-the-world type of mourning:

> "Then they gathered the kings together to the place that in Hebrew is called Armageddon." (Revelation 16:16)

### Universal Mourning

> 12:12-14 "The land will mourn, each clan by itself, with their wives by themselves: the clan of the house of David and their wives, the clan of the house of Nathan and their wives, the clan of the house of Levi and their wives, the clan of Shimei and their wives, and all the rest of the clans and their wives."

Everyone mourns. David represents the royal line; Nathan was David's son. Levi represents the priestly line; Shimei was Levi's grandson. These represent all of Israel—everyone joins in mourning over loss of leadership and, perhaps more deeply, over their own guilt.

### The Pierced One

Zechariah himself was likely killed during this period. But Jesus speaks about this pattern of rejected prophets:

> "Woe to you, because you build tombs for the prophets, and it was your ancestors who killed them... Therefore this generation will be held responsible for the blood of all the prophets that has been shed since the beginning of the world, from the blood of Abel to the blood of Zechariah, who was killed between the altar and the sanctuary." (Luke 11:47-51)

Ultimately, this passage points to Jesus himself—the one they pierced:

> "When they came to the place called the Skull, they crucified him there, along with the criminals." (Luke 23:33)

The world mourned his death:

> "A large number of people followed him, including women who mourned and wailed for him... As the sun was setting, the centurion... said, 'Surely this was a righteous man.' When all the people who had gathered to witness this sight saw what took place, they beat their breasts and went away." (Luke 23:27-28, 47-48)

> 12:10b "They will look on me, the one they have pierced, and they will mourn for him as one mourns for an only child, and grieve bitterly for him as one grieves for a firstborn son."

This is mourning over death—the most profound kind.

### Application: Baptism as Spiritual Death

Baptism is also a day of mourning over death:

> "Or don't you know that all of us who were baptized into Christ Jesus were baptized into his death?" (Romans 6:3)

When we are baptized, we die—not literally, but spiritually. We are baptized into *his* death, joining Christ in his sacrifice for sin. Just as Jesus died for sin, Christians die to sin. We acknowledge that we have failed and cannot handle our sin alone. Baptism is a funeral for our old, weak self—a putting away of our self-sufficiency.

Like Eric learning to swim, that day symbolically kills several things: his swimming skills (his self-reliance), his pretense, his pride, his ego. Similarly, the baptized die to slavery to sin. Their chains are broken. They mourn the person they were.

## Part Two: A Day of Rejoicing (12:10, 13:1-2)

### The Fountain of Cleansing

> 13:1 "On that day a fountain will be opened to the house of David and the inhabitants of Jerusalem, to cleanse them from sin and impurity."

To those mourning comes a spring of water—in a desert, this means life and new hope. Here it serves a cleansing function. Imagine great mourning producing tears—the very tears that remove the dirt from our eyes, flushing our impurities and returning our sight.

### Removal of Idols and Impurity

> 13:2 "On that day, I will banish the names of the idols from the land, and they will be remembered no more. I will remove both the prophets and the spirit of impurity from the land."

The idols Israel followed—their foolishness—are not just removed; even their names are banished and forgotten. When mistakes are wiped out and forgotten, you can be yourself again. Israel becomes clean—a fresh start.

### The Spirit of Grace Replaces Impurity

Contrast two spirits: the spirit of impurity is removed because a new spirit arrives—the spirit of grace.

> 12:10 "And I will pour out on the house of David and the inhabitants of Jerusalem a spirit of grace and supplication."

Grace means unmerited favor. Supplication means mercy—undeserved kindness. When the spirit of grace is poured out, the spirit of impurity departs. It's like the ocean: waves come in, but unless water floods the shore indefinitely, water must also flow out. When grace comes in, impurity goes out.

### Grace Through the Pierced One

For Christians, Jesus has accomplished this. Through him, we've been given grace and mercy. As the pierced one, Jesus was pierced for our transgression. We who deserved punishment are undeservedly freed instead. Through this act, we are forgiven by God.

### New Life After Death

> "We were therefore buried with him through baptism into death in order that, just as Christ was raised from the dead through the glory of the Father, we too may live a new life... our old self was crucified with him so that the body ruled by sin might be done away with... anyone who has died has been set free from sin." (Romans 6:4-7)

Just as entering the water symbolizes death, coming up out of the water symbolizes new life—a gasping breath of fresh air. The old has gone. The new has come.

### Living as Forgiven People

> "For sin shall no longer be your master, because you are not under the law, but under grace." (Romans 6:14)

Where grace moves in, sin moves out. The baptized, and indeed all Christians, will sin again. But the difference is that we call on God to forgive us, and he will—in Jesus' name. We die to sin, mourn our death, but rejoice in our forgiveness. This is God's incredible assurance: he always loves us in a true way.

Like a child who knows he's done wrong yet can go to his Father, admit the truth, and find reconciliation, love, and forgiveness—we too can return. We can rejoice.

## Conclusion: The Day of the Spirit

The Day of the Spirit reveals both realities:

- **The awfulness of sin**—demonstrated in mourning, death, and judgment
- **The sufficiency of grace**—demonstrated in forgiveness, cleansing, and new life

It is wonderful to see people baptized, for it means the Day of the Spirit is here.
