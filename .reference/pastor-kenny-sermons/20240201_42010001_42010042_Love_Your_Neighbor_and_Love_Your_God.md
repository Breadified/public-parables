# Luke 10:1–42

**Title:** Love Your Neighbor and Love Your God
**Date:** 4 February 2024 (ARISE)
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Question of the Lawyer

A lawyer stood up to test Jesus with a crucial question: "Teacher, what must I do to inherit eternal life?" This wasn't a casual inquiry—it was a test of Jesus's authority and understanding of the Law.

Jesus responded characteristically by turning the question back: "What is written in the Law? How do you read it?"

The lawyer answered perfectly, reciting the greatest commandments:

> "Love the Lord your God with all your heart and with all your soul and with all your strength and with all your mind; and, 'Love your neighbor as yourself.'"

Jesus affirmed him: "You have answered correctly. Do this and you will live."

But the lawyer wanted to justify himself, so he asked the critical follow-up question: "And who is my neighbor?"

## Understanding "Neighbor"

The Greek word *plēsion* (neighbor) carries deeper meaning than proximity. In the Old Testament tradition, the word often refers to someone within a covenant relationship—those bound together by mutual agreements and obligations. The term appears in contexts of social contracts and binding agreements, where parties swear oaths to one another and commit to equitable treatment.

In Leviticus, "neighbor" stood in parallelism with "the children of your own people"—fellow Israelites. Later, the command was extended to include the sojourner (*gēr*) living in the land, yet not to strangers beyond the covenant community. This created a boundary question: Who exactly qualifies as my neighbor?

The lawyer was operating within a legal tradition that wrestled with defining these boundaries. Yet Jesus was about to redefine neighborly love entirely.

## The Parable of the Good Samaritan

Jesus responded with a parable that completely reframed the question:

> "A man was going down from Jerusalem to Jericho, when he was attacked by robbers. They stripped him of his clothes, beat him and went away, leaving him half dead."

The journey itself was dangerous—the road from Jerusalem to Jericho descended sharply and was notorious for brigands. The victim was left in a desperate condition, beyond help without intervention.

Three figures encountered him:

**The Priest** passed by on the other side. He saw the man but did nothing. Perhaps he was concerned about ritual purity, unwilling to touch blood or a corpse.

**The Levite** did the same. He also passed by on the other side, ignoring the suffering man.

**The Samaritan** came where the man was. When he saw him, something stirred within him—the Greek word *splagchnizomai*, which means he was moved with compassion from the depths of his being. His visceral reaction compelled action.

> "He went to him and bandaged his wounds, pouring on oil and wine. Then he put the man on his own donkey, brought him to an inn and took care of him. The next day he took out two denarii and gave them to the innkeeper. 'Look after him,' he said, 'and when I return, I will reimburse you for any extra expense you may have.'"

The Samaritan's response was complete: he bound the wounds, provided comfort and healing care, bore the cost personally, ensured continued care, and promised future reimbursement. He treated the stranger as his responsibility.

## The Revolutionary Answer

Jesus then asked the lawyer: "Which of these three do you think was a neighbor to the man who fell into the hands of robbers?"

The lawyer could only answer: "The one who had mercy on him."

Notice—the lawyer couldn't bring himself to say "the Samaritan." A Samaritan was historically an enemy, a heretic in Jewish eyes. Yet Jesus had chosen the most unlikely candidate to embody true neighborly love.

Jesus's final command was unmistakable: "Go and do likewise."

The question had been completely inverted. The lawyer asked, "Who is my neighbor?" expecting a list of criteria and boundaries. Jesus answered by showing that neighborly love isn't about categories—it's about compassion in action. Your neighbor is whoever crosses your path in need and calls forth your mercy.

## Martha and Mary: The Priority of Communion

Immediately following this teaching, Jesus and his disciples came to a village where Martha welcomed them into her home. The contrast with the Good Samaritan parable is instructive—both involve hospitality, but with different emphases.

Martha was distracted by all the preparations that had to be made. She bustled about, managing tasks, ensuring everything was done properly. Then she approached Jesus with a complaint: "Lord, don't you care that my sister has left me to do the work by myself? Tell her to help me!"

Mary, by contrast, sat at the Lord's feet listening to what he said.

Jesus's response to Martha was gentle but clear:

> "Martha, Martha, you are worried and upset about many things, but few things are needed—or indeed only one. Mary has chosen what is better, and it will not be taken away from her."

## The Teaching: Right Action and Right Devotion

These two stories together reveal the fullness of the greatest commandments.

The Good Samaritan exemplifies the second commandment: loving your neighbor with concrete, sacrificial action. When you encounter need, you respond. You don't calculate whether someone deserves your help. You act from compassion.

But Martha and Mary illustrate the first commandment: loving God with your whole heart, soul, strength, and mind. At its core, this means sitting at Jesus's feet. It means prioritizing communion with God over even legitimate work. Service matters, but not at the expense of intimacy with the Lord.

Martha was caught in an inversion—she had made the external work primary and her relationship with Jesus secondary. She was "worried and upset about many things," distracted from the one thing that truly mattered: his presence.

Mary understood the hierarchy. She chose what is better, and that choice will not be taken away from her. Her presence at Jesus's feet was an act of worship that superseded the demands of hospitality.

## The Mercy Posture

Both passages emphasize a quality of response that transcends rules and categories: mercy (*eleos*). The Samaritan was moved with compassion and responded with mercy. The lawyer had to acknowledge that mercy—not legal status, not proximity, not covenant boundaries—defined true neighborly love.

Jesus calls his followers to this same posture: seeing human need, being moved inwardly, and responding with sacrificial love. Not from duty, but from a heart transformed by encounter with God's mercy.

And that transformed heart flows from time spent at his feet, from being shaped by his presence, from choosing the better thing—communion with him that becomes the foundation for genuine love of neighbor.
