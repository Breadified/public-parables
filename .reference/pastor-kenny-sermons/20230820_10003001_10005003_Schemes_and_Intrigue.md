# 2 Samuel 3-5:3

**Title:** Schemes and Intrigue

**Date:** August 20, 2023

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Overview: God Advances His Kingdom

As we survey David's path to becoming king of all Israel, we encounter a season that appears full of political maneuvering, betrayal, and violence. Yet beneath these human schemes lies a profound truth: God alone advances His kingdom. While others plot and position themselves, David's posture reveals a deeper confidence—that righteousness and trust in the Lord's promises matter more than tactical manipulation.

---

## The Context: A Kingdom in Transition

> 3:1 The war between the house of Saul and the house of David lasted a long time. David grew stronger and stronger, while the house of Saul grew weaker and weaker.

After Saul's death, civil war engulfs Israel. Saul's son Ish-Bosheth claims the throne in the north, while David reigns over Judah in the south. The outcome seems predetermined by human strength, yet the path forward involves unexpected twists that test David's character and his trust in God's covenant promise.

---

## Part 1: Abner's Political Calculation

### The Scandal (3:6-11)

> 6 During the war between the house of Saul and the house of David, Abner had been strengthening his own position in the house of Saul. 7 Now Saul had had a concubine named Rizpah daughter of Aiah. And Ish-Bosheth said to Abner, "Why did you sleep with my father's concubine?"

A crisis exposes the true dynamics of power in Saul's kingdom. Abner—Saul's military commander and the real seat of authority—has been consolidating influence by taking one of the former king's concubines. In ancient Near Eastern politics, access to a king's women signified legitimacy and succession rights. By sleeping with Rizpah, Abner was signaling his ambitions.

Ish-Bosheth, the weak king, dares to confront him:

> 8 Abner was very angry because of what Ish-Bosheth said. So he answered, "Am I a dog's head—on Judah's side?"

**The Turning Point:** Abner's fury reveals how fragile Ish-Bosheth's position truly is. The king fears his own commander more than he fears David.

### Abner's Hedge (3:12-21)

Recognizing the inevitable, Abner decides to change allegiances:

> 12 Then Abner sent messengers on his behalf to say to David, "Whose land is it? Make an agreement with me, and I will help you bring all Israel over to you."

This is calculated politics. Abner sees which way the wind is blowing—David is growing stronger, the house of Saul is weakening. Rather than go down with a sinking ship, he offers his considerable military power to David.

David responds strategically but with a moral demand:

> 13 "Good," said David. "I will make an agreement with you. But I demand one thing of you: Do not come into my presence unless you bring Michal daughter of Saul when you come to see me."

**Michal's Story:** Michal was David's wife—she had risked her life warning him when Saul tried to kill him. Saul later gave her to another man, Paltiel, while David was fleeing. Now, as part of the peace covenant, David demands her return.

> 14 Then David sent messengers to Ish-Bosheth son of Saul, demanding, "Give me my wife Michal, whom I betrothed to myself for the price of a hundred Philistine foreskins."

This demand carries layers of meaning. David won Michal by prowess—by killing Philistines. Now, as he consolidates power, he reclaims what was taken from him. But there's something deeper here too: **restoring the marriage covenant matters.**

When Ish-Bosheth orders Michal taken from Paltiel, her other husband follows her weeping—a poignant reminder that covenant bonds don't simply dissolve when circumstances change. Scripture teaches that marriage creates a profound union that remains binding. Even when separated by force or circumstance, the original covenant persists. Paltiel's grief witnesses to this truth.

### Abner Gains Support (3:17-20)

> 17 Abner conferred with the elders of Israel and said, "For some time you have wanted to make David your king. 18 Now do it! For the Lord promised David, 'By my servant David I will rescue my people Israel from the hand of the Philistines and from the hand of all their enemies.'"

Notice what Abner does: he appeals to God's promise. He acknowledges that the Lord Himself has destined David for kingship. Then he moves through the tribes—especially Benjamin, Saul's own tribe—to build consensus.

> 19 Then he went to Hebron to tell David everything that Israel and the whole tribe of Benjamin wanted to do.

> 20 When Abner, who had twenty men with him, came to David at Hebron, David prepared a feast for him and his men. 21 Then Abner said to David, "Let me go at once and assemble all Israel for my lord the king, so that they may make a covenant with you, and that you may rule over all that your heart desires." So David sent Abner away, and **he went in peace.**

Everything appears to be falling into place. The pieces are aligning. Peace seems within reach.

---

## Part 2: Joab's Unauthorized Revenge

### The Murder (3:22-27)

But then Joab enters the story:

> 22 Just then David's men and Joab returned from a raid and brought with them a great deal of plunder. But Abner was no longer with David in Hebron, because David had sent him away, and he had gone in peace. 23 When Joab and all the soldiers with him arrived, he was told that Abner son of Ner had come to the king and that the king had sent him away and that he had gone in peace.

Joab sees the evidence—the feast remains, the news that Abner has been received and dismissed in peace. He understands immediately what has happened.

> 24 So Joab went to the king and said, "What have you done? Look, Abner came to you. Why did you let him go? Now he is gone! 25 You know Abner son of Ner; he came to deceive you and observe your movements and find out everything you are doing."

Joab attempts to sow suspicion. His accusations may contain a grain of truth—Abner was indeed gathering intelligence about Israel's support for David. But Joab's real motive runs deeper: personal vendetta.

> 30 (Joab and his brother Abishai murdered Abner because he had killed their brother Asahel in the battle at Gibeon.)

Years earlier, in a clash between David's forces and Saul's, Abner had killed Joab's brother Asahel. The circumstances suggest it may have been unavoidable—Asahel was pursuing Abner relentlessly, and Abner warned him to turn back. When Asahel wouldn't stop, Abner struck him with the butt of his spear. The blunt end suggests Abner wasn't trying to kill but merely to slow him down. Yet momentum and force killed Asahel nonetheless.

Now Joab takes matters into his own hands:

> 26 Joab then left David and sent messengers after Abner, and they brought him back from the cistern at Sirah. But David did not know it.

> 27 Now when Abner returned to Hebron, Joab took him aside into an inner chamber, as if to speak with him privately. And there, to avenge the blood of his brother Asahel, Joab stabbed him in the stomach, and he died.

**The Irony:** Just as the spear went through Asahel's stomach, so Joab stabs Abner in the stomach. Joab believes he is bringing justice. In reality, he is sabotaging the peace process and violating David's explicit trust.

### David's Response (3:28-39)

David learns of the murder and immediately recognizes the damage:

> 28 Later, when David heard about this, he said, "I and my kingdom are forever innocent before the Lord concerning the blood of Abner son of Ner.

> 29 May his blood fall on the head of Joab and on his whole family! May Joab's family never be without someone who has a running sore or leprosy or who leans on a crutch or who falls by the sword or who lacks food."

David curses Joab's house. He pronounces himself and his reign innocent of this murder. Then he demonstrates his commitment to honoring Abner:

> 31 Then David said to Joab and all the people with him, "Tear your clothes and put on sackcloth and walk in mourning in front of Abner." King David himself walked behind the bier. 32 They buried Abner in Hebron, and the king wept aloud at Abner's tomb. All the people wept also.

> 33 The king sang this lament for Abner:
> 
> "Should Abner have died as the lawless die?
> Your hands were not bound,
> your feet were not fettered.
> You fell as one falls before the wicked."
> 
> And all the people wept over him again.

David's public mourning serves a critical purpose. It demonstrates to all Israel that he had nothing to do with Abner's death—that he grieves the loss of this key figure and respects his memory. The lament emphasizes that Abner died unjustly, like a victim of assassination rather than a lawful execution.

> 35 Then they all came and urged David to eat something while it was still day; but David took an oath, saying, "May God deal with me, be it ever so severely, if I taste bread or anything else before the sun sets!"

> 36 All the people took note and were pleased; indeed, everything the king did pleased them. 37 So on that day all the people there and all Israel knew that the king had no part in the murder of Abner son of Ner.

David's restraint, grief, and public displays of innocence convince Israel. They see a king who is not ruthless, not willing to benefit from treachery, not complicit in murder. His character becomes his vindication.

## Part 3: The Benjamites' Misguided Attempt

### Ish-Bosheth's Fear (4:1)

With Abner gone, the writing is on the wall:

> 4:1 When Ish-Bosheth son of Saul heard that Abner had died in Hebron, he lost courage, and all Israel became alarmed.

The kingdom of the north collapses. Without Abner's military strength, Ish-Bosheth has no support.

### The Murder (4:2-7)

Two Benjamite soldiers—Rekab and Baanah—see an opportunity:

> 4:2 Now Saul's son had two men who were leaders of raiding bands. One was named Baanah and the other Rekab; they were sons of Rimmon the Beerothite from the tribe of Benjamin—Beeroth is considered part of Benjamin...

> 5 Now Rekab and Baanah, the sons of Rimmon the Beerothite, set out for the house of Ish-Bosheth, and they arrived there in the heat of the day while he was taking his noonday rest. 6 They went into the inner part of the house as if to get some wheat, and they stabbed him in the stomach.

> 7 They had gone into the house while he was lying on the bed in his bedroom. After they stabbed and killed him, they cut off his head. Taking it with them, they traveled all night by way of the Arabah. 8 They brought the head of Ish-Bosheth to David at Hebron and said to the king, "Here is the head of Ish-Bosheth son of Saul, your enemy, who tried to kill you. This day the Lord has avenged my lord the king against Saul and his offspring."

These men believe they are doing David a favor. They have eliminated his rival and are presenting the trophy as proof of their loyalty.

### David's Judgment (4:9-12)

But David's response is unequivocal:

> 9 David answered Rekab and his brother Baanah, the sons of Rimmon the Beerothite, "As surely as the Lord lives, who has delivered me out of every trouble, 10 when someone told me, 'Saul is dead,' and thought he was bringing good news, I seized him and put him to death in Ziklag. That was the reward I gave him for his news! 11 How much more—when wicked men have killed an innocent man in his own house and on his own bed—should I not now demand his blood from your hand and rid the earth of you!"

David recalls an earlier incident where someone came claiming to have killed Saul and expecting a reward. David executed that man. He refuses to build his throne on the bones of the innocent or to reward assassination.

> 12 So David gave an order to his men, and they killed them. They cut off their hands and feet and hung the bodies by the pool in Hebron. But they took the head of Ish-Bosheth and buried it in Abner's tomb at Hebron.

David's justice is swift and severe. He will not tolerate the murder of even a weak rival.

## Part 4: God Advances His Kingdom (5:1-3)

### The Culmination

After three chapters of intrigue, scheming, murder, and vengeance, what does David actually do to secure the throne? He waits.

> 5:1 All the tribes of Israel came to David at Hebron and said, "We are your own flesh and blood. 2 In the past, while Saul was king over us, you were the one who led Israel on their military campaigns. And the Lord said to you, 'You will shepherd my people Israel, and you will become their ruler.'"

> 3 When all the elders of Israel had come to King David at Hebron, the king made a covenant with them at Hebron before the Lord, and they anointed David king over Israel.

**The Key Insight:** David does not seize the throne. He does not manipulate events to his advantage. He remains largely passive while others—Abner, Joab, the Benjamites—drive events forward. Yet the outcome is certain because it rests on God's promise.

## The Lesson: Righteousness Advances God's Kingdom

### What David Actually Does

In these chapters, David's active interventions are few:
1. He demands Michal back—fulfilling covenant and demonstrating his commitment to right relationships
2. He mourns Abner and proclaims his innocence
3. He executes Rekab and Baanah to maintain justice

That's it. He does not scheme, does not leverage every advantage, does not build alliances through deception. Instead, he maintains his integrity and trusts God.

### The Pattern We See

- **Abner tries to help David** by switching sides—but acts from self-interest and political calculation
- **Joab tries to help David** by taking revenge and eliminating a rival—but acts from personal vendetta, violating David's explicit wishes, and sabotaging peace
- **The Benjamites try to help David** by murdering the king—but act from opportunism, assuming David will reward treachery

Each attempt to "help" comes from human ambition and scheming. Yet each one is met with David's moral resistance. He refuses to benefit from treachery. He refuses to reward assassination. He refuses to compromise his character.

And in the end, **all Israel comes to David anyway.**

### The Deeper Truth

This passage reveals something crucial about God's kingdom: **human scheming cannot advance it, and human righteousness cannot hinder it.** David has learned to trust that God's promise—spoken through the prophet Samuel—will come to pass regardless of the machinations swirling around him.

This doesn't mean David does nothing. He does what is right. He honors his covenant with Michal. He seeks justice. But he doesn't dirty his hands with the schemes of others, and he doesn't try to force God's promise into being through manipulation.

## Application: Trust Beyond Circumstances

When we survey our own lives, we often see a landscape as complex and discouraging as David's. Schemes unravel. Promises seem delayed. People betray us. Enemies multiply. And we feel tempted to compromise our integrity "just this once" to move things along, to position ourselves correctly, to ensure the outcome we believe God has promised.

Kenny's personal reflection from that week captures this struggle: conversations with friends whose marriages are failing, news of deaths, economic instability, persecutions against Christians. It can feel like God's kingdom is stalled.

But David teaches us otherwise: **God advances His kingdom. Our job is to remain righteous, to honor our covenants, and to trust His timing.**

This doesn't mean passivity in the face of evil. David still pursues justice. But it does mean releasing the compulsion to scheme, to manipulate, to force outcomes. It means believing that righteousness itself—lived out faithfully—carries more power than all the political calculation in the world.

When your marriage struggles and you want to "move on," remember that marriage is a covenant—one flesh. You can't simply walk away without tearing yourself apart.

When persecution rises and you're tempted to compromise your witness to appease critics, remember David's refusal to let Joab's vengeance taint his name.

When the kingdom seems to be stalling and you're tempted to take shortcuts, remember that **God Himself advances His kingdom.** Your faithfulness to righteousness is enough.
