## Hide and Seek: The Game We Know

We love this game. This year's Oscar nominations for Best Picture tell the same story: *Avatar* features heroes chasing hidden resources; *Top Gun: Maverick* shows planes chasing planes; *Everything Everywhere All at Once* has characters hiding from pursuers across multiverses.

But in John's gospel, we encounter a far more serious hide and seek. There's action and a real chase. There's a death threat as authorities converge upon Jesus. Three questions emerge:

- Where is Jesus?
- Where is he from?
- Where is he going?

---

## 1. Where Is Jesus? (John 7:11)

### The Authorities Are Seeking

The Jewish leaders are actively hunting Jesus:

> After this, Jesus went around in Galilee. He did not want to go about in Judea because the Jewish leaders there were looking for a way to kill him. (7:1)

Back in chapter 5, Jesus healed a man on the Sabbath day. The authorities felt humiliated. Now they're seeking to set him up and kill him. But first, they must find him.

### Jesus Is in Hiding

Jesus remains in Galilee—his home turf, a backward part of Israel with sparse population. Six months pass from Passover to the Feast of Tabernacles, and Jesus stays hidden. His brothers come to him:

> Jesus' brothers said to him, "Leave Galilee and go to Judea, so that your disciples there may see the works you do. No one who wants to become a public figure acts in secret. Since you are doing these things, show yourself to the world." (7:2-4)

Their reasoning makes earthly sense: Galilee is rural and insignificant. Jerusalem is the great city with important leaders and buildings. If you want to start a movement and become famous, you go there. But Jesus refuses:

> My time is not yet here. For you any time will do. The world cannot hate you, but it hates me because I testify that its works are evil. You go to the festival. I am not going up to this festival, because my time has not yet fully come. (7:6-8)

Yet the answer surprises us:

> After he had said this, he stayed in Galilee. However, after his brothers had left for the festival, he went also, not publicly, but in secret. (7:9-10)

### The Real Issue: Glory

His brothers wanted him to go **publicly** so he could reveal himself and gain glory. But Jesus goes **in secret**, traveling alone or with a small group. This reveals his true intention:

> Whoever speaks on their own does so to gain personal glory, but he who seeks the glory of the one who sent him is a man of truth; there is nothing false about him. (7:18)

Jesus refuses the world's path to influence. His agenda differs from the world's agenda.

### Jesus Is Present, But Not as Expected

The Jewish leaders ask at the festival:

> Now at the festival the Jewish leaders were watching for Jesus and asking, "Where is he?" (7:11)

The answer: Jesus is there. But not with pomp and ceremony. Not with a royal entourage. He is present in quietness, doing his Father's will, not pursuing personal glory.

### Application: Where Is God When We Suffer?

We often ask, "Where is Jesus? Where is God?" especially in times of suffering and calamity. In Syria, where earthquakes devastated the land and sanctions prevented medical supplies from reaching those who needed them—where was God? God seems far away, absent, not here.

But the lesson from this passage is this: **Jesus is here. Right in front of you.** It's just that he appears not on our terms. He comes without glory, without pomp and ceremony, without a popular Instagram page. Jesus is here in secret, doing quiet work quietly in our lives.

**The kingdom of God grows in secret.**

> He also said, "This is what the kingdom of God is like. A man scatters seed on the ground. Night and day, whether he sleeps or gets up, the seed sprouts and grows, though he does not know how. All by itself the soil produces grain—first the stalk, then the head, then the full kernel in the head." (Mark 4:26-29)

### An Illustration: The Secret Work of Motherhood

Motherhood is like this secret growth. A mother stays home full-time. The house gets messy. The bins stay full. Dinner isn't always ready. A husband comes home and wonders, "What did you do all day?"

But what the mother has done is invisible: she kept a baby alive through 101 threats—disease, bacteria, neglect, starvation. Her body produces life-giving milk around the clock. Every moment, she teaches, guides, prods, encourages. Spontaneous moments cannot be timetabled or organized. And suddenly, the child has grown into a healthy, God-fearing adult ready to face the world.

What did you do? Nothing—no glorious achievements, no pay packet, no commendation, no CV. And yet everything. Quietly. The baby grew by the gram. Mentally, through each loving word spoken, each passage of Scripture read, each example of faith lived out. Just by being there.

This is how Christian growth works. It's not obvious. You cannot measure it in numbers and statistics. It's the quiet work of perseverance, the patient work of resilience—measured only in the long term. **It has to be in secret. It has to be counter-cultural.**

## 2. Where Is He From? (John 7:27)

### The Crowd's Confusion About Jesus' Origins

The crowd struggles to accept Jesus:

> But we know where this man is from; when the Messiah comes, no one will know where he is from. (7:27)

There's widespread whispering (grumbling, like in Exodus):

> Some said, "He is a good man." Others replied, "No, he deceives the people." (7:12)

They're amazed at his teaching:

> Not until halfway through the festival did Jesus go up to the temple and begin to teach. The Jews there were amazed and asked, "How did this man get such learning without having been taught?" (7:14-15)

And their doubts intensify:

> On hearing his words, some of the people said, "Surely this man is the Prophet." Others said, "He is the Messiah." Still others asked, "How can the Messiah come from Galilee? Does not Scripture say that the Messiah will come from David's descendants and from Bethlehem, the town where David lived?" (7:40-42)

How can the Messiah come from this backward town? He's supposed to be a king from Bethlehem. (Of course, Jesus was born in Bethlehem, but he grew up in Galilee, and the crowd didn't know this.)

### Why This Matters: Trustworthiness

The question "Where is he from?" relates to trustworthiness. When a friend from school comes to your house, you ask, "Where is he from?" When you introduce a prospective spouse to your parents, they ask, "Where is she from?" They're really asking: What kind of person is this? Can I trust you? Is this person from a good family? Did you go to the right school?

The Jews don't trust Jesus. He didn't go to the right school. They're skeptical of his origins.

### Jesus' Answer: I Am from the Father

The authorities try to seize him:

> Why are you trying to kill me? (7:19)

The crowd denies they're seeking to kill him:

> You are demon-possessed, the crowd answered. Who is trying to kill you? (7:20)

But Jesus responds with clarity:

> You know me, and you know where I am from. I am not here on my own authority, but he who sent me is true. You do not know him, but I know him because I am from him and he sent me. (7:28-29)

Jesus doesn't talk about Bethlehem. He doesn't produce his birth certificate or CV. Bethlehem is not who he is—it's merely a means to an end, a halfway house. **Jesus is divine. He is from God.**

At this, they tried to seize him, but no one could lay a hand on him, because his hour had not yet come (7:30).

### Hope Emerges

Yet many in the crowd believed:

> Still, many in the crowd believed in him. They said, "When the Messiah comes, will he perform more signs than this man?" (7:31)

There comes a time when enough proof has accumulated. For some in the crowd, that moment had arrived. The Messiah couldn't do much more than what Jesus is doing. This has to be him.

### Application: Jesus as More Than a Teacher

Jesus is a great earthly teacher—ethics, wisdom, moral guidance. But you're only halfway there if you stop there. **Jesus is also from heaven.** He offers the sovereignty of a compassionate God who loves us, assuring us we're not subject to random evil. He offers eternal life itself.

And to understand this fully, we must look at where he is going.

## 3. Where Is He Going? (John 7:33-34)

### The Chase Continues

The Pharisees hear the crowd's favorable response:

> The Pharisees heard the crowd whispering such things about him. Then the chief priests and the Pharisees sent temple guards to arrest him. (7:32)

Jesus makes another startling declaration:

> I am with you for only a short time, and then I am going to the one who sent me. You will look for me, but you will not find me; and where I am, you cannot come. (7:33-34)

### The Jews' Misunderstanding

They think Jesus might run away:

> The Jews said to one another, "Where does this man intend to go that we cannot find him? Will he go where our people live scattered among the Greeks, and teach the Greeks?" (7:35)

The scattered Jews were those dispersed 500 years before Jesus through exile. Perhaps Jesus has relatives there where he can hide and live as a fugitive?

But Jesus has already given the answer: **"I am going to the one who sent me."** That's not among the Greeks. That's not Galilee. That's not Bethlehem.

### Jesus Is Going to His Death

Jesus is going back to the Father. But there's a problem: Why can't others follow him? Isn't following Jesus the whole point—that we get to go to heaven? Yet he says, "You cannot come."

The literal phrase is more precise: "You are not able to come" (7:34).

The answer comes in chapter 8:

> Once more Jesus said to them, "I am going away, and you will look for me, and you will die in your sin. Where I go, you cannot come." (8:21)

Jesus is hinting at his own death. That's why he cannot take others with him there. He's going to a place no one can follow—the cross. Before he returns to heaven, he must stop at a midway point. **He pauses first at the cross.**

And that's what he's protecting his disciples from. The irony comes clear:

> This made the Jews ask, "Will he kill himself? Is that why he says, 'Where I go, you cannot come'?" (8:22)

Not suicide in the wrong sense, but yes—he is heading to his death. And you cannot go there. **Jesus goes where we dare not tread.**

Jesus goes to the halfway point of the cross so that he can ultimately take us completely to the Father. How great is that!

## Seek and Found

At the cross, the chase ends. They found him. They crucified him.

**Did you find him?**

- **Where is he?** He is here.
- **Where is he from?** From the Father, with the authority and will of the Father.
- **Where is he going?** He went to death for us, and now brings us to heaven.

Seek and you shall find.

Father, we thank you for the wondrous cross and the marvel of your love for us.

## The Festival of Tabernacles

We are living on earth temporarily—in our tabernacles, our tents. Our bodies are merely tents. Our houses are just tents. But Jesus, who pitched his tent with us, sacrificially dies out of love for us. So that one day when the tent fails us—as it will—we have a home to go to.

The Festival of Tabernacles commemorates Israel's exodus from Egypt. For seven days, people came to Jerusalem for a great celebration, then lived in tents to remember what the wilderness journey was like. It was one of three major festivals every adult Jewish male had to attend, guaranteeing an enormous crowd—which is precisely why Jesus' brothers urged him to go publicly.

## The Mob and the Kingdom

### A Warning About Democracy

We pride ourselves on democracy. But pure democracy is the rule of the mob—large crowds forming the majority and enforcing their arbitrary will on the minority. And the mob is evil.

Germany, 1939: The majority voted for the Final Solution, killing 6 million Jews. Rwanda, 1994: The Hutu majority murdered 800,000 Tutsis. Today in the Middle East, hundreds die daily—21 Egyptian Christians, 70 in Nigeria, 80 when a church burns. The mob, frenzied by simple slogans and angry over perceived wrongs, is used by manipulative leaders to do great evil.

This is why we need more than democracy. We need **constitutional democracy**—democracy limited by the Constitution and law. This protects minorities, like Bible-believing Christians. The French Revolution inspired sympathy for revolutionaries, yet 40,000 died in the Reign of Terror—worse than Revolutionary China.

### The Modern Mob

We see the modern mob today in protest movements that violate property, incite violence, deliver obscenities and witch hunts over artificial grievances. You can recognize them: they protest the loudest using simplistic sound bites rather than reasoned discussion. They're the first to claim offense to censor debate.

**Think first. Research both sides. Seek to reason, not protest.** Don't join the mob, for it was the mob—led by conspiring leaders—who killed Jesus.

Jesus' brothers perhaps thought he wanted to lead a revolution and take over Jerusalem. But his kingdom works differently. It grows in secret, through the quiet transformation of hearts, not through mob rule.

## The Healing on the Sabbath: Exposing Inconsistency (John 7:21-24)

Jesus points to an inconsistency in their argument. He speaks of the one miracle that sparked their anger—the healing from chapter 5:

> Jesus said to them, "I did one miracle, and you are all amazed." (7:21)

The Jews are angry because Jesus worked on the Sabbath. But Moses commanded circumcision, and if the eighth day falls on a Sabbath, the law requires circumcision anyway. Circumcision involves cutting—which is work. So there's always been a category of acceptable work on the Sabbath.

> Yet, because Moses gave you circumcision (though actually it did not come from Moses, but from the patriarchs), you circumcise a boy on the Sabbath. Now if a man can be circumcised on the Sabbath so that the law of Moses may not be broken, why are you angry with me for healing a man's whole body on the Sabbath? Stop judging by mere appearances, but instead judge correctly. (7:22-24)

How can it be lawful to cut a man on the Sabbath but not to make him whole? The inconsistency exposes their refusal to think clearly. They see a mere appearance, jump to conclusions, and decide to kill him.

## The Living Water (John 7:37-39)

On the last and greatest day of the festival, Jesus stands and cries out:

> Let anyone who is thirsty come to me and drink. Whoever believes in me, as Scripture has said, rivers of living water will flow from within them. (7:37-38)

By this he meant the Spirit, whom those who believed in him were later to receive. The Spirit had not yet been given, since Jesus had not yet been glorified. The festival water ceremonies point to the salvation that flows from the cross and resurrection—living water that satisfies the deepest thirst of the human soul.
