# Micah 5:1-15

**Title:** God's King

**Date:** 1 December 2024

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## A King Is Born (verses 1-4)

We are in the season of Advent—a time of waiting for the arrival of the one foretold in Micah's prophecy. Advent literally means "arrival," and this passage speaks of a promised ruler who will change everything.

> But you, Bethlehem Ephrathah, though you are small among the clans of Judah, out of you will come for me one who will be ruler over Israel, whose origins are from of old, from ancient times.

**Bethlehem** means "house of bread." It was likely a small provisioning town—more of a pit stop between major cities than a destination in itself. Think of it like Goulburn on the way to Canberra: unremarkable, transient, insignificant. Yet from this nothing-special place, something monumental will emerge.

The promise is striking: though Israel will be abandoned, there will come a son whose rule will restore the people. This ruler will have ancient origins—he is not a new invention but something rooted in the eternal purposes of God.

> He will stand and shepherd his flock in the strength of the Lord, in the majesty of the name of the Lord his God. And they will live securely, for then his greatness will reach to the ends of the earth.

When we think of a ruler from Bethlehem, our minds turn to King David, who was also from this small town and was himself "little"—overlooked as the youngest son, tending sheep while his brothers were considered for the kingship. David embodied the pattern of the humble, unlikely leader chosen by God.

But this prophecy points beyond David to a future fulfillment. The language of "ancient times" and "everlasting origins" suggests someone greater than any earthly king.

## The King Will Rule and Bring Peace (verses 5-9)

The opening verses set the context for what follows: Jerusalem faces siege from the Assyrians. In 720 BC, Assyria conquered the northern kingdom of Israel. By 701 BC, King Sennacherib laid siege to Jerusalem itself—a terrifying situation involving starvation, disease, and the anxiety of waiting for the walls to fall.

> And he will be our peace when the Assyrians invade our land and march through our fortresses. We will raise against them seven shepherds, even eight commanders, who will rule the land of Assyria with the sword, the land of Nimrod with drawn sword. He will deliver us from the Assyrians when they invade our land and march across our borders.

The "seven shepherds and eight commanders" represent completeness and abundance—seven being complete, eight being more than enough. These shepherds are the leaders of Israel who will rise up against their oppressors.

Peace here does not necessarily mean tranquility or absence of conflict. True peace means the cessation of war and, more deeply, reconciliation with God. We see this in Paul's letter to the Philippians:

> The peace of God, which transcends all understanding, will guard your hearts and your minds in Christ Jesus.

Paul was far from tranquil—facing affliction and suffering—yet he possessed a deep peace that guarded his heart. We can make a right decision and still lose sleep over its consequences. Conversely, we can make wrong decisions and feel no disturbance. Our feelings are unreliable guides. Scripture, not our emotions, must determine what is right and wrong.

The immediate historical fulfillment of this prophecy was King Hezekiah, a young ruler who trusted in the Lord and stood firm against Assyrian pressure. When Sennacherib laid siege to Jerusalem, God assured Hezekiah of deliverance:

> That night the angel of the Lord went out and put to death a hundred and eighty-five thousand in the Assyrian camp. When the people got up the next morning—there were all the dead bodies! So Sennacherib king of Assyria broke camp and withdrew.

Yet even this victory was incomplete. The peace was temporary.

## The King Will Remove Sin (verses 10-15)

The prophecy continues with idealistic promises of divine cleansing:

> In that day, I will destroy your horses from among you and demolish your chariots. I will destroy the cities of your land and tear down all your strongholds. I will destroy your witchcraft and you will no longer cast spells. I will destroy your idols and your sacred stones from among you; you will no longer bow down to the work of your hands. I will uproot from among you your Asherah poles when I demolish your cities. I will take vengeance in anger and wrath on the nations that have not obeyed me.

These words describe a total cleansing—the removal of all idolatry, all false religion, all opposition to God's rule. But this did not happen in Hezekiah's time. The peace remained temporary. Idolatry persisted. Enemies continued to threaten.

After his miraculous deliverance from Assyria, Hezekiah made a grave mistake. When envoys from Babylon came to congratulate him, he proudly displayed all his treasures—his gold, silver, spices, and armory. The prophet Isaiah confronted him with a sobering word:

> Everything in your palace, and all that your predecessors have stored up until this day, will be carried off to Babylon. Some of your descendants, your own flesh and blood, will be taken away and become eunuchs in the palace of the king of Babylon.

Hezekiah could not deliver himself from his own pride and folly. What he could not accomplish, the Babylonians would do a century later. Judah fell. The temple was destroyed. The people became remnants in exile—scattered among the nations, far from their homeland.

## Hope in the Remnant (verses 7-9, 8-9)

Yet even in exile, there is hope:

> The remnant of Jacob will be in the midst of many peoples like dew from the Lord, like showers on the grass, which do not wait for anyone or depend on man. The remnant of Jacob will be among the nations, in the midst of many peoples, like a lion among the beasts of the forest, like a young lion among flocks of sheep, which mauls and mangles as it goes, and no one can rescue. Your hand will be lifted up in triumph over your enemies, and all your foes will be destroyed.

The remnant—what remains after defeat and exile—is described with two striking images. First, they are like dew and gentle rain, dependent entirely on God's provision. Second, they are like a young lion among sheep, fierce and unstoppable despite their small numbers.

This mirrors the experience of Daniel, a Jewish captive in Babylon, preserved by God even in the lion's den. We too are a remnant—a minority in the world, appearing weak by human standards. Yet we forget the God we follow. Numbers are not what matter. A coordinated group of lions will always triumph over a herd of zebras. Quality and divine purpose overcome mere quantity.

## The Ultimate King

The promise finds its fulfillment in Jesus, who was born in Bethlehem and came as a shepherd of God's people:

> Out of you will come a ruler who will shepherd my people Israel.

For now, we await the ultimate fulfillment of these prophecies. Wars still rage. Families still fight. Christians are still persecuted for their faith. The remnant still waits in exile—not in Babylon, but scattered in a world that does not yet acknowledge God's king.

But we have hope. One day, all war will cease. Every enemy will be destroyed. Idolatry will be no more, and God's name alone will be exalted. Until then, we remain as the remnant—small in number but mighty in God, trusting that His purposes will ultimately prevail.
