# Ephesians 1:15-23

**Title:** Knowing God Better  
**Date:** 8 June 2008  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Knowing Thankfulness (vv. 15-17)

Paul opens this section with gratitude for the Ephesians' faith and love. He's not starting with criticism or correction, but with thanksgiving—a posture that shapes his entire prayer for them.

Notice what Paul prays for: not that they would have more faith or love, but that they would *know God better*. He's asking God to give them spiritual insight and wisdom. The Greek word for this kind of knowledge isn't merely intellectual; it's the deep, relational knowledge that comes from intimacy with God.

Paul prays specifically that "the eyes of your heart may be enlightened" (v. 18). Your heart has eyes. This is the organ of spiritual perception—not the mind alone, but the whole person oriented toward God. When your heart's eyes are opened, you begin to see reality as it truly is.

## Knowing Hope (v. 18)

What does enlightened spiritual vision reveal? Paul says we come to understand "the hope to which he has called you."

Hope, in Scripture, isn't wishful thinking. It's confident assurance rooted in God's character and promises. When Paul prays for the Ephesians to know this hope, he's praying they would grasp the certainty of their future with God—the guarantee that what He has begun, He will complete.

This hope is inseparable from knowing God himself. The more deeply we know Him, the more secure and joyful our hope becomes.

## Knowing Power (vv. 19-23)

Paul continues his prayer by asking them to understand "the immeasurable greatness of his power toward us who believe" (v. 19).

Power isn't something God merely possesses—it's something He exercises *toward us*. God's power isn't abstract or distant; it's directed toward His people with purposeful intention. This is the power that raised Christ from the dead and seated Him at God's right hand, far above all rule and authority.

And here's the extraordinary claim: that same power works *in us*. The resurrection power of God is at work in those who believe. Not a weaker version, not a distant influence, but the actual power that conquered death and hell.

The Church is called Christ's body—not metaphorically, but really. We are so connected to Him that what He accomplished, we participate in. His exaltation becomes our inheritance. His victory becomes our victory.

This is what Paul wants the Ephesians to *know*—not just believe intellectually, but grasp deeply in their hearts. When you truly understand your union with Christ and the power available to you, it transforms how you live.
