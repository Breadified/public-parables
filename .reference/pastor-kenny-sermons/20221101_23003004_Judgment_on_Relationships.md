# Isaiah 3:4

**Title:** Judgment on Relationships  
**Date:** 27 November 2022  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction: The Pattern of Judgment

We're reading through Isaiah for the rest of this year. Two weeks ago, we explored Isaiah's advice to listen. Last week, we saw how Israel's failure to be a role model to the nations brought judgment. This week, we examine how God's judgment profoundly affects our relationships with one another—a topic I'd rate at spice level 8 out of 10. Just as Russians ran out of supplies at the World Cup, Israel will run out of their essential supplies.

## Part One: Young versus Old (Isaiah 3:1-12)

### The Loss of Supplies and Support

> See now, the Lord, the Lord Almighty, is about to take from Jerusalem and Judah both supply and support: all supplies of food and all supplies of water (3:1)

The word "supplies" here carries weight. Food supplies reference locusts—complete devastation of crops. Water supplies speak to drought and dry wells. The emphatic "all" underscores the totality of this judgment.

This sounds like a siege—the method Assyria and later Babylon would use to conquer the city. By cutting off food and water, they could starve the citizens into capitulation. In recent years, we've experienced what supply shortages feel like: empty shelves where toilet paper and flour once sat, no cars at dealerships, no two-day shipping from China. But Jerusalem's deprivation would bring something far more severe: starvation leading to weakened bodies, disease, and death in a vicious spiral that overwhelms everything.

But the judgment isn't only about physical resources. There's also a shortage of **support**.

> the hero and the warrior, the judge and the prophet, the diviner and the elder, the captain of fifty and the man of rank, the counselor, skilled craftsman and clever enchanter (3:2-3)

This passage catalogs the full range of leadership roles across every sphere. Whether for good or evil, these are all leaders—those we look to for guidance.

The military leaders inspire followership through victory. Judges interpret the past correctly; prophets predict the future accurately. Diviners and enchanters, though operating in the spiritual darkness (like King Saul consulting a medium in 1 Samuel 28), still held sway over people's trust. Elders represented accumulated wisdom. Men of rank wielded titles and authority—the Dr.'s, Professor's, Sir's, His Majesty's of ancient society. Counselors offered wisdom. Skilled craftsmen—the technocrats—possessed expertise that made people desperate enough to follow them blindly, saying "yes, yes, yes, just fix this."

In summary, all these leaders are gone. Did they die in battle? Are they missing in action? Too frightened to return? Prevented from coming back by circumstance?

### The Consequence: Young Rule Over Old

> I will make mere youths their officials; children will rule over them. (3:4)
> People will oppress each other—man against man, neighbor against neighbor. The young will rise up against the old, the nobody against the honored. (3:5)

This signals something crucial: **when young lead, it's a sign of judgment**. The natural pattern has elders—the older, presumably wiser—providing leadership. Normally, age brings wisdom and experience. The U.S. Constitution requires a president to be at least 35. India, Indonesia, Hong Kong, South Korea, and Italy maintain similar age requirements for their highest offices.

This isn't arbitrary. The Bible reflects practical wisdom: leaders need time for testing and examination, which naturally comes with age. We're not saying a mature 18-year-old doesn't exist, nor that every 80-year-old proves valuable. But the pattern holds—wisdom typically increases with years lived.

When society follows children and the immature into leadership, judgment has arrived. Think of contemporary examples: young activists with no life experience shaping policy, or politicians more focused on viral moments than substantive governance. Without wisdom guiding leadership, the wrong people take charge and bring society to ruin.

### The Hollow Symbols of Leadership

> A man will seize one of his brothers in his father's house, and say, "You have a cloak, you be our leader; take charge of this heap of ruins!" (3:6)

Notice the criterion: *you have a cloak*—you look rich, well-dressed, successful. Sadly, that's how most of us vote. Tall with hair. Surface appearance becomes the qualification for leadership.

New Zealand has pushed to lower the voting age to 16. The Greens Party—with policies contrary to Christian principles—supports this. Easier to manipulate younger minds. Some argue that if you're old enough to fight in war, you should vote. But Rome required 10 years of military service before earning voting rights. You fought first, proved your worth through testing, *then* gained a voice. Age of enlistment and age of wisdom are different things.

On that day, there will be no functional leaders.

### The Refusal to Lead

> But in that day he will cry out, "I have no remedy. I have no food or clothing in my house; do not make me the leader of the people." (3:7)

Even a man with a cloak refuses the role. The city collapses inward.

> Jerusalem staggers, Judah is falling; their words and deeds are against the Lord, defying his glorious presence. The look on their faces testifies against them; they parade their sin like Sodom; they do not hide it. Woe to them! They have brought disaster upon themselves. (3:8-9)

The nation openly rebels against God with unashamed boldness.

Yet there remains a word for the righteous:

> Tell the righteous it will be well with them, for they will enjoy the fruit of their deeds. Woe to the wicked! Disaster is upon them! They will be paid back for what their hands have done. (3:10-11)

## Part Two: Women versus Men (Isaiah 3:12 and 3:16-4:1)

### The Gender Collapse

> Youths oppress my people, women rule over them. My people, your guides lead you astray; they turn you from the path. (3:12)

The verse pairs two signs of judgment: youth in power and women in leadership. This requires careful handling. We're making a general observation, not an absolute law. Many exceptional women lead wisely. But generally speaking, men take initiative while women typically follow initiatives. Most national and corporate leadership historically falls to men.

Why the difference? Biological factors play a role—testosterone increases aggression and reduces agreeableness. Physically, men are larger and often take the lead in mixed contexts. Culturally, men fight harder for pay increases and tolerate long hours without notice, unencumbered by pregnancy or hormonal cycles.

The pattern shows most clearly in marriage, and we see echoes of it throughout society.

### Gender Relations and the Wage Gap Myth

The gender pay gap statistic (often cited as 77 cents per dollar) needs scrutiny. Christina Hoff Sommers has documented that these calculations ignore occupation, position, education, and hours worked per week. When you control for these factors, the gap shrinks dramatically.

Here's the logical problem: If women earned less for identical work, wouldn't profit-driven businessmen hire only women to maximize returns? Yet we observe the opposite. Moreover, 90% of workplace deaths are men; all soldiers in Ukraine are men. If we truly valued male labor, we'd protect it more.

The reality is more nuanced. In egalitarian Scandinavian countries where choice exists without quotas, men naturally move into leadership positions. The evidence of "discrimination" has become so elusive that it requires inventing micro-aggressions and "embedded misogyny" so subtle it barely exists. It's not discrimination—it's how males and females are built.

Women now dominate school and academia. In pay scales that discount motherhood, women are equal or leading. The issue isn't women's capabilities but the natural complementarity of male and female roles.

### When Women Lead: A Sign of Judgment

> The Lord says, "The women of Zion are haughty, walking along with outstretched necks, flirting with their eyes, strutting along with swaying hips, with ornaments jingling on their ankles. Therefore the Lord will bring sores on the heads of the women of Zion; the Lord will make their scalps bald." (3:16-17)

Pride and seduction characterize these women. Baldness in that culture was a mark of shame and loss—hair was a woman's distinctive glory.

> In that day the Lord will snatch away their finery: the bangles and headbands and crescent necklaces, the earrings and bracelets and veils, the headdresses and anklets and sashes, the perfume bottles and charms, the signet rings and nose rings, the fine robes and the capes and cloaks, the purses and mirrors, and the linen garments and tiaras and shawls. Instead of fragrance there will be a stench; instead of a sash, a rope; instead of well-dressed hair, baldness; instead of fine clothing, sackcloth; instead of beauty, branding. (3:18-24)

Every symbol of feminine beauty and status is stripped away. The judgment is total.

### The Devastating Aftermath

> Your men will fall by the sword, your warriors in battle. The gates of Zion will lament and mourn; destitute, she will sit on the ground. (3:25-26)

The men are gone. There is your gender gap.

> In that day seven women will take hold of one man and say, "We will eat our own food and provide our own clothes; only let us be called by your name. Take away our disgrace!" (4:1)

The gender imbalance becomes catastrophic. Seven women compete for one man—not for love, but for survival and legitimacy. They offer independence: *we'll feed and clothe ourselves*. All they ask is his name, his legitimacy. The disgrace of being unmarried in a shattered society is unendurable.

### The Deeper Pattern

Think about what naturally attracts men and women. Generally, women prefer partners who earn more, are more educated, taller, stronger. Men, in turn, prefer partners who earn less, are younger, shorter. These preferences aren't oppression—they reflect our design.

When societies push "blind equality," the relationship market breaks down. Recent data shows an unprecedented share of Americans not having sex—a proxy for the absence of meaningful relationships. Young men increasingly withdraw into gaming and virtual worlds, losing connection to reality. Young women raise standards that only a tiny percentage of men can meet, leaving most men invisible.

The result: fewer relationships, more isolation, less purpose. Society contracts. This isn't merely a social problem; it's a spiritual judgment playing out in real time.

### A Word to Young Men

Young men, especially here: you must take initiative. Yes, the system is stacked against you. But standing apart requires excellence. Stop daydreaming about missed opportunities. Stop creating false narratives in your head while missing reality. Where does your dopamine come from? Computer games? The gym? Basketball? These aren't inherently wrong, but if they're your only source of adrenaline, you're chasing fake victories while the real world awaits.

Do what you were made to do: work hard, sweat, serve. Help start a Christian community at your workplace. Serve at church. Get off the instant gratification treadmill. Do the difficult, long-term thing.

### A Word to Young Women

Understand the contradiction you're living: demanding equality while simultaneously preferring men of greater status. You may be pricing yourselves out of the market. Consider whether a spirit of humility—not dominance—might be more compatible with the relationship you actually want.

### The Larger Crisis

This trajectory isn't irreversible, but our current path is troubling. Blind "equality" leads nowhere good. Look at Japan: people no longer marry, no longer have children, lost purpose, rising suicides. That's where we're heading.

## Hope of a Remnant (Isaiah 4:2-6)

Yet judgment is never God's final word:

> In that day the Branch of the Lord will be beautiful and glorious, and the fruit of the land will be the pride and glory of the survivors in Israel. Those who are left in Zion, who remain in Jerusalem, will be called holy, all who are recorded among the living in Jerusalem. The Lord will wash away the filth of the women of Zion; he will cleanse the bloodstains from Jerusalem by a spirit of judgment and a spirit of fire. Then the Lord will create over all of Mount Zion and over those who assemble there a cloud of smoke by day and a glow of flaming fire by night; over everything the glory will be a canopy. It will be a shelter and shade from the heat of the day, and a refuge and hiding place from the storm and rain. (4:2-6)

A remnant survives. The survivors—those who endure the judgment—are called holy. God's cleansing fire purifies. And He provides shelter, shade, and refuge.

The world starves and thirsts for righteousness. Godly men. Godly women. These become the salt and light a broken society desperately needs.
