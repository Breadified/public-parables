# Luke 23:32-43

**Title:** A Better Life — Don't Leave It to the Last Minute  
**Date:** May 6, 2007  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction: The Culture of Last-Minute Living

We live in a world that rewards leaving things to the last minute. Some of us have discovered this works: cramming for exams, buying lottery tickets when only the favorite horse remains, haggling at garage sales in the final hours when sellers have given up hope. Even the internet celebrates this with websites offering deeper discounts the closer you get to your departure date.

But last-minute living doesn't always work. I learned this painfully when a childhood friend in Malaysia became seriously ill. For years, I received emails about his battle with liver cancer, but I was too busy. I thought he'd be fine, that I'd call him later. By the time I finally contacted him, it was too late. He was too sick to talk, and the words I'd wanted to share—appreciation for his friendship, memories of our time together—felt hollow and empty. I had waited too long, and that opportunity was gone forever.

## The Last Minute of Jesus' Life

Today we look at another story about leaving something to the last minute—but this time, it's the last minute of Jesus' life.

> **Luke 23:32-43** (the passage we've just read)

Jesus has been betrayed, tried by Pilate and Herod, condemned by the crowds, and now crucified. Two criminals hang beside him, one on each side. With every breath, life drains away. These are Jesus' final moments.

### The First Criminal: "Save Yourself and Us"

The first criminal hurls insults at Jesus:

> "Aren't you the Christ? Save yourself and us!" (Luke 23:39)

This echoes what we've already heard from others:
- The Jewish rulers: "He saved others; let him save himself if he is the Christ" (23:35)
- The soldiers: "If you are the king of the Jews, save yourself" (23:37)

The criminal is mocking Jesus' apparent powerlessness. *Christ*—the Anointed One, the expected King—should be able to do something. Yet here he hangs, dying like everyone else.

**The Logic of the World:** Save yourself first, then others. It's like an airplane oxygen mask—you secure your own mask before helping anyone else. How can you save others if you can't save yourself? An obese dietician giving weight loss advice, a tax analyst who can't get a refund, a lifeguard who can't swim—these don't inspire confidence. By this logic, Jesus is no savior at all.

### The Second Criminal: Understanding Jesus' True Mission

But the second criminal rebukes him:

> "Don't you fear God? We are punished justly, for we are getting what our deeds deserve. But this man has done nothing wrong." (Luke 23:40-41)

This criminal understands something crucial: **Jesus is innocent, yet he suffers punishment.** He recognizes Jesus as the Suffering Servant.

**Jesus' Logic Is Different:** It's not "save yourself and then others." It's "save yourself **or** others." Jesus must choose.

Throughout Luke's Gospel, Jesus has consistently taught this:

> "The Son of Man must suffer many things and be rejected by the elders, chief priests and teachers of the law, and he must be killed and on the third day be raised to life." (Luke 9:22)

Jesus came as a ransom for many—not a conquering king who defeats enemies and establishes earthly power, but a **Suffering Messiah** who dies on behalf of his people. 

There is no way to do both. Either Jesus saves himself and the criminals remain condemned, or Jesus surrenders himself and saves the criminals. The criminal's insult was actually an oxymoron: the very thing a powerful king would do to escape—save himself—is the one thing Jesus cannot do.

And so, out of love and willingness, Jesus chooses self-denial. He does not save himself. In losing himself, he saves us.

### The Cost of Sin

Before Jesus' answer, we must understand what the second criminal recognized: **sin is rebellion against God.**

We rebel actively—by hurling insults, living immorally, making choices in direct defiance. But we also rebel passively—by ignoring God, making decisions without considering what he might think, living for ourselves rather than for others. You can sin against someone by being rude, or by being polite while completely ignoring them. Niceness does not hide sin.

All of us stand guilty of rebelling against God in one way or another.

### The Correct Response: "Remember Me"

The second criminal responds the only way he can:

> "Jesus, remember me when you come into your kingdom." (Luke 23:42)

He makes no conditions. He brokers no deals. He offers no reasons why Jesus should show mercy. Spiked to wood, immobile, minutes from eternity, he simply throws himself on God's grace and asks for forgiveness.

### Jesus' Answer: "Today"

> "I tell you the truth, today you will be with me in paradise." (Luke 23:43)

Jesus doesn't say: "Let's see how you go tomorrow. Wait, let's check your resume first. Let's give you a probationary period. Let's see you go to church first."

He says: **"Today."**

This is not idle hope or fantasy. It is real assurance based on a real cross. As real as Jesus' death is, so is the reality of salvation for those who believe. This is the truth.

## Application: Your Last Minute

Some of you sitting in these pews today may be wondering what lies beyond this life. You may worry about what happens after death. Here is the answer: **you can be in paradise, in God's kingdom. You can know it today and be assured of that promise today.** It is guaranteed by Christ's death on your behalf.

You may have attended church for decades. You may know everything about this congregation, about Jesus, about Christian teaching. But it all comes to nothing if you have not thrown yourself on Jesus and said to him: "Jesus, remember me." It doesn't matter if you've been baptized, confirmed, or attended church for thirty years. If you have not recognized your sin and begged Jesus for mercy, you are no better off than the criminal who hurled insults at the cross.

### A Word About Waiting Until the Last Minute

Can I say this gently: **sometimes leaving Jesus to the last minute can work.** The thief on the cross did it. A lifetime of crime, and in his final moments, he met Jesus in the flesh, had enough sense to repent, and entered paradise.

If you haven't repented, remember: **it is never too late.** Even if you have only minutes to live, Jesus is always willing to listen. Even if you think Jesus could never forgive you, look at the criminal and know that his mercy is real.

But let me also be honest: **sometimes leaving Jesus to the last minute does not work.** Death doesn't always give you a last-minute chance to repent. It can come suddenly. It can come in an incoherent daze. My friend in Malaysia had minutes remaining—but he was too drugged by painkillers to use them. He could no longer talk, say farewell, or repent. That last minute had passed him long ago.

**Don't gamble with your eternity.**

## The Call

Repent today. A better life, a better afterlife, is waiting for you. Don't leave it to the last minute. Don't be a pew warmer. Don't wait until you're hanging on a cross with nothing left to lose.

Fear God and repent. Today.
