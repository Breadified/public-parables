# Daniel 1-7

**Title:** Genre and Structure of Daniel
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Understanding Daniel as Court Tales

The stories in Daniel 1-6 are classified as "court tales"—a literary genre common throughout the Ancient Near East. These accounts share distinctive characteristics with other tales about wise courtiers from that period.

### Characteristics of Ancient Near Eastern Court Tales

Court tales served multiple purposes for their original audiences:

- **Entertainment value** took precedence over strict historical accuracy, which is why characters often appear archetypal—the proud king, the wise courtier who escapes danger
- **Theological and moral instruction** alongside entertainment, using narrative to teach spiritual and ethical truths
- **Reinforcement of ethnic identity and worth**, encouraging readers to maintain hope in their situation

### Daniel's Distinctive Message

What sets the Daniel narratives apart is crucial: the basis for encouragement is not ethnic superiority, but rather **trust in the Most High God, who exercises supreme sovereignty over all earthly rulers and their affairs**. These stories remain fundamentally theological—they teach us about God's character and rule—while remaining genuinely engaging narratives.

## The Literary Structure of Daniel 1-7

The book's architecture reveals its theological emphases through careful arrangement.

### Format and Composition

Daniel 1 stands as an introductory tale, introducing the main characters and establishing the tension between faithfulness to God and service to a pagan empire.

Daniel 2-7 forms a unified literary unit distinguished by:
- **Language**: Primarily written in Aramaic (except 2:1-4a, which is in Hebrew)
- **Structure**: A carefully composed chiasm that highlights three major themes

### The Chiastic Structure

```
A    Daniel 2: A dream about four earthly kingdoms and God's kingdom
B    Daniel 3: Jews remain faithful in the face of death
C    Daniel 4: Royal pride that is humbled
C'   Daniel 5: Royal pride that is humbled
B'   Daniel 6: A Jew remains faithful in the face of death
A'   Daniel 7: A vision about four earthly kingdoms and God's kingdom
```

This symmetrical arrangement is not accidental—it emphasizes the three primary themes running through Daniel.

## The Three Major Themes of Daniel

### 1. The Sovereignty of God (A, A')

The controlling conviction of Daniel is that God holds all history in His hands. The visions and stories alike emphasize that **everything—even apparent catastrophes—remains under God's control**. God's kingdom alone is the kingdom that will ultimately triumph and endure forever. This theme bookends the entire section, anchoring every other concern.

### 2. The Hubris of Human Rulers (C, C')

Human rulers instinctively resist God's sovereignty. They are constantly tempted to claim for themselves the prerogatives that belong to God alone. The two central stories (Daniel 4 and 5) show rulers who yield to this temptation and must learn through hard experience the humbling reality of God's supremacy. Their pride precedes their fall—a pattern as old as human nature itself.

### 3. The Faithful Remnant Under Pressure (B, B')

Daniel addresses the real tensions faced by those seeking to honor God while living under pagan rule. The stories of the three young men and Daniel in the lion's den reveal a hard truth: **those who take seriously God's sovereignty while serving an earthly ruler will sometimes face opposition, ridicule, hatred, and even death**. The visions reinforce this reality, describing in detail how God's people will suffer under successive empires.

This is not a promise of ease or comfort, but of God's faithful presence through suffering.
