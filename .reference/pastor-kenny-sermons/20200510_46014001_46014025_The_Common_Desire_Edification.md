# 1 Corinthians 14:1-25

**Title:** The Common Desire: Edification
**Date:** May 10, 2020
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Big Idea

Prophecy—the intelligent proclamation of the gospel—edifies the church.

## Introduction

We continue our series on chapters 12-14 of Paul's First Letter to the Corinthians, exploring how the church can flourish and reinvent itself even in times of adversity. We've learned that:

1. We are united by one Spirit, hearing one gospel, and confessing that Jesus is Lord through our "breath-gifts" or word-gifts
2. These gifts exist for the greater good of the body—everyone has a role to play
3. Our use of these gifts must be motivated by love
4. Today we examine what loving speech looks like, particularly in how we use our word-speaking gifts

The Bible seems to prioritize prophecy when it comes to loving communication. Though some may find these teachings challenging, we must approach Scripture with open hearts.

## 1. Foretelling: Predicting the Future

> 1 Corinthians 14:1 Follow the way of love and eagerly desire the Spirit, especially prophecy.

### The Meaning of Prophecy

Etymologically, the Greek word "to prophesy" (prophēteuo) means "to speak before." This typically refers to **foretelling**—predicting the future.

Old Testament prophets like Isaiah predicted specific future events with remarkable detail. For example, Isaiah (living around 700 BC) predicted King Cyrus's coming approximately 150 years in advance:

> Isaiah 45:1 "This is what the Lord says to his anointed, to Cyrus, whose right hand I take hold of to subdue nations before him and to strip kings of their armor, to open doors before him so that gates will not be shut."

Other prophets like Jeremiah warned of invasion and exile. The test of a true prophet was whether his predictions came true (Deuteronomy 18:22).

In the New Testament, examples of foretelling include Agabus predicting a severe famine that would spread over the entire Roman world (Acts 11:27-28), and Jesus himself foretelling his death and resurrection (Mark 9:31).

### The Problem with Modern Prophecy Claims

Some Christian movements today claim the gift of foretelling still operates, describing "words of knowledge" or "words of revelation"—predictions like "you will marry someone this year" or "a great job is around the corner."

However, this creates a problem. Old Testament prophecies were specific and detailed, not vague generalities. The standard was extraordinarily high: if a prophecy didn't come true, the prophet faced death (Deuteronomy 18:20-22).

### Why Foretelling Has Ended

> Hebrews 1:1-2 "In the past God spoke to our ancestors through the prophets at many times and in various ways, but in these last days he has spoken to us by his Son, whom he appointed heir of all things, and through whom also he made the universe."

With the coming of Christ, foretelling as we knew it has ended. What more could God tell us about the future? The most important event has been announced: Jesus died, was raised, ascended, and is coming back.

The future is already settled for believers. We know Christ returns. Compared to that ultimate reality, all other predictions pale in significance. As Christians, we possess knowledge of the future's culmination—what else matters?

### A Form of Prophecy Today

Yet there is a legitimate sense in which Christians can prophesy today: **foresight based on God's wisdom**. Through understanding God's law and gospel, we gain wisdom to see consequences that others miss. Like mothers who warn their children based on experience, we can discern what lies ahead spiritually and morally.

> Proverbs 22:3 "The prudent see danger and take refuge, but the simple keep going and pay the penalty."

> 1 Thessalonians 5:19-21 "Do not quench the Spirit. Do not treat prophecies with contempt but test them all; hold on to what is good, reject every kind of evil."

We are called to think long-term, not short-sightedly. When the world sees only immediate crisis, believers apply God's perspective to see far-reaching consequences. This requires asking hard questions about economics, politics, and society—questions that aren't popular but that demonstrate prophetic wisdom.

Are we thinking like prophets, or are we trapped in short-term thinking like the world around us?

## 2. Forth-telling: Speaking God's Word to Others

### What Edifies?

Rather than foretelling, Paul emphasizes the **function** of prophecy:

> 1 Corinthians 14:3 "But the one who prophesies speaks to people for their strengthening, encouraging and comfort."

The Greek word for "strengthening" is better translated as **edification**—building up. Just as a building is made of stones stacked together, the church is built through our communication.

What words build up the church? Scripture shows us:

**The Word of Grace**
> Acts 20:32 "Now I commit you to God and to the word of his grace, which can build you up and give you an inheritance among all those who are sanctified."

**Truth in Love**
> Ephesians 4:15 "Instead, speaking the truth in love, we will grow to become in every respect the mature body of him who is the head, that is, Christ."

**The Gospel**
> Romans 15:20 "It has always been my ambition to preach the gospel where Christ was not known, so that I would not be building on someone else's foundation."

Even in the Old Testament, God worked through his prophets' proclamation. As He told Jeremiah:

> Jeremiah 1:9-10 "Then the Lord reached out his hand and touched my mouth and said to me, 'Now, I have put my words in your mouth. See, today I appoint you over nations and kingdoms to uproot and tear down, to destroy and overthrow, to build and to plant.'"

### Forth-telling Today

Perhaps prophecy is less about **foretelling** and more about **forth-telling**—not telling before time, but telling **before people**. It's about helping those who are ignorant.

The original prophets did this constantly. Moses, the greatest prophet, primarily called Israel to remember: "Remember what God did for you in Egypt and at Sinai" (Deuteronomy). The New Testament prophets preached Christ's death and resurrection—past events—which founded the church.

Today's prophecy means **proclaiming what God has done in the past**. It's like preaching. We're not predicting the future; we're reminding people of God's word and character.

### Practical Application

When we gather, we exercise our gifts through:

- Speaking the words of God to each other
- Reading God's word to each other
- Speaking truth in love
- Offering encouragement to keep going
- Providing comfort when life is difficult

**We prophesy to one another.**

This happens after formal worship—during coffee, in small groups, at dinner. Begin with small talk, but don't stay there. Ask how people are doing spiritually. What's been challenging? How's your Bible reading? Your prayer life? What's been encouraging this week? What's been a struggle?

In all our gatherings—breakout rooms, dinner conversations, basketball games, time with our mothers—we should speak to build each other up. Not for show, but genuinely, in love.

Don't keep others ignorant about what Jesus has done for us on the cross. Bring them in. Use your speech for edification.

## 3. Faux-telling: Speaking in Tongues

### Tongues Versus Prophecy

Paul contrasts prophecy with speaking in tongues:

> 1 Corinthians 14:2 "For anyone who speaks in a tongue does not speak to men but to God. Indeed, no one understands him; he utters mysteries with his spirit."

At first glance this sounds good—speaking to God, uttering mysteries. But Paul's meaning is different: only God knows what you're saying because no one else understands you.

The contrast is stark:

> 1 Corinthians 14:3-5 "But everyone who prophesies speaks to people for their strengthening, encouraging and comfort. He who speaks in a tongue edifies himself, but he who prophesies edifies the church... I would like every one of you to speak in tongues, but I would rather have you prophesy. He who prophesies is greater than one who speaks in tongues, unless he interprets, so that the church may be edified."

Paul even says:

> 1 Corinthians 14:18-19 "I thank God that I speak in tongues more than all of you. But in the church I would rather speak five intelligible words to instruct others than ten thousand words in a tongue."

The comparison is harsh: five Australian cents is more valuable than ten thousand worthless foreign currency notes. In church, intelligibility is everything.

### The Problem: Unintelligibility

> 1 Corinthians 14:6-10 "Now, brothers and sisters, if I come to you and speak in tongues, what good will I be to you, unless I bring you some revelation or knowledge or prophecy or word of instruction? Even in the case of lifeless things that make sounds, such as the pipe or harp, how will anyone know what tune is being played unless there is a distinction in the notes? Again, if the trumpet does not sound a clear call, who will get ready for battle? So it is with you. Unless you speak intelligible words with your tongue, how will anyone know what you are saying? You will just be speaking into the air."

When you speak in a language no one understands, you're essentially talking to yourself. Speaking into the air, not to people. That's self-centered: the speaker edifies themselves alone while the church gains nothing.

Paul identifies the problem in Corinth as **faux-telling**—speaking lots of words for your own benefit, not for others. Gas bags. Show-offs. Selfish exercise of gifts.

> 1 Corinthians 14:11 "If then I do not grasp the meaning of what someone is saying, I am a foreigner to the speaker, and the speaker is a foreigner to me."

Instead of functioning as one body, you become foreigners to each other—aliens unable to communicate.

### Theological Depth: Tongues Reverse Pentecost

There's a deeper theological issue. Paul grounds his argument in the Old Testament:

> 1 Corinthians 14:21 "In the Law it is written: 'With other tongues and through the lips of foreigners I will speak to this people, but even then they will not listen to me, says the Lord.'"

This references Isaiah 28:11. When God speaks through foreign languages, it signals judgment. In the Old Testament, foreign tongues meant invasion, conquest, the curse of Babel when God confused languages as judgment.

But Pentecost reversed this curse! At Pentecost, people from many nations heard the gospel in their own languages:

> Acts 2:5-11 "Now there were staying in Jerusalem God-fearing Jews from every nation under heaven. When they heard this sound, a crowd came together in bewilderment, because each one heard them speaking in his own language... 'We hear them declaring the wonders of God in our own tongues!'"

Pentecost opened the gospel to all people. It was the reversal of Babel.

If the Corinthian church returned to incomprehensible speech, they would reverse Pentecost. They would make the gospel exclusive again—only for the elite who understand the gibberish.

> 1 Corinthians 14:22-25 "Tongues, then, are a sign, not for believers but for unbelievers; prophecy, however, is not for unbelievers but for believers. So if the whole church comes together and everyone speaks in tongues, and inquirers or unbelievers come in, will they not say that you are out of your mind? But if an unbeliever or an inquirer comes in while everyone is prophesying, they are convicted of sin and are brought under judgment by all, as the secrets of their hearts are laid bare. So they will fall down and worship God, exclaiming, 'God is really among you!'"

When unbelievers encounter unintelligible speech, they think the church is mad. When they encounter intelligible proclamation of God's truth, they encounter God himself.

### Intelligibility Matters

> 1 Corinthians 14:26 "What then shall we say, brothers and sisters? When you come together, each of you has a hymn, or a word of instruction, a revelation, a tongue or an interpretation. Everything must be done so that the church may be built up."

The principle is clear: **Everything must serve edification.**

This applies beyond language. It includes cultural context. If you're speaking about K-dramas and someone in the group has never seen one, they become a foreigner. Solution? Don't stop talking about K-dramas. Bring the new person in first, explain the context, then enjoy it together.

The same applies in church. We must speak in ways our community understands. We use English, not jargon. We employ culturally relevant illustrations. We consider our young people alongside our older members. We seek to be intelligible to inquirers who visit.

This is why combined church services across different communities are difficult. A temporary reversal of Pentecost occurs:

- Different location creates unfamiliarity
- Different cultural references create barriers
- Different songs and styles alienate newcomers
- Outsiders feel excluded from an in-group

To maintain the gospel's accessibility and the reality of Pentecost, we keep our services as intelligible as possible to our specific community.

## The Call to Edification

> 1 Corinthians 14:39-40 "Therefore, my brothers and sisters, be eager to prophesy, and do not forbid speaking in tongues. But everything should be done in a fitting and orderly way."

Paul's final command is clear: **Desire prophecy. Build each other up.**

The purpose of church is edification through the intelligent proclamation of the gospel. This happens through:

- One-on-one conversations finished with prayer
- Email exchanges about spiritual matters
- Bible studies together
- Corporate worship

When we use our speech to love, to speak the gospel, to build each other up, we create a wonderful church—a people of love, a community that cares about each other and God's will.

I know this works because it has happened before. You are the fruit of previous generations who spoke God's words faithfully. It's what the Bible teaches us.

**Will you join in this prophecy?**
