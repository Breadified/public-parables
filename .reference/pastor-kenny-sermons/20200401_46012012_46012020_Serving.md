# 1 Corinthians 12:12-20

**Title:** Serving  
**Date:** 26 April 2020  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## The Question We Ask

What is your gift? Does it feel useless? Many of us wonder if what we have to offer truly matters in the church.

This same question troubled the Corinthian church—a city in Greece where believers were fighting over their spiritual gifts and how to exercise them. Paul wrote to answer their questions directly.

> 12 Just as a body, though one, has many parts, but all its many parts form one body, so it is with Christ.

One body. Many parts. This is the fundamental truth that governs everything Paul will say.

---

## The One Body (v. 13)

> 13 For we were all baptized by one Spirit so as to form one body—whether Jews or Gentiles, slave or free—and we were all given the one Spirit to drink.

What does it mean to be truly one?

### The Spiritual Baptism

Baptism in Jewish practice was a ritual washing that identified the person being washed. It was ceremonial cleansing, an outward sign of belonging to something greater. John the Baptist baptized people into the movement, uniting them in a common cause.

But the Corinthians were fighting over who had baptized them—some claimed Paul, others Apollos. They were missing the point entirely.

> 1 Corinthians 1:17 For Christ did not send me to baptize, but to preach the gospel—not with wisdom and eloquence, lest the cross of Christ be emptied of its power.

The water itself means nothing. Our spiritual baptism—the one that truly unites us—is baptism into the Spirit of truth. This is our identification with Christ as our Master, our unification under the same spiritual reality. This happened the moment we believed in the Lordship of Jesus.

When we identify with the Spirit, we identify with Christ. We are united under one Master, bound by the same spiritual truth.

### Internal Cleansing

The baptism we receive is not external washing but internal transformation. The Holy Spirit cleanses us from within, not from the outside in. This is a cleansing that reaches the deepest parts of our being—our hearts, our desires, our identity.

## We All Belong (v. 15-20)

> 15 Now if the foot should say, "Because I am not a hand, I do not belong to the body," it would not for that reason stop being part of the body. 16 And if the ear should say, "Because I am not an eye, I do not belong to the body," it would not for that reason stop being part of the body.

### The Temptation to Exclude Ourselves

We naturally assume some parts are more important than others. A hand seems superior to a foot—it's functional, multi-purpose, attractive. Hands command attention; we show them off proudly. Meanwhile, feet carry weight, gather dirt, stay hidden in shoes.

Similarly, an eye seems more valuable than an ear. In our visual age, eyes draw admiration. People say to those they love, "I love your eyes." Who has ever heard someone say, "I love your ears"? We want our eyes large and visible; we prefer our ears small and concealed.

It's tempting for the foot to say, "I don't belong." It's tempting for the ear to say, "I don't belong."

### The Logic of the Body

But this is foolish reasoning. Each part of the body cannot simply remove itself because it feels inadequate.

> 17 If the whole body were an eye, where would the sense of hearing be? If the whole body were an ear, where would the sense of smell be?

Body parts don't exist individually; they only function as part of a whole. No one looks at their feet and thinks, "These are ugly and smell bad—let me remove them." No one cuts off their ear because it should be hidden. This would be absurd.

> 18 But in fact God has placed the parts in the body, every one of them, just as he wanted them to be.

Even the tiny pinky toe serves a critical purpose—it aids balance. And when you stub it, the pain is disproportionate to its size, reminding you of its importance. Every part belongs. Every part matters.

### What a Body Without Diversity Looks Like

> 19 If they were all one part, where would the body be?

Imagine a congregation where no one cares for anyone else. Everyone speaks at their own pleasure without listening. Everyone does whatever they want, thinking only of themselves. No one welcomes each other. No one prays together. No one confesses to each other. Such a church would be utterly monstrous—filled with "eyes" consumed with self-importance, serving no one but themselves.

**Don't let our church become like that.** Make a difference by taking your part, by being the part you are called to be.

### We All Feel Out of Place

Friends, we must be honest: we all feel out of it at times. We are all minority groups, even within a minority church.

At my previous church, the older members complained the church was too young. When I showed them a distribution chart, it revealed equal numbers across every age group. Yet if you were the oldest, you saw only young people behind you. If you were young, everyone seemed older. If you were in the middle, no one was your age.

We feel this way about many things:

- **The newcomer:** Your first time here, the place feels strange, you know no one. "I don't belong."
- **The unbeliever:** Pressured to attend by someone else, feeling completely out of place. "I don't belong here."
- **The sick or struggling:** Fearing judgment and rejection. "People will find out about my condition. They'll look down on me. I don't belong."
- **Parents with unruly children:** Everyone else seems so composed. "I don't belong."
- **The teenager:** So small among so many adults. No one notices you. "I don't belong."
- **The one struggling with sin:** Everyone seems so godly. "They'll discover my secret. I'll be judged. I don't belong."
- **Even the pastor:** Wondering if your jokes have grown lamer with age. Feeling out of touch. "I don't belong."

But here's the truth: we all feel disenfranchised. Especially when we focus on our differences.

### Look to Our Unity Instead

Don't focus on our differences. Look to our unity in Christ, and you will see how valuable we are to each other.

- **Without newcomers,** how would we learn to love strangers? How would we become like the Good Samaritan?
- **Without the sick,** how would we learn to show concern and genuine love?
- **The person who frustrates you** becomes your teacher in patience and grace.
- **Our elders** bring wisdom.
- **Our young people** bring vitality and hope.
- **Even the unbeliever** keeps us honest, asking the tough questions we need to hear.

**So don't say "I don't belong."** Turn up to church. Be encouraged and encourage others. Sometimes when hard times hit, fellowship is the first thing we abandon because we feel judged. We are committed to making this a safe, caring place to belong.

Please don't disappear on us. We need each other.

## We Need Each Other (v. 21-26)

> 21 The eye cannot say to the hand, "I don't need you!" And the head cannot say to the feet, "I don't need you!"

### The Pride of the Strong

It's tempting for those who are strong and flourishing to say, "I don't need you." If you're doing well in life, if things are fitting together nicely and you're gifted in many ways, it's easy to arrive at church with the attitude, "I don't need this."

- You can study the Bible alone.
- You can attend another Christian conference.
- You're self-sufficient.

Yes, you're right. You may not technically need us.

**But while you don't need us, we still need you.**

### The Indispensable Weaker Parts

> 22 On the contrary, those parts of the body that seem to be weaker are indispensable, 23 and the parts that we think are less honorable we treat with special honor. And the parts that are unpresentable are treated with special modesty, 24 while our presentable parts need no special treatment. But God has put the body together, giving greater honor to the parts that lacked it.

You are strong for a reason: to help the weaker parts.

> Romans 15:1 We who are strong ought to bear with the failings of the weak and not to please ourselves.

We cannot find another hand to replace you. We cannot find another eye. The whole point of the body is diversity working toward the common good. The feet cannot suddenly transform into hands in the absence of a hand. The ear cannot spontaneously become an eye. Body parts aren't that flexible. We need them to be what they were created to be.

### Equal Concern

> 25 So that there should be no division in the body, but that its parts should have equal concern for each other. 26 If one part suffers, every part suffers with it; if one part is honored, every part rejoices with it.

This is how a healthy body functions—through mutual concern and shared emotion. When one member suffers, we all feel it. When one is honored, we all rejoice.

> 27 Now you are the body of Christ, and each one of you is a part of it.

### Building a Church of Belonging

Do you want a place where people can confess their sins to each other? It won't happen without commitment to speak honestly with one another.

Do you want a place to truly learn? It requires regular attendance and engagement.

Do you want us to pray for each other? It demands commitment.

**Who is the most important person in this room?** The person next to you. The other person.

Look at your neighbor on the screen—left, right, above, below. We are all important to each other. We need each other for this body to function properly.

We can hear God's word faithfully each week—not just from the pulpit, but from each other as we share what we've learned from our Lord, as we confess our struggles and difficulties, as we hear one another and offer help and prayer. As outsiders come and hear God's word, they see how love actually operates here.

**This is what it means to be truly spiritual.**
