# Romans 14:1–15:13

**Title:** Serve the Weak
**Date:** 22 June 2024

## When Christians Disagree

Throughout church history, believers have faced many controversies over matters that Scripture doesn't explicitly settle. These disputes range from theology—such as debates over Christ's blood or the nature of communion—to church practices like which day to gather, what to wear, and what music to use.

The early Church Father Origen once famously misread Matthew 19:12 and took Jesus's words about eunuchs for the kingdom of heaven as literal instruction, leading him to castrate himself. This cautionary tale reminds us that how we interpret Scripture matters deeply.

This passage addresses how Christians should treat one another when they disagree on non-essential matters. Over recent weeks, we've been exploring how to serve in light of Christ's service to us. This week, we look at getting along with the church—how to treat fellow believers when we don't see eye to eye.

## 1. Accept the Weak. Do Not Despise

> Accept the one whose faith is weak, without quarreling over disputable matters. One person's faith allows them to eat anything, but another, whose faith is weak, eats only vegetables. (Romans 14:1–2)

Paul describes two types of Christians. The "weak" in faith are those who eat only vegetables, avoid certain foods, or regard some days as more sacred than others. The "strong" in faith are those who eat anything and view all days alike.

### Who Are They?

The context suggests these likely refer to the Jewish and Gentile believers in the Roman church. The weak appear to be Jewish Christians who maintained the Old Testament dietary laws—avoiding pork and keeping sabbaths and festival days. The strong are those (Jews or Gentiles) who felt liberated in Christ to break free from these past observances.

Jesus Himself declared all foods clean (Mark 7), and Peter received a vision confirming this. Since idols are nothing before the Lord, meat sacrificed to them is merely meat.

### The Challenge of Conscience

Imagine someone trained from childhood in strict dietary purity. His reflexes, his very instincts, have been shaped by a lifetime of obedience. Now he's told these restrictions no longer apply. But understanding this intellectually and feeling it emotionally are two different things—much like how we might rationally know a cockroach is just another insect, yet our childhood fear remains.

When the strong eat freely or invite the weak to join them, the weak Christian may see this as encouragement toward sin. The strong, meanwhile, might look down on the weak for being immature or lacking faith.

### How Not to Judge

> The one who eats everything must not treat with contempt the one who does not, and the one who does not eat everything must not judge the one who does, for God has accepted them. (Romans 14:3)

Paul commands: don't judge. Yet he himself uses the terms "strong" and "weak"—isn't that judging? The key is understanding what "judge" means here.

**What Paul Does NOT Mean:**

"Don't judge" does not mean there is no right and wrong, nor that truth is relative. The truth is clear: eating pork is acceptable, and meat sacrificed to idols is acceptable. There is objective truth in these disputable matters.

**What "Judge" Actually Means in Scripture:**

Throughout Scripture, we're actually called to judge—to discern between false and true teaching, between the trustworthy and untrustworthy. Jesus tells us to judge for ourselves (Luke 12:57). Peter and John ask their accusers, "Which is right in God's eyes: to listen to you, or to him? You be the judges" (Acts 4:19). Paul expects believers to judge disputes among themselves (1 Corinthians 5:12; 6:5) and urges the church to be discerning (1 Corinthians 10:15).

Being Christian doesn't mean you surrender your mind in the name of tolerance.

**What Paul Means by "Judge":**

By "judge," Paul means to condemn, despise, look down upon. This becomes clear from the parallel language:

> You, then, why do you judge your brother or sister? Or why do you treat them with contempt? (Romans 14:10)

Here "judge" is used synonymously with "contempt." In verse 1, "quarreling" carries the sense of condemning. The pattern appears throughout Scripture: Jesus speaks of those "confident of their own righteousness" who "looked down on everyone else" (Luke 18:9). Paul warns against those whose way of life is "scorned in the church" (1 Corinthians 6:4).

### The Two Kinds of Judgment

One kind of judgment is to **discern**—to distinguish right from wrong, true from false. We must do this. The other kind is to **condemn**—to hold someone guilty when they have done nothing wrong. We must not do this.

Instead, recognize this truth:

> For we will all stand before God's judgment seat. For it is written: "As surely as I live," says the Lord, "every knee will bow before me; every tongue will acknowledge God." So then, each of us will give an account of ourselves to God. (Romans 14:10–12)

They are someone else's servant—God's servant. You are not their ultimate judge.

## 2. Look After the Weak. Do Not Stumble Them

> Therefore let us stop passing judgment on one another. Instead, make up your mind not to put any stumbling block or obstacle in the way of a brother or sister. (Romans 14:13)

Paul now shifts the emphasis. Even though you know the truth—that all food is clean—don't exercise your freedom in ways that harm others.

> I am convinced, being fully persuaded in the Lord Jesus, that nothing is unclean in itself. But if anyone regards something as unclean, then for that person it is unclean. (Romans 14:14)

This is crucial: **we are not talking about accommodating actual sin.** If someone struggles with pornography, drunkenness, or adultery, we don't call that weakness and ignore it. These are sins requiring rebuke, correction, and encouragement through proper channels (Matthew 18). That's our pastoral duty.

But the weak here are not those committing sin. Their weakness is that they have a more sensitive conscience. They haven't yet grasped Christian freedom fully. Coming from a different background, what you know to be legitimate freedom, they perceive as sin.

When they act against their conscience, **for them it becomes sin**—not because the act itself is sinful, but because they're violating their own conviction:

> But whoever has doubts is condemned if they eat, because their eating is not from faith; and everything that does not come from faith is sin. (Romans 14:23)

### Guard Their Conscience

Pastorally, it's vital not to break someone's conscience. When you persuade a believer to act against their conscience—even if the act is objectively neutral—you set a dangerous precedent. You teach them that it's acceptable to do what you believe is wrong. The conscience is our last defense against sin, our internal "yuck factor." We want people to have an informed and better-trained conscience, not a broken one.

We play a role in that development. By respecting their conscience, we honor the Holy Spirit's work in their lives:

> If your brother or sister is distressed because of what you eat, you are no longer acting in love. Do not by your eating destroy someone for whom Christ died. Therefore do not let what you know is good be spoken of as evil. (Romans 14:15–16)

The "good" is the food itself, which is neutral. The "evil" isn't the food—it's the method of causing someone to stumble.

> For the kingdom of God is not a matter of eating and drinking, but of righteousness, peace and joy in the Holy Spirit, because anyone who serves Christ in this way is pleasing to God and receives human approval. (Romans 14:17–18)

### Practical Applications

**Alcohol and Gambling**

These two vices break apart families and fuel domestic violence through addiction. Yet objectively, alcohol and gambling are neutral things—good gifts from God used appropriately.

However, those from backgrounds of drunkenness or gambling will think you're encouraging sin. Those struggling with these addictions may relapse if you normalize them. The good becomes evil in the context of their weakness.

We don't implement these policies out of legalism. Rather, we do it for the weak among us—and we don't know who they are. Alcoholics don't wear signs. This is why churches maintain policies without alcohol or gambling at church functions and events. It's not about lacking freedom; it's about exercising freedom with love.

**Modesty and Dress**

I dress more formally when I visit our Chinese congregation once a month because they would stumble otherwise. They'd think I don't respect God. It's not loving to them to disregard their conscience.

Similarly, while there's no law about how you dress, consider your brothers and sisters. If you dress immodestly, you make it harder for others to avoid lustful thoughts. The defense that one strong person isn't affected misses the point entirely—we're looking after the **weak**.

**Social Gatherings**

There's nothing inherently sinful about dancing or darkness or loud music. But consider the bigger picture. When you combine alcohol, darkness, and music designed to lower inhibitions—all together—you're setting up a context where most people, not just a few, will likely stumble.

Think about what's best for your friends' spiritual growth. Is this where they're at their godliest? Or will this more likely cause them to fall?

### The Principle

> Let us therefore make every effort to do what leads to peace and to mutual edification. Do not destroy the work of God for the sake of food. All food is clean, but it is wrong for a person to eat anything that causes someone else to stumble. It is better not to eat meat or drink wine or to do anything else that will cause your brother or sister to fall. (Romans 14:19–21)

One day, perhaps, the weak will grow and no longer be worried about these things. But meanwhile, bear with their failings.

### Remember: You Answer to One Master

> So whatever you believe about these things keep between yourself and God. Blessed is the one who does not condemn himself by what he approves. (Romans 14:22)

Each Christian answers ultimately to God alone, not to the church, the pastor, or the Bible study leader. This doesn't mean no accountability—but it means your ultimate judge is God. This is why the Reformation recovered the doctrine of *Sola Scriptura*: Scripture alone, not the church, is the final authority. You can work out biblical truth yourself, though others may help you understand.

That said, if you're convinced your leader is teaching something wrong, you must have a justifiable case. You can't simply dislike something and hide behind this principle. You're still answerable to God.

Because your ultimate accountability is to God, you must be convinced in your own mind about what is right:

> One person considers one day more sacred than another; another considers every day alike. Each of them should be fully convinced in their own mind. (Romans 14:5)

This is individual conviction, settled between you and God. And remember:

> Our judgments are always provisional. We judge by external appearances, but God judges the heart. We only have partial knowledge; God has complete truth. Our judgment is always temporary—P-plate judgment. We're fallible.

> Judge nothing before the appointed time; wait until the Lord comes. He will bring to light what is hidden in darkness and will expose the motives of people's hearts. At that time each will receive their praise from God. (1 Corinthians 4:5)

### Be Humble and Slow to Judge

Be humble. If this isn't your struggle, don't be proud of it. Not everyone faces the same temptations. Be slow to judge, especially if you're quick to criticize. One sentence isn't enough to know someone's story. Take time for more conversation. Avoid caricaturing others' positions.

Remember also: **there is truth**, even if we don't know it yet. On the last day, God will sort out who was right and who was wrong. Some may discover you were both right on something trivial. But we're working together toward truth now. Accept one another as you journey together toward maturity.

## 3. The Strong Must Bear with the Weak

> We who are strong ought to bear with the failings of the weak and not to please ourselves. Each of us should please our neighbors for their good, to build them up. (Romans 15:1–2)

The strong have a particular responsibility. Your freedom isn't the highest value—love is. Your comfort matters less than your brother's spiritual growth.

> For even Christ did not please himself but, as it is written: "The insults of those who insult you have fallen on me." (Romans 15:3)

Christ is our model. He didn't insist on His rights; He bore our shame. He set aside His comfort for our redemption.

> For everything that was written in the past was written to teach us, so that through the endurance taught in the Scriptures and the encouragement they provide we might have hope. May the God who gives endurance and encouragement give you the same attitude of mind toward each other that Christ Jesus had, so that with one mind and one voice you may glorify the God and Father of our Lord Jesus Christ. (Romans 15:4–6)

## Accept One Another

> Accept one another, then, just as Christ accepted you, in order to bring praise to God. For I tell you that Christ has become a servant of the Jews on behalf of God's truth, so that the promises made to the patriarchs might be confirmed and, moreover, that the Gentiles might glorify God for his mercy. (Romans 15:7–9)

Paul makes his point by showing how God Himself included both Jews and Gentiles. Christ served the Jews to confirm God's promises, so that Gentiles might glorify Him too. Unity in diversity—this is God's design.

The same patience and time apply here. It takes time to change. Someone newly converted to Christ can't learn everything overnight. They need time to develop new habits, unlearn old ones, and grow in understanding. This applies whether someone comes from a Jewish background holding to dietary laws or from any other background that shapes their conscience.

The goal is for the weak to become strong. But that happens through patient love, not harsh judgment. Through community that bears one another's burdens. Through the endurance and encouragement of Scripture.

May the God of hope fill you with all joy and peace as you trust in Him, so that you may overflow with hope by the power of the Holy Spirit.
