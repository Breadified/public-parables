**Title:** A New Year

## A New Shoot: God's Promise of Renewal

> A shoot will come up from the stump of Jesse; from his roots a Branch will bear fruit. (Isaiah 11:1)

The prophecy opens with a powerful image of hope. Israel stands devastated—cut down by judgment like a great tree. But from the mere stump, a new shoot emerges. This is Isaiah's promise of restoration: what appears dead will live again.

Like a dried bean seed placed in cotton wool, seemingly lifeless, yet with water it bursts forth with green growth. Death becomes life. Israel's future, though appearing hopeless, will be radically renewed. This new shoot—this remnant—will be fundamentally different from the old Israel.

## 1. A Year of Righteousness: Judgment Beyond What We See

> The Spirit of the Lord will rest on him—the Spirit of wisdom and of understanding, the Spirit of counsel and of might, the Spirit of the knowledge and fear of the Lord. (Isaiah 11:2)

The future ruler possesses a spirit of righteousness characterized by wisdom, understanding, counsel, might, knowledge, and fear of the Lord. These qualities echo the titles given to Jesus in Isaiah 9:6 ("Wonderful Counsellor"). All these attributes point toward one thing: **right-ness**—righteousness itself.

He carries this burden because "the government will be on his shoulders." Governing justly is an awesome responsibility. Past kings failed; their judgments produced no fruit. But this ruler will judge with righteousness.

### The Third Criterion: The Sense of Smell

> He will delight in the fear of the Lord. He will not judge by what he sees with his eyes, or decide by what he hears with his ears. (Isaiah 11:3)

Here lies a stunning reversal: righteous judgment transcends mere sight and sound—our primary senses of communication. We read, we listen, we speak, we write. We judge based on what we can see and hear. But the ideal judge operates differently.

By contrast, he will "smell" justice. This is not merely poetic—it refers to the deeper spiritual intuition that comes from wisdom and experience. To "smell" means to perceive through spiritual sensitivity, through recognition of patterns, through understanding human nature, through historical context, and through the fear of God.

### Modern Application: Media Manipulation

Today, in our age of media saturation, this teaching is urgent. Our world cares only about what can be seen and heard. 

Consider how documentaries shape belief: emotional imagery of suffering children, grieving mothers, distressed animals, accompanied by mournful violins and a trustworthy narrator. We see. We hear. **We believe**—often without deeper investigation.

When billionaires broadcast their grievances to millions through polished productions, people respond to the emotional narrative, not to truth. We wage wars based on images and sounds. We invest billions based on sight and sound.

> Do not show favoritism to a poor person in his lawsuit. (Exodus 23:3)

This strange command warns us: we often side with whoever tells the most sympathetic story. We follow those who best manipulate us through vision and sound.

The Christian must resist this. Righteous judgment requires:
- Wisdom gained from years of experience
- Understanding of human behavior and pattern recognition
- Knowledge of history and context
- Patience to wait for fuller evidence before concluding
- Principles rooted in what is truly right and wrong

This is what it means to "smell"—to have the Spirit of God. The Hebrew word for "spirit" and "smell" share the same root, suggesting that to possess God's Spirit is to possess God's breath, God's scent. To detect the aroma of reverence for God.

### The Christian's Resolution

> He will strike the earth with the rod of his mouth; with the breath of his lips he will slay the wicked. (Isaiah 11:4-5)

The rod of his mouth—like a warrior's weapon—represents the sword of the Spirit, which is the Word of God (Ephesians 6:17). God's Word determines our spiritual scent. It shapes how we perceive reality.

**For those making New Year's resolutions:** Renew your love for God's Word. Read Scripture personally. Read together with the church. Let God's truth transform your behavior into righteousness.

Resolve not to let emotion manipulate you. Reject the media, government, and advertisers who seek to deceive. Base your decisions on God's principles and God's truth, not on what looks good, sounds good, or feels good.

## 2. A Year of Reconciliation: Unity Across Difference

> The wolf will live with the lamb, the leopard will lie down with the goat, the calf and the lion and the yearling together; and a little child will lead them. (Isaiah 11:6-7)

This passage presents reconciliation through stark contrast. The predators—wolf, leopard, lion—are quick, confident, but vicious and cunning. The prey—lamb, goat, calf, yearling—are innocent and gentle but slow.

Yet despite their complete incompatibility, they live together in peace. A child leads them. This child is the one born of a virgin (Isaiah 9), who became Jesus. The child who was bitten by the serpent for our sake.

> They will neither harm nor destroy on all my holy mountain, for the earth will be filled with the knowledge of the Lord as the waters cover the sea. (Isaiah 11:9)

The "holy mountain" points to the eschatological church—God's people filled with knowledge of the Lord. Such knowledge brings reconciliation. Peace breaks out between natural enemies. Lion and lamb dwell together.

### Practical Reconciliation in the Church

While we wait for the eternal fulfillment, we have the church—our "family" to practice reconciliation now.

Our strength as a congregation lies in being a true family church. We have the young, bringing energy. We have the spiritually mature, bringing wisdom and experience. We have those in the middle, building families. This diversity—these differences—require intentional reconciliation.

Some of us are lambs. Some are wolves. The call is to forgive, to reconcile, to live together.

We are blessed to gather weekly—53 family meals each year. Don't skip them. For those who are regular, commit to at least 49 of the 53. This consistency matters.

Reconciliation requires time. Time to pray for one another. Time to speak truth in love (Ephesians 4:15). Time to learn our own errors, to retract harsh words, to ask forgiveness, to repair relationships.

If we are to learn God's truth together, disagreements are inevitable. Resolving them requires commitment and patience. We must wait for fuller understanding, reflect on our positions, and allow time for healing.

In our media-saturated culture, people rarely discuss important things together anymore. This cannot be us. The gospel creates a community where wolves and lambs belong to each other.

## 3. A Year of Reaching: The Banner for the Nations

> In that day the Root of Jesse will stand as a banner for the peoples; the nations will rally to him, and his resting place will be glorious. (Isaiah 11:10)

Notice the progression: stump → shoot → root. From a small beginning, this plant becomes a great tree. The scope expands from Israel to the whole world.

> In that day the Lord will reach out his hand a second time to reclaim the surviving remnant of his people from Assyria, from Lower Egypt, from Upper Egypt, from Cush, from Elam, from Babylonia, from Hamath and from the islands of the Mediterranean. (Isaiah 11:11)

All the exiles scattered by Assyria and Babylon will return. But God's vision exceeds Israel's restoration. He intends to gather people from the four corners of the earth.

> He will raise a banner for the nations and gather the exiles of Israel; he will assemble the scattered people of Judah from the four quarters of the earth. (Isaiah 11:12)

The Jewish tree becomes a nesting place for birds from every nation. They find rest and protection in it. The task of reaching the nations continues as Christ's mission.

### Seizing the Moment

We are emerging from years of pandemic isolation. Society opens. People venture back out. Many are curious about God. After hibernation, people are ready to connect—and some may be drawn to discover what Christians know and why we gather.

Shall we reach out to them?

**This year, share the gospel naturally:** at your workplace, school, and neighborhood. Meet friends for coffee and lunch. Invite them to your home. Talk about Jesus. Or simply invite them to church—our family meals where everyone belongs.

If you make resolutions, adopt this one: **"Each One Reach One."** Bring at least one friend or one family to church this year.

But remember: the ultimate banner is not a physical sign. The ultimate attraction is God himself—specifically, Jesus and his love, his sacrifice, his humility, his wisdom.

> The Lord is my Banner. (Exodus 17:15)

When enemies attack and obstacles loom, the banner that matters is not our promotional efforts or clever marketing. It is the person and work of Jesus Christ himself.

Many people look downward at their circumstances and struggles. They don't see the banner. Our role isn't to do anything dramatic. We simply point upward. We direct attention to Christ.

Evangelism is simple: one beggar telling another beggar where to find bread. We point to the banner. We point to Jesus.

Social media, church gatherings, personal conversations—all are opportunities to point upward, to draw attention to Christ, to make the banner visible to those who haven't yet seen it.

## Closing Vision

New Year's Day rarely falls on Sunday, yet we gather today to begin 2023 together, doing what we love—following our God and loving each other.

Let's make this a year of **righteousness**—judging by God's truth, not the world's sights and sounds.

Let's make this a year of **reconciliation**—wolves and lambs learning to live together in the church family.

Let's make this a year of **reaching**—pointing lost people upward to the banner of Jesus.

Happy New Year!
