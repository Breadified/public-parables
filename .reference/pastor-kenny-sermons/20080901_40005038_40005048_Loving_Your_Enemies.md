# Matthew 5:38-48

**Title:** Loving Your Enemies  
**Date:** 7 September 2008  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Story of Kathy Diosy

Kathy Diosy was a Jewish Hungarian girl whose family was devastated by the Holocaust. After the war ended and the Russians liberated Budapest, she discovered that her father had been killed and that she had no remaining relatives. One morning, she read that Nazi collaborators—men who had organized the slave labor system—had been convicted and sentenced to death. Their bodies would be hung from lampposts in the city square as a public display.

Kathy walked down to see the bodies herself. As she stood there, a stranger approached and muttered bitterly, "Death is too good for them. They should have been put into slave labor gangs themselves—driven and driven until they died." Kathy nodded in agreement. An old Jewish man watched her reach out and touch the boots of one of the dangling bodies. When he asked why she had done this, she replied simply: "I wanted to be sure. If I touch, I am sure."

She wanted confirmation that at least some of those who had caused such suffering had finally paid with their lives. For a moment, she felt the surge of revenge—a grim satisfaction. But as she walked back toward her apartment, the bitterness returned. It wasn't enough. Some had been caught, but many had escaped. Justice felt incomplete.

Do you have an experience like this? Perhaps not as extreme as the Holocaust, but if you live long enough, we all face a bitter enemy and must wrestle with what it means to respond rightly.

We are in our second week studying the Sermon on the Mount, and it is my prayer that this passage will speak to us about the matter of loving our enemies—and what that truly means.

## 1. The Law: "You Have Heard"

Jesus continues his reinterpretation of the Old Testament Law on the Mount, acting as a second Moses who restates the Laws of Moses for his generation. Two laws demand our attention.

### A. Eye for an Eye (Lex Talionis)

> "You have heard that it was said, 'Eye for eye, and tooth for tooth.'" (Matthew 5:38)

This law originates in the Old Testament. In Exodus 21:24, within the context of assault, the law prescribed: "eye for eye, tooth for tooth, hand for hand, foot for foot, burn for burn, wound for wound, bruise for bruise."

In another context—assault by a master against a servant—Exodus 21:26 established what amounts to ancient workers' compensation: "If a man hits a manservant or maidservant in the eye and destroys it, he must let the servant go free to compensate for the eye."

**Why was this law good?**

- **Justice:** It promoted fairness and equal retribution. When someone wrongs another, there is a price to pay. If you total my car, you cannot simply give me your bicycle—the compensation must match the harm.
- **Proportionality:** The law limited retaliation to the degree of harm. Not two eyes for an eye; not the entire tribe executed for one death.
- **Restraint:** This was a restrictive law—an excellent tool for eliminating blood feuds and inter-tribal warfare. Without it, vengeance escalates endlessly. One tribe cuts a hand; the other kills in retaliation; the first tribe slaughters more; the second retaliates further. Think of modern cycles: Palestine and Israel, where one death leads to a suicide bombing, which leads to airstrikes, which leads to invasion—endless escalation. Eye for an eye stopped that cycle by establishing clear boundaries.

### B. Love Your Neighbor (Leviticus 19:18)

> "You have heard that it was said, 'Love your neighbor and hate your enemy.'" (Matthew 5:43)

Leviticus 19:18 commands: "Do not seek revenge or bear a grudge against one of your people, but love your neighbor as yourself. I am the Lord." Later, Leviticus 19:34 extends this to the alien living among you.

This was also a good law. In the context of Israel becoming a new society—transitioning from slaves to citizens—the command to love your neighbor created the bonds of community that made society possible.

### C. Good Laws, Manipulated

**The problem:** Over time, both these laws were distorted from their original intention.

The **Eye for an Eye** law was manipulated in two ways:

1. **From restriction to permission:** The oral tradition accompanying the written law began to shift the emphasis. What started as a limit on vengeance—"you may take exactly this much, no more"—gradually became a justification for vengeance itself: "You must take revenge." People began finding loopholes, stretching the law to permit personal retaliation.

2. **From justice to self-interest:** Like children pushing bedtime boundaries ("one more story, one more prayer, one more cuddle"), the spirit of the law eroded into something unrecognizable. Instead of tempering justice with mercy, it became a tool for justifying personal grudges.

The **Love Your Neighbor** law was similarly corrupted:

The Old Testament never commands hatred of enemies. But some Jewish teachers took the word "neighbor" to mean exclusively "our people"—and from that, they inferred that enemies must be hated. This was convenient theology, especially when dealing with Roman occupiers. The definition of "neighbor" became tribal rather than universal. Jesus would later clarify this through the parable of the Good Samaritan: a neighbor is anyone in your position to help, regardless of ethnicity or nationality.

## 2. Jesus' Recalibration of the Law

Against these manipulations, Jesus reinterprets the Law back to its original purpose. His reinterpretation is not abolition—he comes to fulfill, not destroy—but recalibration.

### A. Do Not Resist the Evil Person (v.39-42)

> "But I tell you, do not resist an evil person. If someone strikes you on the right cheek, turn to him the other also. And if someone wants to sue you and take your tunic, let him have your cloak as well. If someone forces you to go one mile, go with him two miles. Give to the one who asks you, and do not turn away from the one who wants to borrow from you."

Jesus reframes the law entirely. Instead of proportional retaliation, he commands no retaliation. But notice: he doesn't just command passivity. He offers four concrete illustrations of how to love the one who harms you:

1. **The slap:** Turn the other cheek—absorb the blow meant as humiliation and offer dignity instead.
2. **The lawsuit:** Give not just what is demanded, but more—your cloak as well as your tunic.
3. **The forced labor:** Go not just the required mile, but two.
4. **The request:** Give freely to those who ask; lend without turning away.

In each case, Jesus demonstrates a counterintuitive love that breaks the cycle of harm.

### B. Love Your Enemies (v.43-47)

> "But I tell you: Love your enemies and pray for those who persecute you, that you may be sons of your Father in heaven. He causes his sun to rise on the evil and the good, and sends rain on the righteous and the unrighteous. If you love those who love you, what reward will you get? Are not even the tax collectors doing that? And if you greet only your brothers, what are you doing more than others? Do not even pagans do that?"

Jesus presents two arguments for why we must love our enemies:

**First: Because you are becoming like your Father.** To love your enemies is to become functional sons and daughters of God. The Father himself loves unconditionally—his sun rises on the evil and the good alike; his rain falls on the righteous and unrighteous. This is not sentimental preference but active, indiscriminate goodwill.

**Second: Because loving only those who love you is merely self-interest.** Tax collectors do this. Pagans do this. The key question is: "What are you doing more than others?" This echoes the earlier teaching about salt and light—we are called not to the minimum, but to the maximum expression of God's character. Our calling is to transcend natural human reciprocity and embrace supernatural love.

## 3. A Necessary Detour: How Do We Apply These Laws?

Before we can apply Jesus' teaching, we must answer a difficult question: How do Old Testament laws and the Sermon on the Mount apply to us today?

### The Problem

We would like to say the Sermon applies to us directly and literally. But there's a problem: following it seriously is unrealistic.

- If we turn the other cheek in the face of abuse, we enable the abuser.
- If we give our EFTPOS card and PIN to anyone who asks, we invite financial ruin.
- If we physically chop off a body part every time we sin, we would all be dead within a week.

Following these commands literally is impossible.

### The Interpretive Dilemma

Some suggest these are hyperbolic—exaggerations for effect. But then how do we know which teachings are hyperbolic and which are literal? Do I decide based on what I can do? "This one I can follow, so it's literal. That one is too hard, so it must be hyperbole." This way of reading collapses into arbitrary pick-and-choose interpretation.

### Three Failed Approaches

**Approach 1: Direct Application Today**  
This fails because literal obedience is impossible, and we have no principled way to distinguish which commands are hyperbolic.

**Approach 2: The Sermon Drives Us to Grace**  
Some argue the Sermon is intentionally impossible—its purpose is to show us we cannot save ourselves, driving us to God's grace. If this is true, the Sermon becomes irrelevant for daily living. We can try to follow it, but it's an exercise in futility. This dismisses the teaching as merely a tool for conversion, not transformation.

**Approach 3: Temporary Instructions**  
Others argue the Sermon applied only to that peculiar moment when Jesus thought the end of the world was imminent. When the end is near, radical living makes sense—no need to save money, plan for retirement, or accumulate possessions. Jesus and his disciples lived off the generosity of others, with no thought for tomorrow.

This is like HSC students cramming for final exams: all-nighters, no TV, unlimited coffee, no relationships. These practices are appropriate for that intense period but unsustainable for life. Similarly, the Sermon might work for three years of ministry, but not for a lifetime.

The problem with both approaches is that the Sermon has a ring of truth. We sense intuitively that it points to a genuinely Christian ethic—that mercy, generosity, and love are not impossible ideals but beautiful ways to live. To dismiss them as irrelevant feels wrong.

### The Answer: Fulfillment in Christ

I believe the answer lies in understanding that Jesus fulfills the Law. As Jesus says in verse 17:

> "Do not think that I have come to abolish the Law or the Prophets; I have not come to abolish them but to fulfill them."

The six reinterpretations that follow ("You have heard... but I tell you") are not new laws for us. Rather, Jesus is describing himself. He is the one who perfectly embodies what it means to live out these commands.

**Jesus did not resist the evil person.** When brought before Pilate and the Jewish mob, he could have called twelve legions of angels (Matthew 26). He went willingly to his death.

**When struck on the cheek:** The high priests and Sanhedrin spit in his face and struck him with their fists. Jesus did not retaliate (Matthew 26:67). When flogged by Roman guards, he made no threats (Matthew 27:26). They spit on him and struck him on the head again and again (Matthew 27:30).

**When they took his garments:** His clothes were divided among the soldiers by casting lots (Matthew 27:35).

**When forced to go one mile:** He walked to Golgotha, carrying his cross to his own execution.

**When it came to loving enemies:** Jesus spent his ministry among the enemies of God—tax collectors, prostitutes, and sinners. He loved not just those who loved him back, but those who hated and rejected him. He did more than others. He did what only God could do.

Jesus is the fulfillment of this recalibrated Law. In him, the law's true intention is revealed and completed.

### The Law Becomes Wisdom for Us

If Christ has fulfilled the Law, what are we to do with it? I believe the Old Testament Law now functions as wisdom for us.

> "The entire Law is summed up in a single command: 'Love your neighbor as yourself.'" (Galatians 5:14)

The Law is not a binding legal code, but a treasury of wisdom—instruction and principle from the past that guides us in understanding God's will and character. The binding principle beneath all the Law is love.

This is not pick-and-choose interpretation in a vicious sense. Rather, it is wisdom-guided interpretation, where we apply the *principle* of love to various situations, not the *letter* of the law.

Why is this necessary? Because narrow legalism doesn't work:

1. **It doesn't achieve justice.** If someone steals from me, does stealing from them help? Does raping a rapist undo the original rape? Does killing the killer restore life? Tit-for-tat vengeance only perpetuates harm.

2. **It's immeasurable.** How do you measure exact equivalence? If someone killed someone I love, should I kill someone they love? But then they kill someone I love in return. The cycle continues endlessly.

3. **It ignores context.** Physical property can be replaced; a life cannot. A moment of infidelity cannot be undone by identical infidelity.

4. **It misses God's justice.** Our justice is not God's justice. God's justice includes forgiveness. When we forgive, we participate in God's nature—not out of weakness, but out of strength.

Jesus reinterprets the Old Testament Law back to its pristine state, to its original intention: justice tempered with love, mercy, and the recognition that only God can ultimately settle accounts. He fulfills it completely in himself. Now he calls us not to rigid legalism, but to wise, love-guided living.

## 4. Love Your Enemy: Applying Wisdom

The instruction for us today is: **Love your enemy.**

Because the Sermon is wisdom rather than law, we have flexibility to apply its imperatives according to the principle of love, not vengeance or payback. Mercy, not retaliation, is our guide.

### Understanding "Do Not Resist"

The command not to resist an evil person does not mean we must be pacifists or incapable of defending others. Rather, it means that in some situations, out of love, we must actively resist evil. In other situations, out of love, we must absorb the harm.

- Sometimes self-sacrifice is called for. Sometimes it is right to offer the other cheek to break a deadlock—someone must give way first.
- But sometimes this would be wrong. If someone is abusing you or a child in your care, love demands that you resist and protect.

The principle is always love, never vengeance.

### Practical Wisdom

**On giving:** A beggar asks for food. Love might mean giving a Big Mac. But wisdom asks: What is truly loving here? Sometimes, love means giving freely. Sometimes, love means not enabling destructive habits.

**On enforcement of rights:** Sometimes, love means enforcing boundaries. If someone is stealing, it doesn't help to ignore it. Love sometimes requires saying "no" and stopping the harm.

- But sometimes, love means letting small things go. Not tying up police over trivial matters. Not pursuing every debt.

The key is discernment guided by love, not arbitrary rules or rationalization.

### A Personal Example

I don't have many enemies—I'm still young. But I have one person who betrayed me and continues to gossip behind my back. My attempts at reconciliation have failed. I suffer consequences and backhanded comments from him.

What do I do? Wisdom suggests this: I try to love him and look for his interests. But I'm not foolish enough to trust him or consider him a close friend. Given his history of acting according to his own agenda rather than anyone else's welfare, I must be cautious. I don't share private information I know will be gossipped about. That would not be wise, and it wouldn't be loving to him or to others who might be harmed by the gossip.

Yet wisdom also leaves room for hope. Perhaps in the future, he can be trusted again. But for now, love and wisdom coincide in cautious restraint.

### The Danger: Rationalizing Away Love

Here is the danger: We can rationalize our way out of loving our enemies under the guise of "wisdom." We can use prudence as an excuse for bitterness, caution as a cover for unforgiveness.

I think we need to be encouraged—and to encourage others—to keep striving toward love, even when it's difficult. The goal is not perfection overnight, but a trajectory toward the heart of God.

## 5. The Ultimate Foundation: Christ's Forgiveness

Why can we love our enemies? Because Christ has loved us, his enemies.

> "Slaves, submit yourselves to your masters with all respect, not only to those who are good and considerate, but also to those who are harsh. For it is commendable if a man bears up under the pain of unjust suffering because he is conscious of God. But if you suffer for doing good and you endure it, this is commendable before God. To this you were called, because Christ suffered for you, leaving you an example, that you should follow in his steps. He committed no sin, and no deceit was found in his mouth. When they hurled their insults at him, he did not retaliate; when he suffered, he made no threats. Instead, he entrusted himself to him who judges justly. He himself bore our sins in his body on the tree, so that we might die to sins and live for righteousness; by his wounds you have been healed." (1 Peter 2:18-24)

Jesus is the one who, in the end, paid for sin. He paid the price of justice:

- His eye for my eye
- His tooth for my tooth
- His body for my body
- His blood for my blood

As a result of his substitutionary sacrifice, we are now free to love our enemies in wisdom. We are no longer bound to vengeance because Jesus has already settled the account.

## Kathy's Journey: From Bitterness to Forgiveness

The book *Forgiving Hitler* recounts Kathy Diosy's escape from the Nazis and, more importantly, her escape from bitterness. Through years of struggle—witnessing the executions, touching the boots of the hanged collaborators, carrying the weight of her father's death—she carried unresolved rage.

But in the end, it was the power of Christ's forgiveness that liberated her. She discovered that forgiveness is not about excusing evil or pretending injustice didn't happen. Rather, it is about:

- **Restoring relationships:** Forgiveness mends what was broken.
- **Healing the injured party:** It removes the rage and bitterness that color all of life, the sense of outraged injustice, the disappointment that poisons everything.
- **Releasing the burden of judgment:** Forgiveness frees us from the delusion that we are the perfectly innocent sufferer with the right to judge all others.

Forgiveness is profoundly difficult when the injury is severe. It feels like becoming a victim twice over. But it is also one of the most powerful expressions of God's character available to us.

## 6. The Ultimate Challenge: Be Perfect as Your Father

> "Be perfect, therefore, as your heavenly Father is perfect." (Matthew 5:48)

Jesus calls his disciples—the new Israel—to a radical standard: reflect the perfection of God himself.

This is not legalism; it is maximalism. Not the minimum, but the maximum expression of God's character. We follow not by the letter of the law, but by the spirit. We follow by becoming increasingly like the One who loved his enemies to the point of death.

As the end of Matthew 7 will remind us, we face a choice: to be like the wise builder who hears these words and acts on them, or like the foolish builder who hears them and does nothing. The teaching of Jesus is not optional; it is foundational.

We are called to love our enemies—not because it feels natural, not because it's easy, but because it reveals the heart of God and transforms us into his image.
