# Luke 23:26-49

**Title:** The Death of a King
**Date:** 1 April 2012
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## A Word on the Text

Before we dive into this passage, a brief note on textual criticism. The earliest manuscripts (P75, Vaticanus from the 4th century) actually omit verse 34a—"And Jesus said, 'Father, forgive them, because they do not know what they are doing.'" Internally, scholars believe this phrase was likely added later to further highlight Jesus's compassion. However, it's found in later manuscripts and has been retained in many modern translations like the NIV.

This reminds us of something precious: we hold in our hands a Bible with a long history of divine preservation. We must never grow lazy about bringing our Bibles and listening carefully to what God has to say.

## The Death of a King

Just before this sermon, the King of Tonga died. King George Tupou V—head of state for the only monarchy in the South Pacific—passed away in Hong Kong, far from his kingdom. His funeral was extraordinary: a thousand pallbearers carried his coffin to the royal catacombs in Nuku'alofa, with schoolchildren lining the streets and a gold bier leading the procession.

But today we read of another king's death. And His death is unlike any other.

> "An inscription was above Him: THIS IS THE KING OF THE JEWS." (Luke 23:38)

How did we arrive here? Over the past weeks, we've been following Jesus through Luke's Gospel. He came as a king on a donkey, just as the prophets foretold. He was betrayed by His own disciples, tried by both Jews and Romans, and now—after a show-trial—He's been condemned to death.

## 1. Led to Death (23:26-27, 32-34)

> "As they led Him away, they seized Simon, a Cyrenian, who was coming in from the country, and laid the cross on him to carry behind Jesus. A large crowd of people followed Him, including women who were mourning and lamenting Him." (23:26-27)

The authorities led Jesus away with Roman efficiency. On the way, they seized a man named Simon, a Cyrenian from what is modern-day Libya. Interestingly, Simon wasn't a resident of Jerusalem—he was just arriving from the country, perhaps in town for Passover. We'll discover that his story becomes an important detail.

A large crowd followed Jesus to His execution. And He wasn't alone in His fate:

> "Two other criminals were also led away to be executed with Him. When they arrived at the place called The Skull, they crucified Him there, along with the criminals, one on the right and one on the left." (23:32-33)

Golgotha—the Skull, also called Calvary in the Latin—was a place of public execution. The name likely refers to abandoned skulls found there, or to its proximity to a cemetery. It was a place of absolute horror.

Crucifixion was a Roman execution method designed for maximum suffering and public humiliation. The prisoner was first stripped naked, then laid on his back. His hands were nailed or roped to the horizontal beam, his feet to the vertical pole. The cross was hoisted upright and dropped into a socket dug into the ground. A peg or rudimentary seat provided some weight support, but the condemned would hang there helplessly—exposed to intense physical pain, public ridicule, the burning daytime heat, and the freezing night cold. The torture could last for days.

When we watch depictions like Mel Gibson's *The Passion*, we're struck by the graphic detail—the blood, the suffering, the agony. But here's what's remarkable: the Gospel writers don't focus on these details at all. They don't describe the nails, the blood, or the physical torment in vivid terms. Their attention lies elsewhere—and that's what we need to understand.

## 2. A Conversation on Calvary

Three men hang on crosses at Golgotha. Let's listen to their conversation.

### The First Criminal's Mockery

> "Then one of the criminals hanging there began to yell insults at Him: 'Aren't You the Messiah? Save Yourself and us!'" (23:39)

He wasn't alone in this taunting:

> "The people stood watching, and even the leaders kept scoffing: 'He saved others; let Him save Himself if this is God's Messiah, the Chosen One!' The soldiers also mocked Him. They came offering Him sour wine and said, 'If You are the King of the Jews, save Yourself!'" (23:35-37)

Notice the formula: "If You are the King... save Yourself." This echoes the temptations Jesus faced in the wilderness:

> "So he took Him to Jerusalem, had Him stand on the pinnacle of the temple, and said to Him, 'If You are the Son of God, throw Yourself down from here.'" (Luke 4:9-12)

The temptation was real. Jesus could have saved Himself. Angels would have protected Him. As King, He had all authority and splendor at His disposal. He could have descended from that cross, and everyone would have believed. Isn't that what we often ask for—dramatic proof?

But why didn't He?

**The problem is not belief in God. The problem is sin, and specifically the forgiveness of sin.**

### The Second Criminal's Repentance

> "But the other answered, rebuking him: 'Don't you even fear God, since you are undergoing the same punishment? We are punished justly, because we're getting back what we deserve for the things we did, but this man has done nothing wrong.'" (23:40-41)

This criminal understood something crucial: judgment is coming, and they deserve it.

Before this moment, Jesus addressed the women following Him:

> "'Daughters of Jerusalem, do not weep for Me, but weep for yourselves and your children. Look, the days are coming when they will say, "The women without children, the wombs that never bore and the breasts that never nursed, are fortunate!" Then they will begin to say to the mountains, "Fall on us!" and to the hills, "Cover us!"'" (23:28-30)

Don't misunderstand this. This isn't a modern statement about delaying children for career or comfort—it's far more serious. Jesus is saying children are a blessing from God (Psalm 127:3), but when judgment comes, that blessing becomes unbearable agony. When judgment arrives, people will cry out for death itself to escape the wrath.

This echoes the prophet Hosea and John's Revelation:

> "'They will say to the mountains, "Cover us!" and to the hills, "Fall on us!"'" (Hosea 10:8; Revelation 6:16)

The criminals face this terrible reality: they are being punished justly for their sins. And here's the puzzle—Jesus is undergoing the same judgment, yet He deserves none of it.

### The Paradox of the Cross

The criminal's plea reveals the dilemma: "Save yourself and us." But the truth is more costly—it's not "save yourself AND us," it's "save yourself OR us." Jesus dies so that others don't have to.

We think of the cross in terms of its physical suffering, but that misses the point. The cross isn't about how much blood or pain there was—it's that there *was* blood and pain. More importantly, it's about curse. Deuteronomy tells us:

> "'If anyone is found guilty of an offense deserving the death penalty and is executed, and you hang his body on a tree, you are not to leave his corpse on the tree overnight but are to bury him that day, for anyone hung on a tree is under God's curse.'" (Deuteronomy 21:22-23)

We often think the cross is merely an example of sacrifice—and it is that. But if that's all it was, we'd honor it the way we honor soldiers who died for their country. No. It's far more than that.

**The cross is love that pays the debt of sin so that we can be forgiven.**

The penalty for sin must be paid. It cannot be paid by ANZAC soldiers or by human self-sacrifice or even by temple sacrifices. Only one thing suffices: an innocent man—one sinless from eternity. God Himself must pay this price.

### Repentance and Redemption

And so, when Jesus has done this work, the second criminal's words become effective:

> "'Jesus, remember me when You come into Your kingdom!' And He said to him, 'I assure you: Today you will be with Me in paradise.'" (23:42-43)

The criminal asks for remembrance—not because he's done good works or earned favor, but because he recognizes his guilt and calls on Jesus for help. This is true repentance. This is what God desires: not sympathy or external works, but the acknowledgment of our own guilt and a cry for His mercy.

Paradise here means the garden—a return to God's presence. And the criminal's salvation is immediate and complete.

**This is what it means to truly fear God: to realize the calamity we face and then to act appropriately.** The fear of God is not neurotic or unhealthy. Just as we naturally fear bright colors warning us of danger, or flames, or the edge of a cliff, the fear of God is a natural and healthy response to our condition. It moves us to repent.

## 3. The Last Breath

> "It was now about noon, and darkness came over the whole land until three, because the sun's light failed. The curtain of the sanctuary was split down the middle. And Jesus called out with a loud voice, 'Father, into Your hands I entrust My spirit.' Saying this, He breathed His last." (23:44-46)

Light failed. Darkness came at midday. The veil that separated sinners from God's holy presence was torn in two—no longer a barrier, but an open way to the Father.

> "When the centurion saw what happened, he began to glorify God, saying, 'This man really was righteous!' All the crowds that had gathered for this spectacle, when they saw what had taken place, went home, striking their chests. But all who knew Him, including the women who had followed Him from Galilee, stood at a distance, watching these things." (23:47-49)

Even the centurion recognized the truth. The crowds departed in conviction. But the women who had followed Jesus from the beginning stood watching, making up their minds about who Jesus truly is.

## A Question for Us

We leave with one final question: What is Simon of Cyrene doing in this story?

He points us to discipleship. And discipleship is an either-or choice, not a both-and compromise. We cannot follow both the world and Jesus. Luke 9 makes this clear: we must give up our lives to follow Him. Simon Cyrene reminds us that another Simon—Peter—would betray Jesus, but this Simon shows us an alternative path.

**The question for each of us is this: Will we take up our cross and follow Jesus, or will we walk away?**

# Luke 23:50-24:12

**Title:** The King Resurrected
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Burial (23:50-56)

> "There was a good and righteous man named Joseph, a member of the Sanhedrin, who had not agreed with their plan and action. He was from Arimathea, a Judean town, and was looking forward to the kingdom of God. He approached Pilate and asked for Jesus's body. Taking it down, he wrapped it in fine linen and placed it in a tomb cut into the rock, where no one had ever been placed. It was preparation day, and the Sabbath was about to begin. The women who had come with Him from Galilee followed along and observed the tomb and how His body was placed. Then they returned and prepared spices and perfumes. And they rested on the Sabbath according to the commandment." (23:50-56)

Joseph of Arimathea, a member of the Sanhedrin who had dissented from the council's condemnation of Jesus, takes an extraordinary risk. He asks Pilate for Jesus's body and buries it in his own tomb—a place reserved for the honored dead. The women watch, taking note of everything.

## The Resurrection Morning (24:1-8)

> "On the first day of the week, very early in the morning, they came to the tomb, bringing the spices they had prepared. They found the stone rolled away from the tomb. They went in but did not find the body of the Lord Jesus. While they were perplexed about this, suddenly two men stood by them in dazzling clothes. So the women were terrified and bowed down to the ground. 'Why are you looking for the living among the dead?' asked the men. 'He is not here, but He has been resurrected! Remember how He spoke to you when He was still in Galilee, saying, "The Son of Man must be betrayed into the hands of sinful men, be crucified, and rise on the third day?"' And they remembered His words." (24:1-8)

The stone is rolled away. The tomb is empty. But more than that—the women encounter angels who ask a penetrating question: "Why are you looking for the living among the dead?" Jesus had told them this would happen. They just needed to remember.

## The Witness (24:9-12)

> "Returning from the tomb, they reported all these things to the Eleven and to all the rest. Mary Magdalene, Joanna, Mary the mother of James, and the other women with them were telling the apostles these things. But these words seemed like nonsense to them, and they did not believe the women. Peter, however, got up and ran to the tomb. When he stooped to look in, he saw only the linen cloths. So he went home, amazed at what had happened." (24:9-12)

The women become the first witnesses to the resurrection—yet their testimony is received with skepticism. Only Peter goes to verify, and even he goes home in amazement, struggling to comprehend what has occurred.

The resurrection is not something easily believed. It demands a transformation of understanding, a willingness to let the dead truly be dead and the risen truly be alive.

# Luke 24:13-36

**Title:** The King Teaches—The Road to Emmaus
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Journey (24:13-18)

> "Now that same day two of them were on their way to a village called Emmaus, which was about seven miles from Jerusalem. Together they were discussing everything that had taken place. And while they were discussing and arguing, Jesus Himself came near and began to walk along with them. But they were prevented from recognizing Him. Then He asked them, 'What is this dispute that you're having with each other as you are walking?' And they stopped walking and looked discouraged. The one named Cleopas answered Him, 'Are You the only visitor in Jerusalem who doesn't know the things that happened there in these days?'" (24:13-18)

Two disciples walk away from Jerusalem, their hearts heavy with disappointment. They had hoped Jesus was the one to redeem Israel, but now He was dead. As they walk, Jesus joins them—yet something prevents them from recognizing Him.

## The Explanation (24:19-27)

> "'What things?' He asked them. So they said to Him, 'The things concerning Jesus the Nazarene, who was a Prophet powerful in action and speech before God and all the people, and how our chief priests and leaders handed Him over to be sentenced to death, and they crucified Him. But we were hoping that He was the One who was about to redeem Israel. Besides all this, it's the third day since these things happened. Moreover, some women from our group astounded us. They arrived early at the tomb, and when they didn't find His body, they came and reported that they had seen a vision of angels who said He was alive. Some of those who were with us went to the tomb and found it just as the women had said, but they didn't see Him.'" (24:19-24)

They pour out their disappointment and confusion. They'd seen the crucifixion. They'd heard the women's testimony about the angels. But they hadn't seen Jesus themselves.

Jesus responds with a gentle rebuke:

> "'How unwise and slow you are to believe in your hearts all that the prophets have spoken! Didn't the Messiah have to suffer these things and enter into His glory?' Then beginning with Moses and all the Prophets, He interpreted for them the things concerning Himself in all the Scriptures." (24:25-27)

Jesus doesn't scold them for not understanding. Instead, He opens the Scriptures, showing how everything—from Moses through the Prophets—pointed to a Messiah who would suffer before entering glory. The cross wasn't a tragedy that contradicted the plan; it *was* the plan.

## The Recognition (24:28-35)

> "They came near the village where they were going, and He gave the impression that He was going farther. But they urged Him: 'Stay with us, because it's almost evening, and now the day is almost over.' So He went in to stay with them. It was as He reclined at the table with them that He took the bread, blessed and broke it, and gave it to them. Then their eyes were opened, and they recognized Him, but He disappeared from their sight." (24:28-31)

In the familiar act of breaking bread—the gesture of a rabbi or host—their eyes are suddenly opened. They recognize Jesus. And in that moment, He vanishes.

The recognition comes not through explanation, but through encounter. And immediately they understand:

> "'Weren't our hearts ablaze within us while He was talking with us on the road and explaining the Scriptures to us?' That very hour they got up and returned to Jerusalem. They found the Eleven and those with them gathered together, who said, 'The Lord has certainly been raised, and has appeared to Simon!' Then they began to describe what had happened on the road and how He was made known to them in the breaking of the bread." (24:32-35)

Their hearts had been burning within them as Jesus taught. Now they rush back to Jerusalem to share the news: Jesus is alive. The resurrection is real.

## The Final Appearance (24:36-43)

> "And as they were saying these things, He Himself stood among them. He said to them, 'Peace to you!' But they were startled and terrified and thought they were seeing a ghost. 'Why are you troubled?' He asked them. 'And why do doubts arise in your hearts? Look at My hands and My feet, that it is I Myself! Touch Me and see, because a ghost does not have flesh and bones as you can see I have.' Having said this, He showed them His hands and feet. But while they still were amazed and unbelieving because of their joy, He asked them, 'Do you have anything here to eat?' So they gave Him a piece of a broiled fish, and He took it and ate in their presence." (24:36-43)

Jesus appears suddenly in their midst. The disciples are terrified—is this a ghost? But Jesus proves His resurrection is bodily and real. He invites them to touch Him. He eats fish before them. The resurrection is not a vision or a spiritual idea—it is the actual, physical return of the crucified Jesus to life.

## The Commission (24:44-49)

> "'Then He told them, "These are My words that I spoke to you while I was still with you—that everything written about Me in the Law of Moses, the Prophets, and the Psalms must be fulfilled." Then He opened their minds to understand the Scriptures. He also said to them, "This is what is written: The Messiah would suffer and rise from the dead the third day, and repentance for forgiveness of sins would be proclaimed in His name to all the nations, beginning at Jerusalem. You are witnesses of these things. And look, I am sending you what My Father promised. As for you, stay in the city until you are empowered from on high."'" (24:44-49)

Jesus opens their minds to understand Scripture. Everything points to Him. And now He gives them their commission: to proclaim repentance and forgiveness of sins to all nations, beginning in Jerusalem. But first, they must wait for the Holy Spirit—the power promised by the Father.

## The Ascension (24:50-53)

> "'Then He led them out as far as Bethany, and lifting up His hands He blessed them. And while He was blessing them, He left them and was carried up into heaven. After worshiping Him, they returned to Jerusalem with great joy. And they were continually in the temple complex praising God.'" (24:50-53)

Jesus blesses them as He ascends. The disciples, though He is taken from them, return to Jerusalem not in despair but in great joy. They have been witnesses to the death and resurrection of the King. They have been commissioned to proclaim this good news to the world. And they have been promised the power to do so.

The King has died. The King has risen. And the King will come again.
