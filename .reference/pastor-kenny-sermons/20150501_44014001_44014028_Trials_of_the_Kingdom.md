## Free Speech and the Gospel

The concept of free speech traces back to ancient Greek civilization. One pivotal step toward freedom of expression was the involvement of ordinary people in religious life—breaking the monopoly of priestly authority over sacred matters.

Around 700 BC, the lay person Hesiod wrote his *Theogony*, a theological work asserting that "the gods and the universe are a matter of private interest." He declared that his relationship with God was his own concern, not something reserved for the established priesthood.

Paul, living at the tail end of Greek civilization, embodied this principle of free speech. Following his encounter with the resurrected Jesus, he traveled from city to city proclaiming the gospel. As we examine Acts 14, we see three distinct responses to his message—responses that remain strikingly relevant to us today as we seek to speak about Jesus.

## 1. The Idolization of Paul (14:8-18)

### The Miracle at Lystra

> 8 In Lystra there sat a man who was **lame**. He had been that way from **birth** and had **never walked.** 9 He listened to Paul as he was speaking. Paul **looked directly** at him, saw that he had faith to be healed 10 and called out, "Stand up on your feet!" At that, the man **jumped** up and began to **walk**.

This healing echoes an earlier miracle in Acts 3, when Peter healed a lame beggar at the temple gate. Both accounts share striking parallels: a man lame from birth, the apostle's direct gaze, the man jumping up and walking. Luke appears to be demonstrating that Paul possesses the same apostolic authority as Peter.

In fact, comparing the first and second halves of Acts reveals a deliberate pattern:

- **Signs and wonders:** Peter performs them among the Jews in Jerusalem (2:43); Paul and Barnabas perform them among the Gentiles in Asia Minor (14:3)
- **The lame healed:** Peter heals a man lame from his mother's womb at the temple gate (3:1); Paul heals a man lame from birth at Lystra (14:7)
- **Divine deliverance:** Peter is freed from prison by an angel in Jerusalem (12:5); Paul's chains are loosed in the Philippi prison (16:25)

Luke is signaling that Paul carries the same divine authority as Peter—Paul to the Gentiles, Peter to the Jews and Samaritans. Their miracles point not to themselves but to Jesus, whose work of redemption was completed at the cross, though the proclamation of that redemption continues throughout Acts.

### The Dangerous Response: Idolatry

However, the crowd's reaction becomes dangerously excessive:

> 11 When the crowd saw what Paul had done, they shouted in the Lycaonian language, "The **gods have come down to us in human form**!" 12 Barnabas they called Zeus, and Paul they called Hermes because he was the chief speaker.

Zeus, the chief god of Greek mythology, and Hermes, the messenger god—the crowd believed divine beings had visited their city, likely because a temple of Zeus stood nearby.

> 13 The priest of Zeus, whose temple was just outside the city, brought bulls and wreaths to the city gates because he and the crowd wanted to **offer sacrifices** to them.

A Greek sacrifice was a solemn affair: the priest would lead the live animal in procession with flute music and garlands of leaves. After preliminary rituals with barley grains, the priest would slaughter the animal, burn inedible organs and bones for the god, and distribute the flesh as a meal for the celebrants.

### Paul's Urgent Rejection

But Paul and Barnabas reject this idolatry immediately:

> 14 But when the apostles Barnabas and Paul heard of this, they tore their clothes and rushed out into the crowd, shouting: 15 "Friends, why are you doing this? We too are only human, like you.

Tearing their clothes signified distress and ordinary humanity—they ran into the crowd to demonstrate they were not gods but mortal men.

> 15 We are **bringing you good news**, telling you to turn from these worthless things to the living God, who made the heavens and the earth and the sea and everything in them. 16 In the past, he let all nations go their own way. 17 Yet he has not left himself without testimony: He has shown kindness by giving you rain from heaven and crops in their seasons; he provides you with plenty of food and fills your hearts with joy.

Their intention in repeating the miracle—healing the lame, just as Jesus did—was to point people toward the living God, the Creator who provides rain, crops, food, and joy. The miracles demonstrated God's power and character, not the apostles' divinity.

> 18 Even with these words, they had difficulty keeping the crowd from sacrificing to them.

### Application: We Idolize People

We face a similar temptation today. We sometimes elevate Christian speakers and teachers to an unhealthy status. We hear gifted preachers from overseas and can unconsciously form personality cults around them. This is dangerous because we don't truly know them—we haven't seen their private lives, met their families, or experienced their friendships. We're following a persona, not a person.

When such figures inevitably disappoint us (and they will, because they're human), we risk blaming Jesus and the gospel itself for their failures. No one has asked us to follow blindly. Good speakers should remind us to remember Jesus, not exalt the messenger.

We must turn to the true and living God and stay away from idols.

### We Idolize Life Itself

There's another form of idolatry at work in our culture: we worship life itself, treating its preservation as the ultimate good. We fear death and the death of loved ones, yet as Christians, we should not fear death itself.

C.S. Lewis noted that we are all sentenced to death. The question is not whether we will die, but how we choose to live. Would we rather die isolated in our homes, or die on our feet serving others? Would we rather preserve life at any cost, or live purposefully for something greater than ourselves?

The people of Lystra didn't understand that God had already come down in human form—not in Paul or Barnabas, but in Jesus. God became flesh. Jesus gave his life as a sacrifice for our sins. That same God who provides rain and crops provided Jesus for our redemption.

We have another life to live for—the resurrection life Christ offers.

## 2. The Hatred Toward Paul (1-7, 19-20)

### Initial Success at Iconium

> 1 At **Iconium** Paul and Barnabas went as usual into the **Jewish synagogue.** There they spoke so effectively that a great number of Jews and **Greeks** believed.

Paul and Barnabas followed their standard practice: visit the Jewish synagogue first for teaching and hospitality. Their message was powerful, and both Jews and Gentiles believed.

> 3 So Paul and Barnabas spent considerable time there, speaking **boldly** for the Lord, who confirmed the message of his grace by enabling them to perform signs and wonders. 4 The people of the city were **divided**; some sided with the Jews, others with the **apostles**.

But opposition quickly arose:

> 2 But the Jews who **refused** to believe stirred up the other Gentiles and **poisoned** their minds against the brothers.

Those who rejected the message actively worked to turn others against Paul and Barnabas, "poisoning" minds against them—the same Greek word used for oppression in Acts 7:19 and the persecution of the church in Acts 12:1.

### Escalation to Violence

> 5 There was a **plot** afoot among both Gentiles and Jews, together with their leaders, to **mistreat** them and **stone** them. 6 But they found out about it and fled to the Lycaonian cities of Lystra and Derbe and to the surrounding country, 7 where they continued to preach the gospel.

The plot materialized at Lystra:

> 19 Then some Jews came from Antioch and Iconium and won the crowd over. They **stoned Paul and dragged him outside the city, thinking he was dead**. 20 But after the disciples had gathered around him, he got up and went back into the city.

They stoned him and dragged his body through the streets with such violence that people believed he was dead. Yet the very next day, he and Barnabas continued to Derbe.

### Why Such Hatred?

Paul and Barnabas came peacefully, speaking the gospel. They performed no acts of coercion or intimidation. Why such violent rejection?

Because the world does not want to hear the truth. When truth threatens established systems and comfortable lies, the world moves to silence it—first through intimidation, then through violence.

This pattern repeats in our own time. The world doesn't simply disagree with Christian teaching; it seeks to suppress it entirely. First come ridicule and social pressure. Then come threats, boycotts, and legal persecution. Finally, in some places and times, comes violence.

The same progression appears in contemporary culture wars: ridicule and ad hominem attacks; shouting down opposing voices (claiming it as an exercise of "free speech" to silence others' free speech); legal action against those who hold traditional beliefs; and ultimately, the threat of violence against dissenters.

### Free Speech Under Threat

When minority viewpoints are silenced, we've lost something essential. The freedom to speak truth—especially unpopular truth—is the foundation of a healthy society.

Yet many Christians have become self-censoring without realizing it. We've been pummeled by media and popular culture for so long that we censor ourselves before anyone else does. How many of us hesitate to tell strangers we're Christians? How many dumb down what we did at church on Sunday? How many qualify our beliefs with disclaimers and apologies?

We should be relieved when pressure eases temporarily—yet we shouldn't forget that the pressure existed in the first place. It's like being grateful to a bully for stopping his assault instead of asking why he attacked us.

The truth is: we must speak now, while we still can. Staying silent won't make things easier for Christians. The world won't gradually accept our views if we remain quiet. We must proclaim the gospel boldly before we're completely silenced.

## 3. They Believed Paul (21-28)

### The Fruit of Faithfulness

> 21 They preached the gospel in that city and won a large number of disciples.

Despite opposition, the gospel bore fruit. People responded and became followers of Jesus.

> 22 [Paul and Barnabas] strengthening the disciples and encouraging them to remain true to the faith. **"We must go through many hardships to enter the kingdom of God,"** they said.

Paul and Barnabas returned to strengthen the new disciples, preparing them for the trials ahead. Following Jesus isn't a path of ease; it requires perseverance through hardship. But these hardships are the "trials of the kingdom"—not reasons for despair, but evidence of kingdom life.

> 23 Paul and Barnabas appointed **elders** for them in each church and, with prayer and fasting, committed them to the Lord, in whom they had put their trust.

They established leadership structures and entrusted the new churches to God's care through prayer and fasting.

### Why We Speak

We speak not for wealth, power, or worldly gain, but to call disciples for Jesus. We speak to proclaim the good news that the kingdom of God is here, that we can enter it freely because Christ has paid for our sins. The pain of this earth pales beside the surpassing glory of knowing Jesus.

The privilege of speaking the gospel isn't restricted to a priestly caste anymore. Through Christ, all of us are worthy and equipped to tell the world how he has impacted our lives. Go and tell your parents, siblings, friends, neighbors, colleagues, students, and acquaintances. Speak so that all might know the surpassing love of Christ.

### Don't Despair

Notice Paul's response to being stoned: he got up the next day and continued his work. This wasn't because he wasn't hurt, but because he had something more important than self-pity to do.

We must follow his example. Speak while you can. Speak boldly. Don't despair at opposition. The gospel's power doesn't depend on cultural acceptance or legal protection—it depends on the living God.

## The End of the First Missionary Journey

> 24 After going through Pisidia, they came into Pamphylia, 25 and when they had preached the word in Perga, they went down to Attalia. 26 From Attalia they sailed back to Antioch, where they had been committed to the grace of God for the work they had now completed.

Paul and Barnabas returned to Antioch, having completed their first missionary journey. A conflict awaited them upon their return—one that would reshape the early church's understanding of the gospel's scope. Read Acts 15 in preparation for next week.
