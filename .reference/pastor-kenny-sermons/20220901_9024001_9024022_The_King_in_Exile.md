# 1 Samuel 24:1-22

**Title:** The King in Exile
**Date:** 3 September 2022
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction: Recognizing Positions

There are positions and statuses in life that deserve honor—Mr., Mrs., Uncle, Aunty, Sir, Boss, Judge. These titles carry weight. Even after years apart, when roles have shifted and expectations need reorganizing, we must acknowledge the positions people hold. This tension between our past familiarity and someone's current standing shapes how we relate to them.

We continue in the latter part of 1 Samuel, where Saul, threatened and angry at David, hunts him through the wilderness. Now Saul receives word of David's location.

## Acknowledging the Anointed

### The Wilderness Encounter (1 Samuel 24:1-7)

> 24:1 After Saul returned from pursuing the Philistines, he was told, "David is in the Desert of En Gedi." 2 So Saul took three thousand able young men from all Israel and set out to look for David and his men near the Crags of the Wild Goats.

Saul mobilizes his entire elite force—three thousand men—the same number he once commanded as king. David and his men hide in caves among the rocky terrain.

> 3 He came to the sheep pens along the way; a cave was there, and Saul went in to relieve himself. David and his men were far back in the cave.

In a moment of vulnerability, Saul enters the very cave where David hides. David's men see their opportunity—with their enemy defenseless before them, they urge David to strike.

> 4 The men said, "This is the day the Lord spoke of when he said to you, 'I will give your enemy into your hands for you to deal with as you wish.'"

Yet David's heart is "cut" with conviction. Instead of killing Saul, he cuts only a corner of his robe.

> 5 Afterward, David was conscience-stricken for having cut off a corner of his robe. 6 He said to his men, "The Lord forbid that I should do such a thing to my master, the Lord's anointed, or lay my hand on him; for he is the anointed of the Lord." 7 With these words David sharply rebuked his men and did not allow them to attack Saul. And Saul left the cave and went his way.

### David's Core Conviction

David refuses to kill Saul for one fundamental reason: **Saul is the Lord's anointed**. This isn't merely about respecting an office—it's about recognizing someone set apart by God. The Hebrew concept of anointing carried sacred weight; the anointed one belonged to God, not to David's judgment.

This principle echoes in chapter 26, where David's men again urge him to kill the sleeping king:

> 26:8-9 Abishai said to David, "Today God has delivered your enemy into your hands. Now let me pin him to the ground with one thrust of the spear; I won't strike him twice." But David said to Abishai, "Don't destroy him! Who can lay a hand on the Lord's anointed and be guiltless?"

Notice David's repeated refrain across both accounts:

- "Who can lay a hand on the Lord's anointed and be guiltless?" (26:9)
- "The Lord forbid that I should do such a thing to my master, the Lord's anointed" (24:6)
- He is "the anointed of the Lord" (24:6)

### The Paradox: An Anointed King Losing God's Spirit

What makes this remarkable is the spiritual reality David faced: God's Spirit had already departed from Saul (1 Samuel 16:14). David himself had been anointed as Saul's successor. Yet David still honors Saul as the anointed.

The anointing isn't temporary office—it's a permanent standing. Just as a father remains a father for life, irreplaceable and unchangeable, the anointed one retains that status until God removes him. Saul may have lost God's favor, but not God's anointing.

### David's Humble Stance

When Saul finally exits the cave, David approaches him with profound deference:

> 8 Then David went out of the cave and called out to Saul, "My lord the king!" When Saul looked behind him, David bowed down and prostrated himself with his face to the ground.

David addresses Saul as "my father" (v.11), not mockingly but genuinely recognizing his elder status. He holds up the piece of robe as proof he could have killed Saul but chose mercy instead:

> 11 See, my father, look at this piece of your robe in my hand! I cut off the corner of your robe but did not kill you. See that there is nothing in my hand to indicate that I am guilty of wrongdoing or rebellion. I have not wronged you, but you are hunting me down to take my life.

David's logic is compelling: If he harbored rebellion, he would have killed Saul in that cave. Instead, he spared him.

## Respecting Fathers and Earthly Authority

David's use of "father" toward Saul illuminates a broader principle. A father holds an irreplaceable position—you cannot choose your father, and he remains your father regardless of how good or flawed he is. Scripture acknowledges this:

> Proverbs 23:22 Listen to your father who gave you life, and do not despise your mother when she is old.

Some of us have great fathers; others have difficult relationships with theirs. But the position itself is unchangeable. For some, reaching out today with a simple message—"Happy Father's Day. Thinking of you"—might open a door to reconciliation that time has closed.

Similarly, David refuses to take justice into his own hands against Saul. Despite the circumstances, David waits. He stays in exile, biding his time, allowing God to be the judge:

> 26:10 As surely as the Lord lives, the Lord himself will strike him, or his time will come and he will die, or he will go into battle and perish. But the Lord forbid that I should lay a hand on the Lord's anointed.

In societies like Australia with elections and democratic processes, we can channel our frustrations constructively through political participation. Yet even then, we must remember that authority rests in God's hands ultimately. We wait for His judgment.

### Saul's Momentary Repentance

David's humble appeal works—temporarily.

> 16 When David finished saying this, Saul asked, "Is that your voice, David my son?" And he wept aloud. 17 "You are more righteous than I," he said. "You have treated me well, but I have treated you badly. 18 You have just now told me about the good you did to me; the Lord delivered me into your hands, but you did not kill me.

Saul acknowledges the truth: David is righteous; he has been wrong. He even swears an oath that David will be king and asks David to spare his descendants.

Yet this contrition doesn't last. By chapter 26, Saul hunts David again. His acknowledgment fades, showing the instability of a heart not anchored in God's purposes.

## The Fool Who Didn't Recognize the Anointed

### Nabal's Folly (1 Samuel 25)

In stark contrast to Saul's eventual (if temporary) recognition, we meet Nabal—a man who knew David's name, knew his father Jesse's name, yet refused to honor the anointed.

> 25:2-3 A certain man in Maon, who had property there at Carmel, was very wealthy. He had a thousand goats and three thousand sheep, which he was shearing in Carmel. His name was Nabal and his wife's name was Abigail. She was an intelligent and beautiful woman, but her husband was surly and mean in his dealings.

Nabal was wealthy but cruel—the Hebrew word here connotes harshness and stubbornness. David had protected Nabal's shepherds in the wilderness, and when shearing season came, David sent men with a courteous greeting:

> 25:7-8 "Now I hear that it is sheep-shearing time. When your shepherds were with us, we did not mistreat them, and the whole time they were at Carmel nothing of theirs was missing. Ask your own servants and they will tell you. Therefore be favorable toward my men, since we come at a festive time. Please give your servants and your son David whatever you can find for them."

Nabal's response was contempt:

> 25:10-11 Nabal answered David's servants, "Who is this David? Who is this son of Jesse? Many servants are breaking away from their masters these days. Why should I take my bread and water, and the meat I have slaughtered for my shearers, and give it to men coming from who knows where?"

Nabal knew David's identity. He knew of his father. But he refused to acknowledge the anointed. His refusal cost him everything.

### Abigail's Wisdom

Nabal's wife, Abigail, understood what her husband refused to see. When she learned of David's approach with four hundred armed men, she acted swiftly. She gathered provisions—two hundred loaves of bread, wine, sheep, grain, and figs—and rode to meet David.

> 25:23-24 When Abigail saw David, she quickly got off her donkey and bowed down before David with her face to the ground. She fell at his feet and said: "Pardon your servant, my lord, and let me speak to you; hear what your servant has to say."

She acknowledged what Nabal refused:

> 25:25-28 "Please pay no attention, my lord, to that wicked man Nabal. He is just like his name—his name means Fool, and folly goes with him. And now, my lord, as surely as the Lord your God lives and as you live, since the Lord has kept you from bloodshed and from avenging yourself with your own hands, may your enemies and all who are intent on harming my lord be like Nabal. And let this gift which your servant has brought to my lord, be given to the men who follow you. Please forgive your servant's presumption. The Lord your God will certainly make a lasting dynasty for my lord, **because you fight the Lord's battles**, and no wrongdoing will be found in you as long as you live."

Abigail recognized David as God's anointed warrior. She understood his future as Israel's king. Her quick thinking and humble acknowledgment saved her household from destruction.

> 25:32-35 David said to Abigail, "Praise be to the Lord, the God of Israel, who has sent you today to meet me. May you be blessed for your good judgment and for keeping me from bloodshed this day and from avenging myself with my own hands. Otherwise, as surely as the Lord, the God of Israel, lives, who has kept me from harming you, if you had not come quickly to meet me, not one male belonging to Nabal would have been left alive by daybreak. Then David accepted from her hand what she had brought him and said, "Go home in peace. I have heard your words and granted your request."

The consequences came swiftly:

> 25:37-39 When Abigail went to Nabal, he was in the house holding a banquet like that of a king. He was in high spirits and very drunk. So she told him nothing at all until daybreak. Then in the morning, when Nabal was sober, his wife told him all these things, and his heart failed him and he became like a stone. About ten days later, the Lord struck Nabal and he died. When David heard that Nabal was dead, he said, "Praise be to the Lord, who has upheld my cause against Nabal for treating me with contempt. He has kept his servant from doing wrong and has brought Nabal's wrongdoing down on his own head."

Nabal, the fool who refused to honor the anointed, faced God's judgment. Abigail, who recognized and honored David, became his wife and found favor.

## Recognizing Jesus, the Anointed

The pattern established in David's story finds its ultimate fulfillment in Jesus. Just as David was God's anointed one being rejected, Jesus faced rejection from those who should have recognized Him.

> Luke 4:16-21 He went to Nazareth, where he had been brought up, and on the Sabbath day he went into the synagogue, as was his custom. He stood up to read, and the scroll of the prophet Isaiah was handed to him. Unrolling it, he found the place where it is written: "The Spirit of the Lord is on me, because he has anointed me to proclaim good news to the poor. He has sent me to proclaim freedom for the prisoners and recovery of sight for the blind, to set the oppressed free, to proclaim the year of the Lord's favor." Then he rolled up the scroll, gave it back to the attendant and sat down. The eyes of everyone in the synagogue were fastened on him. He began by saying to them, "Today this scripture is fulfilled in your hearing."

The people heard Jesus's gracious words and were amazed. Yet recognition was shallow:

> 4:22 "Isn't this Joseph's son?" they asked.

They knew his family name. They knew his background. But they did not recognize Him as the anointed Messiah. Their failure to acknowledge Jesus led them to reject Him violently:

> 4:28-29 All the people in the synagogue were furious when they heard this. They got up, drove him out of the town, and took him to the brow of the hill on which the town was built, in order to throw him off the cliff.

What happened when people failed to recognize the anointed in Jesus? Destruction followed—not just for Nabal-like fools, but for Jerusalem itself when it rejected its Messiah.

## Application: Learning to Recognize the Anointed

The struggle to recognize the truly anointed mirrors the awkwardness of high school reunions where status has shifted. When you reunite with old classmates years later, the social dynamics become complex. Someone who was once an ordinary peer is now a politician, a successful lawyer, a person of influence. How do you relate? Can you call them by old nicknames? Can you invite them for McDonald's?

Your wellbeing depends on recognizing the right people and offering them appropriate honor. The worst outcome at a reunion is embarrassment; the best is making a renewed friend because you gave them due deference despite the time apart.

But failing to recognize Jesus, the true anointed one? The stakes are infinitely higher. This is a matter of eternal consequence—not mere social awkwardness but spiritual destruction.

**The call to us is clear:** Learn to recognize God's anointed. Whether in the respect we show authority in our lives, the honor we extend to positions God has established, or most critically, our recognition of Jesus as Lord—acknowledgment of the anointed is not optional. It is fundamental to a life aligned with God's purposes.
