# 2 Corinthians 2:14–3:6

**Title:** The Fragrance of Christ and the Ministry of the Spirit
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Triumphant Fragrance (2:14–17)

> But thanks be to God, who always leads us in triumphal procession in Christ and through us spreads everywhere the fragrance of the knowledge of him. For we are to God the aroma of Christ among those who are being saved and those who are perishing. To the one we are the smell of death; to the other, the fragrance of life. And who is equal to such a task? (2:14–16)

Paul begins by celebrating God's work in our lives. When we walk with Christ, we become His fragrance—a living testimony of His presence. This image is striking: to some, the gospel brings life and hope, while to others it speaks of judgment and death. The same message produces radically different effects depending on how it's received.

This awareness compels Paul to ask a profound question: "Who is equal to such a task?" Ministry is not a casual responsibility. To represent Christ faithfully, to carry His presence into a broken world—this demands humility and dependence on God.

> Unlike so many, we do not peddle the word of God for profit. On the contrary, in Christ we speak before God with sincerity, like men sent from God. (2:17)

Paul distinguishes himself from false teachers who exploit the gospel for financial gain. His commitment is to speak with sincerity, as someone commissioned by God Himself, not as a merchant hawking wares.

## Living Letters, Not Written Documents (3:1–3)

> Are we beginning to commend ourselves again? Or do we need, like some people, letters of recommendation to you or from you? You yourselves are our letter, written on our hearts, known and read by everybody. You show that you are a letter from Christ, the result of our ministry, written not with ink but with the Spirit of the living God, not on tablets of stone but on tablets of human hearts. (3:1–3)

Rather than defending his apostleship with formal credentials, Paul points to something far more powerful: the Corinthian church itself. Their transformation is his letter of recommendation. Their lives—changed by the gospel—are written on Paul's heart and visible to all who know them.

This is a stunning reversal of the Old Covenant pattern. The law was written on stone tablets with ink. But the new covenant writes God's character on human hearts through the Spirit. The evidence of true ministry is not parchment and seals, but transformed lives.

## Competence From God Alone (3:4–6)

> Such confidence as this is ours through Christ before God. Not that we are competent in ourselves to claim anything for ourselves, but our competence comes from God. He has made us competent as ministers of a new covenant—not of the letter but of the Spirit; for the letter kills, but the Spirit gives life. (3:4–6)

Paul's confidence rests entirely in Christ, not in personal qualifications. He makes a crucial distinction: we are not competent in ourselves. Our adequacy comes from God. He has appointed us as ministers of the new covenant—a covenant of the Spirit, not merely of law.

The contrast between letter and Spirit is essential. The law, by itself, can only condemn and reveal sin; it brings death. But the Spirit gives life by transforming hearts from the inside out. True ministry operates in the power of the Spirit, not the force of rules.

This passage reveals Paul's understanding of ministry: it flows from God's power, demonstrates itself in changed lives, and operates by the Spirit's transforming work rather than mere external compliance.
