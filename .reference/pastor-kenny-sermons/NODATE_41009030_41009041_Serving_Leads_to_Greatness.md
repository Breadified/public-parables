# Mark 9:30-41

**Title:** Serving Leads to Greatness
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction

We often forget that people we admire for their greatness didn't always start that way. Leonardo DiCaprio, now considered one of the world's finest actors, came from a poor, broken family and was forced to move constantly as a child. Early in his career, he endured over 100 consecutive failed auditions and a stretch of eighteen months without work. He landed only small commercial and educational video roles, growing so discouraged he nearly quit. But with his father's encouragement, he persevered, eventually breaking through on the TV show "Growing Pains" in 1991. His success came through the hard work he invested in humble beginnings.

This principle—that greatness comes through sustained effort and faithfulness in small things—is what Jesus teaches his disciples as he leads them through Galilee following the transfiguration and the healing of the demon-possessed boy.

## First Will Be Last: Jesus Is Last

### Scripture

> He said to them, "The Son of Man is going to be delivered into the hands of men. They will kill him." (Mark 9:31)

### The Shocking Reality of Jesus' Path

Jesus calls himself the "Son of Man"—a title from the Old Testament that emphasizes his humanity rather than his divinity. Using this title, he tells his disciples he will be "delivered into the hands of men" and killed. The language paints a picture of powerlessness: Jesus being betrayed, seized, and utterly defeated.

Death represents the ultimate loss. In any competition—war, games, any contest—the one who dies loses everything. As Ecclesiastes 9:4 reminds us: "Anyone who is among the living has hope—even a live dog is better off than a dead lion." A lion, for all its strength and ferocity, loses status the moment it dies; even a living dog surpasses it.

For Jesus to announce his death is to announce he will become the ultimate loser. And his words came true in the most humiliating way possible: crucifixion, the most degrading form of execution of his time. Stripped, nailed to wood, suffocating in agony while passersby hurled insults at him. Jesus would be last.

### The Disciples' Misunderstanding

> But they did not understand what he meant and were afraid to ask him about it. (Mark 9:32)

The disciples couldn't comprehend Jesus' words. They had just witnessed his glorious transfiguration on the mountain—his face shining with divine glory. They had heard Peter declare him the Messiah. Surely following such a king meant they would become great themselves, like royal officials elevated alongside him.

This misunderstanding becomes painfully clear as they continue their journey:

> They came to Capernaum. When he was in the house, he asked them, "What were you arguing about on the road?" But they kept quiet because on the way they had argued about who was the greatest. (Mark 9:33-34)

While Jesus spoke of his coming death, his disciples argued about their own status. The world teaches us to value greatness as power, reputation, titles—being first, climbing above everyone else. This is the "rat race" our society prizes: win the competition, reach the top, secure your position.

### Jesus' Radical Reversal

> Sitting down, Jesus called the Twelve and said, "Anyone who wants to be first must be the very last, and the servant of all." (Mark 9:35)

Jesus uses the word "first" as the opposite of "last"—the highest position. Yet he commands his followers to seek the opposite: to be last, and to be "servant of all." The Greek word here emphasizes slavery—complete, unquestioning servitude.

Consider what slavery meant in that culture. A slave served everyone: the master, the master's guests, the entire household. Even a master's young children could command a slave. There was no task too small, no order too insignificant. Everyone stood above the slave. The slave ate last, slept last, rested last.

To illustrate this radical call to servanthood, Jesus does something remarkable:

> Taking a little child in his arms, he said to them, "Whoever welcomes one of these little children in my name welcomes me; and whoever welcomes me does not welcome me but the one who sent me." (Mark 9:36-37)

In Jesus' time, children occupied the lowest rung of society—equivalent to slaves. They were expected to serve, perform menial chores, and keep silent. Yet Jesus declares that welcoming a child—serving someone at the absolute bottom of the social hierarchy—is equivalent to welcoming Jesus himself, and welcoming God the Father.

The message is unmistakable: Jesus calls his followers to become last, to serve the powerless and overlooked with the same reverence they would show God himself.

### Application: Serve the Child—Serve in Small, Unflashy Ways

Given your age, you likely won't be asked to lead Bible studies or give talks anytime soon. Your opportunities to serve at church may seem small and insignificant. But Jesus teaches us that these small acts matter deeply. When you serve in humble, unglamorous ways, you are not a "second-class" servant. You are following your Savior's own command to be last.

What does this look like practically? You could serve drinks at lunch. You could stay after service to collect the rubbish around the hall instead of rushing off. You could let others pick their food first at lunch. These acts may not be glamorous, but when performed in Jesus' name, they embody his command to be last—and that is precisely what he requires of his disciples.

## Last Will Be First: The Resurrection Changes Everything

### Scripture

> He said to them, "The Son of Man is going to be delivered into the hands of men. They will kill him, and after three days he will rise." (Mark 9:31)

### The Triumphant Reversal

Yes, Jesus was delivered to men. Yes, he was killed. Yes, he became last. But he did not remain last. He rose from the dead, and in doing so, he became first—the greatest.

Jesus died to fulfill his role as the Messiah, the Servant of all, that he might save everyone who believes in him from their sins. Through his perfect obedience to the Father's will, he earned the right to be exalted. Ephesians 1:20-21 describes his exaltation:

> God raised Christ from the dead and seated him at his right hand in the heavenly realms, far above all rule and authority, power and dominion, and every name that is invoked, not only in the present age but also in the one to come.

Jesus has received the reward for his humble service. He is now forever the greatest, the first—for all eternity.

### Our Reward Is Through Jesus, Not Through Our Own Merit

The good news is that we, too, can become great. But there is a crucial distinction:

> Truly I tell you, anyone who gives you a cup of water in my name because you belong to the Messiah will certainly not lose their reward. (Mark 9:41)

Notice the phrase "in my name." This is essential. Our reward is not something we earn through our own service. That would render the cross unnecessary. Instead, Jesus—having earned his greatness through the cross—now grants his greatness to those who believe in him and serve in his name.

Those who make sacrifices in serving Jesus will receive eternal life (Mark 10:30). But this reward comes entirely through Jesus' name, not through our own efforts or status. We cannot earn it; we can only receive it as a gift rooted in what Jesus accomplished for us.

This is why serving "in Jesus' name" is not merely a formula—it reveals whether we truly belong to the Messiah. It demonstrates our allegiance. Serving only for our own reputation, our family's name, or even our church's reputation shows we are still fighting the old battle for status. But serving in Jesus' name shows we understand that our greatness comes only through him.

### The Danger of Competing for Status

John illustrates this misguided instinct:

> "Teacher," said John, "we saw someone driving out demons in your name and we told him to stop, because he was not one of us." (Mark 9:38)

John was still fighting to be first in a worldly way, drawing divisions based on whether someone belonged to the disciples' "squad." He was focused on status and group identity—his name, his circle—rather than recognizing that this other worker was united with them under Jesus' name.

Jesus' response reframes everything:

> "Do not stop him. For no one who does a miracle in my name can in the next moment say anything bad about me. Whoever is not against us is for us." (Mark 9:39-40)

Our unity is found in Jesus' name, not in status hierarchies or social circles. What matters is that we serve under his authority and for his reputation.

### Illustration: The Power of a Great Name

Consider Jaden Smith, son of actor Will Smith. Jaden's greatness doesn't stem from the acting agencies he signs with or the studios he works with. It comes from his name—from being Will Smith's son. Because of his father's excellence and fame, he inherits a share in that greatness.

Similarly, when we serve in Jesus' name, we inherit his greatness. Our reward comes not from our own status or achievements, but from belonging to the Messiah whose name carries all authority and glory.

### Application: Persevere—Your Reward Is Guaranteed

Because the last will be first in Christ, we can be certain that eternal life awaits those who persevere in humble service. This is profound encouragement for anyone weary from serving in small, unglamorous ways.

Continue serving by picking up rubbish, doing chores, distributing lunch—but do these things in Jesus' name, not for personal reputation or to impress others. When you serve with this motivation, you are demonstrating that you belong to Jesus. And those united in his name will receive the eternal life he won for us on the cross.

Do not lose heart. You will not lose. You serve in Jesus' name, which means his reputation is at stake. Jesus will guard his name and will not allow those who serve in his name to be put to shame. He will vindicate them with eternal life.

## Conclusion

Leonardo DiCaprio's perseverance through 100 failed auditions and years of small roles paid off. Today he is recognized as one of the world's greatest actors and won the coveted Oscar. We can say with confidence that he was right to persevere, right to work hard even in obscurity.

In the same way, serving humbly in Jesus' name is never wasted effort. Like DiCaprio, we will be first—but our greatness comes not from our own effort but from being united in Jesus' name. Because of what Jesus accomplished on the cross, we follow his path to eternal life. We continue to serve as the last, as the servant of all, now, but with full confidence that we will one day share in eternal life with King Jesus.

That is a very good deal indeed.
