# Matthew 24:36-25:13

**Title:** Preparing for the King
**Date:** 20 March 2011
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Part 1: You Do Not Know the Timing (24:36-41)

Jesus teaches that the timing of His return is hidden from us. He grounds this truth in the story of Noah.

> As it was in the days of Noah, so it will be at the coming of the Son of Man. For in the days before the flood, people were eating and drinking, marrying and giving in marriage, up to the day Noah entered the ark; and they knew nothing about what would happen until the flood came and took them all away. (Matthew 24:37-39)

In Noah's time, God was angry at the world's wickedness and brought judgment through a great flood. But no one saw it coming. There were no warning signs, no dark clouds gathering, no instruments to predict what was about to happen. Life went on as ordinary until suddenly, everything changed. The judgment came at an ordinary time—not during crisis or calamity, but when people were simply eating, drinking, and living their daily lives.

Jesus applies this pattern to His return: one person will be taken from the field while another is left behind; two women grinding grain at a mill will be separated. Just as in Noah's day, some will be divided from others at a moment no one expects.

### Three Responses to Uncertainty

Not knowing the future creates three possible responses:

**Anxiety:** When we don't know what will happen, worry can consume us. Imagine if an exam were announced on the day itself with no warning—the uncertainty would create constant anxiety.

**Complacency:** Others respond to uncertainty by simply not caring. "Whatever comes, comes. Why worry about it?"

**Preparedness:** The better response is to prepare. When a master leaves servants in charge of his house, he expects them to be watchful and ready, not knowing exactly when he'll return.

## Part 2: Therefore, Get Prepared (24:42-51)

Jesus's solution is clear: keep watch. Be ready.

> Therefore keep watch, because you do not know on what day your Lord will come. (Matthew 24:42)

> So you also must be ready, because the Son of Man will come at an hour when you do not expect him. (Matthew 24:44)

For the anxious, preparation gives purpose and calms the mind. For the complacent, it awakens them from laziness and stirs them to action.

### Illustration: The Owner of the House

> If the owner of the house had known at what time of night the thief was coming, he would have kept watch and would not have let his house be broken into. (Matthew 24:43)

In earlier times, an owner who knew a thief might come would stay awake and guard his property. There's a practical wisdom here: when you know danger is possible but not when it will strike, you remain vigilant. A man in London who owned a newsagency understood this well—because his stock was valuable and theft was a real threat, he ensured someone from his family was always home to guard the place. He never left it unattended. He was ready. No one could catch him surprised.

### Illustration: The Faithful and the Wicked Servant

Jesus contrasts two types of servants. The faithful and wise servant is put in charge of the household and given the responsibility to feed the other servants at the proper time. When the master returns and finds him doing exactly what was commanded, the master is pleased and promotes him:

> It will be good for that servant whose master finds him doing so when he returns. Truly I tell you, he will put him in charge of all his possessions. (Matthew 24:46-47)

The wicked servant, by contrast, becomes complacent. Thinking the master is delayed, he mistreats the other servants and parties with drunkards—sinning against both God and others.

> The master of that servant will come on a day when he does not expect him and at an hour he is not aware of. He will cut him to pieces and assign him a place with the hypocrites, where there will be weeping and gnashing of teeth. (Matthew 24:50-51)

Watching, in Jesus's teaching, does not simply mean sitting idle. It means actively doing what the master has commanded while He is away.

### Illustration: The Ten Maidens (25:1-13)

The parable of the ten virgins captures this readiness beautifully. An engagement ritual unfolds at night, with a bridegroom coming to meet ten young women carrying lamps. They are divided into two groups:

> The foolish ones took their lamps but did not take any oil with them. The wise ones, however, took oil in jars along with their lamps. (Matthew 25:3-4)

The bridegroom is delayed, and all of them grow drowsy and fall asleep. But at midnight, a cry rings out: "Here's the bridegroom! Come out to meet him!"

All the virgins wake and trim their lamps, but the foolish ones discover their lamps are going out. They ask the wise ones for oil, but the wise refuse:

> There may not be enough for both us and you. Instead, go to those who sell oil and buy some for yourselves. (Matthew 25:9)

While they go to find oil, the bridegroom arrives. The prepared virgins go in with him to the wedding banquet, and the door is shut. When the foolish ones return, they plead, "Lord, Lord, open the door for us!" But the bridegroom replies:

> Truly I tell you, I don't know you. (Matthew 25:12)

The point is stark: preparation is individual. You cannot borrow readiness from another. The consequences of unpreparedness fall on those who failed to prepare. Just as in an exam, nothing much can help when it begins—you're judged by your own preparation and performance.

> Therefore keep watch, because you do not know the day or the hour. (Matthew 25:13)

## Part 3: Are You Ready?

### For the Unprepared

If you are not a Christian today, consider this seriously. We all will face our Maker one day. Whether Jesus returns or we die and meet Him, we cannot escape this encounter with our Creator. The timing is uncertain, but the meeting is certain.

Many ignore this reality, pushing the thought aside. But each year you live, the odds of death increase. Even in ordinary times, death comes unexpectedly—just as Jesus said His return would come. We live in a society that hides death away: the hospital, the morgue, the funeral director. But ignoring death doesn't make it less real.

Do not delay. If you cannot say today that you would go to heaven if you died, you are in trouble. When that day comes, it will be too late. To be left behind is not to be left behind with a chance to repent—it is to be left behind like those outside the ark, facing eternal condemnation from God. That is a terrible fate.

### For the Prepared

For Christians, being prepared means more than personal readiness—it means fruitfulness and bearing witness to the gospel.

## Part 4: Preparedness Involves Fruitfulness

### The Secret of the Kingdom

Jesus teaches that knowledge of the gospel is a sacred trust:

> The knowledge of the secrets of the kingdom of heaven has been given to you, but not to them. Whoever has will be given more, and he will have an abundance. Whoever does not have, even what he has will be taken from him. (Matthew 13:11-12)

Paul echoes this in his letter to Corinth:

> Men ought to regard us as servants of Christ and as those entrusted with the secret things of God. Now it is required that those who have been given a trust must prove faithful. (1 Corinthians 4:1-2)

It is not enough to keep the gospel to yourself and secure your own salvation. You are entrusted with it for others. Why does judgment day not come immediately? For one reason: the salvation of many. God delays to give more time for the gospel to spread and for people to respond. If you are a Christian, your time, energy, and work should be devoted to achieving the Master's end.

Burying the gospel in a bookshelf or keeping it hidden does nothing. It cannot save anyone in the future. A good coupon is only good when it's used. So too, those who have been given knowledge of the gospel must put it to work.

> Whoever has will be given more, and he will have an abundance. (Matthew 25:29)

### Illustration: The Three Servants and Their Talents (25:14-30)

A master goes on a journey and entrusts his wealth to three servants. To one he gives five bags of gold, to another two bags, and to a third one bag—each according to his ability.

The first servant immediately puts his five bags to work and gains five more. The second servant does likewise with his two bags and gains two more. But the third servant, afraid, digs a hole and buries the master's money.

When the master returns, he settles accounts. The first servant presents his doubled wealth:

> His master replied, "Well done, good and faithful servant! You have been faithful with a few things; I will put you in charge of many things. Come and share your master's happiness!" (Matthew 25:21)

The second servant receives the same commendation. But the third servant, who buried his single bag, tries to justify himself:

> I knew that you are a hard man, harvesting where you have not sown and gathering where you have not scattered seed. So I was afraid and went out and hid your gold in the ground. (Matthew 25:24-25)

The master's response is harsh:

> You wicked, lazy servant! So you knew that I harvest where I have not sown and gather where I have not scattered seed? Well then, you should have put my money on deposit with the bankers, so that when I returned I would have received it back with interest. (Matthew 25:26-27)

The talent is taken from him and given to the one who has ten. The principle is relentless: those who use what they have received are given more, while those who bury their gifts lose even what they have.

The talents represent spiritual gifts, abilities, and especially the gospel itself—all the endowments God gives us to use for His kingdom. Using them produces fruit. Hiding them produces nothing and incurs judgment.

### Serving Others Is Serving Christ

In the final scene, Jesus describes the separation of the sheep from the goats. The King invites the righteous into His kingdom:

> Come, you who are blessed by my Father; take your inheritance, the kingdom prepared for you since the creation of the world. For I was hungry and you gave me something to eat, I was thirsty and you gave me something to drink, I was a stranger and you invited me in, I needed clothes and you clothed me, I was sick and you looked after me, I was in prison and you came to visit me. (Matthew 25:34-36)

The righteous are confused. They don't recall doing these things for Jesus Himself:

> Lord, when did we see you hungry and feed you, or thirsty and give you something to drink? When did we see you a stranger and invite you in, or needing clothes and clothe you? When did we see you sick or in prison and go to visit you? (Matthew 25:37-39)

> The King will reply, "I tell you the truth, whatever you did for one of the least of these brothers of mine, you did for me." (Matthew 25:40)

To those on His left, the King says the opposite: they failed to feed the hungry, give drink to the thirsty, welcome strangers, clothe the naked, or visit the sick and imprisoned. When they protest that they never saw Jesus in need, He replies:

> Whatever you did not do for one of the least of these, you did not do for me. (Matthew 25:45)

Service to others is service to Christ Himself. When you care for those in need, you honor Christ. When you neglect them, you neglect Him.

## Conclusion: Be Ready

Stop saying, "I am not ready."

- I am not ready to move out.
- I am not ready to get married.
- I am not ready to have children.
- I am not ready to meet God.
- I am not ready to serve.

Instead, ask yourself: What am I doing to get prepared?

And then say with conviction: I am ready.
