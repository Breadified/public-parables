## Eli's Wicked Sons

> 2:12 Eli's sons were **scoundrels**; they had no regard for the Lord.

The word "scoundrels" (Hebrew) carries the weight of profound wickedness. It appears in Judges 19:22 when evil men attempted rape, and in Judges 20 when the men of Gibeah brutally assaulted a concubine to death. This language sets the gravity of Eli's sons' condition from the outset.

### Their Wickedness Revealed

**They were violent.**

> 2:16 If the person said to him, "Let the fat be burned first, and then take whatever you want," the servant would answer, "No, hand it over now; if you don't, **I'll take it by force."**

When people refused their demands, they resorted to violence. They were thugs and bullies who used force to take what they wanted.

**They were immoral.**

> 2:22 Now Eli, who was very old, heard about everything his sons were doing to all Israel and how **they slept with the women who served at the entrance to the tent of meeting.**

They exploited the women who served at the temple, using their position of power to abuse those beneath them.

**They refused to listen.**

> 2:23-24 So he said to them, "Why do you do such things? I hear from all the people about these wicked deeds of yours. No, my sons; the report I hear spreading among the Lord's people is not good."

Their father Eli confronted them, but they would not heed his rebuke.

### Contempt for God

Most critically, **their wickedness was directed against God Himself.**

> 2:13-15 Now it was the practice of the priests that, whenever any of the people offered a sacrifice, the priest's servant would come with a three-pronged fork in his hand while the meat was being boiled and would plunge the fork into the pan or kettle or caldron or pot. Whatever the fork brought up the priest would **take for himself.** This is how they treated all the Israelites who came to Shiloh. But even before the fat was burned, the priest's servant would come and say to the person who was sacrificing, **"Give the priest some meat to roast; he won't accept boiled meat from you, but only raw."**

The sacrificial system was sacred. Parts of each offering were burned to the Lord as worship and thanksgiving, while other portions sustained the priests and the worshippers. Eli's sons were taking meat meant for God's altar for themselves—meat intended to be consumed in the presence of the Lord. They were treating God's offering with contempt.

> 2:17 This sin of the young men was very great in the Lord's sight, for they were treating the Lord's offering with contempt.

### Eli's Failure as a Father

But the blame extended beyond the sons themselves.

> 2:29 Why do you scorn my sacrifice and offering that I prescribed for my dwelling? **Why do you honor your sons more than me** by fattening yourselves on the choice parts of every offering made by my people Israel?'

Eli honored his sons more than he honored God. He allowed them to continue in their wickedness. In his love for his children, he enabled their sin rather than disciplining them.

The consequences were severe. God declared judgment:

> 2:30-33 But now the Lord declares: 'Far be it from me! Those who honor me I will honor, but those who despise me will be disdained. The time is coming when I will cut short your strength and the strength of your priestly house, so that no one in it will reach old age... Every one of you that I do not cut off from serving at my altar I will spare only to destroy your sight and sap your strength, and all your descendants will die in the prime of life.'

> 2:25 If one person sins against another, God may mediate for the offender; but if anyone sins against the Lord, **who will intercede for them?**

## A Spiritual Application: Honoring Our Children vs. Honoring God

The challenge to us is profound: **Do you honor your children more than you honor God?**

This is especially difficult in our modern culture, where parenting often means being a friend to our children rather than their parent. We want them to like us, and we want them to be like us. But these desires can lead us to compromise our values and enable their sin.

### The Danger of Wanting Them to Like Us

Modern parenting emphasizes equality between parent and child. We want to be friends with them, to be peers. But this reverses a critical biblical principle.

> Hebrews 12:9-11 Moreover, we have all had human fathers who disciplined us and we respected them for it. How much more should we submit to the Father of spirits and live! No discipline seems pleasant at the time, but painful. **Later** on, however, it produces a harvest of righteousness and peace for those who have been trained by it.

Note the word "later." This teaches us that we are not meant to be friends with our children during their childhood. Our role is to be their parent, their teacher, their guide—even when that is unpopular.

We must ask ourselves: Whose approval do we care about most—God's or our child's? When they disregard God's word, do we change our theology to accommodate them?

### The Danger of Wanting Them to Be Like Us

Another subtle trap is projecting our unfulfilled dreams onto our children. We compensate for what we missed by trying to give them experiences we longed for. We force them toward accomplishments that satisfy our pride, not their calling. We ask: "Is this really what the child needs, or am I projecting my own issues?"

More dangerously, **when we want them to be like us and to like us, we get blackmailed into joining them in their sin.** Rather than being parents who train and lead them away from sin, we enable them.

### A Concrete Example

I once counseled a father worried about his high school daughter. She was using recreational drugs, partying, dressed provocatively on social media—clearly heading toward destruction. When I asked how she afforded this lifestyle, he admitted he gave her all her money.

"What if you stopped?" I suggested.

"But then she'll hate me," he replied. "I love my child."

Yes, but undirected love kills. When we enable sin out of fear of losing their affection, we rob them of the most important thing a parent can provide: a steady voice calling them toward righteousness.

### Soft Capitulation

We capitulate in countless small ways:

- We give our children smartphones to "upgrade" our own devices, justifying it as being "for their safety"—despite driving them everywhere.
- When they say "If you make me go to church, I won't study," we let them skip youth group so they don't fail their exam. But is that good for them, or is it satisfying our need for their academic success?
- We give them money, cars, freedom—all things that enable their sin—because we're afraid of their disapproval.

The most serious capitulation comes when our children sin sexually. Some parents actually change their theology rather than maintaining God's standard.

> "I love my child. So I will change my theology to fit their behavior."

But this is honoring your child more than honoring God. And it is the cruelest thing you can do to them.

**It is better to stay firm to God's word and withstand the emotional blackmail. If you give up God, then they have lost their last link to reason and there is no way home in the future.**

## Samuel: A Better Son

While Eli's sons brought shame, there was another young man who acted differently.

> 2:18 But Samuel was ministering before the Lord—a boy wearing a linen ephod. 19 Each year his mother made him a little robe and took it to him when she went up with her husband to offer the annual sacrifice.

Samuel's mother Hannah had made a vow to the Lord. When God answered her prayer with a son, she kept her promise:

> 1 Samuel 1:27-28 I prayed for this child, and the Lord has granted me what I asked of him. So now I give him to the Lord. For his whole life he will be given over to the Lord.

Here is a parent who did not honor her child more than honoring God. In fact, she gave her son up to God.

Imagine if we thought of our children this way:

- Not for my pride—to show off their achievements to friends
- Not to compensate for my failings
- Not to be my friend and do things for me
- **But to raise a child for God**, one who will honor Him, whose whole life is given over to the Lord

If you change this mindset, your entire outlook on raising your child will be transformed. It will be radically different from the world's approach.

> 2:26 And the boy Samuel continued to grow in stature and in favor with the Lord and with people.

And eventually:

> 3:1 The boy Samuel ministered before the Lord under Eli. In those days the word of the Lord was rare; there were not many visions.

Since the days of the Judges, there had been no prophetic word from God—a long silence. But God was about to break that silence through a boy raised in dedication to Him.

> **3:7 Now Samuel did not yet know the Lord: The word of the Lord had not yet been revealed to him.**

God called Samuel three times. After the third call:

> 3:8-10 A third time the Lord called, "Samuel!" And Samuel got up and went to Eli and said, "Here I am; you called me." Then Eli realized that the Lord was calling the boy. So Eli told Samuel, "Go and lie down, and if he calls you, say, 'Speak, Lord, for your servant is listening.'" So Samuel went and lay down in his place. The Lord came and stood there, calling as at the other times, "Samuel! Samuel!" Then Samuel said, "Speak, for your servant is listening."

> **3:11 And the Lord said to Samuel: "See, I am about to do something in Israel that will make the ears of everyone who hears about it tingle."**

The great thing God was about to do was reveal Himself through His word. He would use Samuel the prophet to bring His word to all Israel.

> 3:19-20 The Lord was with Samuel as he grew up, and he let **none of Samuel's words** fall to the ground. And all Israel from Dan to Beersheba recognized that Samuel was attested as a prophet of the Lord.

> 3:21 The Lord continued to appear at Shiloh, and there he revealed himself to **Samuel through his word**.

> **4:1 And Samuel's word came to all Israel.**

## God's Word: A Gift for Our Benefit

It might seem strange that God's revelation now comes through a written word rather than through burning bushes and audible voices. But this is actually God's grace to us.

> Hebrews 1:1-3 In the past God spoke to our ancestors through the prophets at many times and in various ways, but in these last days he has spoken to us by his Son, whom he appointed heir of all things, and through whom also he made the universe. The Son is the radiance of God's glory and the exact representation of his being, sustaining all things by his powerful word.

> 2 Peter 1:20-21 Above all, you must understand that no prophecy of Scripture comes from one's own interpretation. For no such prophecy was ever brought forth by the will of man, but men spoke from God as they were carried along by the Holy Spirit.

Before it became a book, God's word was spoken by the powerful God and by Jesus in spectacular ways. But now we have it in written form—the end result of God's purposes through the prophets.

### Why the Written Word is Better

The written word removes subjectivity. Samuel had to wait, sometimes in long silence, uncertain whether he truly heard from God. But we have Scripture—black and white, objective, permanent. We can read it whenever we wish.

When you face a decision and pray, "Lord, tell me what to do. Send me a sign or a vision," God answers: "Read My word. I have already spoken."

The written word is:
- **Sufficient**: God has said all He intends to say
- **Accessible**: Available to us whenever we need it
- **Objective**: We cannot make it say whatever we wish
- **Reliable**: Tested and proven through centuries

To dismiss the Bible as boring compared to visions is to despise the very purposes of those spectacular events that brought it into being. The prophets and Jesus did not come merely to have personal experiences—they came to give us God's complete word.

## God's Better Priest

The narrative points forward to one greater than Samuel, greater even than the prophets.

> 2:35 I will raise up for myself a faithful priest, who will do according to what is in my heart and mind. I will firmly establish his priestly house, and they will minister before my **anointed one** always.

This is not Eli's line. God's promise to Aaron of a perpetual priesthood is here revoked. The answer to this prophecy comes at the end of the Bible:

> Hebrews 7:28 For the law appoints as high priests men in all their weakness; but the oath, which came after the law, appointed the Son, who has been made perfect forever.

**Jesus is the ultimate Davidic priest.** He does what no other priest could do—He reconciles us directly with God.

> 2:25 If one person sins against another, God may mediate for the offender; but if anyone sins against the Lord, **who will intercede for them?**

Jesus intercedes for us. He is a priest forever who can make our prayers heard and our sins forgiven through His sacrifice.

This is God's good Son—the one God gave up. Like Hannah, God did not keep His Son for Himself but sent Him into the world to give His body for us.

---

## Conclusion: Be Like Jesus

As parents, we must ask ourselves hard questions:

- Do we want our children to **like us**? Only as much as they like righteousness.
- Do we want our children to **be like us**? No—we are sinful.
- Do we want our children to **be like Jesus**? Yes. God's good Son.

When we have done our work faithfully—when we have trained them, disciplined them, pointed them toward God—then something beautiful happens. In maturity, when we both reach spiritual adulthood, we will stand equal together as peers and brothers, alongside our brother Jesus.

That is the vision of Christian parenting. Not friendship in their childhood, but brotherhood in eternity.
