# Isaiah 7

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Historical Context: Assyria's Rise to Power

Assyria emerged as the dominant world power of the ancient Near East, fundamentally reshaping the geopolitical landscape of the region. Understanding this context is essential to grasping the crisis that unfolds in Isaiah 7.

## The Four Stages of Crisis

### Stage 1: Coalition Pressure
Damascus and Israel pressured Jotham and Ahaz to join them in rebellion against Assyria's growing dominance.

### Stage 2: Military Escalation
When Ahaz refused to join the coalition, Isaiah advised Judah to remain neutral. In response, the king of Israel marched on Jerusalem to depose Ahaz and install a puppet ruler—the son of Tabeel (Isaiah 7:6). Though Judah suffered heavy losses in this conflict, Ahaz managed to retain his throne (2 Kings 16:5).

### Stage 3: Capitulation and Vassalage
The mounting pressure overwhelmed Ahaz (Isaiah 7:1-8). Rather than trust God's promise of protection, he sought to preserve his kingdom by submitting to Assyria as a vassal state and offering an enormous tribute. Tiglath-Pileser responded by devastating Damascus in 732 BC and invading Israel as far south as Galilee (2 Kings 15:29).

### Stage 4: Israel's Final Fall
When Tiglath-Pileser's son Shalmaneser V ascended the throne, Israel attempted to shift its allegiance to Egypt. Shalmaneser responded decisively, laying siege to Samaria, conquering it, and deporting the upper classes of Israelite society (2 Kings 17).

## The Central Teaching: Trust in God Alone

Through these unfolding events, Isaiah teaches a profound spiritual principle: God alone is trustworthy, whereas all other nations—no matter how powerful—ultimately fail.

If Judah continues to refuse dependence on God while seeking security in earthly alliances, she will find neither message nor hope to offer the world. But if she recognizes that she has encountered a God greater than all nations combined—a God who can be trusted in every circumstance—then she possesses something vital to declare.

This tension between faithless reliance on nations and faithful dependence on God is the central issue introduced in chapter 7 and continues throughout chapter 39.

## Two Perspectives in Every Crisis

In every circumstance, we encounter two competing perspectives: the human and the divine. These perspectives frequently conflict sharply.

From Ahaz's vantage point, Syria and Ephraim constituted a major, pressing threat demanding immediate diplomatic response. From God's perspective, however, these nations were negligible—mere shadows unworthy of occupying the king's attention or concern.

This disparity reveals a fundamental spiritual truth: it is not always easy to discern God's perspective on our circumstances. Yet unless we deliberately seek it, we inevitably give excessive attention to the temporary while neglecting the truly significant.

Moreover, apart from a diligent search for God's perspective in every situation, we easily fall into the dangerous fallacy that God concerns himself only with spiritual matters, remaining distant from our practical affairs. This error inevitably leads to the loss of God's presence and guidance in all areas of life.
