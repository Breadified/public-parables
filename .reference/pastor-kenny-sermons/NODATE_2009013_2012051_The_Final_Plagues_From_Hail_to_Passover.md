# Exodus 9:13–12:51

**Title:** The Final Plagues: From Hail to Passover  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Seventh Plague: Hail

> "Get up early in the morning, confront Pharaoh and say to him, 'This is what the LORD, the God of the Hebrews, says: Let my people go, so that they may worship me. At this time tomorrow I will send the worst hailstorm that has ever fallen on Egypt, from the day it was founded till now.'" (Exodus 9:13)

God's judgment through hail demonstrates His power over the elements themselves. Yet when the plague ceased:

> "When Pharaoh saw that the rain and hail and thunder had stopped, he sinned again: He and his officials hardened their hearts." (Exodus 9:34)

**Key insight:** Pharaoh's heart remained unchanged. Even experiencing God's power firsthand could not break his resistance to releasing Israel.

## The Eighth Plague: Locusts

> "How long will you refuse to humble yourself before me? Let my people go, so that they may worship me. If you refuse to let them go, I will bring locusts into your country tomorrow. They will cover the face of the ground so that it cannot be seen. They will devour what little you have left after the hail, including every tree that is growing in your fields." (Exodus 10:3–5)

God's appeal is direct and personal: an invitation to humble yourself. The locusts would consume the very last remnants of Egypt's provision.

Yet again:

> "The LORD hardened Pharaoh's heart, and he would not let the Israelites go." (Exodus 10:20)

**Pattern emerging:** God extends repeated invitations to repentance, but Pharaoh continually chooses resistance.

## The Ninth Plague: Darkness

> "Stretch out your hand toward the sky so that darkness will spread over Egypt—darkness that can be felt. So Moses stretched out his hand toward the sky, and total darkness covered all Egypt for three days. No one could see anyone else or leave his place for three days. Yet all the Israelites had light in the places where they lived." (Exodus 10:21–23)

This plague carries profound spiritual significance. The darkness was absolute and tangible—a physical manifestation of separation from God's light. Remarkably, the Israelites experienced light in their dwellings, establishing a crucial distinction between God's people and those who rejected Him.

Still, Pharaoh refused:

> "The LORD hardened Pharaoh's heart, and he was not willing to let them go." (Exodus 10:27)

## The Tenth Plague: Passover

With the tenth plague, God's judgment enters new territory. This is no longer just a demonstration of power—it is redemption.

### The Passover Instructions

God commands Israel to observe a sacred ceremony that would become the cornerstone of their faith:

> "Tell the whole community of Israel that on the tenth day of this month each man is to take a lamb for his family, one for each household... The animals you choose must be year-old males without defect, and you may take them from the sheep or the goats. Take care of them until the fourteenth day of the month, when all the people of the community of Israel must slaughter them at twilight." (Exodus 12:3, 5–6)

The lamb was not merely an offering—it was a substitutionary sacrifice. For four days the people were to examine the lamb, ensuring it was "without defect." This preparation emphasized the seriousness and intentionality of what was about to occur.

### The Covenant Sign

> "Take some of the blood and put it on the sides and tops of the doorframes of the houses where you eat the lambs... The blood will be a sign for you on the houses where you are; and when I see the blood, I will pass over you. No destructive plague will touch you when I strike Egypt." (Exodus 12:7, 13)

**The blood is the sign.** God does not pass over based on geography, wealth, or Egyptian heritage—only those marked by the blood were protected. This foreshadows the gospel truth that salvation comes through substitutionary sacrifice.

### The Meal of Redemption

> "This is how you are to eat it: with your cloak tucked into your belt, your sandals on your feet and your staff in your hand. Eat it in haste; it is the LORD's Passover." (Exodus 12:11)

The posture matters. Standing, ready, willing to depart immediately—this was not a leisurely meal but a sacrament of liberation. The bitter herbs symbolized the bitterness of slavery, the unleavened bread spoke of haste and the exclusion of pride (yeast represents corruption and self-reliance in Scripture).

> "Do not eat the meat raw or cooked in water, but roast it over the fire... Do not break any of the bones." (Exodus 12:8–9, 46)

Every detail carried meaning. The lamb was to be completely consumed by its household, strengthening community bonds and ensuring nothing was wasted or left to spoil.

### A Memorial for the Generations

> "This is a day you are to commemorate; for the generations to come you shall celebrate it as a festival to the LORD—a lasting ordinance." (Exodus 12:14)

God established Passover as an eternal memorial. Every year, Israel would rehearse God's redemptive act, teaching their children:

> "When your children ask you, 'What does this ceremony mean to you?' then tell them, 'It is the Passover sacrifice to the LORD, who passed over the houses of the Israelites in Egypt and spared our homes when he struck down the Egyptians.'" (Exodus 12:26–27)

Worship follows understanding. When the people grasped what God had done for them, they naturally responded:

> "Then the people bowed down and worshiped. The Israelites did just what the LORD commanded Moses and Aaron." (Exodus 12:27–28)

### The Night of Judgment and Deliverance

> "At midnight the LORD struck down all the firstborn in Egypt, from the firstborn of Pharaoh, who sat on the throne, to the firstborn of the prisoner, who was in the dungeon, and the firstborn of all the livestock as well. Pharaoh and all his officials and all the Egyptians got up during the night, and there was loud wailing in Egypt, for there was not a house without someone dead." (Exodus 12:29–30)

The judgment was absolute and universal—touching every level of Egyptian society. There was no escape, no exception, no reprieve. Yet for Israel:

> "During the night Pharaoh summoned Moses and Aaron and said, 'Up! Leave my people, you and the Israelites! Go, worship the LORD as you have requested. Take your flocks and herds, as you have said, and go. And also bless me.'" (Exodus 12:31–32)

Pharaoh's resistance finally broke. The man who had refused to humble himself now desperately pleaded for God's blessing. The Egyptians themselves urged Israel to leave:

> "The Egyptians urged the people to hurry and leave the country. 'For otherwise,' they said, 'we will all die!' So the people took their dough before the yeast was added, and carried it on their shoulders in kneading troughs wrapped in clothing. The Israelites did as Moses instructed and asked the Egyptians for articles of silver and gold and for clothing. The LORD had made the Egyptians favorably disposed toward the people, and they gave them what they asked for; so they plundered the Egyptians." (Exodus 12:33–36)

God worked even through their terror to provide for His people. Egypt, which had enslaved Israel, now voluntarily gave them the resources for their journey.

## The Exodus

> "The Israelites journeyed from Rameses to Succoth. There were about six hundred thousand men on foot, besides women and children. Many other people went up with them, as well as large droves of livestock, both flocks and herds." (Exodus 12:37–38)

A massive multitude—not just Israelites, but also "many other people" who recognized God's power and chose to follow. They traveled with livestock and carried the unleavened bread they had baked in haste.

> "Now the length of time the Israelite people lived in Egypt was 430 years. At the end of the 430 years, to the very day, all the LORD's divisions left Egypt." (Exodus 12:40–41)

**God's precision is remarkable.** Four hundred thirty years of waiting culminated in a single night of deliverance. God had not forgotten; He was keeping vigil:

> "Because the LORD kept vigil that night to bring them out of Egypt, on this night all the Israelites are to keep vigil to honor the LORD for the generations to come." (Exodus 12:42)

## The Regulations of Passover

The observance carried specific requirements that shaped the community's identity:

> "These are the regulations for the Passover: No foreigner is to eat of it. Any slave you have bought may eat of it after you have circumcised him, but a temporary resident and a hired worker may not eat of it... An alien living among you who wants to celebrate the LORD's Passover must have all the males in his household circumcised; then he may take part like one born in the land." (Exodus 12:43–49)

Passover was not merely ceremonial—it was covenantal. Entry into the community and its blessings required entering the covenant through circumcision. The same law applied to native and alien alike, establishing an equality of access to God based on covenant commitment rather than bloodline.

> "All the Israelites did just what the LORD had commanded Moses and Aaron. And on that very day the LORD brought the Israelites out of Egypt by their divisions." (Exodus 12:50–51)

**Obedience brought deliverance.** When God's people aligned their actions with His commands, the work of redemption was accomplished.
