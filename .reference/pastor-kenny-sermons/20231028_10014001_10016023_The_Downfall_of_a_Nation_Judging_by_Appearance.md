## Introduction: A Question of Justice

We find ourselves in a difficult season of 2 Samuel where we must confront topics that extend beyond polite dinner conversation. Yet if these matters appear in Scripture, we should trust that God considers them important lessons for our future. Our generation may find itself in its present predicament precisely because we've neglected to read and grapple with the Bible's harder truths.

I recognize that our younger members hear troubling accounts of how the world can turn ugly, particularly as we witness the downfall of biblical heroes. But you're encountering nothing beyond what Scripture itself presents. Being an adult means becoming a realist—seeing truth rather than viewing the world through rose-tinted glasses.

The passage before us invites a fundamental question: **What is justice?** In a world obsessed with appearances—who appears weaker, who appears stronger—we must learn to discern true justice rather than being manipulated by mere perception.

---

## Part One: Judging by Appearance (2 Samuel 14:1-21)

### The Setup: A Strategic Deception

Absalom, David's third son, has murdered his brother Amnon in retaliation for Amnon's rape of their sister Tamar. Having escaped into exile, Absalom remains separated from his father. King David's heart yearns for his banished son, and Joab—David's aggressive, helpful nephew and right-hand man—recognizes this weakness.

Joab devises a plan. He brings a "wise woman" from Tekoa (a town on Jerusalem's outskirts) to David's court with specific instructions:

> She was to dress in mourning clothes, avoid cosmetics, and present herself as a woman who has grieved for the dead for many days. She was to tell a carefully crafted story to the king.

### The Woman's Plea

The woman approaches David with a false tale: She is a widow whose two sons quarreled in a field. One killed the other. Now her entire clan demands that she hand over the surviving son so they may execute him—both for the murder and to absorb his family's inheritance.

Her story invokes the law:

> Genesis 9:6 reminds us: "Whoever sheds human blood, by humans shall their blood be shed; for in the image of God has God made mankind."

> Exodus 21:12 establishes: "Anyone who kills a person is to be put to death as a murderer only on the testimony of witnesses."

Justice demands a price. Yet the woman laments that if her remaining son is executed, her family loses its inheritance, its name, and its legacy—the very things that ensure a family's continuation.

### David's Apparent Compassion

David, moved by her apparent suffering, grants her request:

> "Go home, and I will issue an order in your behalf."

But the woman persists, asking for his oath before God himself. David, growing increasingly sympathetic, swears:

> "As surely as the Lord lives, not one hair of your son's head will fall to the ground."

Finally, with her real agenda exposed, she pivots:

> "Why then have you devised a thing like this against the people of God? When the king says this, does he not convict himself, for the king has not brought back his banished son?"

The trap is sprung. David, having granted mercy to a widow's son in a murder case, cannot refuse the same grace to his own son. By verse 21, he capitulates:

> "Very well, I will do it. Go, bring back the young man Absalom."

### The Theological Problem: Grace Is Not Justice

The woman's argument sounds compelling on the surface. She appeals to forgiveness through a poetic image:

> "Like water spilled on the ground, which cannot be recovered, so we must die. But that is not what God desires; rather, he devises ways so that a banished person does not remain banished from him."

Her logic echoes Jesus's teaching on forgiveness—that we should forgive seventy-seven times, a symbolic number meaning unlimited forgiveness in Jewish thought.

**But here lies the critical error:** This conflates two distinct spheres of justice.

Personal forgiveness operates in the realm between individuals. If someone damages your property or wrongs you personally, you may graciously forgive that debt. That is your prerogative. God forgives us our personal sins against Him in precisely this way—the debt belongs to Him, so He may forgive it through Christ's payment on the cross.

**However, a murder is not merely a personal offense.** The crime belongs to multiple parties:
- The victim, who can no longer speak
- The victim's family
- The community
- God Himself

David, as king, holds a sacred responsibility to execute justice on behalf of those who cannot defend themselves. The king's monopoly on justice—backed by the sword—exists precisely to protect the vulnerable and punish evildoers without prejudice or favoritism.

By freeing Absalom, David does not extend grace. He abrogates his fundamental duty as a judge. He places his personal preference for his son above the law that protects all people equally.

The woman herself is guilty of the same sin: manipulating justice to serve her own interests rather than submitting to the law's equitable demands.

### The Heart of God: Impartial Justice

Scripture reveals God's design for justice in Leviticus:

> "Do not show partiality to the poor or favoritism to the great."

We easily recognize favoritism toward the powerful—they have wealth and influence, and we instinctively curry favor with them. But we must also recognize a subtler form of corruption: favoritism toward the weak.

The poor can manipulate justice just as effectively as the rich, but through different means—by pulling on heartstrings, evoking sympathy, and exploiting our natural inclination to help the vulnerable. When justice yields to appearance and emotion rather than fact and law, injustice prevails.

Jesus commanded:

> "Stop judging by mere appearances, but instead judge correctly." (John 7:24)

## Part Two: When Appearance Judges Us (2 Samuel 14:22-16:23)

### The Beautiful Facade

David's weakness for appearances becomes painfully evident once Absalom returns to Jerusalem:

> In all Israel, there was not a man so highly praised for his handsome appearance as Absalom. From the top of his head to the sole of his foot, there was no blemish in him. Whenever he cut his hair—once a year because it became too heavy—it weighed two hundred shekels by the royal standard.

The emphasis on Absalom's physical perfection is unmistakable. His very hair symbolizes power and strength, much as Samson's did. His genes are flawless. He becomes a symbol of beauty itself.

Yet David refuses to see him. For two years, Absalom remains in Jerusalem but cannot approach his father's face. The irony cuts deep: though Absalom has the most praiseworthy face in Israel, he cannot look upon the king's face.

### The Manipulation Unfolds

Absalom's character reveals itself through his impatience. Unable to access his father, he calls upon Joab. When Joab refuses to come, Absalom's response is instructive:

> He commanded his servants: "Look, Joab's field is next to mine, and he has barley there. Go and set it on fire."

Violence and intimidation become his tools when manipulation fails. When Joab finally visits, Absalom's words drip with self-righteousness:

> "If I am guilty of anything, let him put me to death."

This is deeply ironic. Absalom *is* guilty—he murdered his brother. Yet he speaks as though innocent, counting on his beautiful face and David's emotional weakness to obscure the truth.

David, unable to bear the sight of his estranged son any longer, summons Absalom. The king kisses his murderous son and accepts him fully back into the court.

### The Strategy of Flattery

With his position secured, Absalom begins a four-year campaign of calculated charm:

> In the course of time, Absalom provided himself with a chariot and horses and with fifty men to run ahead of him. He would get up early and stand by the side of the road leading to the city gate. Whenever anyone came with a complaint to be placed before the king for a decision, Absalom would call out to him and say, "Look, your claims are valid and proper, but there is no representative of the king to hear you. If only I were appointed judge in the land! Then everyone who has a complaint or case could come to me and I would see that they receive justice."

Every person who approached him, he would reach out, take hold of them, and kiss them. Through this calculated display of warmth and accessibility:

> He stole the hearts of the people of Israel.

Everyone is an actor now. Absalom performs the role of the caring judge who listens when the king does not. His theatricality is flawless. The crowds are like sheep, eager to follow the appearance of compassion and justice, never questioning whether his concern is genuine.

### The Coup

After four years of building momentum, Absalom makes his move. He requests permission to go to Hebron—the ancient capital where Abraham was buried and where David himself was first anointed as king—to fulfill a vow to the Lord.

David, still unable to see through his son's manipulation, grants permission:

> "Go in peace."

But Absalom has planned far differently. From Hebron, he sends secret messengers throughout the tribes:

> "As soon as you hear the sound of the trumpets, then say, 'Absalom is king in Hebron.'"

Two hundred men from Jerusalem accompany Absalom, invited as innocent guests, unaware of his true intentions. Among his conspirators is Ahithophel, David's own trusted counselor. The conspiracy gains strength. Absalom's following increases. 

It is all appearance—manufactured momentum created through deception and flattery. The gossip of yesterday becomes the truth of today, and a nation fractures.

### The Ultimate Judgment

The consequences reveal themselves in the darkest possible way. Following Ahithophel's counsel, Absalom commits a final act of desecration:

> They pitched a tent for Absalom on the roof, and he slept with his father's concubines in the sight of all Israel.

This was no accident. Years earlier, God spoke through Nathan the prophet, pronouncing judgment on David for his own sins with Bathsheba:

> "Out of your own household I am going to bring calamity on you. Before your very eyes I will take your wives and give them to one who is close to you, and he will sleep with your wives in broad daylight."

The prophecy is fulfilled precisely. David's failure to execute justice—his willingness to judge by appearance rather than by truth—has set in motion the very judgment God promised.

## The Broader Pattern: A Cautionary Tale for Our Age

### Judging by Appearance in Our Time

Our generation has adopted what might be called "appearance-based justice." We classify people into categories of powerful and powerless, automatically labeling the stronger as oppressors and the weaker as oppressed. This framework—sometimes called Cultural Marxism—assumes that outcomes reveal guilt:

> "The history of all hitherto existing society is the history of class struggles. Freeman and slave, patrician and plebeian, lord and serf, guild-master and journeyman, in a word, oppressor and oppressed."

Once labeled, the powerful are guilty by definition, regardless of their actual actions. The weak are righteous by default, regardless of their actual behavior.

This is not discernment. It is bigotry dressed in different clothes.

Consider examples: If Aboriginal people have higher incarceration rates than whites, we conclude that whites are oppressing Aboriginals—ignoring the roles of alcohol, family breakdown, and systemic poverty that affect people of all races. We erase the reality of poor white people and wealthy Black people, collapsing the complexity of human life into a simplistic power narrative.

Or examine the NBA. Seventy percent of players are Black, while only 0.04% are Asian. If we applied the logic of our age, we might conclude that Black players are oppressing whites and Asians. Yet such a conclusion would be absurd. The reality is far more nuanced—different populations have different interests, different physical gifts, different cultural engagement with the sport. Correlation is not causation. God's gifting, personal preference, and opportunity converge in ways that don't require conspiracy to explain.

Yet this is precisely how we judge today. We look at outcomes and declare the weaker side righteous, the stronger side guilty. We do so without examining facts, without considering context, and without recognizing that our judgments are often based on appearance alone.

### Modern Examples of Appearance-Based Judgment

Think of contemporary controversies through this lens:

- **Homelessness**: "Those developers are oppressors!"
- **Poverty**: "Those capitalists are oppressors!"
- **Transgender issues**: "Those cisgender people are oppressors!"
- **Aboriginal disparities**: "Those whites are oppressors!"
- **Gaza**: "Those Israeli occupiers are oppressors!"

In each case, we jump from observing an inequality to pronouncing moral judgment on entire groups. We ask: "Who is suffering more? Who appears weaker?" And having answered, we declare that group righteous and their opposite guilty—all without examining facts, context, or individual responsibility.

This is the very error David committed. Moved by the widow's tears and Absalom's beautiful face, he abandoned the hard work of justice for the easier path of appearance-based compassion.

### The God Who Judges the Heart

Scripture offers a different standard:

> "The Lord does not look at the things people look at. People look at the outward appearance, but the Lord looks at the heart." (1 Samuel 16:7)

God's justice penetrates beyond surfaces. He weighs hearts and judges with perfect knowledge of circumstances, motivations, and truth. He does not play favorites. He withholds nothing—not even His Son—in order to maintain the demands of true justice.

God came as a man in Jesus Christ to pay the ultimate price. He died on the cross to bear the cost of justice on our behalf. Whereas David withheld his son from judgment, God the Father sent His Son to be an atoning sacrifice—to satisfy both mercy and justice simultaneously.

---

## Conclusion: A Call to Better Discernment

We are guilty of judging badly. We judge by appearances. We let emotion override evidence. We manipulate justice to serve our preferences and protect those we favor, whether they are weak or powerful.

Yet God does not turn a blind eye. He is a God of justice who sees all things. He calls us to change—to repent of our appearance-based judgments and to seek truth with intellectual rigor and pastoral care.

He forgives us for our failures in judgment and invites us to grow in discernment. He does not play favorites. And He proved this supremely by giving His own beloved Son, not withholding Him, so that justice and mercy could be satisfied in one person.

May we learn from David's downfall. May we judge correctly.
