# Matthew 5:38-42

**Title:** Justice and the Good Life
**Date:** 24 June 2018
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Understanding Retributive Justice

> "You have heard that it was said, 'Eye for eye, and tooth for tooth.' But I tell you, do not resist an evil person. If anyone slaps you on the right cheek, turn to them the other cheek also. And if anyone wants to sue you and take your shirt, hand over your coat as well. If anyone forces you to go one mile, go with them two miles. Give to the one who asks you, and do not turn away from the one who wants to borrow from you." (Matthew 5:38-42)

We instinctively understand justice. When someone wrongs us, we expect consequences. This principle of retributive justice—that crimes deserve proportional punishment—appears throughout Scripture, most famously expressed as "an eye for an eye." This comes from the Old Testament law known as *Lex Talionis* (the Law of Retaliation), found in Exodus 21:24, Leviticus 24:20, and Deuteronomy 19:21.

The phrase "eye for an eye" is more than mere retribution—it reflects a profound truth: crimes against others are not trivial, because each person is made in the image of God. When someone commits a crime, they create a debt that must be repaid. Without retributive justice, we descend into chaos where might is right, and only the powerful determine what is fair.

Consider Exodus 21:22-27. Here we see justice applied equally, even across power imbalances. An owner who injures a slave's eye or knocks out a tooth must set the slave free as compensation. Justice is blind, indifferent to wealth, status, or popularity. Whether the assailant is rich or poor, whether the victim is virtuous or flawed, what matters is the action and its consequence.

This biblical framework undergirds Western justice systems. In countries shaped by Hebrew law, honest business is possible. Elsewhere, corruption prevails—the powerful evade accountability while the weak suffer. Without retributive justice, we revert to the rule of the strong.

## Justice Has Limits

Yet "eye for an eye" was never meant to be literal or unlimited. It serves two critical purposes: it establishes *proportionality* and it sets *upper bounds* on retribution.

### Proportionality, Not Exactness

The principle does not demand identical harm. Exodus 21:22 shows premature birth compensated by *fine*, not physical injury. A slave struck in the eye receives *freedom*—genuine compensation for the victim. This was perhaps the first form of workers' compensation. A fine serves the victim better than striking back; it restores what was lost.

The point is not "exactly like the crime" but "punishment proportional to harm done." To interpret literally would be barbaric. If someone is violated through rape, responding with rape does not undo the crime—it compounds the evil. When a child is killed accidentally, murdering the perpetrator's child creates no justice, only more suffering. Such literal interpretations, as seen in some contemporary practices like acid-attack retaliation, represent barbarism, not justice.

### Proportionality Prevents Escalation

Ancient Near Eastern societies were plagued by blood feuds. If you killed my father, I killed you. Then your brother killed me. My brother killed your brother. Generations of families destroyed each other in endless cycles of escalation. The "eye for an eye" principle changed everything—it said, "One for one, then it ends." You cannot kill me over a knocked-out tooth. You cannot burn my whole field because I trampled your corn. The principle places a lid on vengeance.

This matters profoundly because wrongs against us are not mere transactions. When someone damages your car, it is *your* car, purchased through sacrifice and hard work. When someone steals from you, they steal your time, your life. When someone insults you, they wound your dignity. The emotional and moral weight of these injuries is real, and it tempts us toward excessive retaliation—"I demand ten back to teach you a lesson!" But the law restricts that impulse. It says: proportional, not boundless.

Justice on earth is always approximate. How do you calculate the full value of a life, an opportunity lost, or experiences stolen? Many things cannot be restored to their original state. Yet proportionality forces us to be measured, rational, and humane. Without it, we face the Mafia logic of endless revenge cycles, where retaliation leads only to deaths, cruelty, family dysfunction, and spiritual ruin.

Mahatma Gandhi understood this: "An eye for an eye makes the whole world go blind." Without proportional limits, justice becomes barbarism.

## Justice Allows for Mercy

But Jesus does something remarkable here. He does not contradict the law—he *fulfills* it. The phrase "But I tell you" (repeated throughout Matthew 5) means "let me complete what this law was really about."

> "You have heard that it was said, 'Eye for eye, and tooth for tooth.' But I tell you, do not resist an evil person."

The Greek term for "resist" (ἀνθίστημι) does not simply mean "do nothing about evil." It more likely means "do not resist *by evil means*." Paul elsewhere tells us to resist—to guard against false teachers, to resist the devil, to resist authorities when they demand evil. The Pharisees misinterpreted the law legalistically, turning it into a rigid tool of vengeance.

When someone offers you a backhanded slap (the insult of striking the right cheek), you could legally demand justice. But Jesus calls us to something deeper: mercy. In minor matters—insults, small injuries, personal slights—sometimes we must let them go. Not because justice is unimportant, but because there is *more to life than personal rights*.

The examples in verses 39-42 are significant:
- The slap to the right cheek is an insult, not a violent attack
- The lawsuit over undergarment and coat concerns property disputes
- The forced mile walk alludes to Roman soldiers commandeering labor
- Giving to those who ask involves voluntary generosity

These are the small things. Not murder. Not capital crimes. Not the defense of others. But the everyday infractions and insults that plague community life.

If we litigate every offense, we cannot live together. We would spend our lives calculating debts, keeping score, seeking revenge. Eventually, we become what the law meant to prevent—a cycle of escalation and bitterness. Instead, we need mercy in the small things.

This flows from something deeper: *God has shown mercy to you*. He did not give you what you deserved. So when someone wrongs you, why not put it on God's account? Rather than calculating your neighbor's debt to you, consider your debt to God. Instead of maintaining a meticulous ledger of offenses, recognize that you have been forgiven infinitely more.

## God's Justice and God's Mercy

God's justice is indeed retributive. As Creator, He gives the law and expects obedience. His laws are not arbitrary but designed for human flourishing, so that we treat one another rightly. And since each person bears God's image, a crime against another is ultimately a crime against God.

The punishment for rebellion against God is severe—eternal death, because the crime is infinitely evil. We collectively killed our Creator. The debt is infinite.

> "Shall not the Judge of all the earth deal justly?" (Genesis 18:25)
>
> "The wages of sin is death." (Romans 6:23)

Yet in Jesus, we see God's retributive justice *fulfilled through mercy*. Jesus himself embodied the teaching of Matthew 5. He was slapped and did not retaliate. His garments were taken. He was forced to carry His cross. He experienced everything He commanded His followers to endure. But He went further—He took upon Himself the infinite debt that we owed.

> "Do not resist by evil means," He said—and when arrested, He told Peter to put away his sword, though legions of angels stood ready.
>
> "If anyone strikes you on the right cheek, turn to them the other also"—and He was struck repeatedly, spat upon, flogged, yet did not strike back.
>
> "Give to the one who asks you"—and His very garments were divided by lot.
>
> "If anyone forces you to go one mile, go with them two miles"—and He walked to Golgotha carrying His cross.

God's justice required payment. Christ paid it. The debt we created through our crimes against God and against each other is now fully, finally, and infinitely satisfied by His death on the cross. We are pardoned.

This transforms how we live. Because we have been forgiven so much, we can afford to forgive the small things. It is acceptable to let that scratch go, that insult unanswered, that slight unforgiven. We can eat humble pie. We can extend grace.

## Wisdom in Application

The commands of the Sermon on the Mount are not meant to be applied legalistically. Rather, they reflect wisdom that guides us according to the principle of *love*, not vengeance or payback.

This gives us flexibility. The command not to resist evil does not mean we can never be police officers or defend the vulnerable. In some situations, love demands that we fight. In others, love demands that we yield. Sometimes self-sacrifice is right. Sometimes it would be wrong to allow abuse or bullying to continue unchecked.

Similarly, sometimes we enforce our rights; sometimes we release them. The principle is love. Do we give to every beggar? Wisdom asks: What good can this accomplish? But the default should be generosity, not suspicion.

Yes, we must choose which situations demand justice and which demand mercy. But this is not arbitrary picking and choosing—it flows from the principle of love. And it is guided by remembering that we ourselves have received infinite mercy from God.

There is no justice without retribution, no society without proportional consequences. But there is also no good life, no flourishing community, without mercy in the small things. Both are necessary. Both find their ultimate meaning in Jesus, who bore the full weight of God's just judgment so that we might receive unmerited grace.
