# Isaiah 23:1-18

**Title:** Yahweh Has No Allies
**Date:** 13 July 2008
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction: The Need for Allies

We all instinctively seek allies. In war, in business, at work and school—we look for those on our side, those who can protect us and cover our backs. It's natural to want support and security through partnerships and alliances.

But Isaiah presents a radically different picture. As we journey through this book, we discover that Yahweh needs no allies. In fact, He alone is the true King, and His rule extends over all nations.

## The Context: Israel's Troubled Kingdom

To understand this passage, we need to grasp Israel's precarious position. After the glory days of Solomon, the kingdom had fractured into two: the Northern Kingdom (Israel) and the Southern Kingdom (Judah, with Jerusalem as its capital). The unified nation that was meant to be the center of God's purpose had split, much like Australia and Tasmania using the same name for different territories.

During Isaiah's time—the reign of King Uzziah in the eighth century BC (791-740 BC)—there was a season of prosperity. Egypt had declined and was weak with internal strife. Assyria, though ambitious, was distracted by northern threats and succession questions. This gave Israel and Judah a window of opportunity. They recovered much of Solomon's former territory through military expansion and reaped rich economic rewards. It was a proud time of military success, political stability, and great prosperity.

But when Uzziah died, the situation changed dramatically. Five years before his death, in 745 BC, Tiglath-Pileser III rose to power in Assyria—an ambitious and capable ruler. He quickly secured Babylon and his northern border, then turned his attention westward toward Syria, Damascus, Israel, and Tyre. The balance of power shifted. Israel and Damascus formed an alliance and pressured King Ahaz to join them, but he refused and instead allied with Assyria, draining Judah's coffers in the process. Later, under Sennacherib, Assyria would lay siege to Jerusalem itself.

Judah faced constant threats to its prosperity, independence, and lifestyle. Where could they place their hope? The temptation was to seek security through alliances with the great powers of the day. And this is where Isaiah's prophecy about Tyre becomes so relevant.

## The Prophecy: The Humbling of Tyre (23:1-7)

Tyre was the superpower of its time—a Phoenician maritime empire, the merchant navy of the ancient Mediterranean. It was as dominant in its world as England was during the colonial era. Its traders and merchants were princes, renowned throughout the earth. Tyre was the bestower of crowns, the capital of a trading empire that seemed invincible.

> An oracle concerning Tyre:
> 
> Wail, O ships of Tarshish!
> For Tyre is destroyed
> and left without house or harbor.
> Word has come to them from the land of Cyprus.

The ships of Tarshish—large trading vessels that journeyed to the western Mediterranean and beyond—have nowhere to return to. Tyre is destroyed. The great trading center that enriched merchants and brought grain from the Nile, becoming the marketplace of nations, has fallen.

> Be silent, you people of the island,
> you merchants of Sidon, whom the seafarers have enriched.
> 
> On the great waters came the grain of the Shihor;
> the harvest of the Nile was Tyre's revenue,
> and she became the marketplace of the nations.

The vision is devastating. The fortress of the sea, which seemed impenetrable, speaks words of utter desolation:

> "I have neither been in labor nor given birth;
> I have neither reared sons nor brought up daughters."

As though Tyre never existed. As though it had no future, no legacy, no continuation. Even Egypt, hearing of Tyre's fall, is seized with anguish. There is no escape for the people:

> Cross over to Tarshish;
> wail, you people of the island.
> Is this your city of revelry,
> the old, old city,
> whose feet have taken her
> to settle in far-off lands?

Even escape to their far-flung colonies offers no refuge.

## Who Is Responsible? (23:8-14)

Isaiah poses the rhetorical question: Who planned this destruction? Who could bring down such a mighty power?

> Who planned this against Tyre,
> the bestower of crowns,
> whose merchants are princes,
> whose traders are renowned in the earth?

The answer is unmistakable:

> The LORD Almighty planned it,
> to bring low the pride of all glory
> and to humble all who are renowned on the earth.

It is Yahweh who humbles Tyre. Her power—built on trading, money, and influence—comes to nothing. Her wailing and silence are the instruments of God's judgment.

> The LORD has stretched out his hand over the sea
> and made its kingdoms tremble.
> He has given an order concerning Phoenicia
> that her fortresses be destroyed.

To illustrate the futility of trusting in human power, Isaiah points to Babylon:

> Look at the land of the Babylonians,
> this people that is now of no account!
> The Assyrians have made it a place for desert creatures;
> they raised up their siege towers,
> they stripped its fortresses bare
> and turned it into a ruin.

Though Babylon would rise to great power in later centuries, in Isaiah's time it was crushed repeatedly—by Sargon in 689 BC and by Sennacherib in 710 BC. Why, then, would Judah place hope in such kingdoms?

The passage returns to its refrain:

> Wail, you ships of Tarshish;
> your fortress is destroyed!

## Tyre's Postscript: Judgment and Restoration (23:15-18)

Yet the prophecy contains a surprising twist. God's judgment is not permanent annihilation:

> At that time Tyre will be forgotten for seventy years, the span of a king's life. But at the end of these seventy years, it will happen to Tyre as in the song of the prostitute:
> 
> "Take up a harp, walk through the city,
> O prostitute forgotten;
> play the harp well, sing many a song,
> so that you will be remembered."

After seventy years of exile—a number often representing a complete period or generation in Scripture—Tyre will return. But notice the imagery: she is compared to a prostitute, a woman who has lost her honor and must resort to her former trade to survive.

> At the end of seventy years, the LORD will deal with Tyre. She will return to her hire as a prostitute and will ply her trade with all the kingdoms on the face of the earth. Yet her profit and her earnings will be set apart for the LORD; they will not be stored up or hoarded. Her profits will go to those who live before the LORD, for abundant food and fine clothes.

This prophecy was literally fulfilled in the post-exilic period. When the Jewish people returned to rebuild the temple under Cyrus of Persia, Tyre supplied the materials:

> Then they gave money to the masons and carpenters, and gave food and drink and oil to the people of Sidon and Tyre, so that they would bring cedar logs by sea from Lebanon to Joppa. (Ezra 3:7)

Tyre's wealth would serve God's purposes. Even in restoration, she would not accumulate power for herself, but would become a tool in God's hands.

## Application: Who Should We Ally With?

### Judah's Temptation

Judah faced a critical decision. Surrounded by threats, tempted by the economic benefits of trade and alliance, the nation had to choose: Where would they place their trust?

Consider the allure of alliances with powers like Tyre. Trade requires partnerships. Partnerships demand compromise. To trade with the world's leading economy, a nation must often mute its convictions. America trades extensively with China, yet remains largely silent on human rights violations and territorial disputes. In our globalized world, compromises multiply: we listen to Japanese rock, watch Korean dramas on American platforms, eat from restaurants serving every cuisine imaginable.

For Judah, alliance with Tyre or Babylon would have meant compromising her distinctiveness as God's people. To participate in the economic systems of these nations would require following their gods, adopting their values, abandoning what made Israel special.

Isaiah's message was counter-cultural: Don't place your hope in earthly alliances. Yahweh is the ultimate King. These other nations, however mighty, are under His hand. Salvation is found in the Lord alone.

### Our Situation

The principle applies directly to us today. We live in a world of multiple competing systems, philosophies, and powers. The temptation to ally ourselves with worldly thinking is constant. But Christ calls us to a different allegiance.

Yet the question becomes practical: What does separation from the world actually look like?

#### Three Approaches—And Why Two Miss the Mark

**The Separatist Approach:** Some Christians interpret this to mean complete physical withdrawal. The Amish model comes to mind—finding a plot of land away from television, newspapers, and worldly influence. Others in evangelical circles fall into a milder version: a "Christian directory" of Christian plumbers, Christian restaurants, Christian bookstores. We surround ourselves with Christian products, Christian radio, Christian books, Christian concerts, Christian gifts. Everything labeled and baptized. But this is mere tokenism. A Christian teddy bear is just a teddy bear with a label.

**The Pluralistic Approach:** On the opposite extreme, some argue that asserting Christian truth is unloving. "Everyone is right in their own way. It's about love, not about who is correct." They compromise conviction for the sake of winning people through accommodation. But this approach abandons the very foundation of our faith.

#### The Ethical Approach: A Third Way

There is another path between these extremes. Christians are called to separate not *physically* (through seclusion) nor *politically* (through isolation), but *ethically*.

We must be clear: when we assert that Christianity is true, we necessarily assert that other religions, in their contradictory claims, are not fully true. We cannot shy away from this without abandoning intellectual honesty. But recognizing this does not mean we cannot work with non-Christians for the common good.

The apostle Peter explains this beautifully:

> But you are a chosen people, a royal priesthood, a holy nation, a people belonging to God, that you may declare the praises of him who called you out of darkness into his wonderful light. Once you were not a people, but now you are the people of God; once you had not received mercy, but now you have received mercy.
> 
> Dear friends, I urge you, as aliens and strangers in the world, to abstain from sinful desires, which war against your soul. Live such good lives among the pagans that, though they accuse you of doing wrong, they may see your good deeds and glorify God on the day he visits us. (1 Peter 2:9-12)

Notice the tension in Peter's message. He emphasizes that Christians are a holy people, a special priesthood, separated from the world. Yet immediately after, he tells us to live among pagans, to engage with rulers and masters, husbands and wives, doing good in society.

How does this work? The differentiation is not achieved through Christian labels, rituals, or activities. We don't differentiate ourselves by attending church, observing the Lord's Supper, or being baptized. We don't differentiate ourselves through the labels "Christian," "evangelical," or "Anglican."

We differentiate ourselves *ethically*. Negatively, we abstain from sinful desires. Positively, we do good deeds. It is by our behavior, our honesty, our integrity, our kindness—that the world sees we are different.

#### Practical Implications

You can ally yourself with non-Christians in politics, business, work, and school assignments. These are simply part of life, part of the normal interaction with the world. Neither inherently right nor wrong.

But what you *do* within those relationships matters deeply. Your behavior counts. Your ethics count. Will you ally with light or with darkness?

**At Work:** Will you ally with cheating, fraud, and dishonesty? Or with honesty, sincerity, and truthfulness? Does your work bear the mark of ethical darkness or ethical light?

As we complete another financial year and prepare our tax returns, we face a choice:

- Will I ally with darkness? "If I understate my income slightly here and overstate deductions there, the government won't chase me for such a small amount anyway. Everyone does it. No one gets hurt."
- Will I ally with light? "I will give to Caesar what is Caesar's. I will obey the authorities, whether Christian or not. I will trust that God honors my doing what is right, and that He will care for me."

**At School:** Will you ally with plagiarism, selfishness, and cutthroat competitiveness? Or with honesty, generosity, and kindness?

The student with the latest Microsoft Office and Adobe Photoshop will outshine you. But will you pirate software? "No one will know. The store owner already installed it when I sent my computer for repairs. No charge. I did nothing wrong."

But will you trust God? Will you trust that He honors integrity?

#### The Cost of Ethical Separation

Physical separation is easy—any coward with a fence and guns can do it. But ethical separation? That requires courage.

You may lose friendships. You may lose a job. You may lose a promotion. Drunkenness at celebrations—don't join in. Plagiarism in school—don't join in. Pirating software—don't join in. Dirty jokes, gossip, slander—don't join in. Bullying the weak—don't join in.

I was at a casino this week and noticed many Asians at the gambling tables. If gambling is a particular temptation in your culture, then I say to you plainly: *do not join them*.

The cost may be real. But remember: like ancient Judah, you may lose an ally, but you gain God—the One who is in control of all possible allies anyway.

## Conclusion

We all need allies. But our ultimate allegiance belongs to Jesus Christ, the King who came as a suffering servant and rose as the sovereign Lord. In Him, we have an ally who will never fall, never compromise, never fail.

Choose your alliances carefully. In matters of business, politics, and daily life, work with whom you must. But in matters of ethics, in matters of right and wrong, in matters of sin and righteousness—ally only with light. By your good deeds and holy living, let the world see the difference Christ makes.
