# Matthew 21:1-17

**Title:** Ready for the Son

**Date:** September 6, 2020

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Ready for the King (1-9)

Jesus enters Jerusalem in triumph, and Matthew presents this as a royal reception. The disciples bring a donkey and her colt, and the crowds spread their cloaks on the road while others cut branches from the trees. This appears strange to modern readers—we associate donkeys with humility, not majesty. But in ancient Israel, donkeys were the vehicle of kings and the elite.

Solomon rode a mule (belonging to his father David) to his coronation, and all the king's sons rode mules as a sign of their royal status. When Judges 10:4 and 12:14 mention judges with multiple sons who "rode donkeys," this indicates wealth and rulership. The donkey was Israel's animal of choice for leadership—Israelites were forbidden from importing horses (which came from Egypt, their enemy), and donkeys were most suited to Israel's mountainous terrain.

The spreading of cloaks mirrors an earlier event in Israel's history. When Jehu was anointed king by Elisha to destroy the kings of Samaria and Jezreel, the people quickly took their cloaks and spread them under him on the bare steps, then blew the trumpet and shouted, "Jehu is king!" It was a red carpet for royalty.

### The Crowds Acknowledge Him

> The crowds that went ahead of him and those that followed shouted, "Hosanna to the Son of David! Blessed is he who comes in the name of the Lord! Hosanna in the highest heaven!"

They cry out "Son of David" because Jesus is a descendant of the great King David, coming to restore the fortunes of Jerusalem—the city that David himself conquered centuries before. The crowds seem to be quoting Psalm 118, a psalm written when God's people faced enemies on every side and cried out for help: "O LORD, save us; O LORD, grant us success!" "Hosanna" means "save us"—God, please save us!

Notably, throughout Matthew's Gospel, people in need repeatedly call out "Son of David, have mercy on me." The blind men (9:27, 20:30), the Canaanite woman (15:22), the demon-possessed man (12:22), and now the blind and lame in the temple courts—all recognize in Jesus the fulfillment of God's promises. Jesus has come not just as a political conqueror, but as a healer and rescuer: rescuing them from the Romans, from injustice, from demons, and from sickness.

### A People Ready to Serve

Yet before the crowds ever cry out, something remarkable happens. Jesus approaches Jerusalem and tells his disciples:

> "Go to the village ahead of you, and at once you will find a donkey tied there, with her colt by her. Untie them and bring them to me. If anyone says anything to you, say that the Lord needs them, and he will send them right away."

This is strange—you don't simply take someone's donkey! But the people understood. They knew the prophecies. When someone came requisitioning their animal in the name of the Lord, they knew the king had finally arrived and they were ready to serve him.

It's like those movies where the police suddenly jump in front of your car. You brake to a halt, they come to your window and say, "FBI! I'm taking your car in pursuit of a fugitive." There's no hesitation. Your car has been commandeered by authority. The people of Bethphage understood the same principle: the king can requisition what he needs, and you obey without question.

**The Application:** God still requisitions us today. When he calls, will you say yes? When the opportunity comes, will you say, "Here I am"?

These opportunities often aren't the big decisions. They're the small ones: striking up a conversation with a stranger in a lift. As a physio, asking a patient about their faith. Posting a helpful Christian article in your neighborhood Facebook group. As a parent, setting down your phone when your child needs a nappy change.

Your week will look different from mine, but God calls many times a day. Are you ready to obey and work for the kingdom? Will you invite friends for coffee? Will you catch up with neighbors for local ministry?

## Not Ready for the King (10-13)

### The Crowd's Inadequate Answer

Yet even as Jerusalem cheers, something troubling emerges. When Jesus enters the city, it's stirred—but confused:

> When Jesus entered Jerusalem, the whole city was stirred and asked, "Who is this?" The crowds answered, "This is Jesus, the prophet from Nazareth in Galilee."

After all they've witnessed—the healings, the teachings—their answer reveals they haven't matured in understanding. Back in Matthew 16, Jesus asked his disciples, "Who do you say I am?" Some said John the Baptist, others Elijah or Jeremiah or one of the prophets. Now, the answer is the same. They may sing that Jesus is King to defeat their enemies, but do they accept him when he says the enemy is within their own hearts? Perhaps different groups exist in the crowd—some true followers, others merely swept up in excitement.

### The Temple's Corruption

More seriously, the temple itself is not ready for its king:

> Jesus entered the temple courts and drove out all who were buying and selling there. He overturned the tables of the money changers and the benches of those selling doves. "It is written," he said to them, "My house will be called a house of prayer, but you are making it a den of robbers."

The phrase "den of robbers" comes from Jeremiah, spoken when God's house had become a place of immorality and idolatry rather than true worship. Initially, the money changers and animal sellers served a practical purpose during the festival—pilgrims from across the Mediterranean needed to exchange currency and purchase animals for sacrifice without carrying them from home. But convenience became the main enterprise. God's house became a marketplace where business, partying, and profit mattered more than prayer.

Consider the Royal Easter Show—once purely agricultural, now commercialized with ferris wheels and showbags, where agriculture is merely a sideshow. So too the temple: the sacred was sidelined by commercial gain.

Solomon, when he dedicated the temple, prayed:

> "May your eyes be open toward this temple night and day, this place of which you said, 'My Name shall be there,' so that you will hear the prayer your servant prays toward this place. Hear the supplication of your servant and of your people Israel when they pray toward this place. Hear from heaven, your dwelling place, and when you hear, forgive."

The temple existed for prayer and forgiveness. Yet the people felt safe in their "den of robbers"—false security built on the ruin of true worship.

Jesus cleans it up. Remember Jehu? When he came to power, he destroyed the house of Baal in Samaria by assembling Baal worshipers under the pretense of a great sacrifice, then locking them inside and sending his men to slaughter them. He reduced the temple to rubble, fit only for a sewer. Jesus is the king who cleanses his house—and they were not ready for him.

**The Lesson:** Sometimes we fail to recognize who someone truly is. In those undercover boss television shows, the company's CEO pretends to be a new employee, observing how workers behave when they don't know who he is. They do dodgy things, leave messes, speak badly of their boss. Then he reveals himself: "I am your boss!" And he cleans up—restructuring, making changes, removing those who aren't ready.

**Are you ready for the king?** Some of you prepare for the HSC, organizing essays and buying supplies. Some prepare for a virus, stocking masks and sanitizer. Some prepare for a baby, clearing rooms and reading books. But are we also ready for the coming of the King?

The first time, he came on a donkey with no army, cleansing only the temple with a whip. But the second time, God will come as Jehu came to Samaria—with armies, with destruction, cleansing the whole earth. He'll ask what we've been doing with our lives. If he returns soon, perhaps he'll ask what we did during the great lockdown of 2020.

## The Son of David Rescuing the Outsiders (14-17)

### The Blind and Lame Healed

> The blind and the lame came to him at the temple, and he healed them. But when the chief priests and the teachers of the law saw the wonderful things he did and the children shouting in the temple courts, "Hosanna to the Son of David," they were indignant.

There's a powerful irony here rooted in Israel's history. When David conquered Jerusalem from the Jebusites, they taunted him: "You will not get in here; even the blind and the lame can ward you off." They thought David couldn't possibly breach their defenses. But David captured the fortress. Afterward, there arose a saying: "The blind and lame will not enter the palace"—the enemies defeated, excluded from the house of the king.

Yet now, the greater Son of David says something radically different: the blind and lame *will* enter. He heals them. He welcomes them. Why? Because this is no ordinary heir to David's throne.

When the Pharisees later asked Jesus, "What do you think about the Messiah? Whose son is he?" they answered, "The son of David." But Jesus challenged them:

> "How is it then that David, speaking by the Spirit, calls him Lord? The Lord said to my Lord: 'Sit at my right hand until I put your enemies under your feet.' If then David calls him Lord, how can he be his son?"

David himself recognized that this coming king would be greater than himself—not merely an heir to David's kingdom, but the Lord of all. This is the greater Son who has arrived: not just to conquer the Romans, but to conquer sin and death itself. He comes to rescue you from your spiritual blindness, to lift up those who can only limp toward God.

### The Children's Praise

The indignation of the chief priests and teachers of the law deepens when they see:

> The children shouting in the temple area, "Hosanna to the Son of David." "Do you hear what these children are saying?" they asked him. "Yes," replied Jesus, "have you never read, 'From the lips of children and infants you have ordained praise'?"

The children recognize him. The religious leaders do not. Yet Jesus validates their praise by quoting Psalm 8:2, which speaks of praise "ordained" by God "because of your enemies, to silence the foe and the avenger." The humble—the little ones, the servants—they enter the kingdom. The proud do not.

Only the little ones understand. Only they, with childlike humility, cry out "Hosanna, Son of David—have mercy on us! Save us!" And he does.

## Summary

Jesus enters Jerusalem as the promised king, riding a donkey as Zechariah foretold. The crowds recognize him as the Son of David, crying out for salvation. Yet three groups show they are not ready for him: the confused crowds who see him as merely a prophet; the corrupt temple system that has abandoned prayer for profit; and the religious authorities who reject him outright.

But the truly ready—the humble, the afflicted, the children—receive him. The blind and lame find healing. The little ones' praise is validated by God himself. In Jesus, God enters his world. Through the gospel, the king has entered Sydney and the world.

Will you be like the little children? Will you recognize your spiritual blindness and need? Will you cry out with humble faith: "Son of David, have mercy on us. Son of David, save us"?

The king has come. The question is: are you ready for him?
