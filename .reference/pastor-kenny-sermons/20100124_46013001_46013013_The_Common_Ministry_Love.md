# 1 Corinthians 13:1-13

**Title:** The Common Ministry: Love
**Date:** January 24, 2010
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Problem: Gifts Without Love Are Useless

Trivia is wonderful to know, but it's useless for 99.9% of your life. Just like the game Trivial Pursuit—fun at garage sales, but ultimately unpractical. The same is true of our spiritual gifts: they can be good, but without love, they become profoundly useless.

> If I speak in the tongues of men and of angels, but have **not love**, I am only a resounding gong or a clanging cymbal. If I have the gift of prophecy and can fathom all mysteries and all knowledge, and if I have a faith that can move mountains, but have **not love**, I am nothing. If I give all I possess to the poor and surrender my body to the flames, but have **not love**, I gain nothing. (1 Corinthians 13:1-3)

Paul is clear: without love, our gifts leave us empty. We are nothing. We gain nothing. The Greek word for "gain nothing" is *kerdaino*—literally "profitless," the opposite of common profit. Whatever gifts you exercise, without love they are rendered useless.

Think of it like an orchestra without a conductor. Talented musicians, beautiful instruments, but no harmony—just noise. The equation Paul presents is simple: **Gifts × Love = Profit**. Remove love from the equation, and you have multiplied by zero.

Your talents are good. Your time and effort is great. But if in the end it is not done with love, then it's useless.

## What Is This Love? (1 Corinthians 13:4-7)

> Love is patient, love is kind. It does not envy, it does not boast, it is not proud. It is not rude, it is not self-seeking, it is not easily angered, it keeps no record of wrongs. Love does not delight in evil but rejoices with the truth. It always protects, always trusts, always hopes, always perseveres.

This is the love that must motivate our service. Paul describes a love that:

- **Is patient and kind**: overcomes relational difficulties and disconnection
- **Is free from envy, boasting, and pride**: rejects the individualism that poisons community
- **Is not rude or self-seeking**: serves others' interests before our own
- **Does not easily anger or keep records of wrongs**: extends forgiveness and moves forward
- **Rejoices in truth**: aligns itself with what is right and true
- **Always protects, trusts, hopes, and perseveres**: sustains us through difficulty

This is what we need to bring to our service of one another. Love provides the proper focus for our gifts and talents. It determines the motivation by which we judge our activity.

We do this not because we know love in theory, but because Jesus has loved us first. Read Paul's list again and see Jesus there—patient, kind, not self-seeking, bearing all things, enduring all things. His love becomes the pattern for ours.

## Love in Action: Three Corinthian Examples

### 1 Corinthians 8: The Stumbling Brother

The issue: Some Corinthians had knowledge that food sacrificed to idols had no real power. They were right—an idol is just wood, with no spiritual authority. But not everyone possessed this knowledge.

> Be careful, however, that the exercise of your freedom does not become a stumbling block to the weak. (1 Corinthians 8:9)

Knowledge puffs up; love builds up. The Corinthians with mature understanding could eat such food freely, but their freedom became destructive when it caused weaker believers to stumble in their conscience. Rather than defend their rights, Paul calls them to patience and kindness—to seek the good of the other person, not their own vindication.

The solution: Limit your freedom out of concern for your brother. If eating meat causes someone to fall, don't eat meat. That's love applied.

### 1 Corinthians 11: The Lord's Supper

> Whoever eats the bread or drinks the cup of the Lord in an unworthy manner will be guilty of sinning against the body and blood of the Lord... When you come together to eat, wait for each other. If anyone is hungry, he should eat at home, so that when you meet together it may not result in judgment. (1 Corinthians 11:27-34)

The Corinthians were partaking of communion in ways that shamed the poor and divided the body. Some ate their own food while others went hungry. Paul's remedy: wait for each other. Consider your brother's dignity. Every activity—what you eat, when you eat it, what you wear, what you say, what beverages you serve, what games you play—must be judged by the standard of love.

### 1 Corinthians 12: Serving at Church

In our own context, every ministry and service opportunity must be guided by love. We often hear about the "FAT" criteria for service: Faithful, Available, Teachable. These are good, but they're insufficient. I call it **LOVEFAT**—love must come first. A person can be faithful, available, and teachable, but if they serve without love, the result is useless.

## The Question: Why Don't We Believe This?

### Objection 1: "Why Else Would God Give Me This Gift?"

We assume that receiving a gift is a directive to use it. But gifts are tools, not automatic job assignments. Consider academic talent. A student with a high ATAR score doesn't automatically belong in medicine or law. That score is a tool. What you do with it depends on your goal—earning money, pursuing a fulfilling career, helping society, serving others. The tool itself is neutral. It's the purpose that determines the use.

Similarly, don't say, "I must use this gift because I have it." Instead, ask: "What is God calling me to do with this tool?" And if you decide to use it out of love, it is never wasted—even if you apply your abilities below what you're capable of.

For young people especially: "Because I can" is not a sufficient reason for your choices in life.

### Objection 2: "I Have No Gifts to Exercise"

Look around. There is always a need to love. If you don't know where to serve, start by seeing the love needs around you: Who needs care? Who needs to hear the gospel? Who needs encouragement? Who needs someone to listen?

But more importantly, we all have gifts. You have a mouth—speak God's truth to each other. You have legs—walk to someone who's alone. You have a memory—remember others' needs and pray for them. You have eyes—see who's sitting by themselves or rushing away in fear. You have arms—lift chairs, move garbage bins, give hugs, hold someone. You have hands—scribble a note or send an SMS saying "keep following Jesus."

You have a driver's license to give people rides. You have internet access to email someone "youth group is on this week—would you like to come?" You have a wallet to give generously. You have a home to host a dinner party. You have a mobile phone to pray on.

Stop talking about "gifts" as though you need some mysterious spiritual ability. Look for **opportunities**—places where genuine needs exist that you can meet. An opportunity is a gift that actually produces common good and profit. The question isn't "What am I good at?" but "Who needs love, and can I meet that need?"

There's a tendency to focus on the glamorous upfront things—the one percent of church activity that gets attention. We can't all lead or sing. But that's okay. The body needs hands, feet, eyes, and ears far more than it needs more mouths. The problem with some gift seminars and conferences is they start in the wrong place: they focus on what looks impressive and feels good, rather than on what the body actually needs.

And often they start in the wrong location. You want to know your gifts? Come here. Look at the actual people around you. That's where you learn to serve. Not in some conference out in the bushland theorizing about hypothetical people—but right here, noticing the person sitting next to you.

**The key decision: Choose to love first. Your gifts will follow.**

## Why Love Matters More Than Gifts

### Love Never Fails (1 Corinthians 13:8)

> Love never fails. But where there are prophecies, they will cease; where there are tongues, they will be stilled; where there is knowledge, it will pass away. (1 Corinthians 13:8)

One day, prophecies will be gone. Tongues will cease. Knowledge will pass away. All the gifts that dazzle us now will eventually end. But love never fails. Love is permanent.

When things get hard—when service becomes boring, when the novelty wears off, when you're packing up chairs alone or serving without applause—the shallow reasons for serving will vanish. If you're serving for fun, the work will drain that joy. If you're serving to pad your resume, you'll abandon it for something more prestigious. If you're serving to be with cool people, you'll leave when the group loses interest. If you're serving for self-esteem, the criticism and setbacks will crush that motivation.

**Only love lasts.** Love keeps you serving the lonely person when the group has moved on. Love makes you stick with a tedious ministry because people need it. Love sustains you when there's no recognition. Love is the primary motivator when things get hard.

### Love Produces Lasting Fruit (1 Corinthians 13:11-13)

> When I was a child, I talked like a child, I thought like a child, I reasoned like a child. When I became a man, I put childish ways behind me. Now we see but a poor reflection as in a mirror; then we shall see face to face. Now I know in part; then I shall know fully, even as I am fully known. And now these three remain: faith, hope and love. But the greatest of these is love. (1 Corinthians 13:11-13)

One day this church will disappear. You'll forget what you learned here. People won't remember your deeds. But if you did them out of genuine concern for others—if you were not self-seeking, not easily angered, not keeping records of wrongs—then love will count.

True spirituality isn't measured by how talented you are or how wonderful your ministry grows (though we do hope it grows). It's measured by whether it flows from love. God knows your motivation. He sees whether you're serving out of love or out of ambition.

When is a gift not truly a gift? When it is not wrapped in love.

## A Call to Decision

We live in an age of choices—what to study, what career to pursue, what to do with our time. Some call it the "tyranny of choice." But as a friend once told me, "The good is the enemy of the best."

You'll have many good opportunities. Many of them are genuinely valuable. But some of those good things will keep you from the best thing: loving others. Don't trivialize your opportunities by spending them on things merely good rather than things motivated by love.

**If you're burning out in a ministry, or if you're no longer doing it out of love, please come talk to me or another leader.** Service without love ultimately destroys both you and the body. But service flowing from genuine love for Christ and His people? That's the most important work you can do.

Love is not a trivial pursuit. It's the very heartbeat of what it means to be spiritual.
