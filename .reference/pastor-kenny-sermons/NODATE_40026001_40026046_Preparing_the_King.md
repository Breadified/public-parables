# Matthew 26:1-46

**Title:** Preparing the King

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Big Idea

Jesus prepares himself to die for our sakes.

---

## 1. A Woman Preparing Jesus (26:6-13)

While Jesus was in Bethany at the home of Simon the Leper, a woman came to him with an alabaster jar of very expensive perfume and poured it on his head as he reclined at the table. In that culture, perfume at a banquet was customary—a practical courtesy in a time before air conditioning when water was scarce and bathing was infrequent. It was similar to the way a restaurant might offer a fresh towel before the meal.

But the disciples were indignant at what they saw as waste:

> "Why this waste? This perfume could have been sold at a high price and the money given to the poor." (26:8-9)

The word "waste" means destruction or utter ruin. Their objection was rooted in opportunity cost—why spend on mere aesthetics when resources could feed the hungry? On the surface, this sounds right. But Jesus saw something deeper:

> "Why are you bothering this woman? She has done a beautiful thing to me. The poor you will always have with you, but you will not always have me." (26:10-11)

Jesus recognized that some moments transcend ordinary economic logic. There are times when relationship and presence matter more than practical good works. He then revealed the true meaning of her act:

> "When she poured this perfume on my body, she did it to prepare me for burial." (26:12)

### Understanding Jewish Burial Practices

Jewish burial customs differed significantly from Egyptian embalming. Rather than preserving the body, the Jews allowed it to decompose in a tomb. After time, the family would return, collect the bones, and place them in small stone boxes called ossuaries, stored in niches to make room for future burials. This reflected their confidence in resurrection—not concern for preserving the physical remains, since God could "bring life out of dry bones" (Ezekiel 37:5).

So the perfume was not for embalming but for **mourning**. In the Middle Eastern heat, decomposition created odors that made prolonged grieving difficult. The perfume allowed mourners to spend more time with the deceased—days or even weeks—to properly say goodbye, unlike modern Western funerals that conclude in hours.

When Mary and the other women went to Jesus's tomb on Easter Sunday, they brought spices for this very reason. They intended to spend time mourning and needed the perfume to make it bearable (Luke 24:1).

### The Deeper Meaning: Anointing as Kingship

Why did Jesus accept this preparation before his death, not after? The answer lies in a second layer of meaning. Pouring oil on someone's head—**anointing**—was the ritual that made kings. Saul was anointed. David was anointed. This woman's act was recognizing and honoring Jesus as king.

But there was a paradox. Look at Psalm 23:

> "You prepare a table before me in the presence of my enemies. You anoint my head with oil; my cup overflows." (23:5)

David, the anointed king, walks through the valley of death yet fears no evil because God is with him. Now consider Jesus:

- **Like David:** Head anointed with oil before a table
- **Unlike David:** The anointing is for burial, not coronation

The path to Jesus's kingship runs through sacrificial death. His role as king is to die to save his subjects. Jesus's death seals his kingship over all, and through resurrection, he conquers even death itself.

This is the gospel—the good news. Death and victory together in one act. Lordship and sacrificial love combined in a single, redemptive gesture.

So Jesus said of this woman's deed:

> "Truly I tell you, wherever this gospel is preached throughout the world, what she has done will also be told, in memory of her." (26:13)

## 2. Judas Preparing Jesus (26:14-16)

Meanwhile, someone else was also preparing Jesus for death. One of the Twelve—Judas Iscariot—went to the chief priests and asked:

> "What are you willing to give me if I deliver him over to you?" (26:15)

They counted out thirty pieces of silver.

Judas, one of the inner circle, now prepared for Jesus's death in a very different way—through betrayal.

### The Price of Betrayal

Thirty pieces of silver. What did this amount represent?

According to Exodus 21:32, thirty shekels was the compensation owed if a bull gored a slave—the price of a slave's life. Joseph was sold into slavery for the same sum. In Zechariah 11:12-13, a shepherd rejected by his people receives thirty pieces of silver as his wage, then throws it to the potter—a passage Matthew sees fulfilled in Judas's action.

Thirty pieces of silver was enough to buy a small piece of land. Yet it was also the valuation of a slave.

### The Contrast

Compare the two preparations:

**The Woman:** Poured expensive perfume, gave lavishly, prepared Jesus for honorable burial, recognized him as king

**Judas:** Sold Jesus for thirty pieces of silver, prepared him for shameful death, betrayed trust for money

One prepared Jesus's death in love and faith. The other in greed and betrayal. The disciples should have been willing to die for Jesus. Instead, Judas sold him out. The price of Christ became the price of betrayal.

---

## 3. The Disciples Unprepared (26:30-35, 36-46)

But Judas was not alone in his failure to prepare. Jesus told all his disciples:

> "This very night you will all fall away on account of me, for it is written: 'I will strike the shepherd, and the sheep of the flock will be scattered.'" (26:31)

Even Peter protested:

> "Even if all fall away on account of you, I never will." (26:33)

Jesus's response was sobering:

> "This very night, before the rooster crows, you will disown me three times." (26:34)

Peter doubled down: "Even if I have to die with you, I will never disown you." All the other disciples said the same.

Yet when Jesus went to Gethsemane and asked them to watch and pray with him—to support him through his darkest hour—they fell asleep. Three times he returned and found them sleeping, their eyes heavy. They could not even stay awake for one hour.

### A Realistic Look at Sin

This is not mere criticism of the disciples. Jesus was preparing them—and us—for the reality of human weakness. We all fall short. Like a pianist trying to play perfectly for hours without a single wrong note to gain access to God, the task is impossible. We underestimate our capacity to sin.

The subtle perversity of sin is this: we abstain from doing evil and congratulate ourselves, forgetting all the good we've neglected to do. We are deluded into thinking our small acts of goodness somehow balance the evil we harbor in our hearts each day.

We have all fallen away. We will fall away. Sin is serious. Jesus knew this. He understood that sin and judgment demanded something extraordinary—his death.

---

## 4. Jesus Preparing Himself (26:36-46)

Only Jesus could prepare for what lay ahead. In Gethsemane, he took Peter and the two sons of Zebedee and became deeply sorrowful:

> "My soul is overwhelmed with sorrow to the point of death. Stay here and keep watch with me." (26:38)

Then, falling on his face, he prayed:

> "My Father, if it is possible, may this cup be taken from me. Yet not as I will, but as you will." (26:39)

He returned a second time with the same prayer:

> "My Father, if it is not possible for this cup to be taken away unless I drink it, may your will be done." (26:42)

### The Meaning of the Cup

Jesus faced the reality of death without flinching. He did not want to die. He did not want the pain. More than that, he did not want to face the wrath of God—for the "cup" was not merely physical suffering but the judgment of God poured out upon sin.

Jesus would bear not just individual judgment but the accumulated wrath of God against the sin of the world. The anger God holds toward all human rebellion would be laid upon him alone.

Yet he submitted: "Not my will, but yours be done."

### The Solitary Resolution

Jesus realized he could not rely on anyone:

- Not his disciples, who were asleep and unprepared
- Not Judas, who would betray him
- Not the woman, whose beautiful act, though meaningful, could not save him
- Not his own preference to avoid death

No one was prepared to die. Jesus alone could do what only God could do: face the fire alone to protect us from judgment.

When the hour came, he rose and said to his disciples:

> "Are you still sleeping and resting? Look, the hour has come, and the Son of Man is delivered into the hands of sinners. Rise! Let us go! Here comes my betrayer!" (26:45-46)

---

## Conclusion

This is the Jesus we follow. Not a coward hiding in fear, but a king who walked consciously toward the cross because he loved us. He prepared his mind and heart for burial, not as defeat but as victory—the ultimate expression of sacrificial love.

His death and resurrection redeem all our failures, all our fallenness, all our inability to stand before God on our own merit. In his preparation for death, we find our preparation for life with him forever.
