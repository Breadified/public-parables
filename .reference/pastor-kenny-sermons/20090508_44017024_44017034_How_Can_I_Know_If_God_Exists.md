# Acts 17:24-34

**Title:** How Can I Know If God Exists?

**Date:** May 8, 2009

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Introduction

Among the biggest questions people ask about God and religion are: Why does suffering exist? Does God exist? Is the Bible true? Over these next two weeks, we'll explore two of these questions. This week, we tackle the foundational question: How can I know God exists?

But I must start with what seems like bad news: We cannot know God—at least not on our own.

---

## 1. We Cannot Know God (v.24)

> The God who made the world and everything in it is the Lord of heaven and earth and does not live in temples built by hands. And he is not served by human hands, as if he needed anything, because he himself gives all men life and breath and everything else.

From these verses, we learn that God:
- **Made the world** — He is the Creator
- **Does not live in temples** — He transcends human structures
- **Needs nothing** — He is self-sufficient; instead, He gives life and breath to all things
- **Is not made** — He is not gold, silver, or stone; not fashioned by human hands

The crucial insight is this: **God is transcendent.** He exists outside and beyond this world. And here's the problem: those of us who are inside the world cannot know what exists outside the world. Our perspective is limited. We're looking from within creation trying to understand the Creator—it's impossible by our own efforts.

### The Illustration: The Gods Must Be Crazy

Imagine a Bushman in the Kalahari Desert in 1980 who suddenly encounters a Coca-Cola bottle dropped from a plane. He's never seen one before. The bottle's true purpose—to store a black sugary liquid—is completely unknowable to him. He has no framework to understand it. He can only guess at its function.

In the same way, we cannot know God through creation alone. Our attempts are always just stabs in the dark.

### The Evidence of Our Blindness

Notice how Paul himself acknowledges this limitation in verse 27: God reveals Himself so that men "perhaps reach out for him and find him." That word "perhaps" is telling. Without God's help, our search is uncertain.

And look where those efforts have led throughout history: to idols, to gods made of gold and silver, to altars dedicated to "An Unknown God." We are spiritually blind without revelation.

---

## 2. We Can Know God (v.31)

> For he has set a day when he will judge the world with justice by the man he has appointed. He has given proof of this to all men by raising him from the dead.

Here is the turning point. God has not left us to ourselves in our blindness. Instead, He has done something extraordinary: He has crossed the boundary. The One who exists outside the world has entered into the world—in history, at a specific time and place—so that we can know Him.

That "man he has appointed" is Jesus of Nazareth. This is history. If you had been alive then, you could have seen God incarnate, heard His voice, witnessed His resurrection. But even if we weren't there, we're not at a disadvantage. We have something better: the testimony of eyewitnesses recorded in the Gospels. These are not myths or legends—they are historical accounts written by people who were there.

The proof God has given is the resurrection. This is the event that changed everything and validates everything Jesus claimed and taught.

---

## 3. Addressing Objections

### "If God Exists, Why Doesn't He Just Appear to Me?"

I hear this objection often: "If God is real, why doesn't He just show Himself to me right now, in my bedroom? Then I'd believe. I'd be so awe-struck I couldn't deny it."

Here are three responses:

#### First: This is an Unfair Request

Remember, God came into the world precisely because we cannot know Him as He truly is. He is infinite; we are finite. He had to *accommodate* to us—to limit Himself, to become human—in order to reveal Himself in a way we could understand.

But being human has real constraints. A human cannot be in two places at once. If Jesus was born in Bethlehem, He wasn't born in Korea. If He was teaching in Palestine, He couldn't simultaneously be in Sri Lanka. Now, if He is in heaven, He cannot appear at whim in Sydney or wherever you happen to be.

Yet we use this very limitation against Him! We ask Him to prove His divinity by violating the human nature He took on for our sake. That's not a fair request.

#### Second: God Sets the Terms, Not Us

We need to be honest about what we're really asking when we make such demands. What attitude underlies it? We're essentially asking God to be at our beck and call, to prove Himself on our timeline, according to our conditions.

But God is not our servant. He is not an idol made with hands that we can manipulate. Jesus is not a genie in a bottle that we summon to appear at our convenience and do our bidding. God has set His terms of engagement with us through the historical Jesus. If He is truly God, we must accept those terms and follow them—not dictate our own conditions.

#### Third: God Appearing Doesn't Actually Change Hearts

History shows us that direct appearances from God don't necessarily produce faith. Throughout the Old Testament, we have examples of people to whom God appeared directly—and they ignored Him anyway. A spectacular display won't convince a hardened heart.

### The Illustration: The Demanding Child

I have a little daughter who just turned three. She exemplifies what I mean. When I offer her cereal for breakfast, she says, "No, I want toast." When I give her toast, she demands margarine. When I add a little margarine, she insists on lots. And when I finally give her exactly what she demanded, she says, "No, I want cereal."

This is whingeing—constant complaining and changing demands no matter what you offer.

We can be the same way with God. We demand proof on our terms, then if it comes in a different form than expected, we reject it. Or we keep moving the goalposts: "Not that kind of proof—give me this kind instead."

---

## The Resolution

The historical Jesus is enough. He is sufficient proof that God exists and that God loves us. In Jesus, we can know:
- That God exists
- What God is like
- What God wants from us
- What God loves and what God hates
- That God loves us so much He died to take the punishment for our sins

We have everything we need to know God. The question is not whether God has given us enough evidence—He has. The question is whether we will accept it or remain unreasonable skeptics.

---

## The Response (v.32)

> When they heard about the resurrection of the dead, some of them sneered, but others said, "We want to hear you again on this subject."

Notice the two responses. Some sneered—they rejected the message immediately. But others said, "We want to hear more about this."

Which response will be yours? Will you dismiss the claims about Jesus, or will you investigate further? This term, I encourage you to genuinely seek out more about Jesus and His resurrection. Find out for yourself whether He is who He claimed to be.
