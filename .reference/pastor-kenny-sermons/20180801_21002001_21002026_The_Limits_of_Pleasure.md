# Ecclesiastes 2:1-26

**Title:** The Limits of Pleasure  
**Date:** 21 August 2018  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction

We're exploring an ancient wisdom experiment from three thousand years ago. King Solomon conducted a deliberate test: Can true satisfaction be found in pleasure? He's uniquely positioned to answer this question—the smartest, richest, and most powerful person in the world. His conclusion reveals something we all need to hear.

---

## Testing the Limits of Pleasure

> I said in my heart, "Come now, I will test you with pleasure; enjoy yourself." But behold, this also was vanity. I said of laughter, "It is mad," and of pleasure, "What use is it?" (Ecclesiastes 2:1-2)

Solomon sets up his experiment with remarkable honesty. He gives us his conclusion upfront before showing us what he actually did. Notice his emotional state—perhaps curious, perhaps bored, perhaps depressed. He decides to test whether his heart can find satisfaction through pleasure.

### 1. Wine and Substances

> I searched with my heart how to cheer my body with wine—my heart still guiding me with wisdom—and how to lay hold on folly, till I might see what was good for the children of man to do under heaven during the few days of their life. (Ecclesiastes 2:3)

Solomon begins with wine and mind-altering substances. There's an interesting phrase here: "my heart still guiding me with wisdom." He appears to stand apart from the experiment, observing himself objectively, trying to understand what brings pleasure to the human heart.

### 2. Great Achievements and Projects

> I made great works. I built houses and planted vineyards for myself. I made myself gardens and parks, and planted in them all kinds of fruit trees. I made myself pools from which to water the forest of growing trees. (Ecclesiastes 2:4-6)

Solomon pursues tangible accomplishments. He builds—houses, vineyards, gardens, irrigation systems. There's something satisfying about building because you can see the results. These projects flow into one another: houses need fruit, fruit trees need water, water needs pools.

We do this too. University degrees, career achievements, hobbies that cascade into larger projects—crochet becoming K-pop pursuits, bicycles becoming photography ventures. We think these accomplishments will bring lasting pleasure.

### 3. Power and Control

> I bought male and female slaves, and had slaves who were born in my house. (Ecclesiastes 2:7)

Solomon assembled 153,600 workers to build the temple—the ultimate expression of control. There's a famous insight from philosopher Søren Kierkegaard: *"If I had a servant in my employ, who when I asked him for a cup of cold water, brought instead the world's costliest wines blended in a chalice, I would dismiss him, for true pleasure consists not in getting my wine but in getting my way."*

The deepest pleasure of power isn't what you get—it's getting your way.

### 4. Possessions

> I had also great possessions of herds and flocks, more than any who had been before me in Jerusalem. (Ecclesiastes 2:7b)

Herds and flocks meant food, transportation, wealth—the material abundance of his time. Consider the Sultan of Brunei, who owns 7,000 cars: 574 Mercedes-Benzes, 452 Ferraris, 382 Bentleys, 209 BMWs, and 179 Jaguars. If he drove a different car every day, it would take 19 years to cycle through his collection.

Yet the pursuit still leaves him empty.

### 5. Wealth and Entertainment

> I also gathered for myself silver and gold and the treasure of kings and provinces. I got singers, both men and women. (Ecclesiastes 2:8)

All the gold and cultural refinements money can buy. Entertainment, beauty, artistic expression—the finest pleasures civilization has to offer.

### 6. Sexual Pleasure

> ...and many concubines, the delight of the sons of man. (Ecclesiastes 2:8b)

> Now King Solomon loved many foreign women, along with the daughter of Pharaoh: Moabite, Ammonite, Edomite, Sidonian, and Hittite women... Solomon clung to these in love. He had 700 wives, who were princesses, and 300 concubines. (1 Kings 11:1-3)

His wives represented political alliances, children, and dynasty. His concubines—300 women—represented the pursuit of sexual pleasure itself. The ultimate harem. The conquest. Surely this would make him happy? One new partner every day, year-round. Sexual satisfaction at its peak.

## The Verdict: Complete Emptiness

> So I became great and surpassed all who were before me in Jerusalem. Also my wisdom remained with me. And whatever my eyes desired I did not keep from them. I kept my heart from no pleasure. (Ecclesiastes 2:9-10)

Solomon held nothing back. He combined the wealth of Jeff Bezos, the power of Donald Trump, the pleasure-seeking of Hugh Hefner, and the strategic brilliance of Warren Buffett—and still pursued every desire.

But then:

> Then I considered all that my hands had done and the toil I had expended in doing it, and behold, all was vanity and a striving after wind, and there was nothing to be gained under the sun. (Ecclesiastes 2:11)

After everything—wine, girls, Ferraris, absolute power—Solomon wakes up and declares: *Vanity. Ephemeral. Fleeting.*

The truth he discovered: **Pleasure is momentary, like chasing wind. It cannot be kept. It cannot be permanent. Yet we pursue it to death.**

Look at those who have achieved these things. Hollywood celebrities with fame and fortune. Brad Pitt and Jennifer Aniston, then Angelina Jolie and Brad Pitt—always needing more. The most beautiful, most sexually accomplished people often end in misery.

> Then desire when it has conceived gives birth to sin, and sin when it is fully grown brings forth death. (James 1:15)

This is the pattern: Pleasure → Pain → Death

---

## The Paradox: What We're Actually Doing

Yet we know this is true. We see it everywhere. And still:

**Why are you at university?** "I want a good job."

**Why?** "I want to earn money."

**Why?** "So I can buy things."

**Why?** "So I can be happy."

We pursue holidays, food, experiences—searching for moments that will satisfy us. We try to preserve these experiences with photographs we never look at again. We desperately capture moments at places like Wedding Cake Rock, trying to keep something that is impossible to hold.

You cannot keep experiences. They are momentary. They don't ultimately satisfy. Yet advertising and our culture scream that they will.

---

## The Path to Meaning: Thanksgiving and Contentment

But this doesn't mean pleasure is bad:

> There is nothing better for a person than that he should eat and drink and find enjoyment in his toil. This also, I saw, is from the hand of God. For apart from him who can eat or who can have enjoyment? For to the one who pleases him God has given wisdom and knowledge and joy, but to the sinner he has given the business of gathering and collecting, only to give to one who pleases God. (Ecclesiastes 2:24-26)

Solomon identifies the crucial difference: **Enjoyment received as a gift from God, with gratitude**, versus **pleasure pursued as the ultimate goal**.

God's wisdom offers us something different:

**Contentment.** Paul writes:

> But godliness with contentment is great gain. For we brought nothing into the world, and we can take nothing out of it. But if we have food and clothing, we will be content with that. (1 Timothy 6:6-8)

Jesus taught this same truth:

> Watch out! Be on your guard against all kinds of greed; life does not consist in an abundance of possessions. (Luke 12:15)

He told the parable of the rich fool who built bigger barns to store his surplus, thinking, *"You have plenty of grain laid up for many years. Take life easy; eat, drink and be merry."* But God said to him: *"You fool! This very night your life will be demanded from you."* (Luke 12:18-20)

The antidote to chasing pleasure is receiving with thanksgiving:

> For everything created by God is good, and nothing is to be rejected if it is received with thanksgiving, for it is made holy by the word of God and prayer. (1 Timothy 4:4-5)

---

## Conclusion

Your life is an experiment that cannot be repeated. You get one chance to answer the question: Will I build my life on fleeting pleasure, or on something that actually lasts?

Solomon learned that pleasure, by itself, is vanity—empty wind that cannot satisfy. True joy comes only when we receive God's gifts with gratitude, pursue contentment over accumulation, and build our lives on what pleases Him rather than ourselves.
