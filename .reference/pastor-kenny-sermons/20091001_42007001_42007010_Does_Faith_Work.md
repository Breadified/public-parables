# Luke 7:1-10

**Title:** Does Faith Work?
**Date:** 4 October 2009
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction

We're beginning a new series on Christianity this October. Today's message asks a fundamental question: What is faith, and does it actually work?

If you're a Christian or know Christian friends, you hear about faith constantly. That's not surprising—the Christian faith centers entirely on faith. As Paul writes in Romans:

> For in the gospel a righteousness from God is revealed, a righteousness that is by faith from first to last, just as it is written: "The righteous will live by faith." (Romans 1:17)

But what exactly *is* faith? Many definitions exist. Some say faith is "confident belief or trust in the truth or trustworthiness of a person, idea, or thing." Others claim faith is "believing when it is beyond the power of reason to believe"—suggesting faith means trusting in something irrational, something despite the odds.

I believe both those definitions are wrong. Let's see what Jesus himself reveals about faith through this passage.

## The Story: A Centurion's Desperate Need

Jesus had just finished teaching the crowds and performing miracles. He entered Capernaum, a town on the edge of the Sea of Galilee—strategically located at a major crossroads and occupied by Roman forces.

> There a centurion's servant, whom his master valued highly, was sick and about to die. (Luke 7:2)

### 1. A Centurion Hears (Verse 3)

> The centurion heard of Jesus and sent some elders of the Jews to him, asking him to come and heal his servant. (Luke 7:3)

A centurion was no ordinary soldier. The Roman military was rigidly structured: a legion consisted of 10 cohorts, each cohort of 6 centuries, and each century of 80 men under a centurion's command. A centurion earned roughly 65 times what a common soldier made. He was high in the chain of command—a man of authority and means.

Remarkably, this centurion hasn't even met Jesus. Yet Jesus's reputation has preceded him. His miracles and teaching have spread throughout the region. Desperate to save his valued servant, the centurion takes the proper approach: he sends Jewish elders to address Jesus respectfully, as a rabbi of standing.

### 2. He Deserves It (Verses 4-5)

> When they came to Jesus, they pleaded earnestly with him, "This man deserves to have you do this, because he loves our nation and has built our synagogue." (Luke 7:4-5)

The elders make their case: the centurion is a good man. Though Jews and Romans typically didn't get along—Jews resented the occupation and the pagan gods Rome imposed—this centurion had been different. He'd funded the local synagogue, supporting Jewish worship and culture. He deserved Jesus's help.

This reasoning feels familiar. We often grant favors to the wealthy or accomplished: a philanthropist receives special treatment because they've donated generously; a celebrity faces reduced consequences because of their artistic contributions. The logic is simple: good deeds earn rewards.

### 3. I Do Not Deserve It (Verses 6-8)

But then something remarkable happens:

> So Jesus went with them. He was not far from the house when the centurion sent friends to say to him: "Lord, don't trouble yourself, for I do not deserve to have you come under my roof. That is why I did not even consider myself worthy to come to you. But say the word, and my servant will be healed. For I myself am a man under authority, with soldiers under me. I tell this one, 'Go,' and he goes; and that one, 'Come,' and he comes. I say to my servant, 'Do this,' and he does it." (Luke 7:6-8)

The centurion stops Jesus before he reaches the house. "Don't come," he says. "I'm not worthy of that honor."

Here's the contrast: the elders said the centurion *deserved* Jesus's help. The centurion himself says he *doesn't*. Despite his wealth, power, and generosity, he recognizes his true position before Jesus. He calls Jesus "Lord"—not as a polite title, but acknowledging Jesus's authority over him.

The centurion then explains his faith through military metaphor. He understands authority and obedience. He commands soldiers, and they respond without question. If he says "Go," they go. If he says "Come," they come. In the Roman army, disobedience meant death—stoning or beating before the assembled troops. Obedience is absolute.

This centurion grasps something profound: Jesus possesses the same kind of authority. Jesus is Lord over sickness and disease just as a centurion is lord over soldiers. When Jesus commands sickness to leave, it must obey. Jesus need not even be present—his word is sufficient.

Consider what Jesus had done in Capernaum before:

> In the synagogue there was a man possessed by a demon, an evil spirit... "Be quiet!" Jesus said sternly. "Come out of him!" Then the demon threw the man down before them all and came out without injuring him. (Luke 4:33-35)

> When the sun was setting, the people brought to Jesus all who had various kinds of sickness, and laying his hands on each one, he healed them. Moreover, demons came out of many people, shouting, "You are the Son of God!" (Luke 4:40-41)

Jesus commands spiritual forces. He commands sickness. His authority is absolute.

### 4. Jesus Hears (Verse 9)

> When Jesus heard this, he was amazed at him, and turning to the crowd following him, he said, "I tell you, I have not found such great faith even in Israel." (Luke 7:9)

Jesus is amazed. He turns to the crowd and declares that he's found no faith *like this*—and he's been surrounded by Israelites, God's chosen people. Yet this Roman, this Gentile outsider, demonstrates faith that surpasses theirs.

What made the centurion's faith so remarkable? Let's clarify what faith is *not* and what it *truly* is.

## What Faith Is Not

**Faith is not faith in your worthiness.** The centurion knew he didn't deserve Jesus's help. Yet Jesus healed his servant anyway. As Paul writes, "all have sinned and fall short of the glory of God" (Romans 3:23). We cannot earn God's favor through good works or generosity, no matter how substantial.

**Faith is not faith in your confidence.** Some days we feel strong and assured; other days doubt overwhelms us. Our emotional state fluctuates with circumstances. When your world falls apart, when grief or loss strikes, your feelings of confidence evaporate. But faith cannot rest on such unstable ground.

**Faith is not irrational belief in something that doesn't exist.** That's not what the centurion practiced, and it's not what Jesus commends. The centurion had *heard* about Jesus—eyewitness testimony of his deeds and authority. That's evidence. For us, that evidence is preserved in Scripture. You may debate Scripture's reliability, but historically it's the best-preserved document from antiquity and passes rigorous historical tests. Faith in Christ is reasonable, grounded in testimony and evidence.

**Faith is not faith in faith itself.** This confusion pervades Christian circles. People speak of "having enough faith" or "lacking faith," as if faith were a substance you could measure or increase. But faith always points *toward something*. You cannot believe in belief; you must believe in someone or something. To say "I have faith" without an object is meaningless.

## What Faith Actually Is

Faith is **confident belief and trust in the truth and trustworthiness of a person**—specifically, Jesus Christ.

The centurion heard about Jesus and decided to trust him. He never saw Jesus with his eyes, yet he trusted Jesus's authority based on what he'd *heard* about Jesus's actions. He recognized Jesus as Lord.

This is the faith Jesus commends. It's not based on proximity or personal experience. It's not dependent on your feelings or your worthiness. It rests entirely on the character and trustworthiness of Jesus himself.

Jesus is unchanging. He is not subject to your whims or emotional fluctuations. His authority is real and absolute, over sickness, demons, nature, and ultimately, death itself. He died for our sins and rose again. He is now ruler of the universe.

## The Application: Turning to the Crowd

Jesus turns to the crowd and speaks to us:

> "I tell you, I have not found such great faith even in Israel." (Luke 7:9)

This crowd had heard all about Jesus. They'd listened to his teaching. They'd witnessed his miracles. They had access to the same evidence the centurion possessed.

**If you're not yet a Christian:** Thank you for being here. Will you listen as the centurion listened? Will you believe and trust in Jesus, even though you cannot see him with your eyes? The evidence is available to you in Scripture and in the testimony of those who have experienced his transforming power.

**If you are a Christian:** Continue to place your faith in Jesus. Understand that what saves you is not the strength of your confidence or the depth of your feelings. What saves you is *Jesus himself*—his death, his resurrection, his lordship. Your faith is only as strong as its object.

Jesus himself said that faith the size of a mustard seed—the smallest of seeds—is sufficient. The quality of your faith isn't determined by how strongly you feel it, but by the quality of what your faith rests upon: the crucified and risen Lord Jesus.

Don't settle for blind faith. Study Scripture. Engage your reason. Christian faith is not opposed to reason—it's grounded in historical fact and thoughtful consideration. Be like the centurion: hear the testimony, examine the evidence, and trust in Jesus's lordship.

## Conclusion

What is Christian faith? It is believing in the lordship of Jesus Christ based on the historical evidence of what he has done. It is trusting that he is worthy of that trust. It is confidence not in yourself or your feelings, but in him who is unchanging, authoritative, and trustworthy.

Believe him.
