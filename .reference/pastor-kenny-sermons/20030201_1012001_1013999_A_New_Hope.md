# Genesis 12-13

**Title:** A New Hope
**Date:** 3 February 2003
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Foundation: Genesis 1-11

The Bible can be divided into two fundamental parts: Genesis 1-11 and Genesis 12-Revelation 22. This division marks Genesis 12 as monumentally important for understanding God's purposes and the entire biblical narrative.

Before examining Abraham's call, we need to understand the pattern established in the opening chapters:

- **Genesis 1**: Creation—God forms and fills the earth
- **Genesis 2**: Man's creation—humanity's unique place in God's design
- **Genesis 3**: The Fall—sin enters the world
- **Genesis 4-11**: A recurring cycle emerges—humanity's rebellion followed by God's protective mercy

Notice this pattern repeating: Cain murders Abel (4), Noah and genealogy (5), the Flood (6-9), the Table of Nations (10), and the Tower of Babel (11). In each case, humanity chooses rebellion and independence, yet God intervenes with mercy rather than destruction. Yet at Babel, something feels different—the pattern seems broken. Where is God's restorative answer this time?

As we read through the genealogies, our attention is drawn forward to a single man: Abram. Here, finally, is where God's answer to humanity's rebellion becomes clear.

## A New Promise (12:1-3)

> The LORD had said to Abram, "Leave your country, your people and your father's household and go to the land I will show you. I will make you into a great nation and I will bless you; I will make your name great, and you will be a blessing. I will bless those who bless you, and whoever curses you I will curse; and all peoples on earth will be blessed through you."

After cycles of failure and judgment, God raises up a new man with an extraordinary promise. Notice the structure of these promises:

1. **A great nation** will come from him
2. **Blessing** will rest upon him—not merely personal favor, but blessing that overflows to all the earth
3. **A great name** will be established
4. **Universal blessing** will flow through him to all peoples

### The Irony of God's Counter-Promise

These promises stand in direct contrast to Babel. The people at Babel said, "Come, let us build ourselves a city and a tower that reaches to the heavens, so that we may **make a name for ourselves** and not be scattered" (Genesis 11:4). They sought greatness, unity, and renown—but through their own power and independence.

God's response is remarkable: He offers the very same things—a great nation, a great name—but to a single man who must trust Him entirely. Instead of humanity's failed attempt to achieve greatness through self-effort, God promises to accomplish it through one person's faith and obedience.

This is why Abram is "A New Hope." After the failures of Cain, Noah, and the nations at Babel, God introduces a new beginning. Like Luke Skywalker emerging in *Star Wars: A New Hope* to counter the darkness of the Empire, Abram becomes God's new hope for humanity's redemption.

## The Arrival (12:4-9)

Abram responds immediately and obediently:

> So Abram left, as the LORD had told him; and Lot went with him. Abram was seventy-five years old when he set out from Haran. He took his wife Sarai, his nephew Lot, all the possessions they had accumulated and the people they had acquired in Haran, and they set out for the land of Canaan, and they arrived there.

At seventy-five years old, Abram leaves everything familiar. He takes his household, his possessions, and his nephew Lot, and journeys toward an unknown land. This is no small act of faith.

Upon arrival, Abram travels through Canaan to Shechem, near the great tree of Moreh. But there's an immediate problem: the Canaanites already occupy the land. They are stronger, more established, and far more powerful than Abram's small household.

### A Bold Promise in Impossible Circumstances

Yet in this very moment, God appears and makes a stunning declaration:

> To your offspring I will give this land.

Consider the audacity of this promise: The land belongs to another tribe, a powerful people with deep roots. Abram himself has no offspring to speak of. His wife Sarai is barren. Yet God promises that Abram's descendants—people who don't yet exist—will one day inherit this land from the Canaanites.

In response, Abram builds an altar to the LORD, then travels further to Bethel and builds another altar there. These altars are not consecrations of the land in some magical sense. Rather, they represent Abram laying down deposits—markers of faith in a land he doesn't yet possess. It's like putting down a down payment on a house, or like the first footprint on the moon: a small claim that speaks of greater ownership to come.

## The Exit: Faith Falters (12:10-20)

But now the narrative takes a troubling turn:

> Now there was a famine in the land, and Abram went down to Egypt to live there for a while because the famine was severe.

A famine in that ancient world meant genuine hardship. With no pastures, the flocks starve. Without grazing land, there's no food. People face real hunger and death. Yet Abram possesses a promise—God has sworn to give him this land. Shouldn't he trust? Shouldn't he remain and wait for God's deliverance?

Instead, Abram flees to Egypt for security.

### The Compromise

What happens next reveals the depths of Abram's fear. As he approaches Egypt, he says to Sarai:

> I know what a beautiful woman you are. When the Egyptians see you, they will say, 'This is his wife.' Then they will kill me but will let you live. Say you are my sister, so that I will be treated well for your sake and my life will be spared because of you.

Abram sacrifices his wife's honor to save his own life. He essentially sells her into Pharaoh's household, claiming she is his sister rather than his wife. And his plan works—the Egyptians do treat him well "for her sake," and he accumulates wealth: sheep, cattle, donkeys, menservants, maidservants, and camels.

Here we see that Abram is not the heroic figure we might have imagined. He is selfish and faithless. He abandons trust in God's promise, abandons his wife to save his own skin, and compromises his integrity for material gain. The man God called to bring blessing to the world acts with the same self-interest and moral failure that characterizes all of fallen humanity.

## The Return: God's Persistent Mercy (12:17-13:18)

Yet something different happens this time. Rather than destroying Abram or abandoning him to his consequences, God acts with mercy:

> But the LORD inflicted serious diseases on Pharaoh and his household because of Abram's wife Sarai. So Pharaoh summoned Abram. "What have you done to me?" he said. "Why didn't you tell me she was your wife? Why did you say, 'She is my sister,' so that I took her to be my wife? Now then, here is your wife. Take her and go!"

God sends a plague on Egypt to retrieve His chosen one. Remarkably, Abram departs with not only his wife but also all the wealth he accumulated—his ill-gotten gains become his to keep. This foreshadows something crucial: God will one day deliver His people from Egypt with great wealth, bringing them into their promised inheritance.

### The Separation and Renewed Promise

Upon returning to Canaan, Abram and his nephew Lot discover that their flocks have grown so large they cannot share the land without conflict. Quarrels erupt between their herdsmen, complicated by the fact that Canaanites and Perizzites also live in the region, making space precious.

Abram, demonstrating unexpected magnanimity, offers Lot a choice:

> Let's not have any quarreling between you and me, or between your herdsmen and mine, for we are brothers. Is not the whole land before you? Let's part company. If you go to the left, I'll go to the right; if you go to the right, I'll go to the left.

Lot chooses the well-watered plain of the Jordan, which looks like "the garden of the LORD, like the land of Egypt," and settles near Sodom. Abram remains in Canaan.

And then, after the separation, God speaks again:

> Lift up your eyes from where you are and look north and south, east and west. All the land that you see I will give to you and your offspring forever. I will make your offspring like the dust of the earth, so that if anyone could count the dust, then your offspring could be counted. Go, walk through the length and breadth of the land, for I am giving it to you.

The promise is renewed and expanded. The land stretches as far as Abram can see in every direction. His offspring will be as innumerable as dust. And he is called to walk through the land—to claim it, to inhabit it, to make it his own.

In response, Abram moves his tents to Hebron, near the great trees of Mamre, and builds another altar to the LORD.

## A New Beginning in Christ

Throughout the biblical story, we see a pattern: God makes a promise, humanity partially fulfills it, and the promise finds its ultimate completion in Christ.

Abram's story is no exception. Despite his faith and his obedience, Abram never fully realizes the promise. He dies without owning more than a burial plot. His offspring multiply, but never reach the number of dust. The Davidic kingdom that emerges from his line eventually falls into exile and ruin.

Yet God's commitment does not fail. The promise that began with Abram finds its complete fulfillment in Jesus Christ.

### Reading Genesis 12:1-3 Christologically

Read the promises again with Jesus in mind:

> I will make you into a great nation and I will bless you; I will make your name great, and you will be a blessing. I will bless those who bless you, and whoever curses you I will curse; and all peoples on earth will be blessed through you.

- **A great nation** becomes **a great person**—singular. The One in whom all God's purposes converge.
- **A great name** becomes **the name that is above every name**. As Paul writes:

> Therefore God exalted him to the highest place and gave him the name that is above every name, that at the name of Jesus every knee should bow, in heaven and on earth and under the earth, and every tongue confess that Jesus Christ is Lord, to the glory of God the Father. (Philippians 2:9-11)

- **All peoples blessed through you** finds its fulfillment in Jesus, to whom we are called to commit our allegiance, worship, and trust.

Those who are blessed by God are those who bless Jesus—who take refuge in Him, who submit to His lordship. But those who curse Jesus, who reject His offer of grace, come under the curse of God's judgment.

### The Response

The sermon calls us to three things:

**First, trust Jesus.** Like Abram, we are called to leave behind everything—our self-reliance, our idols, our competing ambitions—and follow Christ with the whole of our lives.

**Second, favor Jesus over all else.** The world offers countless alternatives: wealth, status, comfort, security, relationships, achievement. But Jesus calls us to value Him above all other things, to make Him our first allegiance, to recognize that every other blessing flows from Him.

**Third, if you are not following Jesus, today is the day to begin.** The promise of blessing, inheritance, and purpose is not earned through our own efforts. It comes through faith in the One who embodies all of God's purposes: Jesus Christ.
