# 1 Timothy 4:1-16

**Title:** A Godly Life in an Ungodly World
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction: The Need for Clarity and Courage

Timothy faced a daunting task—providing spiritual leadership to a struggling church in Ephesus, a city saturated with competing ideologies, mysticism, and spiritual counterfeits. Rather than writing a fearful retreat, Paul sends a bold pastoral charge. First Timothy 4 forms the heart of that charge, teaching us how to live with clarity, courage, and conviction in an age of error and compromise.

## Part 1: Beware of False Teaching (1 Timothy 4:1–5)

> "Now the Spirit expressly says that in later times some will depart from the faith..." (v. 1)

### Apostasy Foretold

Paul's phrase "the Spirit expressly says" (τὸ δὲ Πνεῦμα ῥητῶς λέγει) reveals direct prophetic insight. The adverb ῥητῶς means "explicitly" or "clearly." The verb for "depart" (ἀφίστημι) appears also in Luke 8:13, describing those who fall away from faith entirely.

### Doctrines of Demons

> "devoting themselves to deceitful spirits and teachings of demons" (v. 1b)

The word προσέχοντες (devoting) comes from προσέχω, meaning to pay close, undivided attention. Paul had warned Timothy earlier about myths and genealogies that distract from the gospel's simplicity.

### The Seared Conscience and Legalism

> "through the insincerity of liars whose consciences are seared" (v. 2)

The phrase συνειδήσεως κεκαυστηριασμένης literally means "having been cauterized"—a conscience so hardened by repeated sin that it no longer responds to truth. This stands in sharp contrast to 1 Timothy 1:5, which calls us to "a good conscience."

### Modern Parallels

False teaching today often masquerades as spiritualized wellness. What appears as virtue—ascetic practices around diet, health, and sexuality—can become a form of neo-Gnosticism that denies the goodness of God's creation. The church must remain alert to ideologies that sound spiritual but contradict Scripture's affirmation of the body and creation.

## Part 2: Discipline Yourself for Godliness (1 Timothy 4:6–10)

### A Nourished Servant

> "being trained in the words of the faith..." (v. 6)

The verb ἐντρέφω means to nourish or bring up. Timothy becomes strong not through abstract philosophy but through τοῖς λόγοις τῆς πίστεως—the concrete, life-giving words of the gospel message.

### Godliness as the Goal

> "Train yourself for godliness" (v. 7)

The Greek gumnazō speaks of rigorous training—the kind of disciplined effort an athlete demands of herself. The target is εὐσέβεια (godliness), a word Paul returns to repeatedly throughout 1 Timothy. Godliness is not passive; it requires deliberate, sustained effort.

### Hope-Fueled Striving

> "we toil and strive, because we have our hope set on the living God..." (v. 10)

The words κοπιῶμεν (toil) and ἀγωνιζόμεθα (strive) convey genuine struggle and exertion. Yet this effort is not grim or hopeless. It flows from confidence in the ζῶντι θεῷ—the living God who sustains us and whose promises are sure.

## Part 3: Set the Example in Life and Teaching (1 Timothy 4:11–16)

### The Young Leader's Charge

> "Let no one despise your youth, but set an example..." (v. 12)

Timothy is young and may face skepticism from older believers. Paul calls him to καταφρονείτω—to let no one look down upon him. Instead, he must become a τύπος (example) in five concrete ways:

- **Speech (λόγος)**: Words that are true, edifying, and Christ-centered
- **Conduct (ἀναστροφή)**: A life that matches his teaching
- **Love (ἀγάπη)**: Genuine pastoral affection for the flock
- **Faith (πίστις)**: Trust in God that shines through adversity
- **Purity (ἁγνεία)**: Integrity and moral wholesomeness

### Ministry of the Word

> "Give attention to the public reading of Scripture, to exhortation and teaching..." (v. 13)

Three essential elements shape Timothy's public ministry: ἀνάγνωσις (public reading of Scripture), παράκλησις (exhortation that draws people toward Christ), and διδασκαλία (teaching that builds understanding). Each is vital; none can be neglected.

### Spiritual Gifts and Visible Progress

> "Do not neglect the gift you have..." (vv. 14–15)

The spiritual gift Timothy received through the laying on of hands must not atrophy through disuse. More than that, his προκοπή (progress) must be evident to all—visible spiritual growth that encourages the congregation.

### Watch Yourself and Your Doctrine

> "Pay close attention to yourself and to your teaching; persist in these things..." (v. 16)

The commands ἐπέχε (pay close attention) and ἐπιμένω (persist) require vigilance and endurance. A leader's responsibility is dual: guard both your own spiritual walk and the purity of doctrine. This dual attentiveness saves both the teacher and those who hear.

## Conclusion: The Shape of a Godly Leader

The church needs leaders shaped by the Word of God, whose lives visibly reflect Christ, and whose ministries are driven by hope in the living God. In a culture marked by confusion and compromise, such leaders become beacons of clarity and courage. The call to godliness is not a burden imposed from outside; it flows from our love for Christ and our confidence in His promises.
