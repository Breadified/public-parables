# Luke 11:14-41

**Title:** Towards Jerusalem: Sign of the Kingdom

**Date:** 3 March 2024

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## 1. Divided House (11:14-23)

### The Miracle

> Jesus was driving out a demon that was mute. When the demon left, the man who had been mute spoke, and the crowd was amazed.

Both the demon and the man are described as mute—perhaps a demon of muteness that caused the man's disability. The demon desired to possess and rule over this person, causing him suffering. When Jesus cast out the demon with remarkable ease, the man could speak. Luke focuses not on the supernatural event itself, but on its theological significance: demons were being defeated.

### The False Accusation

> But some of them said, "By Beelzebul, the prince of demons, he is driving out demons." Others tested him by asking for a sign from heaven.

The Pharisees and experts in the law accused Jesus of deriving his power from Satan himself—that Jesus, as prince of demons, was simply commanding his own followers to withdraw. But this accusation is logically incoherent.

### Jesus Refutes the Logic

> Jesus knew their thoughts and said to them: "Any kingdom divided against itself will be ruined, and a house divided against itself will fall. If Satan is divided against himself, how can his kingdom stand? I say this because you claim that I drive out demons by Beelzebul."

Why would a leader destroy his own forces? A kingdom divided collapses. A house divided falls. If Satan were divided against himself, his kingdom would self-destruct. Yet Satan's kingdom persists—so Jesus cannot be Satan's prince working against himself. The accusation is illogical.

The principle applies universally: to win, you attack the enemy, not yourself. In war, you want your enemies to suffer while you survive. You don't starve yourself, kill your own soldiers, or surrender to maintain equality. That seems obvious, yet our modern culture seems to have forgotten this fundamental truth—we've adopted the misguided notion that virtue lies in losing permanently and everywhere, in being victims. That's not wisdom; that's self-destruction.

### The Second Argument: Inconsistency

> Now if I drive out demons by Beelzebul, by whom do your followers drive them out? So then, they will be your judges.

Casting out demons was common in that era. The Pharisees themselves—and other Jewish exorcists—performed exorcisms. Yet they never accused their own people of serving Beelzebul. This reveals the Pharisees were using the demonic accusation as a pretext to attack Jesus, not because they genuinely believed it. Their inconsistency exposes their true motive: opposition to Jesus, not theological conviction.

### The Kingdom Has Come

> But if I drive out demons by the finger of God, then the kingdom of God has come upon you.

This miracle is not a sign of demonic power, but of divine kingship. It is God himself arriving to rescue his people. The prophet Isaiah, 700 years before Christ, foretold this:

> Be strong, do not fear; your God will come, he will come with vengeance; with divine retribution he will come to save you. Then will the eyes of the blind be opened and the ears of the deaf unstopped. Then will the lame leap like a deer, and the mute tongue shout for joy.

The mute man now speaks. The prayers of Jesus and his disciples are answered. The kingdom is here. When you see the mute speaking, the blind seeing, the lame leaping—you see that God is present, that Jesus is here.

The phrase "finger of God" echoes Exodus, where Pharaoh's magicians acknowledged the plagues as God's work. A finger lifting something represents either the object being very light or the one lifting being very strong. Jesus is very strong. He effortlessly overcomes Satan's grip.

### The Stronger Man Overpowers

> When a strong man, fully armed, guards his own house, his possessions are safe. But when someone stronger attacks and overpowers him, he takes away the armor in which the man trusted and divides up his plunder.

Satan is strong, fully armed, and his possessions—the people he possesses—seem safe in his grip. But a Stronger Man has come. With merely a finger, Jesus overpowers Satan, strips away his armor, and liberates his captives. Satan's plunder becomes the kingdom of God's plunder.

### The Choice Between Two Sides

> Whoever is not with me is against me, and whoever does not gather with me scatters.

This is war. There is no neutral position. You belong either to Jesus or to Beelzebul. Some say, "What about the neutral people, the agnostics, the civilians?" In war, everyone eventually becomes a soldier. The Gaza-Israel conflict illustrates this harsh reality—both sides view every member of the opposing population as a combatant, present or future, and act accordingly. While this is tragic, it reflects the nature of warfare: there are no true civilians in an existential conflict.

Similarly, spiritually, there are no true neutrals. Everyone belongs to one kingdom or the other. The call is urgent and direct: choose Jesus today.

## 2. Empty House (11:24-32)

### The Problem of Cosmetic Change

> When an impure spirit comes out of a person, it goes through arid places seeking rest and does not find it. Then it says, 'I will return to the house I left.' When it arrives, it finds the house swept clean and put in order.

When the demon left, the man's "house" was made clean. He could speak. His life was restored. But "put in order" (Greek: *kosmo*, from which we derive "cosmos" and "cosmetic") suggests only surface arrangement—a cosmetic renovation, not fundamental restoration.

Then it goes and takes seven other spirits more wicked than itself, and they go in and live there. And the final condition of that person is worse than the first.

Why does a swept and ordered house attract more demons? Because the cleanliness was only cosmetic. The house looked good from the outside, but nothing substantial filled it within.

**The Illustration:** A house prepared for sale receives cheap paint covering mold, new door knobs of lowest quality, roof paint. It looks good in photographs and at auction. But six months later, hardware falls apart, mold returns, paint flakes off. It's cosmetic renovation—beautiful outside, hollow within.

This describes someone superficially following Jesus: calling themselves Christian, doing some religious things, adopting certain values, giving up bad habits—but nothing more. Cosmetic treatment. There's nothing wrong with being called Christian or identifying as such. We all begin immature. But cosmetic changes alone are insufficient. We must mature. We must ensure real love for Jesus motivates our desire to change.

For if Jesus is not in the house, it remains empty. The evil spirits find it cleaned and prepared for them—a perfect home. They bring seven companions, more wicked than the first. The final state is worse than the beginning.

### The Pharisees as Empty Houses

> Now then, you Pharisees clean the outside of the cup and dish, but inside you are full of greed and wickedness. Did not the one who made the outside make the inside also? But now as for what is inside you—be generous to the poor, and everything will be clean for you.

The Pharisees cared deeply about external appearance. They cleaned cups and dishes meticulously but neglected the inner condition. God made both outside and inside; he cares about both. Yet they polished the exterior while their hearts remained full of greed and wickedness.

This affliction runs deep in post-Christian Western societies. We inherited Christian ideals from our cultural past—the shell, the external form—but we've abandoned the substance, the biblical conviction and intellectual rigor that gave it meaning. We appear moral while behaving immorally:

- We condemn Israel's actions in Gaza while aborting our own children and euthanizing the elderly
- We champion marriage while dismantling it through no-fault divorce and redefinition
- We proclaim equal rights for women while denying them in sports and bathrooms

We aren't good. We merely look good. An empty, cleanly swept house is still empty. It must be filled with something real.

## 3. Full House (11:27-36)

### Hearing and Obeying

> As Jesus was saying these things, a woman in the crowd called out, "Blessed is the mother who gave you birth and nursed you." He replied, "Blessed rather are those who hear the word of God and obey it."

Hearing alone is insufficient. Obedience is required. The mute man must not merely hear the word of God; he must respond in repentance and obedience.

### The Sign Greater Than All Signs

> This is a wicked generation. It asks for a sign, but none will be given it except the sign of Jonah. For as Jonah was a sign to the Ninevites, so also will the Son of Man be to this generation.

The crowd asked for a sign and received one: the mute man spoke. Yet they demanded more. Jesus points to two ancient examples of faith and obedience:

> The Queen of the South will rise at the judgment with the people of this generation and condemn them, for she came from the ends of the earth to listen to Solomon's wisdom; and now something greater than Solomon is here. The men of Nineveh will stand up at the judgment with this generation and condemn it, for they repented at the preaching of Jonah; and now something greater than Jonah is here.

The Queen of the South (perhaps Ethiopia or Yemen) traveled vast distances to hear Solomon's wisdom. The Ninevites, though pagan, repented at Jonah's preaching. Both listened and obeyed. Now, One greater than Solomon and greater than Jonah stands before this generation. How much more should they listen and obey?

### Being Full of Light

> No one lights a lamp and puts it in a place where it will be hidden, or under a bowl. Instead they put it on its stand, so that those who come in may see the light. Your eye is the lamp of your body. When your eyes are healthy, your whole body also is full of light. But when they are unhealthy, your body also is full of darkness. See to it, then, that the light within you is not darkness. Therefore, if your whole body is full of light, and no part of it dark, it will be just as full of light as when a lamp shines its light on you.

Continuing the house metaphor: a person full of light is a house full of light. What fills your house determines what grows there. Fill it with chips and soda, and your children become overweight. Fill it with wholesome food, and they thrive nutritionally. Fill it with screens and devices, and they become distracted. Fill it with books and learning materials, and they become studious.

Fill your house with the light of Jesus, with the word of God where inhabitants listen and obey—then you and your family will be healthy, immune to the return of demons. A full house. A complete life. A luminous witness to God's kingdom.
