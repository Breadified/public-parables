# Matthew 6:19-24

**Title:** Seeing Treasures in Heaven
**Date:** 11 January 2016
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Ability to See the Future

If you had one superpower, what would it be? Many of us would choose the ability to see the future. We could preview our exam papers, know when to invest or avoid investments, sidestep accidents, win at chess. For those more other-centered, we might rescue people from danger or stop crimes before they happen.

This passage challenges us to develop spiritual vision—the ability to see the eternal consequences of how we use our eyes and hearts right now.

> The eye is the lamp of the body. If your eyes are healthy, your whole body will be full of light. But if your eyes are unhealthy, your whole body will be full of darkness. If then the light within you is darkness, how great is that darkness! (Matthew 6:22-23)

The question before us is simple: **What do you see when you look at the world?**

## Treasures on Earth (Matthew 6:19)

> Do not store up for yourselves treasures on earth, where moths and vermin destroy, and where thieves break in and steal.

### Treasures on Earth Are Good

First, let's clarify what "treasures on earth" means. These are our possessions, property, and material things we value—your books, room, smartphone, laptop, clothes, food. These treasures sustain us and are useful. They are part of God's blessings.

> For everything God created is good, and nothing is to be rejected if it is received with thanksgiving. (1 Timothy 6:4)

Christians are not anti-materialists. You are not holier for living minimally without internet, nor holier for having abundance. Monks are no holier than kings, and kings no holier than monks. The key is how we use what we have and whether we receive it with gratitude.

The real problem isn't material things themselves—it's that material wealth deceives us. It falsely promises happiness, control, and security far beyond what it can actually deliver.

- **Happiness:** Research shows that beyond meeting basic needs (food, shelter, work), more wealth does not increase happiness. We experience diminishing returns of contentment with each additional possession.

- **Control:** We cannot control God's hand. When I once worried about leaving my career for ministry, I thought buying insurance would give me security. But could more money really control disease and death? No amount of wealth can.

Material things don't make our lives complete. They cannot impart meaning, and they can never make us truly happy—yet we pursue them as if they could.

### Treasures on Earth Have No Future

But here's the sobering reality: **every present material thing is future rotting rubbish.**

Look at what happens on kerbside cleanup day. You find formerly good things—top-of-the-line computers that are now obsolete, the best phones on the market, fashionable Air Jordan shoes, comfortable ergonomic chairs. They all get old. They all end up as garbage.

This is the future of all material treasures. They are stolen, they rot, they are destroyed. The passage lists the specific ways:
- Moths lay eggs on clothes, and larvae eat holes in them
- Vermin and rats bite into our food storage
- Thieves break in and steal our TV, car, jewelry, money

Imagine if you could see time's effects on all things, as that wizard in fantasy fiction with "hourglass eyes" could—flowers always wilting, buildings always disintegrating, beauty always aging, humans always decomposing. It would be like Halloween every day.

That's the vision we need: treasures received with thanksgiving, yes, but only for a short while. They won't last.

Consider what awaits your brand-new Christmas gifts. That phone you worked so hard to get, cried over, bribed for—within a year or two, you'll toss it to the back of your drawer. That lovely delicious dinner tonight? Future sewerage. Your precious car? Future scrap metal. Your beloved house? A crumbling edifice with holes in the roof, water streaming through, pigeons nesting and leaving droppings, future rat nest.

When you see things this way, the stupidity of fighting over treasures becomes clear. What silliness to think your life is better because you have more possessions than others. We compete—"I got more stuff than you. I wish I could get more stuff"—as though our worth depends on our collection.

### Do Not Store Up

> Do not store up for yourselves treasures on earth. (Matthew 6:19)

"Store up" means to keep, to over-invest, to hoard. This phrase echoes the Israelites in the wilderness. God gave them manna—bread from heaven—appearing each morning like dew on every surface. They were to collect it fresh each day.

> Then Moses said to them, "No one is to keep any of it until morning." However, some of them paid no attention to Moses; they kept part of it until morning, but it was full of maggots and began to smell. (Exodus 16:19-20)

God was teaching them to depend on Him day by day and to understand that temporary things are only temporary.

Jesus's command is: **Don't over-invest in this world.** Store is another term for investment—keeping something for another day. Don't be fooled that material things are that important. Don't think that if you only get this one thing, your life will be complete, your problems will be solved, your life will be secure.

We deceive ourselves, thinking: "If I just get this ATAR mark, enter this course, earn the big salary, buy things and experiences, then my life will be secure." But that security is an illusion.

## Treasures in Heaven (Matthew 6:20)

> But store up for yourselves treasures in heaven, where moths and vermin do not destroy, and where thieves do not break in and steal.

What does it mean to store treasures in heaven? The phrase is symbolic. It means this:

### First: A Symbolic Expression—Your Relationship with God

> For where your treasure is, there your heart will be also. (Matthew 6:21)

This tells us where our hearts are. If your heart is with God in heaven, invest your treasure there. If your heart is on earth, your treasure will follow.

Verse 24 makes the contrast clear:

> No one can serve two masters. Either you will hate the one and love the other, or you will be devoted to the one and despise the other. You cannot serve both God and money. (Matthew 6:24)

A relationship with money and possessions becomes idolatry. Whether it's the idol of religion or money, idols are human-made and therefore corruptible—they rot, rust, decay, get stolen, and lead to evil.

But a relationship with the true and living God will not rot. The treasure we receive in heaven is eternal life itself:

> In this way they will lay up treasure for themselves as a firm foundation for the coming age, so that they may take hold of the life that is truly life. (1 Timothy 6:19)

Put your hopes and dreams not in earthly things but in God. Here is true security, true comfort, true meaning—where wealth won't be eroded by inflation, where things won't rot, where thieves can't steal, where sickness and disease won't ruin our experience. We will no longer be disappointed or bored.

What a tragedy if we used only our earthly eyes and thought we were collecting treasure, only to end up with a room full of junk.

### Second: A Practical Expression—Using Earthly Works to Build Eternal Treasure

> Command them to do good, to be rich in good deeds, and to be generous and willing to share. (1 Timothy 6:18)

Store your treasures in heaven also means to put what's really important there—to invest in eternal things, in relationships where death and sin won't destroy them.

We in Australia are materially rich—even our average poor have food, cars, bedrooms, mobile phones, WiFi, air conditioning. We are all wealthy enough in this present world. Yet we are **spiritually poor**. We have very little treasure in heaven, very little hope in God, very little kindness and good works. We have very little consideration for the weight of eternity before us. We have little grace as families break down, each side unwilling to give way. We have little truth as our sin redefines reality. We have little understanding of what "life that is truly life" means.

So the command is: **Do good. Be rich in deeds. Be generous. Share.**

If you have control over your money, buy someone lunch, support your youth group, invite the lonely at church to the movies. But also share eternal treasures. Share the great news that Jesus brings eternal life. In doing so, you **lay up treasure for the coming age**.

Tell your friends about Jesus and put them in heaven's safety. Our lives are not secure now. No amount of education, career, or suburb will protect us from eternal death. All have sinned against God and will be punished. Only putting our hopes in God saves us.

## What Do You See?

The central question is: **Where will you invest your time, money, and energy? In things that fade or in things that last?**

Jesus sees a different future for you. In the coming age, He doesn't see spiritual poverty. He sees a people rich toward God—a people who will no longer sin but will be righteous. A people no longer lazy, lacking in self-control, wrecked with self-doubt and addiction. Not mentally weak, doing wrong things just because others do. Not dependent on social media validation or likes on your profile picture. Instead, together with Christ, you will be triumphant and victorious.

**Will you be that people?**

This future Jesus has bought for us by blood. On the cross, evil seemed to have won. The body of Jesus was added to earth's morbid collection. But that's only if you had bad vision.

If you have eyes of the future—eyes of hope and faith—you will see that at that cross, your sins were taken by Him. Failure on earth is temporary, but eternity is secured. That cross is God's proclamation of love, telling us He loves us literally to death. There is no one who will ever love us this way.

If you have eyes of the future, you will know that God accepts all your failings, removes them, forgives you, and calls you to a fresh start.

**So choose: Will you invest in present decay or future glory?** You cannot live for both the future and the present. You must choose.

Do not give up on eternity in your lust for the present. Do not give up finding God, the church, the Bible, Jesus. That is having bad vision for the future.

> Do not store up for yourselves treasures on earth, where moths and vermin destroy, and where thieves break in and steal. But store up for yourselves treasures in heaven, where moths and vermin do not destroy, and where thieves do not break in and steal. (Matthew 6:19-20)
