# Hebrews 2:5-3:1

**Title:** Jesus is a Better Messenger #2
**Date:** 20 February 2009
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## What is Man?

We live in a culture that answers this question in many ways. Some say man is merely the sum of his experiences—make the best of them. Others reduce humanity to chemistry: a few dollars worth of carbon, water, and organic chemicals. Still others see man as merely an economic consumer, or just another animal driven by instincts and hormones.

Last week we explored how Jesus is the best messenger because he is God himself—the exact representation of God. His message cannot be diminished or altered because of who he is.

Today the writer of Hebrews turns to a second reason why Jesus is the better messenger: he is not only God, but also fully human. This distinction matters profoundly for our salvation and our hope.

## The World is For Mankind

The author quotes Psalm 8 to establish this foundation:

> What is man that you are mindful of him, the son of man that you care for him? You made him a little lower than the angels; you crowned him with glory and honor and put everything under his feet. In putting everything under him, God left nothing that is not subject to him.

Here is the paradox: man occupies a low place in the chain of being. We are lower than the angels. We are mortal, fragile, and ordinary—nothing like fire or wind in their majesty. When we consider ourselves honestly, we might ask, as the psalmist does, "Who are we that God would even notice?"

Yet Scripture insists that God does notice. He cares for us. He crowns us with glory and honor—the dignity of kings. He places all creation beneath our feet and leaves nothing outside our dominion. The climax of creation itself is humanity. God made the sun, the moon, the vegetation—all for man. The world exists for our flourishing, not the other way around.

This contradicts modern voices that claim humanity is a plague on the earth. Some argue that humans behave like a virus, spreading and consuming resources until we exhaust an area, then moving on to destroy the next. Others say the world would be better without us. Films like *The Day the Earth Stood Still* present humanity as the problem.

But this worldview is profoundly mistaken. It has even influenced how people think about having children. Groups like No Kidding International ask whether babies are still good news for the world given their environmental cost. Some advocate for population control as though fewer humans would solve our problems.

Yet this perspective loses sight of God's design. Yes, we have abused the earth. Yes, humans contribute to environmental damage. But the solution is not to eliminate people—that would be like using a nuclear bomb to kill a fly in your house. You might destroy the fly, but you'd destroy your meal and your home in the process. We would lose the very purpose for which creation exists.

The truth is this: you are not a biological accident. Love people. Love children. Love humankind. Appreciate that the world was made for you.

## Yet We Do Not See Man in Charge

But here is the problem. Look at the second half of verse 8:

> Yet at present we do not see everything subject to him.

Our eyes tell us otherwise. The world does not submit to human authority. It is not friendly to us. We face death, disease, and suffering. This is the curse of Genesis 3—the broken world we inhabit. We may wear a crown over empires, but we never wear a crown over death and sorrow.

Consider Brad Pitt in his perfect beauty, or the fictional Benjamin Button with his unusual life. Both face the same end—death. No matter how beautiful, how accomplished, or how extraordinary a person becomes, mortality claims them all. The world is not actually subject to humanity as it should be.

## Yet We See Jesus in Charge

But verse 9 offers hope:

> But we see Jesus, who was made a little lower than the angels, now crowned with glory and honor because he suffered death, so that by the grace of God he might taste death for everyone.

Jesus is the man who fulfills what humanity was meant to be. He was made lower—he became mortal, experienced our vulnerability, and tasted death itself. Yet now he is crowned with glory and honor, seated at the Father's right hand, sharing in our humanity even in his exaltation.

This means the world is ultimately for Jesus—the true and superior human. We are, in a sense, prototypes of the humanity Jesus completes and perfects.

### What Makes the Ideal Person?

Who is your ideal? Not the richest among us. Not the smartest. Not the toughest or the most athletic or the most beautiful. The truly ideal person—the real human—is the one who is kindest and most obedient. That is Jesus.

He is the mortal who never sinned. He is the mortal who rules perfectly. He is entirely other-centered, placing the interests of others before his own. He was willing to die for us. He perfectly obeyed his Father in heaven.

If you want to become the ultimate version of humanity, look to Jesus. Be like him.

## Two Implications: How Jesus Helps Us

### He Can Help Us Now

> Because he himself suffered when he was tempted, he is able to help those who are being tempted.

And:

> Both the one who makes men holy and those who are made holy are of the same family. So Jesus is not ashamed to call them brothers.

Jesus became man. He is our family. Because he knows what it means to be human—to struggle, to be tempted, to suffer, to feel pain and disappointment and fear—he can help us now.

When you face temptation, remember: Jesus was tempted as you are tempted. He knows the weight of what you carry. He was a baby once, vulnerable and dependent. He felt betrayal. He experienced the weight of suffering. He understands you more deeply than you can comprehend.

Never think that Jesus is somehow distant from your experience or that he fails to understand your struggle. He does not.

### He Can Free Us From Eternal Death

> Since the children have flesh and blood, he too shared in their humanity so that by his death he might destroy him who holds the power of death—that is, the devil—and free those who all their lives were held in slavery by their fear of death. For surely it is not angels he helps, but Abraham's descendants. For this reason he had to be made like his brothers in every way, in order that he might become a merciful and faithful high priest in service to God, and that he might make atonement for the sins of the people.

This is what makes salvation complete and perfect. Because Jesus shared our experiences, our temptations, and our mortality—yet perfectly obeyed the Father—he alone is able to take the penalty of our sins upon himself. He can bear the weight of death itself.

This means there is no sin he cannot forgive. There is no sinner here too broken for him. God did not send a distant deity untouched by pain, sitting in an ivory tower. He sent himself as a man—one who felt pain, who experienced the curse, who knew disappointment and fear.

Trust in that. This is your hope today.

## What Is Man?

The question we began with deserves a final answer. Man is not merely the sum of his experiences. It is not enough to travel the world and accumulate moments. Man is not just a few dollars worth of chemicals or a mere economic consumer. Man is not a freak of nature, a collection of instincts, or an impersonal machine.

**Man is what Jesus revealed us to be.** When you see Jesus, you see true humanity in all its glory. Trust him today.
