# 2 Samuel 2:1-32; 3:1

**Title:** Civil War
**Date:** 13 August 2023
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Setting: A Kingdom Divided

After Saul's death, the question emerges: What now? David, currently in Ziglag in Philistine territory, seeks the Lord's guidance on where to establish his rule. The Lord directs him to Hebron—a location rich with covenant significance.

Hebron holds profound meaning in Israel's history. It was here that the Lord first showed Abraham the land that would belong to him and his descendants (Genesis 13:14-17). Here the three angels visited Abraham and Sarah with the promise of a son. Here Abraham and Sarah were buried, along with all the patriarchs and their families—a testimony to the fulfillment of God's promise that their descendants would rule this land, be enslaved in Egypt, and return to take possession again.

By directing David to Hebron, God signals the beginning of His kingdom again—a new chapter in fulfilling the Abrahamic covenant. Just as Abraham lived in Hebron, David now takes his family there with his two wives, Ahinoam and Abigail. And there, the men of Judah anoint David king over the tribe of Judah.

This is David's second anointing. Fifteen years earlier, as a teenager, Samuel anointed him (1 Samuel 16). Now, as a mature leader, he is anointed again. This pattern reflects the entire kingdom of God—an exercise in patience. Abraham waited twenty-five years for Isaac. The kingdom has already taken a thousand years to reach this point, and it will continue for another thousand years until Jesus. Even now, we have been waiting two thousand years for His return.

But while one is anointed, another is made king.

## The Rival Kingdom

Abner, Saul's commander and likely cousin, takes Ish-Bosheth, Saul's surviving son, and establishes him as king over the northern tribes from Mahanaim—a location across the Jordan, as far from the action as possible. Ish-Bosheth appears weak and passive, a puppet king with Abner holding the real power. Abner had discovered David years earlier when David defeated Goliath, bringing the young warrior before Saul. Later, David had embarrassed Abner by taking Saul's spear while both slept, proving the commander's negligence. No love is lost between these two men.

Abner declares Ish-Bosheth king over Gilead, Ashuri, Jezreel, Ephraim, Benjamin, and all Israel—essentially all the northern tribes. Meanwhile, David reigns over Judah from Hebron for seven and a half years. The civil war has begun: the house of Saul versus the house of David. The lines are drawn.

## The Battle at Gibeon

Abner and Ish-Bosheth's forces leave Mahanaim and meet David's army under General Joab at the pool of Gibeon. What begins as a possible ritual combat escalates into genuine warfare.

Abner proposes that the young men "play" before them—the Hebrew word suggesting competition rather than warfare. Joab agrees. Twelve men from Benjamin are selected, and twelve from David's forces. But something goes terribly wrong. The account describes each man grabbing his opponent by the head and thrusting a dagger into his side. All twenty-four died simultaneously, falling together.

The place becomes known as Helkath Hazzurim—"Field of Plotters" in the Greek version—suggesting treachery. The Benjamites, from Saul's own tribe, were known for cunning. The story of Ehud, the left-handed Benjamite who assassinated the king of Moab (Judges 3:15), illustrates this reputation. Perhaps what began as friendly competition descended into deception and mutual slaughter.

The battle that day becomes fierce and fierce. Abner and the northern forces are defeated by David's men. The casualty count is devastating: three hundred and sixty Benjamites fall against David's forces.

## Abner and Asahel: A Personal Tragedy

The focus narrows to three sons of Zeruiah: Joab, Abishai, and Asahel. Asahel, fleet-footed as a wild gazelle, pursues Abner across the battlefield. The general recognizes the young man chasing him and twice warns him to stop, knowing that killing David's brother-in-law would bring terrible consequences with Joab.

"Why should I strike you down?" Abner pleads. "How could I look your brother Joab in the face?"

But Asahel refuses to stop. Moving at full speed, unable to slow his pursuit, he crashes directly onto Abner's spear. The general thrusts the butt of his spear forward, and the weapon passes through Asahel's body, emerging from his back. Like a car tailgating another at high speed, Asahel's momentum makes evasion impossible. He falls dead on the spot.

When Joab and Abishai reach their brother's body, they continue pursuing Abner. As sunset approaches and the Benjamites rally behind their general, Abner finally calls for peace.

## The Call for Ceasefire

"Must the sword devour forever?" Abner cries out to Joab. "Don't you realize that this will end in bitterness? How long before you order your men to stop pursuing their fellow Israelites?"

Joab responds with the truth of the moment: "As surely as God lives, if you had not spoken, the men would have continued pursuing them until morning."

The fighting has reached a stalemate. No one is winning. Like the great wars of history—the Hundred Years' War, the World Wars—millions die just to gain an inch of ground. The cost becomes unbearable.

Joab sounds the trumpet. The troops halt. They cease their pursuit and fighting.

Through the night, Abner and his men march back across the Jordan to Mahanaim, leaving David's forces in control of the field.

## The Long War Begins

> The war between the house of Saul and the house of David lasted a long time. David grew stronger and stronger, while the house of Saul grew weaker and weaker. (2 Samuel 3:1)

What began with competing anointings evolves into a prolonged civil conflict. One man anointed by God, another made king by his uncle's ambition. The waiting continues. God's purpose advances slowly, inexorably, while human resistance gradually weakens. David's faith is tested through years of conflict, but the trajectory is clear: God's kingdom will prevail.
