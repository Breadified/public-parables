# Luke 20:1-19

**Title:** God's World
**Date:** 22 February 2025
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## 1. The Challenge to Jesus' Authority (Luke 20:1-8)

> One day as Jesus was teaching the people in the temple courts and proclaiming the good news, the chief priests and the teachers of the law, together with the elders, came up to him. "Tell us by what authority you are doing these things," they said. "Who gave you this authority?"

Jesus had arrived in Jerusalem after his long travel narrative through Judea. He had entered the city to celebration, wept over its future, and cleansed the temple. Now, in his final week before crucifixion, he faced organized opposition from Israel's leadership—the Sanhedrin council comprised of chief priests (hereditary Aaronic Levites who managed the temple), teachers of the law (scribes who interpreted Scripture), and elders (local judges). The Romans controlled certain matters like taxation and the military, but the Sanhedrin held authority over religious, moral, and legal affairs.

These leaders were scandalized. Here was someone entering the temple without their permission, teaching a different message, attracting crowds—all without the credentials or credentials they deemed necessary. Their question was fundamentally about jurisdiction: "Who appointed you? By what authority do you act?"

### Jesus' Counter-Question

> He replied, "I will also ask you a question. Tell me: John's baptism—was it from heaven, or of human origin?"

Rather than answer defensively, Jesus posed a question about John the Baptist's baptizing authority. This was not about John receiving baptism, but about John's ministry of baptizing Jews in the Jordan and calling them to repentance—the very baptism where John called the religious authorities "a brood of vipers." The question was direct: Where did John derive his permission and authority to baptize? From heaven or from human sources?

The leaders faced an impossible dilemma:

> They discussed it among themselves and said, "If we say, 'From heaven,' he will ask, 'Why didn't you believe him?' But if we say, 'Of human origin,' all the people will stone us, because they are persuaded that John was a prophet."

If they admitted John's authority came from heaven, they would have to concede that John—who baptized Jesus and endorsed him—possessed divine authority. They would lose the confrontation entirely.

If they claimed John's authority was merely human, they would contradict the people's conviction that John was a prophet sent by God. They would face the crowd's anger.

> So they answered, "We don't know where it was from."
> 
> Jesus said, "Neither will I tell you by what authority I am doing these things."

Their refusal to answer honestly exposed their cynicism. Jesus' refusal to answer was not evasion—it was a lesson about foolish questions. It is as absurd to demand credentials from one who already possesses them as it is to ask a judge sitting in his robes, or a president with established authority, to prove their right to govern. John the Baptist did not need their permission. Jesus did not need their permission.

### Application: Recognizing Jesus' Authority

We sometimes fall into the same trap as these leaders—asking cynical questions about Jesus' credentials, demanding answers to questions already addressed, or posing rhetorical questions that mask our resistance rather than genuine inquiry. Nothing is wrong with honest questioning, but we must examine our motives.

Many of our deepest questions ultimately reduce to a simple answer: "Because He said so." Why suffering? Why inequality between men and women? Why the mysteries of providence? Eventually, we reach the boundary of what finite minds can comprehend. There is an end to questioning.

Job asked many questions about his suffering, but when God spoke, He turned the tables: "Where were you when I laid the foundations of the earth?" Job had no answer. Neither do we when confronted with divine wisdom.

God is God, and we are not. Jesus' authority is not derived from human appointment—it is divine authority. While mortals can receive adequate and sufficient answers to many questions, our understanding remains limited. Only submission remains.

## 2. The Rejection of God's Messengers (Luke 20:9-15a)

> He went on to tell the people this parable: "A man planted a vineyard, rented it to some farmers and went away for a long time.

Jesus illustrated his point with a parable deeply rooted in Israel's tradition. In Isaiah 5:7, the prophet declared: "The vineyard of the Lord Almighty is the nation of Israel, and the people of Judah are the vines he delighted in." The vineyard represented Israel itself.

The owner was extraordinarily generous. He prepared everything (building a wall, digging a pit for pressing wine, constructing a tower), then rented it to tenant farmers and departed for a long time. The rent was not demanded upfront but would be collected at harvest—a remarkable act of trust.

> At harvest time he sent a servant to the tenants so they would give him some of the fruit of the vineyard. But the tenants beat him and sent him away empty-handed.
> 
> He sent another servant, but that one also they beat and treated shamefully and sent away empty-handed. He sent still a third, and they wounded him and threw him out.

One after another, the landlord's servants were beaten, dishonored, and sent back empty-handed. This pattern reflects Israel's tragic history: God sent prophet after prophet—Isaiah, Jeremiah, Micah, Amos, Zechariah, and finally John the Baptist—calling the nation to justice and righteousness. Many died at the hands of those they came to serve.

### The Root Cause: Refusing to Pay

The tenants' resistance stemmed from a fundamental refusal to acknowledge the landlord's rights. They resented paying rent, viewing the landlord as a parasitic figure who reaped where he had not sown—an economic philosophy that has inspired revolutionary movements throughout history. Yet this critique misses the deeper truth: the landlord had invested, prepared the land, and taken the risk. He deserved his share.

The tenants wanted the inheritance for themselves. They refused to acknowledge that the land was not truly theirs—they held it as stewards, not owners.

> Then the owner of the vineyard said, "What shall I do? I will send my son, whom I love; perhaps they will respect him."

In a final appeal, the owner sent his beloved son. The hope was that respect for the son would motivate them to pay what was owed. Yet the parable reveals their true nature:

> "But when the tenants saw him, they talked the matter over. 'This is the heir,' they said. 'Let's kill him, and the inheritance will be ours.' So they threw him out of the vineyard and killed him.

Their greed eclipsed all reason. They murdered the heir, hoping to seize the property for themselves. This was the sin laid bare: not merely economic resentment, but violent rebellion against the owner's rightful authority.

### The Parable's Meaning

This was not mere storytelling. Jesus directly addressed the confrontation between Israel's leaders and himself. The servants represented the prophets God had sent through the centuries, calling Israel to justice and righteousness but finding only rejection and violence. The son was Jesus himself—the heir, the final messenger, whom the leaders would reject and kill.

God's patience had been extraordinary. He had sent messenger after messenger, giving Israel chance after chance to respond. There was no excuse for their rejection.

> What then will the owner of the vineyard do to them? He will come and kill those tenants and give the vineyard to others.

The owner's judgment would be severe. The Greek word ἀπολέσει (apolesei) means to utterly ruin or destroy. The vineyard—God's kingdom purposes—would be taken from Israel's leaders and given to others, opening the kingdom to the Gentiles.

## 3. The Consequences of Rejecting Christ (Luke 20:15b-19)

### The Cornerstone and the Stone

> Jesus looked directly at them and asked, "Then what is the meaning of that which is written:
> 
> 'The stone the builders rejected
> has become the cornerstone'?
> 
> Everyone who falls on that stone will be broken to pieces; anyone on whom it falls will be crushed."

Jesus quoted Psalm 118:22, spoken by David when he was in distress and surrounded by enemies. Yet David testified that despite rejection and opposition, God had become his salvation. The stone that builders discarded—deemed worthless and unsuitable—had become the cornerstone, the most essential stone in any building's foundation.

The Greek term κεφαλὴν γωνίας (kephalēn gōnias) emphasizes the cornerstone's critical role. This metaphor declared Christ as the very foundation of God's redemptive plan.

The image carries a warning: rejection of this stone brings destruction. Those who fall on it will be broken to pieces (συνθλασθήσεται, sunthlasthēsetai—utterly shattered). Those upon whom it falls will be crushed. It is like a diamond—attempt to scratch it, and whatever you use becomes dull while the diamond remains unharmed. Christ is indestructible; those who oppose Him are the ones who break.

### The Leaders' Response

> When they heard this, they said, "God forbid!"
> 
> The teachers of the law and the chief priests looked for a way to arrest him immediately, because they knew he had spoken this parable against them. But they were afraid of the people.

The leaders understood perfectly. Jesus had spoken the parable directly against them. They recognized themselves in the story of rejected messengers and murderous tenants. Fear of the crowd's favor toward Jesus prevented them from acting immediately, but their intent was clear: they would find a way to arrest him.

### Application: Where Do We Stand?

Rejecting Christ carries eternal consequences. He is either the cornerstone of our lives—the foundation upon which all else rests—or the stone that crushes those who stumble over Him. There is no neutral ground.

The question Jesus posed then remains urgent now: Do we acknowledge His authority in our lives? Are we listening to God's messengers—pastors, apostles, and the Spirit speaking through Scripture? Have we truly made Jesus the cornerstone of our faith, or do we continue to stumble over Him?

God has not been silent. He has sent many witnesses—prophets, apostles, pastors, the written Word, the Spirit's conviction. Second chances, third chances, countless chances have been given. On the day of judgment, none can claim ignorance or excuse.

Jesus acts with the authority of heaven. He does not wait for our permission. It is His world, not ours. The question is simple: Will we humble ourselves and receive Christ as King, or will we find ourselves shattered by the very stone we have rejected?

Let us recognize His authority, heed His messengers, and make Jesus the foundation of our lives. Amen.
