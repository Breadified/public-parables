# Daniel 6

**Title:** Lions  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew  
**Date:** 11 August 2019

## Historical Context

The conquest of Babylon by Darius the Mede is confirmed by ancient Babylonian records (BM35382), which document how Gubaru (Darius) and the army of Cyrus entered the city without opposition. Darius then established a new administrative structure to prevent the corruption that had plagued previous regimes. He appointed 120 satraps accountable to three administrators—one of whom was the prophet Daniel.

> It pleased Darius to set over the kingdom one hundred and twenty satraps, to be over the whole kingdom; and over these, three governors, of whom Daniel was one, that the satraps might give account to them, so that the king would suffer no loss. Then this Daniel distinguished himself above the governors and satraps, because an excellent spirit was in him; and the king gave thought to setting him over the whole realm. So the governors and satraps sought to find some charge against Daniel concerning the kingdom; but they could find no charge or fault, because he was faithful; nor was there any error or fault found in him. (Daniel 6:1–4)

Daniel's rise to prominence reveals a fundamental truth: **righteousness is priceless in any system designed to prevent corruption.**

## 1. The Enemies of God Target the Righteous

### The Persecution of the Faithful

Throughout history, the righteous have been hunted. Consider the martyrs of the early church—men like Ignatius of Antioch, a church leader who was bound and transported to Rome. In 108 AD, he was thrust into the Colosseum where fierce lions killed him. Before his death, Ignatius wrote to the churches, documenting the horrors inflicted on Christians:

> Whatever the cruelness of man's invention could devise for the punishment of man's body was practiced against the Christians—plates of iron laid unto them burning hot, deep dungeons, racks, strangling in prisons, the teeth of wild beasts and tossing upon the horns of bulls. When they were thus killed, their bodies were laid in heaps with dogs left to guard them, so that no man might come to bury them.

Daniel faced a similar assault in his time. Unable to find genuine fault with his character, his enemies weaponized his greatest virtue—his devotion to God.

> At this, the administrators and the satraps tried to find grounds for charges against Daniel in his conduct of government affairs, but they were unable to do so. Finally these men said, "We will never find any basis for charges against this man Daniel unless we find it has something to do with the law of his God." (Daniel 6:4–5)

### The Law Manipulated Against the Righteous

The conspirators then orchestrated a decree designed to ensnare Daniel:

> So these administrators and satraps went as a group to the king and said: "May King Darius live forever! The royal administrators, prefects, satraps, advisers and governors have all agreed that the king should issue an edict and enforce the decree that anyone who prays to any god or human being during the next thirty days, except to you, Your Majesty, shall be thrown into the lions' den." (Daniel 6:6–7)

What makes this particularly insidious is that **the law itself was not unjust—it was corrupted to target the righteous.** The corrupt administrators twisted a reasonable measure meant to prevent corruption into a weapon against the very man who embodied integrity.

This pattern repeats in our own time. When laws are wielded by those with hostile intent toward Christianity, they become instruments of persecution. Laws that once reflected biblical principles now work against believers. We cannot naively assume that the law is our friend—**it is only as good as the hearts of those who enforce it.**

### Persecution Is Promised

Scripture makes clear that opposition awaits those who follow Christ:

> In fact, everyone who wants to live a godly life in Christ Jesus will be persecuted, while evil men and impostors will go from bad to worse, deceiving and being deceived. (2 Timothy 3:12–13)

Yet we are called to trust in God's faithfulness, not in earthly powers.

## 2. Habits of Faith Will Get You Through

### Daniel's Unwavering Practice

When Daniel learned of the decree, he faced a choice: compromise his faith for thirty days, or maintain his habits of devotion despite the threat of death. He chose faithfulness:

> Now when Daniel learned that the decree had been published, he went home to his upstairs room where the windows opened toward Jerusalem. Three times a day he got down on his knees and prayed, giving thanks to his God, just as he had done before. (Daniel 6:10)

Notice the phrase "just as he had done before." Daniel did not alter his practice. **His habits of faith were so deeply ingrained that persecution could not shake them.**

### The Significance of the Open Windows

Daniel's open windows toward Jerusalem were not a display of defiance but an expression of trust rooted in God's ancient promises. Four hundred years earlier, King Solomon dedicated the temple with a prayer that anticipated this very situation:

> Yet give attention to your servant's prayer and his plea for mercy, Lord my God. Hear the cry and the prayer that your servant is praying in your presence this day. May your eyes be open toward this temple night and day, this place of which you said, "My Name shall be there," so that you will hear the prayer your servant prays toward this place. (1 Kings 8:28–29)

By opening his windows toward Jerusalem, Daniel was appealing to God's covenant promise: though he was in exile and persecuted, he would be heard and rescued. **He was putting his hope in God's faithfulness, not in earthly security.**

### Building Habits Before the Trial Comes

The fact that thirty days was chosen as the decree's duration is telling. Research shows that habits form in twenty-one to sixty-six days. Thirty days is precisely long enough to break an established practice—or to establish a new, destructive one.

The enemies were banking on one month of silence before God to fracture Daniel's faith. But Daniel had spent a lifetime cultivating habits of prayer. When the trial came, he didn't have to think about whether to pray; it was second nature.

> Do not forsake our own assembling together, as is the habit of some, but encourage one another; and all the more as you see the day drawing near. (Hebrews 10:25)

> But solid food is for the mature, who because of practice have their senses trained to discern good and evil. (Hebrews 5:14)

**We must cultivate habits of faith now, while the roar of the lions is still distant.** The time to build spiritual resilience is before persecution comes. Habits of prayer, Bible reading, church attendance, and communion with God become the bedrock that sustains us when trials arrive.

These habits—kneeling to pray, opening Scripture, gathering with believers—are not works that earn God's love. Rather, **they are the disciplines that keep us trusting in God when circumstances tempt us to despair.**

## 3. The Powerless King and the Powerful God

### The Limitation of Earthly Authority

King Darius genuinely cared for Daniel and was distressed by the predicament:

> When the king heard this, he was greatly distressed; he was determined to rescue Daniel and made every effort until sundown to save him. (Daniel 6:14)

Yet despite his power and goodwill, the king was helpless. The very law he had signed—designed to prevent corruption—now bound his hands:

> Then the men went as a group to King Darius and said to him, "Remember, Your Majesty, that according to the law of the Medes and Persians no decree or edict that the king issues can be changed." (Daniel 6:15)

**Even a king, for all his authority, cannot change the consequences of his own decrees.** Darius was forced to throw Daniel into the lions' den. That night, the king fasted, could not sleep, and was tormented by what had been done.

> Then the king returned to his palace and spent the night without eating and without any entertainment being brought to him. And he could not sleep. (Daniel 6:18)

### The God Who Rescues

But there is a God more powerful than kings:

> At the first light of dawn, the king got up and hurried to the lions' den. When he came near the den, he called to Daniel in an anguished voice, "Daniel, servant of the living God, has your God, whom you serve continually, been able to rescue you from the lions?" Daniel answered, "May the king live forever! My God sent his angel, and he shut the mouths of the lions. They have not hurt me, because I was found innocent in his sight." (Daniel 6:19–22)

Daniel emerged without a scratch. **Better to be in a lions' den with God than outside that den without Him.**

The lesson is clear: do not fear men. Fear God, who alone has authority over life and death. Do not fear lions—fear the Lion of Judah, who descended into death itself and rose in three days.

### Judgment Comes for the Wicked

The justice of God is also revealed:

> At the king's command, the men who had falsely accused Daniel were brought in and thrown into the lions' den, along with their wives and children. And before they reached the floor of the den, the lions overpowered them and crushed all their bones. (Daniel 6:24)

The conspirators received the very fate they had plotted for Daniel. All will give account before God, even kings.

### The King's Declaration

Even the pagan king recognized God's supremacy and issued a new decree:

> Then King Darius wrote to all the nations and peoples of every language in all the earth: "May you prosper greatly! I issue a decree that in every part of my kingdom people must fear and reverence the God of Daniel. For he is the living God and he endures forever; his kingdom will not be destroyed, his dominion will never end. He rescues and he saves; he performs signs and wonders in the heavens and on the earth. He has rescued Daniel from the power of the lions." (Daniel 6:25–27)

> So Daniel prospered during the reign of Darius and the reign of Cyrus the Persian. (Daniel 6:28)

## Closing Reflection

The martyrs who came after Daniel understood this truth. Ignatius of Antioch, facing death in the Colosseum, wrote to the churches:

> "To be near the sword is to be near to God, in the midst of wild beasts is in the midst of God, if only it be in the name of Jesus Christ, that we may suffer with Him. All things I endure, since He, the perfect man, makes me strong."

And again:

> "I write to all the churches and charge them all to know that I die willingly for God. I am God's grain, and I am ground by the teeth of wild beasts, that I may be found the pure bread of Christ. Then I shall truly be a disciple of Christ, when the world shall not see even my body."

The call to faithfulness in Daniel's story is the call to build godly character through habits of devotion—to develop an inner resilience that persecution cannot shake. The time to prepare is now, while we still have the freedom to pray openly, to gather with believers, and to cultivate the spiritual disciplines that will sustain us.

Our habits are what keep us trusting in God.
