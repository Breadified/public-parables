## Understanding Submission

> Ephesians 5:21 Submit to one another out of reverence for Christ.

Submission is a word that makes us uncomfortable. But biblically, it means to operate under another's authority. Throughout Ephesians, Paul emphasizes Christ's authority over all things:

> Ephesians 1:22 And God placed all things under his feet and appointed him to be head over everything for the church.

The book of Ephesians centers on reconciliation—first with God, then with one another. God has placed all things under Christ's feet, including us, and has provided the way for this reconciliation graciously.

> Ephesians 2:19–22 Consequently, you are no longer foreigners and aliens, but fellow citizens with God's people and members of God's household, built on the foundation of the apostles and prophets, with Christ Jesus himself as the chief cornerstone. In him the whole building is joined together and rises to become a holy temple in the Lord. And in him you too are being built together to become a dwelling in which God lives by his Spirit.

### Reconciliation Between One Another

Notice that verse 21 doesn't say "submit to Christ"—it says "submit to *each other*." This reveals the second dimension of reconciliation: not just between us and God, but between one another.

In the ancient world, Jews and Gentiles were traditional enemies. Yet Paul declares that because both are now united under Christ, they are reconciled to each other. It's like Scotland and England in the Middle Ages—fierce rivals, but through various circumstances, one king came to rule both nations. With both under the one king, they became united, not because they liked each other or shared culture, but because of their common allegiance to their sovereign. As Paul writes:

> Ephesians 4:3–6 Make every effort to keep the unity of the Spirit through the bond of peace. There is one body and one Spirit—just as you were called to one hope when you were called—one Lord, one faith, one baptism; one God and Father of all, who is over all and through all and in all.

The kingdoms of Jews and Gentiles have been reconciled under one king. They are no longer at war. The bond that unites them is faith in Christ. And that is why verse 21 says: *Submit to one another out of reverence for Christ.*

### Submission According to Authority

Here's the key: submission is not mutual or symmetrical. It follows proper order. When Paul says "submit to one another," he means submit according to appropriate authorities—as spelled out in what follows: the household table, with its ordered relationships in society.

## Three Household Relationships

In the Greek and Roman world, the household was the principal institution for organizing human society—the foundation of the state. It was more than a nuclear family; it encompassed extended family, helpers, slaves, and sometimes strangers. It was a hub of activity.

Paul addresses three major relationships within the household:

### a. Husbands and Wives

> Ephesians 5:22–24 Wives, submit to your husbands as to the Lord. For the husband is the head of the wife as Christ is the head of the church, his body, of which he is the Savior. Now as the church submits to Christ, so also wives should submit to their husbands in everything.

Wives submit not because they and their husbands get along, or because they are in love, or because he is a good husband. They submit because he is under Jesus, and she is under him.

> Ephesians 5:25–27 Husbands, love your wives, just as Christ loved the church and gave himself up for her to make her holy, cleansing her by the washing with water through the word, and to present her to himself as a radiant church, without stain or wrinkle or any other blemish, but holy and blameless.

The husband's love is expressed through leadership—leading her toward holiness, just as Christ leads the church.

This teaching is deeply unpopular today, and rightly so in response to real cases of abuse. Those abuses are wrong—they represent husbands who did not love as Christ loved. But in responding to abuse and misogyny, we must not discard this instruction.

**For the unmarried:** This teaching determines how you choose a future spouse.

**For young women:** As you evaluate a potential partner, ask yourself: Can I submit to his authority? If he's selfish or a jerk, why consider him? If he's not a Christian, how can you trust he'll lead you toward holiness before Christ? Even if he's a Christian, that doesn't automatically make him a good husband. If he doesn't read his Bible, isn't interested in the things of God, is immature, or isn't working on his godliness—why risk it? He may grow in the future, but until he does, he shouldn't be an option.

**For young men:** Ask yourself: Who can I present as a bride to Christ? Will she listen to you? Can you lead her? How will you lead her spotless before Christ if she's not a Christian? Are you mature enough to lead her? Marriage is teamwork—you must work well together to manage the business of family.

### b. Children and Parents

> Ephesians 6:1–4 Children, obey your parents in the Lord, for this is right. "Honor your father and mother"—which is the first commandment with a promise—"that it may go well with you and that you may enjoy long life on the earth." Fathers, do not exasperate your children; instead, bring them up in the training and instruction of the Lord.

One of marriage's primary goals is children, and raising them is difficult work. Paul addresses fathers here because the father is deemed the leader of the family, bearing ultimate responsibility—though sadly, many of us are lazy and expect our wives to do all the work. We marry our mothers and expect them to remain our mothers.

*How do children obey their parents "in the Lord"?* It means obeying them as you would obey God.

**For young adults:** Here's my challenge: keep communicating with your parents about your commitments. The problem is often not the activity itself but lack of notice. If you have a friend's party next Saturday, tell them today—don't wait until Friday to drop the news, expecting them to say yes. If you have a church commitment, let them know in advance the moment you learn about it. Most of the time, they'll allow it.

But we're tempted to hide things. We want to attend an event Friday night and Amazing Grace on Sunday, but our parents will say no to both. So we mention only Friday and hope they'll understand when we reveal Sunday on Saturday. That won't work. You must prioritize and honor your parents by being honest about your schedule. They pay for it, take you there, and worry about you—it's the least you can do.

Be a people who are direct and clear, not complicated or opaque. Talk straight with your folks.

### c. Slaves and Masters

> Ephesians 6:5–9 Slaves, obey your earthly masters with respect and fear, and with sincerity of heart, just as you would obey Christ. Obey them not only to win their favor when their eye is on you, but like slaves of Christ, doing the will of God from your heart. Serve wholeheartedly, as if you were serving the Lord, not men, because you know that the Lord will reward everyone for whatever good he does, whether he is slave or free. And masters, treat your slaves in the same way. Do not threaten them, since you know that he who is both their Master and yours is in heaven, and there is no favoritism with him.

**Why doesn't Paul condemn slavery?** This troubling question deserves attention.

#### The Difference Between Slavery and Slave-Trading

The Bible distinguishes between slavery as an institution and the slave trade—the kidnapping and selling of humans. Paul condemns slave-trading explicitly:

> 1 Timothy 1:9–10 [Laws are made] for lawbreakers and rebels, the ungodly and sinful, the unholy and irreligious; for those who kill their fathers or mothers, for murderers, for adulterers and perverts, for slave traders and liars and perjurers.

The Atlantic slave trade was disgusting and terrible. But that's different from the biblical practice of voluntary servitude or debt slavery.

In the ancient world, if you fell into debt or committed a wrong, you could enter slavery to pay it off—a form of indentured servitude. This isn't fundamentally different from what we do today with mortgages. You borrow a large sum and are in bondage until you pay it off. You're not free, but you entered the arrangement by choice. If you try to escape your obligations, you face even worse bondage—prison.

So the idea of bondage itself isn't inherently wrong. The real issue is about what *freedom* means.

#### Freedom Reconsidered

We misunderstand freedom. The issue isn't bondage itself—we're all under some form of bondage. Today, we simply choose the nature of our bondage. We choose our school, our course, our job, our mortgage amount, our lifestyle. We choose our masters.

However, some relationships can't be changed—marriage, parenthood, or in the first-century context, slavery. These are fixed.

#### Working Within the Societal Order

Paul's second concern isn't overthrowing societal institutions but changing people *within* those institutions. He's not interested in revolution; he's interested in how we should behave within the existing order.

In modern society, we have the privilege of changing our circumstances. We can move between jobs, churches, governments, and even countries. But not everyone has that privilege, and historically, people haven't. If you can't change your situation, the Christian response is not to maliciously sabotage the system—that's not Christian. Freedom doesn't mean "do what you want." It means you are *different now*. Be an honest slave. Be a worker with integrity.

#### The Slave's Responsibility

> Ephesians 6:5–7 Slaves, obey your earthly masters with respect and fear, and with sincerity of heart, just as you would obey Christ. Obey them not only to win their favor when their eye is on you, but like slaves of Christ, doing the will of God from your heart. Serve wholeheartedly, as if you were serving the Lord, not men.

Work hard. Don't steal—not office supplies, not time on the internet, not even napkins. Do your job with wholehearted sincerity, as if serving Christ himself.

> Ephesians 6:8 because you know that the Lord will reward everyone for whatever good he does, whether he is slave or free.

Your reward comes from God, not your earthly master.

#### The Master's Responsibility

> Ephesians 6:9 And masters, treat your slaves in the same way. Do not threaten them, since you know that he who is both their Master and yours is in heaven, and there is no favoritism with him.

Masters have a different responsibility: to use their power to look after those under them. Whether you're a boss, a Bible study leader, a committee member, or any person with authority—your role is to care for those weaker than yourself. 

This challenges us. In the 1960s, idealistic young people fought against corrupt authorities, hoping to change the world. But many who came into power repeated the same mistakes as their predecessors. They lost their idealism, bought into money and materialism, and forgot to look after the weak. As a future boss or leader, the bottom line isn't everything. Your employees matter. As a person in power, will you cut your own pay for their sake? Will you remember that Christ is your Master too, and He shows no favoritism?

## Submit to One Another in the Spirit

> Ephesians 5:19–21 Speaking to one another with psalms, hymns and spiritual songs. Singing and making music in your heart to the Lord, always giving thanks to God the Father for everything, in the name of our Lord Jesus Christ. Submitting to one another out of reverence for Christ.

Submission is part of what it means to be *filled with the Spirit*. 

Some submission roles are interchangeable—police, doctors, Bible study leaders, experts, chairpersons—depending on context. Others are not: parent-child, husband-wife, master-servant. These are fixed.

But in *every* case, there's no room for anarchy. In Christianity, we don't do what we feel like regardless of those around us. Spirit-filled life includes self-control and proper order.

Throughout history, genuine revival has sometimes been confused with chaos. The Reformation in Münster became anarchic because there was no order. True spirituality produces order, not disorder. The Spirit produces self-control.

Submit to one another—in the proper order, with reverence for Christ.
