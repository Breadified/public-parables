# Exodus 1:1-2:10

**Title:** The People of God

**Date:** 18 August 2024

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## The World's Trajectory

Our world is moving backward. Recent events—from Olympic corruption and nepotism to the blurring of fundamental categories—reveal a culture in decline. The narcissistic orientation of our age, where children are treated as stepping stones to careers and social media fame rather than as blessings, reflects a deeper spiritual poverty.

Yet in the midst of this cultural darkness, we see a demographic winter descending. The Malthusian Population Bomb never materialized; instead, aging societies are losing their dynamism, creativity, and vitality. When populations shrink, economies stagnate, innovation falters, and civilizations decay. Just as modernity was built on population growth, it will degrade through population decline—visible in the urban decay of Detroit, abandoned Japanese villages, and crumbling European communities.

**Is there a way out?**

The book of Exodus speaks directly to our condition. Israel found itself enslaved, oppressed, stuck in idolatry and sin, seemingly condemned. Yet God's people experienced something remarkable: not escape, but multiplication. Their story is our story.

---

## Part 1: The People (Exodus 1:1-7)

### The Promise Fulfilled

Exodus begins with "and"—a continuation of Genesis. The seventy members of Jacob's family who entered Egypt with Joseph now stand at a generational threshold. Joseph, who saved Egypt from famine and rose to power as prime minister, had secured their welfare and protection. But now a new pharaoh has arisen who remembers nothing of Joseph or Israel's service.

Yet despite the passage of time and the change in leadership, something extraordinary occurs:

> They were exceedingly fruitful; they multiplied greatly, increased in numbers and became so numerous that the land was filled with them.

The language is deliberately repetitive and emphatic:
- **Fruitful** (phar) — bearing fruit abundantly
- **Multiplied** (shara) — swarmed like locusts
- **Greatly** (rav) — exceedingly numerous
- **Filled** (mala) — the land swarmed with them

This is the creation mandate at work. God had commanded Adam and Eve to "be fruitful and increase in number; fill the earth." That same blessing echoes through Noah after the flood, and now through Israel in Egypt. The Israelites were carrying out their divine calling—not merely having children, but trusting in God's promises while fulfilling the purpose He had given humanity at creation.

### The Blessing Multiplies

From 70 to 600,000 fighting men (plus women and children—possibly 2-3 million total). By any calculation, whether literal or symbolic, this represents explosive growth. Over 400 years, needing only four children per generation, one can understand how a small family becomes a nation. But the deeper question remains: *Why did they keep having children in slavery?*

The answer reveals the Israelites' faith. They were putting their hope in God's promises. Though they lived in a foreign land under oppression, they remembered the covenant God had made to Abraham:

> I will make you into a great nation, and I will bless you; I will make your name great, and you will be a blessing. (Genesis 12:2)

And later:

> Look up at the sky and count the stars—if indeed you can count them. So shall your offspring be. (Genesis 15:5-6)

Despite everything, they believed God. They had children because they trusted that God would make good on His word.

### Children as God's Design

This brings us to a crucial question for today: Does the creation mandate still apply? Has Jesus' death and resurrection changed God's view of children?

The mandate to be fruitful was given at creation—before sin, before the fall, before redemption. While the gospel has indeed reoriented our ultimate hope (we now await the heavenly Jerusalem, not an earthly promised land), the creation mandate remains. Marriage remains. And children remain a natural and good fruit of marriage.

Yet look at the vision of the redeemed in Revelation:

> A great multitude that no one could count, from every nation, tribe, people and language, standing before the throne. (Revelation 7:9)

This multitude is made of *people*—countless people, "no one could count them." To populate eternity with worshippers requires children. To accomplish God's purpose of filling the earth with those who bear His image and know His salvation requires childbearing.

Consider Malachi's profound statement about marriage:

> Has not the one God made you? You belong to him in body and spirit. And what does the one God seek? Godly offspring. (Malachi 2:14-15)

God joins couples together specifically to produce godly offspring—the next generation of believers who will know Jesus, serve Him, and extend His kingdom.

### Children as Blessing and Inheritance

The Psalms celebrate this truth:

> Children are a heritage from the Lord, offspring a reward from him. Like arrows in the hands of a warrior are children born in one's youth. Blessed is the man whose quiver is full of them. (Psalm 127:3-5)

Notice the metaphor: children are *arrows*—gifts with future potential. They are *heritage*—not for me, but for the future. They are *reward*—God's gift to the world, not liabilities to my comfort.

Our modern economic lens has inverted this. In agrarian societies, more children meant more helping hands; they were assets. In cities, we see them as liabilities—another mouth to feed, another expense. So we minimize, control, or eliminate. But this thinking contradicts Scripture's entire testimony.

Martin Luther, in his writings on marriage, captured this beautifully:

> In raising children, we help lay the foundation for future societies and civilizations to flourish. Little children who will one day grow up to be oaks of righteousness breathe their first breaths in our home as little saplings. They are the chief end and purpose of the marital union—a gift to be stewarded by mothers and fathers.

### The Internal vs. External Challenge

We face real obstacles. Many are enslaved by economic necessity—both spouses must work, housing is expensive, children require resources. The pressure against having children is enormous: abortion is presented as freedom, smaller families as enlightened, and those who choose fertility as naive or reckless.

But the external obstacles, while real, are not the hardest hurdle. The real battle is *internal*—within our own hearts and minds.

It's a choice between:
- **Cultural values vs. kingdom values** — Will I embrace the world's definition of success (career, travel, possessions, personal fulfillment)?
- **My dreams vs. God's promises** — Will I trust that children are a blessing, or cling to my vision of the perfect life?
- **My ambitions vs. God's glory** — Will I submit to His design for flourishing, or insist on my own?

The Israelites made a different choice. Even in slavery, they had children. They believed God. They trusted His promises. And God honored their faith by multiplying them despite opposition.

## Part 2: Persecution (Exodus 1:8-2:10)

### The Threat

Success breeds opposition. A new pharaoh arises who views the Israelite population not as an economic asset but as a threat:

> The Israelites have become far too numerous for us. If war breaks out, they will join our enemies, fight against us and leave the country. (Exodus 1:9-10)

The king recognizes a basic sociological truth: when a minority population becomes visible and numerous, the majority power feels threatened. Control is slipping. Numbers matter in any society, particularly in a democracy.

Pharaoh implements the first of three escalating attempts to suppress Israel's growth:

### First Attempt: Enslavement

> So they put slave masters over them to oppress them with forced labor. (Exodus 1:11)

The Israelites are conscripted to build store cities—Pithom and Rameses—to serve Pharaoh's ambitions. Their labor becomes harsh, bitter, relentless:

> Ruthlessly. Bitter. Harsh labor. The Egyptians worked them ruthlessly. (Exodus 1:13-14)

Yet something astonishing happens:

> But the more they were oppressed, the more they multiplied and spread; so the Egyptians came to dread the Israelites. (Exodus 1:12)

Oppression backfires. Like trying to eliminate pests only to see them develop resistance and multiply further—Pharaoh is not fighting weakness but divine promise. He is playing against God, and God's plans cannot be thwarted.

### Second Attempt: Infanticide Through Midwives

When slavery fails to control population, Pharaoh turns to the midwives—Shiphrah and Puah—with a horrifying command:

> When you are helping the Hebrew women during childbirth, if you see that the baby is a boy, kill him; but if it is a girl, let her live. (Exodus 1:16)

This is abortion. Gender-selective infanticide. A calculated attempt to eliminate the next generation of male warriors while allowing girls to be absorbed into Egyptian society through intermarriage.

But the midwives *feared God* more than they feared Pharaoh:

> The midwives, however, feared God and did not do what the king of Egypt had told them to do; they let the boys live. (Exodus 1:17)

This is crucial. God's law supersedes human law. The midwives understood what modern conscience has largely forgotten: you cannot simply obey unjust commands and absolve yourself of responsibility. You must *think* and *choose*. You must fear God.

When questioned, they offer an explanation (whether truthful or a clever deflection):

> Hebrew women are not like Egyptian women; they are vigorous and give birth before the midwives arrive. (Exodus 1:19)

And God blesses them:

> So God was kind to the midwives and the people increased and became even more numerous. And because the midwives feared God, he gave them families of their own. (Exodus 1:20-21)

The women who risked their lives to preserve life received the gift of children—those very children they had saved through their obedience.

### Third Attempt: Mass Drowning

Still thwarted, Pharaoh escalates to total terror:

> Every Hebrew boy that is born you must throw into the Nile. (Exodus 1:22)

Now every Egyptian becomes an enforcer of genocide. The Nile—Egypt's god and life-source—becomes the instrument of death. The irony is bitter: the same river that sustained Egypt will be choked with the blood of children.

One family is particularly affected. A Levite couple has a son:

> When she saw that he was a fine child, she hid him for three months. But when she could hide him no longer, she got a papyrus basket for him and coated it with tar and pitch. Then she placed the child in it and put it among the reeds along the bank of the Nile. His sister stood at a distance to see what would happen to him. (Exodus 2:2-4)

The mother follows the letter of Pharaoh's law—she places the baby in the Nile—but she does so safely, in the reeds, with her daughter standing watch. She is not abandoning her son; she is trusting him to God's care.

Then the unexpected occurs:

> Pharaoh's daughter went down to the Nile to bathe. She saw the basket among the reeds and sent her female slave to get it. She opened it and saw the baby. He was crying, and she felt sorry for him. (Exodus 2:5-6)

Pharaoh's own daughter hears the cries of a Hebrew child and feels compassion. She draws him out of the water—the very action that makes his name: *Moses* (he who is drawn out).

Through God's providence, the child who should have died becomes the adopted son of Pharaoh's daughter, raised in the royal household, educated, and prepared—though he doesn't yet know it—to become Israel's liberator.

### The Pattern Emerges

Despite opposition, Israel grew. Despite infanticide, the next generation was born. Despite a new pharaoh, God remembered His covenant. The Israelites' willingness to have children—even in oppression—was an act of faith. And God honored that faith.

## Part 3: Progress Stalled (Exodus 1:10, 22; 2:22)

### The Promise Unfulfilled

Yet there is a problem. The people multiply, yes. But they remain *aliens in a foreign land*:

> Moses named him Gershom, saying, I have become an alien in a foreign land. (Exodus 2:22)

In the ancient Near East, land is everything. It represents identity, security, inheritance, and God's blessing. Abraham received the promise repeatedly:

> To your offspring I will give this land. (Genesis 12:7)

> All the land that you see I will give to you and your offspring forever. (Genesis 13:15)

> I am the Lord, who brought you out of Ur of the Chaldeans to give you this land to take possession of it. (Genesis 15:7)

> The whole land of Canaan, where you are now an alien, I will give as an everlasting possession to you and your descendants. (Genesis 17:8)

The promise was land. But Israel has none. They have multiplied, yes. They have fulfilled the creation mandate, yes. But they remain enslaved in Egypt, far from the promised land, still waiting, still hoping.

**Here is where progress stalls.** Numbers are not enough. Blessing without a home is incomplete. The people have been fruitful, but the promise remains unrealized.

## Part 4: The God Who Hears and Saves (Exodus 2:23-3:7)

### The Cry Reaches Heaven

Forty years pass. Pharaoh dies. But Israel's suffering continues:

> During that long period, the king of Egypt died. The Israelites groaned in their slavery and cried out, and their cry for help because of their slavery went up to God. God heard their groaning and he remembered his covenant with Abraham, with Isaac and with Jacob. So God looked on the Israelites and was concerned about them. (Exodus 2:23-25)

Notice the progression:
- They cry out
- God *hears*
- God *remembers* the covenant
- God is *concerned*

This should not surprise us. If an Egyptian princess can hear a baby's cry and feel compassion, drawing him out of the water (v. 6), and if Moses can hear the cries of Jethro's daughters and rescue them, drawing water for them (v. 19)—then of course God hears His people's cries.

To remember is not to suddenly recall a forgotten promise. Rather, it means God does not abandon His word. He remains faithful.

### The Burning Bush

Then, in the wilderness of Midian:

> Moses was tending the flock of his father-in-law when he came to Horeb, the mountain of God. There the angel of the Lord appeared to him in flames of fire from within a bush. The bush was on fire but did not burn up. (Exodus 3:1-2)

In that impossible moment—fire without consumption—God calls:

> Moses! Moses! (Exodus 3:4)

> I have indeed seen the misery of my people in Egypt. I have heard them crying out because of their slave drivers, and I am concerned about their suffering. So I have come down to rescue them from the hand of the Egyptians and to bring them up out of that land into a good and spacious land, a land flowing with milk and honey. (Exodus 3:7-8)

God's response is immediate, personal, and comprehensive. He has *seen*, *heard*, and is *concerned*. Now He will *act*.

### The Mission and the Fear

God sends Moses:

> Go. I am sending you to Pharaoh to bring my people the Israelites out of Egypt. (Exodus 3:10)

But Moses hesitates:

> Who am I, that I should go to Pharaoh and bring the Israelites out of Egypt? (Exodus 3:11)

God's answer is profound:

> I will be with you. (Exodus 3:12)

Not "you are qualified" or "you are strong enough." Simply: *I will be with you.* That is all that matters.

### The Name of God

Moses then asks the name of God—who should he say has sent him?

> God said to Moses, "I AM WHO I AM. This is what you are to say to the Israelites: 'I AM has sent me to you.'" (Exodus 3:14)

> God also said to Moses, "Say to the Israelites, 'The Lord, the God of your fathers—the God of Abraham, the God of Isaac and the God of Jacob—has sent me to you. This is my name forever, the name by which I am to be remembered from generation to generation.'" (Exodus 3:15)

#### Understanding Yahweh

The name Yahweh (written as LORD in English, from the letters YHWH) emerges from this encounter. "I AM WHO I AM" or "I will be who I will be."

What sort of name is this? It is not like our names, which describe our essence as static things. Instead, it speaks of *being* and *action*. "I AM" is present tense. "I will be" is future. It is as if God is saying: *I am known by what I do. Look to my deeds. That is my name.*

In the ancient Near East, names were deeply significant. Moses' name means "drawn out"—referring to his rescue from the water. Gershom means "alien in a foreign land"—reflecting the family's exile. Similarly, God's name is not an abstract designation but a declaration: *I am the God who acts, who saves, who is present.*

As we read through Exodus, we will see exactly who this God is. Through the plagues, the Passover, the parting of the sea, the provision in the wilderness—God will reveal His character through His mighty works. "I AM WHO I AM" means: *I am the Savior.*

### The Promise Renewed

And now the original promise is renewed:

> I will stretch out my hand and strike the Egyptians with all the wonders that I will perform among them. After that, he will let you go. And I will make the Egyptians favorably disposed toward this people, so that when you leave you will not go empty-handed. (Exodus 3:19-21)

Israel will not merely escape slavery—they will leave with blessing. They will plunder Egypt, taking silver, gold, and clothing. The Lord will restore what was stolen through decades of forced labor.

---

## Application: The God of Our Salvation

Over a thousand years later, another name emerges: *Jesus*, which means "the Lord saves." This is the continuation of God's character. He is still the God who hears. Still the God who cares. Still the God who saves.

On the cross, Jesus enacted the ultimate exodus—a rescue from sin and death itself. His resurrection is the sign that God keeps His promises, that the grave cannot hold His people, that a new world is coming where the redeemed will dwell in God's presence forever.

### What is Your Name?

But here we must ask ourselves: *What is your name?* 

Are you a **Believer**—one who has heard God's call and responded? Have you accepted His salvation, trusting that Jesus drew you out of death into life?

Or are you still **wandering**—like Moses in Midian, far from God's purpose, unaware of the calling God has for you?

God hears the cries of those who suffer. He sees injustice. He remembers His promises. And He comes to rescue. But He calls us to move beyond mere recognition of His power to *active faith*—to believe His promises, to trust His character, and to join Him in His work of redemption.

The Israelites' multiplication was an act of faith. Their willingness to have children despite oppression testified to their belief that God would keep His word. That same faith is available to us: not primarily about having children (though that remains a blessing), but about trusting God with our lives, our futures, our families, and our place in His unfolding story of salvation.

- How does the character of God revealed in Exodus shape your understanding of His care for you today?
- What would it look like for you to have the faith of the Israelites—to continue believing God's promises even in difficult circumstances?
