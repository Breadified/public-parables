# Matthew 16:13-28

**Title:** Discipleship: Follow the Son!
**Date:** 8 February 2010
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Misaligned Expectations

When Jesus asked his disciples, "Who do people say the Son of Man is?" the answers revealed what Israel expected of their Messiah. Some said John the Baptist—the greatest prophet, whom even King Herod feared for his power over people. Others said Elijah or Jeremiah. These were the heroes of Israel, and Jesus was expected to be another great prophet like them.

But Peter's answer went further. "You are the Messiah, the Son of the living God." In calling Jesus the Christ—literally, the Anointed One—Peter identified him as the promised King. The Christ was not a surname but a title. In Jewish tradition, kings were anointed with oil, not crowned. Peter was saying: You are the King who will rule the world, who will bring justice, who will save the oppressed and humble the proud.

Jesus affirmed Peter's confession: "Blessed are you, Simon son of Jonah, for this was not revealed to you by flesh and blood, but by my Father in heaven." From this rock—from Peter's confession of Christ—Jesus would build his church. This gathering echoed God's ancient pattern: the gathering at Mount Sinai, the movement to Jerusalem, the scattering in exile, and now the promise of gathering again. Jesus himself becomes the rock upon which the church is built, just as he is the rock that followed Israel in the wilderness.

In this moment of high expectation, with excitement charged through the room, Jesus delivered a shocking statement: "From that time on Jesus began to explain to his disciples that he must go to Jerusalem and suffer many things at the hands of the elders, the chief priests and the teachers of the law, and that he must be killed and on the third day be raised to life."

The Messiah—the King—would be a servant who dies? It made no sense. When you gain ultimate power, you don't release it. It's like achieving the highest academic score and announcing you'll work as a cleaner. It's like telling your parents you want to be a missionary. The world doesn't operate this way.

Peter rebuked Jesus: "Never, Lord! This shall never happen to you!" He schooled Jesus as though Jesus didn't understand how the world works. But Peter didn't grasp what radical discipleship truly meant.

Jesus turned to Peter with fierce clarity: "Get behind me, Satan! You are a stumbling block to me; you do not have in mind the concerns of God, but merely human concerns." Peter, the rock, had become a stumbling block. The problem was self-interest. Peter didn't want to die. If his leader—his protector—died, who would be next? It was like joining the Imperial Navy just as Kamikaze pilots were being recruited. Bad timing. And more fundamentally, Peter's mind was on human concerns, not God's concerns.

## His Disciples Must Also Be Servants

Then Jesus issued the call that would reshape everything: "Whoever wants to be my disciple must deny themselves and take up their cross and follow me."

This wasn't metaphorical softness. In the first century, taking up one's cross meant literal death. Condemned prisoners carried the heavy crossbeam to the place of execution, where it would be attached to the vertical pole. It was an image of execution, of the end of everything.

For Jesus, this meant something profound: We are sinners condemned to eternal death. Jesus took that punishment upon himself instead—a divine swap. He denied himself and carried his cross so that we would not have to. And if this is true of Jesus, it must be true of those who follow him.

The Christian life is a call to self-sacrifice. We are radical disciples, moving toward our own execution—not a quick, dramatic death like a Kamikaze pilot, but a slow, deliberate laying down of our interests for the interests of Christ. Unlike the Kamikaze pilot, who dies for national pride or personal paradise, we die out of love for others' gospel welfare.

Our ambition cannot be the comfortable trajectory: secure a good career, establish ourselves in a nice suburb, acquire the right possessions, then—if there's room—serve God in whatever spare time remains. This is the trap many fall into. We say, "I'll serve when I'm rich and successful, when people can look up to me and want to follow. Let me be popular first, then I'll do the unpopular thing. I'll sacrifice when it doesn't hurt to sacrifice."

But Jesus demands something else entirely. He wants 100 percent, not 10 percent. He wants disciples whose ambition is fundamentally reoriented: to slowly die for others.

What does this look like in practice? It means:
- Organizing your time around others' needs, not your comfort
- Witnessing to your family and giving up distractions to do it well
- Using university to reach the lost rather than pursue endless parties
- Living simply so you can give generously
- Choosing work based on what the world needs, not what you find comfortable
- Being willing to leave home, security, and the familiar to serve as a pastor or missionary

This is the narrow path Jesus spoke of. It's the path where leaders serve, where the Son of Man had nowhere to lay his head. It's the inverse of worldly wisdom. But consider what we gain:

> Whoever wants to save their life will lose it, but whoever loses their life for me will find it. What good will it be for someone to gain the whole world, yet forfeit their soul? Or what can anyone give in exchange for their soul?

Death itself should never be our fear. Poverty, suffering, social rejection—these should never master us. Our only fear should be losing our eternal life. And Jesus has solved that problem.

As Jim Elliott once said: "He is no fool to give up what he does not have to gain what he cannot lose."

## Realigning Our Expectations

The establishment of Japan's Kamikaze forces proved that recruitment was easier than expected. The qualifications were simple: "youth, alertness and zeal. Flight experience was of minimal importance and expertise in landing a luxury." In other words, they didn't care about comfort or safe landings. They cared about commitment.

Jesus asks the same: Be radical followers of Christ. Live lives not primarily concerned with comfort, luxury, or security. Live lives that are Christ-centered and others-centered. One truly committed Christian is worth more than a dozen half-hearted ones.

The reward for such faithfulness? Jesus promised it himself:

> The Son of Man is going to come in his Father's glory with his angels, and then he will reward each person according to what they have done. Truly I tell you, some who are standing here will not taste death before they see the Son of Man coming in his kingdom.

This is the call. Not a bait-and-switch, but the kingdom Jesus has always proclaimed: the kingdom where the greatest serve, where the last become first, where death leads to resurrection. Jesus is the King who became a Servant. We are his disciples, called to be servants too.
