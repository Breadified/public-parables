# Numbers 11:1–11:35

**Title:** Seduced by Our Desires

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## The Journey Begins

After leaving Mount Sinai, Israel embarked on an eleven-day journey through the wilderness. The Lord went before them in the ark of the covenant, and a cloud covered them by day. Moses prayed as they traveled:

> "Rise up, O LORD! May your enemies be scattered; may your foes flee before you." (Numbers 10:35)

And when the ark came to rest:

> "Return, O LORD, to the countless thousands of Israel." (Numbers 10:36)

But three days into this trek—much like any camping trip—the complaints began. The terrain had changed from the fertile lands they knew to dry, barren desert. The people started grumbling about their hardships.

## The Pattern of Complaint

The wilderness seasons reveal a recurring cycle that repeats throughout Israel's journey:

**Grumbling → Judgment → Intercession → Resolution → Naming**

When the people complained, the Lord's anger was aroused, and fire from the Lord burned in the camp, consuming some of the outskirts. When the people cried out to Moses, he prayed to the Lord and the fire died down. That place was called Taberah—"fire"—a memorial to God's judgment.

Soon the cycle began again. The rabble among them craved other food, and the Israelites wailed: "If only we had meat to eat! We remember the fish we ate in Egypt at no cost—also the cucumbers, melons, leeks, onions and garlic. But now we have lost our appetite; we never see anything but this manna!"

## The Problem with Their Complaint

The Israelites faced three fundamental problems with their dissatisfaction.

### Their Ungrateful Cravings

The people craved cravingly—lusting after food they remembered from Egypt. Yet consider what they actually had: manna was attractive like resin, convenient to gather daily, versatile (it could be ground into flour, cooked in a pot, or made into cakes), pleasant to taste (like cakes baked with oil), and clean (it fell on a protective layer of dew). Beyond the manna, they had brought flocks and herds from Egypt—sheep, goats, and cattle—which provided milk, butter, cheese, and yogurt. In the middle of the Arabian Peninsula, they were not hungry.

They were ungrateful. They were bored. They were being seduced by nostalgia for a past that the present no longer offered.

Consider how we long for a past that no longer exists—a past we wouldn't actually want to return to. I think of Malaysia, where I grew up: the fried noodles, the popiah, the laksa, the satay. When I speak with visitors from home, I find myself thinking, "Why am I in Australia? If I lived in Malaysia, I could get food in the middle of the night from a hawker stall for spare change. How good would that be?" But then I catch myself. I have it good here. I eat Malaysian food once a month at Istana—yes, it's pricier than in Malaysia, but wages are higher here too, and the hygiene is better. And why would I want food in the middle of the night anyway? Life isn't about food. There are far more important things.

### Their Distorted Memory

What they said reveals the heart of their problem: "We remember the fish we ate in Egypt at no cost."

At no cost? They ate for free because they were *slaves*.

They paid with their freedom. They paid with their sweat, laboring from morning until sunset making bricks for Pharaoh's building projects. Their lives were made bitter with hard labor in brick and mortar. They paid with whipping and subjugation. They paid with their lives and their children's lives.

Pharaoh had commanded: "Every boy that is born you must throw into the Nile, but let every girl live." And they call this freedom? They call this "at no cost"?

What a misled memory. The Israelites were not free. There was no additional cost only because they had already paid with their very souls.

**Application:** We have been rescued from slavery to sin. When we think back to our former lives before Christ, we must never be tempted to think that life was better. We were slaves to sin, slaves to the devil, on a road leading to eternal death. We may have been enjoying ourselves, perhaps gaining the whole world, but we were paying the ultimate price—our souls. As Paul reminds us:

> "But thanks be to God that, though you used to be slaves to sin, you wholeheartedly obeyed the form of teaching to which you were entrusted. You have been set free from sin and have become slaves to righteousness." (Romans 6:17–18)

Don't go back. Don't slide back into those patterns. Don't decrease in fervor or commitment. Don't slack off in church attendance, in serving, in ministry. For sliding back means doubting God.

### Their Lack of Trust

"Who will give us meat to eat?" they demanded. In this question lies their gravest sin: they no longer knew, or no longer trusted, their God or their leader.

But who had heard their cry when they were enslaved? Who had rescued them from Egypt? Who had sent the plagues on Pharaoh? Who had parted the Red Sea as they fled? Who had given His firstborn so that their firstborn might live? God had done all these things. He had earned their loyalty with blood.

So it makes complete sense that He grew exceedingly angry.

## The Judgment and Mercy

When Moses heard the people wailing at the entrance of every tent, the Lord became exceedingly angry. Moses, seeing the crisis, cried out to God:

> "Why have you brought this trouble on your servant? What have I done to displease you that you put the burden of all these people on me? Did I conceive all these people? Did I give them birth? Why do you tell me to carry them in my arms, as a nurse carries an infant, to the land you promised on oath to their forefathers? Where can I get meat for all these people?" (Numbers 11:11–13)

Moses was doing something remarkable here. Rather than grumbling like the people, Moses was interceding. He did three things to deflect God's anger from the people:

**First, he redirected God's focus to himself.** All his questions began with "I" and "me"—making it his responsibility, his burden. He was essentially saying, "Be angry with me instead of them."

**Second, he reframed the people's complaint.** The people had asked, "Who will give us meat?" but Moses reported that they were begging *him* to give them meat. This made them sound less blasphemous toward God and more dependent on Moses.

**Third, he reminded God of His own character.** "Did I conceive these people? Did I give them birth?" Of course not—God did. "Am I their nurse?" No, God sustains them. The implication was that these were God's children, and He had promised to bring them to the land. Why should God abandon the work He had started?

The Lord responded to Moses' intercession by providing seventy elders to share the burden. Then He promised the people meat:

> "Tell the people: 'Consecrate yourselves in preparation for tomorrow, when you will eat meat. The Lord heard you when you wailed, "If only we had meat to eat! We were better off in Egypt!" Now the Lord will give you meat, and you will eat it. You will not eat it for just one day, or two days, or five, ten or twenty days, but for a whole month—until it comes out of your nostrils and you loathe it—because you have rejected the Lord, who is among you.'" (Numbers 11:18–20)

The judgment was ingenious. God gave them exactly what they craved—not out of blessing, but as judgment. The meat became their punishment. They gathered quail for two days and nights, mountains of it, yet while the meat was still between their teeth, the Lord's anger burned against them, and He struck them with a severe plague. The place was named Kibroth Hattaavah—"the graves of craving"—because there they buried those who had craved other food.

## The Root Issue: Envy and Comparison

The deeper problem beneath all this complaining is revealed in James:

> "What causes fights and quarrels among you? Don't they come from your desires that battle within you?" (James 4:1)

We love to compare. We look at our neighbors and want what they have—or we want them not to have what they possess. This desire to compare leads to envy, and envy leads to hatred.

When you see someone in a Porsche getting a parking fine, you think they deserve it. When a celebrity is hounded by the media, you think, "Well, you shouldn't be so famous." When you hear of someone's family problems, you think, "That's what happens when you're too rich." We resent those who have more, and rather than thank God for their blessings, we curse them for being proud or wasteful.

This is envy, and it is deadly:

> "You want something but don't get it. You kill and covet, but you cannot have what you want. You quarrel and fight." (James 4:2)

Envy was the very motivation for Jesus' persecution. The chief priests handed Him over out of envy—envious of the attention He received, the influence He wielded, the popularity that exceeded their own. When we are envious, we are no different from those who put Jesus to death.

The antidote is this: friendship with the world and its values of accumulation equals hatred toward God. "Anyone who chooses to be a friend of the world becomes an enemy of God" (James 4:4).

## The True Mediator

But there is One who mediates perfectly between us and God. Jesus Christ took upon Himself all our sin and rebellion. When He distributed bread and fish to the five thousand, He fed them abundantly and said, "Let nothing be wasted." When He was crucified, He said, "Father, forgive them, for they do not know what they are doing."

Jesus is the bread of life. As He tells us:

> "I am the bread of life. Your forefathers ate the manna in the desert, yet they died. But here is the bread that comes down from heaven, which a man may eat and not die. I am the living bread that came down from heaven. If anyone eats of this bread, he will live forever. This bread is my flesh, which I will give for the life of the world." (John 6:48–51)

Unlike the quail that brought judgment, Jesus' flesh brings life. Unlike the manna that sustained but could not save, the living bread offers eternal salvation. In Him, we have no fear of judgment.

## The Call to Contentment

As we look at our own lives, we must ask: What do we crave? Where are we dissatisfied with what God has given us?

We can be bored with the ordinary rhythms of faith—tired of the same manna, forgetting why it is good to live under Christ. We can be dissatisfied with our churches, our work, our families, our spouses. We long for different seasons, different circumstances, different lives.

We live in an age of unprecedented plenty, yet we have record obesity because we are never satisfied. We must add more sugar, more salt, more of everything. Perhaps just rice and vegetables would do. Perhaps just gratitude would do.

But here is the deeper application: If we cannot be content with these little things in life, how will we ever be content with God Himself? 

When we see what our non-Christian friends seem to enjoy—their apparent freedom, their indulgence in whatever they desire, the time they have for their own pleasures—we begin to long for the days when we too were not thinking of God, when we had no conscience to answer to. We remember the "freedom" of that life and grow dissatisfied with the constraints of following Christ.

But that life was a lie. We were slaves to sin. The food may have seemed free, but we paid with our souls.

## A Word about Leadership and Humility

When the seventy elders received the Spirit, Moses was humble enough to rejoice. When Joshua complained that Eldad and Medad were prophesying in the camp without permission, Moses silenced him:

> "Are you jealous for my sake? I wish that all the Lord's people were prophets and that the Lord would put his Spirit on them!" (Numbers 11:29)

This is the mark of a true leader—not jealous for his own position, but desiring that all God's people would know and serve the Lord. This is the opposite of envy. This is humility.

---

**Be content in God. Do not be seduced by the desires of comparison, by nostalgia for a slavery you've been freed from, or by envy of what others possess. Remember what Christ has done, and be satisfied in Him alone.**
