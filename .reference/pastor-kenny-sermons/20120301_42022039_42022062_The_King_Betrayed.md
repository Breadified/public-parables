# Luke 22:39–62

**Title:** The King Betrayed
**Date:** 18 March 2012 (SMAC); 6 April 2025 (ARISE)
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## A Betrayal

What makes betrayal so devastating? It's the breaking of trust—and betrayal can only be perpetrated by those you trust. An enemy's opposition is expected, but a friend's betrayal strikes at the heart.

We read these final chapters of Luke as we approach Easter, encountering Jesus at his moment of greatest need, abandoned by those closest to him.

## The Faithless Disciples

### The Disciples Betray Jesus (45–46)

Jesus had instructed his followers to pray that they would not fall into temptation. Yet when he returned from prayer in Gethsemane, he found them sleeping.

> When he rose from prayer and went back to the disciples, he found them asleep, exhausted from sorrow. Why are you sleeping? he asked them. Get up and pray so that you will not fall into temptation.

Their exhaustion from sorrow reveals something profound—they sensed what was coming, yet they could not remain vigilant. This too is a form of betrayal through failure.

### Betrayed by Judas (47–48)

The betrayal deepens with cruel irony. Judas, one of the Twelve—the inner circle, the closest friends—approaches Jesus with a kiss.

> While he was still speaking a crowd came up, and the man who was called Judas, one of the Twelve, was leading them. He approached Jesus to kiss him, but Jesus asked him, Judas, are you betraying the Son of Man with a kiss?

The kiss itself is significant. In a world where Passover crowds filled Jerusalem and mistaken identity was possible, a kiss served as identification. But beyond mere practicality, the kiss represents one of life's most intimate gestures—a sign of affection and loyalty turned into an act of treachery. With that single gesture, Judas becomes forever synonymous with betrayal itself.

### Betrayed by Peter (54–62)

But the tragedy deepens further. Peter, the foremost of the apostles, the one who had pledged his willingness to go to prison and death with Jesus, now disowns him entirely.

> Then seizing him, they led him away and took him into the house of the high priest. Peter followed at a distance... A servant girl saw him seated there in the firelight. She looked closely at him and said, This man was with him. But he denied it. Woman, I don't know him, he said.

Three times Peter denies Jesus—first to a servant girl, then to another, and finally to a man who recognizes his Galilean accent. Each denial is a complete disowning. He doesn't merely claim ignorance of Jesus; he denies fellowship with the disciples themselves and severs his very history with the one who called him.

Then comes the moment of reckoning:

> Just as he was speaking, the rooster crowed. The Lord turned and looked straight at Peter. Then Peter remembered the word the Lord had spoken to him: Before the rooster crows today, you will disown me three times. And he went outside and wept bitterly.

That look—was it disappointment? Did Jesus's heart break as he gazed upon his closest friend? Peter had promised absolute loyalty and had delivered absolute denial. How had it come to this?

## The Faithful Jesus

### Not My Will, But Yours Be Done (39–42, 49–53)

In stunning contrast to his disciples, Jesus remains faithful. In Gethsemane, knowing what awaits, he kneels and prays:

> Father, if you are willing, take this cup from me; yet not my will, but yours be done.

The cup symbolizes God's wrath—the divine judgment against sin itself. Jesus does not shrink from this suffering out of cowardice but from a true understanding of what separation from God means. Physical pain and rejection pale in comparison to bearing the sins of the world and enduring the judgment those sins deserve.

Yet he drinks the cup. Why? Because he chooses to be identified with transgressors, to bear their penalty, to make intercession for them. As Isaiah prophesied:

> He poured out his life unto death, and was numbered with the transgressors. For he bore the sin of many, and made intercession for the transgressors.

When the moment of arrest comes and his disciples draw swords, Jesus stops them:

> But Jesus answered, No more of this! And he touched the man's ear and healed him.

The Greek phrase here is better translated as "let it be" or "let it proceed"—Jesus is pointing to the inevitability of God's plan. The hour has come. The cup must be drunk. God's will must be fulfilled. Even in his arrest, Jesus heals his enemy's wound, revealing the character of the one being betrayed.

### Faithful to the Elect (31–32)

Before Peter's denials, Jesus had told him:

> Simon, Simon, look out! Satan has asked to sift you like wheat. But I have prayed for you that your faith may not fail. And you, when you have turned back, strengthen your brothers.

This is the promise that sustained the disciples through their failures. Unlike Judas, whose fall appears final, Peter represents restoration. He will fall, but he will turn back. He will repent. And in his restoration, he will strengthen others.

## The Pattern of Betrayal in Our Lives

How easily the disciples' failure becomes our own. Betrayal of Jesus rarely comes as a single, dramatic decision. More often, it comes through gradual drift:

- A little less frequent at church and Bible studies
- Without fellowship, prayer and Scripture reading fade
- Legitimate activities accumulate until there's no room for Jesus
- One day, he becomes a distant memory

Consider the student who paused his faith to focus on exams, intending to return but never did. Or the believer who compromised his convictions for a relationship, postponing the call to gospel witness until it felt too late. These are not dramatic denials—they are the quiet betrayals we commit through neglect and accommodation.

Yet Jesus remains faithful even when we are faithless. He has prayed for us. Our falling need not be permanent. The call to Peter echoes to us: Turn back. Repent. And having been restored, strengthen your brothers.

## The Challenge: Stay Loyal

How are you walking with Jesus? If you sense yourself drifting, why not do something about it now? Talk to a friend. Seek help. Stay loyal.

The call to faithfulness may seem costly—standing out at school, acknowledging your faith, sharing the gospel. But consider the alternative. If we cannot be faithful in small things, in the face of mild embarrassment or social discomfort, how will we stand when real persecution comes?

The psalm reminds us:

> Kiss the Son, lest he be angry and you be destroyed in your way, for his wrath can flare up in a moment. Blessed are all who take refuge in him.

The choice before us is clear: remain faithful to Jesus, the one who remained faithful to us unto death.
