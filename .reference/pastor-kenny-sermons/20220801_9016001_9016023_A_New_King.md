# 1 Samuel 16:1-23

**Title:** A New King

**Date:** 6 August 2022 (ARISE); Draft from 4 June 2017 (SMAC)

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## An Unlikely Leader

Queen Elizabeth was never meant to be queen. When her grandfather King George V died in 1936, her uncle Edward VIII took the throne. But Edward fell in love with Wallis Simpson, an American divorcee still married to her second husband. Forced to choose between the crown and his love, he abdicated. This made Elizabeth's father, George VI, king—and when he died, Elizabeth unexpectedly became queen after seventy years on the throne.

Similarly, David was an unlikely leader. Like Elizabeth, he came from obscurity to lead a nation. This is the story of how God chose the youngest, overlooked son of Jesse to be king over Israel.

## The Spirit Is on David (1 Samuel 16:1-13)

### God's Different Criteria

The Lord told Samuel to stop mourning for Saul and go to Jesse's house in Bethlehem, for God had already chosen one of Jesse's sons to be king.

> 1 The Lord said to Samuel, "How long will you mourn for Saul, since I have rejected him as king over Israel? Fill your horn with oil and be on your way; I am sending you to Jesse of Bethlehem. I have chosen one of his sons to be king."

God instructed Samuel to disguise his purpose by claiming he came to offer a sacrifice. When Samuel arrived, Jesse presented his sons one by one.

When Samuel saw Eliab, Jesse's eldest, he immediately thought, "Surely this is the Lord's anointed." Eliab was tall and impressive—everything a king should look like by human standards.

But God corrected him:

> 7 But the Lord said to Samuel, "Do not consider his appearance or his height, for I have rejected him. The Lord does not look at the things people look at. People look at the outward appearance, but the Lord looks at the heart."

This was Samuel's lesson from the failure of Saul. God does not choose based on appearance, height, or the qualities that impress nations. He looks at something far deeper—the heart (leb in Hebrew), which represents the command center of a person: the seat of conscience, thinking, will, and character.

Samuel watched as Jesse brought Abinadab, then Shammah, then seven more sons. Each time, the Lord said the same thing: "The Lord has not chosen this one either."

> 11 So he asked Jesse, "Are these all the sons you have?" "There is still the youngest," Jesse answered. "He is tending the sheep." Samuel said, "Send for him; we will not sit down until he arrives."

### David: The Overlooked One

When David arrived, the description in verse 12 initially seems puzzling:

> 12 So he sent for him and had him brought in. He was ruddy and had a fine appearance and handsome features.

This appears to contradict what God just said—that He doesn't choose by appearance. But looking at the Hebrew text reveals nuance. "Ruddy" (admoni) suggests David was red-faced, likely flushed from running back from tending sheep. His "fine appearance" refers to bright eyes, not physical strength or height. The description paints a picture not of an impressive warrior but of a young, somewhat scholarly boy.

In fact, by modern standards, David would likely be called a "nerd." He plays the lyre (verse 18 tells us), writes poetry and songs (as we see in the Psalms), and has the sensitive eyes of someone who reads, creates art, and thinks deeply about the world. He's not the captain of the sports team—he's the kid who starts the chess club and joins the school band.

For many of us, teenage years are hard. Perhaps you're being teased at school, called a nerd, a geek, or worse. Talk to your parents or teachers. But know this: you're not weak or odd or wrong. You're just a kid figuring things out. Life is difficult in those years, but it won't always be like this. You will grow up.

And here's the hope: **God picks David.**

### God Chooses According to His Own Heart

The phrase "the Lord looks at the heart" in verse 7 isn't primarily about David's heart. Rather, it points to God's heart—His character and His choices.

> 13:14 "But now your kingdom will not endure; the Lord has sought out a man after his own heart and appointed him ruler of his people, because you have not kept the Lord's command."

"After his own heart" means someone whom God's heart has chosen—someone He wants to succeed. This is about God's sovereign will and purpose.

Notice also how God consistently chooses the lowliest:

> Deuteronomy 7:7 "The LORD did not set His affection on you and choose you because you were more numerous than the other peoples, for you were the fewest of all peoples."

God chose David—not just someone of poor appearance, but **the least**. He was the youngest, forgotten, unnamed ("the boy"). He tended sheep, the most humble task. There was no boasting possible here. This is God's pattern: when He chooses according to His heart, there's no room for human pride.

> 12 Then the Lord said, "Rise and anoint him; this is the one."

### The Spirit Empowers David

> 13 So Samuel took the horn of oil and anointed him in the presence of his brothers, and from that day on the Spirit of the Lord came powerfully upon David.

Just like the heroic judges of Israel before him, David was filled with the Spirit of the Lord. This was the marker of God's choice—divine empowerment to fulfill His purposes.

By verse 18, we see hints of this transformation:

> 18 One of the servants answered, "I have seen a son of Jesse of Bethlehem who knows how to play the lyre. He is a brave man and a warrior. He speaks well and is a fine-looking man. And the Lord is with him."

The same boy who tended sheep was becoming known as a brave warrior. God took what looked like pathetic weakness and transformed it into strength for His glory. And God will do the same with you if you are willing.

> Ephesians 2:8-10 "For it is by grace you have been saved, through faith—and this is not from yourselves, it is the gift of God—not by works, so that no one can boast. For we are God's handiwork, created in Christ Jesus to do good works, which God prepared in advance for us to do."

You think you are weak. You think you are poor. You think you have no self-control. You think you are dumb. You think you have no power. What can I do? Nothing. What can God do through you? Everything.

In you, God is going to transform this world and the lives within it. You are going to be part of God's plans to turn this place around.

## The Spirit Departs from Saul (1 Samuel 16:14-23)

### The Judgment of Rejection

While the Spirit of the Lord came powerfully upon David, the Spirit departed from another:

> 14 Now the Spirit of the Lord had departed from Saul, and an evil spirit from the Lord tormented him.

This "evil spirit" (ruach ra'ah) represents something sent from the Lord as judgment—perhaps a spirit of confusion, guilt, regret, or some form of psychological torment. Throughout Scripture, God sends such spirits as expressions of His judgment when someone has rejected His word. There is no neutral zone for Israel's kings: either God's Spirit inhabits them, or an evil spirit afflicts them.

Saul's attendants recognized his condition and proposed a remedy:

> 15-17 "See, an evil spirit from God is tormenting you. Let our lord command his servants here to search for someone who can play the lyre. He will play when the evil spirit from God comes on you, and you will feel better." So Saul said to his attendants, "Find someone who plays well and bring him to me."

### David Ministers to Saul

One of the servants made a suggestion:

> 18 "I have seen a son of Jesse of Bethlehem who knows how to play the lyre. He is a brave man and a warrior. He speaks well and is a fine-looking man. And the Lord is with him."

There is profound irony here:
- "The Lord is with him" versus the Lord has departed from Saul
- The one with God's Spirit ministers to the one without it
- The boy with a donkey ministers to the king who couldn't even find his donkeys (as we learned in 1 Samuel 9)

> 19-20 Then Saul sent messengers to Jesse and said, "Send me your son David, who is with the sheep." So Jesse took a donkey loaded with bread, a skin of wine and a young goat and sent them with his son David to Saul.

> 21-22 David came to Saul and entered his service. Saul liked him very much, and David became one of his armor-bearers. Then Saul sent word to Jesse, saying, "Allow David to remain in my service, for I am pleased with him."

David was conscripted into Saul's service. The king who was losing the Spirit of God now depended on the boy who was filled with it.

### The Temporary Relief

> 23 Whenever the spirit from God came on Saul, David would take up his lyre and play. Then relief would come to Saul; he would feel better, and the evil spirit would leave him.

When the evil spirit tormented Saul, David's music would soothe him—like taking medicine for a migraine. The relief was real, but temporary. David's music mitigated the judgment for a time, but it could not remove it. While awaiting final judgment, God showed mercy.

This is the paradox of Saul's remaining days: the presence of grace even within judgment.

## The Spirit on Jesus

The pattern continues in Jesus, God's ultimate chosen one:

> Matthew 12:17-21 "Here is my servant whom I have chosen, the one I love, in whom I delight; I will put my Spirit on him, and he will proclaim justice to the nations. He will not quarrel or cry out; no one will hear his voice in the streets. A bruised reed he will not break, and a smoldering wick he will not snuff out, till he has brought justice through to victory. In his name the nations will put their hope."

Like David, Jesus was the suffering servant foretold by Isaiah. There was nothing impressive about His appearance:

> Isaiah 53:2 "There was nothing in his appearance that we should desire him."

Yet through Him, God brings salvation to all nations. He is gentle with the broken and weak, yet brings justice and mercy. And like David, He came to fulfill God's purpose by His own heart aligned with God's heart.

Before the creation of the world, God chose us in Christ:

> Ephesians 1:11-14 "In him we were also chosen, having been predestined according to the plan of him who works out everything in conformity with the purpose of his will, in order that we, who were the first to put our hope in Christ, might be for the praise of his glory. And you also were included in Christ when you heard the message of truth, the gospel of your salvation. When you believed, you were marked in him with a seal, the promised Holy Spirit, who is a deposit guaranteeing our inheritance until the redemption of those who are God's possession—to the praise of his glory."

God has put His Spirit in you. Be for His glory.

## Key Themes

- **God looks at the heart, not appearances**: Human judgment focuses on outward qualities; God's judgment penetrates to character and will.

- **God chooses the lowly**: There is no room for boasting when God selects the youngest, forgotten, seemingly weakest person. His choice glorifies His grace, not human achievement.

- **The Spirit empowers for service**: God's Spirit transforms ordinary people into vehicles for His glory. What appears weak by human standards becomes mighty in God's hands.

- **There is no neutral ground for leaders**: Kings either walk in God's Spirit or fall under His judgment. The choice has consequences.

- **God shows mercy even in judgment**: Though Saul faced judgment for his disobedience, God allowed temporary relief through David's ministry. Grace persists even as justice unfolds.
