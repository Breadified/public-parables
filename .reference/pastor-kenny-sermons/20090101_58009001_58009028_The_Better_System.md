# Hebrews 9:1-28

**Title:** The Better System
**Date:** 25 January 2009
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction: Useless Regulations

Before we dive into Hebrews, I want to illustrate an important principle with something we see in our culture. NSW recently introduced a container deposit scheme—offering 10 cents for every plastic or glass container returned. On the surface, it sounds reasonable. But it's fundamentally useless.

Why? It doesn't actually work. There aren't enough recycling centers. Bottles must be in pristine condition. It doesn't solve the real problem of containers ending up in waterways, parks, and gutters. The evidence from around the world is mixed. Logically, people assume that because there's a deposit on the container, it's no longer their responsibility—someone else will pick it up for the 10 cents.

Interestingly, NSW already has a higher recycling rate than South Australia, where this scheme has operated for 30 years. The regulation simply moves the problem around: pollution shifts from littering to backyard storage, transport to collection centers (which are themselves dirty), and then transport again. It's an expensive sledgehammer to crack a peanut—good for political grandstanding and virtue signaling, but little else.

This regulation embodies a crucial misunderstanding: it tries to produce behavior through guilt rather than genuine transformation. And this brings us perfectly to Hebrews 9, where the author shows us why the Old Covenant system of sacrifices was similarly inadequate—and how Christ fulfilled what regulations never could.

## Part One: The First Tabernacle—A Temporary, Illustrative System

### The Structure and Purpose

The first covenant had specific regulations for worship. The tabernacle consisted of two main areas:

**The Holy Place** contained:
- The golden lampstand (with seven lamps)
- The table with consecrated bread (twelve loaves)

**The Most Holy Place** (behind the second curtain) contained:
- The golden altar of incense
- The gold-covered ark of the covenant, holding:
  - A gold jar of manna
  - Aaron's staff that had budded
  - The stone tablets of the covenant
- Above the ark were the cherubim of glory, overshadowing the atonement cover

### How It Functioned

Priests entered the outer room regularly to carry out their ministry. But only the high priest entered the inner room—and only once a year, never without blood, which he offered for himself and for the sins the people had committed in ignorance.

### The Critical Problem: It Couldn't Clear the Conscience

Listen to what Hebrews tells us:

> The Holy Spirit was showing by this that the way into the Most Holy Place had not yet been disclosed as long as the first tabernacle was still functioning. This is an illustration for the present time, indicating that the gifts and sacrifices being offered were not able to clear the conscience of the worshiper. They are only a matter of food and drink and various ceremonial washings—external regulations applying until the time of the new order. (Hebrews 9:8-10)

Here's the heart of it: **the sacrificial system could not clear the conscience of the worshiper.** To "clear the conscience" means to clear it of sin. The regulations dealt only with external matters—what you ate, what you drank, how you washed. They addressed the flesh, not the soul.

This is one of the primary roles of the law itself. As Paul writes in Galatians:

> If a law had been given that could impart life, then righteousness would certainly have come by the law. But Scripture has locked up everything under the control of sin, so that what was promised, being given through faith in Jesus Christ, might be given to those who believe. (Galatians 3:21-22)

The law was a guardian until Christ came, that we might be justified by faith.

### The Tabernacle as a Living Parable

Think of the tabernacle like a toy car. It looks like a real car—it has wheels, a steering wheel, pedals—but it was never designed to actually drive on the road. It will never be registered. It was never intended to work. Why is it there? To teach. To give a child an idea of what driving is, what vehicles do, how they function.

The sacrificial system was the same. It was never designed, by itself, to take away sin. It wasn't broken or defective—it was working exactly as intended. But its purpose was to *illustrate* and *remind*, not to *redeem*.

Through the tabernacle, God taught His people about:
- The seriousness of sin
- The necessity of blood for atonement
- The precision of His holiness
- The reality that sin makes us unclean, and cleansing is difficult and requires divine intervention

The system was a shadow of the coming reality. It pointed forward to something far greater—the perfect High Priest who would offer the perfect sacrifice.

## Part Two: The Second Tabernacle—The Reality That Fulfills

### Christ as the Superior High Priest

When Christ came as high priest of the good things that are now already here, everything changed:

> He went through the greater and more perfect tabernacle that is not made with human hands, that is to say, is not a part of this creation. He did not enter by means of the blood of goats and calves; but he entered the Most Holy Place once for all by his own blood, thus obtaining eternal redemption. (Hebrews 9:11-12)

The author contrasts the old system with the new through four transformations:

1. **From multiple priests to one Priest**: The old system required many priests because they died and had to be replaced. Christ is one eternal High Priest.

2. **From multiple sacrifices to one Sacrifice**: The high priest entered the Most Holy Place every year with blood that was not his own. But Christ entered heaven itself, offering Himself once for all.

3. **From annual access to permanent access**: In the old system, access to God's presence was restricted and temporary. Through Christ's blood, the way into God's presence stands open forever.

4. **From external cleansing to internal cleansing**: The blood of animals could sanctify people outwardly, making them ceremonially clean. But listen to what Christ's blood accomplishes:

> How much more, then, will the blood of Christ, who through the eternal Spirit offered himself unblemished to God, cleanse our consciences from acts that lead to death, so that we may serve the living God! (Hebrews 9:14)

**Christ cleanses the conscience itself.** Not just the body, but the very seat of your guilt, shame, and fear.

### The Illustration Completed

Remember the toy car? The tabernacle was like that—an illustration, a teaching tool. But the new covenant is not a toy. It's a real car. It's not lacking the essential engine or design; it was lacking the vital ingredient to make it functional: the blood of a perfect, eternal High Priest.

When Jesus shed His blood, the system finally *worked*. The illustration became reality.

### The New Covenant Sealed in Blood

Why does blood matter so much? Because a covenant requires death:

> In the case of a will, it is necessary to prove the death of the one who made it, because a will is in force only when somebody has died; it never takes effect while the one who made it is living. This is why even the first covenant was not put into effect without blood. (Hebrews 9:16-18)

When Moses proclaimed every command of the law, he took the blood of calves, together with water, scarlet wool, and branches of hyssop, and sprinkled the scroll and all the people. He said, "This is the blood of the covenant, which God has commanded you to keep."

In the same way, nearly everything was cleansed with blood. Without the shedding of blood, there is no forgiveness. And so the heavenly things themselves required better sacrifices than these. The copies—the earthly tabernacle and its rituals—were purified with animal sacrifices. But the heavenly reality required the blood of Christ Himself.

## The Result: Freedom from Dead Works

Here's where this becomes intensely personal. The purpose of the sacrificial system was always meant to lead to this: freedom.

> As a result, the new covenant provides cleansing for the conscience (as opposed to the cleansing of the flesh provided by the old covenant). (Hebrews 9:14)

No more dead works. Once you've finished a crossword puzzle, you don't try to do it again. You rub out the pen marks—it's finished, it's dead, you bury it. You take your mock exams, you finish your HSC, it's done. It's dead. You bury it.

That's what Christ accomplished. Your sins—past, present, the guilt, the shame, the weight of them—they're finished. Done. Buried.

And now we serve God, not out of guilt, not out of obligation to perform more dead works. We serve out of:
- Gratefulness
- Love
- Truth

Because the inner place of our hearts has been changed. Jesus went into the Most Holy Place of heaven itself on our behalf, and as a result, the inner place of our hearts—that secret chamber where guilt lives, where shame whispers, where the accuser levies charges—has been transformed.

## The Danger of Useless Regulations

Here's where I bring this back to where we started. Just as that container deposit scheme tries to produce behavior through guilt without addressing the heart, there are many "religious" systems that do the same thing. They burden you with external rules and make you feel perpetually guilty.

But think about this: We Australians already don't litter. We participated in Clean Up Australia Day, and it was boring—there was nothing to clean! We were trained from childhood by our schools, our parents, the golden rule. We know instinctively that it's wrong to throw a can out a car window or into the gutter. This is unconscious competence—we don't even think about it anymore; it's simply wrong. That behavior was solved long ago, not by a regulation with a 10-cent incentive, but by character formation.

For the Christian, the guilt of sin was wiped out long ago—not by your efforts or your penance or your good works, but by the blood of Christ. When you repented, He forgave you. He says to you what He said to the woman caught in adultery: "Neither do I condemn you; go and leave your life of sin."

When Satan attacks you for your past and current evil deeds, when the accuser threatens you with repercussions and demands that you do more dead works to atone for sins, Jesus says this: "I've done it for you long ago. Go in peace."

Don't let anyone—including yourself—burden you with useless regulations that try to produce righteousness through guilt. That's not the gospel. The gospel is freedom. The gospel is transformation of the heart. The gospel is the blood of Christ, which has made you clean, not just outwardly, but in your conscience.

Go and serve the living God—not out of fear, but out of love.
