# Micah 1:1-16

**Title:** God's Warning
**Date:** 3 October 2024
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction

This is the first time preaching through Micah—a book I don't know well. But part of reading the counsel of God is exploring Scripture passages we haven't studied deeply before. Interestingly, Micah 6:8 is quoted in the Main Reading Room of the Library of Congress, inscribed above the statue of Religion: "What doth the Lord require of thee, but to do justly, and to love mercy, and to walk humbly with thy God?" It's remarkable that one of the most recognized expressions of biblical justice appears in America's national library.

## 1. The One Who is Like God (1-5)

### The Prophet's Name and Background

> The word of the Lord that came to Micah of Moresheth during the reigns of Jotham, Ahaz and Hezekiah, kings of Judah—the vision he saw concerning Samaria and Jerusalem.

Micah means "Who is like God?"—a name that carries profound significance for his entire message. The book opens by showing that God's people were decidedly *not* like God, despite bearing his name through their prophet.

Micah was from Moresheth, a small town about 40 kilometers southwest of Jerusalem, on the border between Judah and Philistine territory. He was a country boy, a contemporary of Isaiah.

The list of kings helps us date his prophecy: between 739 BC (Jotham's reign) and 686 BC (end of Hezekiah's rule). Since Hezekiah was a noted reformer, Micah's condemnation of sin likely focuses on the period before those reforms. God selected Micah as his messenger through whom to deliver this word—not men choosing him, but God choosing the prophet (Deuteronomy 18:18-22).

### The Call to Witness

> Hear, you peoples, all of you, listen, earth and all who live in it, that the Sovereign Lord may bear witness against you, the Lord from his holy temple.

God summons all creation as witnesses to his judgment—a courtroom scene where the Sovereign Lord becomes the prosecutor bearing testimony against his own people.

### The Judge Descends

> Look! The Lord is coming from his dwelling place; he comes down and treads on the heights of the earth. The mountains melt beneath him and the valleys split apart, like wax before the fire, like water rushing down a slope.

With vivid apocalyptic imagery, Micah sees the Lord descending from heaven to earth in judgment. The earth itself cannot stand before his power—mountains dissolve like wax, valleys split open. The very footstool of creation buckles under his feet (Isaiah 66:1: "Heaven is my throne, and the earth is my footstool").

### The Root Cause: Spiritual Adultery

> All this is because of Jacob's transgression, because of the sins of the people of Israel. What is Jacob's transgression? Is it not Samaria? What is Judah's high place? Is it not Jerusalem?

The transgression is idolatry—worshiping false gods at "high places." But Micah uses this term more broadly than just physical worship sites. The "high places" represent anything we elevate above God, anything we treat as our ultimate end rather than a means to serve him.

Consider how we make idols of good things: academic success becomes an end in itself rather than a means to serve God and others. Work and career become sources of identity and pride rather than platforms for ministry. Comfort and leisure—rest after labor—becomes something we cannot surrender, draining our budgets and pulling us away from community during seasons when the church gathers.

The real problem is confusion of means and ends. When we treat the means as the end—when academic achievement, professional success, or earthly comfort become what we worship—we commit spiritual adultery against God. We "gain the whole world" of earthly achievement while losing what matters eternally.

## 2. Samaria: The Example of Judgment (6-9)

> Therefore I will make Samaria a heap of rubble, a place for planting vineyards. I will pour her stones into the valley and lay bare her foundations. All her idols will be broken to pieces; all her temple gifts will be burned with fire; I will destroy all her images.

This prophecy, delivered centuries before its fulfillment, came to pass in 722 BC when Samaria—capital of the northern kingdom—fell to the Assyrians. The city that had built itself through human pride and idolatrous worship was completely destroyed. Its people were scattered throughout the Assyrian empire, their bloodlines diluted, their nation erased from history. Those who had "put high places" would never return to their land.

The judgment reverses civilization itself: a great city returns to farmland, then to desert. The stones that built proud structures are thrown into valleys. The foundations—all that was built upon—are laid bare. This is the fate of those who exchange treasures in heaven for temporary earthly glory.

> Since she gathered her gifts from the wages of prostitutes, as the wages of prostitutes they will again be used.

Their ill-gotten wealth returns to them as judgment. All that was built through spiritual unfaithfulness comes to naught.

> Because of this I will weep and wail; I will go about barefoot and naked. I will howl like a jackal and moan like an owl. For Samaria's plague is incurable; it has spread to Judah. It has reached the very gate of my people, even to Jerusalem itself.

Micah weeps as he prophesies—his pastoral heart breaking over coming judgment. But there is no escape. The judgment that fell on Samaria serves as a warning that it will reach even God's own city.

When idolatry becomes our high place:
- What should bring rest becomes a source of conflict
- What should bring comfort becomes pain
- What should bring success becomes a burden
- We gain on earth while losing in heaven
- We gain short-term while losing long-term

## 3. No Escape (10-16)

What follows is a series of towns in the Shephelah—the lowlands between Judah's mountains and the coastal plain—spoken of through clever Hebrew wordplay that builds a portrait of inescapable judgment:

> Tell it not in Gath; weep not at all. In Beth Ophrah roll in the dust.

Gath sounds like the Hebrew word for "tell"—don't tell. Beth Ophrah means "house of dust." Those fleeing Judah pass through these towns. Gath won't weep for them; Beth Ophrah will be a place of mourning.

> Pass by naked and in shame, you who live in Shaphir. Those who live in Zaanan will not come out. Beth Ezel is in mourning; it no longer protects you.

Shaphir means "beautiful"—the beautiful city brings only shame. Zaanan sounds like the word for "exit"—they cannot go out. Beth Ezel, the "nearby city," can no longer provide protection.

> Those who live in Maroth writhe in pain, waiting for relief, because disaster has come from the Lord, even to the gate of Jerusalem.

Maroth means "bitterness." All hope for relief is gone.

> You who live in Lachish, harness fast horses to the chariot. You are where the sin of Daughter Zion began, for the transgressions of Israel were found in you.

Lachish sounds like the word for "horses." The once-strong fortress must flee. This city was where Judah's idolatry began—where spiritual adultery took root.

> Therefore you will give parting gifts to Moresheth Gath. The town of Akzib will prove deceptive to the kings of Israel.

Moresheth, Micah's own hometown, is given as a bride to the conqueror—parting gifts as she passes from one "husband" to another. Akzib means "deceptive"—the city proves a disappointment, falling swiftly.

> I will bring a conqueror against you who live in Mareshah. The nobles of Israel will flee to Adullam.

Mareshah relates to the Hebrew word for "possessor"—the invaders will possess it. Adullam, once David's refuge from Saul, will again shelter Israel's nobles as they hide from judgment.

> Shave your head in mourning for the children in whom you delight; make yourself as bald as the vulture, for they will go from you into exile.

The final image is devastating: parents will mourn their children, shaving their heads in the deepest expressions of grief, knowing their beloved children will be torn away into exile. This is the end of those who have made idols of earthly things.

## The Heart of Judgment

Yet in this judgment lies opportunity for repentance. When God brings a person or nation to their knees—stripped of possessions, naked, in dust and sackcloth—it is the perfect posture for prayer and return. Job's response to devastating loss models this: "Naked I came from my mother's womb, and naked I will depart. The Lord gave and the Lord has taken away; may the name of the Lord be praised" (Job 1:20-21). When everything is stripped away, we finally see what matters.

The question Micah's name poses echoes still: "Who is like God?" The answer comes at Christmas, when God himself enters human history in Jesus Christ—the one who is truly like God, full of grace and truth. As we approach the Christmas season, between our cultural idolatries and Christ's coming, we must ask: will we be following God, or chasing the high places of earthly comfort, success, and pride?

Let us repent of our idolatry and return to the one who alone deserves to be exalted.
