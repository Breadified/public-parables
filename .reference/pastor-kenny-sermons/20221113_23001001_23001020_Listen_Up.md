# Isaiah 1:1-20

**Title:** Listen Up!!

**Date:** November 13, 2022

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Overview

Isaiah's message to Judah spans nearly seventy years of history across four kings—Uzziah, Jotham, Ahaz, and Hezekiah—encompassing the pre-exile, exile, and post-exile periods. This massive book of 66 chapters is written primarily in poetry, making it both accessible to read yet challenging to preach. The opening chapter begins with an urgent call: **Listen.**

---

## 1. You Must Listen Because God Is Your Master (1:4)

> Hear me, you heavens! Listen, earth! For the Lord has spoken.

The repeated call to "hear" and "listen" emphasizes the gravity of what follows. God speaks as Yahweh—the covenant-keeping God who has a rightful claim on His people.

> I reared children and brought them up, but they have rebelled against me.

God frames His relationship with Israel through the lens of a father and his children. Throughout Scripture, we're called to fear only a handful of things: God Himself, and our parents. This isn't arbitrary—fathers and mothers hold a unique place of honor in a child's life, for they have given us life and nourishment.

Yet Israel has rebelled against the One who brought them up. They have forgotten who sustained them.

> The ox knows its master, the donkey its owner's manger.

The tragedy deepens: even animals know their provider. An ox recognizes its master. A donkey knows where its food comes from. The dog hears its master's voice and comes running, understanding that this person feeds it. Animals possess a basic gratitude for their sustenance.

> But Israel does not know, my people do not understand.

By contrast, Israel—God's chosen people—bites the hand that feeds them. They lack even the basic wisdom of beasts.

> Woe to the sinful nation, a people whose guilt is great, a brood of evildoers, children given to corruption!

Israel is described in collective, swarming terms: a sinful nation, people burdened by great guilt, a brood of evildoers. The word "brood" evokes the image of a swarm—like an ant nest suddenly disturbed, or spiders emerging from a hole in the rock.

> They have forsaken the Lord; they have spurned the Holy One of Israel and turned their backs on him.

Three synonyms emphasize their rejection: forsaken, spurned, turned away. This is deliberate abandonment of the God who made them.

**Application:** We must not follow ancient Israel's path, nor the way of a pagan world that tells us to reject both God and parental wisdom. Instead, we must retain God's knowledge, recognizing that by His grace He feeds us, He causes the sun to shine on our faces. Just as children listen to their earthly fathers, we must listen to our Father in heaven.

## 2. Listen Because You Are Suffering (5:9)

> Why should you be beaten anymore? Why do you persist in rebellion?

Israel's suffering is real and undeniable. The imagery is visceral:

> Your whole head is injured, your whole heart afflicted. From the sole of your foot to the top of your head there is no soundness—only wounds and welts and open sores, not cleansed or bandaged or soothed with olive oil.

The nation is like someone who has been beaten in combat, injured from head to foot with no part left whole. This echoes the Old Testament pattern found in Deuteronomy, where a stubborn, rebellious son receives the community's judgment. God is seemingly dealing with His useless child.

> Your country is desolate, your cities burned with fire; your fields are being stripped by foreigners right before you, laid waste as when overthrown by strangers.

The land itself suffers. The promise of Exodus 20:12—to live long in the land God is giving—has been revoked. The blessings of the land are gone.

> Daughter Zion is left like a shelter in a vineyard, like a hut in a cucumber field, like a city under siege.

Jerusalem—once the location of Mount Zion and Solomon's magnificent temple—is reduced to a temporary booth. The imagery echoes the wandering years in Egypt and the references to cucumbers in the wilderness. Israel is going backward, returning to slavery.

> Unless the Lord Almighty had left us some survivors, we would have become like Sodom, we would have been like Gomorrah.

Only God's remnant prevents complete destruction.

**The Purpose of Suffering:** Some suffering is simply the fortune of this broken world, and some is God's testing (as with Job). But for many of us, both collectively and individually, pain serves another purpose. **Pain is God's megaphone to rouse a deaf world.**

When life collapses because we ignored wise counsel, when we face the consequences of disobedience, that is the time to listen. When we see evidence of suffering in the world—the pandemic, economic collapse, mental illness in children, family violence, the perversion of all that is good and wholesome—all of these correlate with our collective ignorance of God. Do we not learn? Do we know?

When life brings us to our knees, we are in the perfect position to pray and to listen.

## 3. Stop Being Busy and Listen Instead (10:17)

> Hear the word of the Lord, you rulers of Sodom; listen to the instruction of our God, you people of Gomorrah!

The leaders are addressed as "rulers of Sodom" and "people of Gomorrah"—they are becoming what they resisted, and judgment awaits.

> The multitude of your sacrifices—what are they to me? says the Lord. I have more than enough of burnt offerings, of rams and the fat of fattened animals; I have no pleasure in the blood of bulls and lambs and goats.

Here is the paradox: God commanded the sacrificial system. These offerings were meant to remind Israel that sin has a cost, that judgment is real, and that God mercifully provides a way through the blood of substitutes. But Israel has transformed the ritual into empty activity. They sacrifice without repenting. They perform the ceremonies without changing their hearts.

> When you come to appear before me, who has asked this of you, this trampling of my courts? Stop bringing meaningless offerings! Your incense is detestable to me.

The temple, meant to be a place of instruction and hearing God's word, has become a mere stage for religious performance.

> New Moons, Sabbaths and convocations—I cannot bear your worthless assemblies. Your New Moon feasts and your appointed festivals I hate with all my being. They have become a burden to me; I am weary of bearing them.

> When you spread out your hands in prayer, I hide my eyes from you; even when you offer many prayers, I am not listening. Your hands are full of blood!

The prayers of sinners who refuse to repent are meaningless. Their "blood"—whether literal bloodshed or the blood of their sins—stains their hands.

**The Principle:** Are we too busy with religious activity to actually listen to God? The story of Mary and Martha illustrates this perfectly. Martha was consumed with serving while Mary sat at Jesus' feet. Only when we sit at the feet of God's Word do our subsequent activities gain meaning and purpose.

In our churches, we have so many people serving—youth leaders, Sunday school directors, the AV crew, musicians. But when we gather to pray and read Scripture, we must pause and listen together, sitting at the feet of the Word. Extend that same reverence to the preaching of God's Word. In your own lives and families, set aside time to hear the Word rather than being perpetually consumed with activity.

## 4. Consider God's Reasonable Offer (18:20)

> Come now, let us settle the matter, says the Lord.

God calls Israel to reason together with Him, to hear His case.

> Though your sins are like scarlet, they shall be as white as snow; though they are red as crimson, they shall be like wool.

The promise is extraordinary: complete cleansing. The image of scarlet and crimson dye—stains that seem permanent and indelible—will be transformed to white as snow and pure wool. Yet how is this possible? How can anyone truly achieve such willing obedience and avoid rebellion?

> Wash and make yourselves clean. Take your evil deeds out of my sight; stop doing wrong. Learn to do right; seek justice. Defend the oppressed. Take up the cause of the fatherless; plead the case of the widow.

The commands are clear: cease from evil, practice righteousness, pursue justice, care for the vulnerable.

> If you are willing and obedient, you will eat the good things of the land; but if you resist and rebel, you will be devoured by the sword.

The choice is presented: obedience brings blessing; rebellion brings judgment.

**The Problem:** Can anyone be perfectly willing and obedient? Can any of us avoid all resistance and rebellion? The answer is no. The stain of scarlet sin seems impossible to remove—much like trying to wash blood from a white shirt.

**The Solution:** Isaiah himself hints at the answer 500 years later in his own prophecy:

> He was pierced for our transgressions, he was crushed for our iniquities; the punishment that brought us peace was on him, and by his wounds we are healed. (Isaiah 53:5)

Jesus Christ—crushed and bruised, bearing the wrath of God—becomes our substitute. His blood satisfies God's justice. Through Him, our scarlet sins truly become white as snow.

**Our Response:** Listen to Jesus. As a church, let us listen to Him together. Write your questions on comment cards. Reason over Scripture. Receive God's Word with readiness of mind and search the Scriptures daily to verify what you've heard (Acts 17). Isaiah is a big book, and we must listen well.
