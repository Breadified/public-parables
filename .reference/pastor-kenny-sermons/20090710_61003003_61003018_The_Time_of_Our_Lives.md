# 2 Peter 3:3-18

**Title:** The Time of Our Lives
**Date:** July 10, 2009
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## The Paradox of Time

We live with a peculiar tension about time. To the world, God's promised return feels impossibly distant—something that never seems to arrive. Yet Scripture reminds us that from God's perspective, time is running out far faster than we realize. Understanding this paradox transforms how we live.

---

## Time Feels Long (3:4)

The scoffers Peter addresses in this passage make a compelling argument—at least on the surface:

> "They will say, 'Where is this coming he promised? Ever since our fathers died, everything goes on as it has since the beginning of creation.'"

Their logic is simple: generations have passed since Jesus ascended. Life continues predictably. Nothing dramatic has happened. Therefore, the promise of Christ's return is empty.

### Why This Argument Resonates

**Empirical observation supports them.** The laws of physics remain unchanged. Rain cycles, weather patterns, climate cycles—all operate as they always have. Ice ages come and go. Warming periods follow cold ones. Roman warmth gave way to medieval warming, which enabled vineyards to flourish in northern England and Greenland. We only recently began measuring temperature systematically (thermometers from 1850 onward), so our modern climate records give us a false sense of uniqueness.

**Time genuinely feels extended for modern Australians.** Our average lifespan is 81.3 years (compared to 40s in parts of Africa). We're healthier than ever before. Retirement extends further each decade. We pack unprecedented experiences into our lives—musical instruments learned by age twelve, two degrees completed and a third job begun by twenty-five, three European trips taken by thirty (after school, after university, for a honeymoon).

For the early Christians receiving this letter, the situation was even more acute. A generation had passed since Jesus' resurrection. No sign of judgment. Just an ordinary world continuing its ordinary patterns.

### The Application of Doubt

When Christians proclaim that judgment is coming while simultaneously pursuing another degree, buying another possession, taking another holiday, they look foolish. This hasn't helped our cause. Foolish predictions throughout Christian history about Christ's imminent return have made this message the most embarrassing aspect of Christianity—more embarrassing than the nativity or the crucifixion. Now it's associated with strange cults and homeless people on street corners with cardboard signs.

Those who are consciously waiting for Christ's return experience a peculiar isolation. It's like standing at a bus stop for a bus that never comes. Morning brings city workers. Afternoon brings mothers with prams. Later, school children. Eventually, you see the same people from the morning returning on the evening bus—and they're staring at you. You feel foolish.

## Time Is Short (3:8)

Yet Peter shifts our perspective entirely. From God's vantage point, time tells a completely different story:

> "With the Lord a day is like a thousand years, and a thousand years are like a day."

This isn't a mathematical conversion formula. Rather, Peter is jolting our perception back into reality. Only a short span has actually passed. To the God of eternity, thirty years since Christ's ascension is less than an hour. The delay is nothing.

### We Live in a High-Risk Zone

The scoffers have forgotten something crucial:

> "But they deliberately forget that long ago by God's word the heavens came into being and the earth was formed out of water and by water... By these waters also the world of that time was deluged and destroyed."

The world was created by a word. It can be unmade by a word with equal ease—returned to the formless, void state when darkness covered the deep. God has done this before. He did it in Noah's time, when scoffers abounded then as now.

> "By the same word the present heavens and earth are reserved for fire, being kept for the day of judgment and destruction of the ungodly."

The fate is sealed. Reserved. Kept. No escape from this destiny.

> "But the day of the Lord will come like a thief. The heavens will disappear with a roar; the elements will be destroyed by fire, and the earth and everything done in it will be laid bare."

Consider an Australian soldier in a war zone. Technically, he has a life expectancy of eighty years. But not really. The risk of sudden end is high. Despite appearances of normalcy, time is actually short. He writes every letter as if it might be his last.

This is our actual situation. The world runs like clockwork, giving us the illusion that time is abundant. But we exist in a high-risk zone. At any moment—in the snap of God's fingers—it could be over. You're planning a trip to the cinema. *Snap.* You're enrolling at university. *Snap.* You're at the Apple store buying the latest iPhone. *Snap.* **And you'll be before God.**

The timing is critical. And the time is short.

---

## Now Is the Time of Salvation (3:9)

Why has God put us in this precarious position? Why not end it now? The answer reveals His heart:

> "The Lord is not slow in keeping his promise, as some understand slowness. Instead he is patient with you, not wanting anyone to perish, but everyone to come to repentance."

God is not lazy or indifferent. He is patient. Judgment is held back—not out of weakness, but out of mercy. God wants to see more people saved. He wants more people to hear what His Son accomplished on the cross. He waits for more believers.

### The Finality of Judgment

When judgment comes, it will be total, absolute, and final. It cannot judge Somalia but exclude Australia. It cannot judge the rapist but spare you or your loved ones. Every person. Every building. Every house. Everything will be destroyed.

That judgment on people is final—no appeals court, no second chance, no going back to change the verdict.

Yet in His mercy, God says: "Wait. Just a moment." Though His justice demands judgment now, His desire for salvation stays His hand. He's waiting for that last person He has elected to walk through the doors of faith.

### A Word to the Unconverted

If you're not yet a Christian (and welcome—we're glad you're here; you've come to the right place): you might be that one person all of us Christians are waiting for. The injustice you see in the world, the suffering, the cries of Christians everywhere for this age to end—you could be the one to bring all that to an end. Of course, once you convert, we'll probably give you a hard time for taking so long! But the point is: **God is waiting for you. Repent now.**

There is no other reason God restrains His judgment. Not for your first kiss. Not so you can marry someone. Not so you can travel or experience life. This age exists for salvation alone. All of us are here because of that agenda.

## The Time of Your Lives (3:11-12)

For those of us already saved, Peter poses a searching question:

> "Since everything will be destroyed in this way, what kind of people ought you to be? You ought to live holy and godly lives as you look forward to the day of God and speed its coming."

### Live Holy and Godly Lives

We have everything we need. There are no more excuses. This is the central theme of 2 Peter.

- Develop godly habits. Stop sluggishness and procrastination.
- Put away that sin today. Don't convince yourself you can keep sinning—whether in relationships, on your computer, or elsewhere.
- Start loving. Do ministry. Meet the lonely person at youth group.
- Learn and grow. Build your knowledge so you won't stray from truth and can help others.

### Speed Its Coming

We participate in hastening the day by:

**Praying for judgment.** Jesus taught us to pray, "Your kingdom come."

**Proclaiming the gospel.** If God exists for the purpose of salvation and wants that last person in, then let's go tell someone about Jesus. Let's get that person in the door.

Some of you may not feel confident in evangelism. That's fine—equip yourself. That's what church community is for. Make use of the time you have. Don't put it off.

### Reframe Time Management

The common wisdom is "manage your time better." That's a furphy. What you actually need is to **manage your priorities and actions.** Be godly and holy. Time will fit around that.

## Living This Out: A Word on Education and Career Choices

Congratulations on your ATARs. But getting into university isn't what life is ultimately about. Time is short. We're in a high-risk zone. We need to reorient our priorities now.

As you pick university courses, consider:

**First, choose a course that won't pull you away from Christianity.** If the demands are so heavy that you have no time for Bible study or church—or you're so exhausted that you fall asleep during the service and sprint home to finish assignments—it's not the right course for you.

**Second, choose a course where you can proclaim the gospel.** Commerce might give you free time for gospel work, but be careful not to let your schedule become a part-time job instead of Christian ministry.

**Third, choose a course that leads to a job where you can actually live godly.** If the only pathway is a job requiring twelve-hour days, weekend work, compromising character to advance, or abandoning family and Christian friends—why start down that road?

**Remember: everything you do is for the proclamation of the gospel.** Some will pastor churches. Some will be doctors, keeping people alive long enough to hear the gospel. Some will be nurses. Some will be lawyers, protecting religious freedom. Some will be politicians, protecting Christian values and children. Some will be teachers, shaping the next generation. Some will be engineers, building housing.

The point isn't to choose fancy careers. It's to choose work where you can live faithfully.

### The Accounting

This is the time of your life. You have perhaps sixty or more years ahead (God willing). How will you use it?

When God looks at your life and asks for an accounting of your time on earth, what will you say? "I studied hard"? "I earned a lot of money"? "Look at my grades"?

Wouldn't it be remarkable to show our Lord a life marked by love and godliness, forged through adversity and perseverance? Wouldn't it be wonderful to demonstrate that we tried to speed His day's coming? And to hear Him say in return: "Well done, good and faithful servant"?

That's something worth investing the time of our lives into.

## Conclusion

> "So then, dear friends, since you are looking forward to this, make every effort to be found spotless, blameless and at peace with him. Bear in mind that our Lord's patience means salvation, just as our dear brother Paul also wrote you with the wisdom that God gave him... Therefore, dear friends, since you have been forewarned, be on your guard so that you may not be carried away by the error of lawless men and fall from your secure position. But grow in the grace and knowledge of our Lord and Savior Jesus Christ. To him be glory both now and forever! Amen."
