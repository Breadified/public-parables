# Mark 4:1-34

**Title:** The Parables of the Kingdom
**Date:** 23 May 2021
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Purpose of Parables: Elimination and Aggregation

Jesus taught by the lake to a massive crowd, positioning himself in a boat to create a natural barrier—a stage for his teaching. He spoke many things in parables, which seems straightforward until we examine his explanation to the disciples.

> "Whoever has ears to hear, let them hear." When he was alone, the Twelve and others around him asked about the parables. He told them, "The secret of the kingdom of God has been given to you. But to those on the outside everything is said in parables so that, 'they may be ever seeing but never perceiving, and ever hearing but never understanding; otherwise they might turn and be forgiven!'"

This is unsettling. We typically think parables are illustrations—stories that make biblical truths easier to understand. But here, parables function more like riddles. They serve as an inside joke, separating insiders from outsiders.

This principle appears throughout Scripture. In Isaiah 6, God sends the prophet Isaiah not to save Israel but to condemn them, hardening their hearts so they will not repent. The parables of Jesus are designed to *eliminate*—to reveal who truly belongs to the kingdom and who remains outside.

### The Illustration of Scam Emails

Consider how scammers intentionally use spelling errors and ridiculous stories in their emails. You might wonder why they don't improve them. But the errors are by design. When reaching thousands of potential victims costs almost nothing, scammers benefit from filtering out troublemakers. The spelling mistakes eliminate the skeptical and clever—isolating only the most gullible. As security experts note, "Anybody who doesn't fall off their chair laughing is exactly who they want to talk to."

Jesus' parables work similarly. They eliminate those who hear but do not understand, separating the truly receptive from those merely curious.

## Four Soils: The Fate of God's Word

> "Listen! A farmer went out to sow his seed. As he was scattering the seed, some fell along the path, and the birds came and ate it up. Some fell on rocky places, where it did not have much soil. It sprang up quickly, because the soil was shallow. But when the sun came up, the plants were scorched, and they withered because they had no root. Other seed fell among thorns, which grew up and choked the plants, so they did not bear grain. Still other seed fell on good soil. It came up, grew and produced a crop, some multiplying thirty, some sixty, some a hundred times."

Jesus explains: "The farmer sows the word."

### The Path: Immediate Loss

> "Some people are like seed along the path, where the word is sown. As soon as they hear it, Satan comes and takes away the word that was sown in them."

In Matthew, birds eat the seed; in Mark, Satan takes it away. The moment we hear God's word, the adversary swoops in. Before the seed can even begin to grow, it is snatched away. Satan acts as accuser and obstructor, finding fault with the word itself.

Consider how this manifests: "Yes, Jesus sounds like a great guy. But isn't there that rumor the Bible is fake?" Or: "I'm convinced by the evidence for the resurrection, but aren't Christians bigots?" These are seeds stolen before they can take root.

### Rocky Ground: Shallow Roots

> "Others, like seed sown on rocky places, hear the word and at once receive it with joy. But since they have no root, they last only a short time. When trouble or persecution comes because of the word, they quickly fall away."

Rocky places have little soil. The seed grows briefly—there's joy and initial enthusiasm. But without depth, without root, survival is only temporary. When trouble comes—exams, suffering, loss of a loved one, bullying from the world or family pressure—these believers quickly wither.

### Thorns: Choking Distractions

> "Still others, like seed sown among thorns, hear the word; but the worries of this life, the deceitfulness of wealth and the desires for other things come in and choke the word, making it unfruitful."

There is soil here—reason for optimism. The seed germinates and grows. But as it develops, thorns entangle and choke it, leaving the plant malformed and unfruitful.

Wealth is not inherently evil, but it deceives. It whispers that you don't need God anymore. With wealth comes the power to satisfy lusts—sexual desires, greed, hunger for power. The word is slowly strangled by competing desires until spiritual growth stops.

**Where are you at? What is your temptation?**

## The Parable's Context: Israel's Failure

This parable is a warning, perhaps aimed directly at the Pharisees who had just plotted to kill Jesus on the Sabbath.

Throughout the Old Testament, "sowing" represents God's work of restoring his people:

> "You will bring them in and plant them on the mountain of your inheritance—the place, Lord, you made for your dwelling." (Exodus 15:17)

But Israel became a plant that went wrong:

> "I had planted you like a choice vine of sound and reliable stock. How then did you turn against me into a corrupt, wild vine?" (Jeremiah 2:21)

Isaiah depicts God's judgment:

> "Now I will tell you what I am going to do to my vineyard: I will take away its hedge, and it will be destroyed; I will break down its wall, and it will be trampled. I will make it a wasteland, neither pruned nor cultivated, and briers and thorns will grow there. I will command the clouds not to rain on it." (Isaiah 5:5-7)

Yet even in judgment, there is hope. Isaiah 6:13 speaks of a remnant:

> "Though a tenth remains in the land, it will again be laid waste. But as the terebinth and oak leave stumps when they are cut down, so the holy seed will be the stump in the land."

**If Israel was the seed that did not grow, Jesus was the Israelite who did grow—the holy seed, the stump of Jesse.**

Jesus did not let Satan distract him. When tempted, he quoted God's word back to Satan. He did not let the temptation of power, wealth, or the lusts of the flesh divert him from listening to God. He did not let persecution stop him. Even on the cross, when he could have called down angels to rescue him, his love and commitment to our salvation kept him there.

## Good Soil: Multiplication and Abundance

> "Others, like seed sown on good soil, hear the word, accept it, and produce a crop—some thirty, some sixty, some a hundred times what was sown."

Imagine soil filled with fertilizer, water, and sunlight. The seed grows and produces abundantly—thirty times, sixty times, even a hundred times its original amount. The fruit overflows.

Before jumping to our own fruitfulness, recognize that the seed here first and foremost refers to Jesus himself. Through his life, death, and resurrection, he became the grain of wheat that fell to the ground and died, producing much fruit (John 12:24).

Now this word—God's word—comes to us. We must hear it.

## The Word Is Meant to Be Disclosed

> "He said to them, 'Do you bring in a lamp to put it under a bowl or a bed? Instead, don't you put it on its stand? For whatever is hidden is meant to be disclosed, and whatever is concealed is meant to be brought out into the open. If anyone has ears to hear, let them hear.'"

The mysteries of God are meant to be revealed, not kept in darkness. The word of God is meant to be heard and, more importantly, **meant to be obeyed**.

Consider how different documents waste words—legal contracts, mandatory agreements, even school essays can be bloated and convoluted. But God's word is like poetry. Each word is chosen with care. Each word is economical, purposeful, producing emotion and meaning. We cannot cherry-pick, ignoring some words while reading others. Every word is meant to be obeyed.

## The Choice Before Us

> "Consider carefully what you hear," he continued. "With the measure you use, it will be measured to you—and even more. Whoever has will be given more; whoever does not have, even what they have will be taken from them."

Like the Parable of the Talents, we are judged not only by our gifts and opportunities but also by what we do with what we hear. These revelations are gifts to us. **What will we do with them?**

Will we ignore God's word? Will we become like the Pharisees, using Scripture against God himself? Or will we receive it, accept it, and bear fruit?

## The Kingdom Grows Secretly

> "This is what the kingdom of God is like. A man scatters seed on the ground. Night and day, whether he sleeps or gets up, the seed sprouts and grows, though he does not know how. All by itself the soil produces grain—first the stalk, then the head, then the full kernel in the head. As soon as the grain is ripe, he puts the sickle to it, because the harvest has come."

God's word works in ways we don't fully understand, producing growth even when we're not watching. The kingdom is not advancing through violent conquest or public demonstration. It grows quietly, almost imperceptibly, until suddenly the harvest is ready.

In declaring "repent and believe," Jesus postponed judgment day, but do not mistake this for inactivity. The kingdom is still growing—silently, steadily, inevitably.

## The Mustard Seed: From Small Beginnings

> "What shall we say the kingdom of God is like, or what parable shall we use to describe it? It is like a mustard seed, which is the smallest of all seeds on earth. Yet when planted, it grows and becomes the largest of all garden plants, with such big branches that the birds can perch in its shade."

What begins invisibly small becomes remarkable in scope. Growth happens when we're not watching. A tomato plant suddenly appears after a seed we'd forgotten we planted. Transformation occurs in God's timing, not ours.

## Our Calling: Sow the Seed

> "Consider carefully what you hear." As we listen to God's word, consider what we speak.

**So sow the seed of the word of God.**

Seeds are not meant to be carefully planted one by one in controlled conditions. We sow intentionally and unintentionally, seizing opportunities wherever they arise:

- **Arrive early to a meeting.** When others are waiting and their guard is down, before "business mode" begins, engage in genuine conversation. Small talk is a seed.

- **Spend thirty seconds on a phone call.** Share that you're a Christian. Quote something relevant from Scripture. Plant seeds in unexpected places.

- **For those creatively gifted:** Write little notes to others. Small gestures carry weight.

- **For parents:** Throughout the day, have little conversations with your children. These are all seeds.

The process itself is a test. Like Google's rigorous interview process, which intentionally includes difficult questions not just to identify the best but to eliminate those uncommitted, so too does the gospel naturally separate the sincere from the superficial. Only those truly committed to listening to God's word will bear fruit.

**It's wonderful that we gather together to hear God's word and share a meal ("chunch"—church and lunch). We have an opportunity to sow the word into each other's lives. Make it a good day.**

## The Vision: Hope for the Distant Islands

The parable's final significance emerges in light of Isaiah 42:4:

> "He will not falter or be discouraged till he establishes justice on earth. In his teaching the islands will put their hope."

Jesus stands in a boat, speaking to an island of people—the nations, the coastlands, the distant shores. While his parables eliminate some, his word simultaneously aggregates others. By the same words that harden some hearts, God draws others to hope and faith.

God might eliminate some through judgment, but by the very same words, he is gathering others to himself. The kingdom expands through a simple process: the word is sown, hearts respond, fruit is produced, and hope spreads to the distant islands of the world.
