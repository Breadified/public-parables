# Mark 15:40-16:8

**Title:** Jesus Brings Life  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Eyewitnesses to the Resurrection

We depend on eyewitnesses every day—from legal records to financial statements. The resurrection of Jesus is no exception. Mark presents us with a trustworthy account built on eyewitness testimony, and the details themselves confirm their reliability.

### Women Watching from a Distance (15:40-41)

> Some women were watching from a distance. Among them were Mary Magdalene, Mary the mother of James the younger and of Joses, and Salome. In Galilee these women had followed him and cared for his needs. Many other women who had come up with him to Jerusalem were also there.

The fact that women are the primary witnesses is actually a strong indicator that this account is historically reliable. In first-century Jewish courts, women were not permitted to testify as legal witnesses. The Jewish historian Josephus explicitly stated that the testimony of multiple women was inadmissible "because of the levity and boldness of their sex." Even the second-century critic Celsus mocked the idea of Mary Magdalene as a resurrection witness, dismissing her as a "hysterical female deluded by sorcery."

If Mark had invented this account to convince his contemporaries, he would have chosen more credible witnesses—male disciples, respected community leaders, anyone the culture would have accepted. The fact that he centers the testimony on women suggests he was recording what actually happened, regardless of how it would play with his audience. **Unless the truth compelled him, Mark would never have made this choice.**

These women had followed Jesus throughout his ministry and cared for his needs. They knew him well and were qualified to witness and testify to what they saw.

### The Importance of Time and Evidence

A common objection is that nearly 2,000 years separate us from these events. But the critical gap in historical credibility is not between when the record was written and when we read it—it's between when the event occurred and when it was recorded.

Mark was likely written around 60 AD, approximately 30 years after the crucifixion and resurrection. While that seems substantial, it's remarkably quick by ancient standards. Most ancient events were transmitted orally for generations before being written down. The fact that Mark's Gospel was written so soon, while eyewitnesses were still alive and could object or correct the record, lends it credibility. People could check the facts. They could challenge false claims. This was not two thousand years of accumulated folklore—this was written during the lifetime of those who witnessed it.

For comparison, the life of Alexander the Great was recorded 400 years after his death, yet historians accept those accounts as reliable. The age of a historical record doesn't automatically render it unreliable if it was accurately recorded in the first place. Good evidence doesn't become bad evidence simply because time has passed. If a bank account is recorded accurately from day one, the passage of time doesn't make that record suspect.

### Addressing the "Miracle Problem"

Some argue that a resurrection violates the laws of nature and contradicts science. But true science is built on observation—facts that drive conclusions. Science doesn't begin with predetermined conclusions that dictate which facts are possible.

The philosopher Hume pointed out that just because the sun has risen every morning for thousands of years doesn't guarantee it will rise tomorrow. This is the problem of induction: past experience cannot logically guarantee future events. By that logic, the fact that no dead person has risen in recorded history doesn't prove that resurrection is impossible. It simply means it hasn't happened—until the evidence suggests it did.

If we follow Hume's own logic, we must examine the evidence. The women's astonishment at finding the tomb empty, the testimony of the guards, the transformation of the disciples from terrified fugitives into bold proclaimers—these are not claims of an everyday occurrence. No one is asserting that resurrection happens regularly. But God, working according to his own purposes, interrupted the normal order of nature for one event of supreme significance. **The real obstacle to belief is not the unusualness of the event or the passage of time. The real problem is unbelief itself.**

We rely on records constantly. Sometimes I check my bank account expecting one balance and find another—higher or lower. I verify that the record is accurate. The amount is unusual, but if the verification checks out, I have to trust the record, not my initial perception. We adjust our understanding according to the evidence. This is the proper inductive approach: change your mind where the evidence leads you.

## Jesus Truly Died (15:42-47)

The women witnessed the crucifixion, but they also witnessed something equally crucial: the burial itself.

> It was Preparation Day (that is, the day before the Sabbath). So as evening approached, Joseph of Arimathea, a prominent member of the Council, who was himself waiting for the kingdom of God, went boldly to Pilate and asked for Jesus' body.

Joseph was no peripheral figure—he was a respected member of the Sanhedrin, the Jewish council. Yet he had been persuaded by Jesus and was waiting for the kingdom of God. It's important to note that not all the Jewish authorities wanted Jesus dead. There was dissent, even among the leadership.

> Pilate was surprised to hear that he was already dead. Summoning the centurion, he asked him if Jesus had already died. When he learned from the centurion that it was so, he gave the body to Joseph.

Pilate's surprise underscores the reality of Jesus's death. Crucifixion was designed to be slow and brutal. Death didn't always come quickly. But Jesus was already dead. The centurion—a professional soldier trained in execution—confirmed it. These were experts in killing. If they said he was dead, he was dead.

> So Joseph bought some linen cloth, took down the body, wrapped it in the linen, and placed it in a tomb cut out of rock. Then he rolled a stone against the entrance of the tomb.

Joseph wrapped the body himself, preparing it according to Jewish burial customs. This ritual washing and anointing was the last act of honor for the deceased—it still is today. When someone dies, we clean and dress them, often in their finest clothes. This wasn't unusual; it was the appropriate response to death.

The burial itself was subject to Jewish law. Deuteronomy commanded that the bodies of executed criminals must be buried on the same day to avoid defiling the land. It was in everyone's interest—Jewish authorities, Roman occupiers, the community—to see Jesus properly buried. There was no conspiracy to hide the body or deny his death. **Everybody's interest was served by ensuring he was buried.**

### The Significance of His Death

Why does Mark emphasize all these details? Because **Jesus truly died.** This rules out certain theories:

- The "swoon theory" suggests Jesus merely lost consciousness and revived in the cool of the tomb. But after losing liters of blood, after 24 hours of torture, after the shock of crucifixion, a brief rest doesn't resurrect a man. He would have had to move an enormous stone, overpower trained Roman guards, and escape. The hypothesis collapses under scrutiny.

- Some suggest that Simon of Cyrene was crucified instead of Jesus, or that someone else took his place. Mark makes clear that it was Simon who carried Jesus's cross, not the reverse.

Perhaps there's symbolic significance in Joseph's tomb itself. The tomb belonged to Joseph of Arimathea, yet Jesus was laid in it. Once again we see the theme of substitution: Jesus taking the place of another. He became cursed for our sakes, fulfilling what was written in Deuteronomy—that anyone hung on a tree is under God's curse.

Jesus died *for our sins*. This is the heart of the Gospel. We have all sinned, and sin brings death. Our own end is burial—whether in an 8x4 cemetery plot in Sydney (where premium double plots cost $65,000), a crematorium, the ocean, or a mausoleum. We're all heading in one direction. In our thirties we begin to slow down; by our mid-fifties, our DNA begins to degenerate and cancer risk increases. This is our fate. This is where sin leads.

But Jesus offers to take your place. He enters your burial plot so that for the Christian, death is not the end. Because the women didn't only observe his substitutionary death—they also witnessed his resurrection.

## The Resurrection Witnessed (16:1-8)

> When the Sabbath was over, Mary Magdalene, Mary the mother of James, and Salome bought spices so that they might go to anoint Jesus' body. Very early on the first day of the week, just after sunrise, they were on their way to the tomb and they asked each other, "Who will roll the stone away from the entrance of the tomb?"

The same women returned. They had noted exactly where Jesus was buried, so they couldn't mistake the tomb. They came to anoint his body, to perform the final service of love for their teacher. They couldn't do this on the night of burial—there wasn't time. The Sabbath made it unlawful to travel or work after nightfall. When the Sabbath ended, it was dangerous to go out alone. So at the first lawful opportunity—sunrise on the first day of the week—they made their way to the tomb.

Their question—"Who will roll the stone away?"—reveals their practical concern. They had no plan to move that massive stone. Yet something compelled them to go anyway. This is the devotion of disciples to their master.

> But when they looked up, they saw that the stone, which was very large, had been rolled away. As they entered the tomb, they saw a young man dressed in a white robe sitting on the right side, and they were alarmed.

The stone was already rolled away. Inside the tomb, they encountered a young man in a white robe—an angel, though Mark's spare language doesn't explicitly say so. The women's fear is palpable. This is not a vision. This is reality breaking in upon them.

> "Don't be alarmed," he said. "You are looking for Jesus the Nazarene, who was crucified. He has risen! He is not here. See the place where they laid him. But go, tell his disciples and Peter, 'He is going ahead of you into Galilee. There you will see him, just as he told you.'"

**Jesus has risen.** Not metaphorically. Not spiritually in some abstract sense. The tomb is empty. It's a distinctly modern phenomenon to interpret the resurrection as merely metaphorical—no one in the history of Christianity thought this way until recently. The resurrection was understood as physical, real, bodily.

Jesus is alive, just as he predicted he would be. He has gone ahead to Galilee, just as he told his disciples he would. Death has been defeated—that final enemy that none of us can control despite our efforts with cosmetics and antioxidants and vitamins. We might look better, but the expiration date remains the same. Death is the source of all pain, misery, and decay. And it is defeated.

Jesus has overcome multiple enemies throughout his ministry: the Jewish authorities, the Roman powers, even members of his own family who didn't believe in him. He faced temptation, confronted sin, and battled Satan himself. But the last enemy—death itself—has been conquered once and for all. This is Easter. **This is what it means that Jesus brings life.**

> Trembling and bewildered, the women went out and fled from the tomb. They said nothing to anyone, because they were afraid.

Mark ends his Gospel abruptly. The women were alarmed at the empty tomb, astonished at the angel's words, trembling and bewildered as they fled. And they said nothing to anyone because they were afraid.

This ending puzzles readers. What were they afraid of? Perhaps they feared getting into trouble—being questioned about the body. Perhaps they feared the centurion or the Jewish authorities, worrying about accusations of tampering with the corpse. Perhaps they feared spreading rumors that would incite riots. Or perhaps it was simply the terror of encountering the supernatural, of witnessing something that shattered their understanding of how the world works.

But Mark's curious ending invites us into something deeper. By leaving us with the women's fear, Mark places us in their position. We, like those early disciples, stand at a distance and witness these events through Mark's reliable historical testimony. And the question becomes personal: **What do you fear in following Jesus?**

- Do you fear persecution? Remember that nothing can separate you from his love.
- Do you fear ridicule from friends who mock your faith? The disciples faced this too.
- Do you fear being wrong? We all can be wrong—and there's nothing shameful in that. Acknowledging fallibility keeps us humble. But the fact that we *can* be wrong doesn't mean we *are*. We follow where the evidence leads, and that's all any mortal can justifiably do. If we're wrong, what other option do we have but to keep seeking truth?
- Do you fear death? Jesus brings life.

### The Call to Resurrection Life

Consider the nature of union with Christ. If we are bound to him in his death, we are bound to him in his life. Our fates become intertwined. If you associate with corrupt people during their good times, you fall with them when their fortunes collapse. But if you invest in something good—purchasing stock when its value is low—you rise when it recovers.

When you sign up for Jesus, you humble yourself. You admit your wrongdoing. You acknowledge that he died for you. You die with him—death to self, death to autonomy, death to the illusion that you can save yourself. When you join him in that death, you participate in his resurrection. **Jesus brings life to the forgiven.**

And if you know that Jesus brings life, you cannot remain silent. You will tell everyone. You will speak. This is not a message to be hidden or whispered in fear. It is the proclamation of hope to a world bound by death.
