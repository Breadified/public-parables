# Matthew 5:31-32

**Title:** Marriage and the Good Life

**Date:** 9 June 2018

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

> 31 "It has been said, 'Anyone who divorces his wife must give her a certificate of divorce.' 32 But I tell you that anyone who divorces his wife, except for sexual immorality, makes her the victim of adultery, and anyone who marries a divorced woman commits adultery.

## When Are You Really Married?

This question seems simple but carries profound implications. In Chinese tradition, marriage was considered official when parents arranged it and money was exchanged—as good as married before the ceremony itself. The tea ceremony, the exchange of dowry, the consummation of the marriage—different cultures mark different moments as the "official" beginning of marriage.

Even in modern times, we struggle with the question: Is it the engagement? The church ceremony? When you sign the register? When you consummate the marriage? When a child is born?

In Scripture, the answer is revealing. Adam and Eve are considered married simply because they are made for each other and they procreate. Jacob marries Leah by consummating their union in the darkness of a tent, not through any formal ceremony. Jesus attends a wedding at Cana that consists of a family party with no described ceremony at all.

## When Are You Really Unmarried?

This is even more difficult. When the love is lost? When the other person says it's over? When adultery occurs? When you say "I divorce you" three times? When you separate? When the government says so? When the church says so? When you remarry?

The very complexity of these questions reveals something crucial: our culture has lost any clear understanding of what marriage actually is.

## The Teaching: No Divorce

The law of God on marriage is clear—there is no divorce allowed.

> 32 But I tell you that anyone who divorces his wife, except for sexual immorality, makes her the victim of adultery, and anyone who marries a divorced woman commits adultery.

Let's set aside the exception clause for a moment. The core teaching states that anyone who divorces his wife makes her a victim of adultery, and anyone who marries a divorced woman commits adultery themselves. The Greek word for divorce means "to loosen, to let go, to separate." If someone marries this divorced woman, that person becomes an adulterer.

This teaching is consistent throughout Scripture. In Luke 16:18, Jesus clarifies that the one who divorces (not just the one who remarries) also commits adultery. Mark 10:1-16 records a fuller version of this teaching, where Jesus explains that divorce violates the "one flesh" unity that God created. When God joins two people together in marriage, no one—absolutely no one—should separate them.

Paul repeats this teaching in 1 Corinthians 7:10-11: "To the married I give this command (not I, but the Lord): A wife must not separate from her husband. But if she does, she must remain unmarried or else be reconciled to her husband. And a husband must not divorce his wife."

Marriage is for life. Romans 7:2-3 makes this crystal clear: "A married woman is bound to her husband as long as he is alive, but if her husband dies, she is released from the law that binds her to him."

Even the Old Testament is unambiguous. Malachi 2:16 records God's own words: "For I hate divorce...So be on your guard, and do not be unfaithful to the wife of your youth."

Marriage consists of three essential elements:

- **Complementarian**: One man and one woman
- **Monogamous**: Exclusivity, no one else
- **For life**: Until one of us dies, for better or worse, for richer or poorer

Without these elements, what's the point? Try proposing with: "I will love you forever—until the love is gone, or you do something stupid." No one would marry you. The promise of marriage is unconditional by its very essence. When you remove that unconditionality, everything about marriage collapses.

## The Apparent Exception

Yet Matthew includes something puzzling—an exception clause: "except for sexual immorality." Mark's account omits this entirely. Why would Matthew add this qualification?

The answer lies in Matthew 1:18-20. Here, before the narrative of Jesus's ministry even begins, we see Joseph in a difficult situation. Mary is "pledged to be married" to Joseph when she is discovered to be pregnant before they come together. Joseph, described as "faithful to the law," considers divorcing her quietly.

This seems contradictory. How can a righteous man—one faithful to the law—divorce without violating God's law against divorce?

The resolution is that the "divorce" being discussed here is not the divorce of a consummated marriage, but rather the dissolution of an engagement—a betrothal period. In Jewish culture, engagement carried legal weight equivalent to marriage itself. Dowry was exchanged. The couple were considered as good as married. Yet the engagement period, before consummation, held a different status than the completed marriage itself.

This likely explains both Deuteronomy 24 and Matthew's exception clause. Deuteronomy 24 addresses situations where a man finds "something indecent" about his betrothed—possibly pre-marital sexual immorality—and issues a certificate of divorce to break the engagement. This protects the woman by freeing her to remarry, crucial in a subsistence world where a woman without a husband faced destitution.

The Pharisees, however, took this loophole and stretched it. They asked Jesus: "Is it lawful for a man to divorce his wife for any and every reason?" They had expanded "something indecent" to mean virtually anything displeasing to the husband—even burning his dinner.

Jesus's response is severe. He points them back to creation itself. At the beginning, God made them male and female. For this reason, a man leaves father and mother and is united to his wife, and the two become one flesh. What God has joined together, let no one separate.

One flesh means something profound. You cannot separate from your own flesh without violence and loss. The unity created in marriage transcends legal categories or cultural customs.

## Why the Ambiguity?

Several solutions have been proposed for the apparent conflict between Deuteronomy 24 and Jesus's teaching:

**First**, perhaps Deuteronomy 24 is not prescribing divorce but limiting damage. Just as the Bible mentions polygamy in certain contexts without prescribing it, perhaps the certificate of divorce was a remedial measure for a sinful situation—"a necessary evil," as one scholar puts it. The law assumes divorces will happen and seeks to protect the woman. The "if" in "if the husband divorces his wife" doesn't mean you should; it means if this evil occurs, here's how to minimize harm.

**Second**, the exception clause may refer specifically to the engagement period, not consummated marriage. The teaching would then be: once you consummate a marriage, there is no divorce, period. But engagement, before consummation, can be dissolved if sexual immorality is discovered.

**Third**, when clear and ambiguous passages of Scripture conflict, the clear passage should govern our interpretation. The teaching that marriage is for life is unambiguous throughout Scripture. The divorce exception clauses and Deuteronomy 24 are genuinely difficult to interpret. In such cases, the clearer teaching should control our understanding of the more ambiguous.

## A Troubling Shift in Christian Practice

Sadly, many Christians have abandoned the permanence of marriage. But this represents a dramatic shift. The Church of England separated from Rome not primarily over divorce itself, but over annulment—yet both traditions believed marriage was for life. The Catholic Church and ancient Anglican Church always held that marriage was indissoluble. This position remained constant until the sexual revolution of the 1960s, when suddenly theology began to shift to accommodate culture.

Is this what Christianity is supposed to do? Rather than stand for everlasting truth, we reshape truth according to the spirit of the age? Is being salt and light about making sin more palatable?

No. Being salt and light means being different. If we divorce just as the world divorces, we offer no alternative vision. We differ only in degree, not in kind.

True Christians are radical. We believe in a faithful God who, though He divorced Israel for her unfaithfulness, never remarried another nation. Instead, He remained free to woo and reclaim Israel when she repented. That's the picture of faithful love we're called to embody.

If your spouse abandons you, the radical Christian response is to wait in hope—not for your own will, but for God's timing. In this way, you learn to appreciate Christ's love for us. We abandoned Him, yet as our groom, He longs for us to return to His loving arms. That's true and faithful love.

Make sure your love looks like that too.

## The Undefinition of Marriage

What's actually happening in our culture is the undefinition of marriage itself.

In the 1960s, contraception severed the link between sex and children for the first time in human history. Marriage became primarily about personal fulfillment and enjoyment rather than procreation and covenant. Without children as the raison d'être of marriage, permanence became optional.

By the 1970s, once children were removed from the equation, there was no reason to remain committed for life. The exceptions (which shouldn't have existed in the first place) opened the door to no-fault divorce. You no longer needed any reason at all. Wedlock became wedlease.

Today, the undefinition continues with the assault on complementarity—the one man and one woman component. Tomorrow, it will come to monogamy. Marriage will become "monogamish," and polygamy will be normalized.

And once marriage is completely undefined, society loses the life script that creates healthy adults and perpetuates the next generation. That's when civilization begins to collapse.

But this fight doesn't have to be lost—not in our circles, at least. If we believe God's word and live it out, starting within our own communities, the world will eventually follow.

The rich already understand this intuitively, which is why their divorce rates are leveling off. They're smart enough to work through their problems. It's the poor who remain trapped in the chaos created by bad laws and a culture that has abandoned marriage.

Believe the word of God. Start with the family of believers. And the world will follow.
