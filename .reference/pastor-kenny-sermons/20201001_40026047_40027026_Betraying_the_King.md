# Matthew 26:47–27:26
**Title:** Betraying the King
**Date:** 19 October 2020
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Violence of Betrayal

Few things in human experience compare to the pain of betrayal. When someone we trust turns against us, it cuts deeper than any physical wound. Consider a prime minister who has placed trust in deputies and colleagues, who has celebrated victory with the system that elected them. Then one night, those closest allies gather in secret and orchestrate their downfall. By morning, the leader who once wielded power is suddenly nobody—stabbed in the back by the very people they worked with.

Betrayal is devastating because it involves more than loss; it involves broken trust, shattered loyalty, and the crushing realization that those we relied on have abandoned us.

Today we step into Jesus' sandals as he faces a series of betrayals. We'll see him betrayed by his disciples, by the nation's leaders, by the Roman government, and ultimately by his own people. Yet through these betrayals, we witness something remarkable: Jesus doesn't run or retaliate. Instead, he takes responsibility. He absorbs the judgment meant for others. He identifies with sinners in order to redeem them.

## 1. Betrayed by Disciples

### a. Judas's Kiss (26:47–54)

During Passover, Jerusalem swells with pilgrims. The temple courts overflow, and people camp in gardens like Gethsemane. Judas uses this crowded setting to his advantage—he arranges a signal with the authorities: "The one I kiss is the man; arrest him."

When Judas approaches Jesus, he greets him as "Rabbi"—teacher. Matthew's careful use of language matters here. In Matthew's gospel, only Jesus' enemies call him rabbi. The kiss, a gesture of affection among friends, becomes a weapon. Judas calls Jesus "friend," but his actions betray the deepest treachery.

Jesus' response is striking: "Do what you came for, friend." Despite knowing what is about to happen, Jesus addresses Judas with remarkable composure. He doesn't rage or condemn. He simply allows events to unfold as Scripture predicted.

### b. The Disciples' Flight (26:55–56)

But the disciples misunderstand Jesus' mission entirely. One of them draws a sword and strikes the servant of the high priest, cutting off his ear. They think Jesus will fight back, that he'll use force to save himself. Jesus corrects them:

> "Put your sword back in its place. All who draw the sword will die by the sword."

He explains that he has access to twelve legions of angels—roughly 72,000 heavenly warriors. A Roman legion alone consisted of 6,000 soldiers. The number twelve echoes Israel, God's people. Jesus isn't powerless; he's choosing obedience to God's plan.

> "Do you think I cannot call on my Father, and he will at once put at my disposal more than twelve legions of angels? But how then would the Scriptures be fulfilled that say it must happen in this way?"

Jesus makes clear that he isn't leading a rebellion. He taught openly in the temple courts. The authorities didn't arrest him then—why the swords and clubs now? This detail matters: Jesus wants them to understand that his arrest isn't necessary for security reasons. It's about fulfilling God's redemptive plan.

This doesn't mean Jesus opposes all warfare. He commends centurions and military officers elsewhere. Defensive warfare and controlled justice against evil are legitimate. God is the Lord of Hosts—the commander of armies. But here, Jesus refuses violence not because he lacks the right to self-defense, but because his mission is the cross. He has already prayed in Gethsemane, "Father, if it is possible, may this cup be taken from me." Now, seeing God's will clearly, he moves toward Golgotha. This is how the world and God will be reconciled. This is salvation.

The disciples can't see this. They only see the temporal—the arrest, the danger. They don't grasp the gospel unfolding before them. Fear overtakes them:

> "Then all the disciples deserted him and fled."

They scatter to the mountains, as Jesus himself predicted. One by one, his community abandons him.

### c. Peter's Denial (26:69–75)

Even Peter, Jesus' most loyal disciple, falls away. While Jesus faces interrogation in the high priest's courtyard, Peter lurks nearby—curious? Concerned? Perhaps hoping to help?

A servant girl confronts him: "You also were with Jesus of Galilee."

Peter denies it: "I don't know what you're talking about."

A second girl sees him by the gateway and tells the crowd: "This fellow was with Jesus of Nazareth."

Again, Peter denies it, this time with an oath: "I don't know the man!"

The accusations escalate. A third time, bystanders point out his accent gives him away: "Surely you are one of them."

Now Peter's denials intensify. He begins to curse and swear: "I don't know the man!"

The Greek text here is worth noting. The old NIV reads "curse himself," but the original Greek lacks "himself." It's reasonable to conclude that Peter curses Jesus—that in his panic, Peter doesn't merely distance himself from Jesus; he actively disowns him.

Think about the progression: "I don't know what you're talking about" (vague denial) → oath (formal denial) → curses and swearing (violent denial). Each step deepens Peter's betrayal. He curses the Jesus who called him from the fishing nets by the sea of Galilee. He curses the Jesus whose miracles he witnessed, the Jesus that multitudes hailed as "the prophet of Nazareth." He commits perjury, swearing false oaths in the courtyard of judges—forgetting Jesus' own teaching about oaths in the Sermon on the Mount.

This is what happens under pressure when we no longer want to associate with someone: we deny, deny, deny. We slander. We distance ourselves completely. Modern corruption scandals show the same pattern—everyone distances themselves from the one who falls.

Then the rooster crows. Peter remembers Jesus' words: "Before the rooster crows, you will disown me three times." And Peter goes outside and weeps bitterly.

The shepherd is struck, and the sheep scatter. All of Jesus' friends have left him.

## 2. Betrayed by the Nation

### a. Israel's Leaders—A Fake Trial (26:57–68)

The high priests, teachers of the law, and elders—the Sanhedrin, Israel's governing council—assemble against Jesus. These leaders are supposed to protect their people from injustice, to shield them from pagan influence. Instead, they betray one of their own.

They begin by seeking false evidence. The chief priests and Sanhedrin search desperately for testimony that will justify executing Jesus. Many false witnesses come forward, but their stories don't align. Finally, two witnesses make an accusation:

> "This fellow said, 'I am able to destroy the temple of God and rebuild it in three days.'"

Even this is a distortion. Jesus had spoken of his body as the temple, not the physical structure.

The high priest demands: "Are you not going to answer? What is this testimony that these men are bringing against you?"

Jesus remains silent. He doesn't dignify false accusations with a response.

Then the high priest escalates, putting Jesus under oath by the living God: "Tell us if you are the Messiah, the Son of God."

Jesus responds: "You have said so. But I say to all of you: From now on you will see the Son of Man sitting at the right hand of the Mighty One and coming on the clouds of heaven."

This statement claims equality with God. To sit at God's right hand is to be equal with God. The high priest immediately tears his clothes:

> "He has spoken blasphemy! Why do we need any more witnesses? Look, now you have heard the blasphemy."

They get him on the truth. Instead of defending himself with false evidence, Jesus tells the truth, and it becomes the charge against him. The council pronounces: "He is worthy of death."

Then comes the violence: "They spit in his face and struck him with their fists. Others slapped him and said, 'Prophesy to us, Messiah. Who hit you?'"

This brutality echoes Isaiah 50:6—the Suffering Servant passage:

> "I offered my back to those who beat me, my cheeks to those who pulled out my beard; I did not hide my face from mocking and spitting."

Jesus endures the abuse. He doesn't strike back. He doesn't curse. He takes it.

### b. The Roman State—Pilate's Cowardice (27:1–26)

The pattern repeats with Rome's governor. The chief priests and elders hand Jesus over to Pilate, who alone has authority to execute criminals in the province.

Pilate questions Jesus: "Are you the king of the Jews?"

Jesus replies: "You have said so."

When accused by the leaders, Jesus gives no answer. Pilate asks, "Don't you hear the testimony they are bringing against you?" But Jesus makes no reply—not even to a single charge. The governor is amazed at his silence.

Sometimes wisdom means saying nothing. In modern trials by social media, silence often shows more strength than desperate explanations.

Pilate realizes Jesus is innocent. He knows the leaders handed Jesus over out of self-interest, not justice. Even Pilate's own wife warns him: "Don't have anything to do with that innocent man, for I have suffered a great deal today in a dream because of him."

But Pilate is trapped. An uproar is starting. The crowd demands action. Pilate washes his hands in front of everyone:

> "I am innocent of this man's blood. It is your responsibility!"

The irony is stunning. "I am innocent," Pilate declares, while condemning an innocent man.

This echoes Judas' earlier moment. When Judas returns the thirty pieces of silver, seized with remorse, he confesses: "I have sinned, for I have betrayed innocent blood."

The chief priests dismiss him: "What is that to us? That's your responsibility."

Both Judas and Pilate try to escape their complicity. They both say the same thing: this is not my problem. Justice isn't my concern. Doing the right thing isn't my burden. Dealing fairly is someone else's job.

But Jesus doesn't escape. He doesn't deflect. Instead of washing his hands like Pilate, Jesus takes on the responsibility. He says, "Not my will, but God's will." He doesn't shrink from his duty. He walks toward the cross.

## 3. Betrayed by His Own People (27:20–26)

### a. Barabbas—The Wrong Choice (27:15–26)

The worst betrayal comes last: Jesus is betrayed by his own people.

Pilate tries to find a way out. There's a custom at festival time to release a prisoner chosen by the crowd. Pilate presents the choice: "Which one do you want me to release to you: Jesus Barabbas, or Jesus who is called the Messiah?"

The irony cuts deep. Two Jesuses. One named Barabbas—likely meaning "son of the father." One called the Messiah—the true Son of the Father. The choice should be obvious.

But the chief priests and elders persuade the crowd. They manipulate the very people Jesus came to save. Pilate asks again: "Which of the two do you want me to release to you?"

"Barabbas!" the crowd shouts.

Barabbas is no ordinary prisoner. He's a well-known criminal. Everyone knows who he is. Yet the crowd chooses a criminal over the Messiah. They choose him over the one who taught in their temple courts. They choose him over their own people's rescuer.

Pilate presses: "What shall I do, then, with Jesus who is called the Messiah?"

"Crucify him!" they cry.

"Why? What crime has he committed?"

"Crucify him!" they shout louder.

This is the wisdom of the crowd. The sheeple. Democracy divorced from truth. Yet through this terrible wrong decision, Jesus uses the cross to bring salvation to the world. Jesus faces judgment while Barabbas goes free. A criminal escapes while the innocent man is condemned.

And here's the gospel: Jesus faces judgment so that we might go free. We are the criminals. We are the sinners. And Jesus takes our place.

### b. Identified with Sinners (26:55, 27:38)

Back in the trial before the Jewish leaders, Jesus makes one protest:

> "Am I leading a rebellion, that you have come out with swords and clubs to capture me? Every day I sat in the temple courts teaching, and you did not arrest me."

The word translated "rebellion" can also mean "robber." Jesus asks: "Am I a robber?" He's treated as a criminal, given a label he doesn't deserve. Yet next week we'll see him die between two robbers—identified with criminals.

But here's the deeper truth: the real robbers are in the temple courts. Jesus himself called the temple "a den of robbers" (Matthew 21:13). He's been betrayed by that very den of robbers. Yet he doesn't condemn them. He dies for them.

The leaders claim, "His blood is on us and on our children!" They take responsibility in the worst way—through curse rather than repentance. But the blood is not on them. The blood is on Jesus. His blood cleanses. His blood redeems. His blood saves—even those who betray him.

## The Gospel in the Betrayal

Jesus did not betray us.

It is we who betray Jesus.

We are like the crowds who sing "Hosanna!" on Palm Sunday, who welcome Jesus as king. But when the tables turn, when times are tough, when we don't get what we want—we betray him.

When we lust for material goods, for power, for worldly desires—we betray Jesus.

When we seek approval from the government, from those in power—we betray Jesus.

When we fear the powerful more than we fear God—we betray Jesus.

Yet Jesus, rather than dismiss us as Pilate dismisses him, takes responsibility for us. Out of love, he identifies with sinners. He walks toward the cross not in anger but in mercy.

If ever you doubt the goodness of God, look to what he has done. Jesus dies for those who betray him. He absorbs the judgment. He takes the curse. He offers freedom to the guilty.

This is the gospel. This is grace. This is why we say: Thank God.
