# Luke 16:1-14

**Title:** Popularity
**Date:** 1 December 2014
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction: The Question of Popularity

We all want to be popular. We seek quality friendships—connections with influential people who can open doors for us. We want quantity too—the validation of many Facebook friends, the dopamine hit of likes on our selfies. And we'll do whatever it takes: wear the right clothes, spend the right money, keep up with current trends, watch popular movies, listen to popular songs, eat at the right places, and carry the latest gadgets and fashion.

But here's what might surprise you: **Jesus was also into popularity.**

The difference is this: If you're not popular, you ask, "How can I become more popular?" But the real question Jesus teaches us is, "I am popular—how can I use this to gain friends in eternal dwelling?"

## Part One: Gaining Earthly Friends (Luke 16:1-8)

Jesus tells a parable about a manager who has been squandering his master's possessions. He played computer games all day, watched Korean dramas—essentially wasted his employer's time and money.

When the rich man calls him to account, the manager faces unemployment. Looking desperately for a solution, he realizes he's in trouble.

> "Then the manager said to himself, 'What should I do, since my master is taking the management away from me? I'm not strong enough to dig; I'm ashamed to beg." (Luke 16:3)

This man must have been Asian—he's too skinny and pale for manual labor, too proud to go on welfare. He can only work in an air-conditioned office. That's why so many end up as accountants.

But he comes up with a solution. He simply needs an open door.

> "I know what I'll do so that when I'm removed from management, people will welcome me into their homes." (Luke 16:4)

He needs favors. He needs a patron. He needs friends. And he needs them fast.

So he calls his master's debtors and begins offering them discounts: fifty percent off the olive oil, twenty percent off the wheat. On the surface, there's creative accounting happening here. But look deeper at what he's actually doing—**he's generating favors.** He's quickly making deals with his clients so that they'll owe him, so that when he loses his job, they'll welcome him in.

This is the accountant principle: When accountants lose their jobs, they often go to their clients they've treated well in the past. Those clients remember the good treatment and later, when they're in new positions, they send work back to their old accounting firm friends.

And here's the remarkable part:

> "The master praised the unrighteous manager because he had acted astutely. For the sons of this age are more astute than the sons of light in dealing with their own people." (Luke 16:8)

This praise seems strange. The manager is dishonest, gaining friends at his master's expense. Yet Jesus praises him—not for dishonesty, but for **shrewdness**.

The word "astute" means shrewd, wise, street-smart, discerning, perceptive—having the ability to accurately assess situations and turn them to your advantage. The manager knew his time was up. Instead of sitting around bemoaning his fate, he used his earthly brains to find the best solution. He used every legitimate means to escape his predicament.

### The Balance: Astuteness Without Dishonesty

**We are not to be dishonest.** This passage does not condone dishonesty. Christians are called to be people of truth. Dishonesty and lies have no place in the Christian life—they belong to the devil, the father of all lies.

**But we are to be astute.** Jesus taught this explicitly:

> "Look, I'm sending you out like sheep among wolves. Therefore be as shrewd as serpents and as harmless as doves." (Matthew 10:16)

Use your brains to solve difficult problems. When you're in trouble, admit the truth and fix it. There are two critical things to be astute about:

1. **Recognize reality.** Know when your situation is genuinely difficult and requires action.
2. **Act with wisdom.** Find solutions that don't compromise your integrity but do leverage what resources and relationships you have.

## Part Two: Friendship with God (Luke 16:9-15)

Now Jesus pivots. He's not just talking about gaining earthly friends—he's pointing us toward something far more important: gaining friends in eternal dwelling.

Notice the parallel he draws:

> "I know what I'll do so that when I'm removed from management, people will welcome me into their homes." (Luke 16:4)

vs.

> "Make friends for yourselves by means of the unrighteous money so that when it fails, they may welcome you into eternal dwellings." (Luke 16:9)

The story isn't about merely finding friends on earth—it's about finding **friends in heaven**. It's about the life after.

This becomes crystal clear in verse 13:

> "No household slave can be the slave of two masters, since either he will hate one and love the other, or he will be devoted to one and despise the other. You can't be slaves to both God and money." (Luke 16:13)

There is no middle ground. We cannot serve two masters. We will hate one and love the other, or be devoted to one and despise the other. It's like being half-pregnant—it's not possible.

The problem is that we've been ignoring God because we've been idolizing material things. Money itself isn't wrong, but we need to see beyond money to the Creator of money and all things.

Consider this illustration: Your parents gave you good things. They migrated to a new country, worked two jobs to learn English and establish themselves. But imagine if you only took those good things, enjoyed them, never thanked your parents, and when people asked where you got them, you claimed you earned them yourself. That's a betrayal.

Similarly, becoming a Christian costs something. It costs money:

- **Joining a church** means no part-time job on Sundays—some Sunday jobs pay 2.5 times more.
- **A change in lifestyle** toward generosity costs money.
- **Supporting Bible ministries** costs money.
- **Not being overly ambitious** about career costs money.
- **University camps and discipleship experiences** are expensive—don't let that stop your growth. Save up for them.
- **Getting to know your church community** requires small investments in meals and time together.

Don't let these financial commitments intimidate you. Be realistic about your lifestyle. Keep up with fashions and gadgets moderately. Eat at home more often. Avoid materialism and greed. Spend wisely. Get a job if you need to, but not one that affects your church involvement or family relationships.

The warning is clear:

> "Whoever is faithful in very little is also faithful in much, and whoever is unrighteous in very little is also unrighteous in much. So if you have not been faithful with the unrighteous money, who will trust you with what is genuine? And if you have not been faithful with what belongs to someone else, who will give you what is your own?" (Luke 16:10-12)

### The Pharisees' Failure

The problem with the Pharisees was that they let money get between them and God:

> "The Pharisees, who were lovers of money, were listening to all these things and scoffing at Him." (Luke 16:14)

They were devoted to money, not to God. They weren't using their money in the right way. They were calculative and legalistic rather than generous and faithful. In the end, it's friendship with God that matters—not merely popularity or buying the judges of public opinion.

> "You are the ones who justify yourselves in the sight of others, but God knows your hearts. For what is highly admired by people is revolting in God's sight." (Luke 16:15)

God cannot be bought. God cannot be tricked. God is not fooled by popularity or appearances. What matters is the orientation of your heart.

## Part Three: Gaining Friends in Eternal Dwelling (Luke 16:16)

The Pharisees were in a unique position. They had been given the responsibility to bring the good news of God's forgiveness to the Gentiles (Isaiah 42:6; 49:6). They were to use their wealth and influence to proclaim God's word to those outside their nation. This was their calling.

The promise was this:

> "The Lord of Hosts says this: 'In those days, 10 men from nations of every language will grab the robe of a Jewish man tightly, urging: Let us go with you, for we have heard that God is with you.'" (Zechariah 8:23)

The Jews were meant to be **popular—in the godly sense.** But the nation of Israel failed. Instead of elevating standards, they sank to the depravity of the nations around them. They had the same political troubles, fought among themselves just like other nations, and followed the same materialistic idol gods. They failed to be the "people of light."

In judgment, Jesus rejects them:

> "The Law and the Prophets were proclaimed until John. Since that time, the good news of the kingdom of God is being preached, and everyone is forcing his way into it." (Luke 16:16, NIV84)

What happened? The situation reversed. The Jews heard the good news and hesitated—is this a good idea or not? But the Gentiles came eagerly: "Let us in!"

It's like the housing market in Hurstville. Some investors hesitate, trying to bargain down the price. But the mainlanders just grab—it's so cheap! **Everyone is forcing their way in. The Gentiles are coming.**

And so the reversal comes: Instead of ten Gentiles clinging to one Jewish man, it becomes ten Jews hanging onto one Gentile. The Pharisees failed to use their position and resources to make friends with the lost.

### Application for Today

We are not in the same position as the Jews, but we can learn from them: **Make friends with the lost.** Use your resources for it.

You might regret many things in heaven, but the deepest regret will be this: "I didn't do a little bit more. I didn't spend that extra time with that unbeliever. Life got busy, and although I cared, I was careless."

**Use money and make a friend.** Buy them lunch. Pick them up (petrol money). Get them a book or a Bible.

If you're not popular, that's fine. But if you **are** popular—if you have charm, the ability to communicate, friendliness, or emotional intelligence—then you have to use that popularity so others might hear the good news. You may lose some earthly popularity in the process, but that's okay. Get people around you and tell them about Jesus. **Use your popularity to gain friends in eternal dwellings.**

### Social Media: A Tool for Kingdom Purposes

Social media can be both dangerous and helpful. Be astute in how you use it:

- **Tag good articles** for others to read.
- **Add friends and invite them to church.** You can find anyone nowadays.
- **If you're brave, engage more controversial topics.** Many have hundreds of friends and do nothing. They simply share cat videos and NBA highlights. Be more useful. Tag articles on important truths. Everyone is listening. Yes, you'll face confrontation—be polite. Ultimately, you're not writing for those who will never change their minds. You're writing for the middle group—those who don't yet know what to think, who don't have an opinion. The louder the opposition, the more credible your reasoned position becomes.

## Conclusion: Two Core Callings

We all want to be popular. We're all looking for friends. But God calls us to something higher:

**1. Be friends with Him.** Don't give that up. Don't let money or earthly popularity get in the way of your relationship with God.

**2. Be friends with the lost.** Use your money and earthly popularity for their sake. Invest in relationships that lead to eternal fruit.

Friendship costs something—money, time, commitment, loyalty, steadfastness. Real friendships are not fair-weather. But they're worth everything when they lead someone to know Jesus.

**Social media can be both a danger and a help. Be astute in how you use it.**
