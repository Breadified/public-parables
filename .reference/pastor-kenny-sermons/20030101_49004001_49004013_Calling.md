# Ephesians 4:1-13

**Title:** Calling

**Date:** January 2003

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## One Calling

> As a prisoner for the Lord, then, I urge you to live a life worthy of the calling you have received. Be completely humble and gentle; be patient, bearing with one another in love. Make every effort to keep the unity of the Spirit through the bond of peace. There is one body and one Spirit, just as you were called to one hope when you were called; one Lord, one faith, one baptism; one God and Father of all, who is over all and through all and in all.

Paul saturates this passage with the language of calling. What is this fundamental calling? It's the gospel itself—the one gospel of the one Lord, brought about by the one God, and effected by the one Spirit. We enter it through one faith and one baptism, belong to one body, and await one hope at his return.

In essence, we are called to be Christians. This is our supernatural calling, regardless of whether we're Jewish or Gentile, regardless of our background or time period. All believers share in this oneness.

Yet we often take this for granted. Once we become Christians, we ask, "So what else is there?" We miss the magnitude of what God has done. Consider someone enjoying a million-dollar view and unlimited seafood at the top of a tower—there is nothing else to experience that night. Similarly, being called to the one Lord and adopted as sons and daughters of the Most High God, seated in the heavenly realms with all riches, power, and wisdom—there is nothing beyond this. This is enough.

The Bible uses "calling" (Greek: *klesis*) over 300 times, and overwhelmingly it refers to being called to be Christians and to be holy. While Scripture mentions specific callings to prophets, priests, and apostles like Paul to the Gentiles, these are exceptional Old Testament appointments. The normative biblical calling is salvation itself—new birth and election.

## The Question of Vocational Ministry

Many Christians today speak of being "called" to full-time ministry or to specific careers. But Scripture provides no evidence for this kind of vocational calling. When we examine those who claim such callings, we notice something curious: the Spirit seems remarkably selective about his career guidance, always steering people toward prestigious, famous, or well-paid positions. Strangely, no one ever reports being called to be a plumber or a nurse. The Holy Spirit, it seems, functions as an upper-middle-class career counselor.

I don't believe there's biblical warrant for a "spooky calling" into full-time ministry. When our pastors and missionary friends speak of feeling called, I don't think they're deliberately being unbiblical. Rather, they're trying to express a genuine conviction about their path, a natural and commendable desire—but unfortunately they're clothing it in hyper-spiritual language that implies waiting for supernatural, direct guidance from God. This can be misleading.

## Multiple Opportunities in Ministry

But the passage doesn't end with discouragement. Paul writes:

> To each one of us grace has been given as Christ apportioned it. When he ascended on high, he took many captives and gave gifts to his people. He who descended is the very one who ascended higher than all the heavens, in order to fill the whole universe. So Christ himself gave the apostles, the prophets, the evangelists, the pastors and teachers, to equip his people for works of service, so that the body of Christ may be built up until we all reach unity in the faith and in the knowledge of the Son of God and become mature, attaining to the whole measure of the fullness of Christ.

Paul quotes Psalm 68, where God the conqueror comes to Jerusalem. But notice the transformation: In Psalm 68, the nations bring tribute to the king. In Ephesians, the king gives gifts to his people.

Christ distributed different kinds of leaders—apostles, prophets, evangelists, pastors, and teachers—to build up the body. The apostles and prophets functioned as foundational teachers of the Old Testament, now embodied in Scripture. Pastors and teachers represent the ongoing generation of leaders who equip God's people.

The goal is threefold: unity in faith, knowledge of the Son of God, and maturity. Paul contrasts this with spiritual infancy—being "tossed back and forth by the waves, blown here and there by every wind of teaching and by the cunning and craftiness of people in their deceitful scheming."

Instead, we grow by "speaking the truth in love" as a mature body joined together, with each part doing its work.

## All of Us in Ministry

Here's the key insight: We are all called to ministry. Not in the sense of holding a paid position, but in the reality that each of us is a gift to one another for mutual edification. We are to encourage each other, build each other up, and speak God's truth to one another. This is full-time in the sense that it's always on our radar—a constant lookout for opportunities to minister.

This happens everywhere: in university, at work, with family, in the grocery store. Wherever we go, we can speak truth in love to those around us.

## Discerning Professional Ministry

So when should someone pursue professional, paid ministry? It's not because of a mystical calling—you already received that calling at your conversion. It's not because it's "full-time ministry"—you're already there in that sense.

Rather, it's a wisdom decision. As churches and Christian organizations form, there's a practical need for mature, godly people to give up day jobs to concentrate on Bible learning and teaching. Economically, it makes sense to support those who are relatively stronger in character, maturity, responsibility, and skill.

Charles Spurgeon outlined helpful criteria for discerning this path:

- Does the individual have godly character?
- Do they genuinely want to do ministry?
- Are they actually effective at it?
- Do others agree they're effective?

The decision itself involves evaluating opportunities, your own gifts, and what those around you observe about your ministry effectiveness. This is wisdom—discerned through prayer, counsel, and circumstance.

My own story illustrates this. Since my conversion in secondary school, I've loved ministry, learning, teaching, and organizing. Years later, facing a choice between government work (which offered time flexibility for ministry) and formal ministry training (which concerned me financially), I realized the core decision had already been made long ago. At conversion, I signed a blank check with God: "Do whatever you want with my life." When that settled reality was firm, the subsequent career decisions became manageable details.

The real question isn't about today's job choice. It's: Do you believe the gospel? Have you truly answered Christ's call to conversion? The rest are details.
