# Matthew 2:1-12

**Title:** The Birth of Jesus and the Magi's Worship

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Arrival of the Magi

> After Jesus was born in Bethlehem in Judea, during the time of King Herod, Magi from the east came to Jerusalem and asked, "Where is the one who has been born king of the Jews? We saw his star when it rose and have come to worship him." (Matthew 2:1-2)

The Magi—wise men from the east—traveled a significant distance guided by a star, compelled by a singular purpose: to worship the newborn king. Their journey represents genuine seeking, a willingness to travel far and traverse obstacles in pursuit of worship. These were men of knowledge and discernment who recognized something extraordinary in the heavens and responded with devotion.

## Herod's Disturbance

> When King Herod heard this he was disturbed, and all Jerusalem with him. (Matthew 2:3)

Herod's reaction stands in stark contrast to the Magi's worship. News of a born king of the Jews troubled him deeply—and his anxiety spread throughout Jerusalem. This reveals the fundamental difference between the Magi's posture (seeking to worship) and Herod's posture (seeking to protect his throne). Where there is genuine faith seeking, there is often fear and resistance from those threatened by the kingdom of God.

## The Search for the Messiah

> When he had called together all the peoples chief priests and teachers of the law, he asked them where the Messiah was to be born. In Bethlehem in Judea, they replied, for this is what the prophet has written: "But you, Bethlehem, in the land of Judah, are by no means least among the rulers of Judah; for out of you will come a ruler who will shepherd my people Israel." (Matthew 2:4-6)

The Jewish leaders possessed the prophetic knowledge—they knew exactly where the Messiah would be born. Yet this intellectual knowledge did not lead them to worship or seek Jesus themselves. They could cite the Scripture, understand the prophecy, yet remain unmoved. This highlights a crucial spiritual lesson: knowing the truth about Jesus intellectually is not the same as seeking Him devotionally.

## Herod's Deception

> Then Herod called the Magi secretly and found out from them the exact time the star had appeared. He sent them to Bethlehem and said, "Go and search carefully for the child. As soon as you find him, report to me, so that I too may go and worship him." (Matthew 2:7-8)

Herod's words mask his true intentions. He claims to desire worship, but his heart harbors murderous intent. He seeks information—the exact timing of the star's appearance—to locate the child. This deception reveals that not all who speak of seeking God are genuine in their pursuit. Herod represents false worship masked by religious language.

## The Magi's Worship

> After they had heard the king, they went on their way, and the star they had seen when it rose went ahead of them until it stopped over the place where the child was. When they saw the star, they were overjoyed. On coming to the house, they saw the child with his mother Mary, and they bowed down and worshiped him. Then they opened their treasures and presented him with gifts of gold, frankincense and myrrh. (Matthew 2:9-11)

The Magi's arrival at Jesus transforms their journey from intellectual pursuit to experiential worship. Their overflowing joy—described vividly—demonstrates the satisfaction found in meeting the living God. Their gifts—gold (representing kingship), frankincense (representing priesthood), and myrrh (representing suffering and death)—foreshadow the fullness of Christ's redemptive work. They gave their treasures not out of obligation but from a heart overflowing with worship.

## Divine Protection and Obedience

> And having been warned in a dream not to go back to Herod, they returned to their country by another route. (Matthew 2:12)

God graciously protected both the Christ child and the Magi through a divine warning. The Magi's obedience to this warning—taking a different route home rather than reporting back to Herod—demonstrates their authentic faith. They aligned themselves with God's purposes rather than with political authority. Their obedience became part of the protective plan for the Messiah.

## Reflections on Worship and Seeking

This passage invites us to examine our own hearts: Are we seeking Jesus with the genuine devotion of the Magi, willing to travel far and offer our treasures? Or do we possess intellectual knowledge about Christ without the transformative pursuit of worship? The contrast between Herod and the Magi, between the Jewish leaders and the wise men, challenges us to move beyond mere information into genuine encounter with the living Christ.
