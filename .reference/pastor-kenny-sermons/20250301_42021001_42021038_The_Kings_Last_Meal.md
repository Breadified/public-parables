# Luke 21:1-38

**Title:** The King's Last Meal
**Date:** 23 March 2025
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Conspiracy and Betrayal

> Now the Festival of Unleavened Bread, called the Passover, was approaching, and the chief priests and the teachers of the law were looking for some way to get rid of Jesus, for they were afraid of the people. Then Satan entered Judas, called Iscariot, one of the Twelve. And Judas went to the chief priests and the officers of the temple guard and discussed with them how he might betray Jesus. They were delighted and agreed to give him money. He consented, and watched for an opportunity to hand Jesus over to them when no crowd was present. (Luke 22:1-6)

The darkness deepens as Jesus approaches His final hours. While the religious leaders plotted against Him, fear gripped their hearts—not fear of Jesus, but fear of the people who loved Him. Into this moment of human conspiracy came Satan himself, entering Judas's heart. This collision of human scheming and demonic influence reveals the cosmic stakes of the Crucifixion.

## The Passover Preparation

> Then came the day of Unleavened Bread on which the Passover lamb had to be sacrificed. Jesus sent Peter and John, saying, "Go and make preparations for us to eat the Passover." (Luke 22:7-8)

Jesus orchestrated every detail of His final meal with His disciples. When Peter and John asked where to prepare, Jesus gave them precise instructions—they would meet a man carrying water, follow him to his house, and ask the owner about the guest room. The Gospel tells us: "They left and found things just as Jesus had told them. So they prepared the Passover." (Luke 22:13)

This wasn't accident or coincidence. Jesus knew exactly what would unfold. Even in the face of betrayal and death, He moved with complete authority and purpose.

## The Last Passover

> When the hour came, Jesus and his apostles reclined at the table. And he said to them, "I have eagerly desired to eat this Passover with you before I suffer. For I tell you, I will not eat it again until it finds fulfillment in the kingdom of God." (Luke 22:14-16)

Notice the depth of Christ's longing: "I have eagerly desired to eat this Passover with you." Despite knowing the agony that awaited Him, His heart was fixed on His disciples. He was about to become what the Passover lamb foreshadowed—the ultimate sacrifice for God's people.

## The New Covenant

> After taking the cup, he gave thanks and said, "Take this and divide it among you. For I tell you I will not drink again from the fruit of the vine until the kingdom of God comes." And he took bread, gave thanks and broke it, and gave it to them, saying, "This is my body given for you; do this in remembrance of me." In the same way, after the supper he took the cup, saying, "This cup is the new covenant in my blood, which is poured out for you. (Luke 22:17-20)

In these sacred moments, Jesus instituted what the Church has observed ever since. The bread represents His body broken for us. The cup represents the new covenant—His blood shed not just for Israel, but for all who would believe. The old covenant, sealed with animal sacrifices, now gives way to the eternal covenant sealed with His own blood.

## The Shadow of Betrayal

> But the hand of him who is going to betray me is with mine on the table. The Son of Man will go as it has been decreed. But woe to that man who betrays him! (Luke 22:21-22)

Even as Jesus spoke of redemption, He made clear the reality of Judas's coming treachery. Yet notice: Jesus did not say Judas *would choose* betrayal, but that it *has been decreed*. God's sovereignty and human responsibility both stand true, though the mystery of how they work together remains beyond our full comprehension.

## True Greatness

> A dispute also arose among them as to which of them was considered to be greatest. Jesus said to them, "The kings of the Gentiles lord it over them; and those who exercise authority over them call themselves Benefactors. But you are not to be like that. Instead, the greatest among you should be like the youngest, and the one who rules like the one who serves. For who is greater, the one who is at the table or the one who serves? Is it not the one who is at the table? But I am among you as one who serves." (Luke 22:24-27)

Even at the Last Supper, the disciples argued about who was greatest. How human, how sad. Yet how perfect is Jesus's response. He didn't shame them for their ambition—He redirected it toward a kingdom where greatness is measured by service, not status.

Jesus embodied this truth. The King of kings washed their feet and served them at table. He alone possessed the right to demand honor, yet He chose the servant's towel. This is the measure of Christian greatness: not power over others, but power surrendered in love for others.

## The Kingdom Reward

> You are those who have stood by me in my trials. And I confer on you a kingdom, just as my Father conferred one on me, so that you may eat and drink at my table in my kingdom and sit on thrones, judging the twelve tribes of Israel. (Luke 22:28-30)

For all their weakness and coming failures, Jesus honored His disciples. They had stayed with Him through His trials. And now He conferred on them a kingdom—not because they earned it through perfect faithfulness, but as a gift of grace. The promise is breathtaking: they would sit on thrones, judging the twelve tribes of Israel. This speaks to the dignity and authority that awaits the faithful in God's future kingdom.

## Peter's Test

> "Simon, Simon, Satan has asked to sift all of you as wheat. But I have prayed for you, Simon, that your faith may not fail. And when you have turned back, strengthen your brothers." (Luke 22:31-32)

Jesus called Peter by his given name—twice—to capture his full attention. Satan had requested permission to *sift* the disciples like wheat in a sieve, testing their faith to its breaking point. Yet Jesus's prayer for Peter is extraordinary: "Your faith will not fail." Jesus didn't promise Peter would never stumble; He promised that Peter's foundational faith would hold.

And there's a future mercy implicit in these words: "When you have turned back, strengthen your brothers." Jesus knew Peter would deny Him. But He also knew Peter would repent and eventually become a pillar of the Church, strengthening countless believers with the gospel.

## Peter's Confidence

> But he replied, "Lord, I am ready to go with you to prison and to death." (Luke 22:33)

Peter's bold declaration is touching and tragic. He truly believed he could follow Jesus anywhere. But he didn't yet know his own weakness. Within hours, he would deny even knowing Jesus.

> Jesus answered, "I tell you, Peter, before the rooster crows today, you will deny three times that you know me." (Luke 22:34)

The rooster's crow became Peter's humiliation and—ultimately—his restoration. Jesus spoke this not as condemnation but as fact. He knew Peter more deeply than Peter knew himself.

## Provision and Preparation

> Then Jesus asked them, "When I sent you without purse, bag or sandals, did you lack anything?" "Nothing," they answered. He said to them, "But now if you have a purse, take it, and also a bag; and if you don't have a sword, sell your cloak and buy one. It is written: 'And he was numbered with the transgressors'; and I tell you that this must be fulfilled in me. Yes, what is written about me is reaching its fulfillment." The disciples said, "See, Lord, here are two swords." "That's enough!" he replied. (Luke 22:35-38)

Jesus reminded His disciples of the provision He had given them during His ministry. They went out with nothing, yet lacked nothing. But now, as His rejection and death drew near, the conditions were changing. He told them to prepare—to take purse and bag. And the words about the sword can be puzzling until we remember: Jesus was about to be arrested and executed. He would be "numbered with the transgressors." The time of peaceful ministry was ending; the time of suffering and sacrifice had come.

The disciples' literal interpretation of "buy a sword" misses Jesus's deeper point. He wasn't calling them to armed resistance. Rather, He was preparing them for the dark hours ahead when Jesus Himself would face the full force of human rejection and divine judgment.
