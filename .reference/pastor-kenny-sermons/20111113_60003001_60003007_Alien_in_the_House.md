## The Challenge of Living as an Alien at Home

We've been exploring Peter's call to live as aliens in this world, declaring God's praises to those around us. But what happens when this command meets our most intimate relationships? How do we witness to an unbelieving spouse? How do we respond when our parents don't share our faith?

Peter's answer is surprising and counterintuitive: **win them over without words**.

> Wives, in the same way be submissive to your husbands so that, if any of them do not believe the word, they may be won over without words by the behavior of their wives. (1 Peter 3:1)

This applies not only to wives with unbelieving husbands, but to all wives. Scripture confirms this pattern elsewhere:

> Wives, submit to your husbands as to the Lord. (Ephesians 5:22)

---

## Understanding Submission: The Word Nobody Wants to Say

Let's face it—submission is a dirty word in our culture. In modern Australia, where women now comprise a higher percentage of university attendees than men, where girls' schools consistently outrank boys' schools academically, the very concept of submission feels wrong.

We envision the "Stepford wife": perfectly coiffed, perfectly obedient, perfectly inauthentic. Or worse, we think of the doormat wife, trapped in exploitation and abuse.

Men fear being labeled misogynists for even mentioning it. Church leaders tiptoe around the passage with endless qualifications. Women who embrace it are accused of betraying the feminist cause.

If you're uncomfortable reading this, that's natural. Please stay with me. I want to make two critical points about biblical submission, then address your objections.

### Submission Flows from the Heart

Biblical submission is fundamentally about what happens inside you, not the rules you follow externally. Peter explains this in verses 2-4:

> Your beauty should not come from outward adornment, such as braided hair and the wearing of gold jewelry and fine clothes. Instead, it should be that of your inner self, the unfading beauty of a gentle and quiet spirit, which is of great worth in God's sight. (1 Peter 3:3-4)

Submission means:
- **A pure life**—one that doesn't harbor resentment, disdain, or constant complaint
- **A reverent life**—one that takes God's Word seriously and seeks to live it out
- **A gentle spirit**—not nagging, manipulating, argumentative, or impatient
- **A quiet spirit**—one that happily accepts your husband's leadership without constantly trying to usurp it

This is why Peter encourages women not to find their identity in elaborate hairstyles, gold jewelry, or fine clothes. True beauty—the kind the holy women of Scripture cultivated—is found in a pure, reverent, gentle, and quiet heart.

> For this is the way the holy women of the past, who put their hope in God, used to make themselves beautiful. They were submissive to their own husbands. (1 Peter 3:5)

**A Reality Check on Beauty**

We've lost perspective on what beauty actually is. Our grandmothers and great-aunts lived nearby. We watched them age, grow frail, and eventually pass away. That regular exposure taught us a profound truth: physical beauty is temporary. What endures is character—a gentle and quiet spirit.

Today, most of us live in isolated age cohorts. We surround ourselves with friends our own age, dressed from the same shops, reinforced by media images of impossibly young and thin celebrities. We attend a hundred weddings where brides have spent a fortune on their appearance. Our sisters compete over who's thinner, who's cuter. It's counterproductive.

**To our young women:** God cares about your true beauty. He values what's on the inside far more than what's on the outside. Don't be pressured by worldly standards. It's perfectly fine to wear modest, ill-fitting clothes. Plain Jane is okay.

**To our young men:** Stop pursuing the wrong kind of girls. When you fixate on physical appearance, you make it harder for our sisters to embrace godliness. Pay attention to the godly and modest.

**To all of us:** Take advantage of church's multi-generational community. Go to fewer weddings and more funerals—even strangers' funerals—to rebalance your perspective on what matters.

### Submission Is Unqualified

The second crucial point: biblical submission has no conditions attached.

> Like Sarah, who obeyed Abraham and called him her master. You are her daughters if you do what is right and do not give way to fear. (1 Peter 3:6)

**Sarah's Example Matters**

Sarah is no pushover. In Galatians, Paul uses her as a symbol of Christian freedom:

> The Jerusalem that is above is free, and she is our mother. (Galatians 4:26)

She was brave enough to laugh at God's angel. She had the strength to kick out Hagar when necessary. Yet this woman of courage and character called Abraham "master." She used her freedom to submit.

This is the paradox: submission is not coerced weakness—it's chosen strength. The great Sarah, mother of Israel, exemplified it. If submission was only expected in that patriarchal era, Peter wouldn't hold her up as an example for us.

**What Unqualified Means**

Submission has no built-in exceptions. I often hear women say, "I believe in submission... if." But biblical submission doesn't come with conditions:

- Not just for the big decisions
- Not just for spiritual matters
- Not just when you agree with his decision
- Not dependent on how well you get along, or whether you're in love, or whether he's a good husband

You submit because you are under Christ. This is the right thing to do for wives—full stop. The same Peter calling aliens to do the right thing calls you to submit.

This is why verse 6 adds: "Do not give way to fear."

What fear? The fear of losing control. The fear of being taken advantage of. The fear that submission means being treated harshly. These are real fears, but submission is still the calling.

## Addressing the Objections

### What About Domestic Abuse?

Domestic abuse is a terrible evil. There is no excuse for it. But let me clarify several things:

**First, abuse is mutual.** Yes, husbands can abuse through physical violence and harsh words. But wives can abuse through sharp, stinging words and the persistent dripping nag that erodes a man's confidence until he becomes a shell of himself—frightened of his own decisions. Both do real damage; we just see one more clearly because it leaves visible bruises.

**Second, abuse doesn't invalidate authority.** The problem is not that husbands have authority—it's the abuse of that authority. We don't solve police brutality by removing police; we call for police to be better at their jobs. Similarly, we don't solve spousal abuse by eliminating headship; we call for headship to be exercised rightly.

**Third, there is already protection in place.** In our government, domestic abuse carries legal consequences—arrest, jail, court intervention. If you're being abused, go to the police. Withdraw from fellowship temporarily. Seek counseling and help. This is your right and your protection.

**Fourth, husbands need authority to carry their responsibility.** This is crucial. God has given husbands the responsibility to present their wives "as a radiant church, without stain or wrinkle or any other blemish, but holy and blameless" (Ephesians 5:27). Fathers are responsible to raise children in the Lord. These are unqualified responsibilities—not "unless there's an interesting TV show on," not "unless your career takes priority."

Nothing is more frustrating than being held responsible for something while being stripped of the authority to act. If we remove husbands' authority but keep their responsibility, we've created an impossible situation. Therefore, the authority they need must be proportional to the responsibility they bear.

**Application:** For those dating, this is your training ground. Learn to fight well and resolve conflict healthily. These are the habits that will determine whether your marriage thrives.

### What About Mutual Submission?

Some point to passages like Galatians 3:28 and Ephesians 5:21:

> There is neither Jew nor Greek, slave nor free, male nor female, for you are all one in Christ Jesus. (Galatians 3:28)

> Submit to one another out of reverence for Christ. (Ephesians 5:21)

But context matters. Galatians is addressing salvation—that spiritually, we're all equally valuable in Christ. It's not saying that gender, ethnicity, or social status are eliminated in practice.

Ephesians' call to mutual submission doesn't mean symmetrical, equal submission. It means that within our various relationships, we submit to one another appropriately. The language of mutuality can be asymmetrical:

- "Bear one another's burdens" (Galatians 6:2) doesn't mean everyone exchanges burdens equally; it means the stronger help the weaker.
- "Men will slay one another" doesn't mean they kill each other simultaneously.

The pattern is not A→B and B→A. It's A→B→C, where each submits in their proper role. Peter goes on to discuss different relationships, and notably, he only mentions wives submitting to husbands—not the reverse.

**Submission in Reality Is Asymmetrical**

Asymmetrical submission is not controversial; it's how functional systems work everywhere:

- **In business:** Employees submit to managers; it doesn't alternate.
- **In the military:** There's a chain of command. When the commanding officer dies, the next ranking officer automatically takes over. This hierarchy saves lives.
- **In sports:** A team needs a captain. Someone holds authority; others follow. Democracy on the field is chaos.
- **In government:** The President and Vice President never travel together—because clarity of succession is essential.

The worst thing any team faces is confusion about authority lines. Peter isn't writing to idealistic couples in perfect marriages. He's writing to real people in difficult situations—where one spouse is a believer and the other isn't, where preferences clash, where someone has to make the call.

Practically speaking, a marriage of two cannot be a pure democracy. You can't have a committee meeting about every family decision. Someone has to decide. (And if you vote on dinner, the children win—candy for everyone!)

**This is the point:** Submission makes marriage work as a team. It's beautiful and practical, like dancing. One person leads; one follows. Both are essential. Both require skill and grace.

### What If My Husband Is Ungodly?

The passage specifically addresses wives of non-Christian husbands. Surely God doesn't expect submission to an ungodly husband?

The text actually calls wives to submit to ungodly husbands *as a means of winning them to Christ*. Like other institutions we respect—the office, the government—we submit to the position, not the person's merit. We submit to the boss because he's the boss, not because he's better than us. This protects us from making excuses for our own sin.

Here's an interesting thought: if leadership were based on merit, wives would probably lead. Women, on average, live longer than men (widows thrive; widowers often decline within months). Perhaps God's irony in this is profound—he placed the mentally stronger in the submissive role, knowing it would take a confident, secure woman to truly submit.

If your husband is not a Christian, God still calls you to submit. Godly wives have found creative ways: they skip church services to honor their husband's wishes, but they attend weekday Bible studies and mothers' groups. They read Scripture faithfully at home, teaching their children God's Word. Their ministry may look different, but it's not less.

God does not ask you to do ministry at the expense of your marriage. How much more, then, should wives submit when their husbands are Christian?

**The Real Cost:** A Christian wife who refuses to submit might lead Bible studies and women's groups, but she may end in divorce. A Christian wife who submits might do less public ministry—more laundry, more meals, more housework—but her marriage remains intact. That stability witnesses far more powerfully than any program.

### What If My Husband Doesn't Want to Lead?

This is a common issue. Since Genesis 3, many wives love to take charge while many husbands lazily abdicate responsibility. If this describes your marriage, you may need to repent of trying to run things, actively seek your husband's advice and input, and gently encourage him toward greater leadership. Most husbands would actually welcome this encouragement.

It's like gambling: the problem isn't winning, it's not coming back. The problem isn't that he makes mistakes—it's that he abandons responsibility when things get difficult.

## The Husband's Responsibility

At the heart of this passage is not primarily about submission—it's about fatherhood and male leadership:

> Husbands, in the same way be considerate as you live with your wives, and treat them with respect as the weaker partner and as heirs with you of the gracious gift of life, so that nothing will hinder your prayers. (1 Peter 3:7)

> Husbands, love your wives, just as Christ loved the church and gave himself up for her. (Ephesians 5:25)

**Why Authority Requires Consideration**

Notice Peter tells husbands to be "considerate" and treat their wives "with respect as the weaker partner." Women are weaker in specific, physical ways:

- Men are, on average, 15-20% larger
- Motherhood exacts a physical toll: pregnancy depletes calcium, breastfeeding weakens bones, hormones shift, backs ache permanently, skin stretches. Women's bodies are never quite the same afterward.

Because of this vulnerability, husbands must lead with consideration and respect. You're not ruling over weakness to exploit it—you're protecting it because you love her as your own body.

**What Love Requires**

- **Never abandon her:** Don't leave when boredom strikes, when ambition calls, when you feel inadequate. "She'd be better off without me" is a lie. Staying in the hard seasons is not cowardice—deserting when the battle is fiercest is.

- **Use your authority responsibly:** You have power. Use it well. Don't freeze in fear of making mistakes. Do your job. Fulfill your responsibility to care for, nourish, love, and provide for your family. The buck stops with you.

- **Be an all-rounder:** You're not just a paycheck. Invest physically (exercise with them), emotionally (know their hearts), and spiritually (lead them toward Christ). Make sure their inputs in life are high-quality. Television is not a substitute for your presence.

## Limits on All Freedom

The conclusion Peter builds toward is this: Nobody gets to do whatever they want.

- Home owners can't do whatever they want with their property
- Wives can't do whatever they want in marriage
- Children can't do whatever they want in families
- Fathers can't do whatever they want—they submit to God and to the state

We all live under authority. The question is whether we submit willingly to God's design, or whether we resist and suffer the consequences.

## Submission in Practice

Submission isn't a set of rigid rules. It's an attitude that affects everything—your thoughts, feelings, words, and actions, every moment of every day.

**Submission affects your thoughts:** Reject critical, bitter, resentful thoughts about your husband. Choose to think of him with respect and love. This doesn't mean ignoring his faults; it means responding with patience and forgiveness, trusting that God placed you together, respecting his role even when you're struggling to respect him, and extending grace.

**Submission affects small things:** Honor his preferences—keep the house tidy, fill the kettle, wear your hair the way he likes. These small acts communicate respect and care.

**Submission affects your words:** Don't nag. Don't complain about him to others. Be careful how you speak to him in front of people. Robust discussion and teasing are fine, but there's a difference between partnership and undermining.

**Submission affects how you respond:** Practice saying "yes" first, then "but have you considered...?" instead of the automatic "no" or sigh.

**Submission affects decisions:** Seek his guidance on your budget, your schedule, your plans. Don't take advantage of his easygoing nature to make unilateral decisions.

**Submission affects leadership:** Graciously receive his authority. Build him up rather than tear him down. Obey when it doesn't mean disobeying Christ. Support, encourage, and pray for him as he leads.

**Submission affects how you influence:** There's a place for wise counsel and honest discussion (Proverbs 31:26). But your primary influence—especially when words fail—is through prayer, godly living, reverence, and honoring his leadership. Not through sulking, nagging, manipulating, or complaining.

**Submission affects how you receive love:** Learn to graciously accept compliments, welcomes, encouragement, affection, and protection. Don't resist being looked after.

**Submission affects how you use time:** Give yourself first to helping your husband, caring for his needs, supporting his work. Christ is your first love and God's glory your first goal—but your husband comes next on the list.

## For the Unmarried

There is a place to consider merit before you get married: **choose wisely.**

**For young women:** Before saying yes, ask yourself: *Can I submit to his authority?* If he's selfish or a jerk, why bother? If he's not a Christian, can you trust him to lead your family toward Christ and present you as "a radiant church, without stain or wrinkle or any other blemish, but holy and blameless" (Ephesians 5:27)?

Even if he's a Christian, that doesn't automatically make him husband material. If he doesn't read his Bible, if he's indifferent to God's things, if he's spiritually immature, if he can't control his temper, if his faith seems doubtful—don't overlook these red flags. He may grow up someday, but until then, he's not a viable option.

**For young men:** Ask yourself: *Who can I present as a spotless bride to Christ?* Will she listen to you? Can you lead her? How can you lead her before Christ if she doesn't share your faith? Are you mature enough spiritually to be her spiritual leader?

**For unmarried women:** Talk to married women about their real experiences. Watch how they model a gentle and quiet spirit. Begin training now in submission—if you're in a group with a guy, see how you can encourage him to lead, what it means to follow, and test these dynamics in a dating relationship.

## The Gift of Education

A final word on whether women should be educated: yes, absolutely. Education is not a waste. You don't know if you'll marry. Education builds intelligence and critical thinking—qualities that make good mothers and citizens.

The government funds education through HECS-HELP precisely because it wants an educated population, not just an educated workforce. Women get to study without crippling debt. That's a gift.

Marriage and children do change priorities. Women's rights are largely single women's rights. Career may defer. But education itself remains invaluable.

There's divine irony here: God has wired women with greater mental resilience and longevity. Perhaps he did this knowing the role he was calling them to play.
