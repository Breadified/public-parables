# Mark 12:13-40

**Title:** Impossible Choices

**Date:** ARISE 27 March 2022

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Introduction

Throughout passion week, Jesus finds himself surrounded by those seeking to trap him in impossible dilemmas—situations where any answer appears to lead to defeat. Yet each time, Jesus transcends the false dichotomy with a third way that reframes the entire question. This is the pattern of his wisdom: he refuses to accept the limiting categories his opponents impose.

The Pharisees and Herodians employ an ancient logical device called the "horns of a dilemma"—like a bull with two horns charging at you, no matter which direction you dodge, you're gored. Whatever choice Jesus makes, he loses. But as always, he finds a way through that his opponents never anticipated.

---

## 1. God vs. Government (12:13-17)

### The Trap

The Pharisees (anti-government spiritual revivalists) and Herodians (pro-government monarchy supporters) approach Jesus with flattery. They acknowledge his integrity and fearlessness—his refusal to show favoritism—before asking their question: "Is it right to pay the imperial tax to Caesar or not?"

The poll tax was particularly despised. Romans levied it on individuals, making people walk among themselves as taxable units. A family with nine children faced nine times the burden. It symbolized subjugation to a foreign conqueror.

The dilemma appears inescapable:

- **If Jesus says yes:** He appears disloyal to God, who alone is King. Scripture declares that God is Israel's King and Redeemer (Isaiah 44:6), and that rulers govern by God's decree (Proverbs 8:15). Endorsing Caesar seems to betray monotheism.

- **If Jesus says no:** He becomes a revolutionary, guilty of sedition against Rome, marked for execution as a rebel.

### The Answer

Jesus asks to see a denarius—a Roman coin bearing Caesar's portrait and inscription. He then offers an elegant solution: "Give back to Caesar what is Caesar's, and to God what is God's."

Since the coin bears Caesar's image and inscription, it logically belongs to Caesar. The coin itself reflects Caesar's governing authority; therefore, subjects rightly submit to that authority and pay the tax. Taxation, in principle, is not theft. A government creates its own currency within its realm—just as a casino owns its chips and Disneyland owns its tokens.

But Jesus is not finished.

### The Greater Claim

The second part of his answer contains the revolutionary insight: "Give to God what is God's."

What bears God's image and inscription? **You do.**

> Genesis 1:26-27: "Then God said, 'Let us make mankind in our image, in our likeness... So God created mankind in his own image, in the image of God he created them; male and female he created them.'"

If coins belong to Caesar, then you—bearing God's image—belong entirely to God. You owe God every breath, every ounce of energy, your complete allegiance, loyalty, and faithfulness. This is not secondary devotion. This is total surrender.

> Revelation 4:11: "You are worthy, our Lord and God, to receive glory and honor and power, for you created all things, and by your will they were created and have their being."

> Mark 8:34: "Whoever wants to be my disciple must deny themselves and take up their cross and follow me."

### The Profound Shift

Before this moment, the choice seemed binary: either Caesar or God. Either loyalty to earthly rulers or loyalty to heaven. Either security under Rome or freedom for Israel.

Jesus dissolves this false choice. He affirms that earthly government is real and deserves respect—but it is not absolute. The king is limited. God's authority supersedes all earthly power.

This was radically countercultural. In Jesus' time, Caesar was worshipped as divine—not merely as a political leader, but as a god, a deliverer, a savior. Julius Caesar had been deified. Octavian (Augustus) was called "son of God."

Jesus quietly but firmly states that such imperial pretensions are false. Government exists within a hierarchy. There is a higher allegiance, a deeper loyalty that cannot be compromised.

This makes possible something previously unthinkable: Christians living faithfully in non-Christian states. The earthly king, whatever his claims, is not ultimate.

## 2. More Wives or No Lives? (12:18-27)

### The Trap

Now the Sadducees enter the fray. As temple elites and theological liberals, the Sadducees rejected belief in the resurrection. (Wealthy aristocrats who benefit from the status quo rarely hope for eschatological revolution.)

They present a scenario rooted in the ancient law of levirate marriage—when a man dies without children, his brother marries the widow to produce offspring and continue his name and inheritance.

> Deuteronomy 25:5-6: "If brothers are living together and one of them dies without a son, his widow must not marry outside the family. Her husband's brother shall take her and marry her and fulfill the duty of a brother-in-law to her."

The Sadducees construct an elaborate hypothetical: seven brothers in succession marry and die, each leaving no children. Finally, the woman dies. Their question: "At the resurrection, whose wife will she be, since the seven were married to her?"

Their logic contains two components:

**First: Children as Immortality**

If there is a resurrection and afterlife, why would Moses prescribe levirate marriage? The law makes sense only if immortality comes through offspring—if "raising children" (v. 19) substitutes for literal resurrection. Children provide a form of immortality: your genes continue, your name persists. Some legacies span generations and centuries. In this view, earthly continuation through descendants replaces heavenly resurrection.

**Second: The Practical Absurdity**

If heaven exists and the woman was married to all seven brothers, who is her husband in the resurrection? The mere question exposes what the Sadducees see as an incoherent doctrine. The scenario becomes reductio ad absurdum—a logical proof by showing the contradictory consequences.

Again, Jesus appears trapped between affirming either polygamy in heaven or denying the resurrection altogether.

### The Answer

Jesus responds with two corrections:

**They do not know the Scriptures:**

> Mark 12:24-25: "Are you not in error because you do not know the Scriptures or the power of God? When the dead rise, they will neither marry nor be given in marriage; they will be like the angels in heaven."

Marriage ends at death. This is not merely an assertion but a logical implication of the very law the Sadducees cite. If levirate marriage exists as a divine law, it presupposes that death terminates the marriage covenant. Otherwise, a widow remarrying would violate the seventh commandment—committing adultery against her deceased husband.

The law itself proves that death severs the marriage bond.

**They do not know the power of God:**

> Mark 12:26-27: "Now about the dead rising—have you not read in the Book of Moses, in the account of the burning bush, how God said to him, 'I am the God of Abraham, the God of Isaac, and the God of Jacob'? He is not the God of the dead, but of the living."

Jesus quotes Exodus 3:6, spoken at the burning bush. The deeper insight: God identifies himself as the God of Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob—using the present tense *is*. If these patriarchs were truly dead and gone, God would not define himself through covenant with them. The fact that God maintains his covenant with them demonstrates they remain alive in God's sight.

God's covenants are inviolable. His promises do not end at death. If Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob died millennia before Moses, yet God still calls himself *their* God, it necessarily follows that they continue to exist in God's presence. Death cannot nullify God's commitments.

### Application

The God of the living resurrects his people. Jesus himself, resurrected, lives to intercede for us (Hebrews 7:25). The resurrection is not speculation about an afterlife—it is the revelation of God's faithfulness that transcends death itself.

## 3. King or Kin (12:28-34)

### The Question

A teacher of the law, impressed by Jesus' wisdom, asks: "Of all the commandments, which is the most important?"

The Jewish tradition enumerated 613 commandments in the Law and Prophets. The question invites Jesus to rank them—to identify the supreme principle.

Again, the questioner forces a choice: one command above all others. King or kin. First things and second things. A hierarchy that divides.

### The Answer

Jesus refuses the false binary:

> Mark 12:29-31: "The most important one is this: 'Hear, O Israel: The Lord our God, the Lord is one. Love the Lord your God with all your heart and with all your soul and with all your mind and with all your strength.' The second is this: 'Love your neighbor as yourself.' There is no commandment greater than these."

Notice: Jesus says there is no commandment greater than *these*—plural. Not "the greatest" (singular), but two commandments standing together as the irreducible foundation.

What does it mean that "the Lord is one"? It means God possesses one mind, one consistent character throughout all of Scripture. He is not one person in Genesis, another in Jeremiah, and yet another in the New Testament. He is not an actor playing different roles. God is truthfully and consistently himself—the same God who spoke to Abraham, who gave the law through Moses, who called the prophets, who became incarnate in Jesus.

### The Unity Cannot Be Divided

Love for God and love for neighbor are not separate categories. They form a unified whole. You cannot authentically love God while despising your neighbor. You cannot love your fellow human—made in God's image—while remaining estranged from God.

Some emphasize the vertical relationship (love God) and treat the horizontal as secondary. Others prioritize the social (love each other) and hope God follows. But Scripture integrates both. They are one commandment, not two competing loyalties.

The teacher of the law affirms this: "To love him with all your heart, with all your understanding and with all your strength, and to love your neighbor as yourself is more important than all burnt offerings and sacrifices" (v. 32-33).

Both—not one—are greater than all religious ritual.

> Matthew 22:40: "All the Law and the Prophets hang on these two commandments."

The entire corpus of Scripture finds its coherence in these two loves. They cannot be separated.

## 4. Lord vs. Lord (12:35-40)

### The Question

Jesus now poses his own conundrum. While teaching in the temple, he asks: "Why do the teachers of the law say that the Messiah is the son of David?"

The Jewish expectation was for a Messiah descended from David—a political and military liberator who would restore Israel's greatness and expel Rome. David was revered as Israel's greatest king: a powerful leader, a warrior, a nation-builder. The longing for "another David" burned in Jewish hearts.

Yet Jesus quotes Psalm 110:

> "The Lord said to my Lord: 'Sit at my right hand until I put your enemies under your feet.'"

David himself, the psalmist, calls the Messiah "my Lord." David reverences him. How then can the Messiah be David's son? A son is subordinate to his father. Yet here, the father addresses the son as his superior.

### The Resolution

The paradox resolves when we understand Jesus' identity. The Messiah is indeed David's son—but also his Lord. His standing is greater than David's because he sits at God's right hand—the position of supreme power and authority.

This reframes messianic expectation entirely. The Jews hoped for political liberation. Jesus reveals a greater reality: the Messiah is the cosmic Lord, seated in heaven, wielding authority over all creation. If David himself submitted to this greater Lord, how much more should Israel?

> "If the Jews were eager to follow David, how much more should they follow the Messiah Jesus?"

Jesus is Lord. He precedes creation, sustains all things, redeems his people, and will judge all humanity at the end of time. His authority is absolute and eternal.

### Warning Against False Teachers

The passage concludes with Jesus' warning against the teachers of the law:

> Mark 12:38-40: "Watch out for the teachers of the law. They like to walk around in flowing robes and be greeted with respect in the marketplaces, and have the most important seats in the synagogues and the places of honor at banquets. They devour widows' houses and for a show make lengthy prayers. These men will be punished most severely."

The irony is sharp: the teachers who confused Scripture, who imposed false either/or choices, who used their status to exploit the vulnerable—*they* are the ones in error. Jesus consistently refused their traps and clarified God's truth. Yet they hardened themselves against his wisdom.

---

## Conclusion: The Pattern of Wisdom

Throughout Mark 12, opponents force Jesus into impossible choices:
- God or Caesar?
- Resurrection or children?
- Love of God or love of neighbor?
- David's son or David's Lord?

Each time, Jesus transcends the false dichotomy. He reveals a deeper logic where apparent opposites cohere. Government and God's kingdom, afterlife and earthly faithfulness, vertical and horizontal love, human descent and divine authority—all find reconciliation in God's unified, consistent character.

The wisdom of Jesus lies not in choosing one good over another, but in revealing how the goods belong together. This requires looking beyond surface contradictions to the underlying coherence of God's revealed truth.

In every sphere of life, we face false choices that demand we betray one good to honor another. Jesus calls us to a higher vision: one where submission to earthly authority need not compromise allegiance to God, where hope for heaven energizes earthly faithfulness, where love of God and love of neighbor form an inseparable whole, and where the descendant of David is worshipped as the Lord of all creation.
