## Introduction: Fear and Faith

We live with many fears—exams, careers, finances, disease—and we multiply them by fearing the same things for our children. Yet beneath all these specific anxieties lies a deeper fear: the fear of the unknown, of ambiguity. Today's passage speaks directly to this tension between fear and faith.

---

## 1. Heading to the Other Side (Mark 4:35–41)

The disciples have spent time with Jesus hearing about the kingdom of God. Now comes the transition from recruitment to mission: the teaching is over, and the time has come to see fruit and action.

> That day when evening came, he said to his disciples, "Let us go over to the other side." Leaving the crowd behind, they took him along, just as he was, in the boat. There were also other boats with him.

Jesus appears almost passive here—"they took him along" rather than Jesus directing them. Many of the disciples were experienced fishermen, so perhaps they felt confident in their maritime expertise. The Sea of Galilee is vast, roughly three or four times the size of Sydney Harbour, surrounded by mountains in a geographical depression. It's a rift valley where the land has cracked and shifted, creating what is actually the lowest freshwater lake in the world. The topography creates a natural phenomenon: winds funnel through the surrounding heights and transform into violent squalls.

> A furious squall came up, and the waves broke over the boat, so that it was nearly swamped.

A terrible storm erupts. Yet in the midst of this chaos:

> Jesus was in the stern, sleeping on a cushion. The disciples woke him and said to him, "Teacher, don't you care if we perish?" He got up, rebuked the wind and said to the waves, "Quiet! Be still!" Then the wind died down and it was completely calm.

### Old Testament Echoes: The Pattern of Deliverance

Rather than simply noting that "Jesus showed He is God," I find it more illuminating to look for connections in Scripture itself. The Old Testament contains a striking pattern.

In Psalm 107, we encounter merchants sailing on mighty waters who witness the Lord's wonders. Yet when they see the greatness of the storm, their courage melts away. They reel and stagger like drunkards, at their wits' end. Then comes the turning point:

> Then they cried out to the Lord in their trouble, and he brought them out of their distress. He stilled the storm to a whisper; the waves of the sea were hushed. They were glad when it grew calm, and he guided them to their desired haven.

Again in Psalm 89, we encounter language about the Messiah—the Davidic king who will rule forever. But this psalm contains crucial details about what kingship means:

> You rule over the surging sea; when its waves mount up, you still them. You crushed Rahab like one of the slain; with your strong arm you scattered your enemies.

Mountains to the north and south sing for joy—references to Mount Tabor and Mount Hermon, which bracket the Sea of Galilee itself. This is about the Messiah coming to rule.

And in Psalm 106, the same verb appears:

> He rebuked the Red Sea, and it dried up; he led them through the depths as through a desert.

The word "rebuke" (*epetimêsen* in Greek) is the exact language Mark uses when Jesus commands the wind. This is not simply a display of power—it echoes the exodus, the great act of deliverance when God conquered the sea on behalf of His people. Jesus is acting as the liberator, the Messiah who brings divine rule.

### The Disciples' Real Fear

The disciples were experienced fishermen. They weren't necessarily afraid of the sea itself. What truly frightened them was what lay ahead: the mission itself, the "other side." They had been recruited as "fishers of men," following a rabbi who performed incredible miracles. But fishermen understand nets, boats, and storms. What they don't understand is becoming messengers of the kingdom, stepping out to perform miracles, to challenge the powers of darkness.

Consider the contrast with Jonah, who fled the nations because he didn't want to preach repentance to them. Jesus, by contrast, is heading *toward* the nations. The disciples sensed this shift and balked.

> He said to his disciples, "Why are you so afraid? Do you still have no faith?"

Jesus isn't denying the reality of the storm. He's pointing to something deeper: their lack of trust in Him, their inability to rest in His presence even in chaos.

### A Practical Illustration

Think of volunteering for something—building a granny flat extension, for instance—and discovering it's far harder than you imagined. You might think, "Maybe I should quit." But you also know the challenge is part of the calculated risk you signed up for. Sometimes we discover unexpected difficulties, but that's part of the wonder of pressing forward. When panic comes, we can either retreat or press on.

This is the Christian life. We know our direction—we follow Christ—even when we don't know how the journey will unfold. The temptation is to panic when things become hard or ambiguous. But Jesus calls us to something deeper.

### Life as Negotiation, Not Retail

We often think life should work like a Western shopping center: browse the catalog, select what you want, pay a fixed price, and if it doesn't work, return it for a refund. But real life is more like negotiating at a garage sale or in a Third World market. It's ambiguous. Prices aren't fixed. You negotiate and do the best you can, and if something doesn't work, you can't take it back—you have to fix it and keep moving forward.

Similarly, we often treat the Christian experience like school: follow the syllabus, complete the homework, pass the exam, done. But discipleship is more ambiguous. Every situation is different, calling for different responses. And that's actually good, because it demands that we trust God rather than a formula.

> They were terrified and asked each other, "Who is this? Even the wind and the waves obey him!"

The question is not merely academic. If the wind and waves obey Him, will the disciples? Will we?

## 2. Fighting the Enemy (Mark 5:1–21)

The mission begins. They arrive at the region of the Gerasenes, somewhere in the Decapolis—a confederation of Greek city-states in Transjordan, built on the foundations of ancient Ammonite and Moabite cities. The precise location matters less than the significance: they've crossed into Gentile territory.

> When Jesus got out of the boat, a man with an impure spirit came from the tombs to meet him. This man lived in the tombs.

Tombs in this region were likely caves carved into cliffs. In Jewish law, the dead and their dwelling places were unclean. So too was the "unclean spirit" possessing this man. Everything about him is ritual contamination.

> No one could bind him anymore, not even with a chain. For he had often been chained hand and foot, but he tore the chains apart and broke the irons on his feet. No one was strong enough to subdue him. Night and day among the tombs and in the hills he would cry out and cut himself with stones.

Here is a man of great physical strength yet in tortured existence, self-harming, isolated. Yet even in this state, something remarkable happens:

> When he saw Jesus from a distance, he ran and fell on his knees in front of him. He shouted at the top of his voice, "What do you want with me, Jesus, Son of the Most High God? In God's name don't torture me!"

The unclean spirit recognizes Jesus. No conversation between Jesus and a mere mortal occurs first—instead, Jesus speaks directly to the spirit, commanding it to leave.

> Then Jesus asked him, "What is your name?" "My name is Legion," he replied, "for we are many."

"Legion" is a Roman military term—several thousand soldiers. This is no lone demon; many spirits possess this man, and they recognize Jesus' authority.

> And he begged Jesus again and again not to send them out of the area. A large herd of pigs was feeding on the nearby hillside. The demons begged Jesus, "Send us among the pigs; allow us to go into them."

There's something about evil spirits needing a habitation. The demons negotiate—not banishment from the region, but entry into the pigs. Jesus grants the request:

> He gave them permission, and the impure spirits came out and went into the pigs. The herd, about two thousand in number, rushed down the steep bank into the lake and were drowned.

I suspect the demons were outwitted. Instead of a thousand years wandering in the abyss, they got drowned in a lake. There's an echo here of what Jesus said about spiritual warfare:

> "No one can enter a strong man's house without first tying him up. Then he can plunder the strong man's house."

Jesus is the stronger man. He binds this Legion—these demons that no chains forged by human hands could subdue—and casts them into the sea to destruction.

### The First Messianic Battle

This is Jesus' first battle as Messiah. He enters Gentile territory, encounters a legion of demons, and defeats them decisively. He brings deliverance and restoration to a man who had known only torment and isolation. The man is now clothed and in his right mind—healed, whole, himself again.

The parallel to Psalm 107 continues:

> Then they cried out to the Lord in their trouble, and he brought them out of their distress. He stilled the storm to a whisper; the waves of the sea were hushed. They were glad when it grew calm, and he guided them to their desired haven.

### The Community's Response: Fear and Rejection

> Those tending the pigs ran off and reported this in the town and countryside, and the people went out to see what had happened. When they came to Jesus, they saw the man who had been possessed by the legion of demons, sitting there, dressed and in his right mind; and they were afraid.

This is profound irony. The community has lived with this man's torment for years, perhaps decades. Yet when Jesus heals him, they respond with fear. Why? Because change is unsettling. Even positive change disrupts the status quo, and ambiguity terrifies us. We become comfortable with our familiar demons.

> Those who had seen it told the people what had happened to the demon-possessed man—and told about the pigs as well. Then the people began to plead with Jesus to leave their region.

As Jesus drives out the demons from the area, the people of the Decapolis drive Jesus out. The irony is stinging. Yet this echoes a pattern from Scripture:

> I revealed myself to those who did not ask for me; I was found by those who did not seek me. To a nation that did not call on my name, I said, "Here am I, here am I." But you who sit among the graves and spend nights keeping secret vigil, who eat the flesh of pigs and whose pots hold broth of impure meat—you say, "Keep away; don't come near me, for I am too sacred for you!"

Here is a people surrounded by pagan practices (sitting in graveyards, eating unclean animals), practicing false worship, yet rejecting the true Messiah when He stands before them.

### The Freed Man's Witness

But one person was not afraid:

> As Jesus was getting into the boat, the man who had been demon-possessed begged to go with him. Jesus did not let him, but said, "Go home to your own people and tell them how much the Lord has done for you, and how he has had mercy on you." So the man went away and began to tell in the Decapolis how much Jesus had done for him. And all the people were amazed.

Here's something strange: In Galilee, when Jesus cast out demons, He told them to be quiet. Here, He tells the man—in Gentile territory—to proclaim publicly what Jesus has done. The mission to the Gentiles begins not with the disciples but with this healed man's testimony.

> When Jesus had again crossed over by boat to the other side of the lake, a large crowd gathered around him while he was by the lake.

The episode closes with Jesus returning, now with crowds gathering. His first mission has borne fruit, not through dramatic authority but through the simple testimony of someone whose life has been transformed.

## Application: Fear and Faith in Our Own Lives

**What do you fear?** As Christians, we know our direction even if we don't know how we'll get there. The challenge is learning to trust Christ amid ambiguity.

**Practical responses to fear:**
- Take smaller, decisive steps rather than demanding all the answers upfront
- Make decisions that can be mitigated or adjusted
- Get comfortable with making mistakes
- Put more weight on what you do know; less on what you don't
- Remember: you're not uniquely incapable. If others have navigated HSC exams or career changes, why assume you alone will fail?
- Understand that exposure and time make things better

Being a disciple of Christ is harder than we initially thought. But it's nothing we didn't anticipate when we signed up. Consider the challenges in church life: serving teams struggle when cooking for many without a proper kitchen. But we knew that—it's part of what we wanted.

Persecution will come. The deceitfulness of wealth and other desires will tempt us. We follow a man who died painfully, tortured, and the symbol of our faith is a cross—an instrument of execution. This suffering is part and parcel of our belief.

**So the real question is:** Will we, like the Gerasene demoniac, abandon our old lives and follow Jesus? Or will we, like the townspeople, choose the comfort of our familiar demons over the discomfort of transformation?

The choice determines everything.
