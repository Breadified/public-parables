# Galatians 6:1-18

**Title:** The Law and Spirit  
**Date:** August 2021  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Law of Christ vs. Legalism

We've experienced a change of administration—moving from the Mosaic Law to the law of Christ. This freedom, however, is not a license to sin. Rather, it calls us to a higher standard rooted in love and grace.

> Carry each other's burdens, and in this way you will fulfill the law of Christ. (Galatians 6:2)

The phrase "law of Christ" captures something essential: the entire law is fulfilled in one command—"Love your neighbor as yourself" (Galatians 5:14; James 2:8). To carry each other's burdens means to bear with one another, to forbear, to show patience and forgiveness.

As Martin Luther wrote, "Christians must have strong shoulders and mighty bones, that they may bear the weakness of their brethren." This reflects Christ's example: he bore our suffering and our sin (Isaiah 53:4-5). When we bear one another's burdens, we practice forgiveness—we carry the penalty of another's sin through patience, tolerance, and gentleness.

### The Problem with Legalism

Why did the Galatians turn to legalism? The "circumcision party" promised control through law. We see this pattern everywhere: when sin feels out of control, we add more rules. More laws. More enforcement. But here's the fundamental problem: **the heart is the issue, not the law.**

Consider the tax code. Every year brought new regulations, and yet the code kept growing. Why? Because at the heart, people don't want to pay taxes. More laws create more loopholes, more avoidance, more burden. The law becomes a source of guilt that doesn't help anyone—it punishes and shames rather than restores.

Jesus spoke directly to this:

> The teachers of the law and the Pharisees sit in Moses' seat... They tie up heavy, cumbersome loads and put them on other people's shoulders, but they themselves are not willing to lift a finger to move them. (Matthew 23:2-4)

### Restoration Over Retribution

The law, by nature, is retributive: you do something wrong, you get punished. But personal relationships between brothers and sisters cannot operate on retribution. We live thousands of interactions a day with countless opportunities to stumble. We cannot calculate each slight or word. As someone wisely said, "Offense is taken, not given."

If we are to live together as a community, we need patience. We need persuasion. We need to be gentle. We need to carry each other's burdens.

> Brothers and sisters, if someone is caught in a sin, you who live by the Spirit should restore that person gently. (Galatians 6:1)

The word "restore" (Greek: *katartizo*) means to repair, to prepare, to make complete and functioning, to bring to maturity. It's the language of transformation, not punishment. The law does not restore; it only judges. But restoration requires us to be led by the Spirit, to embody the fruit of the Spirit—love, patience, gentleness.

## Beware of Pride in Restoration

Yet there's a trap in restoration: pride. When we help others, we risk looking down on them, comparing ourselves, thinking we're better.

> If anyone thinks they are something when they are not, they deceive themselves... So, if you think you are standing firm, be careful that you don't fall! (Galatians 6:3; 1 Corinthians 10:12)

The warning is clear: **test your own work, not others.** Don't measure your moral achievements against those who sin; measure yourself against the law of Christ.

> Each one should test their own actions. Then they can take pride in themselves alone, without comparing themselves to someone else, for each one should carry their own load. (Galatians 6:4-5)

This isn't to abandon those in sin. Rather, it's a call to humility and boundaries. We must carry our own load and remember that everyone must carry theirs. To do otherwise is to infantilize them, to treat them like children who cannot think for themselves. That's an insult to their dignity and their responsibility before God.

### The Danger of Overreaching Help

We must be careful not to try to control others under the guise of helping. Sometimes people don't want our help, even if they're going the wrong way. To treat adults like children—to protect them from every scratch and fall—is to rob them of independence and resilience. They reap what they sow, and we must allow that harvest to occur.

The only control we truly have is over ourselves, and even that we struggle with. We cannot depend on systems or people to do "the right thing." As vulnerable individuals, we can only rely on ourselves and our closest companions—not on millions of people making identical choices.

Rather than trying to help those who don't want our help, we should pray for them. We should let go of the desire to control through law and live as free people coming from a gospel of freedom.

## Sowing and Reaping

> Do not be deceived: God cannot be mocked. A man reaps what he sows. Whoever sows to please their flesh, from the flesh will reap destruction; whoever sows to the Spirit, from the Spirit will reap eternal life. (Galatians 6:7-8)

This isn't arbitrary punishment—it's the nature of the spiritual universe. Our choices bear fruit. The question for each of us is: **What seeds are you sowing?** Seeds of good works? Seeds of the flesh? In heaven, God judges us as individuals, and this serves as a sobering warning about the trajectory of our lives.

## Perseverance in Doing Good

This work of restoration is genuinely difficult. It requires tolerance, persuasion, and humility. It's easier to make a law, issue a pronouncement, add another rule. But that doesn't work. We need to persevere.

> Let us not become weary in doing good, for at the proper time we will reap a harvest if we do not give up. Therefore, as we have opportunity, let us do good to all people, especially to those who belong to the family of believers. (Galatians 6:9-10)

Notice: we "do good" twice. We bear each other's burdens. We love. We forgive. And we reap a reward. Sometimes that fruit takes time to grow—your patience itself is sowing seeds. But James reminds us of the eternal significance of turning someone from sin: "Whoever turns a sinner from the error of their way will save them from death and cover over a multitude of sins" (James 5:19-20).

Start locally. We often want to solve Afghanistan, world hunger, Aboriginal reconciliation—but have we reconciled with one another? Have we learned to bear each other's burdens in our own homes, our own churches? Practice restoration here. Do good to all, especially those in the family of believers.

## Boasting in the Cross Alone

Paul closes with a warning about motive:

> Those who want to impress people by means of the flesh are trying to compel you to be circumcised. The only reason they do this is to avoid being persecuted for the cross of Christ. Not even those who are circumcised keep the law, yet they want you to be circumcised that they may boast about your circumcision in the flesh. (Galatians 6:12-13)

If you must boast, boast in Christ:

> May I never boast except in the cross of our Lord Jesus Christ, through which the world has been crucified to me, and I to the world. Neither circumcision nor uncircumcision means anything; what counts is the new creation. (Galatians 6:14-15)

We don't rely on man-made laws or external marks of righteousness. A good Christian becomes a good citizen, but a good citizen is not necessarily a good Christian. Our identity and our boast are in the cross—in the transformation that makes us new creations.

> The grace of our Lord Jesus Christ be with your spirit, brothers and sisters. Amen. (Galatians 6:18)
