# Psalm 1:1-6

**Title:** Rhythm for Your Day  
**Date:** January 4, 2020  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Blessing of God

When we wish someone "Merry Christmas" or "Happy New Year," we're doing more than exchanging pleasantries. We're invoking a blessing—literally wishing God's favor upon them. To bless someone is to show them favor. When we say "God bless you," we're praying that they might experience God's favor in that season.

Psalm 1 is fundamentally about blessing. But it begins not by telling us who is blessed, but by showing us who is not.

## Who Is Not Blessed (Verse 1)

> Blessed is the one who does not walk in step with the wicked or stand in the way that sinners take or sit in the company of mockers.

The psalmist describes the blessed person through contrasting negatives. Notice the bodily postures used: walking, standing, and sitting. These aren't accidental choices. In our productive waking hours, we can occupy only these three postures—walking, standing, or sitting. Together, they symbolically represent every moment of our active lives.

The rhythm is deliberate:
- Does not **walk** in the counsel of the **wicked**
- Does not **stand** in the way of **sinners**  
- Does not **sit** in the seat of **mockers**

Each action represents a different level of engagement: walking past, stopping to listen, and settling in to stay. The blessed person rejects this entire wicked lifestyle—not just occasionally, but completely and daily.

The metaphor is vivid: the ways of the wicked are like falling into a sewer—dirty, foul, utterly repulsive. Once you realize where you are, you scramble out immediately. You want nothing to do with it.

## What the Blessed Person Does (Verse 2)

> But whose delight is in the law of the Lord, and who meditates on his law day and night.

Here we find the crucial contrast. By contrast, the blessed person finds their delight not in the ways of wickedness, but in the law of the Lord. 

The word "law" here means more than just the legal commandments. It encompasses Torah—God's instruction, His complete will and way. To meditate on it isn't transcendental religious meditation, but rather to think deeply about it, to muse on it, to wrestle with it.

"Day and night" is a merism—a figure of speech meaning "all the time." This person orders their entire life around God's instruction. They make every activity an opportunity to walk with God, to stand firm in faith, to sit at His feet and listen. This is the rhythm of the blessed life: constant attention to God's ways.

## The Blessed Person Bears Fruit (Verse 3)

> That person is like a tree planted by streams of water, which yields its fruit in season and whose leaf does not wither—whatever they do prospers.

When you align yourself with God's law, you thrive. You're like a tree planted by a constant water source—never parched, always nourished, always productive. In season, it yields fruit. Its leaves never wither. Everything it does prospers.

This is not a promise of easy prosperity, but of genuine flourishing. Those who follow God's ways experience blessing, stability, and fruitfulness. The contrast is stark: alive versus dead, fruit versus barrenness, rooted versus weightless.

This is what it means to be blessed: to have a right relationship with God, a pure mind, and a stable foundation.

## The Wicked Have No Foundation (Verses 4-5)

> Not so the wicked! They are like chaff that the wind blows away. Therefore the wicked will not stand in the judgment, nor sinners in the assembly of the righteous.

The image comes from threshing: grain and chaff are tossed into the air together. The grain, being heavy, falls back to earth. The chaff, being worthless and weightless, blows away on the wind. It's dead matter, good for nothing but to be scattered and dispersed.

The wicked cannot stand before judgment. They have no stability, no foundation, nothing to anchor them. They will not stand in the assembly of the righteous because they've rejected the very thing that makes one righteous—delight in God's law.

## God's Favor Rests on the Righteous (Verse 6)

> For the Lord watches over the way of the righteous, but the way of the wicked leads to destruction.

Now the psalmist introduces the word "righteous" for the first time. The righteous one is the blessed man described throughout this psalm—the one who totally rejects the wicked way and completely follows God's ways. This is the one whom God favors.

But as we read this psalm and consider the ideal it presents, we must ask: Who truly lives this way perfectly? When we examine ourselves, we fall short. Our New Year resolutions are forgotten within weeks. We fail to maintain this perfect alignment with God's will.

## The Ultimate Fulfillment: Jesus

Here's the wonder: Psalm 1 is not primarily a personal self-help psalm. The Psalms were written as Israel's prayer book, and their deepest subject is God's king—the one who represents God perfectly. In fact, Psalms 1 and 2 work together as an introduction to the entire Psalter, and they speak of the ideal representative of God's kingdom.

This ideal is realized in one person: Jesus Christ.

Jesus is the righteous one (1 John 2:1). He alone truly delights in the law of the Lord and in His Father's will. At twelve years old, we find Him in the temple courts, sitting among the teachers, listening and asking questions—utterly absorbed in God's Word.

He is righteous in His death: "Christ also suffered once for sins, the righteous for the unrighteous, to bring you to God" (1 Peter 3:18).

He refused to walk, stand, or sit in the ways of the wicked. Instead:
- He walked obediently to Golgotha
- He stood steadfastly against the accusations of Herod and Pilate
- He sat on the cross, submitting to God's will without mocking or cursing Him

And because of His perfect righteousness, He received God's favor in fullness: "You are my Son, whom I love; with you I am well pleased" (Mark 1:11). Now He sits in the heavenly realms, in the assembly of the righteous, at God's right hand.

Psalm 1, read through the lens of the New Testament, is ultimately about Jesus—the blessed one, the righteous one, the one whom God favors.

## Our Blessing

Our blessing comes not through our own righteousness, but through receiving Jesus. When we trust in Him, we become righteous in Him. We are covered by His perfection, sustained by His favor.

This is the rhythm for our day: to follow Jesus daily, to delight in His will, to align our walking, standing, and sitting with His ways. In Him, we too become like trees planted by living water, bearing fruit, prospering in all we do.

A blessed new year is a year of following Jesus—every single day.
