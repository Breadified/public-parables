# Luke 16:14-31

**Title:** Impossible Reversals
**Date:** 13 April 2008
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Context: A Modern Parable

Before we dive into Jesus's teaching, consider the story of Eddy Groves. Not yet forty, he became Australia's second-richest young businessman with a personal wealth of $272 million. Through ABC Learning Centres, he built an empire—starting from a single centre in Brisbane to 2,300 facilities across three continents, serving 40,000 children and capturing 20% of Australia's childcare market. By 2006, he was at the top of the BRW Young Rich List with $260 million. He lived the dream: a $4 million river-view mansion on the Gold Coast, a Ferrari, designer suits, first-class travel, even ownership of the Brisbane Bullets basketball team.

But then came the share market correction. Within two years, a fall in profit forecasts triggered a massive sell-off. ABC shares plummeted from $8.80 to $1.15—a $760 million wipeout. Worse, since Groves had borrowed heavily against his shareholdings, his creditors reclaimed the vast majority. From 12.17 million shares, he was left with just 3,000.

It was an impossible reversal of fortune. And today, we turn to another impossible reversal—one far more profound.

## 1. A Contrast of Fortunes (Luke 16:19-21)

> There was a rich man who was dressed in purple and fine linen and lived in luxury every day. At his gate was laid a beggar named Lazarus, covered with sores and longing to eat what fell from the rich man's table. Even the dogs came and licked his sores.

The contrast here is absolute. The rich man lived in what was likely a grand mansion—his "gate" refers to a towering pylon, the kind you might find at temple entrances or royal palaces, impressive structures designed to display wealth and power. This man dressed himself in purple and fine linen, fabrics reserved for kings and the extremely wealthy. Purple, in particular, was extraordinarily expensive to produce in the ancient world.

Meanwhile, at that same gate sat Lazarus, a beggar immobile with disease, his body covered in open sores. He was so desperate for nourishment that he craved even the scraps that fell from the rich man's table. The degradation is complete: stray dogs—unclean animals in Jewish understanding—licked his wounds. For a Jewish audience, this would have been unspeakable defilement, placing him beyond the pale of human dignity.

One man had everything: wealth, status, prestige, luxury. The other had nothing but suffering and longing.

## 2. The Impossible Reversal (Luke 16:22-25)

> The time came when the beggar died and the angels carried him to Abraham's side. The rich man also died and was buried. In hell, where he was in torment, he looked up and saw Abraham far away, with Lazarus by his side. So he called to him, "Father Abraham, have pity on me and send Lazarus to dip the tip of his finger in water and cool my tongue, because I am in agony in this fire." But Abraham replied, "Son, remember that in your lifetime you received your good things, while Lazarus received bad things, but now he is comforted here and you are in agony."

Both men died. And in death, everything reversed—utterly, completely, impossibly.

Lazarus, the beggar covered in sores and abandoned by everyone, was carried by angels to Abraham's side. The phrase "Abraham's side" comes from an old English word meaning "bosom"—the place of intimate closeness, like a child cradled against a mother's chest. This outcast, ritually unclean in every way by Pharisaic standards, now enjoys the ultimate intimacy with Abraham himself.

The rich man, meanwhile, found himself in hell, tormented in fire, begging for a drop of water. The profound irony cuts deep: he who ignored Lazarus's hunger now begs for even the smallest relief from his thirst. And Lazarus, the man he never even named in life, is now known to him by name.

## The Challenge to Jewish Sensibilities

This reversal was not accidental or incidental. Jesus told this parable directly to the Pharisees, who had gathered along with tax collectors and sinners. They had murmured against Jesus: "This man welcomes sinners and eats with them."

For the Pharisees, this was scandalous. They were the righteous ones, the keepers of God's law, the people set apart by ritual cleanliness and moral superiority. Tax collectors and sinners—prostitutes, the diseased, the outcast—were synonymous with gentiles in their minds: unclean, outside God's covenant, irredeemable.

It's like imagining the top student, the dux, the one who passed every exam and excelled by every measure, ending up sweeping floors in a shopping centre, while the student who failed and dropped out became a sophisticated investment banker and prime minister. Impossible! We would all bet on the top. No one would bet on the bottom.

Yet this is exactly what Jesus describes. The "top dogs"—the ones confident in their righteousness—end up separated from God. The one who was with the dogs, the outcast, ends up in Abraham's bosom.

For the Pharisees, this was more than a challenge. It was a warning. They believed themselves to be the children of Abraham, inheritors of God's kingdom, privileged with the law and the prophets while all other nations remained in darkness. Yet here Jesus suggests that these very leaders might miss out entirely.

## 3. Too Late: The Impossible Gap (Luke 16:24-26)

> So he called to him, "Father Abraham, have pity on me and send Lazarus to dip the tip of his finger in water and cool my tongue, because I am in agony in this fire." And besides all this, between us and you a great chasm has been fixed, so that those who want to go from here to you cannot, nor can anyone cross over from there to us.

The most devastating word in this passage may be "fixed." The chasm is fixed. It is permanent. Irreversible.

In this life, believers and unbelievers can coexist. We eat together, work together, build society together. We can even be family—a Christian parent with an unbelieving child, a believing spouse with an unbelieving partner. We share this world.

But the afterlife is different. The division there is permanent. Once death comes, once we cross from this world to the next, our fate is sealed. Hebrews 9:27 tells us: "People are destined to die once, and after that to face judgment." There is no second chance. There is no way across the chasm.

Notice the rich man's change of heart. He now begs for mercy. He wants to send Lazarus to warn his brothers. He has, in a sense, repented. But it comes too late. The agony is not merely the fire—it is the agony of regret. Of knowing what might have been. Of understanding too late what truly mattered.

Repentance, Jesus teaches us through this parable, only counts in this life. Once we die, our opportunity is gone.

### A Sobering Reality

Consider the weight of this: the only certainty we have is that one day, our hearts will stop beating. We don't know when. It could be today. It could be decades from now. But every heartbeat brings us closer to that moment.

We live in a time of comfort and security. We plan for the future—retirement, education, property. But how many of us have "done our business with God"? How many have truly settled our account with Him?

The rich man, we can assume, wasn't thinking about eternity the day before he died. He was carrying on his merry way, living his comfortable life, right up until the end came. And then it was too late.

Jesus asks us: "What good is a man to gain the whole world but to lose his soul?" Yes, following Jesus may cost us. It may mean different priorities, less worldly comfort, smaller security in this life. But consider what we gain: all that the beggar lacked on earth was provided for him in eternity. All the advantages of this life that the rich man enjoyed meant nothing in the next.

## 4. Too Late: The Impossible Ignorance (Luke 16:27-31)

> He answered, "Then I beg you, father, send Lazarus to my father's house, for I have five brothers. Let him warn them, so that they will not also come to this place of torment." Abraham replied, "They have Moses and the Prophets; let them listen to them." "No, father Abraham," he said, "but if someone from the dead goes to them, they will repent." He said to him, "If they do not listen to Moses and the Prophets, they will not be convinced even if someone rises from the dead."

The rich man's request is noble. He cannot save himself, but perhaps he can save his brothers. He begs for a sign—a true, unmistakable sign. Let Lazarus rise from the dead and go warn them. Surely that would convince them!

But Abraham's answer is clear: they already have everything they need. They have Moses and the Prophets—the entire Old Testament, God's complete message to His people. They have the law and the words of God spoken through generations of prophets. What more could they possibly need?

"If someone from the dead rises," Abraham says, "they still won't listen."

This is a profound truth often overlooked. It's easy to think we would believe if we just had a sign—a miracle, a clear vision, an undeniable proof. But Jesus is saying something far more challenging: the problem is not a lack of evidence. The problem is a lack of willingness to listen.

People don't believe because they don't want to. Even if someone rose from the dead tomorrow, most would find a way to explain it away. They would call it a trick, a hallucination, a hoax. Our resistance to God's message isn't rooted in ignorance. It's rooted in stubborn pride.

The Bible is good enough. God's Word, faithfully handed down through the ages, pointing ultimately to Jesus Christ—this is the revelation we have been given. There are no secret formulas, no special keys to unlock the mysteries of eternity. There is only the steady call of God through His Word, asking us to listen and believe.

## The Ultimate Impossible Reversal

But there is one more impossible reversal to contemplate—the greatest one of all.

Jesus himself was truly a "top dog" in God's eyes. He was perfect, sinless, truly righteous—not hypocritically like the Pharisees, but genuinely. He possessed the highest status in all creation.

And He willingly gave it up.

Unlike the rich man, whose riches were stripped away against his will, Jesus chose to lay down His status. He became "dogs' meat" for us. He took the place of shame and humiliation. He swapped His righteousness for our unrighteousness, His status as the beloved Son for our status as rebels.

And in exchange, He gives us His status. We who are sinners, we who deserve judgment like the rich man, we are now invited to God's bosom. We stand clothed in His righteousness. We eat at His table, not as beggars eating scraps, but as beloved children.

This is what 1 Peter tells us: "Christ died for the unrighteous to bring you to God."

This sounds impossible. The righteous dying for the unrighteous? The King laying down His throne for rebels? It defies logic.

But Jesus said: "What is impossible with men is possible with God."

## Will You Trust Him?

The chasm between this life and the next is fixed. But today, you stand at the crossroads. Today, there is still time. Today, you can cross from death to life.

Some of you may think, "I'm fine. I'm a good person. I come to church. My parents were Christians." But remember the Pharisees. They too were confident. They too believed they had a guaranteed place in God's kingdom. And they were wrong.

Others may say, "I need more proof. I need a sign." But you have Moses and the Prophets. You have the Gospel. You have the message of Christ crucified and risen. That is enough.

Here is the truth: your foundation matters. Where is your confidence truly placed?

If you trust in Jesus—in the fact that He died to pay for your sins, that He rose again, that He is the only way to God—then hold fast to that confidence. That is the only unshakeable foundation. Boast in Christ.

But if your confidence is based on anything else—your family background, your church attendance, your spiritual experiences, your own goodness—then I must tell you plainly: you should worry. You are not on the solid ground you think you are.

God has no grandchildren. You cannot inherit the kingdom from your parents. Only a personal, living relationship with Jesus Christ—through the Bible, by faith—will save you.

If you haven't made that decision, I'm asking you now: will you trust Jesus? Will you cross from death to life today?

## Prayer of Commitment
