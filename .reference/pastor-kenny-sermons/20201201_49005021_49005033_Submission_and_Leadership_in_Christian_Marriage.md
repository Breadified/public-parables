# Ephesians 5:21-33

**Title:** Submission and Leadership in Christian Marriage  
**Date:** 10 December 2020  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Understanding Submission

The word "submit" is deeply unpopular in modern society. Yet when we hear a wife promise to submit to her husband in Christian marriage vows, we're witnessing something counter-cultural and beautiful—a woman choosing, of her own free will, to place herself under another's authority.

It's natural to have reservations. Submission conjures troubling images: the slave trade, exploitation, domestic abuse. These are rightful concerns. But the submission God calls for in marriage is fundamentally different. It is chosen, mutual in spirit, and always held within the context of sacrificial love.

> Wives, submit yourselves to your own husbands as you do to the Lord. (Ephesians 5:22)

This is no light commitment. The comparison here is profound: the relationship between a wife and husband mirrors the relationship between the church and Christ.

> For the husband is the head of the wife as Christ is the head of the church, his body, of which he is the Savior. Now as the church submits to Christ, so also wives should submit to their husbands in everything. (Ephesians 5:23-24)

## The Structure: Asymmetrical but Complementary

The submission called for is one-directional, but not because women are inferior. Rather, it makes sense only because the responsibility is asymmetrical.

The wife's submission serves a purpose: it creates space for her husband to lead. And what does that leadership look like? It is defined entirely by Christ's example.

> Husbands, love your wives, just as Christ loved the church and gave himself up for her. (Ephesians 5:25)

## What Leadership in Love Demands

Leadership in this sense means dying to self. It means sacrifice at every level:

**Physically:** The husband stands as protector. When danger threatens the home, he is the shield between his family and harm.

**Materially:** He provides food and shelter. If circumstances force him into humble work beneath his training or ambitions, he sets aside his pride to ensure his family's security.

**Emotionally:** He initiates healing and reconciliation. When conflict comes, he moves first toward repair. When his career ambitions threaten to overshadow family needs, he sacrifices those dreams to care for those he loves.

**Spiritually:** He leads his family toward holiness.

> To make her holy, cleansing her by the washing with water through the word, and to present her to himself as a radiant church, without stain or wrinkle or any other blemish, but holy and blameless. (Ephesians 5:26-27)

The husband cannot accomplish all of this alone, nor is he solely responsible for his wife's spiritual growth. But the principle stands: the buck stops with him. The responsibility rests on his shoulders.

> In this same way, husbands ought to love their wives as their own bodies. He who loves his wife loves himself. After all, no one ever hated their own body, but they feed and care for their body, just as Christ does the church. (Ephesians 5:28-29)

## The Beautiful Dynamic

Here is the paradox: Brandon cannot lead without Sharon's permission. He cannot fulfill this calling without her support. He cannot die to himself for her benefit unless she allows him to do so through submission.

By submitting, Sharon makes room for Brandon to lead. Her submission is part of the larger picture of his sacrifice for her. This is not coercion; it is chosen. Sharon had every option to refuse, but she chose to accept.

And so there is equality and symmetry in the vows, though they are spoken differently:

- The man initiates and promises first: "I will die for you."
- The woman responds: "And I will follow you."

He texts, she replies. He invites her to lunch, she accepts. He proposes, she accepts. He pledges to sacrifice his life for her, and she pledges to submit to his leadership.

And so begins a beautiful dance.

## Beyond Marriage: Christ and the Church

This mystery of submission and leadership extends far beyond marriage itself.

> This is a profound mystery—but I am talking about Christ and the church. (Ephesians 5:32)

God invites each of us into this same dynamic with Christ. We are called to submit to Him, not because He is tyrannical, but because He has already proved His love through absolute sacrifice. He died for us. He rose from the dead. He continues to protect and lead His church.

Our submission to Christ is not servitude to an authoritarian ruler. It is a willing choice to follow One who has already shown He will give everything for us. It is the path to healthier, more fulfilled lives—both in this world and the next.

If this teaching resonates with your heart and you haven't yet made that choice to follow Christ, ask about it. Let us help you understand what it means to submit to the One who loves you most.
