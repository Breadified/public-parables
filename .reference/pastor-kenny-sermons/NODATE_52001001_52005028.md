# 1 Thessalonians 1:1–5:28

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Opening Greeting and Thanksgiving

Paul writes to the church of the Thessalonians with a heart overflowing with gratitude. He greets them with grace and peace, then immediately launches into thanksgiving—not as mere formality, but as a genuine overflow of pastoral joy.

> "We always thank God for all of you, remembering you constantly in our prayers."

What moves Paul to such constant intercession? Three things: their **work of faith**, their **labor of love**, and their **endurance of hope** in Christ. These aren't abstract virtues but lived realities—faith that produces action, love that costs something, hope that perseveres through difficulty.

## The Power of Gospel Witness

Paul reminds them how the gospel came to them—not through words alone, but **in power, through the Holy Spirit, with deep conviction**. There's a difference between hearing the message and encountering the reality of God's kingdom at work.

The Thessalonians became imitators of Paul and the Lord himself, receiving the message with joy despite severe persecution. This is remarkable. Most people abandon faith when persecution comes. But these believers welcomed suffering as the cost of following Jesus. Their faith became so visible that news of it spread throughout Macedonia and Achaia without Paul needing to say a word.

### Turning from Idols to the Living God

What specifically happened? The Thessalonians **turned from idols to serve the living and true God, and to wait for His Son from heaven**. This captures the whole arc of conversion: repentance (turning from false gods), devotion (serving the true God), and hope (anticipating Christ's return). And they understood why—Jesus rescues us from the coming wrath.

## Paul's Integrity and Pastoral Care

Paul now addresses a deeper concern: Was his ministry authentic? Did he come with mixed motives?

He recalls his suffering in Philippi—being beaten and imprisoned—yet he came to Thessalonica emboldened to speak the gospel despite opposition. This wasn't the behavior of someone seeking comfort or wealth. His exhortation came from no error, impurity, or deception. Instead, having been approved by God to be entrusted with the gospel, he speaks to please God, not people.

> "For we never used flattering speech, as you know, or had greedy motives—God is our witness—and we didn't seek glory from people."

Paul's pastoral approach was marked by gentleness. **Like a nursing mother tending her children**, he cared for them sacrificially—sharing not only the gospel but his own life because they had become dear to him. Like a father with his own children, he encouraged, comforted, and implored each one to **walk worthy of God, who calls you into His own kingdom and glory**.

This is what authentic ministry looks like: vulnerability, sacrifice, and deep personal investment.

## Reception of God's Word

The Thessalonians didn't treat Paul's teaching as human opinion. They received it as what it truly is—**the message of God, which works effectively in you believers**. This is the power of Scripture: when we receive it as God's word rather than human philosophy, it transforms us from the inside out.

## Suffering as Fellowship with Christ's Church

The Thessalonians were experiencing persecution from their own countrymen, just as believers in Judea suffered from the Jews. Paul doesn't shy away from naming the opposition: those who killed Jesus and the prophets, who persecuted the apostles, who displease God and are hostile to everyone. They resist the gospel spreading to the Gentiles so that they might be saved. But their resistance only adds to their judgment.

Yet for the Thessalonians, this suffering is a badge of belonging to Christ's true church—they share in the same trials as believers everywhere.

## Paul's Longing and Timothy's Mission

Paul's pastoral heart is evident in his deep desire to return to Thessalonica. He was forced to leave them—in body, not in heart—and wanted nothing more than to see them face to face. Even Satan seemed to hinder his plans.

> "For who is our hope, or joy, or crown of boasting in the presence of our Lord Jesus at His coming? Is it not you? For you are our glory and joy!"

His affection is genuine. Unable to bear the separation any longer, he sent Timothy to strengthen and encourage them in their faith, especially concerning the persecutions they were facing. Paul had already warned them that persecution would come—and it had. Now he feared the tempter might have shaken their conviction.

The good news Timothy brought back was a tremendous relief: the Thessalonians remained strong in faith and love, they remembered Paul warmly, and they longed to see him as much as he longed to see them. This news encouraged Paul immensely in his own trials. He reminds them: **"For now we live, if you stand firm in the Lord."** Their faithfulness sustains his own joy and ministry.

## The Practical Call to Sanctification

Paul shifts to concrete exhortation. They've been taught to walk and please God—now do so even more.

### Sexual Purity as Sacred Stewardship

> "For this is God's will, your sanctification: that you abstain from sexual immorality, so that each of you knows how to possess his own vessel in sanctification and honor."

God's will for every believer is holiness, beginning with sexual integrity. To "possess your own vessel" means understanding your body as a sacred trust from God, not as an instrument for lustful desire like those who don't know Him. This isn't prudishness—it's reverence for the body as God's dwelling place.

Moreover, brothers must not defraud one another in these matters. Sexual immorality damages the community of believers. The Lord avenges all such offenses because He cares deeply about the dignity and trust within His church.

### Brotherly Love and Quiet Diligence

The Thessalonians are already taught by God to love one another and are doing so throughout Macedonia. Yet Paul encourages them to excel even more. Specifically, he calls them to **lead a quiet life, mind their own business, and work with their own hands**.

This is wisdom for every age: quiet faithfulness, minding your own affairs, earning your own living. In doing so, believers "walk properly in the presence of outsiders and not be dependent on anyone." This builds credibility for the gospel—people see Christians as responsible, self-supporting members of society, not as burdens.

## Hope in the Face of Death

Paul addresses a pastoral concern: some believers were grieving the death of other Christians with the hopelessness of unbelievers.

> "We do not want you to be uninformed, brothers, concerning those who are asleep, so that you will not grieve like the rest, who have no hope."

The foundation of Christian hope is the resurrection of Jesus. Because Jesus died and rose again, **in the same way God will bring with Him those who have fallen asleep through Jesus**. Death for believers is not the end—it's sleep, temporary and reversible.

Paul then describes the Lord's coming with remarkable clarity:

> "For the Lord Himself will descend from heaven with a shout, with the archangel's voice, and with the trumpet of God, and the dead in Christ will rise first. Then we who are still alive will be caught up together with them in the clouds to meet the Lord in the air; and so we will always be with the Lord."

Those who have died in Christ will be raised first. Then living believers will be caught up together with them to meet the Lord. The result: **we will always be with the Lord**. This is our comfort. Death separates us temporarily from believers' bodies, but nothing can separate us from the Lord's presence.

> "Therefore encourage one another with these words."

## Watchfulness for the Day of the Lord

Paul reminds them that the Day of the Lord comes like a thief in the night—suddenly, without warning. The world will be unprepared, saying "Peace and security" when sudden destruction comes upon them like labor pains on a pregnant woman. They will not escape.

But the Thessalonians are different. They are **sons of light and sons of the day**, not children of darkness or night. Therefore, they must **stay awake and be sober**—not drunk with the world's intoxicants, but clear-eyed and vigilant.

> "So then, we must not sleep, like the rest, but we must stay awake and be sober. For those who sleep, sleep at night, and those who get drunk are drunk at night. But since we are of the day, we must be sober and put the armor of faith and love on our chests, and put on a helmet of the hope of salvation."

The armor of readiness consists of **faith and love on our chests** (protecting the heart) and **the helmet of the hope of salvation** (protecting the mind). This is hope as a protective force—because we trust in salvation through Christ, we cannot be shaken by the world's chaos.

God has not appointed us to wrath but to salvation through Christ. Whether we're awake or asleep at His coming, we will live together with Him.

> "Therefore encourage one another and build each other up as you are already doing."

## Final Exhortations and Blessings

Paul offers a series of short, powerful instructions for community life:

**Honor Your Leaders:** Recognize and highly esteem those who labor among you, lead you in the Lord, and admonish you—because of their work, honor them in love.

**Pursue Peace and Healing:** Be at peace among yourselves. Warn the lazy, comfort the discouraged, help the weak, be patient with everyone.

**Choose Good Over Evil:** See that no one repays evil for evil. Instead, **always pursue what is good for one another and for all**.

**Three Marks of the Christian Life:**
- **Rejoice always**—not because circumstances are perfect, but because Christ is Lord
- **Pray constantly**—maintain unbroken communion with God
- **Give thanks in everything**—gratitude as a spiritual discipline, recognizing God's sovereignty

Paul then addresses the work of the Holy Spirit:

> "Don't stifle the Spirit. Don't despise prophecies, but test all things. Hold on to what is good. Stay away from every form of evil."

Don't suppress the Spirit's work through fear or excess caution. Don't dismiss prophecy. But exercise discernment—test everything against Scripture and reason, hold fast to what is good, and reject what is evil.

## Final Benediction

Paul's closing blessing captures the fullness of redemption:

> "Now may the God of peace Himself sanctify you completely. And may your spirit, soul, and body be kept sound and blameless for the coming of our Lord Jesus Christ. He who calls you is faithful, who also will do it."

Sanctification is God's work, not ours. He is the God of peace—the one who brings harmony and wholeness. His faithfulness guarantees that what He calls us to, He will accomplish in us.

Paul closes with requests for prayer and a final exhortation that the letter be read publicly to all the brothers—ensuring the whole church hears his words.

> "May the grace of our Lord Jesus Christ be with you!"

Grace, not judgment. Grace, the unearned favor of God through Christ. This is the final word—not a closing formula, but a blessing that encompasses all of Paul's teaching and the Thessalonians' journey ahead.
