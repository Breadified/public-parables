# 1 Timothy 2:1-7

**Title:** Pleasing God
**Date:** October 12, 2003
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Introduction: What Does It Take to Please God?

We often wonder what it takes to please God. Some think it's keeping the Ten Commandments. Others imagine it's through meditation and chanting, doing good works, or simply being a decent person who doesn't harm anyone. But these assumptions miss something crucial.

On a fateful night exactly one year ago, two men—Amrozi and his brother Ali Imron—believed they were pleasing God. They purchased a Mitsubishi van and filled it with a tonne of chemicals: potassium chlorate, sulfuric acid, and aluminum powder. After removing the rear seats and replacing the wheels, they drove from East Java to Bali by road and ferry. The van was parked on Legian Street, a location they had carefully scouted.

At 11:07 PM, a suicide bomber detonated explosives in his vest. Thirty seconds later, the van exploded—the blast so massive it registered on Indonesian earthquake measuring instruments. The fireball stretched eighty metres into the sky. Two hundred and two people perished that night. Eighty-eight were Australians. Three hundred and fifty more were horrendously burned or injured. Amrozi sat at his home cheering when he heard the news on television.

For Amrozi and Imron, this was their understanding of pleasing God. But they were profoundly, eternally wrong.

So what truly pleases God? Scripture gives us four clear answers.

---

## 1. Prayer Pleases the Personal God

> First of all, then, I urge that supplications, prayers, intercessions, and thanksgivings be made for all people, for kings and all who are in high positions, that we may lead a peaceful and quiet life, godly and dignified in every way. This is good, and it is pleasing in the sight of God our Saviour. (1 Timothy 2:1-3)

Paul urges us to pray for all people—especially those in government—that we might live peaceful, quiet lives in dignity. This reveals something vital: God is personal. He cares. He wants to hear from us.

Think about how crucial governments are for peace. We take them for granted, but governments undergird our prosperity, well-being, and dignity. Anarchy is chaos. Even bad governments maintain better order than no government at all. When Saddam Hussein fled Baghdad, the city descended into looting, violence, and mayhem. Governments enable people to live meaningful lives and make rational choices in freedom.

God cares about this. He wants us to ask Him to provide wise leadership so we can flourish in peace and dignity. Why? Because He loves us and desires relationship with us.

But here's the problem: we don't believe God cares.

A South Park episode illustrates this tragic misconception. After a character dies, Chef tells Stan that God is a distant, vengeful deity who takes away what we love just to drink "the sweet milk of our tears"—like a bored boy torturing ants with a magnifying glass. In this view, God is as impersonal as the stars.

But that is profoundly wrong. Here in this passage stands a personal God who cares deeply—even about how John Howard does his job as Prime Minister. He is the kind of Father who says "Tell me everything about your day" not "Leave me alone." God delights to hear our prayers because He cares for our peace and flourishing.

---

## 2. People Being Saved Pleases God

> God desires all people to be saved and to come to the knowledge of the truth. (1 Timothy 2:4)

There is something God loves and desires far more than our prayers: He wants people to be saved.

What does "saved" mean? Saved from what?

> The saying is trustworthy and deserving of full acceptance, that Christ Jesus came into the world to save sinners. (1 Timothy 1:15)

The word "sinner" conjures images of breaking moral rules—no smoking, no drinking, no swearing. But sin is deeper than mere rule-breaking. A sinner is someone who rebels against God's authority.

It's not like running a red light. It's like committing treason against the King—refusing His right to rule. Amrozi didn't simply break Indonesian law; he waged war against Indonesian sovereignty, wanting to establish his own rule instead. Similarly, we all rebel against God by ignoring His authority and creating our own rules. We do this publicly through obvious sins—theft, dishonesty, cruelty—and privately through selfishness and pride. The root is always the same: we reject God's right to tell us what to do.

And there are consequences. Just as there is a death penalty for terrorism against a nation, there is a death penalty for rebellion against God. Death is the just reward for sin.

Consider the words of those who lost loved ones in the Bali bombing:

David Spike Stewart, whose 29-year-old son Anthony was killed, said his only birthday wish was that Amrozi receive the death sentence. Maria celebrated her first wedding anniversary with the death of her twin sister, her bridesmaid, and her cousin—all killed in the blast. She said, "I can't wait for their execution. I'd like some of my blood on the bullets used, on behalf of my sisters." Danny Hanley, whose two daughters were killed, said, "Amrozi has got his just reward: death."

This anger is righteous. When someone has murdered the people you love most, you cry out for justice.

Friends, this is how God sees us. God is likewise angry at our rebellion. We deserve death. His own Son—His one and only Son—was killed by sinners. You and me. Judgment falls not only on Amrozi but on everyone who rebels against God.

God's judgment is thorough. When He cleans up, He doesn't hide the dust under the bed. He cleans down to the atom. Whether we've sinned much or little, whether our sins are great clumps or tiny specks, it all goes. God promises to do away with this world and bring about a new one where sin and rebellion will not exist. And we, being part of the old world, will be cleaned away just as surely as the great sinners.

From God's perspective, we are no different from Amrozi.

Yet God desires all people to be saved from this judgment. How? Through "the knowledge of the truth." What is this truth?

> For this I was appointed a preacher and an apostle (I am telling the truth, I am not lying), a teacher of the Gentiles in faith and truth. (1 Timothy 2:7)

Paul isn't speaking opinion or philosophy. He's proclaiming reality—whether we like it or not. This is the truth that saves us.

## 3. Jesus Pleases God

> For there is one God, and there is one mediator between God and men, the man Christ Jesus, who gave himself as a ransom for all, which is the testimony given at the proper time. (1 Timothy 2:5-6)

**Jesus is the truth that saves us. Jesus pleases God.**

A mediator is someone who acts between opposing parties to effect agreement and reconciliation. Think of the United Nations Secretary-General trying to broker peace between hostile nations. He arranges talks, explores solutions, seeks compromise. But even the greatest mediator would never die himself to make peace.

Jesus does exactly that. He acts as the mediator between us and God, bringing reconciliation. And the price He pays is His own life.

God's justice demands a ransom—a payment to redeem us from the captivity of death. On the cross, Jesus is tortured and crucified. His infinite worth, poured out on two beams of wood, infinitely pays for our infinite sins. He gives Himself as a ransom for all.

Jesus is the unique mediator. There is only one God and only one mediator between God and men. No Buddha, no Muhammad, no priest in a confessional, no religious leader in robes can broker this peace. There are not many ways to God. Jesus is the one way.

And here's what's remarkable: God is pleased. Not pleased by death as though He were some sadist delighting in suffering. Rather, God is pleased because justice is finally done. He is pleased as a parent would be when a criminal who harmed their child is brought to justice. In fact, God is so pleased with Jesus that He raises Him from death, gives Him the highest honor, the greatest titles and names, and seats Him in the place of supreme authority in heaven.

Because Jesus pleases God perfectly, we can now please God through Him. Our prayers alone are not enough to please God. Our goodness cannot earn His favor. Only Jesus can truly please Him. Only His intercession is effective. This is why we pray in Jesus' name—not as magic words, but because we have no standing before God except through Jesus, our great Mediator.

---

## 4. Pleasing God Through Jesus

We now understand what pleases God:
- Prayer, because He cares for us
- People being saved, because He desires our redemption
- Jesus most of all, because He alone satisfies God's justice and enables our salvation

**But the greatest truth is this: we can now please God through Jesus.**

We cannot please God on our own. But in Christ, we can. We can now participate in God's desire for all people to be saved. This is good and pleasing to God.

Consider Amrozi's brother Imron. He was equally guilty. He had studied in Islamic schools in Pakistan, trained in militia camps in Afghanistan, provided the bomb-making knowledge, and drove the van loaded with explosives and suicide bombers to the nightclub. He was the better-trained terrorist. But he did not receive the death sentence.

Why? Because he repented. He apologized. He said he was sorry. Rather than appealing his sentence or arguing his case, he requested clemency. He recognized his guilt, acknowledged he could not save himself, and begged for forgiveness.

We are guilty too. We have rebelled against God. We cannot save ourselves. But we can beg for forgiveness. We can turn from our excuses and rationalizations—"Why doesn't God show Himself?" "It's His fault for not making me a Christian"—and instead simply admit our guilt and ask for mercy. When we do, God forgives us completely.

Put Jesus in charge as the Ruler of your life. Stop rebelling. Accept His lordship.

## For Those Considering Christ

If you're a Christian here tonight, keep following Jesus faithfully.

If this is your first time hearing this, take time to understand. Let the truth sink in. But understand that it demands action. It might mean no more excuses to avoid church or Bible study groups. It might mean finally taking up that Christian friend's invitation to study Scripture together—not because your friend is some spiritual guru, but simply because they want to know you and share the truth that pleases God to save you.

If you understand the good news of Jesus and are ready to make it right with God, tonight is the night. Don't try to justify your past. Don't wait for perfect circumstances. We live in peace—not in war, chaos, or anarchy. We have freedom to choose. Tonight, as you enjoy the peace that God has given, turn from sin and rely on Jesus, the one Mediator, to save you.

Pray this prayer with genuine intention:

> Dear God, my Saviour and Lord,
> 
> Thank you for our government, for the peace and dignified lives they provide. We ask that they continue to govern wisely.
> 
> I am sorry that I have not pleased you in the past, and I see why you are angry with me.
> 
> I thank you for sending your Son, Jesus, to save me by pleasing you on my behalf.
> 
> Please help me now to please you by following Jesus as my Lord.
> 
> In Jesus' name we pray, Amen.

If you've prayed this prayer sincerely, be confident: God has heard you and has forgiven you. You may not feel it yet, but angels are rejoicing and God is very pleased.

Tell someone what God has done in your life. Your friends want to know. We want to help you grow as a Christian.

## Epilogue: The Pause in Time

Imagine we're back in that Bali pub on that fateful night. Amrozi and Imron have planted the bomb. The van is outside, ready to detonate. The suicide bomber has clicked the trigger. But just before the explosion—just before 202 lives are taken—imagine time pauses. Imagine the victims are given one more chance to get things right: to catch a different flight, go to a different club, do things differently.

Friends, we live in that pause. With the death of Jesus, the bomb of God's judgment has already detonated, but in His incredible mercy, He has given us a chance to get right with God before it all ends. Jesus is the one Mediator through whom we achieve peace with God.

The 88 Australians in Bali would have taken that chance. Would you? Don't delay. Please God.
