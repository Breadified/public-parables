# Joshua 7:1-8:35

**Title:** The Unfaithful Church
**Date:** July 9, 2016
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction: The Problem of Tolerance

Many megachurches today prioritize cultural relevance and downplay theology, particularly concepts that sound negative in our culture. They become "liberal" churches in the sense of emphasizing freedom that dilutes biblical truth rather than genuine liberty in Christ. This isn't a new problem—it has plagued God's people since Israel's founding. Even during their greatest moments of victory, hidden sin threatened everything.

## Part 1: The Perception of Winning But Losing (7:1-26)

### Fresh Victory, Hidden Compromise

Israel had just conquered Jericho in an unprecedented victory—zero casualties, zero resistance, complete success. Yet beneath this triumph lay something rotten within the camp.

> But the *Israelites* were unfaithful in regard to the devoted things; *Achan* son of Karmi, the son of Zimri, the son of Zerah, of the tribe of Judah, took some of them. So the Lord's anger burned against *Israel*. (7:1)

God had commanded that certain things be "devoted"—destroyed to purge pagan influences and prevent the contamination of idolatry. Achan, a family leader, took some of these devoted things for himself and hid them. His action violated the covenant and brought God's anger not just on him, but on all Israel.

### The Consequences Ripple Through the Community

When Joshua sent men to scout Ai, the spies reported it was small—no need to send the whole army. But the 3,000 soldiers who went were routed. Thirty-six Israelite soldiers died unnecessarily. The people's hearts melted in fear.

> 2 Now Joshua sent men from Jericho to Ai... 3 When they returned to Joshua, they said, "Not all the army will have to go up against Ai. Send two or three thousand men to take it..." 4 So about three thousand went up; but they were routed by the men of Ai, 5 who killed about thirty-six of them. (7:2-5)

This was not a matter of poor tactics or overconfidence. The spies had been reliable. The real problem, revealed in verse 1, was sin—iniquity, not strategy.

### Three Reasons Sin Demands Judgment

**a) Sin Affects Everyone, So Judgment on One Is Just**

Achan's actions harmed the entire community. His personal greed resulted in the deaths of 36 soldiers and the devastation of their families. Because sin's consequences spread broadly, judgment on the one responsible affects many.

As Scripture records elsewhere:

> The son of Karmi: Achar, who brought **trouble on Israel** by violating the ban on taking devoted things. (1 Chronicles 2:7)

> When Achan son of Zerah was unfaithful in regard to the devoted things, did not wrath come on the **whole community of Israel?** He was not the only one who died for his sin. (Joshua 22:20)

Consider an illustration: When a father bankrupts himself, his entire family suffers poverty. We don't think it's fair for the wife to keep a mansion and children to drive Ferraris while the man is ruined. The whole community suffers because of one man's foolishness—why shouldn't his community suffer the consequences?

**b) Sin Affects Everyone, So Judgment Extends to All**

God explained to Joshua that Israel's defeat wasn't about Israel's name or honor, but about sin.

> *Israel* has sinned; they have violated my covenant, which I commanded them to keep. They have taken some of the devoted things; they have stolen, they have lied, they have put them with their own possessions. That is why the Israelites cannot stand against their enemies; they turn their backs and run because they have been made liable to destruction. I will not be with you anymore unless you destroy whatever among you is devoted to destruction. (7:11-12)

Achan sinned; therefore all sinned. Therefore all suffered. This principle extended to his family—perhaps they were accomplices, or at minimum beneficiaries of the theft.

**c) Achan Showed No Genuine Repentance**

When Joshua performed the lottery to identify the sinner, the entire nation watched as God narrowed it down: tribe by tribe, clan by clan, family by family, man by man. The process was deliberate and lengthy, yet Achan still harbored hope he wouldn't be caught.

When finally confronted, Achan confessed:

> It is true! I have sinned against the Lord, the God of Israel. This is what I have done: When I saw in the plunder a beautiful robe from Babylonia, two hundred shekels of silver and a bar of gold weighing fifty shekels, I coveted them and took them. (7:20-21)

But where was the sorrow? His confession was merely a statement of facts. His sin was covetousness—looking out for himself while the war effort and his brothers' lives hung in the balance. His family knew what he was doing and shared in the theft. By the time he confessed, true repentance was already too late.

### The Pattern: Devoted Things and Hidden Sin

The "devoted things" represent more than objects—they represent the principle that sin cannot coexist with God's kingdom. Sin must be destroyed in the Christian's life and in the Christian community.

> Then all Israel stoned him, and after they had stoned the rest, they burned them. Over Achan they heaped up a large pile of rocks, which remains to this day. Then the Lord turned from his fierce anger. Therefore that place has been called the Valley of Achor ever since. (7:25-26)

### Application: The Church's Tolerance of Sin

Churches often tolerate sin far too much. The apostle Paul made this clear:

> Your boasting is not good. Don't you know that a little yeast leavens the whole batch of dough? Get rid of the old yeast, so that you may be a new unleavened batch. (1 Corinthians 5:6-7)

> You must not associate with anyone who claims to be a brother or sister but is sexually immoral or greedy, an idolater or slanderer, a drunkard or swindler. Do not even eat with such people... "Expel the wicked person from among you." (1 Corinthians 5:11, 13)

This doesn't mean expelling people struggling with sin who genuinely desire change. But someone living in unrepentant sin will dilute the church's witness in three ways: we may be tempted toward political correctness to accommodate them, they set a bad example, or they lead others astray.

Some sins are inherently anti-social, anti-community, and anti-fellowship. They affect everyone. A church may look impressive on the outside, but if it tolerates unrepentant sin, it's losing its way. Better to be a small church standing firm on truth than a megachurch compromising the gospel.

## Part 2: The Perception of Losing But Winning (8:1-35)

### A New Strategy for a Purified People

After Achan's judgment, God gave Joshua a new strategy. But the transformation wasn't primarily tactical—it was spiritual. A purified army, now free from internal corruption, would approach the battle differently.

> Then the Lord said to Joshua, "Do not be afraid; do not be discouraged. **Take the whole army** with you, and go up and attack Ai. For I have delivered into your hands the king of Ai, his people, his city and his land. 2 You shall do to Ai and its king as you did to Jericho and its king, except that you may carry off their plunder and livestock for yourselves. **Set an ambush behind the city.**" (8:1-2)

The changes were significant:
- **More soldiers:** 30,000 men instead of 3,000 (no complacency)
- **Better strategy:** An ambush coordinated with a feigned retreat
- **Leadership presence:** Joshua himself stayed with the troops
- **Most importantly:** A community cleansed of internal sin

### How to Look Like You're Losing While Actually Winning

Joshua's plan involved deception. His main force would advance and then retreat, appearing to flee in panic just as they had before. The King of Ai, seeing apparent victory, would abandon the city to chase the Israelites. This left Ai undefended.

> I and all those with me will advance on the city, and when the men come out against us, as they did before, we will flee from them. They will pursue us until we have lured them away from the city... Then you are to rise up from ambush and take the city. The Lord your God will give it into your hand. (8:5-7)

The ambush forces would then enter the city and set it on fire. When the King of Ai saw the smoke and realized the trap, his army would be caught between Joshua's main force and the forces emerging from the burning city. Complete victory.

This is like a chess game where you appear to be losing—one move away from defeat with an opponent's piece blocking your path. So you offer a tempting sacrifice, and when your opponent takes it, you've already set up checkmate. What looked like a losing position was actually a winning one all along.

### Application: The Paradox of Following Christ

This story illuminates Jesus's teaching about losing and winning:

> Whoever wants to be my disciple must deny themselves and take up their cross and follow me. For whoever wants to save their life will lose it, but whoever loses their life for me will find it. What good will it be for someone to gain the whole world, yet forfeit their soul? (Matthew 16:24-26)

Following Christ looks like losing. You lose:
- Friends who oppose your faith
- Friends who won't join your pursuits
- Money and material wealth
- The fashion, popularity, and attractiveness the world offers

Others see you as a fool clinging to a fairy tale. Yet Jesus promises that whoever loses their life for His sake will find it. The world's temptations are an illusion—gaining the whole world while losing your soul is the real catastrophe.

### One for Many: The Pattern of Substitution

Just as one man, Achan, brought trouble on Israel, one man can bring rescue. This pattern reaches its fulfillment in Christ:

- Achan's sin affected the whole community; therefore his family suffered justly
- Jesus's death affects the whole community; therefore we are justified
- One man's sin brought judgment on many; one man's sacrifice brings salvation to many

> Without the shedding of blood, there is no forgiveness. (Hebrews 9:22)

The cost of sin is violent—it demands a life. Christ shed His blood on behalf of all to bring rescue from sin's judgment.

## Part 3: The Pure Church (8:30-35)

After victory, Joshua built an altar on Mount Ebal according to Moses's law. He wrote the law on stones and gathered all Israel—men, women, children, foreigners, native-born—to hear it read. They stood on both sides of the valley as the blessings and curses were proclaimed.

> There, in the presence of the Israelites, Joshua wrote on stones a copy of the law of Moses. All the Israelites, with their elders, officials and judges, were standing on both sides of the ark of the covenant of the Lord... Both the foreigners living among them and the native-born were there... Joshua read all the words of the law—the blessings and the curses. (8:32-34)

This was a covenant renewal. The people, now purified from Achan's sin, reaffirmed their commitment to God's law. A small, holy community rooted in Scripture is infinitely more valuable than a massive congregation that has compromised the gospel.

### The Church's Choice

We face the same choice today. We can pursue:
1. **Numbers without faithfulness:** The biggest church with the best vibe, but accommodating to the world and losing the gospel
2. **Faithfulness in smallness:** A smaller congregation committed to biblical truth and living out repentance from sin

Better to be small and holy than large and immoral.

The church must address sin clearly—lying, covetousness, sexual immorality, false teaching, idolatry. Some may object: "I can't invite my friends here." But their salvation is at stake. Someone must tell them the truth about sin and Christ's redemption. Where else will they hear that there's hope—that Christ died to free us from the ugliness and power of sin?

We cannot hide from sin or censor biblical teaching because the world opposes it. That's cowardice. We must preach the whole counsel of God, trusting that a purified community standing on Scripture is ultimately a winning position, even when the world calls us losers.
