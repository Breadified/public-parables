# Mark 9:30-41

**Title:** Serving Leads to Greatness
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction: The Paradox of Greatness

A few weeks ago, Elon Musk—the world's richest man—arrived in Canada as a seventeen-year-old international student with humble beginnings. As an immigrant from South Africa, he took on whatever work he could find: tending vegetables and shoveling grain bins at a cousin's farm in tiny Waldeck, cutting logs with a chainsaw in British Columbia, and eventually landing the most brutal job of all. At the unemployment office, he asked for the position with the best wage and was directed to clean the boiler room of a lumber mill for eighteen dollars an hour.

The work was almost impossibly difficult. Wearing a hazmat suit, workers had to shimmy through a tunnel barely wide enough to fit through, then shovel steaming hot sand and grime out through the same opening. If you stayed in there longer than thirty minutes, the heat would become lethal. Of the thirty people who started that week, only five remained by day three. By week's end, only Musk and two others were left doing the work.

Janitors. Society's losers. At the bottom of the ladder.

Thousands of years ago, God became a janitor too.

## Part 1: Jesus Became Last

### The Cost of Humility

Jesus was teaching his disciples about his coming passion:

> "The Son of Man is going to be delivered into the hands of men. They will kill him, and after three days he will rise." (Mark 9:31)

The phrase "Son of Man" is self-referential, but it emphasizes Jesus' humanity. He is both divine—seated at the right hand of God, spiritual, eternal—yet he comes down to become an ordinary mortal man. He is "delivered," which means betrayed. He suffers insult, backstabbing, lies. God who becomes man is placed into the **hands of men**.

Those hands are not the caring hands of a parent. When many hands come at you, the image becomes one of overwhelming powerlessness—manhandled by a mob, bullied and roughed up. Jesus would be treated as a loser, pushed around with no dignity. And then the final indignity: they would kill him. After the immortal was made mortal, the mortal became fatal.

Jesus is disgraced, betrayed, and ultimately executed. By worldly standards, he is a complete loser—last.

### The Disciples Reject the Message

Yet this is precisely what Jesus was teaching his disciples. But they refused to understand:

> "But they did not understand what he meant and were afraid to ask him about it." (Mark 9:32)

Their confusion and fear had a deeper cause. Look at what happened:

> "They came to Capernaum. When he was in the house, he asked them, 'What were you arguing about on the road?'" (Mark 9:33)

> "But they kept quiet because on the way they had argued about who was the greatest." (Mark 9:34)

The disciples didn't want to be last. They didn't want to be losers. They were preoccupied with being first, with greatness, with power, with having no one's hands on them—the privilege of the elite, far removed from the common people.

Jesus was describing servanthood, and they wanted dominion. The disciples chose not to understand because they disagreed with Jesus' entire vision of what greatness means.

### What Greatness Really Means

When we argue to be first, we argue to win. We argue to prove the other side wrong. We argue to embarrass our opponent. And when we argue like this, it deteriorates rapidly—we shout, we rage, we become angry and malicious. It's no longer about the argument itself; it becomes personal. The other person isn't wrong; they *are* bad.

How do you distinguish a good argument from a bad one? After a good argument, you shake hands, fellowship, and both walk away enlightened with no hard feelings. After a bad argument, you walk away angry and bitter, vowing never to speak to that person again.

Rather than fighting for greatness and dominance, Jesus offers a radically different vision:

> "Sitting down, Jesus called the Twelve and said, 'Anyone who wants to be first must be the very last, and the servant of all.'" (Mark 9:35)

To be first, you must be last. To be great, you must be a servant. What does this mean in concrete terms?

A servant prioritizes others' needs before their own. They are the last to eat, the last to sleep. But more than that, a servant "of all" cannot discriminate. Everyone has authority over them. A slave at a party doesn't get to choose which guests to serve; the owner's neighbor is above them, the neighbor's wife is above them, the owner's disabled brother is above them, even the owner's dog is above them. Everyone is above you. You serve whoever asks, whenever they ask.

### The Living Example: The Child

As Jesus describes this radical servanthood, he does something unexpected:

> "He took a little child whom he placed among them." (Mark 9:36)

In our modern context, children are privileged and protected. They receive food, shelter, education, music lessons, sports coaching—everything provided to help them "shine" and "become who they were meant to be."

But in ancient times, children were equivalent to slaves. Every household member had to be productive, and children were the least skilled, least useful members who performed the most menial work. Think of Cosette in Les Misérables, collecting water from the river and wood for the fire. Children did the lowest work for minimal wages.

Notice Jesus emphasizes the word "little"—insignificant, unimportant, unnoticed. If they disappeared, no one would miss them. This is the model Jesus holds up: "Be like this child. Be last."

## Part 2: The Last Become First

### Death and Resurrection: The Reversal

But here's the stunning reversal that the disciples missed. Look back at verse 31:

> "They will kill him, and after three days he will rise." (Mark 9:31)

The disciples heard only the first part—death, betrayal, execution. They missed the second part entirely: **he will rise**.

Jesus does not stay dead. On that third day, he defeats death itself and rises to victory over his enemies. He becomes great. Through his sacrificial servanthood, he elevates servanthood itself to greatness.

Jesus illustrates this elevation when he takes up the little child:

> "Taking the child in his arms, he said to them, 'Whoever welcomes one of these little children in my name welcomes me; and whoever welcomes me does not welcome me but the one who sent me.'" (Mark 9:36-37)

Welcome the child—the lowest, the loser, the servant—and you welcome Jesus himself. You welcome God the Father. By welcoming those who serve, you are welcoming the divine. Jesus is saying that he considers the servant to be on par with the divine level.

Why? Because Jesus himself served. The child serves. In function, they are equal. When you serve others, you participate in the divine activity of God himself.

### The Promise of Reward

But there is more than just the honor of participating in God's activity:

> "Truly I tell you, anyone who gives you a cup of water in my name because you belong to the Messiah will certainly not lose their reward." (Mark 9:41)

A cup of water—the smallest act of service. Yet in serving, you serve Jesus in secret. There *is* a reward. Persevere. Do not be discouraged. We will not lose our reward. It comes in his name, which means we are fighting for his reputation, and he will certainly protect his reputation.

Serving family, serving church, serving community may often feel thankless and fruitless in the moment. But the reward is coming—tenfold, a hundredfold. Before we grow proud, remember: this reward comes **through Jesus**, particularly through Jesus' name.

> "Truly I tell you, anyone who gives you a cup of water in my name because you belong to the Messiah will certainly not lose their reward." (Mark 9:41)

"In my name" means we receive his imputed righteousness and his imputed greatness. We get his greatness. Those who "belong to the Messiah" are held in his arms, safe and secure. And those who belong to Jesus serve in his name.

### Serving in His Name

Look at how Jesus addresses sectarian division:

> "Teacher," said John, "we saw someone driving out demons in your name and we told him to stop, because he was not one of us." (Mark 9:38)

> "Do not stop him," Jesus said. "For no one who does a miracle in my name can in the next moment say anything bad about me, for whoever is not against us is for us." (Mark 9:39-40)

"Not one of us" meant not from their particular group or denomination. But Jesus reframes the question entirely. Our deepest unity is not found in our church's name or denomination—it's found in Christ's name. We might be united in our local church, but that's not our most important unity. Our supreme unity is in Christ himself.

This transforms how we ought to argue. We don't need to fight over which church has better singing, better lunches, or better theology in secondary matters. The question becomes: Is this a fight for our "first" place? Or is it a fight for his name?

Christian denominations have historically fought each other, but the call today is different: those "not against us" are "for us." We serve together where the need is greatest. If we have gifts to contribute, we contribute them. Our loyalty transcends institutional boundaries.

## Application: The Servant's Path

You might think you are small and insignificant, with nothing to contribute. But even the smallest acts matter. There is no hierarchy of service—helping with cables, offering drinks at lunch, collecting rubbish—it's all equally important as teaching or leading Bible study. What can you do? Do it.

We move through life like Elon Musk did—starting as a janitor, then moving to SpaceX and Tesla, climbing a career ladder. We call it a career path. And we can fall into this worldly thinking in ministry too. We start helping with lunch, move on to Sunday School, then the youth group, then leading young adults, then preaching. We think: I'm doing well, I'm moving up. More responsibility. But we never actually move from helping with lunch.

Jesus doesn't call us to a career path in ministry. He calls us to servanthood without graduation. We serve like a child—always. The temptation is to think some ministries are beneath us after we've "paid our dues." But when the need arises, we serve. Not because it suits us, but because the priority is them, not us. That's what servanthood means.

Imagine a servant saying to their master: "I've already swept the floor. I've done my time. Let someone else do it." That's not servanthood.

Look at Glenn Stevens, the Governor of the Reserve Bank. On weekends, he teaches Sunday School at his local church. He didn't graduate from service work. He serves as a calling.

I belong to a professional clergy that, unfortunately, can set the wrong example. But ministry is not a career. It's a calling rooted in servanthood.

## Conclusion: From Last to First

Being last sounds terrible. But it's not. Because the last become first.

Elon Musk rose from $27 billion in 2020 to $300 billion today. Yet he still janitors. He sleeps on the floor of Tesla's vehicle-assembly plant in Fremont, California, during production crises. He doesn't even have time to shower. "I don't believe people should be experiencing hardship while the CEO is off on vacation," he said. He wanted to suffer more than any employee.

"I move myself to wherever the biggest problem is in Tesla," Musk explained. "I really believe that one should lead from the front lines, and that's why I'm here."

That's the attitude of the servant-leader. You move yourself to wherever the greatest need is at any moment. You prioritize the problem over your comfort.

**That attitude of servitude is what propelled him to the top.**

And that same attitude—rooted not in ambition but in love and obedience to Jesus—is what will make us great in heaven.
