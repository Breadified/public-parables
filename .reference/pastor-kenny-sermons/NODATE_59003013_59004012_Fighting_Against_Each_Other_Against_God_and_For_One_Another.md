# James 3:13 – 4:12

**Title:** Fighting: Against Each Other, Against God, and For One Another  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Problem: Our World is Full of Fighting

From international conflicts to personal disputes, fighting permeates every level of human society. We fight over material possessions, relationships, status, and countless other things. Yet beneath all this conflict lies a deeper issue that James identifies for us.

> What causes fights and quarrels among you? Don't they come from your desires that battle within you? You want something but don't get it. (James 4:1-2)

The root cause of our fighting is simple: **our desires**. When we want something, we pursue it. But when we can't obtain it through legitimate means, we resort to other methods—sometimes at the expense of others.

## 1. Fighting Against Each Other

Envy lies at the heart of interpersonal conflict. We see something someone else has—their clothes, their phone, their appearance, their relationships—and we want it too. This creates a competitive mentality where we begin to view others as obstacles to our own satisfaction rather than as brothers and sisters.

The escalation is predictable. We try to acquire what we desire through our own efforts. When that fails, we may ask God for it. But when God doesn't answer according to our timeline or preferences, we take matters into our own hands. We fight, we scheme, we manipulate—even to the point of harming others.

Consider the examples James implicitly references: people fighting in parking lots, pushing and shoving for sales, quarreling over food, or spreading gossip and slander about those who have what we lack. The motivation is always the same: **infinite wants meeting finite resources**. This is the economic reality of our fallen world, and it drives much of human conflict.

> You kill and covet, but you cannot have what you want. You quarrel and fight. (James 4:2)

Notice the progression: desire → frustration → violence (literal or figurative). This applies even in our most intimate relationships. When we cannot be someone's best friend as we hoped, or when romantic feelings are not returned, we become bitter and resentful rather than accepting the reality gracefully.

## 2. Fighting Against God

When our interpersonal conflicts arise, where should we turn? To God, of course. Yet James identifies a critical mistake we make in our prayer life:

> You do not have, because you do not ask God. When you ask, you do not receive, because you ask with wrong motives, that you may spend what you get on your pleasures. (James 4:2-3)

The problem is not that God refuses to hear us. The problem is that we ask with **selfish motives**. Our prayers are self-centered, self-gratifying, designed entirely for our own comfort and benefit. We want God to function as a cosmic vending machine, supplying our desires on demand.

When God doesn't answer these prayers as we expect, we begin to doubt Him. We question His goodness, His power, His love. We shake our fist at Him in frustration. But this reveals the deeper issue: **we have made worldly desires more important than our relationship with God**.

> You adulterous people, don't you know that friendship with the world is hatred toward God? Anyone who chooses to be a friend of the world becomes an enemy of God. (James 4:4)

The word "adulterous" is striking. We are like unfaithful spouses, cheating on God by pursuing what the world offers instead of what He offers. When we prioritize our selfish desires over obedience to God, we become His enemies. This is not a status God imposes on us arbitrarily—it is the natural consequence of turning our backs on Him.

Yet God's response to this rebellion is not what we might expect. He does not leave us to our own devices:

> Or do you think Scripture says without reason that the spirit he caused to live in us envies intensely? But he gives us more grace. That is why Scripture says: "God opposes the proud but gives grace to the humble." (James 4:5-6)

God yearns intensely for us—not with the envious, self-centered desire we experience, but with a jealous love that wants to protect us and draw us back to Himself. And He offers us grace, not as a reward for our efforts, but as a gift to the humble.

The solution begins with humility:

> Submit yourselves, then, to God. Resist the devil, and he will flee from you. Come near to God and he will come near to you. Wash your hands, you sinners, and purify your hearts, you double-minded. Grieve, mourn and wail. Change your laughter to mourning and your joy to gloom. Humble yourselves before the Lord, and he will lift you up. (James 4:7-10)

This is not about self-loathing or performative sadness. Rather, it is about recognizing the seriousness of our sin—our envy, our pride, our competition with one another—and turning away from it. When we humble ourselves and draw near to God, He draws near to us.

## 3. God Fights For Us

The ultimate example of humility is Jesus Christ. He possessed every right to demand honor, yet He chose to serve. He humbled Himself completely, becoming obedient even unto death on a cross.

> Humble yourselves before the Lord, and he will lift you up. (James 4:10)

Jesus died for those who fought each other over possessions and status. He died for those who fought against Him—who took up swords against Him, who stole His clothes, who spat upon Him, who crucified Him. His death was the ultimate act of fighting *for* us, bearing our judgment so that we might be reconciled to God.

> There is only one Lawgiver and Judge, the one who is able to save and destroy. But you—who are you to judge your neighbor? (James 4:12)

God alone is Judge and Lawgiver. He has the authority to condemn, but He also has the power to save. Through Christ, He chooses to save those who turn to Him in humility. His jealousy over us is not petty possessiveness—it is the protective love of a Creator for His beloved creation.

## 4. Fighting For Each Other

Understanding God's grace toward us should transform how we treat one another. Instead of competing for scarce resources and status, we should redirect that energy toward caring for each other's needs:

> We are not competitors but brothers and sisters.

This means practicing genuine sacrificial love. If someone needs a chair, offer one. If someone is hungry, share food. If someone needs a loan, lend generously. Love requires that we sometimes give up what we want for the sake of others.

One way we can actively fight *for* each other's wellbeing is to **refrain from tempting** them with displays of our possessions. Modest dress, restraint in showing off expensive items, and discretion in romantic relationships all demonstrate care for others' struggles with envy. We should not be instruments of temptation for our brothers and sisters.

Additionally, we must speak carefully and truthfully about one another:

> Don't slander one another. Anyone who speaks against his brother or judges him speaks against the law and judges it. But when you judge the law, you are not keeping it, but sitting in judgment on it. There is only one Lawgiver and Judge, the one who is able to save and destroy. But you—who are you to judge your neighbor? (James 4:11-12)

Yet there is an important distinction between slander and **rebuke**. When we lovingly correct a brother or sister in sin according to what Scripture teaches—not to elevate ourselves but to help them grow—we are fighting *for* their godliness, not against them. Such correction should come from a heart of humility, rooted in God's Word rather than personal judgment.

This requires that we study Scripture carefully so we understand God's standards and can encourage others to live by them.

## Conclusion: Set Your Heart on What Lasts

It is easy to fall into the trap of endless desire. The latest technology, the newest fashion, the next upgrade—these things call to us constantly. Yet they are temporary. We must learn **contentment** with what we have and recognize that most of what we desire is luxury, not necessity.

> Set your hearts on things above, where Christ is, seated at the right hand of God. Set your minds on things above, not on earthly things. For you died, and your life is now hidden with Christ in God. (Colossians 3:1-3)

How we pray reveals what we truly value. When we pray in the name of Jesus, we pray in light of who He is and what He has done for us. This means our prayers should reflect His priorities, not merely our own comfort. Since Jesus has already dealt with our greatest need—forgiveness and reconciliation with God—we can trust Him to provide for our lesser needs.

Keep your eyes fixed on Jesus. Desire what He desires. Trust Him to provide what you truly need. And extend this same trust-centered perspective to your relationships. A marriage or romantic partnership that is meant to last must be rooted not in mutual attraction alone, but in a shared commitment to God and His love. God is the foundation of all lasting love, and Christ is our model.
