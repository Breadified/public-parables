## An Alien Race

When aliens from space visited Australia without prior knowledge of human culture, what strange observations might they make? They'd notice we worship LCD screens, that dogs appear to be our masters, and that we follow peculiar rules about footwear and eating rice with forks.

As a young migrant boy arriving in Sydney from Malaysia over two decades ago, I too was an alien observing unfamiliar customs. Yet there's something far more significant about being alien to this world: Christians are described as aliens who have been transplanted into another realm entirely.

## 1. Christians Have Come to the Living Stone

### The Chosen Precious Stone (vv. 4, 6-7)

> As you come to him, the living Stone—rejected by men but chosen by God and precious to him. (1 Peter 2:4)

Peter describes Jesus as *the living stone*—first chosen by God, and second, rejected by men. This echoes Old Testament prophecy:

> See, I lay a stone in Zion, a chosen and precious cornerstone. And the one who trusts in him will never be put to shame. (1 Peter 2:6, from Isaiah 28:16)

**The cornerstone was critical in ancient construction.** It was the first stone laid, setting the reference point for every stone that followed. If the cornerstone was crooked or improperly placed, the entire building would be skewed. Finding and preparing a large, perfectly straight stone required immense effort—they were hand-chiseled from raw rock. When found, such a stone was invaluable.

God, the expert builder, carefully chose a precious, flawless stone as the foundation. All other stones are set in relation to Christ, determining the position of the entire spiritual structure. This stone will not lead us astray; it will not construct a crooked building.

> Now to you who believe, this stone is precious. (1 Peter 2:7)

Those who recognize Jesus' value have come to him through belief. But the stone is not universally valued.

### Rejected by Men (vv. 7b-8)

> But to those who do not believe, "The stone the builders rejected has become the capstone." (1 Peter 2:7)

The foolish builders—perhaps seeking something more aesthetically pleasing, or deceived in their measurements—rejected the very stone that would have perfected their building. Like the rumor of a diamond ring carelessly thrown away, the rejected stone is precious beyond comprehension to those who later realize their mistake.

The leaders of Israel rejected and killed Jesus, the perfect stone. But the rejected stone is not passive:

> And, "A stone that causes men to stumble and a rock that makes them fall." They stumble because they disobey the message—which is also what they were destined for. (1 Peter 2:8)

The carelessly discarded stone becomes an obstacle. Those who rejected Jesus stumble over Him and fall, injuring themselves spiritually.

**Why do they stumble?** The answer has two perspectives:

**From the human perspective:** They disobey the message of God's word and refuse to believe (see 1:23, 2:7).

**From God's perspective:** They were destined for this fall. The same word used for "destined" in verse 8 appears in verse 6 ("laid"), indicating that God has established both the cornerstone for believers to build upon and the stumbling stone for unbelievers to trip over.

This is a *living* stone—not a passive rock on the ground, but an active force that affects people. To some, Christ is the foundation upon which to build. To others, He is an obstacle that causes their ruin.

**There is no neutral ground.** The word of God demands a response. You either accept Jesus or reject Him. The question is stark: where do you stand? Will you be among those who reject Him, or will you follow Jesus and be added to His spiritual building?

## 2. We Are the New Temple

### The Temple Is People, Not a Building (v. 5)

> You also, like living stones, are being built into a spiritual house to be a holy priesthood, offering spiritual sacrifices acceptable to God through Jesus Christ. (1 Peter 2:5)

As a result of coming to the living stone, we become the new temple of God—no longer a physical building, but a community of people.

**A Brief History of God's Presence:**

In the Garden of Eden, God walked visibly with Adam and Eve (Genesis 3:8). But sin severed humanity from that intimate presence.

Around 1500 BC, God's glory—the visible evidence of His presence—led the Israelites out of Egypt as a pillar of cloud by day and fire by night (Exodus 13:21). This same glory filled the tent of meeting where God met with Moses (Exodus 33:8-13).

Around 1000 BC, Solomon's temple was built, and God's glory filled it (1 Kings 8:10-11)—the house God promised to David.

In 586 BC, because of the people's sin, God's glory departed from the temple (Ezekiel 10:4), and the Babylonians destroyed it. Israel faced exile.

Upon returning from exile around 520 BC, the temple was rebuilt, but God's glory never returned to fill it as it had before. Yet the prophets promised something greater: *The latter glory of this house will be greater than the former* (Haggai 2:9).

For 400 years, the faithful waited. Then Jesus came—the true temple of God Himself (John 2:19). In His body dwells the fullness of God (Colossians 1:19).

After Pentecost, Jesus promised His people: *I will be with you always* (Matthew 28:20), and *I will dwell within you* (John 14:17, 14:23). By His Word and Spirit, He resides in His people.

**Therefore, we—the church—are now the true temple of God, the dwelling place of God among humanity (1 Corinthians 6:19).**

The church began with the cornerstone laid in Zion. Living stones have been added—the apostles, early believers, and now *you*. The temple is us—not the building.

This doesn't mean the physical space is unimportant for practical reasons. We gather in various settings: beautiful buildings, tents, school halls, or renovated houses. But the sacred reality is *people*, not the structure. Think of a human pyramid—the temple is the assembly of people, stone by stone, perfectly fitted together.

### Treat Each Other Sacredly

In the Old Testament, one approached the temple with great reverence. You were ritually cleansed. You brought sacrifices. You were respectful. The temple was *sacred*.

When we gather, we don't need ritual washing or ceremonial dress—those external forms are not sacred. But *people* are sacred. Each Christian carries the presence of God through His Word and Spirit.

We must honor each other. We must hold each other as sacred. When you treat a brother or sister carelessly, you desecrate the temple.

> Now that you have purified yourselves by obeying the truth so that you have sincere love for your brothers, love one another deeply, from the heart. (1 Peter 1:22)

**Commit to Each Other:**

This means genuine commitment to your Christian community—not casual attendance, but sacred obligation. Your friends are waiting for you. Your leaders have prepared. New visitors need someone to welcome them.

The excuse that "it's just a once-off" rings hollow when "once-offs" accumulate throughout the year. One group assignment, one piano concert, one oversleep, one exam, one camp, one baptism, two months of holiday—and suddenly you're only present eight months out of twelve. No other enterprise tolerates 2/3 absenteeism.

**You cannot love casually.** You cannot flirt with one group, then another, then another. Love demands commitment, like a gym membership: if you interrupt attendance, all benefits are lost. The church isn't a convenience store where you grab what you want and leave.

**You are living stones to each other—cemented by the blood of Jesus, added brick by brick by God Himself.** Treat each other sacredly.

## 3. We Are the New Priesthood

### Our Priestly Task (vv. 5, 9-10)

> You also, like living stones, are being built into a spiritual house to be a holy priesthood, offering spiritual sacrifices acceptable to God through Jesus Christ. (1 Peter 2:5)

Just as Old Testament priests offered sacrifices, we now offer *spiritual* sacrifices—not bulls or goats, but our sacrificial love for one another. Sacrifices aren't mere external piety (how we dress or appear at church). True sacrifice is *serving each other*.

In particular, **we serve each other by speaking about God:**

> But you are a chosen people, a royal priesthood, a holy nation, a people belonging to God, that you may declare the praises of him who called you out of darkness into his wonderful light. Once you were not a people, but now you are the people of God; once you had not received mercy, but now you have received mercy. (1 Peter 2:9-10)

### Declare Praise to Each Other

**What does "praise" mean?** It simply means *to advertise*.

Suppose we observe our friend Matt doing something excellent or displaying a beautiful character quality. We tell others about it—we advertise his nobility and virtue. This is praise. It's not necessarily a religious exercise or music-based activity, but simply *declaring the excellence of someone*.

When we declare God's praises, we advertise His character and deeds throughout history so that everyone might honor Him. The Psalms overflow with this pattern: commands to praise God followed by declarations of His mighty character and works for nations and individuals.

**When we praise, we advertise to someone and about something.** Praise is inherently public. We're not praising God by saying privately, "Thank you, Jesus." We're praising Him by telling others—our Christian family and the world—about His wonderful works.

**Declare praise to each other:** This means mutual encouragement. Share how God is changing you. What anger have you overcome? What malice have you resisted? What events strengthened your faith? Which talks challenged you? Share your testimony, your conversion story. This is advertising God's mighty, saving deeds to one another.

When we do this, we are built up. We are reminded, again and again, that the gospel works. We are doubters who need constant reassurance that God's promises are true.

### Declare Praise to the World

Beyond our Christian family, we're called to advertise Jesus to those still in darkness.

As we approach the end of the year, ask yourself: *How many friends have I told about Jesus? How many have I invited to church? How many have I prayed for?*

Too often, we spend excessive time in "pre-evangelism"—building a relationship for a year before mentioning Jesus, thinking it will seem more natural. But that's backwards. After a year of silence, suddenly announcing "By the way, I'm a Christian" feels jarring and strange. It's like saying to someone you've known for a year, "Oh, I'm married"—no one operates that way.

Instead, **speak of your faith naturally and early.** Yes, build a genuine friendship, but in natural conversation—when asked about yourself—"I'm a committed Christian" should surface quickly. Be authentic. If someone asks an open question, your faith should be among the first things you mention.

Around you every day are people in darkness. **You have the opportunity to help them step into light.**

Some believers engage in beach missions. Others hang around public spaces like Eastwood, intentionally seeking conversations about life's deeper meanings. As this year ends and people's schedules open, now is the time to invite those friends you've wanted to reach.

## Our Identity as Aliens

### What Will Aliens—or Jesus—Observe?

When aliens visit next weekend (after the initial excitement of a church event fades), when being Christian feels less fun and you're tired—what will they see? What will they conclude is important to you?

More importantly, when Jesus returns and observes your life, **what will He find is sacred to you?** Will He see commitment to ISCF or to James Ruse? What occupies your priorities?

Notice that you have come to the Living Stone.

**First, Jesus will observe your love for one another.** He will see whether you love deeply from the heart, whether your love is sincere. He will see if you've fled from malice, deceit, hypocrisy, envy, and slander. He will see whether you actually care about your Christian family—whether your commitment to them is as sacred as the ancient Jews held their temple.

**Second, He will find a people declaring His Son's praises to each other and to the world.** He will come and find His people talking—about what He has done, how He has changed lives, how He has called people from darkness into His marvelous light.

This is our calling as aliens in a foreign land: **to be living stones, carefully fitted together into God's true temple—a holy priesthood, declaring His praises.**
