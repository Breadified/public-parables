## Introduction

Before we begin, I need to offer several important disclaimers. First, I'm still learning about parenting myself. My wife and I are very new to this, with our eldest daughter at 3.5 years old and our second daughter at 1 year old. While I've read theories and been a teenager myself, we're actively testing these principles in our own home. In ten years, she will be a teenager, and many of you here may become parents soon, so I hope this proves helpful as you prepare for that season.

Second, I should acknowledge that the Bible doesn't actually say much about parenting explicitly. The core message is simple: love your children, discipline them, but don't exasperate them. The "how" is where we find silence in Scripture. I believe wisdom from doctors and psychologists can be genuinely helpful here, and I'll recommend some resources at the end.

Finally, remember that every home is different. What I share today is general wisdom drawn from various experiences. Take it with a grain of salt. Every family has exceptions, and as parents, you know what's best for your own children better than I do.

## 1. What Is Parenting?

Interestingly, the word "parenting" doesn't actually appear in the Bible. When the NIV uses "parent," it's translating the words "father" and "mother."

Regarding mothers, besides caring for and comforting their children, Scripture offers limited description. Titus 2 instructs older women to teach younger women to love their husbands and children, to be self-controlled, pure, and kind. But there's scant detail about what else mothers ought to do.

Fathers, however, have much clearer instruction:

> Fathers, do not embitter your children, or they will become discouraged. (Colossians 3:21)

> Fathers, do not exasperate your children; instead, bring them up in the training and instruction of the Lord. (Ephesians 6:4)

And Proverbs speaks extensively to the responsibility of discipline and teaching.

The Bible seems to concentrate on fatherhood as the model for describing parenting. This doesn't mean mothers have no role—parenting is a joint venture. Rather, the Father takes primary responsibility for ensuring the joint task of raising a child to maturity is accomplished.

This emphasis on fatherhood is appropriate because when we look to God as our model, He is described as Father:

> For this reason I kneel before the Father, from whom his whole family in heaven and on earth derives its name. (Ephesians 3:14-15)

When we observe what the Father in heaven is like, we see:

> Which of you, if his son asks for bread, will give him a stone? Or if he asks for a fish, will give him a snake? If you, then, though you are evil, know how to give good gifts to your children, how much more will your Father in heaven give good gifts to those who ask him! (Matthew 7:9-11)

> Every good and perfect gift is from above, coming down from the Father of the heavenly lights, who does not change like shifting shadows. (James 1:17)

### The Father's Role

**Provider and Protector.** Together with mothers, fathers are called to provide good gifts, love, comfort, and protection. The core responsibility is to nurture, protect, and nourish the life they have created, so the child can reach maturity. In this area, I think many parents do well. We work hard for our families, provide material security, and ensure our children have clothes and food. There's no real danger of parents failing to provide materially.

**Teacher and Disciplinarian.** The role that I believe parents struggle with most—and where I struggle—is that of Teacher and Disciplinarian. These are two aspects of the same activity: raising a child to maturity. Teaching and discipline form the life and character of children by helping them learn about life, about themselves, and about God. A father cannot fulfill this responsibility without the authority to teach and to discipline his children.

> Fathers, do not exasperate your children; instead, bring them up in the training and instruction of the Lord. (Ephesians 6:4)

The word "training" here carries the sense of practice and correction—like football training with its content, drills, and discipline. The issue many parents face is developing a fair and consistent system of discipline. Too often we let small infractions accumulate and then suddenly react harshly, rather than addressing behavior when it happens.

### The End Goal: Independence

But teach them what? The ultimate goal is independence—raising well-functioning adults who can care for themselves and eventually care for others. This isn't just about teaching skills; it's about teaching wisdom. A child may be able to solve math questions but lack the wisdom to navigate life. 

> Apply your heart to instruction and your ears to words of knowledge. Do not withhold discipline from a child; if you punish him with the rod, he will not die. Punish him with the rod and save his soul from death. My son, if your heart is wise, then my heart will be glad; my inmost being will rejoice when your lips speak what is right. Do not let your heart envy sinners, but always be zealous for the fear of the LORD. There is surely a future hope for you, and your hope will not be cut off. Listen, my son, and be wise, and keep your heart on the right path. (Proverbs 23:12-19)

Children truly honor their parents through independence. It would be embarrassing to have a thirty-year-old son who can't choose his own clothes or prepare a meal. When children reach adulthood, honor toward parents doesn't mean they continue to follow our instructions—it means they've learned to make wise, godly choices on their own.

The tragedy of a mentally disabled child is precisely this loss of independence. Parents grieve not just the disability itself, but the reality that their child will never fully care for themselves. This reveals how deeply parental love is connected to the vision of our children becoming independent, mature adults.

## 2. Obstacles to Parenting Today

Several things make this work harder for parents in our contemporary world.

### Insecurity Leading to Over-Control and Exasperation

Why are many Asian parents, myself included, so controlling? I notice this pattern in my own culture—we often dress our children in more layers than necessary, carry umbrellas at the slightest cloud, employ confinement periods after birth, and worry about camera flashes affecting babies' eyes.

**Where does this come from?** Perhaps from memories of poverty in China, cold winters without enough food, or the mentality that comes from immigration—the sense that you have only one chance to get it right. Perhaps the one-child policy in some families created intense pressure, or over-competitiveness drove parents to live vicariously through their children's achievements.

**The Results:** In our desire to give our children the opportunities we never had, we sometimes inadvertently create oppressive situations. Yes, we want them to have choices—but then we panic if they don't choose medicine. We're so afraid of consequences that we over-protect them, forgetting that independence requires some risk and failure. We get caught in nit-picking minor things while losing sight of the long-term goal. We exasperate our children rather than guide them. We become obsessive, perfectionist, trying to control every detail—and this is damaging.

We prevent them from forging their own identity and experiencing freedom. We create oppressive homes. I see this with my own daughter—I can worry excessively about things like eating lollies in public (which won't harm her) or fitting in with peers, when I should be teaching her wisdom instead.

### The Difficult Teenage Transition

Furthermore, the transition from childhood to adulthood is a tough road, and many Asian parents don't pull back soon enough. When should we begin loosening control? It depends on personal maturity—perhaps around puberty, or in high school when the natural search for independence emerges.

There's a time when parenting must end. At some point, you have to see how good a job you've done and step back, letting the world—not just you, but friends and mentors—become their teachers. They need to enjoy life beyond your instruction. This mirrors what Paul describes: the law as a schoolmaster has done its work, and now maturity requires something different.

The transition shouldn't be sudden; it should be gradual. But many parents create a vicious cycle: without training, there's no reason to trust them with responsibility; without trust, they don't develop independence; without independence being granted, we nit-pick at minor things; without permission to grow, trust erodes further. It's a downward spiral.

Yet this transition cannot be avoided. It's actually better for children to make mistakes while they're still under the home and school structure, where the stakes are lower, than to first fail when they're completely on their own. We must start this process before they're perfect or satisfy our standards—because that waiting point may never come.

### Lack of Time: The Outsourcing Problem

Modern life works against meaningful parenting. Work and school schedules pull us in opposite directions. Children attend specialized sports, ballet, Chinese lessons. The only interaction many families have is in car trips or perhaps at dinner. Meanwhile, we watch television instead of engaging together.

Consider how different this is from previous generations. Teaching used to happen through apprenticeship—a father and son spending the whole day together, learning a trade, having conversations naturally woven into the work. A parent might teach a child about life while gardening together or doing household chores.

How do we increase time with our children in our current context? Perhaps by:
- Being intentional on weekends
- Doing necessary tasks together (buying milk, household chores) rather than outsourcing them
- Talking during these times
- Cutting back on work where possible
- Reducing the number of extracurricular activities

I've found that working part-time has been good for spending more time with my daughter. These moments create natural opportunities for deeper conversation—the kind of "drug-proofing" that happens through genuine relationship, not lectures.

## 3. The Higher Goal: Discipleship

We want to raise independent, socially competent children. But as Christians, there's a larger goal. The real aim is to help our children reach heaven.

Here's where the wisdom of doctors and child psychologists reaches its limits—they say nothing about disciples of Jesus. So the question becomes personal: Where do we stand with God? Are we, under the blood of Jesus, confident of heaven? And if so, what are we doing to help our children get there?

Recently, my wife heard a talk by a child development expert who identified four stages of a child's faith journey:

**Experienced Faith:** The child is Christian because being Christian feels good and they have positive experiences.

**Affiliative Faith:** The child is Christian because it's a good club—their family is Christian, their friends are Christian, the church community is welcoming.

**Searching Faith:** The child begins to question and wrestle with faith as their own.

**Owning Faith:** The child moves beyond their parents' faith and develops a personal, authentic relationship with God.

The critical transition happens in the searching and owning stages. A child cannot have genuine faith in Jesus until they own it themselves—not borrowed from their parents, but genuinely their own.

This ownership is easier when children can see authentic faith in their parents. Not fake faith. Not faith confined to Sunday mornings. But faith lived out in daily life—the kind of faith that shapes decisions when no one is watching.

You teach your children as you go about daily routines. They're watching everything. They see when you tell your husband to lie on the phone and say you're not home. They notice when you swear or talk badly about others. They observe your choices. My own father taught me more through his decisions about sick leave—how he lived with integrity—than through any lecture he could have given.

### The Hard Question

But here's the challenging question for us as parents: Do we really want our children to be disciples of Jesus?

Because discipleship means:
- Being persecuted and ridiculed by schoolmates for standing up for what they believe
- When they suffer hardship, trials, prejudice, rejection, and mistreatment, will we rejoice that they're earning their stripes as disciples of the Master?
- Will we congratulate them if they drop out of medical school to teach Scripture at a local high school?
- Will we be pleased when they sense a call to the mission field, with its danger, insecurity, and financial disadvantage? Will we actually pray for them to follow that call?

The life of Isobel Kuhn shows us what this looks like—a woman who answered God's call even though it meant sacrifice and uncertainty.

You can now see why Proverbs makes teaching and discipline a matter of life and death:

> Punish him with the rod and save his soul from death. My son, if your heart is wise, then my heart will be glad; my inmost being will rejoice when your lips speak what is right. Do not let your heart envy sinners, but always be zealous for the fear of the LORD. There is surely a future hope for you, and your hope will not be cut off. (Proverbs 23:14-18)

> Discipline your son, for in that there is hope; do not be a willing party to his death. (Proverbs 19:18)

## 4. Application and Prayer

I believe the parents here at St. Matthew's do put God as a priority. I see you transporting your children to church every week. I'm encouraged when parents give children permission to attend church activities even during HSC exams. You're teaching your children that spiritual formation matters alongside career planning and academic achievement. You're modeling that this life is not all there is.

Keep doing this hard work. Remember that home is the first school. You'll always be adjusting, always trying to be one step ahead—or at least not one step behind. And you do all this while carrying the baggage from your own parents, what they did or didn't do. It's hard work, shaping a soul.

The parental role shifts over time: more disciplinarian in early years, more friend in later years.

To those who aren't parents: appreciate your parents. They've carried this weight for you.

**Let's pray:**

Thank you for parents who love their children. Balance so many competing priorities. It's easy to criticize from the sidelines, but so hard in real life. We pray for children to appreciate the sacrifices made for them. We pray for adults to become truly independent, making their parents proud not by blind obedience, but by making godly choices in life. May we all chat more, especially at home—not as lectures, but as genuine conversation. And may we never stop learning from one another how to do this sacred work of parenting.
