# Matthew 1:18-25

**Title:** The Birth of Hope  
**Date:** December 17, 2011  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Names and Their Meaning

Every newborn receives a name, and while some names seem frivolous—chosen on a whim or borrowed from favorite nouns—the most meaningful names carry weight. They tell a story. They represent either a significant history or the hopes and dreams of a family.

In Chinese culture, names are chosen with purpose. A name might honor a grandparent, or it might express a family's deepest longing. My own Chinese name means "peace," given to me around the time of my grandmother's death in hopes that God would bring comfort to our family during that season of grief. Some Asian families name their children "Harvard," not because they live near the university, but because that name embodies their aspirations for their child's future.

The birth of Jesus embodies both profound history and humanity's deepest hopes. The names given to Him tell the entire story of what His coming means.

## 1. The Christ—Heir of History

> This is how the birth (genesis) of Jesus Christ came about. (Matthew 1:18)

The word "genesis" here refers not merely to birth, but to origins—we must look to the past to understand the significance of this name. Matthew's gospel opens with a genealogy of forty names spanning forty generations, a number that echoes Israel's forty years of exile. This genealogy is not incidental; it establishes that Jesus is the climax of all of Israel's history.

The title "Christ" carries all of this history within it.

**Christ** means "Messiah" or "Anointed One." In Hebrew tradition, anointing with oil was how Israel's kings were established—not with a crown, but with sacred oil. This was how the line of David was established. The Christ is the anointed heir of King David, the one the prophets promised would come.

Yet there is an irony in Matthew's account:

> His mother Mary was pledged to be married to Joseph, but before they came together, she was found to be with child through the Holy Spirit. (Matthew 1:18b)

The Holy Spirit—God's breath, God's wind—hovered over this conception, just as God's breath hovered over the deep at creation (Genesis 1:1). This was not a natural birth. Jesus was a direct creation of God, not the decision of any man. This is why the genealogy breaks at Joseph: "Jacob the father of Joseph, the husband of Mary, of whom was born Jesus" (Matthew 1:16). Joseph did not beget Jesus.

But here is the beautiful resolution to this apparent contradiction: Joseph legally adopted Jesus as his son. Because Joseph was from the line of David, and because he gave Jesus his name (Matthew 1:25), Jesus became the legal heir of David's throne. The irony is stunning—previously, the sons of David were adopted by God. Now, the true Son of God is adopted by David.

This creates a tension for Joseph, however.

### The Problem for Joseph

In the ancient Near East, betrothal was not like modern engagement. It was a binding legal contract—as serious as marriage itself, though it could last six months to a year. The betrothed couple was considered married in the eyes of the law, even though they had not yet lived together or been intimate. A betrothed woman could be called her fiancé's "wife," and if he died, she was considered a widow. She was bound to faithfulness; unfaithfulness was punishable by death.

When Joseph discovered Mary's pregnancy, he faced an agonizing situation:

> Because Joseph her husband was a righteous man and did not want to expose her to public disgrace, he had in mind to divorce her quietly. (Matthew 1:19)

Joseph took the law seriously. He took adultery seriously. As a righteous man, he was obligated to dissolve the betrothal. Yet because he was also righteous in his compassion, he determined to do so quietly, protecting Mary from public shame. This shows us the character of Joseph: committed to justice, yet tempered with mercy.

### The Dream

But then came the revelation:

> But after he had considered this, an angel of the Lord appeared to him in a dream and said, "Joseph son of David, do not be afraid to take Mary home as your wife, because what is conceived in her is from the Holy Spirit. (Matthew 1:20)

Joseph had made his decision in the darkness of confusion and pain. But God spoke to him in a dream, as He had spoken to the patriarchs of old. The angel called Joseph "son of David," reminding him of his place in God's unfolding story. And Joseph obeyed. He took Mary as his wife, and in doing so, became part of the greatest miracle in human history.

## 2. Jesus—The Savior

> She will give birth to a son, and you are to give him the name Jesus, because he will save his people from their sins. (Matthew 1:21)

The name **Jesus** comes from the Hebrew *Yoshua*, which means "Yahweh saves." It is a functional name, chosen to declare the purpose of His life.

Jesus came to save His people from their sins. This is not merely forgiveness in the abstract sense, but actual redemption—the transaction of His life for ours. The Old Testament had long prophesied this:

> He himself will redeem Israel from all their sins. (Psalm 130:8)

And later:

> Here I will make a horn grow for David and set up a lamp for my anointed one. (Psalm 132:17)

This anointed one would deal with the people's sins—not by overlooking them, but by bearing their penalty Himself. Throughout His ministry, Jesus made this clear:

> When Jesus saw their faith, he said to the paralytic, "Take heart, son; your sins are forgiven." (Matthew 9:2)

And at the Last Supper:

> This is my blood of the covenant, which is poured out for many for the forgiveness of sins. (Matthew 26:28)

Jesus took His name to its ultimate expression. He saved by pouring out His blood. He saved by refusing to save Himself.

Let me illustrate this with a story from my own family. One of my cousins in Malaysia was dying of a blood disease and needed a transplant. To find a match, my family made the extraordinary decision to conceive another child specifically to provide the transplant. That little girl endured painful procedures, but because of her sacrifice, her sister lived. You could say she was born for one specific reason: to save her sister's life.

Inherent in the name Jesus is this very purpose—He was born to save us from our sins. He would endure the agony of the cross to do it. When you see the baby in the manger this Christmas, do not think merely of something cute or sentimental. Think of a heart transplant—with your name on it. Think of His willingness to bear your defective, sinful heart away and replace it with His own. This is love. This is what the name Jesus means.

## 3. Immanuel—God With Us

But the story does not end there. Matthew tells us:

> All this took place to fulfill what the Lord had said through the prophet: "The virgin will be with child and will give birth to a son, and they will call him Immanuel"—which means, "God with us." (Matthew 1:22-23)

This prophecy comes from Isaiah 7:14, spoken in a time of great crisis. The Northern Kingdom was plotting to destroy the South. War was coming. Extinction seemed possible. Yet God promised a sign to King Ahaz: a virgin would conceive and bear a son, guaranteeing the future of the Davidic dynasty. His name would be Immanuel—God is with us.

Matthew identifies Jesus as the ultimate fulfillment of this prophecy. Jesus is God's presence made visible. At that first Christmas, among 150 million people on earth, God became a human being whom you could touch, hold, and be with. God was with us.

But here is the remarkable promise that extends to us today. Jesus rose from the dead, and He promised:

> And surely I am with you always, to the very end of the age. (Matthew 28:20)

God is no longer present merely through a burning bush or a pillar of fire or even in the person of Jesus walking the earth. Through His Spirit and through His Word, He is with us now. He is Emmanuel—God with us—in our worship, in our service, in our trials, in our joys, until the end of time.

## Your Name This Christmas

So I ask you: Who will you be identified with this Christmas?

The Christian faith calls you to bear a name—the name Christian. Paul wrote:

> If you suffer as a Christian, do not be ashamed, but praise God that you bear that name. (1 Peter 4:16)

To be Christian is to follow the Christ—the anointed King of David's line. It is to trust in Jesus, who saves us from our sins through His blood. It is to embrace Immanuel, who promises to be God with us forevermore.

This is not a trivial or frivolous name to bear. It carries the weight of history—all those who have gone before you, who have suffered and served and worshiped through the centuries. It carries the weight of hope—in your service to God, in your faithfulness, in your trust that He is with you.

Let these three names transform how you understand Jesus and your own identity this Christmas: The Christ who fulfills all of God's promises, Jesus who saves you from your sins, and Immanuel who will never leave you alone.
