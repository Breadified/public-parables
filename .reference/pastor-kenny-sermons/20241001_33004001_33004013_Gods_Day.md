# Micah 4:1-13

**Title:** God's Day
**Date:** 24 October 2024
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Vision of Peace

Micah opens his final vision with a glimpse of what God will accomplish in the last days—a time when Jesus has completed His work through His death and resurrection. This is a prophecy about the future kingdom of God, when His rule will transform everything.

> In the last days the mountain of the Lord's temple will be established as the highest of the mountains; it will be exalted above the hills, and peoples will stream to it. Many nations will come and say, "Come, let us go up to the mountain of the Lord, to the temple of the God of Jacob. He will teach us his ways, so that we may walk in his paths." The law will go out from Zion, the word of the Lord from Jerusalem.

The mountain here represents Zion—the high place from which God comes (see Micah 1:3). Notice the movement in this vision: peoples and nations, the Gentiles (goyim), are streaming to God's mountain. This is remarkable because it shows the accessibility of God's kingdom to all people.

### Why Do They Come?

The nations come with a specific desire: to be taught God's ways so they may walk in His paths. This reminds us of the Queen of Sheba's visit to Solomon. When she heard of his wisdom and fame, she traveled from afar to test him with hard questions. But when she arrived and witnessed his wisdom, the palace he had built, and the order of his household, she was overwhelmed. Her report had not captured the full reality—Solomon's wisdom was even greater than the rumors suggested.

Similarly, something about the wisdom and fruit of Christianity attracts people. In Asia, the best schools and universities are Christian institutions. The finest hospitals are Christian-run. Immigrants from around the world, like the Queen of Sheba of old, like Kenny's own family, are drawn to Christian nations seeking Christian wisdom and Christian civilization.

### The New Testament Fulfillment

This prophecy finds its fulfillment in John 12, at the raising of Lazarus. The crowds who witnessed the miracle spread word of what Jesus had done. Many people came to meet Him because they had heard about this sign. The Pharisees despaired, saying, "Look how the whole world has gone after him!" Then Greeks—Gentiles—came to the festival and sought to see Jesus. They came to Philip asking, "Sir, we would like to see Jesus." This moment signals that Jesus' hour of glorification has come, and the Gentiles are beginning to stream to Him.

While the language differs from Micah's prophecy, the principle is the same: Gentiles streaming to encounter God.

## The Reign of God

From this high point of God's kingdom, the vision expands:

> He will judge between many peoples and will settle disputes for strong nations far and wide. They will beat their swords into plowshares and their spears into pruning hooks. Nation will not take up sword against nation, nor will they train for war anymore. Everyone will sit under their own vine and under their own fig tree, and no one will make them afraid, for the Lord Almighty has spoken.

### Understanding the Peace

This is not merely the absence of war. War emerges when different values compete with one another, when people fight to see whose values will prevail—whether through military conflict or trade disputes like tariffs. Peace, by contrast, comes when all people are ruled by a common king and live not by their own conflicting values, but by His values.

The vines and fig trees symbolize farming, industry, and productive work. Plowshares represent agriculture. Together they signify prosperity and blessing—the fruit of living under God's rule when there is no fear.

### A Difficult Verse

Verse 5 presents a challenge:

> All the nations may walk in the name of their gods, but we will walk in the name of the Lord our God forever and ever.

At first glance, this seems to advocate some form of religious pluralism—nations following their own religions while Israel follows God. But this contradicts verse 2, where nations are streaming to the mountain of God to follow Him. The whole context of the passage is positive about people following the Lord.

The issue may be one of translation. An older rendering helps clarify: "For all people will walk every one in the name of his god, and we will walk in the name of the Lord our God forever and ever" (KJV). The meaning is that everyone's own god will become the Lord our God. Their gods, which used to be idols, are now the Lord God. All nations will recognize and follow the one true God.

## A Timely Challenge for Today

There is something profoundly attractive about Christianity's wisdom and its fruit. Yet we face a curious irony in our generation: while record numbers of people from around the world are immigrating to Australia—coming to us—record numbers of Australians, including many Christians, are emigrating elsewhere. We are flying away from a mission field that God is bringing directly to our doorstep. The timing could hardly be worse.

### Are Our Churches Ready?

When these seekers arrive, will they find the law of God? Will they encounter the word of the Lord? Will they be taught God's ways?

Our churches must ask themselves honestly: Are we prepared for this moment? Are we equipped to disciple the nations that God is sending to us?

### Will Our Schools Be Ready?

Will our schools maintain Scripture in Schools (SRE) programs? Do we have the teachers? Do we have the volunteers and financial support needed to sustain this ministry?

The harvest is ripe. Migrants come with a readiness that is often lost on those born into Christian culture. They expect change, they are prepared to learn a new language, to adapt to a new culture, to make compromises. They understand that becoming part of a new nation requires transformation. Even among the local population, there are signs that Generation Z is turning back to the faith and traditions of their grandparents.

The opportunity before us is extraordinary. The question is whether we have the courage, commitment, and resources to answer the call.
