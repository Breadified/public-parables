# Luke 18:1-14

**Title:** True Justice  
**Date:** 29 June 2014  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Introduction: Why Justice Matters

Justice resonates deeply within us. When your car is scratched, your laptop stolen, or you discover someone cheated on an exam you both took, something inside demands that it be made right. This sense of righteousness isn't arbitrary—it connects to fundamental values: truth, rightness, order, consistency, and fairness. These matter because they reflect the character of God Himself.

This sermon explores justice through Jesus's teaching in Luke 18, examining what true justice is and how it relates to mercy, God's character, and our response as believers.

---

## Part 1: God the Judge (Luke 18:1-8)

### The Story: Two Characters in Contrast

Jesus teaches through a parable featuring two characters at opposite ends of the power spectrum.

**The Unjust Judge** (v. 2)

> "In a certain town there was a judge who neither feared God nor cared about men."

This judge occupies a position meant for justice, yet he embodies its opposite. His problem isn't incompetence—it's his complete absence of fear of God. In 2 Chronicles 19:6-7, when judges were appointed in Israel, they were explicitly told:

> "Consider carefully what you do, because you are not judging for man but for the LORD, who is with you whenever you give a verdict. Now let the fear of the LORD be upon you. Judge carefully, for with the LORD our God there is no injustice or partiality or bribery."

Without this fear, the judge has no restraint. He can be unjust, show partiality, accept bribes. Combined with his indifference to human welfare, he becomes a powerful, merciless ruler answerable to no one.

**The Widow** (v. 3)

> "And there was a widow in that town who kept coming to him with the plea, 'Grant me justice against my adversary.'"

If the judge occupies the peak of power, the widow stands at the opposite extreme. Powerless. Without a husband in a society where women depended entirely on men for protection, support, and belonging. Likely poor, unable to afford legal representation. Yet she persists, returning again and again, pleading for justice. She is, as Kenny observes, "praysistent"—persistent in her prayers.

### The Judge's Reluctant Capitulation (v. 4-5)

> "For some time he refused. But finally he said to himself, 'Even though I don't fear God or care about men, yet because this widow keeps bothering me, I will see that she gets justice, so that she won't eventually wear me out with her coming!'"

Justice comes not from the judge's righteousness or sense of duty, but from sheer exhaustion at her persistence. He grants her case to be rid of her harassment.

### The Argument: How Much More (v. 6-8)

> "And the Lord said, 'Listen to what the unjust judge says. And will not God bring about justice for his chosen ones, who cry out to him day and night? Will he keep putting them off? I tell you, he will see that they get justice, and quickly.'"

This is a "how much more" argument. If an unjust judge—who cares nothing for God or people—will eventually grant justice simply because someone won't stop asking, how much more will God Himself bring justice? We are not insignificant to Him like the widow was to the judge. We are His chosen ones.

### God's Character: The Foundation of True Justice

God's commitment to justice flows from His very nature. Scripture reveals this consistently:

**God is just in all His ways.** Isaiah 30:18 declares: "Yet the LORD longs to be gracious to you; therefore he will rise up to show you compassion. For the LORD is a God of justice. Blessed are all who wait for him!"

Deuteronomy 32:4 affirms: "He is the Rock, his works are perfect, and all his ways are just. A faithful God who does no wrong, upright and just is he."

**God loves justice.** Psalm 11:7 states: "For the LORD is righteous, he loves justice; upright men will see his face."

Justice is the natural expression of God's goodness. He hates evil and loves good. Amos 5:15 summarizes the essence: "Hate evil, love good; maintain justice in the courts."

True justice, then, means:
- Evil punished
- Good rewarded
- Expressed through consistent, predictable laws
- Administered through fair procedures and proper retribution and restoration

Israel was called to mirror God's justice. Before kings ruled, judges governed—the foundation of the nation was justice itself. And when Jesus came, He repeated the call: "Stop judging by mere appearances, and make a right judgment" (John 7:24).

### Confusion About Justice Today

Unfortunately, modern discourse has muddled the meaning of justice.

**The shift from retributive to rehabilitative justice**

Historically, justice meant: commit a crime, face punishment. Law courts ensured procedural fairness; guilt resulted in retribution. Today, we've renamed prisons "correctional facilities" or "rehabilitation centres," shifting the primary purpose from punishment to rehabilitation. This reflects a fundamental confusion about what justice actually is.

**The conflation of justice with economic equality**

Far more damaging is the modern tendency to equate "social justice" with economic and social equality. But these are entirely different things.

Justice concerns what is right and wrong. Economic equality concerns sameness.

When Martha complained to Jesus about unequal household labor, He didn't intervene to equalize the workload. Instead, He gently rebuked her misplaced values (Luke 10:38-42). When a man asked Jesus to force his brother to divide an inheritance fairly, Jesus sharply rebuked him for covetousness, not his brother's unfairness (Luke 12:13-15).

**Why enforcing equality would be unjust**

Consider Cristiano Ronaldo earning €21 million annually for playing football while average workers struggle on 50-60 hours per week. Is this unjust? No. He has worked extraordinarily hard, God has given him remarkable athletic gifts, and he lives in an age where that skill is valued. This is precisely how justice should work: effort and talent produce reward.

To "correct" this disparity would require injustice. Either Ronaldo must become mediocre, or everyone else must match his excellence. Disabling his gifts to achieve equality would be unjust to him and to those who value his talent.

The principle is simple: harder work, greater skill, fortunate circumstances—these justly produce greater reward. That some grow richer faster than others isn't an injustice requiring correction; it's justice functioning properly.

**The poverty confusion**

Modern activists speak of poverty in Australia, often citing figures of 15-20% living in poverty. Yet Australia has virtually no poverty in any meaningful sense—perhaps 1% experience genuine destitution.

The discrepancy exists because statisticians use "relative poverty"—typically defining poverty as earning below 50% of the median income. By this definition, many people classified as "poor" own cars, air conditioning, multiple televisions, gaming systems, refrigerators, washing machines, and cordless phones. They struggle not with starvation but with affording cable TV, mobile phone bills, and air conditioning alongside food.

Real poverty—homelessness, hunger, inability to meet basic needs—is rare in Australia. What's often called poverty is better described as relative inequality, which is not the same as injustice.

**A call for clarity**

We need greater clarity in discussions of social justice. Traditional media often prioritizes equality over mercy or justice. When everyone demands something, we should be suspicious. Be careful not to invent new categories of injustice while remaining blind to actual injustice.

## Part 2: The Problem with Justice (Luke 18:8b)

### Justice Is Elusive

Jesus closes with a troubling question:

> "However, when the Son of Man comes, will he find faith on the earth?"

The faith here refers to faithful, persistent prayer for justice. And the reality is stark: justice on earth remains elusive.

In Jesus's time and throughout Israel's history, justice seemed impossible to find:

**External injustices** abounded. Foreign powers invaded. The Romans desecrated the temple, killed men, assaulted women, corrupted youth, levied oppressive taxes. Where was justice?

**Internal injustices** festered. The leaders Jesus addressed were corrupt or indifferent (Luke 11:42): "Woe to you Pharisees, because you give God a tenth of your mint, rue and all other kinds of garden herbs, but you neglect justice and the love of God."

Even in our own time and place—where Australia's legal system, built on biblical foundations, is relatively just—injustice persists and is often compromised.

### Why Justice Remains Incomplete

This is the result of sin and the fall. God permits us to experience the effects of sin in our daily lives (Romans 1), and these effects pervade not only criminal acts but the entire machinery of justice itself.

**We lack omniscience.** We cannot know all the facts. Witnesses lie or are unavailable. Security cameras capture only fragments. We cannot infallibly distinguish truth from lies. We do not know a person's intent (mens rea)—whether an action was deliberate or accidental.

**We lack omnipotence.** We cannot catch all offenders. Statutes of limitations expire. Jurisdiction is limited. Nazi war criminals evade justice; corrupt officials flee to countries unwilling to extradite. Wealthier defendants afford better lawyers.

**We lack omnipresence.** We cannot see everything and be everywhere to prevent injustice or collect evidence.

These limitations mean that while we should be grateful for our legal systems, they are ultimately band-aid solutions applied to a deep, flesh wound. They work temporarily, then fail. Ultimate justice belongs to God alone.

### A Sobering Warning

This is why Jesus calls for faithful prayer—not for activism, but for intercession. We look forward to Christ's return and the kingdom to come, when Jesus will establish ultimate justice.

We should pray for judges, governments, law courts, and institutions. But we must pray with caution, recognizing a hard truth: when justice comes, it will be all-encompassing. God will not judge only Hitler, Pol Pot, mafia bosses, bullies, or the person who scratched your car. He will judge everyone—including you and me.

The question we must ask ourselves: Will I be at peace when I pray for God's justice?

## Part 3: The Mercy of God (Luke 18:9-14)

### Two Prayers, Two Outcomes

Jesus now tells a second parable, this time with explicit application:

> "To some who were confident of their own righteousness and looked down on everybody else, Jesus told this parable..."

Again, two characters. Again, a stark contrast.

**The Pharisee's Prayer for Justice** (v. 10-12)

> "Two men went up to the temple to pray, one a Pharisee and the other a tax collector. The Pharisee stood up and prayed about himself: 'God, I thank you that I am not like other men—robbers, evildoers, adulterers—or even like this tax collector. I fast twice a week and give a tenth of all I get.'"

The Pharisee is the righteous man of the street—a keeper of the law, a moral leader. He walks the walk. Fasting twice weekly as a sign of piety. Giving 10% to charity. He could be the man running marathons for Oxfam, camping out for the Sleepless Night, giving blood, collecting food cans, doing forty-hour famines. He's doing far more than most.

Yet his prayer is fundamentally a prayer for vindication and judgment. He thanks God that he is not like "robbers, evildoers, adulterers"—and especially not like the tax collector beside him. He is asking God to affirm his righteousness and condemn others' wickedness.

**The Tax Collector** (v. 13)

> "But the tax collector stood at a distance. He would not even look up to heaven, but beat his breast and said, 'God, have mercy on me, a sinner.'"

In Jesus's time, tax collectors were despised equally with robbers, evildoers, and adulterers. They were Jews who served as intermediaries between Rome and the Jewish population—betrayers working for the occupier. And they enriched themselves through overtaxing, cheating both their own people and their Roman masters. Everyone despised them. (Zacchaeus, mentioned later in Luke, was a chief tax collector and very wealthy—a case study in exploitation.)

This tax collector stands at a distance, too ashamed even to enter fully into the temple. He cannot bear to look toward heaven, knowing God sees his guilt. He beats his breast in contrition, as if physical pain might somehow assuage his inner shame.

His prayer is radically different: "God, have mercy on me, a sinner."

He does not pray for justice. Why would he? He is guilty. He has been unjust—ripping people off, causing suffering, possibly even contributing to someone's death. He has cheated and lied. He knows he deserves punishment. So instead of demanding justice, he begs for mercy. Do not give me what I deserve. Withhold your justice. Show me mercy instead.

### The Shocking Verdict (v. 14)

> "I tell you that this man, rather than the other, went home justified before God. For everyone who exalts himself will be humbled, and he who humbles himself will be exalted."

The tax collector—the corrupt, guilty, despised sinner—goes home justified. Declared right before God. While the righteous, upstanding Pharisee goes home unjustified.

### The Paradox: How Can Mercy Be Just?

This raises an uncomfortable question: How can God justify the guilty and still be just? Justice is justice. The guilty should be punished. God cannot simply overlook sin as if it doesn't matter. Unlike us, He knows exactly what the tax collector did. He witnessed every deception, every theft, every act of exploitation. God is capable of destroying him. How can He show mercy without compromising His justice?

The answer is the gospel. Romans 3:23-26:

> "For all have sinned and fall short of the glory of God, and are justified freely by his grace through the redemption that came by Christ Jesus. God presented him as a sacrifice of atonement, through faith in his blood. He did this to demonstrate his justice, because in his forbearance he had left the sins committed beforehand unpunished—he did it to demonstrate his justice at the present time, so as to be just and the one who justifies those who have faith in Jesus."

At the cross, justice and mercy meet. When Jesus died, He took the punishment we deserved. In that single act:
- **Justice was accomplished:** Jesus paid the price for sin.
- **Mercy was accomplished:** Jesus forgives us our debt.

Both justice and mercy are demonstrated fully and simultaneously.

### Application: From Mercy to a Merciful Life

Perhaps some of you here recognize yourself as a sinner. You have cheated. You have lied. You have stolen. Guilt eats at you. The message is simple: it is time to say sorry to God. Pray for mercy. Humble yourself before Him. God will forgive you—not because you deserve it, but because of Jesus.

But forgiveness is not the end. It is the beginning of transformation.

After receiving mercy, humble yourself to changing your life under God's direction. Repair the damage you've caused. If you have stolen, return what you took and never steal again. The life that has received mercy must become a life that extends mercy to others.

This is the heart of Christian social action:
- Love your neighbor as yourself
- When someone strikes you on one cheek, present the other
- Show mercy to others, knowing how much you have been forgiven
- Be generous with one another—not because it is justice, but because of mercy

**This is the critical distinction:** The call for social justice often springs from envy. The call for Christian action springs from mercy. Mercy, not social justice, is the Christian motivation.

A Christian is not called to campaign for economic equality or income redistribution. Christ's gospel is not concerned with equity but with relationships—with reconciliation and restoration. This begins locally, with those around us. And God even provides tax incentives to encourage such generosity.

The tax collector went home justified not because he was better than the Pharisee, but because he recognized his sin and sought mercy. The Pharisee went home unjustified not because he lacked righteousness, but because he failed to recognize his own sinfulness.

---

## Conclusion: True Justice

Before we pray for justice, we must first pray for mercy—mercy for ourselves, recognizing our own guilt and need for God's forgiveness. Only then are we positioned to pray rightly for justice in the world. For we cannot ask God to judge others justly while we ourselves hide from His just judgment.

True justice, then, is inseparable from true mercy. And both are found supremely in Jesus Christ.
