# Malachi 2:17–3:16

**Title:** Messaging the World through Our Purses
**Date:** 17 June 2012
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Question: Where Is God's Justice?

The ancient Jews had just returned from exile in Babylon to their promised land, but they found themselves strangers in a foreign country. The towns and fields had long since been occupied by nomads and opportunists who'd moved in during the decades of emptiness. Starting from nothing, they had to rebuild the temple while hostile neighbors overcharged for materials. They reploughed abandoned land, rebuilt houses, dug irrigation channels—all while facing harassment and famine. No wonder they cried out in desperation:

> You have wearied the LORD with your words. "How have we wearied him?" you ask. By saying, "All who do evil are good in the eyes of the LORD, and he is pleased with them" or "Where is the God of justice?" (Malachi 2:17)

Their plea was simple and raw: Where is God? If this is His promised land, why isn't He protecting His own people? Is God unfair?

## God's Answer: I Will Come Myself

### God Is Just and Will Bring Justice

God's response to their despair is breathtaking. He doesn't argue or minimize their suffering—He promises something far greater: His personal intervention.

> See, I will send my messenger, who will prepare the way before me. Then suddenly the Lord you are seeking will come to his temple; the messenger of the covenant, whom you desire, will come. (Malachi 3:1)

This is no distant promise. God Himself will return to the temple He abandoned centuries before because of Israel's sin. He takes injustice so seriously that He will come personally to deal with it. When the boss shows up to check on customer service, you know things are about to change.

God is fundamentally a God of justice. He created the world to overflow with righteousness, and He actively works to restore what has been wronged. Yet this promise raises an unsettling question.

### The Problem: God's Judgment Is Complete

> But who can endure the day of his coming? Who can stand when he appears? For he will be like a refiner's fire or a launderer's soap. (Malachi 3:2–3)

When God judges, He judges everything. His judgment isn't selective or partial—it's complete and thorough, like weed spray that kills everything it touches.

> I will be quick to testify against sorcerers, adulterers and perjurers, against those who defraud laborers of their wages, who oppress the widows and the fatherless, and deprive aliens of justice. (Malachi 3:5)

Here's the danger in calling for justice: when you ask God to destroy the wicked, punish the oppressors, and judge the defrauders, you're asking Him to judge completely. But are you wicked? Are you an oppressor? A defrauder? In God's court of equity, the law is harsh, and those who approach must do so with clean hands.

When we cry out for justice on the rich while we ourselves are rich, on the greedy while we ourselves struggle with greed, we're essentially asking God to judge ourselves too. This is why the psalmist says God doesn't change and Israel isn't destroyed—it's an act of mercy (Malachi 3:6).

## Two Errors to Avoid

### Error One: Thinking God Doesn't Care

God absolutely cares about justice and the vulnerable. He calls us to compassion in tangible, practical ways:

- **In small ways:** Stand against bullies at school, refuse to tolerate racism at work, give generously to those in need, simplify your own life to share more with others, join with others to advocate for the vulnerable.
- **In larger ways:** Work in government or volunteer for charities to fight for the rights and dignity of refugees and the marginalized.

James will teach us much more about this later in the year. God's heart for justice and mercy is real and demands our response.

### Error Two: Thinking Compassion Is God's Only Agenda

Yet Jesus himself rejected the notion that relieving suffering was His primary mission. When His disciples found Him teaching, they said: "Everyone is looking for you!"

> Jesus replied, "Let us go somewhere else—to the nearby villages—so I can preach there also. That is why I have come." (Mark 1:38–39)

Jesus came with a larger, more urgent agenda. He came to address the greatest injustice of all: our separation from God through sin. He came to prepare the way for judgment and to provide the solution to that judgment.

### The Gospel Changes Everything

As it says in Malachi 3:2–4, when God refines His people like silver and gold, they will bring righteous offerings and become acceptable before Him. This is where Jesus enters the story. He is the refined one. He is the one who brings righteousness and makes others acceptable before God.

Because Jesus took God's judgment upon Himself, we—with our unclean hands—can now approach God and petition for justice. By bearing our judgment, Jesus has made it possible for God to hear our prayers for the world and to grant them. This is God's long-term agenda: that those with unclean hands might become friends with Him through His grace.

This is the gospel, and it will ultimately bring about a new heaven and new earth where sin, suffering, and injustice are no more.

Social justice and human compassion, by comparison, are temporary measures. We often don't even know if our help truly serves others. We can't always discern what real justice looks like. Our efforts, however sincere, are at best band-aid solutions to a cancer growing in society. Without the gospel, we're only making people slightly more comfortable on their way to eternity separated from God.

**The best thing we can do for others is tell them about Jesus.** Warn them of His coming. Help them understand God's solution for their sin. Bring them to hear Scripture. Transport them to church not merely out of kindness, but so they might encounter the God who alone can truly bring justice.

This is the heart of Christian generosity: we are generous with everything, but especially with the gospel.

## Israel's Injustice: Robbing God

### The Sin: Withholding the Tithe

Yet while Israel cried out for justice, they themselves were committing injustice. God confronts them directly:

> "Will a man rob God? Yet you rob me. But you ask, 'How do we rob you?' In tithes and offerings." (Malachi 3:8)

The tithe—a tenth of all produce—was established in the Old Testament as the means of supporting the temple and the priesthood. The Levites were an entire tribe dedicated to God's service, representing roughly 8.3 percent of the population. The tithe supported both the temple's operation and the Levites' livelihood. It was also meant to care for the vulnerable:

> When you have finished setting aside a tenth of all your produce in the third year, the year of the tithe, you shall give it to the Levite, the alien, the fatherless and the widow, so that they may eat in your towns and be satisfied. (Deuteronomy 26:12)

Yet despite having both a functioning temple and priesthood, Israel had withheld their tithe. God calls this robbery.

### Generosity Under Grace

For us today, the principle isn't the tithe itself but generosity flowing from grace. Paul writes:

> Out of the most severe trial, their overflowing joy and their extreme poverty welled up in rich generosity. They gave as much as they were able, and even beyond their ability. Entirely on their own, they urgently pleaded with us for the privilege of sharing in this service to the saints. And they did not do as we expected, but they gave themselves first to the Lord and then to us in keeping with God's will. (2 Corinthians 8:2–5)

Paul's standard isn't a fixed percentage but sincerity: giving ourselves first to the Lord, letting our finances reflect His priorities.

True generosity is liberating. It's not bound to 10 percent, but neither does it mean we can give less. Some seasons call for 11 percent, or even far more. What matters is that giving becomes our first priority in our yearly budget, automatic and intentional. Place it at the top. Set up direct debit.

If you've never given to gospel work, start now. If you're already giving, evaluate whether you're giving sacrificially. If you're young with low income, forming this habit now is invaluable. Remember: no one else will give to gospel work. It's your privilege and responsibility. Make it your priority, even before supporting your parents, even before your own comfort.

### Blessing and Curse

God presents a clear choice to Israel: obedience brings blessing; disobedience brings curse.

**Under a curse:**

> You are under a curse—the whole nation of you—because you are robbing me. (Malachi 3:9)

The curses of Deuteronomy 28 are extensive and severe: cursed in the city and in the field, cursed in your labor and your crops, your children taken into captivity, aliens rising above you while you sink lower and lower. All this because they did not obey God's commands.

**But if they return:**

> Bring the whole tithe into the storehouse, that there may be food in my house. Test me in this, and see if I will not throw open the floodgates of heaven and pour out so much blessing that you will not have room enough for it. I will prevent pests from devouring your crops, and the vines in your fields will not cast their fruit. Then all the nations will call you blessed, for yours will be a delightful land. (Malachi 3:10–12)

The blessings are equally abundant: blessed in the city and in the field, blessed in your labor and crops, enemies defeated before you, rain in season, prosperity in all you touch. You will lend to nations but borrow from none. You will be the head, not the tail.

### What Does "Blessed" Really Mean?

We must be clear: blessing in Scripture is not the prosperity gospel. It's not about becoming wealthy so people will follow God. True blessing is this:

> Blessed are they whose transgressions are forgiven, whose sins are covered. Blessed is the man whose sin the Lord will never count against him. (Romans 4:7–8)

Blessing is being protected from judgment. It's persevering under trial and receiving the crown of life (James 1:12). It's being insulted for Christ's name and having the Spirit of glory rest upon you (1 Peter 4:14). It's having your robes washed clean and gaining the right to enter the city of God (Revelation 22:14).

When Malachi says "all the nations will call you blessed," he means they will see in God's people the evidence of His favor: a land where justice prevails, where the vulnerable are protected, where generosity and righteousness flourish. This is the blessing God offers—not luxury, but the deep peace that comes from walking with Him and obeying His commands.

## The Final Word: A Scroll of Remembrance

As Malachi concludes, God addresses those who remained faithful even amid widespread unfaithfulness:

> Then those who feared the LORD talked with each other, and the LORD listened and heard. A scroll of remembrance was written in his presence concerning those who feared the LORD and honored his name. (Malachi 3:16)

> They will be mine, says the LORD Almighty, in the day when I make up my treasured possession. I will spare them, just as in compassion a man spares his son who serves him. (Malachi 3:17)

Even in exile, even in hardship, God remembers those who fear Him and honor His name. On the day of judgment, He will make them His treasured possession. And while the arrogant and evildoers will be like stubble burned in a furnace, those who revere His name will see the sun of righteousness rise with healing in its wings (Malachi 4:1–2).

This is God's promise to us: faithfulness is seen. Obedience is remembered. And in the end, justice—true, complete, and redemptive justice—belongs to God alone.
