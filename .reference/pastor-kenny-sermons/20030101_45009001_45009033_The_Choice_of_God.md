# Romans 9:1-33

**Title:** The Choice of God
**Date:** January 2003
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## When God Seems to Fail

There are moments in life when it feels like God has failed us. A young child dies in sleep. A teenager takes their own life. A loved one passes away without ever believing in Jesus. In these dark seasons, we wonder: Is God truly as powerful as we believe? Is He really in control of people and events?

The early church struggled with this same despair. Paul writes with unmistakable anguish:

> I speak the truth in Christ—I am not lying, my conscience confirms it in the Holy Spirit—I have great sorrow and unceasing anguish in my heart. For I could wish that I myself were cursed and cut off from Christ for the sake of my brothers, those of my own race. (Romans 9:1-3)

Paul's pain runs deep: great sorrow, unceasing anguish, a willingness to be cursed himself. Why? Because it appeared that God had failed to save Israel. Despite all God's promises, Israel as a nation was rejecting Christ—they killed their Messiah, they persecuted His followers, and they continued to turn away. The evidence seemed overwhelming: God's Word had failed.

## The Promise to Israel

To understand Paul's concern, we must remember what God had promised to Israel:

> The people of Israel. Theirs is the adoption as sons; theirs the divine glory, the covenants, the receiving of the law, the temple worship and the promises. Theirs are the patriarchs, and from them is traced the human ancestry of Christ, who is God over all, forever praised! Amen. (Romans 9:4-5)

This was not a casual promise. God declared it eternal:

> You have established your people Israel as your very own forever, and you, O LORD, have become their God. (2 Samuel 7:24)

And it applied to all Israel:

> Only if these decrees vanish from my sight will the descendants of Israel ever cease to be a nation before me... Only if the heavens above can be measured and the foundations of the earth below be searched out will I reject all the descendants of Israel because of all they have done. (Jeremiah 31:36-37)

God promised that His rejection of Israel would be as impossible as measuring the heavens or fathoming the earth's depths. Yet here was the reality: most Jews were not believing in Jesus. How many Jews are Christians today? More Chinese have embraced the faith than Jews. Had God's Word truly failed?

## God Has Not Failed

Paul's answer is direct and clear:

> It is not as though God's word had failed. (Romans 9:6)

The resolution lies in understanding a single word: "all."

> For not all who are descended from Israel are Israel. Nor because they are his descendants are they all Abraham's children. (Romans 9:6b-7)

This distinction runs through Scripture from the very beginning. When God made promises to Abraham, He never meant that every biological descendant would inherit the promise. God has always distinguished between individuals within Israel.

### The Example of Isaac

Consider Abraham. He had many descendants, but God chose only one to carry forward the covenant line:

> On the contrary, "It is through Isaac that your offspring will be reckoned." For this was how the promise was stated: "At the appointed time I will return, and Sarah will have a son." (Romans 9:7b-9)

Abraham's servant Hagar bore him Ishmael—a biological son. Yet Ishmael was rejected. God chose Isaac instead, the son born to barren Sarah, because Isaac was the *child of the promise*. Therefore:

> In other words, it is not the natural children who are God's children, but it is the children of the promise who are regarded as Abraham's offspring. (Romans 9:8)

The lesson is profound: blood does not determine belonging to God's people. This was Israel's fatal mistake—they relied too heavily on their traditions, their heritage, their connection to Abraham. They thought, "I am related to Abraham, so I am secure before God." But Ishmael was also related to Abraham, yet he was not a child of the promise.

God does not desire grandchildren through bloodlines. He wants children—individuals in personal relationship with Him. Many of you here are first-generation Christians, while others came to faith because your parents were Christians. The critical question is this: Do you have a personal relationship with God, or does your relationship come through your parents? God relates to individuals, not through family trees. He wants to be Father, not Grandfather.

### The Example of Jacob

The pattern continues with Isaac's family:

> Not only that, but Rebekah's children had one and the same father, our father Isaac. Yet, before the twins were born or had done anything good or bad—in order that God's purpose in election might stand: not by works but by him who calls—she was told, "The older will serve the younger." Just as it is written: "Jacob I loved, but Esau I hated." (Romans 9:10-13)

Jacob and Esau were twins—identical circumstances, same father, born together, having done neither good nor evil. Yet God chose Jacob before they were even born, based not on their works but on His sovereign purpose.

This principle of divine choice appears throughout Scripture:

> Are not two sparrows sold for a penny? Yet not one of them will fall to the ground apart from the will of your Father. (Matthew 10:29)

> No one can come to me unless the Father who sent me draws him. (John 6:44)

God has always exercised choice. Abraham was chosen. Isaac was chosen. Jacob—whose name was changed to Israel—was chosen. And within that group, God continues to choose individuals according to His purposes.

This is not cause for despair but for wonder. **What a privilege to be chosen by God!**

> Praise be to the God and Father of our Lord Jesus Christ, who has blessed us in the heavenly realms with every spiritual blessing in Christ. For he chose us in him before the creation of the world to be holy and blameless in his sight. In love he predestined us to be adopted as his sons through Jesus Christ, in accordance with his pleasure and will—to the praise of his glorious grace, which he has freely given us in the One he loves. (Ephesians 1:3-6)

If it were based on anything in us, we would have nothing. Our election is entirely God's gracious choice.

## God Is Just

Yet here comes the natural objection:

> What then shall we say? Is God unjust? (Romans 9:14)

This sounds deeply unfair. Some don't get chosen. We live in a culture that recoils from discrimination. Don't people have a right to be saved? Don't we have the right to choose for ourselves?

Paul's answer is remarkably gentle yet firm:

> Not at all! For he says to Moses, "I will have mercy on whom I have mercy, and I will have compassion on whom I have compassion." It does not, therefore, depend on man's desire or effort, but on God's mercy. Therefore God has mercy on whom he wants to have mercy, and he hardens whom he wants to harden. (Romans 9:14b-18)

The heart of this teaching is mercy. And mercy—true mercy—requires freedom. If God cannot freely choose to show compassion, then it ceases to be mercy and becomes a reward. A reward must be earned through human effort. But if salvation is earned through our effort, we deserve only judgment.

We must cling to mercy. There is no other path to salvation. And if God chooses those He will save, then necessarily He also appoints the rest for judgment.

### The Example of Pharaoh

Paul illustrates this with Pharaoh:

> For the Scripture says to Pharaoh: "I raised you up for this very purpose, that I might display my power in you and that my name might be proclaimed in all the earth." (Romans 9:17)

In Exodus, God repeatedly told Pharaoh to release the Israelites from slavery. Pharaoh refused again and again. Yet God said to him:

> For by now I could have stretched out my hand and struck you and your people with a plague that would have wiped you off the earth. But I have raised you up for this very purpose, that I might show you my power and that my name might be proclaimed in all the earth. (Exodus 9:15-16)

Yes, Pharaoh made his own decisions, and he deserves punishment for them. But notice how God describes it: "I have raised you up for this very purpose." God was working out His purposes through Pharaoh's very rebellion.

This truth appears consistently throughout Scripture:

> They stumble because they disobey the message—which is also what they were destined for. (1 Peter 2:8)

> For certain men whose condemnation was written about long ago have secretly slipped in among you. (Jude 4)

Just as God chooses to show mercy to some, He chooses to harden others according to His sovereign design.

## I Am Not Just—But God Is

A troubling question immediately arises:

> One of you will say to me: "Then why does God still blame us? For who resists his will?" (Romans 9:19)

The logic seems inescapable: If God controls everything, nothing is my fault. I'm not responsible. I'm merely a robot following God's predetermined program.

Paul's response is both humble and decisive:

> But who are you, O man, to talk back to God? "Shall what is formed say to him who formed it, 'Why did you make me like this?'" Does not the potter have the right to make out of the same lump of clay some pottery for noble purposes and some for common use? (Romans 9:20-21)

God is the potter. We are the clay. This is not cruelty—it is the fundamental reality of Creator and creature.

Consider a parent preparing a meal for their children. You prepare a lovely dish with care and skill. Your children complain: "Why so much salt? Why this color? Why did you cut the meat this way? Why didn't you cook the vegetables longer?" At the end of the day, the answer is simple: because I am the cook.

We may question God similarly: "Why this way? Why that way?" But God has the sovereign right to make us and to determine our purpose. Some He makes for judgment, like Pharaoh. Others He makes to enjoy His mercy.

The crucial question is not whether God's choice is fair by our standards, but which kind of vessel are *you*?

If you want to become an object of mercy—it is simple: Confess that Christ is Lord and you are saved! If you want to remain an object of wrath—it is equally simple: Stay as you are. Don't submit to God.

### The Robot Objection

Some object that this makes us robots, stripped of genuine choice. But what troubles us about being a robot?

If brilliant humans at universities can create AI robots that learn and make decisions through complex logic, why can't God create a perfect "robot" so exquisitely designed that we experience complete freedom while operating under divine sovereignty? What if God is so brilliant that He can create creatures who feel like they're making choices because they truly *are* making choices—yet those choices are always aligned with His perfect purposes?

Both humans and robots are creatures subject to the Creator. Both are works of the Creator who determines whatever He chooses. If "robot" simply means a creature under a sovereign God, then I have no problem admitting I am a robot—especially since I am a robot who is deeply loved.

God designed it this way for the benefit of those who enjoy His mercy:

> What if God, choosing to show his wrath and make his power known, bore with great patience the objects of his wrath—prepared for destruction? What if he did this to make the riches of his glory known to the objects of his mercy, whom he prepared in advance for glory—even us, whom he also called, not only from the Jews but also from the Gentiles? (Romans 9:22-23)

He did all of this for you. Even Israel's hardening served a divine purpose—it opened the door of salvation to the Gentiles. What seemed like God's failure with Israel was actually God's provision for the nations.

> As he says in Hosea: "I will call them 'my people' who are not my people; and I will call her 'my loved one' who is not my loved one," and, "It will happen that in the very place where it was said to them, 'You are not my people,' they will be called 'sons of the living God.'" (Romans 9:24-26)

Israel's hardening became the pathway of salvation for the Chinese, for the Gentiles, for all nations. God has not failed.

## The Sovereignty of God for Our Good

Though tragedy and disaster come—and they do come—they fit within the purposes of an all-powerful God. We live with many constraints: we cannot fly unaided, we cannot think cancer away, we cannot change our genes. Yet we somehow expect to be free from God, our Creator. This desire is not really about making free decisions—we make decisions that feel free every single day. The real problem is that we want to be free *from* the Creator Himself.

Instead, let us surrender. Let us stop demanding independence from God. Rather, let us live in deliberate dependence on Him—dependent for life, for sustenance, for mercy.

God's sovereignty is for our good. If God were not predestining—if He were not in sovereign control—the world would be a place of pure terror. It would mean the world operates outside His control, that disasters are truly random, that we cannot even ask God for help with confidence (His response would be "I'll try, but I'm not sure"). It would mean your future is uncertain, the devil could still win, and eternal loss remains possible.

But when we rest our hope in a predestining God, everything changes. Random tragedies are not random at all. They make sense within the larger purposes of an all-knowing, all-powerful God who has a plan and maintains absolute control of that plan to do good for those who love Him. This is the foundation of genuine peace: a God who is sovereign and good, whose purposes cannot be thwarted, and whose heart is toward our ultimate blessing.
