# John 13:1-38

**Title:** Washing the Feet of the Saints & The Hour Has Come
**Dates:** 2 April 2023 (ARISE), 28 February 2016 (SMAC)
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## I. Accepting the Service of the King (13:1-11)

Jesus knew that his hour had come to leave this world and return to the Father. Having loved his own who were in the world, he loved them to the very end—this speaks not just of duration but of the depth and completeness of his love.

> Now before the Passover Festival, Jesus knew that the hour had come for him to leave this world and go to the Father. Having loved his own who were in the world, he loved them to the end.

What unfolds in these opening moments is extraordinary. Jesus was fully aware that the Father had put all things under his power, and yet he chose an act of profound humility. He rose from the meal, removed his outer clothing, wrapped a towel around his waist, and began to wash the disciples' feet—the work reserved for the lowliest of servants.

Foot-washing was a menial task. Feet accumulated the dirt and filth of walking through streets and fields. It was a task so degrading that even Jewish law exempted Jewish servants from this duty—it was work fit only for Gentile slaves. In 1 Samuel 25:41, we see it described as the posture of complete servitude.

The contrast is striking. At the beginning of Jesus' ministry in John 2, he transformed water into wine at a wedding feast—a glorious moment. But at his last feast with his disciples, he lowered their feet into a basin and the water turned black with their dirt.

### Peter's Resistance

When Jesus came to Simon Peter, Peter protested: "Lord, are you going to wash my feet?" The emphasis in the original Greek is emphatic—"You shall never wash my feet." Peter could not accept this inversion of order. How could the Master serve the disciple?

Jesus responded with a statement that cut to the heart of the matter: "Unless I wash you, you have no part with me."

The washing was far more than an act of hospitality or cultural custom. It was linked directly to Peter's relationship with Jesus and his inheritance with him. Unless Jesus cleanses you, you have no share in him, no inheritance, no eternal life.

Why did Peter hesitate? Several reasons emerge:

**First, vulnerability.** To have one's feet washed meant exposing the part of the body one most hopes others won't notice—dirty, calloused, imperfect. It's like someone scrolling through your browser history without the benefit of incognito mode. Peter was embarrassed to expose himself this way to Jesus.

**Second, unwillingness to remain unclean.** Perhaps Peter didn't want Jesus to see or touch that part of him. He wanted to keep some area of his life hidden. "Don't wash my feet, Jesus. Ignore that part of me. The rest is acceptable, right?"

But Jesus insists: "Unless I wash you, you have no part with me."

Being a Christian means admitting our wrongs, exposing our vulnerable selves to Jesus in prayer and confession. It means saying, "I cannot do this task of being totally righteous before God. I have failed to love you and to love each other. I need you to cleanse me." To be part of Jesus is to accept his service—to be humbled, exposed, and dependent upon his grace.

### Peter's Overcorrection

Peter then swung to the opposite extreme: "Then, Lord, not just my feet but my hands and my head as well!"

Jesus answered: "Those who have had a bath need only to wash their feet; their whole body is clean. And you are clean, though not every one of you."

Jesus had taught them for three years. This final act—their cleansing—was something only he could accomplish through his death. He understood something of Judas that the others did not.

## II. Serving Like the King (13:12-35)

After washing their feet, Jesus put on his clothes and returned to his place. Then came the teaching moment: "Do you understand what I have done for you?"

This was an object lesson. The disciples called him "Teacher" and "Lord," and rightly so. But if he, their Lord and Teacher, had washed their feet, then they must wash one another's feet. This is a lesser-to-greater argument: if the greater can humble himself to serve, how much more must the lesser serve one another?

> Now that I, your Lord and Teacher, have washed your feet, you also should wash one another's feet. I have set you an example that you should do as I have done for you.

Sometimes we hesitate to accept service from others because accepting creates an obligation—we then owe them service in return. If the CEO of the company starts vacuuming the office after 5pm, you know what's next: you'll be expected to do the same.

The disciples understood this immediately. Jesus had served them, so now they must serve one another. There is no servant greater than his master, nor a messenger greater than the one who sent him. Those who understand and do these things will be blessed.

### The Paradox of Service

But here lies a remarkable paradox: when God serves, he doesn't want anything back in return. We operate on the principle of reciprocity—"I scratch your back, you scratch mine." But Jesus says, "I scratch your back, now you scratch someone else's. Pass it on."

This is the logic of the parable of the forgiving king in Matthew 18. A king forgave his servant a debt of ten thousand coins. But the servant turned around and demanded payment from another debtor for a mere hundred coins. The king's forgiveness wasn't meant to be hoarded—it was meant to overflow into mercy for others.

We owe God our lives. But he doesn't want our lives back in the currency of animal sacrifice or endless worship songs. What does he want? He wants us to lay our lives down for others. He wants us to love one another.

### The New Commandment

After Judas departed into the night, Jesus spoke to the remaining disciples:

> A new command I give you: Love one another. As I have loved you, so you must love one another. By this everyone will know that you are my disciples, if you love one another.

This phrase—"a new command"—gave rise to the term "Maundy Thursday," from the Latin "mandatum" (mandate). It is Christ's concluding pronouncement, his final command before the cross.

What is remarkable is that the God of the Christian faith is fundamentally different from all other gods. Other gods demand sacrifice—the Aztecs demanded child sacrifices; Hindu traditions burn offerings to ancestors; various religions require us to do things before the gods will lift a finger to help us. But our God serves first. He serves us at the cross, pouring out his life as a ransom for many.

The next time Jesus took off his outer clothing, it was to lay aside his life on the cross. He would be crucified naked. The next time he served would be to give himself as a ransom for many.

## III. Judas: The One Who Refuses Service (13:21-30)

But Judas stands apart. He did not accept Jesus' service.

> For he knew who was going to betray him, and that was why he said not everyone was clean.

Judas appears repeatedly throughout this passage as the one who rejects Jesus' love and ministry. The devil had already prompted Judas to betray Jesus. Yet Jesus knew those he had chosen, and he spoke of the betrayal in such a way that only Judas understood the message. Jesus left the door open even to Judas.

When Jesus dipped bread and gave it to Judas, saying "What you are about to do, do quickly," Satan entered into him—not merely influencing him, but Judas fully embraced the devil's work. Judas then went out into the night.

In John's Gospel, night is never merely the absence of light. It is a symbol of spiritual darkness and danger. "Are there not twelve hours of daylight?" Jesus once asked. "Anyone who walks in the daytime will not stumble, for they see by this world's light. It is when a person walks at night that they stumble, for they have no light."

Judas chose to walk in darkness, apart from the light of the world. He chose a path where he would lose his footing and turn from the right way.

Yet throughout all this, Jesus' love for Judas never wavered. "Having loved his own who were in the world, he loved them to the end." Jesus loved Judas to the very end, even when Judas refused that love.

## IV. Peter: The One Who Boasts (13:36-38)

Peter represents a different kind of refusal. When Jesus said he was going somewhere Peter could not follow—not yet—Peter asked, "Lord, why can't I follow you now? I will lay down my life for you."

It was a grand gesture. Peter imagined himself climbing Golgotha, sacrificing himself to save Jesus. But Jesus didn't need that. He went to the cross because Peter could not. No human can make the atoning sacrifice that pleases God the Father.

Yet Jesus didn't ask for that kind of death from Peter. Instead, he asked something simpler and more demanding: "Love one another."

> Will you really lay down your life for me? Very truly I tell you, before the rooster crows, you will disown me three times.

Peter didn't understand yet. His faith was still built on grand gestures and his own strength. He would learn, through his own failure and restoration, that laying down one's life means the daily work of loving others, serving one another, and seeking not human praise but the glory that comes from God.

## V. Three Responses to the Hour (12:37-50)

As John draws the book of signs to a close, he presents three types of responses to Jesus' claims:

### A. Those Who See But Do Not Believe (12:37)

> Even after Jesus had performed so many signs in their presence, they still would not believe in him.

This pattern recurs throughout John's Gospel. People witness signs—the feeding of the five thousand, the raising of Lazarus—and initially believe. But as soon as Jesus begins to teach them, they turn away. In chapter 6, the crowds murmured, "This is a hard saying; who can listen to it?" In chapter 12, they objected, "But the Messiah doesn't die." They saw the signs yet refused to believe.

The problem was not the signs themselves but what Jesus said. The Pharisees never questioned whether the miracles happened; they questioned his authority to perform them on the Sabbath or his identity as the promised Messiah. Jesus nearly had them—if only he would stop speaking!

But he doesn't. And so this group, though they see, do not believe. Their refusal is not intellectual but spiritual. Like a road sign pointing to Carlingford, its purpose is not to be admired but to direct travelers to their destination. Jesus' signs point to his words and his identity. Those who stop at the sign and fail to follow its direction have missed the point entirely.

### B. Those Who Believe But Fear (12:42-43)

> Nevertheless, many even among the leaders believed in him. But because of the Pharisees they would not openly acknowledge their faith for fear they would be put out of the synagogue; for they loved human praise more than praise from God.

This group is different. They did believe Jesus. But fear gripped them. Fear of losing status among the ruling authorities. Fear of being cast out of the synagogue—their entire community, their basis of belonging and identity. In a culture where community is everything, such ostracism was devastation.

John's diagnosis is clear: their real problem was not fear but pride. They "loved the glory that comes from men more than the glory that comes from God." They chose the praise of others over the approval of God.

This fear remains our struggle today. We fear being called weird or puritanical. We fear losing friendships if we stand for what we believe. We hesitate to pray before a meal with friends, to rebuke one another in love, to ask tough questions—because we fear being cast out of the group.

But Jesus offers comfort: "Fear not! The King is here." He brings forgiveness and restoration, not condemnation. The choice, though, remains ours.

### C. Those Who Hear and Believe (12:44-50)

> Whoever believes in me believes not in me only, but in the one who sent me. And whoever sees me sees the one who sent me.

There is no large third group of believers recorded in the Gospel. By the end of the book of signs, unbelief dominates the landscape. But Jesus describes the nature of true belief for those willing to hear:

True believers are those who hear and keep Jesus' words. They recognize that his words carry the authority of God the Father—his commandment, his life-giving word. Belief is fundamentally about hearing, not seeing. Signs point; words declare. Words carry the weight of eternity.

In John 20:29, Jesus says to Thomas, "Blessed are those who have not seen and yet believe." A superior state. We are like those who have never visited Carlingford but know exactly how to get there because we've studied the directions. We need not the sign itself but the words it points to.

This is consistent with John's entire purpose: "These are written so that you may believe that Jesus is the Christ, the Son of God, and that by believing you may have life in his name."

## Application: Three Paths

We face the same choice the crowds faced. Will we be like those who see but do not believe—looking for signs while missing the words? Will we be like those who believe but fear—unwilling to stand for Jesus because of social pressure? Or will we be like those who hear and believe—taking Jesus' words seriously, trusting them, and allowing them to shape our lives?

The teaching is clear: do not look for signs. Listen to the words. Read them carefully. Eat them up. These words have been written down for us. These are the words that will judge us in the last day. When you stand before God, you will not be able to say, "I didn't know." The words speak for themselves.

And this is our hope: the God who serves is the God who saves. Jesus came as the light of the world so that whoever believes in him might not remain in darkness. He came not to judge the world but to save it. His judgment falls only on those who reject his words and refuse his love.

The hour has come. Through Jesus' death and suffering—the final Passover—salvation is open to all. Jew or Gentile, rich or poor, powerful or powerless. There is no distinction. The only question is: will you hear and believe?
