# Mark 13:1-37

**Title:** Jesus Brings Judgment  
**Date:** 6 April 2014  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Question of the End Times

The disciples asked Jesus the same question many ask today: When will the end of the world come? As Jesus was leaving the temple, his disciples marveled at its massive stones and magnificent buildings. But Jesus redirected their attention with a sobering prediction:

> "Do you see all these great buildings? Not one stone here will be left on another; every one will be thrown down."

Rather than gazing at the buildings around them, Jesus instructed his disciples to watch for something far more important—the signs of coming judgment.

## Three Signs to Watch For

Jesus gave his followers three categories of signs to recognize:

### 1. Watch for Deceivers

> "Watch out that no one deceives you. Many will come in my name, claiming, 'I am he,' and will deceive many."

> "At that time if anyone says to you, 'Look, here is the Messiah!' or 'Look, there he is!' do not believe it. For false messiahs and false prophets will appear and perform signs and wonders to deceive, if possible, even the elect."

One of the primary signs would be confusion about identity—questions of who the Messiah actually is. Throughout history, this pattern has repeated itself. In ancient times, figures like Hippolytus of Rome and others predicted Jesus would return in 500 AD. In modern times, religious figures like Sun Myung Moon have claimed to be a Messiah figure, deceiving countless followers.

### 2. Watch for Wars and Rumors of Wars

> "When you hear of wars and rumors of wars, do not be alarmed. Such things must happen, but the end is still to come. Nation will rise against nation, and kingdom against kingdom. There will be earthquakes in various places, and famines. These are the beginning of birth pains."

The twentieth century became the most murderous in recorded history, with an estimated 187 million deaths from wars—over 10% of the world's population in 1913. Yet even with such unprecedented violence, Jesus assured his followers that these signs, while real, do not necessarily signal the immediate end.

### 3. Watch for Persecution

> "You must be on your guard. You will be handed over to the local councils and flogged in the synagogues. On account of me you will stand before governors and kings as witnesses to them."

Yet even in persecution, believers need not fear:

> "Whenever you are arrested and brought to trial, do not worry beforehand about what to say. Just say whatever is given you at the time, for it is not you speaking, but the Holy Spirit."

The persecution of Christians continues to this day. German Chancellor Angela Merkel noted in 2013 that Christianity is "the most persecuted religion in the world." Approximately 200 million Christians—representing 10 percent of global Christians—face social disadvantage, harassment, or active oppression. This persecution is especially severe in the Middle East, the birthplace of Christianity itself, where the Christian population has declined from over 1.2 million in Iraq (1990) to fewer than 200,000 by 2013. The Middle East, home to Jesus and his disciples, has become a place where Christians are increasingly rare.

Jesus also warned of a more personal betrayal:

> "Brother will betray brother to death, and a father his child. Children will rebel against their parents and have them put to death. Everyone will hate you because of me, but the one who stands firm to the end will be saved."

### 4. Watch for Cosmological Distress

> "When you see the abomination that causes desolation standing where it does not belong—let the reader understand—then let those who are in Judea flee to the mountains."

The urgency of this calamity is captured in vivid imagery. There will be no time to gather possessions or even clothing:

> "Let no one on the housetop go down or enter the house to take anything out. Let no one in the field go back to get their cloak."

The distress will be especially severe for the most vulnerable:

> "How dreadful it will be in those days for pregnant women and nursing mothers!"

> "There will be days of distress unequaled from the beginning, when God created the world, until now—and never to be equaled again."

Yet even in this darkest hour, mercy remains:

> "If the Lord had not cut short those days, no one would survive. But for the sake of the elect, whom he has chosen, he has shortened them."

The heavens themselves will respond:

> "The sun will be darkened, and the moon will not give its light; the stars will fall from the sky, and the heavenly bodies will be shaken."

## A Question About Timing

We might ask: Haven't calamities, wars, and suffering been happening for two thousand years? What use is a sign if it's been part of everyday life? And scientifically, some of these predictions seem fantastic—the sun darkening when it has billions of years of fuel remaining.

Yet Jesus himself addressed this apparent delay:

> "Now learn this lesson from the fig tree: As soon as its twigs get tender and its leaves come out, you know that summer is near. Even so, when you see these things happening, you know that it is near, right at the door. Truly I tell you, this generation will certainly not pass away until all these things have happened. Heaven and earth will pass away, but my words will never pass away."

## The Signs Were Already Fulfilled

I believe Jesus was speaking of something that had already happened—or would happen in his very generation. To understand this, we must read the subsequent chapters of Mark and examine the events surrounding Jesus's death and resurrection.

### The Deception Surrounding Jesus's Death

The disciples were told to watch for confusion about identity. At Jesus's arrest:

> "The one I kiss is the man; arrest him and lead him away under guard." (Judas's signal at Gethsemane)

Ironically, they released the wrong man:

> "Do you want me to release to you the king of the Jews?" asked Pilate. But the chief priests stirred up the crowd to have Pilate release Barabbas instead.

### The War That Wasn't

Jesus predicted rumors of war. At his arrest:

> "Just as he was speaking, Judas, one of the Twelve, appeared. With him was a crowd armed with swords and clubs, sent from the chief priests, the teachers of the law, and the elders."

A brief moment of violence erupted:

> "Then one of those standing near drew his sword and struck the servant of the high priest, cutting off his ear. 'Am I leading a rebellion,' said Jesus, 'that you have come out with swords and clubs to capture me?'"

But it was no true war—merely the appearance of one.

### The Persecution and Betrayal

Brother betrayed brother when Judas, one of the Twelve, went to the chief priests to betray Jesus. Peter, who had walked with Jesus from the beginning, denied even knowing him in a moment of fear.

The disciples, terrified, abandoned their master:

> "Then everyone deserted him and fled. A young man, wearing nothing but a linen garment, was following Jesus. When they seized him, he fled naked, leaving his garment behind."

Such panic that even the clothes on their backs were left behind—fulfilling Jesus's own words about having no time to gather possessions.

Peter stood before the council and was interrogated, as Jesus had warned his followers would happen.

### The Cosmological Sign

And then came the ultimate calamity—the judgment itself. Jesus, the Son of God incarnate, was arrested on false charges, tried unjustly, and sentenced to death. He hung on the cross in agony and blood.

And the heavens responded:

> "At the sixth hour darkness came over the whole land until the ninth hour. And at the ninth hour Jesus cried out in a loud voice, 'Eloi, Eloi, lama sabachthani?'—which means, 'My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?'"

This was the abomination that causes desolation. This was the end of the world—God in Christ, experiencing the judgment that humanity deserved.

## The Nature of Judgment

But this was no mere act of divine vengeance or arbitrary punishment. Here is the profound mercy at the heart of the gospel: judgment began not with us, but with Jesus himself. He took upon himself the horror that we should have faced.

His mother watched from a distance as her son died. The women who had followed him and cared for his needs stood witness to his suffering. And he did this, not because he is an evil and uncaring God, but precisely because he loves humanity. He went to the cross out of love, to spare us from the judgment we deserved.

## Watch in the Present

> "But about that day or hour no one knows, not even the angels in heaven, nor the Son, but only the Father. Be on guard! Be alert! You do not know when that time will come. It's like a man going away: He leaves his house and puts his servants in charge, each with their assigned task, and tells the one at the door to keep watch. Therefore keep watch because you do not know when the owner of the house will come back—whether in the evening, or at midnight, or when the rooster crows, or at dawn. If he comes suddenly, do not let him find you sleeping. What I say to you, I say to everyone: Watch!"

For believers, the call is to stand firm and remain watchful. You will be protected on that final day.

For those who have not yet believed: Can you see the sign that the end of the world has come? Will you take advantage of the protection that Jesus brings through his judgment and love?

## The Echoes of the End

There are no more signs left to come. What we experience now are echoes—reverberations of that final judgment at the cross. Like a dying sun that does not disappear immediately but expands into a superheated supernova before collapsing into a dwarf star, taking a very long time to wind down, so too does this world gradually fizzle out. Yet make no mistake—it is already the end of the world, even if the echoes have been repeating for thousands of years and throughout our entire lifetimes.

We become numbed to these echoes, thinking "this is just the way the world is" rather than recognizing its death throes. But once in a while, reminders pierce through our distractions. When a loved one dies, the end of the world becomes suddenly, acutely real. Death for those who pass is truly the end of their world. And for those left behind, such losses awaken us to ask the deepest questions: What is wrong with this world? When will it truly end?

## Conclusion

When was the end of the world? It was when Jesus died and sentenced this world to judgment. Scholars calculate this occurred in AD 33—approximately 1,981 years ago, remarkably close to this very weekend when many observe Passover and remember Christ's resurrection.

> "At that time people will see the Son of Man coming in clouds with great power and glory. And he will send his angels and gather his elect from the four winds, from the ends of the earth to the ends of the heavens."

Jesus brings judgment, and he started it upon himself. He did this for you, out of love, so that death need not be the end of us. Cling to him.
