# Hebrews 12:1-3

**Title:** The Christian Race
**Date:** 6 July 2024
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Running the Christian Race

The Christian journey is described through many metaphors—types of soils, a building, a battle, a growing tree—each highlighting different aspects of our faith. But when it comes to persevering as a Christian, the metaphor of a race captures something essential: we are called to finish what we've started.

> Therefore, since we are surrounded by such a great cloud of witnesses, let us throw off everything that hinders and the sin that so easily entangles. And let us run with perseverance the race marked out for us. (Hebrews 12:1)

The Christian race has three key characteristics. First, there is a **marked course**—whether flags marking a marathon route or lines on a track, we run along a defined path, not aimlessly on a treadmill. Second, there is an **endpoint**—a finish line where the race concludes. Paul captured this urgency when he wrote, "I press on toward the goal for the prize of the upward call of God in Christ Jesus" (Philippians 3:14). Third, there are **rules**—we must compete fairly without shortcuts or cheating (2 Timothy 2:5).

But races are often won at the start. The beginning of the Christian journey requires two essential things: understanding the gospel and committing ourselves to Christ's work on the cross and His Word as our final authority.

---

## 1. Throw Off Everything That Hinders

> Let us throw off everything that hinders and the sin that so easily entangles.

In a race, runners wear only what's necessary—lightweight shirts and shorts, with shoes engineered to shed every possible milligram of weight. Ancient marathoners often ran naked. Every ounce of energy must go toward covering distance, not carrying weight.

**Sin entangles us** like a heavy bodysuit and frilly skirt would entangle a runner. Sin twists our minds, wastes our energy, and spoils our joy. It might be unhelpful habits, unhealthy relationships that pull us away from Christ, or the guilt of our past that keeps us looking backward instead of forward.

But the passage says more than just "throw off sin"—it says **throw off everything that hinders**. Many things are not sinful in themselves, yet they weigh us down and distract us from the race. Like the rabbit in Fables, we don't lose the race because we're slow; we lose it because we get distracted.

Consider what clutters our lives. We accumulate possessions without purpose. The average consumer today owns 120 clothing items, yet 80% go unworn. We fill our garages with layers of complexity, losing track of what we even have. Our lives become cluttered with cultural expectations—overseas holidays because everyone else goes, career paths we follow without thinking, pursuits we chase for social credit rather than genuine calling.

Kenny asks: Why do we go on overseas holidays? To enjoy God's creation? To rest? Or because everyone else is going? Australia itself offers mountains, beaches, snow, and dunes—yet we all end up in the same foreign countries, taking the same Instagram photos at the same landmarks. We follow the culture because there's social credit in it, not because it serves our faith.

**What entangles you?** When you resolve to get serious about Jesus, what holds you back? Is it sin that violates God's law, or distractions that simply weigh you down? Both must be thrown off if we are to run well.

## 2. Fix Your Eyes on Jesus

> Fixing our eyes on Jesus, the pioneer and perfecter of faith. For the joy set before him he endured the cross, scorning its shame, and sat down at the right hand of the throne of God. Consider him who endured such opposition from sinners, so that you will not grow weary and lose heart. (Hebrews 12:2-3)

Jesus is both the **pioneer**—the one who begins—and the **perfecter**—the one who finishes. He is the beginning and end of faith, and He is our example for running the race.

Jesus endured the cross and its shame because He kept His eyes on the **prize ahead**—the joy set before Him. He was willing to suffer present pain for future reward. In the same way, we endure hardship and opposition because we look toward our goal: the city of God, the New Jerusalem. Pheidippides, the Greek messenger who ran 40 kilometers to Athens to announce victory, ran toward a city. We run toward an eternal city.

In long-distance running, coaches teach runners to fix their gaze well ahead—on the horizon, not on other competitors. **We must not look at others and follow them off course.** When we look to Jesus as our goal, we gain the strength to endure opposition, shame, and weariness without growing discouraged or losing heart.

---

## 3. Run Surrounded by Witnesses

The first verse opens with a crucial phrase: "since we are surrounded by such a great cloud of witnesses." We do not run alone. The heroes of faith described in Hebrews 11—Abel, Enoch, Noah, Abraham, Moses—surround us as examples of those who persevered.

**Why does this matter?** When people around you are accountants and lawyers, you become an accountant or lawyer. When you're surrounded by business owners and property developers, those paths seem possible and natural. When you hang around people running the same race—people who get rid of sin that entangles, who finish what they start—you find courage to do the same.

Sometimes the finish line feels impossibly far away. But when you know someone else has already finished the race, you realize it's not impossible. It's not rocket science. The cloud of witnesses—people who persevered despite difficulty—reminds us that we can do this too.

---

## Reflection

**What are you throwing off?** Is it sin, or the cultural baggage and distractions that hinder your faith?

**Where are you looking?** Are you fixing your eyes on Jesus and the prize ahead, or following others and the world's expectations without thinking?

**Who surrounds you?** Are they running the same race—people who are committed to Christ, who persevere through difficulty, who finish well?

The Christian life is not a leisurely jog or a treadmill going nowhere. It is a marked race with a finish line, run according to rules, with everything that hinders stripped away, our eyes fixed on Jesus, surrounded by the example of those who have gone before.
