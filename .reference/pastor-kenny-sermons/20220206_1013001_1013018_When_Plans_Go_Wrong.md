# Genesis 13:1-18

**Title:** When Plans Go Wrong
**Date:** February 6, 2022
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Introduction: The Reality of Broken Plans

Life rarely unfolds as we envision. COVID disrupted plans for home and work. University applications were rejected. Job searches yielded no responses. Dreams for marriage and children take longer than anticipated. We're studying Abraham's life during the coming weeks—one of Scripture's great figures—and we discover that his plans, too, went terribly wrong.

## Stuck in Egypt (Genesis 12:9-20, 13:1-4)

### God's Original Promise

God called Abram with a sweeping vision:

> "Go from your country, your people and your father's household to the land I will show you. I will make you into a great nation, and I will bless you; I will make your name great, and you will be a blessing."

This promise contained three components: **land**, **offspring**, and **blessing**. Abram was meant to possess the promised land, build a great nation through his descendants, and become a channel of blessing to all peoples.

### The Crisis: Famine Strikes

> "Now there was a famine in the land, and the famine was severe."

In Scripture, famine represents a curse—the opposite of God's blessing. It symbolizes the withdrawal of God's provision: no rain, no sunshine, no fertile soil. In the promised land meant to flow with milk and honey, there was only scarcity. Abram faced an impossible situation: he couldn't establish his nation or fulfill God's promise while starving.

### A Wrong Turn: Egypt

> "Abram went down to Egypt to live there for a while because the famine was severe."

Egypt had food. The Nile ensured prosperity and abundance. It was a rich civilization with leeks, onions, and garlic. Logically, it made perfect sense to go there.

Yet Egypt carries deep theological weight in Scripture. It represents exile and slavery—later, the Israelites would cry out from "the house of slavery" in Egypt. Egypt symbolizes idolatry, filled with gods like Ra, Isis, and Osiris. It appears attractive on the surface, like a gangster's mansion—beautiful but dangerous. The prophets warned against trusting Egypt:

> "Woe to those who go down to Egypt for help... Behold, you are trusting in Egypt, that broken reed of a staff, which will pierce the hand of any man who leans on it."

Abram went to the anti-promised land, the wrong place.

### A Desperate Deception

Facing the Egyptians, Abram said to Sarai:

> "I know what a beautiful woman you are. When the Egyptians see you, they will say, 'This is his wife.' Then they will kill me but will let you live. Say you are my sister, so that I will be treated well for your sake and my life will be spared because of you."

This wasn't entirely a lie—Genesis 20 reveals they shared the same father but different mothers. Yet Abram was concealing the full truth, emphasizing only the part that served his safety. His plan worked exactly as predicted: Pharaoh's officials noticed Sarai's beauty, praised her to Pharaoh, and took her into his palace.

The problem was catastrophic. Sarai became one of Pharaoh's wives, presumably one among many concubines. How could Abraham fulfill the promise of offspring when his wife was in another man's harem?

### God Intervenes

But God's plans cannot be thwarted by human scheming:

> "The Lord inflicted serious diseases on Pharaoh and his household because of Abram's wife Sarai. So Pharaoh summoned Abram and said, 'What have you done to me? Why didn't you tell me she was your wife? Why did you say, "She is my sister," so that I took her to be my wife? Now then, here is your wife. Take her and go!' Then Pharaoh gave orders about Abram to his men, and they sent him on his way, with his wife and everything he had."

God's judgment on Pharaoh paralleled the later plagues of Exodus. Just as God would liberate His people from Egypt through divine curses, He liberated Abram and Sarai. Pharaoh, struck by inexplicable diseases, released them—and they departed not empty-handed but enriched:

> "Abram went up from Egypt to the Negev, with his wife and everything he had, and Lot went with him. Abram had become very wealthy in livestock and in silver and gold."

Abram's detour to Egypt, though sinful and fearful, did not derail God's purpose. God took matters into His own hands and brought him back to the promised land.

> "From the Negev he went from place to place until he came to Bethel, to the place between Bethel and Ai where his tent had been earlier and where he had first built an altar. There Abram called on the name of the Lord."

## Stuck Being Alone (Genesis 13:5-18)

### The Problem of Abundance

Lot, who had journeyed with Abram, became wealthy alongside him. Whatever Abram received, Lot received. They were building a dynasty together. Yet success created an unexpected crisis:

> "The land could not support them while they stayed together, for their possessions were so great that they were not able to stay together. And quarreling arose between Abram's herders and Lot's herders. The Canaanites and Perizzites were also living in the land at that time."

Too many livestock exhausted the available pasture. The blessing itself became a burden. Their partnership, once promising, fractured into conflict.

### Abram's Generous Response

Rather than assert his authority as the elder and senior partner, Abram chose peace:

> "Abram said to Lot, 'Let's not have any quarreling between you and me, or between your herders and mine, for we are close relatives. Is not the whole land before you? Let's part company. If you go to the left, I'll go to the right; if you go to the right, I'll go to the left.'"

Abram surrendered the choice. He gave Lot first pick of the land.

### Lot's Fateful Choice

> "Lot looked around and saw that the whole plain of the Jordan toward Zoar was well watered, like the garden of the Lord, like the land of Egypt. (This was before the Lord destroyed Sodom and Gomorrah.) So Lot chose for himself the whole plain of the Jordan and set out toward the east."

Lot chose by sight. The Jordan plain looked like Eden itself, fertile and green—like Egypt, a place of worldly prosperity. But Lot's vision was spiritually blind. He pitched his tents near Sodom, a city whose people "were wicked and were sinning greatly against the Lord."

### The Danger of Choosing by Sight

We make many decisions based on surface appearances rather than deeper truth. We choose spouses by physical beauty rather than character. We pursue jobs for salary rather than moral alignment. We select entertainment for excitement rather than righteousness. We pick schools for prestige rather than values. We compare ourselves to the world's standards—thinking, "Well, it's not as bad as that"—rather than measuring against God's character.

Yet Scripture calls us to a different standard:

> "We walk by faith, not by sight."

> "Faith is confidence in what we hope for and assurance about what we do not see."

Abraham walked by faith. Lot walked by sight. For Abram, losing Lot and the seemingly better land was a painful blow. But God consoled him:

> "The Lord said to Abram after Lot had parted from him, 'Look around from where you are, to the north and south, to the east and west. All the land that you see I will give to you and your offspring forever. I will make your offspring like the dust of the earth, so that if anyone could count the dust, then your offspring could be counted.'"

The promise was renewed and expanded. What Abram lost in earthly territory, he gained in eternal certainty. God confirmed:

> "Go, walk through the length and breadth of the land, for I am giving it to you."

> "Abram went to live near the great trees of Mamre at Hebron, where he pitched his tents. There he built an altar to the Lord."

---

## The Lesson: Trust Beyond Sight

Abraham's journey teaches us that our plans often fail, but God's never do. When we face hunger, like Abram in the famine, we may be tempted to flee God's promises for the security of "Egypt"—the world's substitutes for blessing. When we face loss, like Abram losing Lot, we may grieve what we can see slipping away.

Yet we possess what Abraham possessed: the faithfulness of God demonstrated supremely in Jesus. We see in Christ's sacrifice the ultimate proof of God's devotion to His people. We have no reason to doubt.

The call is to walk in obedience even when we cannot see the outcome, even when the path leads to the desert. Trust in the promises of God, build an altar to Him, and continue the journey of faith.
