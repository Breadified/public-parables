# 2 Corinthians 1:23–2:6

**Title:** Grieving Each Other  
**Date:** 27 May 2009  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Understanding the Context

Paul's correspondence with the Corinthian church reveals a painful but ultimately redemptive journey. When Paul originally planned to visit Corinth after his first letter (1 Corinthians), he was forced to delay. This wasn't indecision or broken promises—his commitment to truth ("his yes is a yes") remained firm. Rather, the Corinthian church's response to serious sin had grieved him deeply, and he needed to address this through a written rebuke before returning in person.

The passage before us emerges from a complex situation involving multiple letters, a painful personal visit, and the heart-wrenching process of pastoral discipline and reconciliation.

---

## Three Movements of Grief

### 1. The Corinthians Grieve Paul: Bad Grief

The Corinthian church faced a severe moral crisis. One man was sleeping with his stepmother—sexual immorality "of a kind that does not occur even among the pagans" (1 Corinthians 5:1). Paul had previously instructed the church to excommunicate this man, to "hand him over to Satan" for the sake of his redemption.

> **2 Corinthians 2:5** — "If anyone has caused grief, he has not so much grieved me as he has grieved all of you."

The tragedy was not only the sin itself, but the church's failure to act. Despite Paul's apostolic authority—he had founded the church—the Corinthians either defended the offender or remained passive. Some even used his opponents' arguments against him, claiming he wasn't truly an apostle.

This kind of grief is devastatingly real in ministry. We grieve when:
- Young Christians slowly drift away through unequal relationships with non-believers
- Those we've invested in prioritize busyness over faith until it becomes a permanent lifestyle
- People fall into destructive patterns—drinking, wrong companions—they can never escape
- We stand for truth, expecting those we've trained to stand with us, only to face betrayal

Paul's anguish was legitimate. To watch potential dry up, to see sin entangle someone you had placed such hope in—this causes real pain, real distress.

### 2. Paul Grieves the Corinthians: Good Grief

In response to their disobedience, Paul wrote a stern letter—a severe rebuke delivered by Titus. This wasn't the 1 Corinthians we know; scholars believe this is the "missing letter" referenced in 2 Corinthians 2:3-4. Paul wrote it "out of much affliction and anguish of heart and with many tears."

> **2 Corinthians 7:8-11** — "Even if I caused you sorrow by my letter, I do not regret it... Yet now I am happy, not because you were made sorry, but because your sorrow led you to repentance. For you became sorrowful as God intended and so were not harmed in any way by us. Godly sorrow brings repentance that leads to salvation and leaves no regret, but worldly sorrow brings death. See what this godly sorrow has produced in you: what earnestness, what eagerness to clear yourselves, what indignation, what alarm, what longing, what concern, what readiness to see justice done."

Paul drew a crucial distinction here. There are two kinds of sorrow:

**Worldly sorrow** leads to death. It's the regret of being caught, the shame of exposure, the despair of consequences. It produces nothing but destruction.

**Godly sorrow** leads to repentance and salvation. It produces something beautiful: earnestness, eagerness to correct wrongs, indignation at sin, concern for holiness, a desire to see justice done.

When the Corinthians received Paul's stern letter, their sorrow was godly sorrow. They turned. They disciplined the offender. They reconnected with Paul. This kind of grief—the kind that leads to transformation—Paul doesn't regret at all.

### 3. Paul Grieves Not: Good Love

Now we arrive at our passage. The offender has been punished. The church has repented. Paul's response is not to deepen the wound but to heal it:

> **2 Corinthians 2:6-7** — "The punishment inflicted on him by the majority is sufficient for him. Now instead, you ought to forgive and comfort him, so that he will not be overwhelmed by excessive sorrow."

There is a limit to grief, even necessary grief. If we continue to grieve someone after they've repented, we risk crushing them entirely. The goal was never destruction but restoration.

Paul extends this same mercy to the Corinthians themselves. He had planned another visit but didn't come, "in order to spare you." Why? Because he asks himself:

> **2 Corinthians 1:23-24** — "I call God as my witness that it was in order to spare you that I did not return to Corinth. Not that we lord it over your faith, but we work with you for your joy, because it is by faith you stand firm."

Paul's refusal to visit was an act of love, not rejection. He delayed his presence so he wouldn't add more pain before they had time to repent.

The heart of Paul's message to both the offender and the church is love overflowing:

> **2 Corinthians 1:4** — "For I wrote you out of great distress and anguish of heart and with many tears, not to grieve you but to let you know the depth of my love for you."

> **2 Corinthians 2:8, 10-11** — "I urge you, therefore, to reaffirm your love for him... If you forgive anyone, I also forgive him. And what I have forgiven—if there was anything to forgive—I have forgiven in the sight of Christ for your sake, in order that Satan might not outwit us. For we are not unaware of his schemes."

Paul forgives completely, not merely for the offender's sake but for the church's sake, "in order that Satan might not outwit us." Unforgiveness fractures the body of Christ and gives the enemy leverage.

## Application: When Sinners Sin

The questions are immediate and practical: When those we care for fall into sin, how do we respond? How can we speak truth without becoming hypocrites ourselves?

### Truth and Love Must Be Together

**Our Duty:** We have the responsibility to tell the truth. This is not optional. When someone we know is going astray, we must be willing to speak honestly, even when it causes grief. If we remain silent out of false compassion, we allow problems to compound, and people assume we're simply being unkind.

**Our Challenge:** But truth divorced from love becomes cruelty. Many of us are skilled at speaking hard truths—that's not our problem. Our problem is *relationship*. We critique and rebuke people to whom we haven't demonstrated genuine love. We confront before we've built trust.

Consider Paul's example. His love is overflowing throughout this entire passage. Before he disciplines, he has shown care. Before he confronts, he has proven his investment in their wellbeing.

### Questions for Leaders

As we lead and counsel others, we must ask ourselves:

- When someone under our care fails to keep their commitments or misses important meetings, what is our first response? Do we have a real relationship with them, or do we only connect when we need something from them?

- If someone is struggling financially because they've prioritized material things, do we berate them—or do we first show concern and offer help?

- If a young Christian begins dating a non-believer, do we shoot and run? Or do we first build relationship, offer counsel, and only then take necessary action?

- When we do need to rebuke or discipline, are we also actively working to help fix the problem? Are we doing this within the context of genuine relationship?

### The Power of Earned Trust

My own experience is instructive here. I find it far easier to receive rebuke when I *feel* cared for. When someone has demonstrated genuine concern for my wellbeing, I listen even to their hard words. But when I sense that a person no longer cares about me—that they're simply using me—no amount of truth in their words can penetrate my defensiveness.

**The pathway forward is trust.** When people trust that you genuinely love them, they will almost always listen.

So earn that trust:
- Look after your people well
- Buy them food
- Give them lifts
- Call them
- Tell them that you love them

In a culture increasingly hostile to correction and authority, trust is the currency that makes all pastoral intervention possible. Build it first. Speak truth within it. Then watch how grace transforms lives.
