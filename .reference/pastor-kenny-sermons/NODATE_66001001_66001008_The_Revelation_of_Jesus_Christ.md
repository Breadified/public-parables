# Revelation 1:1-8

**Title:** The Revelation of Jesus Christ
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Understanding Revelation

### Common Perceptions
Many approach Revelation with particular interpretive frameworks:
- **Historicist view:** The symbols outline the history of Western Europe, extending to Christ's return
- **Futurist view:** Focused exclusively on end-times events and the final Armageddon

It's crucial to remember that Revelation is fundamentally about Jesus. When we encounter the strange symbolism ahead, we must not lose sight of this central focus.

## Structure: Title and Letter Formalities

Revelation 1:1-8 divides naturally into two sections: a title (verses 1-3) and the formalities of a letter (verses 4-8).

### The Title (1:1-3)

The revelation flows through a divine chain: God reveals concerning Jesus Christ, an angel (messenger) communicates it, John receives it, and the content reaches the servants of God—all about what must soon take place.

**The key takeaway:** This letter is about Jesus. Don't lose that focus.

### Understanding Genre

When reading any document, we must recognize its genre. Revelation is a letter, and this recognition shapes how we interpret it. Genre gives us the framework for understanding the author's intent.

## The Letter: From and To Whom (1:4-8)

**Addressed to:** The seven churches

**Written by:** John (most commentators identify him as the apostle) along with greetings from:
- God, "him who is, and who was and who is to come"
- The Spirit, represented as "seven spirits before his throne"
- Jesus, described with titles that unfold his character

### Jesus Described (1:5b-8)

The passage gives Jesus a "big spiel" that reveals who he is:

> The faithful witness, the firstborn from the dead, and the ruler of the kings of the earth.

**His work for us:**
- He loves us
- He freed us from our sins by his blood

**His position:**
- He made us to be a kingdom and priests to serve our God (echoing Exodus 19:6 and Zechariah 3)
- He is coming in the clouds (Daniel 7 imagery)
- Even those who pierced him will see him (Zechariah 12)

**His identity:** The passage identifies Jesus with God himself, using the language of Alpha and Omega—the beginning and the end.

## The Purpose of Revelation

Revelation is not a manual for predicting nuclear wars or geopolitical conflicts. Rather, it is a letter designed to comfort suffering Christians by revealing that Jesus—the crucified and risen one—holds all authority in heaven and on earth.

The comfort extends to us through understanding that the one in control of history is Jesus Christ. We are not abandoned to the chaos of this world. There is a reality beyond what we see, and Jesus has overcome.

# Revelation 1:9-20

**Title:** The Vision of the Living One
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## John's Circumstance

John writes from exile on the island of Patmos, enduring suffering and persecution for the word of God. Yet in the midst of this affliction, he receives a vision that will transform his fear into faith.

He experiences:
- Suffering for his testimony to Jesus
- Patient endurance in exile
- Persecution because of the word of God

## The Vision of the Son of Man (1:12-20)

### His Appearance (1:12-16)

John sees a figure standing among seven golden lampstands (representing the churches):

> Dressed in a robe reaching down to his feet and with a golden sash around his chest. His head and hair were white like wool, as white as snow; his eyes were like blazing fire. His feet were like bronze glowing in a furnace, and his voice was like the sound of rushing waters. In his right hand he held seven stars, and out of his mouth came a sharp double-edged sword. His face was like the sun shining in all its brilliance.

This description draws heavily on Old Testament imagery of God (compare Daniel 7:9-10), yet it's applied to the risen Jesus. He is the God-man who holds all power.

### His Identity and Authority (1:17-20)

The figure identifies himself:

> I am the First and the Last. I am the Living One; I was dead, and now look, I am alive for ever and ever! And I hold the keys of death and Hades.

The keys of death and Hades are the ultimate symbol of authority—he has conquered death itself.

## Why Fear? Why Comfort?

John falls at the figure's feet in fear, likely terrified by this overwhelming presence and perhaps by thoughts of death itself. But this very figure is the one who defeated death. He holds power over the ultimate threat.

**The comfort for John—and for us:**
Jesus has died and risen again. He now holds dominion over death and Hades. For those who are friends with the Living One, there is nothing to fear from death itself. As the saying goes, "If death cannot take us, we have nothing to fear but fear itself."

## Application

Jesus's death and resurrection comfort us in our suffering. When we face persecution, loss, or the fear of death, we remember that we know the one who holds the keys. We are not abandoned. Our suffering is witnessed by the one who suffered first and triumphed.

This vision reminds us to pray through our deepest fears—whether of physical suffering, loss of friendship, rejection, financial ruin, or death itself. We have been given a vision of the Living One who invites us to trust him completely.

# Revelation 2-3

**Title:** Letters to the Seven Churches
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Overview: Jesus Speaks to His Churches

Throughout these chapters, the risen Jesus addresses seven churches, each letter opening with a description of Jesus drawn from the previous visions. Each description is perfectly tailored to the specific needs of that church.

Remember: Revelation is all about Jesus. As we study these letters, notice how the characteristics of Jesus directly address the struggles, strengths, and warnings unique to each community.

## The Seven Churches

### Ephesus (2:1-7)
**Jesus described as:** The one who holds the seven stars and walks among the seven lampstands

**Strengths:** This church demonstrates remarkable faithfulness—hard work, perseverance, intolerance of wickedness, testing of false apostles, and patient endurance in hardship. They have not grown weary.

**The problem:** Yet they have forsaken their first love. They have fallen from their original height.

**The call:** Repent and return to the works you did at first. Otherwise, Jesus will remove their lampstand—their existence as a church.

**The promise:** To those who overcome, the right to eat from the tree of life.

### Smyrna (2:8-11)
**Jesus described as:** The First and the Last, who died and came to life again

**Their reality:** Affliction, poverty, and slander. They are suffering.

**The encouragement:** Do not be afraid of suffering. The devil will test you, but be faithful. Jesus, who died and rose again, knows your pain.

**The promise:** You will not be hurt by the second death. Your faithfulness now secures your eternity.

### Pergamum (2:12-17)
**Jesus described as:** The one who has the sharp double-edged sword

**Their challenge:** They remain true in a spiritually hostile environment, refusing to renounce faith in Jesus even while living among pagan practices.

**The danger:** Yet some in the church hold to teachings of Balaam—participating in idol food and sexual immorality. Others follow Nicolaitan teachings.

**The call:** Repent, or Jesus will come to fight against them with the sword of his mouth.

**The promise:** Hidden manna and a white stone with a new name—intimacy with Jesus and a new identity.

### Thyatira (2:18-29)
**Jesus described as:** The one whose eyes are like blazing fire and whose feet are like burnished bronze

**Their strengths:** Deeds, love, faith, service, and perseverance. They are doing more now than before.

**The problem:** Yet they tolerate Jezebel, a false prophetess who teaches sexual immorality and idol food. This is serious.

**The consequence:** Jezebel will suffer, and those who follow her will face judgment. Children will die. Each will be judged according to their deeds.

**The call to those faithful:** Hold on to what you have.

**The promise:** Authority over the nations and the morning star—a radiant future with Jesus.

### Sardis (3:1-6)
**Jesus described as:** The one who holds the seven spirits and seven stars

**The verdict:** You have a reputation for life, but you are dead. A few remain with unsoiled clothes, but most of your deeds are incomplete.

**The call:** Wake up! Strengthen what remains and is about to die. Remember what you have received and heard; obey it and repent.

**The warning:** If you do not wake up, Jesus will come like a thief.

**The promise:** Those who overcome will walk with Jesus in white, their names never blotted from the book of life, and Jesus will acknowledge them before God.

### Philadelphia (3:7-13)
**Jesus described as:** The Holy and True One, who holds the key of David and determines who enters and who is shut out

**Their reality:** They have little strength, yet they have kept Jesus's word and have not denied his name.

**The call:** Hold on to what you have.

**The promise:** A pillar in God's temple, permanently at home with God. Jesus will write on them the name of God and the name of Jesus's new city. They will have a permanent place.

### Laodicea (3:14-22)
**Jesus described as:** The Amen, the faithful and true witness, the ruler of God's creation

**The indictment:** You are neither cold nor hot, but lukewarm. You think you are rich and have need of nothing, but you are wretched, pitiful, poor, blind, and naked.

**The call:** Repent! Jesus stands at the door and knocks. If you open, he will come in and eat with you.

**The promise:** Those who overcome will sit with Jesus on his throne, just as Jesus sat with the Father on his throne.

## Key Patterns Across All Letters

**For churches that are sinning:** Repent and turn away from the sin.

**For churches that are doing things right:** Hold on; do not fall away or grow complacent.

**For all churches:** "He who has ears to hear, let him hear." Listen to what the Spirit is saying.

## The Purpose: Comfort for Suffering Christians

In each letter, notice how the description of Jesus directly addresses the church's struggle. 

- To the suffering church of Smyrna, Jesus is revealed as the one who conquered death.
- To the church tempted by idolatry, Jesus appears with eyes of blazing fire—nothing is hidden from him.
- To the lukewarm church, Jesus is the faithful witness—he alone is truly worthy.

The characteristics of Jesus are not abstract doctrine but comfort for real people in real pain. When we understand that Jesus holds the keys of death, that he walks among his churches, that he sees all our deeds and knows our hearts, we find the courage to endure, to repent, to hold on.

## Application

Who are we compared to these seven churches? Which struggles resonate with our own? Do we face the temptation of Smyrna to compromise under pressure, or the complacency of Laodicea? Are we like Ephesus, doing much but losing our love for Jesus?

The call to each church is the call to us: Listen to what the Spirit is saying. The world is not all there is. Jesus has overcome, and he calls us to overcome with him.

# Revelation 4

**Title:** The Throne of God
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Vision of Heaven's Throne Room

John is taken up to heaven and sees a vision of cosmic worship. At the center stands a throne—the throne of God—and around it, the twenty-four elders bow in worship.

The scene unfolds with overwhelming imagery:
- A throne established in heaven
- One seated on the throne, surrounded by a rainbow and brilliant light
- Twenty-four elders clothed in white, wearing crowns of gold
- Lightning, thunder, and seven lamps burning before the throne
- A sea of glass like crystal before the throne
- Four living creatures full of eyes, surrounding the throne

This vision is deliberately strange and overwhelming—the weirdness itself points us to the specialness of what John is seeing. Compare this with Ezekiel's vision in Ezekiel 1, which contains similar strange imagery. These are visions of heaven itself, using earthly language to describe a reality that transcends ordinary experience.

## The Meaning of the Throne

**What the throne represents:** Power, authority, and the center of all reality.

**The qualities of its occupant:** He is worthy of all honor, all worship, all obedience. Everything that exists was created by him and for him.

## How He Is Treated

The elders bow down. The four living creatures never rest, day and night saying:

> Holy, holy, holy is the Lord God Almighty, who was, and is, and is to come.

And the elders cast their crowns before the throne, saying:

> You are worthy, our Lord and God, to receive glory and honor and power, for you created all things, and by your will they were created and have their being.

## Why This Worship? Why This Honor?

The reason is given in verse 11: He is worthy. He is worthy because he created all things. He alone has the right to receive all glory and honor and power.

## The Symbolism

- **Four living creatures:** All creation—the wild beasts, the domestic animals, the birds, and mankind—all worshiping together
- **Twenty-four elders:** God's people, the redeemed (12 tribes + 12 apostles), representing the whole people of God across all ages
- **Worship:** Paying homage through the act of bowing down, recognizing God's supreme authority

## What This Vision Says

In summary, this vision displays God in heaven receiving all honor. It shows us the reality beyond what our eyes can see. While we cannot physically bow to God as the elders do, this vision teaches us how we ought to treat him.

**How should we treat God?**

We should treat him with obedience and trust. We should recognize his authority over all things. We should surrender our will to his. We should acknowledge that he alone is worthy.

## Application

Name one area of your life where God is not currently God. Where are you still trying to maintain control? Where do you refuse to bow? 

Repent and pray. Bring that area under his lordship. The God who created all things and holds all power deserves nothing less than your complete submission and trust.

# Revelation 5

**Title:** The Lamb Takes the Scroll
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Four Scenes of Expanding Worship

The chapter unfolds in four movements, each beginning with "Then I saw":

**Scene 1:** John sees one sitting on the throne holding a scroll sealed with seven seals. The question is asked: Who is worthy to open the scroll? No one in heaven or on earth can do it. John weeps.

**Scene 2:** Then he sees a Lamb, looking as if it has been slain, standing in the center of the throne. The Lamb takes the scroll from the one on the throne.

**Scene 3:** When the Lamb takes the scroll, the twenty-four elders and the four living creatures fall down before the Lamb, holding harps and bowls of incense, singing a new song: "You are worthy to take the scroll and to open its seals, because you were slain, and with your blood you purchased for God persons from every tribe and language and people and nation."

**Scene 4:** Then John hears the voice of many angels—thousands upon thousands—surrounding the throne and the living creatures and the elders, saying in a loud voice: "Worthy is the Lamb, who was slain, to receive power and wealth and wisdom and strength and honor and glory and praise!" And every creature in heaven and on earth and under the earth and on the sea joins the song.

## The Focus

The focus of all four scenes is the Lamb—and the people paying homage to him. Jesus is worthy.

## What Has Changed

**From scene 1 to scene 4:** The numbers expand. What began with one weeping man grows to the elders and creatures, then to myriads of angels, then to every creature in creation. The worship spreads and multiplies.

**From Revelation 4 to Revelation 5:** A new person appears on God's throne—not replacing God, but standing at the center beside him. A new song is sung. Jesus, the Lamb who was slain, is now revealed as worthy to receive all that the Father receives.

## How We Have Been Changed

The most revolutionary change is personal. Look at verse 9:

> You have made them to be a kingdom and priests to serve our God.

**We are now priests.** There are no longer holy men and laypeople—everyone is holy. We mediate the gospel to non-Christians. We take care of one another by praying to God on behalf of our brothers and sisters. This is priestly work.

We have been changed from spectators into priests, from outsiders into the kingdom, from separated from God into intimately connected to him through the Lamb.

## The Lamb's Greatness

What has the Lamb accomplished? Everything:
- He was slain, taking our sins upon himself
- He rose from the dead, demonstrating his power over death
- He was exalted to the throne, receiving all authority
- He purchased people for God from every nation
- He made them a kingdom of priests
- He transformed worship itself—now all creation sings his praise

## Application: From Impossible Habits to New Life

Just as we cannot change our deepest, most entrenched habits through willpower alone, we cannot transform ourselves spiritually. But the Lamb who was slain for us has already won the victory. 

When we trust in him, we are made new. We are priests. We are part of his kingdom. We have been redeemed.

**How can we change our ungodly habits?** By remembering what the Lamb has done for us and by trusting in his finished work. As we grow in understanding of his love and sacrifice, we are transformed.

**How can we help others?** By pointing them to the Lamb. By praying for them as priests. By living as those who have been ransomed and made new. By inviting them to join in the song: "Worthy is the Lamb!"
