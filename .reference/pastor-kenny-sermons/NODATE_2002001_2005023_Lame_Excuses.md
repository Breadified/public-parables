# Exodus 2:1–5:23

**Title:** Lame Excuses  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Introduction

We've all heard excuses—especially from children or those we rely on. "I'll do it later." "I don't have the money." "I forgot." "I didn't know." "It's too late." "I don't have the time." The list goes on, and they're frustrating to hear.

This morning, we're going to examine excuses from two very different people: Moses and Pharaoh, both refusing to obey God's commands. Keep your Bibles open as we follow the text closely.

---

## Moses' Excuses

### The Command

God gives Moses a clear instruction in Exodus 3:10:

> "So now, go. I am sending you to Pharaoh to bring my people the Israelites out of Egypt."

But Moses responds with excuses—lots of them.

### Excuse #1: "Who Am I?"

**Exodus 3:11**

> "But Moses said to God, 'Who am I, that I should go to Pharaoh and bring the Israelites out of Egypt?'"

Moses begins with false humility: "I am nobody. I'm not qualified for this task." But God's response cuts to the heart of the matter. It's not about who Moses is—it's about who God is.

**Exodus 3:12**

> "And God said, 'I will be with you. And this will be the sign to you that it is I who have sent you: When you have brought the people out of Egypt, you will worship God on this mountain.'"

The answer to inadequacy is God's presence.

### Excuse #2: "Who Are You?"

**Exodus 3:13–14**

> "Moses said to God, 'Suppose I go to the Israelites and say to them, "The God of your fathers has sent me to you," and they ask me, "What is his name?" Then what shall I tell them?' God said to Moses, 'I AM WHO I AM. This is what you are to say to the Israelites: "I AM has sent me to you."'"

Moses shifts the question: "Okay, maybe it's not about me—but who are you?" God's response is profound. "I AM WHO I AM"—Yahweh reveals His self-existent nature. He is the God who will do what He said He will do. His character is defined by faithfulness and the fulfillment of His promises.

### Excuse #3: "They Won't Believe Me!"

**Exodus 4:1**

> "Moses answered, 'What if they do not believe me or listen to me and say, "The LORD did not appear to you"?'"

Now Moses worries about credibility. "Even if I go, they won't believe me. They'll say I made this all up." So God gives him three miraculous signs to prove His authority.

**The Staff** (4:2–5)

> "Then the LORD said to him, 'What is that in your hand?' 'A staff,' he replied. The LORD said, 'Throw it on the ground.' Moses threw it on the ground and it became a snake, and he ran from it. Then the LORD said to him, 'Reach out your hand and take it by the tail.' So Moses reached out and took hold of the snake and it turned back into a staff in his hand."

**The Leprous Hand** (4:6–7)

> "Then the LORD said, 'Put your hand inside your cloak.' So Moses put his hand into his cloak, and when he took it out, it was leprous, like snow. 'Now put it back into your cloak,' he said. So Moses put his hand back into his cloak, and when he took it out, it was restored, like the rest of his flesh."

**Water into Blood** (4:8–9)

> "Then the LORD said, 'If they do not believe you or pay attention to the first miraculous sign, they may believe the second. But if they do not believe these two signs or listen to you, take some water from the Nile and pour it on the dry ground. The water you take from the river will become blood on the ground.'"

God chooses signs that use what's readily available: the staff in Moses' hand, and the Nile he'll encounter in Egypt. But the point isn't the specific signs—it's that God provides not one, not two, but *three* physical demonstrations of His power. Surely Moses has no more excuses now?

### Excuse #4: "I Have a Heavy Tongue"

**Exodus 4:10**

> "Moses said to the LORD, 'O Lord, I have never been eloquent, neither in the past nor since you have spoken to your servant. I am slow of speech and tongue.'"

Moses is literally saying he has a "heavy tongue"—perhaps a stutter or speech impediment. Whether this is real or perceived, Moses uses it as his next excuse. (Truthfully, unless we've had lots of practice, most of us struggle with public speaking!)

At this point, God's patience begins to fray.

**Exodus 4:11–12**

> "The LORD said to him, 'Who gave man his mouth? Who makes him deaf or mute? Who gives him sight or makes him blind? Is it not I, the LORD? Now go; I will help you speak and will teach you what to say.'"

God is frustrated because Moses is circling back to his first excuse. This question echoes "Who am I?" from 3:11. Moses keeps questioning his ability to be God's messenger, forgetting that it's Yahweh who gives mouth and speech, who gives ears and understanding. It is by Yahweh's power alone that Pharaoh—or anyone else—will listen. And Yahweh promises to help Moses speak.

### Excuse #5: The Real Problem

**Exodus 4:13**

> "But Moses said, 'O Lord, please send someone else to do it.'"

Finally, Moses drops the pretense. It's not about his identity. It's not about God's identity. It's not about Pharaoh's response. The real issue is this: *I just don't want to do it.*

This is where the mask falls away and we see the heart of the matter.

### God's Response: Provision Through Aaron

**Exodus 4:14–17**

> "Then the LORD's anger burned against Moses... and he said, 'What about your brother, Aaron the Levite? I know he can speak well. He is already on his way to meet you, and his heart will be glad when he sees you. You shall speak to him and put words in his mouth; I will help both of you speak and will teach you what to do. He will speak to the people for you, and it will be as if he were your mouth and as if you were God to him. But take this staff in your hand so you can perform miraculous signs with it.'"

God's anger burns, but He doesn't abandon Moses. Instead, He provides Aaron as a mediator. The mediator needs a mediator. But here's an important question: Why doesn't God simply go through Aaron, who seems far more competent? Perhaps because God delights in demonstrating His power through weakness. He wants the most powerful man on earth—Pharaoh—to be confronted by a stammering Hebrew slave. That speaks volumes about God's character.

With the way clear for Moses to depart—his father-in-law approving, Egypt safe to travel—we might think we've heard all the excuses. But we haven't. Moses isn't the only one with reasons to disobey God.

## Pharaoh's Excuses

### The Command

In Chapter 5, Moses and Aaron approach Pharaoh with God's word:

**Exodus 5:1**

> "Afterward Moses and Aaron went to Pharaoh and said, 'This is what the LORD, the God of Israel, says: Let my people go, so that they may hold a festival to me in the desert.'"

But Pharaoh has his own list of excuses.

### Excuse #1: "Who Is the Lord?"

**Exodus 5:2**

> "Pharaoh said, 'Who is the LORD, that I should obey him and let Israel go? I do not know the LORD and I will not let Israel go.'"

Pharaoh's first excuse is simple: he doesn't know this God and sees no reason to obey Him. Moses and Aaron respond:

**Exodus 5:3**

> "Then they said, 'The God of the Hebrews has met with us. Now let us take a three-day journey into the desert to offer sacrifices to the LORD our God, or he may strike us with plagues or with the sword.'"

### Excuse #2: "You're Too Busy"

**Exodus 5:4–5**

> "But the king of Egypt said, 'Moses and Aaron, why are you taking the people away from their labor? Get back to your work! Then Pharaoh said, 'Look, the people of the land are now numerous, and you are stopping them from working.'"

Pharaoh's second excuse: the slaves have too much work to do; they're indispensable. But notice what Pharaoh does next—he doesn't actually believe his own excuse.

**Exodus 5:6–9**

> "That same day Pharaoh gave this order to the slave drivers and foremen in charge of the people: 'You are no longer to supply the people with straw for making bricks; let them go and gather their own straw. But require them to make the same number of bricks as before; don't reduce the quota. They are lazy; that is why they are crying out, "Let us go and sacrifice to our God." Make the work harder for the men so that they keep working and pay no attention to lies.'"

Pharaoh's response is to *increase* their burden, contradicting his claim that they're already too busy.

### Excuse #3: "It's Moses' Problem"

The third excuse comes not from Pharaoh directly, but from the foremen he appointed:

**Exodus 5:14–15**

> "The Israelite foremen appointed by Pharaoh's slave drivers were beaten and were asked, 'Why didn't you meet your quota of bricks yesterday or today, as before?' When they left Pharaoh, they found Moses and Aaron waiting to meet them, and they said, 'May the LORD look upon you and judge you! You have made us a stench to Pharaoh and his officials and have put a sword in their hand to kill us.'"

The foremen blame Moses for their suffering. They've become intermediaries of Pharaoh's cruelty, passing judgment on the very man trying to free them.

### Excuse #4: "It's God's Problem"

And now Moses himself makes an excuse—blaming God:

**Exodus 5:22–23**

> "Moses returned to the LORD and said, 'O Lord, why have you brought trouble upon this people? Is this why you sent me? Ever since I went to Pharaoh to speak in your name, he has brought trouble upon this people, and you have not rescued your people at all.'"

Moses, discouraged by Pharaoh's response and the people's suffering, turns his frustration on God. "Why did you send me if this is the result?"

## The Judgment for Disobedience

At this critical moment, we return to Exodus 4:14: "Then the LORD's anger burned against Moses."

What does this anger mean? We find clues in the passage that follows, one of the most difficult sections in Scripture.

### Pharaoh's Judgment

**Exodus 4:21–23**

> "The LORD said to Moses, 'When you return to Egypt, see that you perform before Pharaoh all the wonders I have given you the power to do. But I will harden his heart so that he will not let the people go. Then say to Pharaoh, "This is what the LORD says: Israel is my firstborn son, and I told you, 'Let my son go, so he may worship me.' But you refused to let him go; so I will kill your firstborn son."'"

Pharaoh's judgment is severe: his firstborn son will die because of his refusal to obey God.

### Moses' Judgment: A Difficult Passage

But the judgment extends to Moses as well. The text that follows is deeply troubling and requires careful attention:

**Exodus 4:24–26**

> "At a lodging place on the way, the LORD met him and was about to kill him. But Zipporah took a flint knife, cut off her son's foreskin and touched his feet with it. 'Surely you are a bridegroom of blood to me,' she said. So the LORD let him alone."

The original Hebrew is ambiguous here—it doesn't specify who the LORD met. It could be Moses or his son Gershom. The context suggests the LORD was about to kill Gershom because of Moses' stubbornness—which mirrors Pharaoh's stubbornness.

Notice the parallels with the coming Passover:

- At the Passover, the Angel of Death will pass indiscriminately over the firstborns of both Egyptians and Israelites. Both are under the same judgment.
- At the Passover, the Israelites are spared because of blood on their doorposts. Here, Zipporah takes the bloody foreskin and touches the feet (possibly Moses' feet) with it. The blood acts as atonement, just as it will in the Passover.
- Circumcision appears here as a sign of covenant belonging. In Joshua's time, circumcision was required before celebrating the Passover.

Zipporah's act saves her son's life. The "bridegroom of blood" she speaks of refers to circumcision—the sign of the covenant.

This difficult passage illustrates the seriousness of God's call. Disobedience carries real consequences, even for those God has chosen to use mightily.

After this judgment, God seeks out Aaron:

**Exodus 4:27–28**

> "The LORD said to Aaron, 'Go into the desert to meet Moses.' So he met Moses at the mountain of God and kissed him. Then Moses told Aaron everything the LORD had sent him to say, and also about all the miraculous signs he had commanded him to perform."

The big idea is stark: **There are consequences for disobeying God's call to service and obedience.**

## Application: Our Excuses Today

As Christians, we also have a calling from God. It's probably not to deliver a nation from slavery—Moses and the Israelites are a unique case in God's redemptive history. But Jesus calls us to something equally serious.

### The Call to Believe: Luke 14:16–24

Jesus illustrates this through a parable about a great banquet:

**Luke 14:16–20**

> "Jesus replied: 'A certain man was preparing a great banquet and invited many guests. At the time of the banquet he sent his servant to tell those who had been invited, "Come, for everything is now ready." But they all alike began to make excuses. The first said, "I have just bought a field, and I must go and see it. Please excuse me." Another said, "I have just bought five yoke of oxen, and I'm on my way to try them out. Please excuse me." Still another said, "I just got married, so I can't come."'"

The invited guests make excuses—a field to see, oxen to test, a wedding to attend. Their priorities have shifted away from the master's invitation. And what is the consequence?

**Luke 14:24**

> "I tell you, not one of those men who were invited will get a taste of my banquet."

Jesus is calling us to believe in Him and respond to His invitation to the heavenly banquet. But many of us make excuses. Don't lose out on this feast. Make sure you believe and follow Jesus.

### The Call to Serve: Galatians 5:13–14

**Galatians 5:13–14**

> "You, my brothers, were called to be free. But do not use your freedom to indulge the sinful nature; rather, serve one another in love. The entire law is summed up in a single command: 'Love your neighbor as yourself.'"

Jesus also calls us to serve Him by serving one another. Yet we keep finding excuses not to serve.

#### Excuse #1: False Modesty ("Who Am I?")

We echo Moses: "I'm not good enough. I don't know the Bible well enough. I don't have the skills to help." This echoes Moses' first excuse. But sometimes this false humility is just a way to avoid serving.

Here's the reality: if a church leader has asked you for help, they obviously believe you're capable. There are always ways to overcome limitations—we can study Scripture, we can develop skills. The answer isn't "I'm no good"; it's "Let me try, and let me grow." God will enable you to love and serve others. Trust that.

#### Excuse #2: "I'm Too Busy"

Another popular excuse. "I'll serve when I have more free time."

But here's the truth: **we will always be busy if we let ourselves be busy.**

I see school children every week, and they're perpetually busy. From Year 4 onwards, there are OC tests, then selective school entrance exams ("I'll serve in high school after I get into the right school"). Then the HSC looms large ("I'll serve after the HSC"). Then university exams ("I'll serve when I finish my degree"). Then work and career pressures ("I'll serve when I retire"). And when retirement comes? ("I've earned my rest; now I'm traveling the world").

We will always find reasons to be busy. The question is: **Are you serious about following God's command?** If you are, you must prioritize. You must give up some things so you're not consumed by busyness.

#### Excuse #3: Poor Time Management

Here's another hard truth: we say we have no time, but we make time for TV dramas, anime, parties, movies, and shopping. We're so busy, yet we all watched MasterChef. We're so busy, yet when there's a 50% sale, we're suddenly free. We're so busy, but we scroll Facebook for hours a day. We're so busy, yet we find weeks for holidays.

The issue isn't that we have no time. It's that we use our time poorly.

### The Parable of the Talents: Matthew 25:21–30

Jesus tells a parable that puts all our excuses in perspective:

**Matthew 25:14–23**

> "Again, it will be like a man going on a journey, who called his servants and entrusted his property to them. To one he gave five talents, to another two talents, and to another one talent, each according to his ability. Then he went on his journey. The man who had received the five talents went at once and put his money to work and gained five more. So also, the one with the two talents gained two more. But the man who had received the one talent went off, dug a hole in the ground and hid his master's money."

When the master returns, he commends those who used their talents faithfully:

**Matthew 25:21, 23**

> "'His master replied, "Well done, good and faithful servant! You have been faithful with a few things; I will put you in charge of many things. Come and share your master's happiness!"'

But for the one who buried his talent:

**Matthew 25:24–30**

> "'Then the man who had received the one talent came. "Master," he said, "I knew that you are a hard man, harvesting where you have not sown and gathering where you have not scattered seed. So I was afraid and went out and hid your talent in the ground. See, here is what belongs to you." His master replied, "You wicked, lazy servant! So you knew that I harvest where I have not sown and gather where I have not scattered seed? Well then, you should have put my money on deposit with the bankers, so that when I returned I would have received it back with interest. Take the talent from him and give it to the one who has the ten talents. For everyone who has will be given more, and he will have an abundance. Whoever does not have, even what he has will be taken from him. And throw that worthless servant outside, into the darkness, where there will be weeping and gnashing of teeth."'"

The servant's excuse—fear—becomes his ruin. He does nothing with what he's been given.

---

## Conclusion

The pattern is clear throughout Scripture: **God calls, and excuses have consequences.** Moses learned this the hard way. Pharaoh learned it through plague after plague. The unfaithful servant in the parable lost everything.

The question before us is simple: **What response do you want from God?**

Do you want to hear "Throw them outside into the darkness"? Or do you want to hear "Well done, good and faithful servant"?

Jesus' yoke is easy to bear. His burden is light. Serving Him is not a hardship—it's a privilege. Whatever excuses we're tempted to make, whatever reasons we think we have to delay or decline, the answer is the same: commit to serving your Master faithfully and cheerfully. The rewards are eternal.
