## Living Good Lives Among the Pagans

Peter presents a unique strategy for Christians living as aliens in a hostile world. Rather than rebellion or retreat, he calls us to a counter-cultural approach: living such visibly good lives that our moral difference becomes undeniable.

> 1 Peter 2:11-12
> Dear friends, I urge you, as aliens and strangers in the world, to abstain from sinful desires, which war against your soul. Live such good lives among the pagans that, though they accuse you of doing wrong, by observing your good deeds they may come to glorify God on the day he visits us.

### What the "Good Life" Actually Means

The good life Peter calls us to is not the world's version of success. It's not the Australian dream of leisure by the beach, nor the Asian dream of professional achievement and material comfort. It's not displaying worldly prosperity to impress others.

**The good life is fundamentally moral.** It's a life that abstains from sinful desires—not storing up resentment, disdain, or complaints, but living with integrity that others can see and verify.

This goodness must be visible and public. Even our personal piety—like Daniel's prayer—happened in plain sight. Our moral stances and actions can be observed by those around us. When we live differently from the world in the everyday decisions that come up in conversation—what we study, what we watch, what we buy—we inevitably create opportunities for inquiry.

### The Strategy: From Silence to Gospel

Peter's strategy unfolds in stages:

**First, our good works silence ignorant accusations.** While some will continue to accuse us of doing wrong, our consistent moral behavior eventually makes their slander ineffective. Good works confound critics by rendering their accusations implausible. When someone claims Christians are hypocritical, hypocrisy becomes harder to sustain.

> 1 Peter 2:15
> For it is God's will that by doing good you should silence the ignorant talk of foolish men.

**Second, our different lives create openings for the gospel.** The world's confusion about why we live as we do becomes the doorway for spiritual conversation.

> 1 Peter 3:15
> But in your hearts set apart Christ as Lord. Always be prepared to give an answer to everyone who asks you to give the reason for the hope that you have. But do this with gentleness and respect.

Good deeds are not an end in themselves. The danger of letting only our actions speak is that people assume goodness is the path to Christianity itself, not realizing we live morally because we've already been saved, not to earn salvation. **We must eventually speak.** The world needs words to understand the gospel.

### We Must Be Different to Be Heard

Our problem is that we are too much like the world. We do the same Friday night activities, wear the same clothes, spend money on the same things, study the same subjects, have the same ambitions. What's visibly different about us that would warrant a question?

**We are aliens and that should mean we do things obviously differently.**

Consider how visible contrast creates conversation:

- Taking only a weekend for a wedding when you could have stayed longer—people wonder why you don't maximize the trip, opening dialogue about priorities and the resurrection.
- Having multiple children in an age of materialism—naturally generates questions about how you view God's blessing and what life is truly about.
- Professional success yet modest living—shocks people and becomes a conversation starter about values.
- Inviting neighbors to church instead of trick-or-treating—that's different enough to spark inquiry.
- Prioritizing church attendance or summer missions—these create opportunities to be different in ways people notice.

The constant question is: **Can you think of another way you could shape your life differently? What regular conversations could you redirect to reflect God's values?** Use your life to create opportunities for conversation about what matters eternally.

## Submission to Kings and Authorities

### The Foundation: Government Authority Comes from God

> 1 Peter 2:13-14
> Submit yourselves for the Lord's sake to every authority instituted among men: whether to the king, as the supreme authority, or to governors, who are sent by him to punish those who do wrong and to commend those who do right.

**Government authority is actually from God.** They are sent by the Lord to serve a purpose: punishing wrongdoing and rewarding righteousness. This echoes Romans 13:1-2, where Paul writes that all governing authorities have been established by God. Rebellion against authority is rebellion against God's design.

Even though governments are imperfect—and what's worse than a dictator is anarchy—we're called to obey the laws of our nation, kingdom, or empire. Government exists for our good.

### A Critical Qualification: God Is Bigger Than Government

Yet submission to government is not absolute submission. Verse 17 establishes crucial boundaries:

> 1 Peter 2:17
> Show proper respect to everyone: Love the brotherhood of believers, fear God, honor the king.

Notice the hierarchy: God comes first. In light of the resurrection, there is one Lord and therefore one law over all. God has created multiple institutions and authorities to govern different spheres of life—not just civil government, but families, churches, workplaces, and communities. Each has its own God-given authority and sphere.

> Ephesians 5:21-6:4 and 1 Corinthians 11:3 illustrate these different authorities: government (v.13), masters (v.18), and husbands (3:1-6).

Each institution has its own goals and methods. **None is expected to do everything.** Government cannot procreate, feed, nurture, or teach as families do. Families cannot enforce justice or protect national interests as government does. The Church cannot legislate or police as civil authorities do. This separation of powers—different institutions for different purposes—is foundational to healthy society.

**Therefore, submission is not absolute when authorities overstep their God-given boundaries.** When an institution demands what only God can grant, or commands what God forbids, we face a choice guided by biblical examples.

### When Submission Has Limits

Scripture records instances of civil disobedience rooted in submission to a higher authority:

The Hebrew midwives feared God and refused Pharaoh's command to kill Hebrew boys (Exodus 1:15-17). Shadrach, Meshach, and Abednego refused to worship the golden image despite Nebuchadnezzar's decree (Daniel 3:18-19). Daniel continued praying despite the law against it (Daniel 6:10). Peter and John told the Sanhedrin, "We must obey God rather than you" (Acts 4:19-20).

**The principle is absolute: you cannot break God's laws.** Government cannot command you to steal, murder, or lie. Government cannot demand you renounce your faith. When this happens, we say **NO** and stand firm. **But for everything else, we submit.**

Much of what government requires is arbitrary—speed limits, tax rates, building codes—not moral absolutes. **Submission means we give up our liberty, not our minds or God's laws.** We live as free people who have chosen to live as servants of God, understanding that:

> 1 Peter 2:16
> Live as free men, but do not use your freedom as a cover-up for evil; live as servants of God.

This stands against the world's approach. When authorities do things people dislike, the world revolts—burning cars, blocking streets, creating chaos. The Christian response is different: we obey through legal and democratic means if we disagree, but we don't resort to lawlessness.

When a government is elected fairly, now is the time to submit—you may not like the outcome, but you can't burn cars. If you disagree with policies, use the political process. A plebiscite on marriage law, for example, was fair and square—if the result displeases you, use democratic tools in the future, not ongoing protest.

**But submission to law does not mean changing our beliefs.** If religious liberty protections are lost and we're told we must participate in things that violate conscience, we may have to withdraw from certain industries. But if government simply restricts our liberty—making some activities illegal—we accept that restriction while maintaining our faith. **There's surprising freedom even within restrictions.**

### Two Examples of Societies Under Pressure

**China's approach:** Under communist ideology, any organization threatening social harmony faces state pressure. Christians have responded creatively—not with rebellion, but through underground family networks, one-on-one discipleship, and Scripture memorization. Yet there's also a counternarrative: many Chinese churches are legally registered organizations, and increasingly the government consults with Christian leaders to understand Western liberal democracy rooted in Christian values.

**Peter's own example:** The apostle Peter lived under Nero, one of history's most brutal persecutors. Yet he didn't organize resistance movements. According to tradition, when urged to flee Rome, Peter requested to be crucified upside-down, saying he was unworthy to die as his Master did. **His death came through faithful submission, not rebellion.** He got on with preaching the gospel and living his good life as best he could, trusting vindication to the last day.

## Submission to Masters at Work

### The Hard Case: Submitting to Harsh Authority

> 1 Peter 2:18-20
> Slaves, submit yourselves to your masters with all respect, not only to those who are good and considerate, but also to those who are harsh. For it is commendable if a man bears up under the pain of unjust suffering because he is conscious of God. But how is it to your credit if you receive a beating for doing wrong and endure it? But if you suffer for doing good and you endure it, this is commendable before God.

We must understand biblical slavery differently from the Atlantic slave trade horrors. Biblical slavery involved strict protections: slaves couldn't be abducted or kidnapped (slavery trafficking was punishable by death). Slaves had rights to rest, food, and dignity. Hebrew slaves were freed at the Jubilee. Slavery was often the solution to poverty, war, or debt—harsh by modern standards, but justified within that context.

**Peter's point is that we submit even when it's difficult.**

**Submission is to the office, not the personality.** We don't submit to harsh masters because they're morally superior—we submit because of the position they hold. This prevents us from masking our own sin behind selective obedience: "I'll obey if they're nice, but if they're difficult, I get to decide." Authority by nature makes decisions we don't like and coerces us in ways we resist. The office remains, regardless of the person holding it.

**What makes submission truly commendable is enduring unjust suffering while remaining innocent.** If you deserve punishment, that's expected. But if you're doing good and face mistreatment anyway, that reflects Christ-like character.

### The Example: Jesus' Unqualified Submission

> 1 Peter 2:21-25
> To this you were called, because Christ suffered for you, leaving you an example, that you should follow in his steps. He committed no sin, and no deceit was found in his mouth. When they hurled their insults at him, he did not retaliate; when he suffered, he made no threats. Instead, he entrusted himself to him who judges justly. He himself bore our sins in his body on the tree, so that we might die to sins and live for righteousness; by his wounds you have been healed.

Jesus made no qualifications in His submission. He didn't say, "I'll obey until they insult me." He didn't say, "I'll accept injustice until it hurts too much." He didn't retaliate or threaten. Instead, He entrusted Himself to the One who judges justly, leaving vindication to the last day.

Jesus:
- Committed no sin
- Spoke no deceit
- Did not retaliate
- Made no threats
- Entrusted Himself to God
- Died for our sins
- By His wounds, we are healed

He modeled the servant's heart we're called to have—not because we're better or because others deserve it, but because Christ was the ultimate servant who surrendered everything.

### Application: Modern Workplaces

While we're not literal slaves today, workplace relationships often carry similar dynamics. We face:

- **Contracts and commitments** that bind us to employers for set periods
- **Mortgages, debt, and family obligations** that keep us from simply leaving uncertain situations
- **Lines of work** where every employer operates similarly, making "just find another job" impractical
- **Seasons of bondage** where circumstances limit our freedom

**Do not sin to get ahead.** Don't steal supplies to compensate for unpaid overtime. Don't waste employer time on social media (research shows millions of workers spend over an hour daily on social platforms while being paid). Don't gossip or undermine colleagues.

**Honor your employers.** Even when they're unfair, when pressures are unreasonable, when the work feels thankless—the principle of submission applies. Your boss also has pressures; when you become the boss, you'll understand the complexity.

**Create opportunities for gospel conversation through consistent integrity.** Be the person who's honest about overpayment, who doesn't participate in office gossip, who shows genuine care for colleagues. This raises questions that open doors for faith conversations.

**If your employer demands you sin, escalate through proper channels or resign.** This is not rebellion—it's standing firm on God's boundaries. But the default posture is submission, not selective obedience.

## The Counter-Cultural Nature of Submission

### Why Submission Matters Today

For 200 years since the Enlightenment, we've emphasized freedom, personal rights, and individual authority. Submission has become a swear word. We've seen the collapse of authority structures:

- Teachers are now guidance counselors, afraid to lead
- Parents are friends with money, afraid to guide
- Police lose authority when afraid to act
- Governments are servants to personal goals, not leaders

**But if we don't understand authority, we won't understand God.** If we teach children to submit to no one but themselves, they won't submit to God. If we're suspicious of all authority except our own autonomy, we'll never truly follow Christ.

This matters profoundly for marriage, the next subject Peter addresses. Without understanding submission—what it is and why it matters—we can't understand healthy relationships where someone takes loving responsibility and another trusts that leadership.

### Submission is Actually About Effective Community

Submission isn't oppressive—it's practical. In any team, organization, or family, someone must make final decisions:

- **In the military,** clear chains of command save lives. When authority is confused, people die.
- **In government,** we ensure the president and vice president are never in the same place—we need continuity.
- **In sports teams,** a captain leads and players follow.
- **In families,** someone must make the hard calls when consensus isn't possible.

"Mutual submission" sounds nice theoretically, but in reality, two people in a democracy can't make decisions by vote. What happens at dinner? Do you take the vote seriously when a five-year-old votes for candy? **Someone must submit.** The alternative is chaos.

The "soft" approach—"we'll just figure it out together"—often means **no one really submits,** which leads to resentment, power struggles, and breakdown. **Submission, paradoxically, enables freedom** because everyone knows the decision-making process and can trust the system.

## The Beauty of a Gentle and Quiet Spirit

### What Submission Looks Like: The Heart

Peter moves to the household (1 Peter 3:1-7), addressing wives specifically:

> 1 Peter 3:1-4
> Wives, in the same way be submissive to your husbands so that, if any of them do not obey the word, they may be won over without words by the behavior of their wives, when they see the purity and reverence of your lives. Your beauty should not come from outward adornment, such as braided hair and the wearing of gold jewelry and fine clothes. Instead, it should be that of your inner self, the unfading beauty of a gentle and quiet spirit, which is of great worth in God's sight.

**Submission is fundamentally a matter of the heart.** It's about having a pure life that doesn't store resentment, disdain, or complaints. It's about reverence—taking God's word seriously and wanting to live it out. A gentle spirit is not nagging, manipulative, argumentative, or impatient. A quiet spirit happily accepts the husband's leadership and doesn't try to usurp it.

Peter challenges us to rethink beauty entirely. In formal seasons and work functions, we see cultural messages about what makes someone beautiful—expensive clothes, elaborate hair, jewelry, a certain body type. **But Peter says true beauty is a pure, reverent life with a gentle and quiet spirit.**

> 1 Peter 3:5
> For this is the way the holy women of the past who put their hope in God used to make themselves beautiful. They were submissive to their own husbands.

The ancient women of faith made themselves beautiful through submission and reverence. This shocks our modern ears, yet Peter presents it as self-evident truth: **submission is beautiful.**

### The Problem We Face

We've lost touch with reality about beauty. In extended families and communities, we naturally saw people age, grow frail, and die. This taught us that physical beauty is fleeting. But modern life has skewed this:

- Relatives live overseas, so we don't see them age
- We attend many weddings where brides spend fortunes on appearance, but few funerals
- We only spend time with friends our age, reinforcing narrow beauty standards
- Media shows only the youngest, skinniest people in trendy clothes
- Sisters compare themselves to each other—who's skinnier, cuter—which is foolish

**God cares about you deeply. He values your true beauty far above outward appearance.** It's okay to have boring hair, plain clothes, or an unconventional body type.

### Practical Steps for Young Women

**First, take advantage of church.** Interact across age groups. See what beauty looks like at 40, 60, 80. Let these examples ground your understanding of what truly matters.

**Second, remember that physical beauty is a gift, not an identity.** Some girls naturally enjoy styling their hair and wearing nice things, and that's fine. But know that's not where true beauty lives.

**Young men, this applies to you too: stop looking for the wrong kind of girls.** When you consistently pursue the skinniest or most physically attractive, you make it harder for your sisters to develop godly character. **Pay attention to the modest and godly.** Let them know you value who they are beyond appearance.

### The Radical Nature of Sarah

Peter points to Sarah as the model:

> 1 Peter 3:6
> Like Sarah, who obeyed Abraham and called him her master. You are her daughters if you do what is right and do not give way to fear.

**Sarah was no pushover.** She laughed at an angel from God. She kicked out Hagar when threatened. Yet she called Abraham her master—using the same word for absolute lordship.

Sarah in Galatians represents Christian freedom itself—she's the symbol of those born free in Christ. Yet **even free Sarah submitted to her husband.** Her submission wasn't forced or culturally coerced; it was her choice, reflective of her faith.

**Submission is unqualified:** not limited to big decisions, not restricted to spiritual matters, not conditional on the husband's merit or character. She submitted because of the office Abraham held, not because he was always right.

**You are Sarah's daughters if you do what is right and don't give way to fear**—fear of losing control, fear of being taken advantage of, fear of being hurt by an imperfect authority.

## Addressing the Objections

### What About Domestic Abuse?

Domestic abuse is terrible and inexcusable. **There is no excuse for abuse.** And it's not only a male problem; while physical abuse by husbands gets more attention, emotional abuse by wives—words that wound, constant nagging that erodes confidence, manipulation—is equally real and devastating.

The solution to abuse is not to remove authority; it's to **call for proper use of authority.** Like addressing police abuse—we don't replace the police or bind their hands; we demand they be better police.

**In extreme cases, there are already limits on authority:** civil law. In our society, domestic abuse leads to arrest and jail. If abused, seek police help, withdraw from fellowship temporarily, and pursue counseling. These are legitimate, God-honoring responses.

But we must not throw out the baby of husbandry with the bathwater of abuse. **The problem is not authority; it's abuse of authority.** Husbands are given unqualified responsibilities:

> Ephesians 5:27
> To present her to himself as a radiant church, without stain or wrinkle or any other blemish, but holy and blameless.

Husbands must lead their families toward godliness. They must grow their children in the Lord. They must love their wives as Christ loved the church—willing to die for them. **These are unqualified responsibilities, so the authority must be equally unqualified.**

All couples fight and hurt each other in unhealthy ways, often mimicking patterns from their families of origin. **The problem isn't authority; it's conflict resolution.** For those dating, learning to fight well—with honesty, humility, and respect—is perhaps the most important skill you can develop.

### What About Mutual Submission?

Some cite Galatians 3:28 ("neither male nor female") and Ephesians 5:21 ("submit to one another") as evidence for equal, mutual submission.

But **Galatians 3:28 speaks of salvation equality**, not role elimination. There are still distinct roles—the text doesn't mean gender disappears or slavery ends.

And **Ephesians 5:21's "mutual submission" doesn't mean symmetrical, quid pro quo submission.** It means collectively, we submit within our God-given relationships. The passage then details **asymmetrical relationships:** wives submit to husbands; children obey parents; servants obey masters. Mutuality in language doesn't require symmetry in practice.

Consider Galatians 6:2: "Carry one another's burdens." This doesn't mean everyone exchanges burdens simultaneously; it means those more able help bear the burdens of those less able. Language of mutuality can be asymmetrical.

Or Revelation 6: "So that men should slay one another" doesn't mean each killed the other at precisely the same moment. **Reality is asymmetrical.**

Symmetric mutual submission avoids the hard question: someone must submit when disagreement arises. A democracy of two doesn't work practically. Debates about every family decision waste time and create paralysis. **Someone must submit, and that's healthy, not oppressive.**

Peter writes because marriages aren't perfect. He addresses situations where one partner is non-Christian, where one abuses the other, where frustration and conflict arise. **Mutual submission is fancy talk that ignores reality.** Submission—one person taking the lead while the other willingly follows—is what makes marriages function.

Consider dancing: for the dance to flow, one person leads and one follows. Both are essential; both require skill and trust. But if both try to lead simultaneously, you crash. **Submission is like learning to follow in a dance.** It takes confidence and strength.

### What If My Husband Is Not a Christian?

Peter addresses this directly. Yes, if your husband demands you sin, you don't obey him. But the passage actually calls wives to submit to ungodly husbands **as a means of winning them to Christ** (1 Peter 3:1-2).

**Like other institutions, we submit to the office, not the personality.** We don't submit to bosses because they're better than us; we submit because they hold the position. We don't require merit; we require submission to the role. This prevents us from masking our own sin.

**Choose wisely before marriage.** Once married, the standard of submission doesn't change based on his spiritual maturity. But before marriage—when you're evaluating a potential husband—merit absolutely matters.

**For women:** Can you submit to his authority? If he's selfish or unkind, why consider it? If he's not Christian, how can you trust him to present you as "a radiant church, without stain or wrinkle"? Even if he's Christian, if he doesn't read Scripture, if he's immature, if he's not pursuing godliness—he's not yet ready to be a husband. Be optimistic about future growth, but hold the bar high for present readiness. Blacklist him until he grows.

**For men:** Can you lead her toward Christ? Can you present her as a spotless bride? If she's not Christian, how can you lead her spiritually? Are you mature enough to take responsibility for her spiritual growth and the family's direction?

**Submission is about an attitude:** encouraging your spouse to lead, happily letting the spotlight shine on them rather than yourself, maintaining confidence even when they stumble. This matters whether they're perfect or deeply flawed.

### The Core Issue: Fatherhood and Leadership

At stake isn't really submission—it's **fatherhood and whether fathers will lead.**

> 1 Peter 3:7
> Husbands, in the same way be considerate as you live with your wives and treat them with respect as the weaker partner and as heirs with you of the gracious gift of life, so that nothing will hinder your prayers.

**Nothing is worse than being responsible for something while having no power to do anything about it.** If a father is responsible for raising children but has no authority, he's set up for failure. If a husband must present his bride spotless before Christ but cannot lead, he's been given an impossible task.

Generally speaking, women are weaker in physical size (men are 15-20% larger on average) and in the demands of motherhood. Pregnancy and nursing affect a woman's body—bones become brittle from calcium loss during breastfeeding; hormones loosen joints for childbirth. If we believe children matter, then the physical reality of motherhood matters.

**Men must take leadership seriously.**

- **Never leave.** Whether tempted by boredom, ambition, or feeling inadequate, cutting and running when the battle is hardest is cowardice. She is never better off without you.
- **Don't abrogate responsibility.** You have the power to do something—use it responsibly, even when you might fail. Better an imperfect father than an absent one.
- **Don't play the hero.** You're simply doing your job—fulfilling your responsibility to care for, nourish, love, and provide for your family. The buck stops with you.
- **Talk and listen constantly.** Don't only speak when there's a problem. Be interested in her day, the children's lives. Mind-reading doesn't work.
- **Be an all-rounder.** Provide materially, yes, but also physically (exercise together), emotionally (be present), and spiritually (lead the family toward God).
- **Ensure high-quality inputs.** TV is a poor substitute for your presence and guidance.

## Living as Aliens: A Call to Coherence

Throughout this passage, Peter's theme is clear: **we are aliens and strangers, living according to a different kingdom.** Our submission—to government, to employers, to spouses—is never absolute because we belong to a higher authority.

The world has lost touch with authority structures that serve actual purposes. Government can't be everything. Families can't be everything. Churches can't be everything. Each institution has its sphere, its authority, and its limits.

**Our task is to live visibly good lives while submitting to legitimate authority, knowing that vindication comes on the last day.** We don't gain a hearing through rebellion. We earn the right to speak by living differently. We create opportunities for the gospel through quiet, persistent goodness—even in difficult circumstances, even under harsh authorities, even when misunderstood.

Like Peter himself, who faced Nero's brutality yet continued preaching and living faithfully, **we trust that the One who judges justly will vindicate us.** Until then, we submit where we must, stand firm where God's boundaries are crossed, and live in such a way that even our enemies are left speechless.
