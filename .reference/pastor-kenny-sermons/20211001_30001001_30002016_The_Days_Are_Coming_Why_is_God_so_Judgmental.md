# Amos 1:1-2:16

**Title:** The Days Are Coming: Why is God so Judgmental?
**Date:** October 2021
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction

In our culture, the prevailing sentiment is "don't judge me." We live in an age obsessed with avoiding judgment—"don't judge me and I won't judge you." Yet when we turn to Scripture, we encounter a God who appears deeply judgmental. This seems contradictory, especially in Amos, where God pronounces judgment with remarkable intensity. The question becomes: Why is God so judgmental? Isn't He a God of love?

## 1. God Is Judge

Amos begins with the prophet's credentials—or apparent lack thereof. He is not a trained prophet or priest, but a shepherd and part-time gardener from Tekoa, a small town south of Jerusalem. The very choice of this nobody to deliver God's judgment is itself significant.

> 1 The words of Amos, one of the shepherds of Tekoa—the vision he saw concerning Israel two years before the earthquake, when Uzziah was king of Judah and Jeroboam son of Jehoash was king of Israel.

The historical context is crucial. During Amos's time, Israel had split into two kingdoms: the Northern Kingdom (Israel, with ten tribes, led by Jeroboam II) and the Southern Kingdom (Judah, with two tribes, led by Uzziah). These kingdoms despised each other. The prospect of a Judean shepherd journeying north to condemn the Northern Kingdom would understandably provoke outrage: "How dare you judge us?"

Yet God immediately establishes His authority to judge through powerful imagery:

> 2 He said: "The Lord roars from Zion and thunders from Jerusalem; the pastures of the shepherds dry up, and the top of Carmel withers."

This opening declares God's judgment through cosmic imagery—His roar and thunder suggest unstoppable power. The withering of pastures from the low ground to the high mountain (Carmel) suggests that judgment reaches everywhere, from high to low, with no possibility of escape.

## 2. Judgment on the Nations

God begins not with Israel, but with six surrounding nations—a pattern commentators call the "Book of Doom." The formula is repeated with remarkable consistency:

**The formula contains three elements:**

First, God names the city to be judged. Second, He provides evidence of its crimes. Third, He announces the specific punishment.

### Damascus (Aram)
> 3 This is what the Lord says: "For three sins of Damascus, even for four, I will not relent. Because she threshed Gilead with sledges having iron teeth..."

The phrase "three sins...even for four" conveys repeated transgression—God has tolerated them again and again, but the fourth offense is the breaking point. In Hebrew numerology, three plus four equals seven, the number of completion and fulfillment. Aram has filled its quota of crimes.

God provides evidence: Damascus brutally crushed the people of Gilead—threshing them like grain, grinding them under iron wheels. This is not merely warfare; it describes genocide.

> 4-5 I will send fire on the house of Hazael that will consume the fortresses of Ben-Hadad. I will break down the gate of Damascus; I will destroy the king who is in the Valley of Aven and the one who holds the scepter in Beth Eden. The people of Aram will go into exile to Kir, says the Lord.

### Gaza (Philistia)
> 6-8 This is what the Lord says: "For three sins of Gaza, even for four, I will not relent. Because she took captive whole communities and sold them to Edom... I will send fire on the walls of Gaza that will consume her fortresses. I will destroy the king of Ashdod and the one who holds the scepter in Ashkelon. I will turn my hand against Ekron, till the last of the Philistines are dead, says the Sovereign Lord."

Gaza's crime: systematic slave trading. They captured entire populations and sold them into bondage.

### Tyre
> 9-10 This is what the Lord says: "For three sins of Tyre, even for four, I will not relent. Because she sold whole communities of captives to Edom, disregarding a treaty of brotherhood... I will send fire on the walls of Tyre that will consume her fortresses."

Tyre's crime parallels Gaza's, but with an added element: they violated a treaty. They were bound by brotherhood to Edom, yet broke that sacred bond.

### Edom
> 11-12 This is what the Lord says: "For three sins of Edom, even for four, I will not relent. Because he pursued his brother with a sword and slaughtered the women of the land, because his anger raged continually and his fury flamed unchecked, I will send fire on Teman that will consume the fortresses of Bozrah."

Edom's crime: pursuing family with violence, killing women with unrelenting fury. This is betrayal at its worst—violence against blood relatives.

### Ammon
> 13-15 This is what the Lord says: "For three sins of Ammon, even for four, I will not relent. Because he ripped open the pregnant women of Gilead in order to extend his borders... I will set fire to the walls of Rabbah that will consume her fortresses amid war cries on the day of battle, amid violent winds on a stormy day. Her king will go into exile, he and his officials together, says the Lord."

Ammon's crime: killing pregnant women to terrorize and expand territory. This represents extreme cruelty—murdering not just people, but the unborn and defenseless.

### Moab
> 2:1-3 This is what the Lord says: "For three sins of Moab, even for four, I will not relent. Because he burned to ashes the bones of Edom's king, I will send fire on Moab that will consume the fortresses of Kerioth. Moab will go down in great tumult amid war cries and the blast of the trumpet. I will destroy her ruler and kill all her officials with him, says the Lord."

Moab's crime: desecrating the dead through cremation. In the ancient Near East, this represented the ultimate dishonor—denying proper burial and respecting the deceased.

### Judah
> 2:4-5 This is what the Lord says: "For three sins of Judah, even for four, I will not relent. Because they have rejected the law of the Lord and have not kept his decrees, because they have been led astray by false gods, the gods their ancestors followed, I will send fire on Judah that will consume the fortresses of Jerusalem."

Judah is condemned not for violence against other nations, but for spiritual apostasy—rejecting God's law and following idols.

## Understanding God's Justice

At this point, the reader may recoil: God seems vindictive, even tribal—rooting for the South while punishing the North. But we must consider the context of these judgments. The nations condemned are guilty of:

- Genocide
- Slavery and human trafficking
- War crimes and mass killing
- Violence against women and children
- Desecration and dehumanization

Every person would agree these deserve justice. We rightly condemn Hitler and demand accountability for such atrocities. Yet without judgment, there is no justice.

**The fundamental truth: Judgment is the flip-side of justice.**

To have justice requires a judge. To have justice requires a lawgiver who enforces moral law. Morality itself requires an ultimate authority—a God who judges.

In our age of moral relativism, we often hear that "right and wrong are subjective." But this reasoning leads to darkness. If there is no ultimate judge, then morality reduces to the preferences of the powerful. When might makes right, the weak have no recourse. This is not freedom; this is tyranny.

God judges because God loves justice. Without His judgment, we have no foundation for morality at all.

## 3. Judgment of God's People

The shocking turn comes at verse 6 of chapter 2. After condemning six nations, we expect the pattern to end. Yet God reserves judgment for a seventh—Israel itself.

> 2:6 This is what the Lord says: "For three sins of Israel, even for four, I will not relent."

What follows is not one sin, but seven complete transgressions:

> (6) They sell the innocent for silver, and the needy for a pair of sandals. (7) They trample on the heads of the poor as on the dust of the ground and turn the lowly out of the way.

Israel exploits the poor as slaves, buying and selling people for trivial prices.

> (8) Father and son use the same girl and so profane my holy name. They lie down beside every altar on garments taken in pledge. In the house of their god they drink wine taken as fines.

Israel sexually exploits enslaved women. They rest on stolen property in their places of worship. They consume the wealth extracted from the poor through legal fines.

Yet God reminds them of His grace:

> (9-11) Yet I destroyed the Amorites before them, though they were tall as cedars and strong as the oaks. I brought you up out of Egypt and led you forty years in the wilderness to give you the land of the Amorites. I also raised up prophets from among your children and Nazirites from among your youths. Is this not true, people of Israel?

God had fought for Israel, given them the land, and provided prophets and spiritual leaders. Yet:

> (12) But you made the Nazirites drink wine and commanded the prophets not to prophesy.

Israel silenced God's messengers and corrupted those set apart for holiness.

The consequence is total devastation:

> (13-16) Now then, I will crush you as a cart crushes when loaded with grain. The swift will not escape, the strong will not muster their strength, and the warrior will not save his life. The archer will not stand his ground, the fleet-footed soldier will not get away, and the horseman will not save his life. Even the bravest warriors will flee naked on that day, declares the Lord.

The most powerful warriors—those who should be strongest—will flee naked and defenseless. The destruction of the surrounding nations, which Israel thought was God's protection, turns out to be a trap. The ring of safety becomes a noose.

## Application: The Need for Righteous Judgment

Our contemporary culture rejects judgment, equating all discernment with prejudice. Yet Jesus taught differently:

> **Matthew 10:16** "I am sending you out like sheep among wolves. Therefore be as shrewd as snakes and as innocent as doves."

> **John 7:24** "Stop judging by mere appearances, but instead judge correctly."

The Bible does not forbid judgment; it forbids *unjust* judgment. We are called to distinguish right from wrong, to be discerning, to recognize patterns and evidence. There is a difference between prejudging (judging before the facts) and judging (making decisions based on evidence and character).

Matthew 7:1-5 addresses hypocrisy, not judgment itself:

> **Matthew 7:1-5** "Do not judge, or you too will be judged. For in the same way you judge others, you will be judged, and with the measure you use, it will be measured to you. Why do you look at the speck of sawdust in your brother's eye and pay no attention to the plank in your own eye? How can you say to your brother, 'Let me take the speck out of your eye,' when all the time there is a plank in your own eye? You hypocrite, first take the plank out of your own eye, and then you will see clearly to remove the speck from your brother's eye."

This passage calls us to humble self-examination before we judge others—not to abandon judgment altogether.

We live in an age where we mistake kindness for silence and compassion for refusal to distinguish right from wrong. Yet without the ability to judge, we cannot pursue justice. Without God as the ultimate judge, we have no foundation for morality at all.

## The Hope of the Gospel

Yet even in judgment, there is hope. The day described in Amos 2:13-16 finds its ultimate fulfillment in the passion of Christ. In the Garden of Gethsemane, as judgment falls, Jesus stands firm while others flee:

> **Mark 14:43-52** Just as Jesus was speaking, Judas appeared with a crowd armed with swords and clubs. One of those standing near drew his sword and struck the servant of the high priest, cutting off his ear. Jesus said, "Am I leading a rebellion, that you have come out with swords and clubs to capture me? Every day I was with you, teaching in the temple courts, and you did not arrest me. But the Scriptures must be fulfilled." Then everyone deserted him and fled. A young man, wearing nothing but a linen garment, was following Jesus. When they seized him, he fled naked, leaving his garment behind.

The prophecy of the bravest warrior fleeing naked comes true—not for Israel's warriors, but fulfilled in the disciples of Christ. Yet Jesus does not flee. He stands and accepts judgment on behalf of all who trust Him.

Within hours, Jesus would bear the full weight of God's judgment, taking upon Himself the condemnation that should fall on us. For all who turn to Him, the judgment has already been executed—and executed in our place.
