# Exodus 20:12

**Title:** Honour Your Mother and Father

**Date:** 10 November 2002

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Puzzle: Jesus's Harsh Words

At first glance, Jesus's teachings about family seem to contradict the command to honour parents. He tells us:

> If anyone comes to me and does not hate his father and mother, his wife and children, his brothers and sisters—yes, even his own life—he cannot be my disciple. (Luke 14:26)

> Let the dead bury their own dead, but you go and proclaim the kingdom of God. (Luke 9:59-60)

These are shocking statements, especially in the context of Jewish culture where family relationships held the highest value. Yet Jesus also affirmed the commandment:

> "Honor your father and mother"—which is the first commandment with a promise—that it may go well with you and that you may enjoy long life on the earth. (Ephesians 6:2-3)

So what is he really saying?

## Why God Cares About Honouring Parents

### 1. Parents Reflect God's Design

God could have created humanity instantly, but he chose to populate the world through families. Parents are built into the very structure of how we exist and who we become. This establishes the proper pattern of relationships: parents with authority to teach and discipline, children with the responsibility to obey and honour.

### 2. Parents Are a Metaphor for God the Father

Just as a marriage is a pale reflection of Christ and the church (Ephesians 5), or T-ball reflects cricket, our earthly parent-child relationship reflects the eternal Father-Son relationship within the Godhead.

God the Father is the origin of all family. As Paul writes:

> For this reason I bow my knees before the Father, from whom every family in heaven and on earth is named. (Ephesians 3:14)

God the Father and God the Son eternally exist in this relationship of honour and submission. Our families on earth are merely reflections of that eternal reality. We are made in God's image; he is not made in ours.

Therefore, when God gave the commandment—"Honour your father and mother, so that you may live long in the land the LORD your God is giving you"—he was asking Israel to follow the pattern of relationships that mirrors his own nature.

### 3. Rebellion Against Parents Is Rebellion Against God

In Jewish understanding, sin's essential characteristic is rebellion. Notice how Paul describes the depraved mind:

> They are full of envy, murder, strife, deceit and malice... they disobey their parents. (Romans 1:29-30)

And in the last days:

> People will be... disobedient to their parents, ungrateful, unholy... having a form of godliness but denying its power. (2 Timothy 3:2-5)

Rejection of parental authority is the ultimate display of rebellion against God's order. This is why the Old Testament treated it so seriously:

> Anyone who curses his father or mother must be put to death. (Exodus 21:17)

> Cursed is the man who dishonors his father or his mother. (Deuteronomy 27:16)

Dishonouring parents—through attack, curse, or bringing shame—is not merely disobedience; it is sin itself.

## What Does It Mean to Honour Your Parents?

Honour is not merely an action; it is **an attitude and disposition of the mind and will whereby you highly prize the person**. It means respect, valuing them as significant in your life.

This understanding protects us from hypocrisy. You can perform all the right gestures—using proper titles, showing outward respect—while hating your parents in your heart. Jesus condemned this exact practice:

> These people honor me with their lips, but their hearts are far from me. They worship me in vain; their teachings are but rules taught by men. (Matthew 15:7-9)

## The Priority of the Kingdom

Jesus's shocking statements—"hate your father and mother," "let the dead bury their own dead"—are not contradictions. They are clarity about priority.

When a rich young man asked Jesus what he must do to enter eternal life, Jesus first affirmed the commandment:

> Honor your father and mother. (Matthew 19:19)

But then he revealed the deeper test:

> If you want to be perfect, go, sell your possessions and give to the poor, and you will have treasure in heaven. Then come, follow me. (Matthew 19:21)

The young man went away sad because he had great wealth. Jesus was demonstrating that **the Kingdom of God takes priority over all earthly relationships, including family**.

This doesn't mean we stop honouring our parents. It means our loyalty to Christ must be supreme. If a choice is forced between following Christ and obeying family, there is no other choice. Relative to our love for Christ, any other love—even family—is like hating them.

## Practical Implications

### Broken and Abusive Families

Some of us have had distant fathers, abusive parents, or broken homes. The command to honour still stands. This requires:

- Continuing to forgive, even when they don't deserve it
- Seeking reconciliation, even if they reject us
- Refusing to reject them in return, though they may have rejected us
- Finding healing in knowing the real Father through Jesus

We are part of a wider family—the church—where we experience the love and community broken earthly families cannot provide.

### Non-Christian Families

The imperative to honour your parents doesn't change simply because they don't share your faith. However, obedience takes different forms depending on your situation:

- Some may need to find ways to attend Bible studies rather than church services
- Others may need to deepen their personal Bible reading as compensation
- You might find creative ways to spend time with believing friends outside family time

Your constant goal is to honour your parents and pray for them, trusting that your faithful witness may win them to Christ.

### The Danger of Legalism

The Pharisees had created a loophole called *Korban*. A man could dedicate his wealth to God and claim he couldn't help his aging parents because it was a "gift devoted to God." This freed him from the actual burden of caring for them.

Jesus exposed this hypocrisy:

> You say that if a man says to his father or mother, "Whatever help you might otherwise have received from me is a gift devoted to God," he is not to honour his father with it. Thus you nullify the word of God for the sake of your tradition. (Matthew 15:5-6)

Genuine honour is not about perfect rules or clever exceptions. It's about the condition of your heart.

## Growing Into Maturity

There is a natural progression in how we honour parents:

- **As children**, we obey our parents in everything, for this pleases the Lord.
- **As adults**, we leave father and mother and are united to our spouse, becoming one flesh.
- **In all seasons**, we care for them as they age, just as they once cared for us.

Paul writes to the Corinthians with gentle wisdom:

> Children should not have to save up for their parents, but parents for their children. So I will very gladly spend for you everything I have and expend myself as well. (2 Corinthians 12:14-15)

The pattern is reciprocal love at different life stages—not control, but genuine care flowing both directions.

## Conclusion

Honouring your parents is not legalism or cultural obligation. It is an expression of your heart's alignment with God's order. When you honour your parents, you honour the Father. When you rebel against them, you reveal rebellion against God.

Yet this honour must never compete with your ultimate loyalty to Christ. The Kingdom comes first. But when the Kingdom is first, genuine honour to parents naturally follows—not from duty, but from a heart transformed by the love of the true Father.
