# Mark 15:40-16:8

**Title:** A New Adventure  
**Date:** 17 April 2022  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Seeking Truth in a World of Disinformation

We live in the age of information, yet ironically, we've never had more disinformation. Information is deliberately planted to distract us from truth. Consider current events—conflicting reports about wars, who to believe about tragic events. Some care so deeply about truth that they invest their fortunes in it. Others worry that concentrating truth in any one person's hands is dangerous.

Yet both sides agree on something fundamental: they want truth. They're seeking true information.

This weekend, we must ask the central question: What is the truth concerning Jesus' death and resurrection?

## Witnessing the Death (15:40-47)

### The Reliability of Eyewitnesses

> Some women were watching from a distance. Among them were Mary Magdalene, Mary the mother of James the younger and of Joseph, and Salome. In Galilee these women had followed him and cared for his needs. Many other women who had come up with him to Jerusalem were also there.

Here lies something remarkable about Christianity's foundation: it rests on objective eyewitness testimony, not subjective personal visions. In other religions, we find individuals receiving private revelations—Muhammad receiving the Quran alone over twenty years, Buddha meditating in solitude, Joseph Smith being given mysterious spectacles to read golden plates (which conveniently disappeared before anyone could verify them). But Christianity emerged from multiple, public eyewitnesses.

The prominence of women as witnesses is actually evidence of trustworthiness. In first-century Jewish law, women were not permitted to testify in court. Josephus, the famous Jewish historian, stated that women's witness was unacceptable "because of the levity and boldness of their sex." Even in the second century, the pagan critic Celsus mocked Mary Magdalene as a "hysterical female deluded by sorcery."

If Mark invented this account to convince people, he would have chosen more credible witnesses. Instead, he recorded those least likely to be believed in his culture—precisely because he was reporting what actually happened.

### The Death of Jesus

> At noon, darkness came over the whole land until three in the afternoon. And at three in the afternoon Jesus cried out in a loud voice, "Eloi, Eloi, lema sabachthani?" (which means "My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?")
>
> When some of those standing near heard this, they said, "Listen, he's calling Elijah." Someone ran, filled a sponge with wine vinegar, put it on a staff, and offered it to Jesus to drink. "Now leave him alone. Let's see if Elijah comes to take him down," he said.
>
> With a loud cry, Jesus breathed his last.
>
> The curtain of the temple was torn in two from top to bottom. And when the centurion, who stood there in front of Jesus, saw how he died, he said, "Surely this man was the Son of God!"

Multiple witnesses watched Jesus die. A whole company of soldiers—perhaps eighty to two hundred men—were present. These soldiers divided Jesus' garments, mocked him, pressed him to carry his cross. There were more than enough soldiers not just for one crucifixion, but to manage three executions simultaneously.

Dozens of people watched him die.

### Confirmation of Death

> It was Preparation Day (that is, the day before the Sabbath). So as evening approached, Joseph of Arimathea, a prominent member of the Council, who was himself waiting for the kingdom of God, went boldly to Pilate and asked for Jesus' body. Pilate was surprised to hear that he was already dead. Summoning the centurion, he asked him if Jesus had already died. When he learned from the centurion that it was so, he gave the body to Joseph.

Notice the emphasis: Jesus was **already dead**. Pilate himself was shocked, confirming with the centurion—a professional soldier, an expert in killing. When such men declare someone dead, they mean dead. They had every reason to ensure it.

> So Joseph bought some linen cloth, took down the body, wrapped it in the linen, and placed it in a tomb cut out of rock. Then he rolled a stone against the entrance of the tomb.

The women weren't foolish about burials. Joseph of Arimathea personally witnessed the body being wrapped and placed in the tomb. Women noted exactly where he was laid, so there could be no mistake about location later.

### Why This Matters Theologically

Jesus truly died—not metaphorically, but physically. This is essential because sin has real consequences. Death is the wages of sin (Romans). Every human faces this reality. We all decline from our thirties onward. By our mid-fifties, our DNA begins to degenerate, increasing cancer risk. This is our fate for sin.

But here is the hope: Jesus dying means he has paid for our sins. For the Christian, death is not the end. The women witnessed not only his substitutionary death but also something far greater—his resurrection.

## Witnessing the Resurrection (16:1-8)

### The Empty Tomb

> When the Sabbath was over, Mary Magdalene, Mary the mother of James, and Salome bought spices so that they might go to anoint Jesus' body. Very early on the first day of the week, just after sunrise, they were on their way to the tomb and they asked each other, "Who will roll the stone away from the entrance of the tomb?"

The same women returned the next day. They noted precisely where he was buried—no confusion about location. They came to anoint his body, as was the custom for showing love and respect. Though the task seemed impossible (who would move the great stone?), they came at first light, the moment they were permitted after the Sabbath.

> But when they looked up, they saw that the stone, which was very large, had been rolled away. As they entered the tomb, they saw a young man dressed in a white robe sitting on the right side, and they were alarmed.

The stone was moved. Inside, they encountered a figure—whether angel or the risen Jesus himself, Mark leaves open. But the message was unmistakable:

> "Don't be alarmed," he said. "You are looking for Jesus the Nazarene, who was crucified. He has risen! He is not here. See the place where they laid him. But go, tell his disciples and Peter, 'He is going ahead of you into Galilee. There you will see him, just as he told you.'"

Empty tomb. Risen Lord. Jesus, exactly as he had promised.

### Addressing Objections

**The Metaphorical Interpretation:** Some today claim the resurrection is merely metaphorical. This is a modern invention. No one in the history of Christianity thought this way. The witnesses—eyewitnesses—saw an empty tomb as objective fact.

**The Stolen Body Hypothesis:** Could the disciples have taken the body? They would have needed to overcome trained Roman guards, hide a body, obtain an impersonator, and then convince themselves and others to die for a known lie. No human movement has ever been sustained on the basis of a deliberate, shared deception.

**The Swoon Theory:** Did Jesus merely revive in the cool tomb? After enduring crucifixion—nails through limbs, crown of thorns, hours without water, massive blood loss—he would need to unwrap himself from tightly wound linen, move a heavy stone, overcome trained Roman soldiers, and escape without a trace. This is less plausible than resurrection.

**The Miracle Objection:** "Miracles violate the laws of nature." But real science doesn't begin with pre-conceived conclusions. It observes facts and follows evidence. The philosopher Hume noted that just because the sun has risen for millennia doesn't guarantee tomorrow's sunrise—this is the problem of induction. We cannot predict the future from past patterns alone. 

So if we follow Hume's logic, the fact that no man has risen in recorded history doesn't prove it never could happen. The evidence determines the conclusion—not the reverse. The women weren't reporting an everyday occurrence. They were astonished. No one claims resurrection happens regularly. God interrupted the natural order for an event of ultimate significance.

### The Significance: Death Defeated

Jesus faced many enemies—Jewish authorities, Roman authorities, temptation, sin, Satan himself. But the final enemy—the one no one truly controls despite our anti-aging creams and vitamins—was death itself. This source of decay, pain, and misery has been defeated once and for all.

> 1 Corinthians 15:55-57: "Where, O death, is your victory? Where, O death, is your sting?... But thanks be to God, who gives us the victory through our Lord Jesus Christ."

Jesus really died. Jesus really resurrected. Death is defeated.

### The Women's Fear and Our Response

> Trembling and bewildered, the women went out and fled from the tomb. They said nothing to anyone, because they were afraid.

Mark ends strangely at this moment of fear. The women were alarmed, bewildered, afraid—yet they had received the most joyful news imaginable. Why fear?

Perhaps fear of persecution. Fear that friends would ridicule them. Fear of being wrong (though we can all be mistaken—that's not shameful). Fear even of death itself. Or fear of speaking up, of telling others.

Yet the messenger gave clear instruction: go, tell the disciples. Just as Jesus had promised, he went ahead into Galilee.

Through Mark's reliable historical account, we occupy the same position as those early disciples. We too watch from a distance through reliable testimony. The question comes to us: Will you respond with faith or fear? Will you tell others?

### Historical Reliability

Mark likely wrote around 60 AD—about thirty years after the events. This gap is actually respectful by ancient standards. Most historical accounts were recorded centuries later. Alexander the Great, by contrast, was recorded four hundred years after his life, yet historians accept those accounts.

The crucial gap isn't between the event and our reading (nearly two thousand years), but between the event and the recording (thirty years). This meant eyewitnesses were still alive to object, to correct false claims, to verify. That's when a record is most trustworthy.

Good evidence doesn't become bad evidence simply because time passes. Consider your bank account—its accuracy doesn't depend on how long the money has been there, but on whether it was recorded correctly from the start.

The real barrier to belief isn't time or the unusualness of the event. It's unbelief itself. We often don't want to believe—because we fear the consequences, or we're indifferent, or we have other agendas. But if we examine the evidence carefully and find no reason to reject it, we must change our minds. This is true inductive reasoning: follow the evidence wherever it leads.

There is no reason to dismiss this testimony. Though resurrection is unusual, if examined honestly and found trustworthy, our perceptions must adjust to reality.
