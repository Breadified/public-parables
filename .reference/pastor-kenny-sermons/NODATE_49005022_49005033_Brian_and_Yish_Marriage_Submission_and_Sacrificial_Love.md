# Ephesians 5:22-33

**Title:** Brian and Yish: Marriage, Submission, and Sacrificial Love
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Context: A Wedding and a Word We Don't Like

We gather to celebrate the wedding of Brian and Yish—a beautiful union that brings together two people committed to building a life together according to God's design. Yet as the vows were exchanged, some of you may have flinched at a particular word: *submission*.

In our modern world, this word carries baggage. We think of oppression, exploitation, and abuse—dark images of people forced into subjugation against their will. How could an educated, capable woman like Yish freely choose to promise respect and submission to her husband?

Yet here she stands, having made exactly that choice. She has decided to put herself under her husband's authority for life. This is not coercion. This is covenant. And as strange as it may seem in our age, this instruction comes directly from Scripture:

> Wives, submit to your own husbands, as to the Lord. (Ephesians 5:22)

The comparison is staggering: just as the church submits to Christ, so wives are called to submit to their husbands. And just as Christians would never dream of picking and choosing which commands from Jesus to obey, Christian wives are called to submit "in everything" to their husbands (v. 24).

But here's the crucial question: Why does this make sense? Why would God call for this?

## Reason One: To Allow the Husband to Lead in Love

Notice the remarkable imbalance in Paul's instructions. Wives receive one sentence. Husbands receive an entire paragraph.

> Husbands, love your wives, as Christ loved the church and gave himself up for her. (Ephesians 5:25)

This is the key. Submission exists within a larger framework—and that framework is built on *sacrifice*.

The husband is called to lead like Christ leads the church. And how did Christ love the church? He protected her. He fought for her. He gave His very life on the cross to save her. He conquered death, rose victorious, and continues to protect His beloved today.

This is what biblical love means: *sacrificing oneself for the other*.

The practical dimensions of this are real and concrete. Physically, the husband is called to be the protector—the first to stand between danger and his family. Materially, he must provide food, shelter, and security, even if it means setting aside his pride and taking work beneath his status. Emotionally, he must initiate reconciliation when conflict arises, being the first to apologize, the first to pursue healing. Spiritually, he bears responsibility to guide his family toward holiness and the knowledge of God.

> Husbands should love their wives as their own bodies. He who loves his wife loves himself. For no one ever hated his own flesh, but nourishes and cherishes it, just as Christ does the church. (Ephesians 5:28-29)

Of course, he cannot do all of this alone. He must delegate wisely. But the fundamental responsibility rests with him. The buck stops with him.

Here's the beautiful paradox: **Brian cannot lead without Yish's wholehearted support. Brian cannot lead without Yish's submission.**

Think of any time you've led a team or project at work. You gather input, listen to your teammates, discuss options thoroughly. But then comes the moment when someone must make the call. As the leader, that responsibility falls to you. And if it's a wrong decision, you're the one who bears the consequences.

But what if, after making that call, your team refuses to follow? What if they drag their feet, complain behind your back, and withhold the space you need to lead? Then leadership becomes impossible. By definition, there can be no leader without followers willing to submit.

When Yish agrees to submit, she is creating the space for Brian to lead. Her submission is not separate from his sacrifice—it is part of the same beautiful dance. She enables him to fulfill his calling to love her as Christ loved the church.

And notice the sequence in the marriage vows: the bridegroom promises first. Only after hearing his commitment to die for her does the bride agree to follow. The man initiates; she accepts. He asks her to lunch, and she accepts. He asks her to join him in faith and community, and she accepts. He proposes marriage and promises to sacrifice his life for her, and she submits.

## Reason Two: A Tiebreaker for Irreconcilable Differences

You might ask: "Can't couples simply submit to one another? Can't they take turns making decisions, or vote, or consult with parents?"

Of course. In most situations, couples should seek counsel, discuss options openly, and often simply defer to one another for the sake of harmony and love. Part of the husband's sacrificial love is knowing when to give in for his wife's sake. There is room for flexibility, compromise, and mutual consideration.

But God's instruction on submission is not designed for those situations. It addresses a specific, painful reality: the moment when irreconcilable differences emerge.

Imagine this: one spouse believes the family should move to Brisbane. The other insists on Melbourne. Both have compelling reasons. Both are immovable. What happens then?

- You could consult your parents—but didn't you leave home to become independent?
- You could vote—but in a marriage of two, a tie vote solves nothing.
- You could separate—but under family law, "irreconcilable differences" means divorce. You destroy the family to escape the problem.

But what if you believe, as Michael and Bonnie have just vowed, that marriage is for life? What if you believe marriage is something greater than your feelings, stronger than your disagreements, worth fighting for? What if you see marital love as a covenant—a sacred promise before God?

Then you need a structure. You need a tiebreaker.

God, in His wisdom and mercy, has prepared that structure in advance. Someone must ultimately yield. God says: let the husband make the final call, and let the wife submit to that call. Could He have reversed it? Certainly. But someone must give way, and this is His design.

This is not tyranny. This is *protection*. God sees this young couple standing before Him, deeply in love, having made vows for life. He says: "I will help you. I will structure your marriage so that when you face irreconcilable differences, you will never have to divorce. There is a tiebreaker. There is a way forward."

This is God's gift to preserve their love—to keep them together, not separated by impossible choices.

## The Deeper Mystery: Christ and the Church

Here is where the text reveals its deepest meaning:

> This is a profound mystery—but I am talking about Christ and the church. (Ephesians 5:32)

Marriage is not merely about two individuals. It reflects the relationship between Christ and His beloved church. God is calling each of us—like a bride—to submit to Christ as our head and Lord.

But understand what this means. This is not submission to an authoritarian deity who demands obedience through fear. This is submission to a God who has proven His love by sacrificing Himself for us. This is a God who promises to protect us, guide us, and lead us toward wholeness and eternal life.

It is submission in the sense of being willing to follow His way, trusting that His leadership will result in healthier, more fulfilled lives—both now and in eternity.

> Let each one of you love his wife as himself, and let the wife see that she respects her husband. (Ephesians 5:33)

## A Final Word to the Couple

Michael and Bonnie, you have made vows today before God and this community. You have promised to love and honor one another for life. Remember these principles when you face your own irreconcilable differences. Remember that you follow the same God who loves you with a love that transcends all understanding—a love proven on the cross.

> A man shall leave his father and mother and hold fast to his wife, and the two shall become one flesh. (Ephesians 5:31)

May you become truly one—unified in purpose, bound together by love, sustained by God's grace.
