# Acts 19:1-41

**Title:** The Name of the Lord Jesus
**Date:** January 2, 2003
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## What Makes a Good Name?

When we think about what constitutes a good name in this world, we recognize two essential ingredients: **power** and **influence**. A name must be able to make a difference and open doors, yet power alone is insufficient—everyone must know about it. There's little value in being the greatest at something if nobody recognizes it.

This is particularly evident in Asian culture, where titles and academic credentials carry substantial weight. They represent both power—the ability to make a difference—and influence. An Asian mother ensures the entire community knows her son is a lawyer or a doctor (or, if circumstances have changed, that he *used to be* a lawyer or a doctor). A good name in this world requires both substance and widespread recognition.

In Acts 19, we encounter the same question from God's perspective: What does the Lord consider to be a truly good name?

## The Name of John Is Not Sufficient

*Acts 19:1-7*

John the Baptist stands as one of history's greatest prophets. Born miraculously, great in the sight of the Lord, filled with the Holy Spirit from birth, he was the New Elijah—baptizing in the desert and preaching repentance for the forgiveness of sins. By any measure, this represents the makings of an exceptional name.

Yet in Acts 19:3-5, we see a decisive moment. Paul encounters some disciples and asks about their baptism. They reply, "John's baptism." Paul responds that John's baptism was a baptism of repentance, calling people to believe in the one coming after him—Jesus. Upon hearing this, they are baptized into the name of the Lord Jesus.

A baptism bearing John's name—however distinguished that name may be—is insufficient. Even John himself recognized this (as Mark 1:5 attests). The name of Jesus far exceeds it. This passage reminds us of the Mandaean sect, people who still believe John the Baptist was the Messiah. But no—the name of Jesus is infinitely greater.

Notice the manifestation of power in verse 7: signs and wonders follow when they are baptized into Jesus' name. This is a pivotal moment. Like Samaria in chapter 8, Ephesus represents a decisive turning point where Asia would be reached, paralleling the experience of the twelve disciples themselves.

## Name-Dropping Is Not Sufficient

*Acts 19:13-16*

So the name of John, as magnificent as it is, lacks the ultimate power. But what if we simply invoke the name of Jesus instead? Would that suffice?

In verse 13, we read of some Jewish exorcists who attempted to drive out evil spirits by invoking the name of the Lord Jesus, saying, "In the name of Jesus, whom Paul preaches, I command you to come out."

As a child, I was terrified of going upstairs at night in the darkness. I remembered what Sunday School had taught me—that in the name of Jesus, demons must flee. So I would climb those stairs calling out repeatedly: "In the name of Jesus, in the name of Jesus." (Interestingly, I found myself doing the same thing more recently after watching *The Ring*.)

But is merely using Jesus' name sufficient, divorced from genuine knowledge of him? Is it simply a cantation, a spell, a mantra—garlic to ward off vampires? The answer comes in verses 14-16. The demon-possessed man responds, "Jesus I know, and I know about Paul, but who are you?" Then the man overpowers all seven sons of Sceva, the high priest. They flee the house naked and bleeding.

Mere name-dropping, even of Jesus himself, is not enough. There must be genuine authority behind the name.

## The Name of Artemis Is Not Sufficient

*Acts 19:23-36*

What about Artemis, the great Greek goddess of fertility? Surely her name possessed sufficient power and influence?

Artemis is described as the "great goddess" (verse 27), worshipped throughout the entire province of Asia and beyond. So revered is her name that a thriving economy exists entirely around her worship. The craftsmen who make silver shrines for her temple depend on her reputation for their livelihood.

Imagine the crowd's response in verse 28: "Great is Artemis of the Ephesians!" The chant spreads like wildfire. The entire city erupts in chaos. Paul's companions are seized and dragged into the theatre. Most remarkably, verse 34 tells us that even in the confusion—despite the assembly being in complete disorder with people shouting different things and most not even knowing why they're there—the crowd united for two solid hours, shouting in unison: "Great is Artemis of the Ephesians!"

The name of Artemis possessed such widespread influence that even amidst confusion, it unified the entire population. If you had stood in that theatre, you might have concluded there was no greater name in the world than Artemis of the Ephesians.

Yet that name is not good enough either. Who worships Artemis today? Ephesus is now an uninhabited ruin, being excavated for its historical significance. Her great temple was destroyed in 267 A.D. The very panic described in verse 27—that Artemis would be "robbed of her divine majesty"—came true. Her influence, once dominant throughout Asia, evaporated entirely.

## The Name of the Lord Jesus Christ—The Best Name

The name of John, mere name-dropping, and the name of Artemis—all ultimately proved insufficient. So what name does God declare to be the greatest?

In Philippians 2:9, Paul writes:

> Therefore God exalted him to the highest place and gave him the name that is above every name, that at the name of Jesus every knee should bow, in heaven and on earth and under the earth, and every tongue confess that Jesus Christ is Lord.

Here is the name that possesses both power and widespread influence.

### The Power of Jesus' Name

The power of this name is evident in the most unexpected ways. Handkerchiefs and aprons—merely the work clothes of Paul—become instruments powerful enough to drive out demons when the seven sons of Sceva, despite their position as high priests, utterly failed.

But there is a power even greater than this. In verses 17-19, when news of these events spread to the Jews and Greeks of Ephesus, they became gripped with fear, and the name of the Lord Jesus was held in high honor. Many who believed came forward and openly confessed their evil deeds. Those who had practiced sorcery brought their scrolls together and burned them publicly. The scrolls were worth fifty thousand drachmas—representing decades of wages.

This power operates differently than the Sceva brothers attempted. It is not a magic formula or incantation. Rather, it is the Holy Spirit convicting hearts through the proclamation of Jesus' resurrection. Genuine repentance emerges, and people willingly sacrifice what they once valued. This transformation occurs because "the name of the Lord Jesus was held in high honor."

### The Spread of Jesus' Name

And the influence of this name? It spreads with remarkable speed. Though Artemis had been revered for centuries throughout Asia, the name of Jesus reached the entire province in merely two years (verse 10). So powerful and widespread did this name become that the entire regional economy was threatened—a detail that demonstrates just how thoroughly it penetrated the culture.

Verse 20 captures this beautifully: "In this way the word of the Lord spread widely and grew in power."

### How Jesus Earned This Name

How did Jesus obtain such an exalted name? We learn from verse 5 that it is through his death and resurrection. The Father raises him to life and grants him the name above all names. Through the resurrection, God declares to the entire universe that Jesus is the supreme name. This is the name that is both most powerful and most influential—but it was earned through obedience unto death.

## Holding the Name of the Lord Jesus in High Honor

As we approach Day 7 of the Summer Leadership Mission, we are armed with three crucial truths:

### First: The Name of Jesus Is Our Source of Unity

One commonly cited strength of our gathering is that we are multicultural, representing different church backgrounds and denominations. This is true and valuable. Yet it matters little whether we are monolithic in culture or wonderfully diverse. We are not ultimately unified by multiculturalism. We are gathered in the name of Jesus as Lord. That is our true source of unity, and nothing else can compare.

### Second: The Name of Jesus Brings Repentance

The power of Jesus' name is manifest in the present work of the Holy Spirit, calling sinful and rebellious people to repentance. What evil practices do we cling to? Broken relationships? Godless patterns of living? We must confess and renounce these things. Are we enslaved to materialism in this pagan world? Perhaps we should be gathering outside the temples of our own age—the shopping malls—burning our catalogues and credit cards as crowds unite on Boxing Day outside the great Westfield.

### Third: The Name of Jesus Demands Proclamation

The name of Jesus as Lord is great and powerful. Therefore, like verse 19:17 instructs, we must hold the name of the Lord Jesus in high honor. It is this name that makes the ultimate difference, and it is a name Jesus earned through his obedience unto death. Only through the proclamation of this name can real transformation occur.

The first question every person at Summer Leadership Mission learned to ask was: "What is your opinion of Jesus?" This remains the essential question. For in the end, that is what matters most—not our opinions, not our cultural backgrounds, not our accomplishments. What do you truly think about the name of Jesus?
