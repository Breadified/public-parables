# 1 Timothy 6:1-21

**Title:** The Olders
**Date:** 6 July 2025
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Overview

We come to the final chapter of 1 Timothy. The last sections of a letter often feel miscellaneous, but Paul's closing words here are unified by a single theme: our relationship with wealth and work. Throughout this passage, we see two groups—those under authority and those in authority—both facing the same core struggle: to view their positions correctly and to seek godliness rather than gain.

The ancient household included both slaves (workers) and masters (employers). While slavery in Paul's day was not the chattel slavery of later history, but rather bonded servanthood, the principle applies to us through the employment relationship. The teaching here cuts both ways: respect for those in authority, and responsibility for those with wealth.

## Part 1: Slaves—Respect in the Workplace (vv. 1-16)

### Slaves Under All Masters (v. 1)

> All who are under the yoke of slavery should consider their masters worthy of full respect, so that God's name and our teaching may not be slandered.

The word "slavery" here refers to bonded servanthood—work undertaken often to repay debt, whether temporary or long-term. A helpful modern parallel is employment: someone hired under contract, bound by obligation, with limited freedom to leave.

The "yoke" is that burden of obligation. It feels heavy. The conditions could be better. There is a power disparity, and as employees, we often feel that our employers profit from our labor while we carry the burden of PAYG taxation.

**Yet the command is clear: show respect.**

The Greek word for "respect" (timē) means honor, derived from a word meaning "to repay." You owe respect. Why? Because the job came first. Someone had to invest capital, rent an office, hire accountants, and secure clients before you arrived. They took the risk. The rent gets paid, the accountant gets paid, you get paid, and only then does the master receive what's left.

You might say, "I could work elsewhere for better pay." Perhaps. But if you haven't left, then something keeps you there—proximity to home, better work conditions, less risk, flexibility, good colleagues, familiarity. Whatever it is, this job, all things considered, is the best available to you. We often imagine grass greener elsewhere, but the reality is simpler: this job or unemployment.

**Therefore: you owe respect. Be thankful. Be grateful.**

This gratitude reflects well on God and our Christian teaching.

### Believing Masters: The Harder Call (vv. 2-3)

> Those who have believing masters should not show them disrespect just because they are fellow believers. Instead, they should serve them even better because their masters are dear to them as fellow believers and are devoted to the welfare of their slaves.

There's a temptation here: we take believing masters for granted. We're brothers and sisters, we're equal in Christ, we're family. So perhaps we don't owe as much respect?

This logic is backwards. When your master is a believer, you owe *more* respect, not less. Not because of the employment contract, but because you love them. You want them to succeed. If your boss were your family member, would you envy their success or want them to win? We naturally want family to prosper.

Some rationalize: "The boss believes in grace, so I can show up late." Or "He believes in compassion, so I can slack off." This is a profound misunderstanding of grace and compassion. A Christian master still provides you livelihood—without that job, you face unemployment, hunger, and poverty.

**Serve believing masters better, not worse.** Love them. See their position as God-given. When all things are equal, would you hire a Christian tradesperson or a non-believer? Would you choose a Christian accountant, dentist, or business partner? Of course—because there's a reasonable hope they'll conduct themselves with integrity. There's a premium on Christians of principle doing good work.

If you cannot tell the difference between Christian and non-Christian employers, your learnings are confused.

### The Danger of False Teaching (vv. 3-5)

> If anyone teaches otherwise and does not agree with the sound instruction of our Lord Jesus Christ and to godly teaching, they are conceited and understand nothing. They have an unhealthy interest in controversies and quarrels about words that result in envy, strife, malicious talk, evil suspicions and constant friction between people of corrupt mind, who have been robbed of the truth and who think that godliness is a means to gain.

Notice the rhetoric here: arguments about words, endless quarrels, people who manipulate doctrine to justify their wishes. This is not careful biblical reasoning. This is using doctrine as a tool for self-justification.

**The critical question: Is your heart on godliness or on gain?**

Some think godliness is a means to financial success. They take the teaching of Christ and corrupt it into a tool for their own ambitions. But the true question is whether you're after God's heart or your own heart.

### True Gain: Godliness with Contentment (vv. 6-8)

> Godliness with contentment is great gain. For we brought nothing into the world, and we can take nothing out of it. But if we have food and clothing, we will be content with that.

"Great gain" in Greek is literally "mega gain"—but here's the irony: **not seeking selfish gain results in spiritual, eternal gain.**

The truth is simple: we come into the world naked and exit the same way. The Pharaohs tried to take wealth into the afterlife; their treasures were plundered, sold, melted down, scattered to museums. We cannot take it with us.

Yet we live in a hedonistic age. When we hear "you can't take it with you," some conclude: "So I should spend it all today." This is the error. We pursue experiences—vacations, luxury, concerts, the latest technology. We tick off bucket lists. The one with the most stamps in their passport, the fanciest cars, the nicest clothes, supposedly wins.

But here's the paradox: **even these pleasures, you cannot take with you.** Heaven will be so glorious, so pleasurable, so joyful that our earthly experiences will seem like smoke and vapor by comparison. The fleeting pleasures of this life won't be worth remembering against the reality of eternity.

"Food and clothing" are metaphors for contentment—those basic, survival-level provisions that sustain us day to day. The point is not that some Christians lack even these; rather, it's that we be content with what meets our needs, not driven by craving for more.

**The paradox of true gain:** Godliness by itself is great gain. Why? Because godliness isn't a means to an end—godliness is the end.

## Part 2: Masters—The Responsibility of the Rich (vv. 17-19)

### The Peril of Arrogance and Misplaced Hope (v. 17)

> Command those who are rich in this present world not to be arrogant nor to put their hope in wealth, which is so uncertain, but to put their hope in God, who richly provides us with everything for our enjoyment.

Notice the caveats. "Rich in this present world"—it's temporary, not forever. The wealthy are mere stewards of God's assets, entrusted to deploy capital.

Therefore: Don't be arrogant. Wealth does not make you worth more. A billionaire CEO is not worth 300 billion. They own 300 billion. The person themselves—biologically, spiritually—is worth the same as everyone else.

And don't place hope in wealth. It's uncertain. Markets crash. Inflation erodes value. Death takes it all.

**Instead, put hope in God, who richly provides everything for our enjoyment.** Living in Sydney—what a gift. Within twenty minutes lie incredible foods from every nation. Within an hour are beaches and mountains of delight. Just taking the Metro to Martin Place is an enjoyment. These are gifts. Some are given more, some less. They are gifts, not pursuits. Don't live for them; don't make them the priority.

### The Purpose of Wealth: Good Deeds (vv. 18-19)

> Command them to do good, to be rich in good deeds, and to be generous and willing to share. In this way they will lay up treasure for themselves as a firm foundation for the coming age, so that they may take hold of the life that is truly life.

The rich have a special privilege and responsibility: **be rich in good deeds.**

"Generous" means distributing liberally. "Willing to share" is koinonia—fellowship, community. In investing capital, wealth finds its true purpose. Those given much will be asked much (Luke 12:48).

Notice the irony: the rich are commanded to *lay up treasure* for the coming age. How? Not by hoarding, but by investing in good works that transcend their lifetime. Building schools, churches, institutions, creating jobs for the poor—not just feeding the hungry, but empowering them.

Interestingly, many who became wealthy did so not by pursuing richness for its own sake, but by pursuing good works. They delayed gratification, invested capital rather than consumed it. Warren Buffett still drives his car from the 1970s and eats McDonald's daily. Their wealth is a byproduct of discipline and service.

### Why We Need the Rich

We need Christian wealth. The rich sometimes receive criticism from both the world and the church, partly because we've been taught that capitalism is greed. But the term "capital" itself tells us otherwise: capital is what remains after consumption. It's the money *not* spent on lifestyle.

The equation is simple: **Y = C + I** (Income = Consumption + Investment).

Those who became wealthy did so by minimizing consumption and maximizing investment in businesses that serve others. In a free market, you must provide a product or service that others find valuable. Capital goes into building businesses, or shares in companies doing good works, or banks that lend to startups.

We can each support our church with $1,000-$10,000 per year. We can bring food and clothing to the poor. But only the rich can fund $50,000-$100,000 annually to build schools, institutions, and systems that create jobs and change communities. They leave legacies not just for today, but for generations.

**We need the rich Christian.** Not just to consume less and give more—but to deploy capital for kingdom purposes that we cannot yet accomplish.

## Part 3: The Heart Exposed (vv. 9-11, 20-21)

### The Temptation of Riches (v. 9)

> Those who want to get rich fall into temptation and a trap and into many foolish and harmful desires that plunge people into ruin and destruction.

The danger isn't in being rich; it's in *wanting* to be rich. It's the desire for gain that becomes the snare.

I once spoke with students at a local school. One girl said her ambition was to be "a wife... in Dubai." Notice the operative word: Dubai. Not motherhood or marriage as godly callings, but as a vehicle to luxury and servants and wealth. Her real motivation was revealed in that one word.

### The Love of Money (v. 10)

> For the love of money is a root of all kinds of evil. Some people, eager for money, have wandered from the faith and pierced themselves with many griefs.

Money itself is neutral. The problem is **philarguria**—a lust for silver, a misplaced affection. Judas Iscariot walked with Christ yet sold him for thirty pieces of silver. His heart was sold.

> You cannot serve both God and money (Matthew 6:24).

It's a question of devotion. Are you devoted to godliness or to selfish gain?

### The Call to Flee and Pursue (v. 11)

> But you, man of God, flee from all this, and pursue righteousness, godliness, faith, love, endurance and gentleness.

The first battle is internal—against our own lusts and desires, to live for a higher goal than appetite. To see with eyes of faith that perceive the eternal, not with earthly eyes fixed only on the present.

You are the next witness to the teaching passed down.

### The Good Fight (vv. 12-16)

> Fight the good fight of the faith. Take hold of the eternal life to which you were called when you made your good confession in the presence of many witnesses. In the sight of God, who gives life to everything, and of Christ Jesus, who while testifying before Pontius Pilate made the good confession, I charge you to keep this command without spot or blame until the appearing of our Lord Jesus Christ, which God will bring about in his own time—God, the blessed and only Ruler, the King of kings and Lord of lords, who alone is immortal and who lives in unapproachable light, whom no one has seen or can see. To him be honor and might forever. Amen.

Imagine living life as a genuine non-profit. Not those NGOs with CEOs earning standard executive salaries—those are profit-making corporations wearing a non-profit mask. But genuine non-profits: the Salvation Army, Vinnies, churches, true volunteers. They minimize costs, avoid luxury, seek no gain. They run out of devotion to God and to others, following the example Christ left.

Not devotion to money, consumption, or experience. Devotion to good works.

### Final Charge (vv. 20-21)

> Timothy, guard what has been entrusted to your care. Turn away from godless chatter and the opposing ideas of what is falsely called knowledge, which some have professed and in so doing have departed from the faith.
>
> Grace be with you all.

## The Heart Question

Many of our decisions in life are hidden. No one sees them. But within a family—and the church is family—our priorities are exposed.

**What's being exposed in your life?**

- Is your heart ruled by contentment or by craving?
- Do you believe that godliness is its own reward?
- What do you want out of life?
- Where is your heart?

This is not about condemning the wealthy or despising work. It's about alignment. Where you place your hope, what you pursue with your energy, what you're willing to sacrifice for—these reveal what you truly believe about the eternal versus the temporary.

Godliness with contentment is great gain. Everything else, in the end, you cannot take with you.
