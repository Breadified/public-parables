# Exodus 21:22-27

**Title:** An Eye for an Eye

**Date:** 10 January 2016

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Introduction

"An eye for an eye" is one of Scripture's most misunderstood phrases. In popular culture, it's become synonymous with revenge, bloodthirstiness, and endless cycles of retaliation. But when we examine this teaching carefully, we discover something radically different—a profound act of mercy embedded within a framework of justice.

This morning, we'll explore how this ancient law actually revolutionized human society and how it reveals God's character: both His justice and His mercy working together perfectly through Christ.

---

## 1. Justice is Retributive

> 22 "If people are fighting and hit a pregnant woman and she gives birth prematurely but there is no serious injury, the offender must be fined whatever the woman's husband demands and the court allows. 23 But if there is serious injury, you are to take life for life, 24 eye for eye, tooth for tooth, hand for hand, foot for foot, 25 burn for burn, wound for wound, bruise for bruise. 26 "An owner who hits a male or female slave in the eye and destroys it must let the slave go free to compensate for the eye. 27 And an owner who knocks out the tooth of a male or female slave must let the slave go free to compensate for the tooth."

### The Definition of Justice

"An eye for an eye" defines justice fundamentally: *a crime creates a debt that must be repaid*. This appears throughout Scripture (Leviticus 24:17, Deuteronomy 19:16) and establishes that wrongdoing is not trivial. We cannot simply look the other way when harm is done.

We instinctively know this to be true. If someone deliberately scratches your car, should they escape consequences? If someone steals your child's bike, should you simply let it go? No—because crime against another person is injustice. Justice requires that wrongs be addressed.

### Proportionality, Not Vengeance

Here's the crucial point: "an eye for an eye" means **exactly that**—not two eyes for an eye, not an eye for a tooth. The punishment must be *proportional* to the crime.

This principle prevents the escalating blood feuds that plagued ancient Near Eastern societies. In those days, if you killed my father, my brother killed you. Then your brother killed mine. Generation after generation, families were decimated in endless cycles of revenge. "An eye for an eye" put a limit on this madness by insisting: the punishment stops here. It is measured. It is exact.

This isn't bloodthirsty—it's the opposite. It's *merciful to the offender* because it sets an upper bound on retribution. Without such limits, whoever is most powerful decides what justice means.

### Justice Must Be Equal

Notice verse 26-27: even though there's a power disparity between master and slave, justice remains equally available. The slave has legal recourse. This is radical equality before the law.

Justice cannot be bought or bent by privilege. It is blind to status. Without retributive justice proportionally applied, "might makes right" prevails—the wealthy escape accountability while the poor suffer.

### A Humanitarian Step Forward

In practical terms, verse 22 shows how proportionality works: for minor injury (premature birth with no serious harm), compensation is monetary. For serious injury, the law matches the severity. This is the principle of workers' compensation—justice calibrated to actual harm.

The Old Testament law was genuinely progressive for its time. Today, we've moved further from retributive justice, and we see the consequences: serious criminals escape accountability through legal loopholes, while innocent people are harassed over trivial matters. We ignore major injustices but criminalize minor infractions. This is exactly backwards.

## 2. Justice Allows for Mercy

> **Matthew 5:38** "You have heard that it was said, 'Eye for eye, and tooth for tooth.'
>
> **39 But I tell you,** do not resist an evil person. If anyone slaps you on the right cheek, turn to them the other cheek also. 40 And if anyone wants to sue you and take your shirt, hand over your coat as well. 41 If anyone forces you to go one mile, go with them two miles. 42 Give to the one who asks you, and do not turn away from the one who wants to borrow from you.

### Jesus Fulfills, Not Rejects

When Jesus says "but I tell you," He's not contradicting the Law. He's fulfilling it to its deepest intention. "An eye for an eye" was always meant to be merciful—it set an upper limit on justice. Jesus calls us to something higher: to sometimes go beyond what justice requires, to choose mercy voluntarily.

Note that the specific examples Jesus gives are *minor matters*—a slap (an insult), a lawsuit over clothing, forced labor, borrowing. He's not saying "let murderers go free." He's speaking about the daily minor infractions that happen between people living together.

If we litigated every small offense—every insult, every careless word, every accidental bump—life together would become impossible. We'd spend all our time keeping score, calculating debts, plotting revenge. But living as Christ-followers means sometimes choosing to absorb loss for the sake of community and love.

### The Deeper Meaning of "Do Not Resist"

The Greek word for "resist" (anthistēmi) can also mean "resist by evil means." Jesus may be saying: do not resist evil *through evil methods*. This allows for legitimate defense and law enforcement while forbidding personal vengeance pursued through wicked means.

The Sermon on the Mount describes personal morality—how Christ-followers treat each other. It doesn't eliminate the role of government in punishing serious crimes. But it does call us to let go of personal grievances through the instrument of mercy.

### A Living Example

Everything Jesus taught in Matthew 5:38-42, He lived out in Matthew 26-27:

- He spoke of not resisting evil, yet when arrested, He submitted to authority (26:51-54) and told Peter to put away his sword.
- He spoke of turning the other cheek—and the high priests struck Him, spat on Him, flogged Him. He did not retaliate (26:67, 27:26, 27:30).
- He spoke of those who take your clothes—and His garments were divided by lot (27:35).
- He spoke of walking an extra mile—and He walked to Golgotha carrying His cross (27:32).

Jesus didn't just teach mercy; He embodied it perfectly.

## 3. God's Justice

God, as Creator and lawgiver, expects His laws to be obeyed. These aren't arbitrary rules but guidelines for human flourishing. Since every human bears God's image, a crime against another person is ultimately a crime against God Himself.

For God to be just, He must punish proportionally. What is the appropriate punishment for rebelling against your Creator—against the One who gave you everything? The answer is sobering: eternal death. Infinite punishment for infinite rebellion.

> "It is mine to avenge; I will repay." (Deuteronomy 32:35)

God's justice is retributive. Every sin creates a debt. And that debt must be paid.

---

## 4. God's Mercy

But we also see God's mercy. Not a different God in the Old and New Testaments, but the same God revealing His character more fully. He tried to teach through Law and Prophets, but humanity didn't listen. So finally, He sent Jesus.

### Christ Fulfills Retributive Justice

Here's the stunning truth: God's justice *and* mercy meet perfectly in Jesus.

When Jesus spoke about "an eye for an eye," He was ultimately speaking about Himself. His entire passion narrative fulfills the teaching of Matthew 5:39-42:

- **The slap**: He was struck on the face (26:67) and didn't retaliate.
- **The clothing**: His garments were taken and divided (27:35).
- **The forced mile**: He carried His cross to execution.
- **The wound**: His side was pierced (John 19:34).

But more than this—Jesus took upon Himself the *retributive justice* that we deserve.

The debt I created through my crimes against God and against my fellow human beings—a debt that requires infinite punishment—was paid completely, equivocally, and infinitely by His death on the cross.

**It was still an eye for an eye, but it was His eye, not mine.**

**It was still a hand for a hand, but His hands were nailed to the cross, not mine.**

**It was still a wound for a wound, but His side was wounded for my sin.**

This is how justice and mercy come together. God didn't lower His standard or ignore our guilt. He satisfied His own justice by bearing the penalty Himself through Christ.

---

## Application: Wisdom Over Legalism

The Old Testament Law and Jesus's Sermon on the Mount should be read as *wisdom*, not rigid rules to be followed mechanically in every situation.

The principle underlying all of this is **love**—not vengeance, not payback. Mercy, not retaliation.

This gives us flexibility:

- **When to enforce justice**: Sometimes love means stopping injustice. A child being abused needs intervention, not mercy toward the abuser. Serious crimes demand legal consequences.

- **When to show mercy**: Sometimes love means absorbing a personal loss. When minor wrongs occur between people who share life together, sometimes the loving response is to let it go and preserve the relationship.

- **The standard**: Not arbitrary rules, but the principle of love in context. Ask: What does love require here? What serves the good of the other person and the community?

This isn't picking and choosing selfishly. It's practical wisdom—the same wisdom that guided God to give us a Law to protect us, then fulfilled that Law through Christ to free us.

---

## Conclusion

We live in a society that has largely abandoned the wisdom God gave us through the Law. We've swung to extremes: either ignoring serious crimes while criminalizing trivial matters, or else pursuing personal vengeance outside the courts.

But God calls us to something better: a justice that is proportional, administered fairly, and always tempered with mercy.

And for those of us who have rebelled against God, there is good news. The debt we cannot pay has been paid. The punishment we deserve fell on Christ. We are invited simply to accept His pardon and live in gratitude.

In this way, justice and mercy kiss. The Old Testament and New Testament are one. And God is revealed as both perfectly just and perfectly merciful—in Jesus Christ.
