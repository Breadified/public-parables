# Luke 1:26-38

**Title:** Mary: A Young Girl, A Virgin, A Servant

**Date:** 23 December 2018

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Opening: Strength in the Face of Burden

This Christmas sermon opens with the story of Sophie Smith and her husband Ash, who lost their premature triplets in 2007. Rather than despair, they channeled their grief into purpose, founding "Running for Premature Babies." Over a decade, their team of 3,000 runners raised over $3 million for neonatal equipment, directly benefiting 5,000 premature babies and their families. After Ash's death from brain cancer, Sophie continues this work—a testament to resilience and strength born from faith.

Today we meet another strong woman: Mary, the mother of Jesus.

## Part 1: A Young Girl (Luke 1:26-33)

Mary is an ordinary Jewish girl, bearing the common name Miriam. She was likely a teenager—perhaps thirteen, fourteen, or fifteen years old—young in both age and experience. The angel Gabriel appears to her with an announcement that troubles her deeply.

> **"Mary was greatly troubled at his words and wondered what kind of greeting this might be."** (Luke 1:29)

### Why Mary is Troubled

**The angel's greeting:** Gabriel's presence itself is extraordinary. The last time Gabriel appeared in Scripture was to Daniel, announcing the end of Israel's exile and God's plan to restore the world. Now he comes to Mary with similar weight: "Greetings, you who are highly favored! The Lord is with you."

**The pregnancy:** Mary will conceive and give birth to a son, to be called Jesus. This is momentous news, but for a young unmarried girl, it is terrifying.

**The scandal:** Mary is betrothed to Joseph—legally bound to him through a marriage arrangement, though they have not yet consummated the union. In Jewish culture, betrothal was as binding as marriage itself, requiring an official divorce to break. The engagement period allowed time for the groom to prepare a home, sometimes lasting a year or more.

An angel appears and tells her she will be pregnant—not by her husband, but by the Holy Spirit. She faces the prospect of:
- Pregnancy out of wedlock
- Shame and stigma in her community
- The real possibility that Joseph could divorce her, leaving her a single mother
- The loss of her reputation and future prospects

For a young girl, this is an unbearable burden.

### God's Grace Despite Burden

Yet the angel's response is crucial:

> **"Do not be afraid, Mary; you have found favor with God."** (Luke 1:30)

This burden is not a curse—it is *grace*. It is a gift, though not the kind one expects to unwrap at Christmas. John Piper, when diagnosed with cancer, wrote provocatively: "Don't Waste Your Cancer." He called his illness a gift. This seems strange until we understand that some of life's deepest troubles—suffering, illness, shame—can become opportunities to trust God more deeply, to seek His comfort, to repent, to witness to His goodness.

As Paul writes:

> **"For it has been granted to you on behalf of Christ not only to believe in him, but also to suffer for him."** (Philippians 1:29)

Trouble is not a gift in the sense we desire it. But when circumstances beyond our control—disability, unemployment, sickness, shame—come through no fault of our own, we have the power to transform them. We can curse God, or we can trust Him more. We can become bitter, or we can use our suffering to deepen our faith, cherish Christ more fully, and witness to others.

The greatest gift of all—God's Son, who died for our sins and rose again—overcomes every earthly trouble. With that gift already given, we can live with smaller "dud presents" of circumstance.

## Part 2: A Virgin (Luke 1:34-37)

Mary asks the reasonable question:

> **"How will this be, since I am a virgin?"** (Luke 1:34)

### The Meaning of "Virgin"

In the Hebrew Old Testament, the word translated as "virgin" can mean a young woman of marriageable age. Some have objected that Isaiah 7:14 merely prophecies a young woman, not a virgin conception. However, when Jewish scholars translated the Hebrew Bible into Greek (the Septuagint, before Jesus's time), they deliberately chose the Greek word *parthenos*, which specifically means a woman who has never had sexual intercourse.

Mary's own words clarify her meaning: "How can this happen since I do not *know* a man?" In Scripture, "know" is a euphemism for sexual intercourse. Mary is asserting her virginity.

### The Miracle: Pneumagenesis

The angel's answer is breathtaking:

> **"The Holy Spirit will come on you, and the power of the Most High will overshadow you. So the holy one to be born will be called the Son of God."** (Luke 1:35)

This is not *parthenogenesis*—asexual reproduction, which occurs in some animals but not in humans. This is *pneumagenesis*: conception by the Holy Spirit. The Spirit of God overshadows Mary, and a zygote, embryo, and fetus develop supernaturally.

#### The Theological Significance

This echoes Creation itself:

> **"The Spirit of God was hovering over the waters."** (Genesis 1:2)

In Genesis, the Spirit moves over chaos, and creation happens. Now, the Spirit moves over Mary, and new creation happens. Jesus is the Second Adam—fully human, descended from Eve, yet not from Adam's seed. This matters theologically: the sin that passes to all humanity through Adam does not pass to Jesus. He begins without inherited sin, with the capacity to obey or disobey, just as Adam did. But where Adam chose sin, Jesus chose obedience.

As Paul teaches:

> **"If by the trespass of one man death reigned through that one man, how much more will those who receive God's abundant provision of grace and of the gift of righteousness reign in life through the one man, Jesus Christ!"** (Romans 5:17)

#### The Scientific Objection

Critics ask: How can this be scientifically possible? The answer requires honesty about our presuppositions. We come to Scripture with prior beliefs about whether miracles can happen. But this objection applies to all biblical miracles—the resurrection, the parting of the Red Sea, healings, Jesus walking on water.

If we believe the universe had a beginning, we must believe God created the laws of science. That first creative act—bringing something from nothing—is already a miracle. If we accept the miracle of creation, why reject the miracle of new creation?

We need not claim miracles happen carelessly or constantly. Rather, at certain extraordinary moments—like God entering the world as a human—extraordinary signs occur. Christmas is precisely such a moment.

Gabriel's final word seals it:

> **"For no word from God will ever fail."** (Luke 1:37)

## Part 3: A Servant (Luke 1:38)

After hearing of her trouble, her burden, and the miraculous conception, Mary's response is stunning:

> **"I am the Lord's servant. May your word to me be fulfilled."** (Luke 1:38)

### The Humility of Acceptance

Mary chooses obedience. She chooses to be a *servant* of God—to accept His will, to embrace the circumstances before her, to surrender her own plans.

The Magnificat, Mary's song of praise, reveals her understanding:

> **"My soul glorifies the Lord and my spirit rejoices in God my Savior, for he has been mindful of the humble state of his servant. From now on all generations will call me blessed, for the Mighty One has done great things for me—holy is his name."** (Luke 1:46-49)

She accepts not just pregnancy, but humility itself:
- The humility of bearing a child out of wedlock
- The humility of potential divorce and single motherhood
- The humility of child-rearing—feeding, clothing, wiping, nurturing—work unpaid and often invisible
- The humility of setting aside ambitions for a career or position

In our modern world, the pressure is relentless: climb the corporate ladder, build a resume, wear a power suit, earn big money. Child-rearing is seen as inferior, humble work. But Mary saw it differently. She accepted the humble calling to serve God through motherhood.

Had this event occurred today, the likely outcome would be abortion, abandonment of Joseph, and pursuit of career advancement. But Mary was strong and resilient. She said yes to God.

### The Pattern of Obedience

Just as Abraham trusted God's command to leave his homeland without knowing where he would go, Mary trusts God's command without full understanding. God says: "Trust me." And she does.

## Part 4: A Restart with God (Luke 1:32-33)

The angel tells Mary of her son's future:

> **"He will be great and will be called the Son of the Most High. The Lord God will give him the throne of his father David, and he will reign over Jacob's descendants forever; his kingdom will never end."** (Luke 1:32-33)

This echoes the covenant to David in 2 Samuel 7: a son will come from David's line who will reign forever. The promises made centuries earlier—to end Israel's exile, to bring restoration, to establish an everlasting kingdom—will finally be fulfilled in Jesus.

## Application: Troublesome Gifts and Greatest Gifts

Think of your own circumstances, however hard. What troubles you? What shame or burden have you been given through no fault of your own?

If we know we are loved by God—truly loved, ransomed by Christ's blood—we can be less bitter about our circumstances. We can love others more freely. If we know that God transformed grief and suffering into glory and salvation through Jesus, we can be more optimistic about the future, about heaven.

We can live with our smaller troubles if we have already received the greatest gift of all.

As Ivan Lee, facing pancreatic cancer, testified: "I have a firm trust in God's goodness. He has already given us the greatest gift of all—his Son the Lord Jesus, who died for our sins and gave us eternal life."

**The world calls us to self-promotion and self-protection. But Christ calls us to servanthood.** However hard your circumstances, be God's servant. Trust His word. Accept His grace. And know that you, like Mary, are favored ones.

This year may feel like a loss. But next year, we restart—not by our strength, but by the grace of God. We restart as the Lord's servants, weak yet shown grace, bearers of the greatest gift that transforms all other gifts.
