# Philippians 1:2-15

**Title:** Living For Him / Drink  
**Date:** 29 January 2023 (ARISE); Originally 24 February 2013 (SMAC)  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Why We Worship

When people ask why we come to church, the common answer is "to worship God." We've built an entire industry around worship—songs, candles, religious activities. But Paul calls us to worship in a way that's fundamentally different from our modern understanding of the word.

The Greek word Paul uses here (latreia) means "service in the temple," not the sentimental, feeling-based worship we often imagine. It's the work of temple priests offering sacrifices. Understanding this matters because it reframes what worship actually is: **it's service**. Not emotion, but action. Not passive reception, but active devotion.

### Offer Your Bodies as Living Sacrifices

> Therefore, I urge you, brothers, in view of God's mercy, to offer your bodies as living sacrifices, holy and pleasing to God—this is your spiritual act of worship. (Romans 12:1)

Paul grounds this call in "God's mercy"—the culmination of everything taught in Romans 1-11:
- Romans 1: We are condemned because of our sin
- Romans 3: Yet God offers a righteousness not our own
- Romans 5: Christ died for us while we were still sinners
- Romans 9: God has predestined us to mercy by grace alone

Our proper response to such mercy is sacrifice—but not the dead animals of the temple. We offer our bodies *living*, continually giving up the desires and deeds of the flesh. This is the daily carrying of our cross, not a one-time death but a recurring pattern of self-denial.

The opposite of worshiping idols isn't autonomy—it's service. When the Thessalonians turned from idols, they turned "to serve the living and true God."

## Renew Your Mind

> Do not conform any longer to the pattern of this world, but be transformed by the renewing of your mind. Then you will be able to test and approve what God's will is—his good, pleasing and perfect will. (Romans 12:2)

Our age is fundamentally self-serving. We have an "I-generation," with products named after ourselves (iPhone, MySpace). But a living sacrifice won't step off the altar to save itself. Only those who have genuinely surrendered their pride can truly serve.

**Transformation begins with your mind, not your emotions.** The word translated "spiritual" actually means "logical" or "rational"—this is thoughtful, deliberate service. You cannot mature as a Christian without engaging your mind. Some want to follow Jesus by "feeling" their way through faith, but Christianity is fundamentally a religion of the mind.

History confirms this: wherever Christianity spread, education flourished. Monasteries preserved learning through the Dark Ages. The Reformation drove the printing press. Universities began as seminaries—Cambridge, Oxford, Harvard, Princeton. Nearly every early American college had Christian origins. Christian missionaries translated languages no one had written down. In Australia, the first schoolhouse was built by a chaplain in 1793. Public lending libraries—not government-run, but open to the public—were created by churches in the 1790s.

This isn't coincidence. Luther called it "shameful and despicable" for parents not to educate their children. Calvin insisted that all truth is God's truth, even when discovered by unbelievers: "If we regard the Spirit of God as the sole fountain of truth, we shall neither reject the truth itself, nor despise it wherever it shall appear."

Christians should be the most intellectually engaged people on earth, not because we're proud, but because understanding Scripture requires careful study, memorization, and reflection. This is how we discern God's will.

## Serve With Humility

> For by the grace given me I say to every one of you: Do not think of yourself more highly than you ought, but rather think of yourself with sober judgment, in accordance with the measure of faith God has given you. (Romans 12:3)

**Pride prevents service.** Only the humble can truly sacrifice themselves for others.

Consider a difficult season: you've been a leader, directing others, holding a certain status. Now life shifts—you're busy with a new job or a newborn. Someone younger steps into leadership. It's hard to step down, not because you can't let go of responsibility, but because you can't let go of how you see yourself. In our careers, we climb the ladder. We don't go backwards. Yet here's the call: think of yourself with sober judgment. Recognize what God has actually entrusted to you in this season.

If you can maintain your responsibilities or grow them—wonderful. But be honest about what you have capacity for. If you can't, then step back humbly. Support the younger leader. It's not beneath you to be accountable to someone with less experience. Many of us struggle with the idolatry of work—we overwork, then feel entitled to indulgence as a reward. Instead, serve in whatever capacity God has given you this season.

## Serve the Body

> Just as each of us has one body with many members, and these members do not all have the same function, so in Christ we who are many form one body, and each member belongs to all the others. (Romans 12:4-5)

Look at our congregation: ten nationalities, different languages, multiple schools and talents, various ages. We are radically diverse. Yet:

> We have different gifts, according to the grace given us. If a man's gift is prophesying, let him use it in proportion to his faith. If it is serving, let him serve; if it is teaching, let him teach; if it is encouraging, let him encourage; if it is contributing to the needs of others, let him give generously; if it is leadership, let him govern diligently; if it is showing mercy, let him do it cheerfully. (Romans 12:6-8)

Serving each other is serving the King. In Matthew 25, Jesus identifies with the hungry, the thirsty, the stranger, the naked, the sick, the imprisoned. "Whatever you did for the least of these brothers of mine, you did for me."

God's love language is service. Church should be like a healthy family kitchen, not a restaurant. In a restaurant, things are done for you. In a healthy family, everyone participates in serving one another. We talk, we share life, we love.

This is why we have Bible studies—not lecture-only, but S-teams: **Serving Teams**. These groups study Scripture together, but they're also committed to serving the broader church body. They're not introverted communities caring only for themselves, but places where knowledge of God overflows into prayer for the lost, other-centeredness, and love.

## Love Marks the Christian

> Love must be sincere. Hate what is evil; cling to what is good. Be devoted to one another in brotherly love. Honor one another above yourselves. Never be lacking in zeal, but keep your spiritual fervor, serving the Lord. (Romans 12:9-11)

> Be joyful in hope, patient in affliction, faithful in prayer. Share with God's people who are in need. Practice hospitality. Bless those who persecute you; bless and do not curse. Rejoice with those who rejoice; mourn with those who mourn. Live in harmony with one another. Do not be proud, but be willing to associate with people of low position. Do not be conceited. (Romans 12:12-16)

Love toward outsiders:

> Do not repay anyone evil for evil. Be careful to do what is right in the eyes of everybody. If it is possible, as far as it depends on you, live at peace with everyone. Do not take revenge, my friends, but leave room for God's wrath, for it is written: "It is mine to avenge; I will repay," says the Lord. On the contrary: "If your enemy is hungry, feed him; if he is thirsty, give him something to drink. In doing this, you will heap burning coals on his head." Do not be overcome by evil, but overcome evil with good. (Romans 12:17-21)

We have the privilege of gathering together. Some young people wanted to be here today but couldn't. Don't take this time for granted. Love one another. Encourage each other with God's Word. Look for ways to serve, to welcome newcomers, to practice hospitality. This is the life we're called to—not just at church, but in all things.
