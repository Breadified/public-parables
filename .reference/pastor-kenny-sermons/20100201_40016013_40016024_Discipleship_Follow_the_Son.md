# Matthew 16:13-24

**Title:** Discipleship: Follow the Son!
**Date:** 8 February 2010
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Misaligned Expectations

Something surprising happens in this passage. To understand it, imagine this scene: Elle Wood goes to her boyfriend expecting a proposal. Instead, she gets the opposite—a breakup. He tells her he needs to marry a "Jackie, not a Marilyn" if he's going to be a senator by thirty. The shock and disappointment are profound.

We encounter something equally surprising in Jesus's words to his disciples.

## Jesus Says He Is the Suffering Servant (21-23)

### The Setting: High Expectations

When Jesus asks his disciples, "Who do people say the Son of Man is?" the responses are telling:

> Some say John the Baptist; others say Elijah; and still others, Jeremiah or one of the prophets.

People expected a **prophet**—someone like John the Baptist, a voice calling for repentance and proclaiming God's judgment, one of Israel's great heroes.

But Peter goes further. He declares:

> You are the Christ, the Son of the living God.

Here Peter recognizes Jesus as **king**. *Christ* is not a surname but a Jewish title meaning "anointed one"—the Messiah. This is the king the prophets foretold, the king who will rule the world, bring justice, save the oppressed, and humble the proud.

Jesus receives this confession warmly and blesses Peter:

> Blessed are you, Simon son of Jonah, for this was not revealed to you by man, but by my Father in heaven. And I tell you that you are Peter, and on this rock I will build my church, and the gates of Hades will not overcome it.

Jesus promises to build his church starting with Peter—a gathering of God's people, echoing the great assemblies of Israel's past, now being renewed.

### The Shocking Reversal

Into this charged, expectant atmosphere—when the disciples finally understand that Jesus is the prophet and king—Jesus says something that deflates everything:

> From that time on Jesus began to explain to his disciples that he must go to Jerusalem and suffer many things at the hands of the elders, chief priests and teachers of the law, and that he must be killed and on the third day be raised to life.

This is incomprehensible. A king who dies? Which king goes first into battle? Kings fight last, if at all. And an eternal king—how can he die?

It's like a newly elected president announcing the day after his election that he's quitting. Totally unexpected.

Peter reacts with shock and rebuke:

> Never, Lord! This shall never happen to you!

But Jesus's counter-rebuke is severe:

> Get behind me, Satan! You are a stumbling block to me; you do not have in mind the things of God, but the things of men.

Jesus calls Peter "Satan"—not demon-possessed, but an adversary, an accuser. The rock has become a stumbling block. And the problem is clear: Peter thinks like a man, not like God. He's concerned with earthly survival and self-interest. If the leader dies, who protects the followers? Peter has just joined this movement and doesn't want to sign up for a death sentence. It's like enlisting in an army only to discover you've arrived just as they're recruiting kamikaze pilots.

**The heart of the issue: self-interest.** Even Peter's objection, born perhaps from love, carries within it a degree of self-preservation that misses God's purposes entirely.

## His Disciples Must Also Be Servants (24-28)

### The Call to Follow

Now Jesus speaks not just to Peter but to all his disciples:

> If anyone would come after me, he must deny himself and take up his cross and follow me.

**Follow me.** But what does following Jesus mean?

It means doing what Jesus did: denying yourself and carrying your cross to save others' lives.

To take up one's cross in that time was not metaphorical. Condemned prisoners carried the heavy cross-beam to their execution. It was a symbol of death—certain, public, humiliating death.

### The Swap

This is what Jesus did for us. We are sinners, condemned by the judge of the world to eternal death. But Jesus takes that condemnation upon himself instead. A swap has taken place. He dies so we don't have to.

And now Jesus calls his followers to live the same way—to embrace a road of self-sacrifice, not for ourselves but for the welfare of others and the gospel.

### What This Looks Like

Many of us have never thought of the Christian life this way—as a path toward death, a call to gradually put away our own interests for the interests of Christ and others. It means:

- Our attitude toward money
- Our attitude toward material possessions
- Our attitude toward time
- Our attitude toward career
- Our attitude toward how we treat others
- Our willingness to die for him if called

But the death Jesus asks for is not the sudden, dramatic kind. It's the slow, deliberate kind—the daily putting away of our interests for Christ's. Not like a terrorist's self-centered act ("I get paradise"), but like a lover's sacrifice ("for others").

### The Problem

We want to make our own terms with God. "I'll serve when I'm rich and successful, so people will respect me and want to follow." Or, "Let me be popular first, before I do the unpopular thing." Or, "I'll sacrifice when it doesn't hurt to sacrifice."

This is the prosperity gospel in miniature: it's about me, and God gets a cut. We create acronyms like WWJD as though we're still unsure what Jesus would do—when really, the answer has always been clear. He calls for 100%, not 10%.

### Realigning Our Expectations

This isn't a bait-and-switch. Jesus has been consistent all along. He spoke of a narrow path, a kingdom where leaders serve, a kingdom where he had nowhere to lay his head. This is what it means to be Christian: **Jesus is the king who served us. We are now to be servants too.**

We must give up all the other kings ruling our lives and serve others instead.

### The Encouragement

Yet listen to Jesus's promise:

> For whoever wants to save his life will lose it, but whoever loses his life for me will find it. What good will it be for a man if he gains the whole world, yet forfeits his soul? Or what can a man give in exchange for his soul?

Here is the encouragement: **whoever loses his life for Christ will find it.** Our soul, our eternal life—that is what matters.

Death should never be our fear. Poverty, suffering, lacking the right status or possessions—these should never be our fear. The only fear that matters is losing our eternal soul. And if that's settled through Jesus, we have nothing to worry about.

As Jim Elliott said: "He is no fool to give up what he does not have to gain what he cannot lose."
