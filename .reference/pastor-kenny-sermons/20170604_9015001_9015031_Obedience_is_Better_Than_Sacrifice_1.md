# 1 Samuel 15:1-31

**Title:** Obedience is Better Than Sacrifice
**Date:** June 4, 2017
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Value of Sacrifice

From childhood, we're taught that sacrifice is a mark of character and success. Sacrifice defines the immigrant experience—leaving everything to start over in a foreign land. It shapes family life—parents working long hours for their children's welfare. It's required for academic achievement, financial goals, and professional success. We celebrate sacrifice as essential to accomplishment.

Consider Margaret Court, one of Australia's greatest athletes. She won her first Grand Slam at just seventeen years and six months—the youngest ever to win the Australian Open. She went on to win 62 Grand Slam titles, more than any male or female player in history. In 1970, she became only the second woman ever to win the Grand Slam (Australian, French, U.S., and Wimbledon), returning after retirement, motherhood, and becoming Australia's #2 ranked squash player.

Such honor required extraordinary sacrifice. Court came from a humble background where tennis was inaccessible. But she lived across from the local Tennis Association and would sneak in to play. At ten, the club owner gave her membership and lessons—but only in exchange for part-time work to earn her court time. In her late teens, she moved to train intensively with the club's staff, working as a receptionist at the squash courts and gymnasium to pay for coaching. She insisted on training with men, sacrificing her entire teenage years—no parties, no social life—entirely for tennis.

To be successful and honored requires sacrifice. But today I want to tell you something better: **obedience is better than sacrifice.**

This truth is illustrated through King Saul's story. We've been reading through 1 Samuel together, which chronicles the search for Israel's true leader. We've learned that Samuel the prophet wasn't the right leader, and last week received hints that Saul wasn't either. Today we see the confirmation.

Our teaching unfolds in three movements:
1. Obedience is good
2. Obedience is better than sacrifice
3. Obedience and sacrifice together represent the fullness God desires

## Part One: Obedience is Good (1 Samuel 15:1-8)

Samuel comes to Saul with a command from God:

> "I am the one the Lord sent to anoint you king over his people Israel; so listen now to the message from the Lord. This is what the Lord Almighty says: I will punish the Amalekites for what they did to Israel when they waylaid them as they came up from Egypt. Now go, attack the Amalekites and totally destroy all that belongs to them. Do not spare them; put to death men and women, children and infants, cattle and sheep, camels and donkeys." (1 Samuel 15:1-3)

This commands sounds terrible and violent. But we must do the hard work of studying Scripture carefully rather than reacting emotionally to difficult passages.

### The Amalekites' Pattern of Rebellion

The Amalekites had a long history of disobedience toward God and violence toward Israel. The command references their attack during the Exodus:

> "The Amalekites came and attacked the Israelites at Rephidim." (Exodus 17:8)

This occurred when Israel was most vulnerable—before reaching Sinai, without water, fresh from slavery. Yet Israel had never attacked the Amalekites in the 300 years between Moses and Saul. The Amalekites, however, repeatedly attacked Israel:

- **Numbers 14:45**: They attacked Israel again in the desert while Israel was still wandering
- **Judges 3:13**: They joined the Moabites in attacking Israel
- **Judges 6:3-4**: They invaded whenever Israelites planted crops, destroying everything, leaving no sheep, cattle, or donkeys

Generation after generation, the Amalekites were at war with Israel and with God. What can you do with a nation bent on your destruction, that refuses negotiation and repeatedly attacks you?

God had already declared this outcome in Exodus:

> "Write this on a scroll as something to be remembered: I will completely blot out the name of Amalek from under heaven. The Lord will be at war against the Amalekites from generation to generation." (Exodus 17:14-16)

This command to Saul is not genocide—it's a defensive war against a relentless enemy. God even provided rules of engagement in Deuteronomy 20: offer peace terms first. Only if the city refuses and chooses war must you act.

### God's Mercy in Judgment

Remarkably, God's desire is mercy, not bloodshed. Notice what happens:

> "Saul went to the city of Amalek and set an ambush in the ravine. Then he said to the Kenites, 'Go away, leave the Amalekites so that I do not destroy you along with them; for you showed kindness to all the Israelites when they came up out of Egypt.' So the Kenites moved away from the Amalekites." (1 Samuel 15:5-6)

The first two peoples Israel encountered after Egypt were the Amalekites (who attacked) and the Kenites, Jethro's family (who helped). The Amalekites fought Israel; the Kenites sought God's blessing and worshiped Israel's God. 

Saul offers the Amalekites a way out. The Kenites could leave freely. Even the Amalekites could escape if they abandoned their identity and opposition to Israel. When a city faces destruction, people flee if they choose to. Those who remain do so by their own decision.

The command is not "kill everyone unconditionally," but rather "those who refuse to surrender their hostility toward Israel and cling to their identity as Amalekites will face judgment." Escape was available to all willing to let go of their opposition to God's people.

### Application: The Consequences of Rebellion

We have all spent our lives attacking God, rebelling against His purposes. Some of us have recognized we cannot win against God and have chosen peace—the "Kenite option." But others continue fighting like the Amalekites, attacking God and His people despite His goodness to us.

What will be your fate if you persist in rebellion? The Amalekites faced judgment across generations—you and your family, and your children as you've taught them. But it's not too late. There's still time to surrender, to ask for peace, to move to safety.

> "While we were God's enemies, we were reconciled to him through the death of his Son." (Romans 5:10)
> "Since we have been justified through faith, we have peace with God through our Lord Jesus Christ." (Romans 5:1)

This is what being Christian means: choosing peace with God over endless rebellion.

## Part Two: Obedience is Better Than Sacrifice (1 Samuel 15:9-35)

The same rules that applied to the Amalekites apply equally to Israel's king. Notice what Saul does:

> "But Saul and the army spared Agag and the best of the sheep and cattle, the fat calves and lambs—everything that was good. These they were unwilling to destroy completely, but everything that was despised and weak they totally destroyed." (1 Samuel 15:9)

Saul disobeyed. God's response was immediate:

> "I regret that I have made Saul king, because he has turned away from me and has not carried out my instructions." (1 Samuel 15:11)

Samuel confronts Saul:

> "Why did you not obey the Lord? Why did you pounce on the plunder and do evil in the eyes of the Lord?" (1 Samuel 15:19)

What follows reveals Saul's true heart through his responses.

### Saul's Excuses

**He wanted his own glory.** When Samuel arrives, Saul isn't there:

> "Saul has gone to Carmel. There he has set up a monument in his own honor." (1 Samuel 15:12)

Yet Samuel reminds him:

> "Although you were once small in your own eyes, did you not become the head of the tribes of Israel? The Lord anointed you king over Israel." (1 Samuel 15:17)

Saul's honor should come from God. Did he think his military success was his own achievement?

**He blamed others.** When confronted about the animals:

> "The soldiers brought them from the Amalekites; they spared the best of the sheep and cattle to sacrifice to the Lord your God, but we totally destroyed the rest." (1 Samuel 15:15)

He contradicts himself—first claiming he obeyed, now claiming the soldiers acted without his knowledge.

**He gave in to pressure.** Later, Saul confesses:

> "I have sinned. I violated the Lord's command and your instructions. I was afraid of the men and so I gave in to them." (1 Samuel 15:24)

He chose the soldiers' approval over God's command.

**His "apology" was hollow.** When Samuel turns to leave, Saul grabs his robe and it tears. Samuel declares:

> "The Lord has torn the kingdom of Israel from you today and has given it to one of your neighbors—to one better than you." (1 Samuel 15:28)

But even facing rejection, Saul's concern remains public perception:

> "I have sinned. But please honor me before the elders of my people and before Israel; come back with me, so that I may worship the Lord your God." (1 Samuel 15:30)

His pronouns betray his heart: "Please honor *me*... come back with *me*... so that *I* may worship."

Compare this to David's prayer:

> "Against you, you only, have I sinned and done what is evil in your sight; so you are right in your verdict and justified when you judge." (Psalm 51:4)

True repentance focuses on God, not on protecting one's reputation.

**He never fully obeyed.** It falls to Samuel to finish the work:

> "Bring me Agag king of the Amalekites. Agag came to him in chains. But Samuel said, 'As your sword has made women childless, so will your mother be childless among women.' And Samuel put Agag to death before the Lord at Gilgal." (1 Samuel 15:32-33)

Saul couldn't complete even the partial obedience he'd already compromised.

### The Heart of Saul's Failure

Saul fundamentally believed sacrifice was better than obedience. He rationalizes:

> "I went on the mission the Lord assigned me. I completely destroyed the Amalekites and brought back Agag their king. The soldiers took sheep and cattle from the plunder, the best of what was devoted to God, in order to sacrifice them to the Lord your God at Gilgal." (1 Samuel 15:20-21)

He claims obedience while openly disobeying. His reasoning: "What difference does it make whether I kill the animals at Amalek or at Gilgal?" 

But it makes all the difference. God didn't want a story of conquest and plunder. This was about justice, not wealth. By taking the animals, Saul redefined God's purposes. Like Adam and Eve in the garden, he decided for himself what was good and evil:

> "Saul and the army spared Agag and the best of the sheep and cattle... everything that was good. These they were unwilling to destroy completely, but everything that was despised and weak they totally destroyed." (1 Samuel 15:9)

"I say it's good, so I won't destroy it." They didn't want to destroy the animals because of selfishness—in sacrifices, the priests often kept portions of meat as food. They "pounced on the plunder" just as they had done before:

> "They pounced on the plunder and, taking sheep, cattle and calves, they butchered them on the ground and ate them, together with the blood." (1 Samuel 14:32)

Samuel's response is devastating:

> "Does the Lord delight in burnt offerings and sacrifices as much as in obeying the Lord? To obey is better than sacrifice, and to heed is better than the fat of rams. For rebellion is like the sin of divination, and arrogance like the evil of idolatry. Because you have rejected the word of the Lord, he has rejected you as king." (1 Samuel 15:22-23)

### Understanding Sacrifice Rightly

Don't misunderstand—sacrifice itself is good:

> "Bulls or rams or goats are the animals that you may burn on the altar as sacrifices to please me. The smell of the smoke from these sacrifices is pleasing to me." (Leviticus 15:1-3)

God is pleased by sacrifice. But He is not pleased when you disobey one law in order to follow another. The Scriptures repeatedly emphasize what matters more than sacrifice:

**Righteousness and justice:** "To do righteousness and justice is more acceptable to the Lord than sacrifice." (Proverbs 21:3)

**Listening:** "Guard your steps when you go to the house of God. To draw near to listen is better than to offer the sacrifice of fools, for they do not know that they are doing evil." (Ecclesiastes 5:1)

**Knowledge of God:** "For I desire steadfast love and not sacrifice, the knowledge of God rather than burnt offerings." (Hosea 6:6)

**Love:** "To love him with all the heart and with all the understanding and with all the strength, and to love one's neighbor as oneself, is much more than all whole burnt offerings and sacrifices." (Mark 12:33)

**Justice and mercy:** "Woe to you, scribes and Pharisees, hypocrites! For you tithe mint and dill and cumin, and have neglected the weightier matters of the law: justice and mercy and faithfulness. These you ought to have done, without neglecting the others." (Matthew 23:23)

### An Illustration

It's like when my child is supposed to do her chores or homework but skips them to play with her siblings. "But Dad, you always said to love my siblings—I'm doing that right now!"

That's legalism. She's following one law (love your siblings) by breaking another (do your homework)—usually for an ulterior motive (laziness) or because one law is easier than the other.

Saul did the same: he chose the law of sacrifices (which could happen anytime, which God never commanded) to avoid the obvious duty (cleanse the land). Usually for a selfish motive (keeping desirable animals for food).

### Application Today

We do this constantly. We struggle to distinguish sacrifice from obedience, but here's the point: **being Christian isn't compartmentalizing one law against another or one activity against another. It's all of life, every day, all of Scripture.**

We can't nullify one Scripture with another. We might:

- Steal from someone but justify it by giving to the church (breaking one law to keep another)
- Work hard to provide for our families but neglect being present for our children (claiming work-sacrifice exempts us from parental duty)
- Fulfill religious duties—attending church, praying—and skip loving and encouraging others (as Hebrews 10 calls us to do)
- Consider ourselves good people because we avoid smoking or drugs, then ignore God in other ways
- Perform our "Sunday Christian" sacrifice then live like pagans the rest of the week

The point isn't that you shouldn't sacrifice. Rather, **sacrifice without obedience is useless. Sacrifice is not equivalent to obedience—sacrifice is what follows obedience.**

## Part Three: Obedience and Sacrifice Together

We understand obedience is better than sacrifice because the ultimate sacrifice has already been provided:

> "It is impossible for the blood of bulls and goats to take away sins. Consequently, when Christ came into the world, he said, 'Sacrifices and offerings you have not desired, but a body have you prepared for me; in burnt offerings and sin offerings you have taken no pleasure.'" (Hebrews 10:4)

There is only one person who is both perfectly obedient and offers sacrifice righteously without one undermining the other: Jesus Christ.

Since He has provided the sacrifice, all that remains for us is to obey. To put our faith in Him.

The world says: "You must do, do, do." Christ says: "It is finished. All is done."

### Margaret Court: Obedience Over Glory

Margaret Court isn't popular these days. She was ABC Sportsman of the Year in 1963 and 1970, received the Order of Australia, an honorary doctorate in Law, had her likeness on a stamp, and won tennis's highest honor—the Philippe Chatrier Award.

But she fell out of favor when she spoke out against Qantas's support for same-sex marriage, expressing her Christian convictions on this issue. (If you're unfamiliar with the Christian position on this, I welcome conversation—there are thoughtful, logical, non-homophobic reasons Christians hold this view.)

The response was fierce. The media ridiculed her, celebrities condemned her, activists called for removing her from the tennis hall of fame and renaming the Margaret Court Arena. They will likely succeed in vilifying and disgracing the most decorated tennis player in history simply for speaking her convictions.

Why would she do this?

Because Margaret Court is a born-again Christian. After her tennis career, she became a Christian, a Bible teacher, and started a church. Unlike other prominent church leaders, Court is using her platform to speak truth according to her convictions.

People ask: "Won't you lose everything you worked for? What about those sacrifices in your past? You're wasting them!"

Her answer, by her actions: **Obedience is better than sacrifice.**

She's willing to give up the trophies, the stadium bearing her name, the worldly glory and honor that comes from being the greatest—in order to gain honor from God. "Can't you glorify God through tennis?" Not if it comes at the expense of obedience.

As you begin a new week, may you be emboldened to obey God—not for the approval of people, not for the comfort of compromise, but because obedience is better than any sacrifice the world offers.
