## Introduction: What Are We Here For?

The fundamental question we must ask ourselves is: What is the purpose of man? If we understand this, we can direct our efforts accordingly. Whether we chase money, possessions, or pleasure, we're essentially asking the same question: What's the end game? What are we truly working toward?

Last week, we examined the creation account in Genesis 1 with its poetic rhythm: "and God said, and it was good" across six days of creation. This week, Genesis 2 presents something different—a second account that initially seems to contradict the first.

**Genesis 1 answers:** Who is God? (contrasting pagan deities)  
**Genesis 2 answers:** What is the purpose of man?

These aren't sequential accounts but different perspectives on creation—one poetic and cosmic, the other focused and practical.

## Part 1: Man's Purpose—To Cultivate and Serve

### The Pattern in Genesis 2:4-17

Reading Genesis 2 carefully, we see a repeating pattern:

**The land is uncultivated.** Verse 5 tells us there was no vegetation yet because "there was no man to work the ground."

**The land needs water.** Verse 6 describes streams coming up from the earth to water the ground—but water alone isn't enough.

**The land needs Adam.** In verse 7, God forms man from dust and breathes into him the breath of life. The earth needs someone to tend it, to irrigate it, to care for it.

This pattern repeats when God plants the Garden of Eden (verses 8-9). Eden is beautiful and fruitful, yet it still needs water (the four rivers of verses 10-14) and it still needs Adam's work and care (verse 15):

> The LORD God took the man and put him in the Garden of Eden to work it and take care of it.

**Conclusion:** Adam is given a clear purpose—to serve the world by cultivating it, by working the ground, by ruling over creation and bringing order to it.

### What Does It Mean to Serve the World?

We serve the world through our work. We build, we clear land, we plant forests. We care for creation and its inhabitants. We work not just to provide for ourselves, but to lift others' burdens, to be generous and helpful, to make the earth a better place.

However, sin corrupts this work. Genesis 3 tells us that sin has poisoned humanity's ability to serve. Yet we look forward to a new creation where work will be pure again—where we're never bored, never caught in politics, and our labor is never wasted.

This raises an important question: Is it worth investing in work now if it will all be destroyed?

**Yes and no.** Yes, because our work still does good today. But no in the sense that we shouldn't stake our entire lives on material accomplishments. That beautiful harbor bridge, seventy-five years old, will one day be gone. The materials fade away.

**But there is work that lasts forever.** Gospel work. This isn't limited to ministers—every Christian does this. Whenever you speak to someone about Jesus, you're building something that will never perish or fade. You're serving the world with something eternal.

## Part 2: A Helper Is Needed—The Purpose of Marriage

### Why a Helper?

After establishing man's task, God says something remarkable: "It is not good for the man to be alone. I will make a helper suitable for him" (verse 18).

God brings all the animals to Adam—livestock, birds, beasts of the field. Adam names them all, but among them, "for Adam no suitable helper was found" (verse 20).

Why? Because the task is too great for one person alone. The work of serving the world—of cultivating, building, raising children, passing on truth to future generations—requires partnership.

### The Creation of Woman

Then God does something beautiful: He causes Adam to fall into deep sleep, takes one of his ribs, and creates a woman (verses 21-22). This isn't about romance or loneliness being cured by a pet. This is about partnership in purpose.

**What can a woman do that a man cannot?** Children. Only through woman can man have children—more workers to serve the world, more carriers of truth, more generations to continue the work long after the parents are gone.

Man and woman together create stability in which children can grow, learn, and eventually serve. This is why they enter into a covenant:

> For this reason a man will leave his father and mother and be united to his wife, and they will become one flesh.

Bone of my bones, flesh of my flesh. Through good times and bad times, thick and thin, they serve together—naked, without shame, without fear.

## Application: Marriage and Singleness

### If You Are Struggling with Loneliness

Some argue that loneliness justifies any relationship—whether same-sex partnerships or casual cohabitation. But the Bible teaches us that companionship doesn't require marriage or sex.

Look at 1 John 4:7-21—a profound teaching on love with nothing to do with sex. Or 1 Thessalonians 2:6-8, where Paul describes the deep love he has for the church through shared life. Even 1 Corinthians 13, the great hymn of love, isn't about marriage. Jesus called His disciples not "lovers" but "friends" (John 15:15).

Marriage isn't the only answer to loneliness. If you feel alone, that's okay. Find friends. Build community. Share your life with others who love Jesus.

### If You're Considering Marriage

If you seek to marry, do it for the right reasons. Not simply to have a permanent companion (though you will), but to serve the world together—and in these last days, to serve with the gospel.

Two are better than one. Together, you can reach non-Christian friends and families more effectively. Together, you can raise children in the faith and pass the gospel to the next generation. Remember: the gospel is only one generation away from extinction. It spreads not through organizations or creeds, but through people sharing faith with people.

This has profound implications for dating. Ask yourself: Does this person want to serve the gospel? How can you serve the gospel together if your spouse doesn't believe as you do? How can they help you reach a lost world if they don't understand the gospel themselves?

Look instead for a helper—a godly man or woman ready to serve, ready to raise children in faith, ready to build something eternal together. Physical beauty fades, but a partner committed to gospel work and godliness endures.

### If You Never Marry

And if you never marry, that's okay too. You are not alone. Your purpose can still be fulfilled through gospel work, through friendships, through the body of Christ. Singleness is not a second-class calling. It's a valid and honored way to serve the world.
