# James 2:1-15

**Title:** The Normal Life of Service
**Date:** 29 January 2023
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## The Life of Service

> Therefore, I urge you, brothers and sisters, in view of God's mercy, to offer your bodies as a living sacrifice, holy and pleasing to God—this is your true and proper worship. (Romans 12:1)

God's mercy unveiled throughout Romans chapters 1-11 brings us to this pivotal call: we are to present ourselves as living sacrifices. This is fundamentally different from the Old Testament temple sacrifices. Unlike the Levites who were conscripted into service, or Nazarites whose parents dedicated them—we must voluntarily offer ourselves. We are not forced, not compelled by birth or obligation. This voluntary offering is what makes it worship that is both rational and considered.

The word translated "worship" (latreia) literally means "service in the temple"—it was the work of the priests and Levites. Now Paul invites us into that same service, offering our very bodies as instruments of God's kingdom. The opposite of idolatry is not independence or autonomy; it is service to the living God.

As we return to normal life after the season of refreshment, we must ask: What does this normal life of service look like?

---

## 1. Serve with Your Mind

> Do not conform to the pattern of this world, but be transformed by the renewing of your mind. Then you will be able to test and approve what God's will is—his good, pleasing and perfect will. (Romans 12:2)

True transformation begins not with emotion or feeling, but with the renewal of our minds. The world around us constantly pressures us toward self-centeredness—the "i-generation," the age of self-service. But Paul calls us to think differently, to think deeply and carefully about what we do and why.

The phrase "true and proper worship" carries the Greek word *logikos*, from which we derive "logic." Your service to God is to be **intelligent service**—reasoned, considered, thought-out. You cannot serve God properly without engaging your mind.

**The Danger of Good Intentions Without Thinking:**

There are many people with good intentions. Yet "the road to hell is paved with good intentions" because they do not think. Consider the debate over Australia Day. Many propose changing it without careful consideration of consequences. Or consider political movements driven by emotion rather than reasoned analysis—good intentions that cause real harm when not paired with disciplined thought.

This is why the Christian faith is fundamentally a religion of the mind. Education itself emerged and flourished wherever Christianity spread. The monasteries preserved learning through the Dark Ages. The Reformation drove the development of the printing press so people could read Scripture for themselves. Universities—Cambridge, Oxford, Harvard, Princeton—originated as seminaries. Australia's first schoolhouse was built by Rev. Richard Johnson in 1793, serving dual purpose as both church and school. Why? Because Christians understood that you cannot read the Bible if you are illiterate.

The Puritans, Wesleyans, Presbyterians, and Lutherans all established schools where the Bible was the central study. Public libraries in the 1790s were run by churches—open to the public, but by Christian initiative. Schools and universities are not incidental to Christianity; they are its fruit.

If you call yourself a Christian, you cannot leave your mind at home. Superstition has no place in our faith. We are guided not by feng shui, tarot cards, or the reading of signs—we are guided by Scripture, a text that can be studied, memorized, contemplated, and understood through careful attention to context and meaning.

This is why churches have Sunday school and Bible study. This is why we bring our Bibles and are expected to think, to learn, to understand God's will. Yes, it can be difficult to sit through a talk when you're young. But discipline in learning is a gift—it teaches you how to learn in every area of life.

## 2. Serve with Your Limbs

> For just as each of us has one body with many members, and these members do not all have the same function, so in Christ we, though many, form one body, and each member belongs to all the others. (Romans 12:4-5)

> We have different gifts, according to the grace given to each of us. If your gift is prophesying, then prophesy in accordance with your faith; if it is serving, then serve; if it is teaching, then teach; if it is to encourage, then give encouragement; if it is giving, then give generously; if it is to lead, do it diligently; if it is to show mercy, do it cheerfully. (Romans 12:6-8)

The church is a body. Just as a human body has different limbs that perform different functions, so the body of Christ consists of individual believers with different gifts and callings. This is not about what you want to do or what you're best at—that's too ego-centric. Instead, Paul teaches us to think first and foremost: **What is needed? Where is the opportunity? What does my brother or sister require?**

> For by the grace given me I say to every one of you: Do not think of yourself more highly than you ought, but rather think of yourself with sober judgment, in accordance with the faith God has distributed to each of you. (Romans 12:3)

In a passage full of thinking and reasoning, Paul here instructs us *not to think* of ourselves with inflated importance. Think with sober judgment. Ask: Where is the gap? How can my gifts fill this need? Only then do you ask what limbs you have to offer.

**Serving in Small Things:**

Serving is not always glamorous. If rice and drink spill on the floor, someone will slip or the mess will spread. Do you have the limbs to clean it? If the toilet is left in an unpleasant state, the next person will have an unpleasant experience. Do you have five seconds and a toilet brush? If someone in your church is struggling in faith, do you not have a few words of encouragement to offer? Does their struggle matter less because it's not "strategic"?

What is needed? Where can you be most useful? This requires humility. Our abilities are not meant for the praise and recognition of others—they are meant for an unseen King.

**The Standard of Judgment:**

Matthew 25 reveals God's perspective on how we use our limbs:

> When the Son of Man comes in his glory, and all the angels with him, he will sit on his throne in heavenly glory. All the nations will be gathered before him, and he will separate the people one from another as a shepherd separates the sheep from the goats. He will put the sheep on his right and the goats on his left.
>
> Then the King will say to those on his right, 'Come, you who are blessed by my Father; take your inheritance, the kingdom prepared for you since the creation of the world. For I was hungry and you gave me something to eat, I was thirsty and you gave me something to drink, I was a stranger and you invited me in, I needed clothes and you clothed me, I was sick and you looked after me, I was in prison and you came to visit me.' (Matthew 25:31-36)

The righteous were surprised. They didn't realize they were serving Jesus himself. But in every act of mercy—feeding the hungry, welcoming the stranger, clothing the naked, visiting the sick—they were serving the King.

We serve the stranger. Perhaps a new visitor comes to church—we welcome them. When you have a baby, you welcome a stranger into your home: someone you don't yet know, yet you feed them, clothe them, care for them when they're sick. This too is mercy.

> Then the righteous will answer him, 'Lord, when did we see you hungry and feed you, or thirsty and give you something to drink? When did we see you a stranger and invite you in, or needing clothes and clothe you? When did we see you sick or in prison and go to visit you?'
>
> The King will reply, 'I tell you the truth, whatever you did for one of the least of these brothers of mine, you did for me.' (Matthew 25:37-40)

There are many ways to serve in this church. Take time to observe where help is needed—often it happens before and after the service when many have already left. Or fill out a comment card expressing your interest in serving, and we can connect you with the needs around us. Various serving teams would welcome your participation.

## 3. Serve with Love

> Love must be sincere. Hate what is evil; cling to what is good. Be devoted to one another in love. Honor one another above yourselves. Never be lacking in zeal, but keep your spiritual fervor, serving the Lord. Be joyful in hope, patient in affliction, faithful in prayer. Share with the Lord's people who are in need. Practice hospitality.
>
> Bless those who persecute you; bless and do not curse. Rejoice with those who rejoice; mourn with those who mourn. Live in harmony with one another. Do not be proud, but be willing to associate with people of low position. Do not be conceited. (Romans 12:9-16)

In all that we do, our motivation must be love. The church is not a restaurant where things are done for you and you consume passively. The church is more like a healthy family kitchen—a place where everyone is committed and involved in serving one another, where there is genuine love and connection.

In a healthy family, not the mother alone works while children eat and run off to play. Rather, everyone participates. Everyone is invested. Everyone talks to each other. Everyone serves out of love.

Different people experience and express love differently. The "Five Love Languages" concept recognizes that people receive love through different means: words of affirmation, quality time, gifts, physical touch, and acts of service. But if there is one language that captures God's love toward us, it is acts of service. God served us on the cross. Our love language to Him and to one another must be service.

**Serving in Everyday Life:**

The normal Christian life is service. It might seem monotonous to go to work, but you are serving—serving your boss, your customers, your clients, your family through the provision you make. That is okay. It might feel hard to care for children or grandchildren when you have greater gifts and talents. It might feel degrading. But the opportunity is there to be present, to care, to serve. That is okay.

Normal life is serving. And that is exactly as it should be.

## 4. Serving Those Outside the Faith

> Do not repay anyone evil for evil. Be careful to do what is right in the eyes of everyone. If it is possible, as far as it depends on you, live at peace with everyone. Do not take revenge, my dear friends, but leave room for God's wrath, for it is written: "It is mine to avenge; I will repay," says the Lord. On the contrary:
>
> "If your enemy is hungry, feed him; if he is thirsty, give him something to drink. In doing this, you will heap burning coals on his head."
>
> Do not be overcome by evil, but overcome evil with good. (Romans 12:17-21)

Our service extends even to those outside the faith—even to our enemies. We do not return evil for evil. We do not seek revenge. Instead, we overcome evil with good. We feed our enemies when they hunger. We give them drink when they thirst.

This is not weakness. It is the triumph of love over hatred, of mercy over judgment. When we respond to evil with kindness, we demonstrate the transforming power of Christ's gospel. We show that there is a better way.
