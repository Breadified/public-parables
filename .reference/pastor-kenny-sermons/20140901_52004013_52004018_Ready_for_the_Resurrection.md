# 1 Thessalonians 4:13-18

**Title:** Ready for the Resurrection
**Date:** 7 September 2014 (SMAC) / 28 May 2023 (ARISE)
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Opening: A Community in Grief

We gather today with heavy hearts. One of our brothers in Christ, Chris Chan, passed away last Sunday night, likely from a heart condition. He was 28 years old. It came as a shock to us all—just days before, we had celebrated together at a wedding and spent time in fellowship. Now he is gone.

The family is organizing a funeral and welcomes your support. During our collection time, you may contribute to help cover funeral expenses. If you bank transfer, please note "Chris" in the description. The family is also collecting letters and memories—please speak with Isaac if you'd like to contribute.

This loss is deeply felt. But our passage today speaks directly to our grief, to Chris's memory, and to the baptism candidates among us, addressing the profound questions we all face about death and eternity.

## The Grief of Missing Out (GOMO)

We're all familiar with FOMO—fear of missing out. Today we speak of GOMO: grief of missing out.

When someone passes, especially when young, we grieve on two levels. We miss their company, their laughter, their encouragement. But we also grieve *for them*, mourning the life they'll miss—marriage, children, grandchildren, the fullness that a complete life brings.

This is where Paul's letter meets us. The Thessalonians faced a pressing concern: What of those who die before Christ's return? Will they miss out on the resurrection?

## 1. The Dead Do Not Miss Out

The Thessalonians were the first generation of Christians. Expecting Christ's imminent return, some had even abandoned work and marriage, convinced He would come in their lifetime. But as believers began to die, a painful question arose: *Will the dead miss the resurrection?*

Paul's answer is clear and emphatic. Notice the key verses:

> For we believe that Jesus died and rose again, and so we believe that God will bring with Jesus those who have fallen asleep in him. (1 Thessalonians 4:14)
>
> According to the Lord's word, we tell you that we who are still alive, who are left until the coming of the Lord, will certainly not precede those who have fallen asleep. (1 Thessalonians 4:15)
>
> For the Lord himself will come down from heaven, with a loud command, with the voice of the archangel and with the trumpet call of God, and the dead in Christ will rise first. (1 Thessalonians 4:16)

Paul uses the metaphor of "sleep" for death—not because death is the end, but because it resembles sleep from which we awaken. Jesus Himself used this language when speaking of Lazarus: "Our friend Lazarus has fallen asleep; but I am going there to wake him up" (John 11:11). The dead will be raised from their graves.

Our confidence rests on a historical fact: **Christ's resurrection guarantees ours.** Death has already been defeated. Jesus rose two thousand years ago, and because He rose, so shall we.

> Brothers and sisters, we do not want you to be uninformed about those who sleep in death, so that you do not grieve like the rest of mankind, who have no hope. (1 Thessalonians 4:13)

Here is the critical distinction: Christians grieve, but we grieve differently. We grieve *with hope*. Death is sorrowful, and some carry such heavy grief they cannot move forward for years. But for those in Christ, death is temporary—like sleep before the morning comes. We will see our loved ones again.

## 2. Guaranteed by Christ's Resurrection

Jesus personally oversees the resurrection. This is no delegated task:

> The Lord himself will come down from heaven. (1 Thessalonians 4:16)

When something truly matters, leadership handles it personally. At work, tasks are delegated, but when something critically important is at stake, the head office comes down to your desk to ensure it's done right. So it is with the resurrection—Christ Himself descends.

The fanfare accompanying His arrival fulfills Old Testament prophecies about God's rescue:

> In that day the Lord will thresh from the flowing Euphrates to the Wadi of Egypt, and you, O Israelites, will be gathered up one by one. And in that day a great trumpet will sound. (Isaiah 27:12-13)

> The Lord will march out like a mighty man, like a warrior he will stir up his zeal; with a shout he will raise the battle cry and will triumph over his enemies. (Isaiah 42:13)

> The Sovereign Lord will sound the trumpet; he will march in the storms of the south, and the Lord Almighty will shield them. The Lord their God will save his people on that day as a shepherd saves his flock. (Zechariah 14:14-16)

A people oppressed by enemies need a defender. God comes Himself to rescue them.

The resurrection arrives with:
- A loud command
- The voice of the archangel  
- The trumpet call of God

We are oppressed by death. Though mortally wounded, it still haunts us, curses us, mocks us. But God promises one final rescue: Jesus will come and deliver us from death through the resurrection. There is no way any of us will miss it. Everyone will know.

His coming will not be secret or invisible. Some cults teach that Jesus returned invisibly in 1914, walking the earth unseen. This contradicts Scripture entirely. When Christ comes, it will be unmistakable—like an invasion with helicopters and ground troops, or a major event that shuts down the internet. Everyone will know.

> And so we will be with the Lord forever. And the dead in Christ will rise first. (1 Thessalonians 4:16-17)

**Application:** For the Christian, death is only temporary. Baptism symbolizes this: we are buried in water (representing death and the old self) and rise again (representing new life in Christ). This doesn't mean sudden perfection—your families can attest to that next week! But it means we commit to living Christ's way.

The Christian life is often difficult. We may miss worldly temptations designed to pull us from Christ. We may miss opportunities that come with prioritizing Jesus above career and comfort. We may miss temporal comfort, health, and ease.

But we will *not* miss this: the new resurrection when sin and death are finally defeated. There will be no more tears, no more grief, no more fragility. We will no longer be weak. No matter what—even if struck by a bus—if we belong to Christ, we will not miss out. Death comes only on God's timing, and even then, we will rise.

## 3. In Fact, First, Not Last

But there's something more remarkable: **the dead don't just participate—they go first.**

> The dead in Christ will rise first. (1 Thessalonians 4:16)
>
> We who are still alive, who are left until the coming of the Lord, will certainly not precede those who have fallen asleep. (1 Thessalonians 4:15)

God operates on a FIFO principle: First In, First Out.

Those who die in Christ will be the first to experience new bodies. The first to see Jesus. The first to experience true rest. The first to enjoy a sinless life. The first to experience eternity.

As Paul wrote elsewhere: "To live is Christ. To die is gain" (Philippians 1:21). They are not missing out—they are ahead of us.

After the dead rise, the living follow:

> After that, we who are still alive and are left will be caught up together with them in the clouds to meet the Lord in the air. And so we will be with the Lord forever. Therefore encourage one another with these words. (1 Thessalonians 4:17-18)

## The Case of Chris

Chris didn't miss out. For those here who didn't know him well, understand that Chris was socially awkward, faced learning difficulties, and sometimes struggled socially. By worldly measures, one might think he missed out on much of life.

But he didn't.

Last weekend was a great weekend for Chris. He woke at 6 AM for a run in Cherrybrook. Quentin gave him a ride to North Sydney to celebrate his close friends' wedding. He wore his famous formal shorts, sat with friends at a fancy restaurant in Mosman overlooking the Sydney Heads with blue sky and white yachts, sipping orange juice. That evening he spent time with the guys over movies and dinner. Sunday, he was with us at church, had lunch together, finished his Bible quiet time with his father. He was surrounded all weekend by friends and family who loved and cared for him.

He did not miss out.

Most importantly, he will not miss out on the resurrection. In fact, by God's timing, he's beaten all of us to it.

## The Challenge Before Us

Today is complex. We grieve, yet we also celebrate Chris's life and the baptism of our brothers. We can do both.

These new believers, having died symbolically to their old selves in baptism, now rise to live a new resurrected life. Like Chris, their confidence is in Christ.

Let us stick around for lunch and encourage one another with these words. He was ready for Christ. Let's get ourselves ready also.

## A Call to Hope

For those here today not yet confident in Christ, you have an opportunity. If you're uncertain about where you'll ultimately go, please speak with one of us or mark your comment card. We would love to give you hope.

For those of us already in Christ, remember: Death will not be the end. And what happens in the afterlife should profoundly shape how we live now.

Maybe things aren't going well for you at the moment. There is a light at the end of the tunnel—not death, but resurrection. Don't be anxious about missing opportunities or not getting everything from this life. Things may not turn out as you thought. But we already see the end of the story.

At heart, Christians are optimists. The culture around us despairs, complains, offers nothing. But we have hope. Not wishful thinking or mere sentiment, but grounded in the historical resurrection of Jesus Christ, witnessed by five hundred people.

Therefore, encourage one another. Let us gather together, support one another, and remind each other constantly that there is hope in the future. In grieving those we've lost, we grieve with hope. In death, we die in hope. And those we leave behind will grieve with hope.

Because we have been intertwined with Christ through faith—and death cannot break that bond.
