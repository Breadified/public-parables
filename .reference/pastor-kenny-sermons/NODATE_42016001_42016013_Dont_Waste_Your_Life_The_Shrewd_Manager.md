# Luke 16:1-13

**Title:** Don't Waste Your Life: The Shrewd Manager

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Understanding "Shrewd"

The word "shrewd" appears in this passage and describes something we don't often talk about. Shrewd means showing accurate judgment—keen awareness and sharp intelligence. It's being clever, alert, and perceptive. Think of characters like James Bond or Jason Bourne: they're shrewd. They assess situations carefully and act with intelligence.

I want to be shrewd in how we organize and lead. I want to ensure we care for people appropriately, utilize our leaders well, use our time wisely, and preach Jesus accurately—all done shrewdly, with clear thinking and smart judgment. The opposite of shrewd is stupid, and none of us wants to do anything stupidly.

In Luke 16, Jesus tells us about a shrewd manager. But notice: shrewd doesn't mean morally right. The manager in this story is shrewd and dishonest—smart but unethical.

## Part One: The Dishonest Manager's Scheme

> "There was a rich man whose manager was accused of wasting his possessions. So he called him in and asked him, 'What is this I hear about you? Give an account of your management, because you cannot be manager any longer.'" (Luke 16:1-2)

The manager faces the consequences of his misbehavior. He assesses his situation: he's losing his job, he's not strong enough for manual labor, and he's too proud to beg. So he devises a plan.

> "The manager said to himself, 'What shall I do now? My master is taking away my job. I'm not strong enough to dig, and I'm ashamed to beg.'" (Luke 16:3)

His strategy is brilliant but corrupt. He calls the people who owe his boss money and secretly alters their debts. He cuts a debt of 100 measures of oil (about 3,000 liters) in half. He reduces another debt of 100 measures of wheat (about 40,000 liters worth) by 20%. He's giving massive discounts at his boss's expense—all to create a network of indebted friends who will support him when he's unemployed.

His aim is clear: get rich. He's willing to be dishonest, to use what isn't even his own, to embezzle and scheme his way to security.

## Part Two: Money as Master

This is how the world works. The operating principle is: do what it takes to get ahead. If it means ignoring dodgy accounting, do it. If it means sleeping with the boss, do it. If it means overworking and neglecting family and church, do it. If it means cheating on exams or cutting corners or overstating productivity, do it. Money is the way you keep score, and the world commends shrewdness in its pursuit.

> "The master commended the dishonest manager because he had acted shrewdly." (Luke 16:8)

We see this principle everywhere. During the financial crisis, greedy bankers destroyed economies and still received their bonuses. People thought, "Wish I'd done that too!" The world admires the shrewd pursuit of wealth, even when it's corrupt.

For many of us, money has become our master too. In our society, money defines happiness, purpose, meaning, and identity. We've been told that more money means more stuff, more experiences, and therefore a happier life. Money determines our status—how important or useful we are. It defines our identity through what we can afford: our clothes, our travels, our lifestyle.

Money becomes a master because we must play by its rules to achieve a fulfilled life. It drives capitalism, specialization, globalization. We're all under this master. We play by money's rules. Most of us probably serve money as our current master.

Why are we pursuing certain courses or careers even though we don't enjoy them? Let's be honest—it's because of the money. Graduates earn twice as much as non-graduates and over a million dollars more in their lifetime. So we serve the master of money.

## Part Three: Jesus Calls Us to a Better Master

Jesus doesn't commend the dishonest manager's dishonesty. But He does make a sharp observation:

> "For the people of this world are more shrewd in dealing with their own kind than are the people of the light." (Luke 16:8)

Non-believers are far more shrewd and strategic in pursuing their worldly goals than Christians are in pursuing eternal ones. And Jesus is calling us to be even shrewder—not dishonest, but infinitely smarter about what matters.

> "I tell you, use worldly wealth to gain friends for yourselves, so that when it is gone, you will be welcomed into eternal dwellings." (Luke 16:9)

The shrewd manager ensures he'll be welcomed into earthly dwellings for the rest of his life. But Jesus points us to something far greater: eternal dwellings. While the world is clever about temporary gains, we should be far more clever about infinite returns.

Think about it this way: Would you spend the same amount on a disposable paper cup as on a reusable one? Would you pay the same for a plastic ring as a diamond ring? No—because we wisely invest more in what lasts longer. The shrewd person invests in diamonds and gold that appreciate over time. The fool spends everything on disposable cups.

The earth won't last. Heaven is eternal. What a waste to spend your whole life investing in the wrong thing—like a child collecting shells on the beach, unaware that the tide is coming in.

## Part Four: Be Faithful Now for Eternal Rewards

> "One who is faithful in a very little is also faithful in much, and one who is dishonest in a very little is also dishonest in much. If then you have not been faithful in the unrighteous wealth, who will entrust to you the true riches? And if you have not been faithful in that which is another's, who will give you that which is your own?" (Luke 16:10-12)

Jesus teaches us to be faithful with worldly wealth now in hope of being entrusted with true riches in heaven. What does this look like practically?

If the history of Jesus is keeping you from the kingdom, use the intelligence this world gave you to learn about the next world. Read books. Spend money on knowledge of God's kingdom. Use your time here and now to understand eternity.

Make friends with God the way you make friends with people. You know how to invest time—meeting strangers, adding them on social media, inviting them places. Meeting Jesus takes time too. Invest that time.

Most importantly, be faithful by realizing the amazing truth that Jesus Christ took the punishment for our sins so we can have a personal relationship with God. Being Christian means believing the Bible is God's truth and committing to serve God as your master. It means repenting from other masters and sins. It means putting your faith in Jesus—that He took the death penalty you deserved. It means turning to Scripture and following what it says.

Following Jesus is not a waste of life. It's the shrewdest investment you can make.

## Conclusion

Invest in the eternal: shrewd. Waste on the temporary: stupid. I don't want to be stupid. Be shrewd. Serve Jesus as your master, and spend your finite wealth gaining infinite returns.
