# Obadiah 8:1-15

**Title:** Giving: Encouraging Generosity
**Date:** 13 December 2009
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Pursuit of Excellence

What kind of excellence should we pursue as Christians? Australians excel in sport. Asian Australians compete fiercely in academics and careers. But what about in our faith?

Paul calls the Corinthian church to pursue a different kind of excellence—not academic achievement or athletic prowess, but excellence in the grace of giving.

> But just as you excel in everything—in faith, in speech, in knowledge, in complete earnestness and in your love for us—see that you also excel in this grace of giving. (2 Corinthians 8:7)

## 1. Excel in the Grace of Giving

Paul writes this letter from Macedonia to the Corinthians in the south, encouraging them to participate in a relief offering for poor Christians in Jerusalem. The Macedonian churches serve as his primary example.

### The Macedonian Example

The Macedonians had received the grace of God, and they responded with extraordinary generosity:

> Out of the most severe trial, their overflowing joy and their extreme poverty welled up in rich generosity. For I testify that they gave as much as they were able, and even beyond their ability. Entirely on their own, they urgently pleaded with us for the privilege of sharing in this service to the saints. And they did not do as we expected, but they gave themselves first to the Lord and then to us in keeping with God's will. (2 Corinthians 8:2-5)

Notice their circumstances: severe trial, extreme poverty. Yet from these difficult conditions flowed overflowing joy and rich generosity. They gave not just what they could afford, but beyond their ability. They didn't give because Paul pressured them—they begged for the opportunity to participate.

## 2. Three Principles for Giving

### a. Understanding Grace

At the heart of Christian giving is understanding what Jesus has done for us. Paul points to the ultimate example of generosity:

> For you know the grace of our Lord Jesus Christ, that though he was rich, yet for your sakes he became poor, so that you through his poverty might become rich. (2 Corinthians 8:9)

Jesus was eternally rich in glory and majesty. Yet He became poor—leaving His throne to be born as a helpless infant, living a humble earthly life, and ultimately giving His very life on the cross. He did this so that we might be spiritually rich, inheriting eternal life and resurrection hope.

This verse does not teach that Jesus wants us materially wealthy. Rather, it establishes the precedent: those who are rich in this world give so that others might be rich in the world to come.

When we truly grasp what Jesus gave up for us, we understand that our gifts to others are not losses but opportunities to mirror His grace. God doesn't want our money for itself—He became poor. He doesn't want to reclaim what He's given. Rather, He wants us to understand that gifts are meant to circulate, to bless others, not to end with ourselves.

Often we think: "God gave me this gift, so He wants me to enjoy it." But why arbitrarily assume that's God's will? Perhaps His will is that we receive His gifts in order to pass them on to others. The Macedonians grasped this. They received grace and saw it as an opportunity to give grace to others.

### b. Understanding Willingness of Heart

Paul emphasizes that sincerity of motivation matters more than the size of the gift:

> I am not commanding you, but I want to test the sincerity of your love by comparing it with the earnestness of others. (2 Corinthians 8:8)

The Macedonians "gave themselves first to the Lord." Their priority was obedience to God, not pleasing Paul or earning approval from others. They truly understood God's grace and acted from that understanding.

> For if the willingness is there, the gift is acceptable according to what one has, not according to what he does not have. (2 Corinthians 8:12)

A small gift given from a sincere, grateful heart is more valuable to God than a large gift given grudgingly or from obligation.

Think about Christmas giving. How often do we give gifts out of peer pressure, tradition, or guilt? We exchange items we don't want, regift, and waste money on things no one needs. Compare this to birthday giving, where people thoughtfully contribute because they genuinely want to bless someone.

God doesn't want Christmas-type giving—obligatory, impersonal, driven by duty or embarrassment. He wants the cheerful giver, the person whose heart has been transformed by understanding what Jesus gave up. Not someone motivated by guilt, but someone who has encountered grace and cannot help but be generous.

### c. Understanding Means

Willingness without action remains incomplete. Paul urges the Corinthians to match their desire with actual giving:

> Now finish the work, so that your eager willingness to do it may be matched by your completion of it, according to your means. (2 Corinthians 8:11)

The Macedonians had both the willing heart and they followed through according to their means—even beyond their means. The Corinthians had expressed desire to give, but now Paul calls them to complete that good intention rather than let it be eroded by apathy and time.

A noble thought unacted does no good. If we truly desire to be generous, we must translate that desire into concrete action, using the resources God has given us.

### d. Understanding Equality

Paul's ultimate vision for giving is not about laws or percentages, but about equality:

> Our desire is not that others might be relieved while you are hard pressed, but that there might be equality. At the present time your plenty will supply what they need, so that in turn their plenty will supply what you need. Then there will be equality, as it is written: "He who gathered much did not have too much, and he who gathered little did not have too little." (2 Corinthians 8:13-15)

Equality means no one has excessive abundance that breeds pride, and no one lacks the necessities that would drive them to despair. Paul calls for proportional giving and receiving so that the body of Christ is balanced and healthy.

John Wesley embodied this principle. Beginning in 1731, Wesley limited his expenses so he could give more to the poor. His income grew throughout his life—eventually reaching 1,400 pounds annually—but he kept his personal expenses at just 30 pounds per year, giving away the rest. Tax commissioners investigated him, convinced a man of his income must own expensive silver plates. He replied that he possessed only four silver spoons total, explaining, "I shall not buy any more while so many round me want bread." When Wesley died in 1791, his will contained only the coins found in his pockets. Of the 30,000 pounds he had earned in his lifetime, most had been given away.

Wesley refused to let his standard of living creep upward as his income increased. He made deliberate choices about simplicity early and maintained them throughout his life.

## Application: Living Out Giving

We must ask ourselves hard questions:

- Do we see money as a gift from God meant to bless ourselves alone, or as grace to be shared with others?
- Are we willing to become poorer so that others might be richer?
- Do we give only from our abundance, or are we willing to sacrifice?
- Have we truly allowed understanding of Jesus's sacrifice to transform our hearts?

Our lifestyle standards tend to creep upward as our income increases. Now is the time to set our standard of living deliberately, before we are earning real money and the temptations become overwhelming. Find those who live simply and model yourself after them. Compete with one another in generosity, modesty, and simplicity rather than in consumption.

For those committing to support the church financially, consider these practical steps based on your circumstances:

**Workers:** Set up a monthly direct debit so giving becomes regular and automatic.

**Students with part-time jobs:** Allocate a portion of your earnings to gospel work from the start, before the money feels like "yours to spend."

**Those still studying:** If you receive pocket money, consider setting aside a percentage. Give up a movie, a video game, or junk food once a week. Check with your parents about what's appropriate, but begin training yourself in generous stewardship.

The point is not a rigid percentage or legalistic requirement. The point is that understanding God's grace transforms us from the inside out, and that transformation naturally expresses itself in generous giving. Let the three principles guide you: understand grace, understand willingness, and understand your means. Then complete that willingness with action.
