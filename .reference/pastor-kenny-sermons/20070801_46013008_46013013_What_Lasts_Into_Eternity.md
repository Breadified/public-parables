# 1 Corinthians 13:8-13

**Title:** What Lasts Into Eternity?
**Date:** 13 August 2007
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction: What Lasts?

I recently stumbled upon a fascinating experiment from the documentary "Supersize Me." Researchers tested how long McDonald's French fries could last compared to regular fast food. They set up burgers and fries from McDonald's (cheeseburgers, Big Macs, Filet-O-Fish) alongside ordinary corner store burgers and fries.

Within the first week, the regular burgers started turning black and decomposing. The McDonald's burgers followed soon after, though the Big Mac was the slowest to decay. But the fries—nothing happened. Week after week, they remained perfect. No black spots, no mold. After ten weeks, the experiment had to be abandoned when someone accidentally threw the chips away, but they were still in pristine condition. Imagine that lasting in your stomach!

This question frames everything: **What lasts?**

A regular chip lasts maybe a day. Pizza lasts longer—though I've heard it lasts two weeks in some households. The clothes you're wearing last a couple of years, or maybe just one season if you're fashion-conscious. This weekend's homework, written neatly on lined paper—will you keep it next year? What about your house? Will it last your lifetime? Your children's lifetime?

Even impressive structures like the Opera House or the Harbour Bridge—which have stood for 75 years—eventually pass away. These are important questions because how we spend our time, money, and effort depends entirely on how long something lasts. Buying a house is a different decision than buying lunch. Studying for the HSC requires different commitment than studying a map to a birthday party. Building a paddle-pop stick bridge is not the same investment as building the Harbour Bridge.

Today's passage speaks directly to this question: **What lasts?**

## 1. Love Never Fails

> **1 Corinthians 13:8** Love never fails. But where there are prophecies, they will cease; where there are tongues, they will be stilled; where there is knowledge, it will pass away.

### Understanding "Never Fails"

**Love** here means loving others, doing what's best for them, being other-centered rather than selfish.

**Never fails** doesn't mean failing a test. Rather, the Greek word literally means to fall to one's ruin or destruction—to be done away with, come to an end, pass away. So Paul is declaring that love will not pass away. It will not come to an end.

Later Paul reinforces this:

> **1 Corinthians 13:13** And now these three remain: faith, hope and love. But the greatest of these is love.

Paul is saying love never falls away. It doesn't get destroyed. Its effects don't come to an end. **Love lasts.**

### The Context: Corinthian Conflict

The Corinthian church was divided over spiritual gifts and ministry roles. Members were arguing about whose contributions mattered most. The finger was saying to the eye, "I'm more important." The eye was saying to the foot, "I'm more important." Everyone was fighting over recognition of the popular, upfront gifts—prophecy, speaking in tongues, and knowledge.

In response, Paul declares:

> **Verses 8b-9a** But where there are prophecies, they will cease; where there are tongues, they will be stilled; where there is knowledge, it will pass away.

Prophecy—the proclamation of God's truth (not necessarily something spooky, but the wisdom to apply Scripture, to call people to repentance and faith)—will cease. Tongues—languages and translation, which were vital in that diverse, multilingual gathering—will be stilled. Knowledge itself will pass away. All these gifts, however impressive, have an expiration date.

The Corinthian situation was like a combined service with people speaking multiple languages. Everyone thought their role was most important: the preacher, the translator, the learned teacher. Paul's response cuts through all that: **None of these things will last.**

### Why This Matters

Paul seems contradictory at first. In Chapter 12, he ranks gifts:

> **1 Corinthians 12:28-31** And in the church God has appointed first of all apostles, second prophets, third teachers, then workers of miracles, also those having gifts of healing, those able to help others, those with gifts of administration, and those speaking in different kinds of tongues... But eagerly desire the greater gifts. And now I will show you the most excellent way.

And in Chapter 14, he says:

> **1 Corinthians 14:1** Follow the way of love and eagerly desire spiritual gifts, especially the gift of prophecy.

So is Paul saying gifts matter or not? Here's what he's really doing: **He's shifting our focus from function to motivation.** Gifts are important, but the motivation underlying those gifts is what truly matters.

Love is not just another gift to pursue. It's an entire way of life—a complete perspective that transcends tasks, functions, and talents. If believers focus too much on which gifts are most prestigious, they'll completely miss the crucial importance of **love as the character that should define every Christian.**

Without love as your foundation, every gift becomes worthless.

### Love as Way of Life

Love operates in two dimensions:

**Love as motivation** (Chapter 14 illustrates this):

> **1 Corinthians 14:4-5** He who speaks in a tongue edifies himself, but he who prophesies edifies the church... He who prophesies is greater than one who speaks in tongues, unless he interprets, so that the church may be edified.

The key word: **edified.** What will build others up? That's the question love asks. Not "What makes me look good?" but "What helps others?"

> **1 Corinthians 14:6,9** Now, brothers, if I come to you and speak in tongues, what good will I be to you, unless I bring you some revelation or knowledge or prophecy or word of instruction?... Unless you speak intelligible words with your tongue, how will anyone know what you are saying? You will just be speaking into the air.

**Love as attitude** (Chapter 13:4-7 describes this):

> **1 Corinthians 13:4-7** Love is patient, love is kind. It does not envy, it does not boast, it is not proud. It is not rude, it is not self-seeking, it is not easily angered, it keeps no record of wrongs. Love does not delight in evil but rejoices with the truth. It always protects, always trusts, always hopes, always perseveres.

Love affects everything you do—regardless of your gifts or abilities. It's not "I have the gift of love, so I'll be loving," or "I don't have the gift of love, so I can't be expected to care." No. Love is the lens through which you exercise every gift, every talent, every opportunity. The motivation and attitude of love must characterize your work. **That's what lasts forever.**

## 2. What Passes Away, What Endures

### All Knowledge and Wisdom Are Partial

> **1 Corinthians 13:9-10** For we know in part and we prophesy in part, but when perfection comes, the imperfect disappears.

No matter how clever or wise you are, your knowledge is only partial. Your prophecy, only partial. But when the end comes—when perfection arrives—all this partiality vanishes.

### Illustration: Like a Child

> **1 Corinthians 13:11** When I was a child, I talked like a child, I thought like a child, I reasoned like a child. When I became a man, I put childish ways behind me.

A child's knowledge is partial. Their reasoning is limited. But when adulthood comes, you leave childish ways behind. Your whole way of thinking changes. You used to think the world revolved around you, that your mother's job was to clean up after you, wash your clothes, cook for you. As an adult, you realize—well, life's a bit more complicated than that! You don't act or think like a child anymore.

### Illustration: Like a Mirror

> **1 Corinthians 13:12** Now we see but a poor reflection as in a mirror; then we shall see face to face. Now I know in part; then I shall know fully, even as I am fully known.

In New Testament times, mirrors were polished metal, not the clear glass we have today. A metal mirror gives only a poor, fuzzy reflection—missing details, incomplete. But when the end comes, we'll see face to face. No more incompleteness. Absolute clarity.

This reveals something sobering: **All our deeds now are imperfect and partial.** Can we really put confidence in what we can do, in the talents we possess, in the wonderful gifts we think we're contributing? How can we trust something so incomplete?

Consider this: You're reading one small paragraph in the Mitchell Library, which spans four basketball courts across three levels. How can you think, "I'm such a gift to this church—God's gift to you!"—when you really know just one paragraph out of an entire library of books? Or you know one webpage out of millions of webpages that exist?

In that reality, how can you think so highly of yourself? Don't you actually need the help of others—people who may have read a different page, but another page nonetheless? We're all working with partial information, partial abilities, partial understanding.

That's why Paul says: **There is one thing that lasts.**

Love does not fall away. Prophecies may pass. Knowledge may pass. Tongues may pass. But the **motivation of love** that characterizes your work lasts. The **attitudes of love** you put into your work last. That's what endures into eternity.

## 3. Application: Love in Every Context

### The Principle: Love Over Task

In our groups—and in all the groups you're part of (home, school, Bible study, friendships)—the application is straightforward: **We are to love.**

Love is more important than the actual task itself. Love must drive what we do, because we do things for the good of others. Love must characterize how we do things, with an attitude of care and concern.

### Example: Church Leadership Rosters

Consider our piano and chair-moving rosters. Everyone wants to play music, lead worship, or preach—those feel more attractive. But if you're a musician and there are already plenty of musicians, maybe the more loving choice is to say, "Perhaps it's more loving to move chairs instead."

It's not about music versus chairs. It's about **love.**

Before our gathering starts, musicians often congregate around the piano practicing. Meanwhile, a new person is wandering around looking lost. Or someone is sitting alone. Or the chairs haven't been set up yet. Wouldn't it be more loving for a couple of musicians to take the bold step of welcoming that lost person or helping with the chairs? We need ten ears but only one pair of hands. Can it really be loving if everyone gravitates to music?

### Example: Bible Study Groups

What about in your Bible study groups? Maybe your group is very quiet—not much participation. What could be more loving than speaking up to help the group, to support whoever's leading? By contrast, if your group is very talkative and nothing gets learned, maybe more loving to be quieter or gently redirect back to the study.

It's not really about being talkative or quiet. **It's about love.**

### Understanding God's "Gifts" Differently

The language of spiritual gifts can be misleading. We think, "God gave me this one grand gift—music, preaching, leadership." So we feel obligated to use it, even when there's no real need. "I have the gift of drumming, so I must drum—even though there are ten other drummers here!"

But actually, God has given each of us hundreds of gifts and opportunities:

- The ability to pick up trash from the floor
- The ability to move microphone stands to the cupboard
- The ability to stack chairs (or whatever you can do with your abilities)
- The ability to greet a stranger with warmth
- The ability to encourage someone with Scripture
- The ability to give someone a ride home
- The ability to listen
- The ability to lend books
- The ability to be generous

That list is endless. Better to think of these as **opportunities for love** rather than "gifts to use." You simply need to decide which of your many abilities are best exercised in light of love. What will serve others best? What will be most loving?

### Living This Out

God says: "Use all these abilities in light of love for people and for God's kingdom." Use them motivated by love, not selfish ambition, pride, or the desire for attention. Because if your motivation is to be a show pony—to look impressive—then even if you're great at it, **what comes of it? Nothing.** You become like a resounding gong or a clanging cymbal.

> **1 Corinthians 13:1** If I speak in the tongues of men and of angels, but have not love, I am only a resounding gong or a clanging cymbal.

That gift will pass away. There will be nothing left to show for it.

This principle extends far beyond your church group. It applies to school, university, work, and home. You have multiple gifts in those contexts too—use them out of love for others, out of a desire for them to follow Jesus.

### The Attitudes That Matter

Love isn't just about motivation; it's also about the attitude we bring to our work.

**In church:** Let patience, kindness, and gentleness characterize your attitude. It's a voluntary organization—no need to grumble about challenges. If it's too tough, you don't have to do it. Sit on the floor. But if you're there, do it with love.

**At school and university:** Do you complain about assignments? Feel tempted to grumble excessively? Counter that with patience, doing your best, not comparing yourself to others. Avoid becoming a workaholic. Pursue excellence, but with love for those around you.

**At work:** The same principle applies. It's not just about the job itself.

For example: You produced spreadsheets in love—to help someone accomplish their work. You built a bridge in love—to help others cross safely. As a doctor, you worked with love and compassion. As a teacher, you cared for students' growth.

**This love will last.**

But I recognize not everyone has satisfying work. And not all jobs offer obvious opportunities to help others. Sometimes it's just work—you do your thing, get paid, spend it, and the next day starts again. What does love have to do with it?

Even in those situations, there may be opportunities for love toward colleagues. Be interested in their welfare—both their earthly wellbeing and their spiritual wellbeing.

For some, though, the job itself won't provide such opportunities. Perhaps then you need to be content that your job is simply a job—but a noble one. By working faithfully, you put food on your family's table. You pay the bills. You keep your family alive and sheltered. **This is done out of love, and it is as good as any task.** Your Father sees that faithful motivation and is pleased.

## Conclusion: Eternity's Perspective

What lasts? The question seems whimsical when my corner store sells expired coffee for $0.99. But the real question is: **What lasts past the end? What lasts past judgment day?**

On judgment day, the whole earth will be destroyed and recreated. Consider what will pass away:

- Your homework—all those A's and C's—will burn up
- Your degrees and university qualifications—gone
- Your spreadsheets, reports, and accounting work—burned up
- The people you've healed as a doctor—they'll still die
- Your well-toned body, plastic surgery, laser-corrected eyes—decomposed into carbon and water and minerals
- The car you loved, the fashionable clothes, the house you built—all melted down

> **1 Corinthians 13:13** And now these three remain: faith, hope and love. But the greatest of these is love.

Only three things survive eternity: **faith, hope, and love.** More specifically, faith and hope in Jesus who rescues us from the dominion of darkness, and **our love.**

In a world of decay, ultimately only the motivation and attitude of love will prevail. Everything else crumbles. But love—the way you treated people, the care you showed, the other-centered choices you made—that echoes into eternity.

**Therefore, pursue love. Invest in the wellbeing of others. Let love affect every decision you make.**
