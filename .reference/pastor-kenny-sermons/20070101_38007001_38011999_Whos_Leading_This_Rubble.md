# Zechariah 7-11

**Title:** Who's Leading This Rubble?
**Date:** 14 January 2007
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Series Overview

This sermon is part of a three-part study on Zechariah:
1. "I Will Return" (Chapters 1-6)
2. "Who's Leading This Rubble?" (Chapters 7-11)
3. "One Day More" (Chapters 12-14)

## The Big Picture

God promises to rebuild Jerusalem in the midst of destruction. His return to His people happens through the temple, accomplished by His anointed leadership, and fulfilled in the day of the Lord.

The central theme echoes throughout: **"Return to me and I will return to you"** (Zechariah 1:3). God's return is by the temple. Yet surrounding nations and discouragement nearly paralyzed the people's rebuilding efforts. Zechariah urges them forward: *Build, and God will return. When God returns, He will conquer the nations, and the city and temple will be glorious.*

## The Vision Cycle

Zechariah received eight visions showing God's plan unfolds in three stages:
- **What** God will do: Return via the temple (Visions 1, 2, 3, 8)
- **Who** will lead it: His anointed priest and ruler (Visions 4-7)
- **When** it will happen: The day of the Lord (Chapters 12-14)

## Part 1: A Sinless Priest

### The Fourth Vision

> Then he showed me Joshua the high priest standing before the angel of the LORD, and Satan standing at his right side to accuse him. The LORD said to Satan, "The LORD rebuke you, Satan! The LORD, who has chosen Jerusalem, rebuke you! Is not this man a burning stick snatched from the fire?" (Zechariah 3:1-2)

God chooses a priest—not a king. This surprises us. Kings were prominent in Israel's history, yet after the exile, they had either been killed or rendered useless. The priesthood naturally rose to leadership. More importantly, the priest represents Israel before God through mediation. He stands in the gap for his people.

But there's a problem: Joshua is unclean.

### The Problem of Uncleanness

> Now Joshua was dressed in filthy clothes as he stood before the angel. (Zechariah 3:3)

Joshua is a "burning stick snatched from the fire"—rescued from judgment by mere inches. As the representative of Israel, he embodies the nation's condition: sinful, judged, exiled, yet spared. Satan, the accuser, points to his filthiness as disqualification. How can an unclean priest represent the people before a holy God?

### God's Solution: Cleansing

> The angel said to those who were standing before him, "Take off his filthy clothes." Then he said to Joshua, "See, I have taken away your sin, and I will put rich garments on you." (Zechariah 3:4)

God does not see hopelessness. He removes Joshua's filthy garments and clothes him in pure priestly vestments, places a clean turban on his head, and declares: *"I have taken away your sin."* This signals renewal of the covenant. God promises that if Joshua continues in obedience, he will govern the temple and stand among the angels (Zechariah 3:6-7).

### Why a Sinless Priest Matters

God and sin cannot coexist. They are mutually exclusive—like water and oil. The exile itself was God's judgment on Israel's rebellion. Now, cleansed and restored, God can return and dwell among His people once more.

The symbol of Joshua's cleansing points to something greater:

> "Listen, O high priest Joshua and your associates seated before you, who are men symbolic of things to come: I am going to bring my servant, the Branch. See, the stone I have set in front of Joshua! There are seven eyes on that one stone, and I will engrave an inscription on it," says the LORD Almighty, "and I will remove the sin of this land in a single day." (Zechariah 3:8-9)

What took seventy years of exile to accomplish—the purging of the land from impurity—God will do in a day through His servant, the Branch. When sin is removed, prosperity returns:

> "In that day each of you will invite his neighbor to sit under his vine and fig tree," declares the LORD Almighty. (Zechariah 3:10)

### Visions 6 and 7: Sin Removed

The sixth and seventh visions reinforce this principle with striking imagery.

> I looked again—and there before me was a flying scroll! (Zechariah 5:1-2)

A massive scroll flies across the land—a curse that banishes thieves and those who swear falsely. It enters their houses and destroys them completely.

> Then the angel who was speaking to me came forward and said to me, "Look up and see what this is that is appearing." He replied, "It is a measuring basket. And he added, "This is the iniquity of the people throughout the land." Then the cover of lead was raised, and there in the basket sat a woman! He said, "This is wickedness," and he pushed her back into the basket and pushed the lead cover down over its mouth. Then I looked up—and there before me were two women, with the wind in their wings! They had wings like those of a stork, and they lifted up the basket between heaven and earth. "Where are they taking the basket?" I asked. He replied, "To the country of Babylonia to build a house for it." (Zechariah 5:5-11)

Wickedness is contained, sealed, and carried away to Babylon—far from God's restored temple. The message is clear: God is making a place for His presence, and there is no room for two houses—one for God and one for sin. Sin must be banished.

### Application: God and Sin Don't Mix

The priests from Aaron through Joshua all sinned. None fulfilled the sinless criteria. But Jesus did.

> Day after day every priest stands and performs his religious duties; again and again he offers the same sacrifices, which can never take away sins. But when this priest had offered for all time one sacrifice for sins, he sat down at the right hand of God. Because by one sacrifice he has made perfect forever those who are being made holy. (Hebrews 10:11-14)

Jesus is the promised Branch—the sinless priest who removes sin in a single day.

For those of us who follow Jesus, our relationship to sin has fundamentally changed. We were born captive to sin's power, but through Jesus, sin's sting has been dealt with. We have been given new clothes—righteousness that isn't our own.

Even in our ongoing struggles and failures, we need not fear the accuser. Jesus has clothed us. The question then becomes: why would we return to the dirt when we've been given clean garments? Why go back to instant noodles when we've tasted steak? Why return to the Commodore 64 when we have a modern computer?

**We don't mix with sin anymore—not from fear or guilt, but because that's not who we are anymore.** We are people of Jesus. We've left that past behind.

If you find yourself tempted by what Zechariah names—stealing, sexual sin, greed, selfishness, dishonesty—don't do it. You've left that behind. Because Jesus is the Branch who cleanses sin once for all, we simply don't hang out in that territory anymore.

## Part 2: Two Leaders—Or One?

### The Fifth Vision: The Lampstand and Two Olive Trees

> Then the angel who talked with me returned and wakened me, as a man is wakened from his sleep. He asked me, "What do you see?" I answered, "I see a solid gold lampstand with a bowl at the top and seven lights on it, with seven channels to the lights. Also there are two olive trees by it, one on the right of the bowl and the other on its left." (Zechariah 4:1-3)

The vision presents a puzzle. The lampstand symbolizes God's presence—a visible reminder that He dwells with His people. The seven lights represent the eyes of God watching over all things. Normally, servants would refill the bowl with oil to keep the lamp burning.

But something is different. Two olive trees stand beside the bowl, connected by golden pipes. They supply the oil directly, without human intervention.

> Again I asked him, "What are these two olive branches beside the two gold pipes that pour out golden oil?" He replied, "These are the two who are anointed to serve the Lord of all the earth." (Zechariah 4:12-14)

Two anointed ones. Two branches. But we expected one leader—one priest, one king, one messiah. What's happening?

### The Two Leaders Identified

The first olive tree is **Joshua**, the high priest we've already considered.

The second is **Zerubbabel**, governor of Judah, appointed by the Persian king Cyrus.

> "This is the word of the LORD to Zerubbabel: 'Not by might nor by power, but by my Spirit,' says the LORD Almighty." (Zechariah 4:6)

Zerubbabel will not succeed through human strength or political power. God's Spirit will enable him to finish the temple construction (Zechariah 4:7-10). He descends from the line of David—a king's line—yet serves as builder and administrator.

### The Vision's Message

The lampstand at the center represents God's presence. The two olive trees on either side—Joshua and Zerubbabel—pour out oil directly from the source. This is a message of endorsement for Judah's leadership. If God is with the leaders, He is with the people. They bring God's presence to the nation.

### A Hint of Convergence

Yet hints suggest these two roles are more unified than separate. Scripture later describes them merging into one figure:

> Take the silver and gold and make a crown, and set it on the head of the high priest, Joshua son of Jehozadak. Tell him this is what the LORD Almighty says: "Here is the man whose name is the Branch, and he will branch out from his place and build the temple of the LORD. It is he who will build the temple of the LORD, and he will be clothed with majesty and will sit and rule on his throne. And he will be a priest on his throne. And there will be harmony between the two." (Zechariah 6:11-13)

Notice the strange mixing: Joshua receives the crown (a king's symbol). Joshua will build the temple (Zerubbabel's task). Joshua will rule on a throne (kingly authority). He will be a priest on that throne (priestly service). The language shifts to singular—*the Branch*—as if two roles are merging into one future figure.

### Part 2 Application: Jesus Is Both Priest and King

Jesus fulfills both roles completely. He is descended from David's line—a king. He is also a priest according to the order of Melchizedek (Hebrews 7), superior to all earthly priests.

**As priest**, He represents sinful people and atones for their sin. He pleads for us before God.

**As ruler**, He is Spirit-filled and empowered to reign. He builds the true temple—His own body, which is the church.

Sometimes Christians emphasize one role to the neglect of the other. Some see Jesus only as a compassionate savior who forgives sin but has no authority to rule our lives—a kind of spiritual Santa Claus. Others see Him as a judge and ruler but minimize His grace and solution to sin. But **Jesus is both Savior and King**. He deals with our sin and reigns over our lives. Both roles are essential.

## Who's Leading You?

### The Question of Leadership

The title asks: "Who's leading this rubble?" In Zechariah's day, it was the priest and governor, working under God's Spirit. For us, the question remains urgent: Who leads you?

Structural leadership exists—Bible study leaders, committees, pastors, bishops. These are gifts to the church. But ultimately, **Jesus must be your leader**. Not because a charismatic person tells you to follow, not because someone holds a prestigious position, not because you're told what to think without examining Scripture for yourself.

As you mature in faith—moving from childhood where others told you what to believe and do—you must learn to think independently. Don't trust anyone merely because they ask for it. Trust based on whether they are trustworthy and whether their teaching aligns with Scripture. Take time to assess. Use the mind God gave you.

Read Scripture repeatedly. When you disagree with others' interpretations, discuss respectfully. Discern whether disagreements are fundamental or secondary. Keep reading. Keep thinking. Keep checking everything against God's Word.

### Amidst Flawed Leaders, One Perfect King

Human leaders will fail. They are fallible and temporary. But Zechariah points us beyond them to God's ultimate leader—the Messiah.

The closing picture is striking: the King enters on a donkey, hailed as ruler. Yet in His very body—the temple of His flesh—He atones for sin. He is both the priest who cleanses and the king who reigns.

**Let us follow God's chosen leader.**
