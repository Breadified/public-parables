# 2 Timothy 2:1-26

**Title:** Pass the Gospel  
**Date:** 13 February 2011  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Relay of the Gospel

The apostle Paul uses a striking analogy to communicate the urgency of his message to Timothy. Imagine a relay race—the 4×100 metres where four runners pass a baton in succession. Each exchange is critical. In the 1952 Helsinki Olympics, the Australian women's sprint relay team was leading right up to the final exchange when their star runner, Marjorie Jackson, dropped the baton. Three perfect exchanges meant nothing. One dropped baton meant defeat.

This same principle applies to something far more significant than athletic glory. Throughout history, families have passed down names and values across generations. Chinese families, for example, sometimes embed a generational poem in their naming patterns—a 20-word poem distributed across 20 generations of names. But imagine a family that migrates, loses touch with its heritage, and names their children incorrectly. The information is broken. The chain is severed.

Today's passage presents Paul's urgent concern: the gospel must not be dropped. It is a baton to be passed down faithfully from one generation to the next—and we are that generation responsible for passing it forward.

## Entrust the Gospel to Faithful Men (2:1-2)

> You then, my son, be strong in the grace that is in Christ Jesus. And the things you have heard me say in the presence of many witnesses entrust to reliable men who will also be qualified to teach others.

This is Paul's last letter—his final will and testimony before his imminent death in prison. There is an urgency in his words as he addresses Timothy not merely as a student but as a son, passing his legacy downward. He echoes his earlier reminder: "What you heard from me, keep as the pattern of sound teaching, with faith and love in Christ Jesus" (1:13). Just as he charged Timothy to "guard the good deposit that was entrusted to you" (1:14), so now he calls Timothy to become a trustworthy guardian and transmitter.

Notice the generational chain Paul envisions in verse 2:

- Paul passes the gospel to Timothy
- Timothy passes it to reliable (faithful) men and women  
- These faithful teachers pass it to others
- Four generations—stretching indefinitely into the future

Paul anticipates perpetual transmission. Those qualified to teach will train others to teach, who will train still others. The gospel is meant to flow continuously, unbroken, through the centuries.

### Why Must Teachers Be Reliable?

Leaders must embody sound doctrine and sound judgment. They must be qualified—not perfect, but trustworthy stewards of the truth. This is not a minor concern. **The gospel is always only one generation away from extinction.** 

Think of the dodo, the Tasmanian tiger—species lost because of one weak generation. Similarly, one generation that fails to teach, one leader who distorts the truth, one dropped baton—and the gospel could disappear from a family, a community, a nation. Those entrusted with passing on the gospel must be trained, disciplined, and faithful.

### Application: All Are Called to Train

At St. Matthew's, our formal leaders—Bible study coordinators, committee members—are carefully chosen people who have undergone rigorous training and testing. They deserve your respect. They have invested tremendous effort simply to have the privilege of serving you.

But Paul's command extends beyond formal leaders. **His call is for every Christian.** You are the leaders of tomorrow. Many of you will become future Bible study leaders. Some may be called to full-time ministry. Most certainly, you will be future mothers and fathers, leading your own children in the knowledge and love of Christ.

Consider this sobering reality: if you do not teach the gospel to your children, why should you expect it to flourish in their lives? Some of your own fathers were pastors; others grew up in gospel homes. These influences shaped you. Will you shape the next generation the same way?

Therefore, **commit yourself to training.** Become one of those reliable, faithful people worthy of such trust. Pursue knowledge in godliness. Get trained.

### Getting Trained

St. Matthew's is exceptionally well-resourced. We offer small groups, training workshops, specialized ministries with instruction. Yet sometimes we fail to take full advantage of what we have. Some Christians remain content at a Sunday school level of understanding—stuck with childish ideas about church as merely a place of music and ritual, unable to articulate their faith or testify intelligently about what they believe.

Consider this: we are the smartest people in the smartest part of the country. We earn high school diplomas, bachelor degrees, even master's degrees. Yet our Christian education often remains at a children's story level. We remember only Sunday school songs. But the author of Hebrews challenges us: "Let us leave the elementary teachings about Christ and go on to maturity" (Hebrews 5:12). Let us move from milk to solid food.

Compare your commitment to training:

- **Time spent on your sport** vs. time spent studying Scripture
- **Hours tutoring** vs. hours in Bible study
- **Piano lessons** vs. Christian knowledge
- **School subjects** vs. Christian books and reflection

If these deserve your dedicated time, why not the gospel? At school, commit to Christian knowledge as you would one subject—read books, think critically about biblical texts, research and write essays on theological questions. At university, spend as much time on Christian formation as on your coursework—three hours of Christian lectures for three hours of secular lectures; hours in Bible study matching your tutorial schedule; Christian books matching your textbooks.

Why now? Because you are in the training phase of your life. This is when your capacity for learning peaks. Your knowledge when you leave university may represent the high point of your intellectual formation. Don't let your Christian qualification languish at Sunday school level.

For parents and workers, the season of formal study has passed, yet training continues. Consider short intensive blocks when circumstances allow. If possible, take a year for Bible college.

**Get trained. Get qualified. Get trusted. Invest the time.** It is hard work, but it is worth it—not for yourself alone, but for others, especially for the next generation.

## Three Pictures of Gospel Commitment: Endure Hardship (2:3-6)

Paul illustrates the cost of gospel faithfulness through three vivid images: the soldier, the athlete, and the farmer.

### The Soldier (2:3-4)

> Endure hardship with us like a good soldier of Christ Jesus. No one serving as a soldier gets involved in civilian affairs—he wants to please his commanding officer.

A soldier suffers hardship. Basic Fitness Assessment standards are rigorous—for males under 25: forty pushups, seventy situps, a 2.4 km run in 11 minutes and 18 seconds. This demands tremendous discipline and endurance.

More importantly, a soldier is singleminded. He does not get distracted by civilian concerns. In Paul's day, soldiers were separated from home, unable to communicate with families, entirely devoted to their commanding officer's orders. Even today, soldiers request special leave to exit camp. They cannot say, "I cannot deploy because I have a birthday party" or "Can I fight next week? I'm scheduled for part-time work" or "I'm tired from playing computer games all night."

Yet this is exactly where we struggle. We want to follow Christ, but we resist the cost. A recent audit of the Australian military found that more than one-third of troops were not battle-ready due to poor fitness, bad teeth, or lack of recent weapons training. Only 53 percent could be deployed at short notice.

The question haunts us: **Are we ready?** Are we distracted from the task of testifying to the gospel in our world? Are we fit to serve, singleminded in our commitment?

### The Athlete (2:5)

> Similarly, if anyone competes as an athlete, he does not receive the victor's crown unless he competes according to the rules.

Paul references Greek wrestling, the most prestigious Olympic sport. Wrestling was regarded as the purest expression of strength. But victory comes only through disciplined adherence to rules: no intentional striking, no eye-gouging, no biting. Get your opponent on his back or out of the ring for points. Three points wins the crown.

When Ian Thorpe returned from retirement, he had to endure strict training, meet fitness standards by precise dates, avoid performance-enhancing drugs. It was grueling. But there is no victory without the rules.

So too with the gospel. We cannot take shortcuts. We cannot lie, manipulate, or use flattery. We cannot distort the truth to make it more palatable. It would be easier to preach a prosperity gospel—that Christians will be wealthy and suffering-free. It would be easier to turn the church into a concert venue, attracting thousands through spectacular music. But **truth in love requires painstakingly slow, careful, humble ministry.** It is suffering ministry.

As Paul himself testifies: "Remember Jesus Christ, raised from the dead, descended from David. This is my gospel, for which I am suffering, even to the point of being chained like a criminal" (2:8-9).

### The Farmer (2:6)

> The hardworking farmer should be the first to receive a share of the crops.

We need no convincing that farming is hard work. The Australian sun beats down relentlessly. Farmers fix fences, plant seeds, fertilize, irrigate, tend crops—only to see them wiped away by storms. Then they begin again. It is exhausting, unending labor.

Such is the work of being qualified to teach others. **Are we prepared for that hard work?**

Hard work begins with self-control—cutting away distractions. Excessive computer games, television, sports, hobbies, social media. YouTube surpasses two billion views daily. Australians spend an average of 7 hours and 19 minutes on social networking sites. We have allowed ourselves to be captivated by the trivial.

Hard work means prioritizing training. Imagine if you spent on Christian formation what you spend on your sport, your music lessons, your tutoring. Why shouldn't Christian training receive equal priority?

As you leave school and university, recognize this truth: **what you know then may be your high point.** Don't keep your Christian qualification at Sunday school level. Invest now while you can.

## For the Sake of the Elect (2:9-13)

> Therefore I endure everything for the sake of the elect, that they too may obtain the salvation that is in Christ Jesus, with eternal glory.

The purpose of all this training, all this hardship, is not self-aggrandizement. **It is love for others.** David Knox, principal of Moore College, said it simply: the purpose of learning is to tell the gospel better. It doesn't matter what you learn—the end is always the same: to proclaim the gospel more faithfully.

Paul endures imprisonment not for himself but "for the sake of the elect." Though Paul is chained, "God's word is not chained" (2:9). The gospel is not meant to be preserved like a precious heirloom locked away in a garage. **It is meant to be passed on so that it may save others.**

Paul concludes with a trustworthy saying—a rhythm of reward and warning:

> If we died with him, we will also live with him;  
> if we endure, we will also reign with him.  
> If we disown him, he will also disown us;  
> if we are faithless, he will remain faithful,  
> for he cannot disown himself.

This is a chiasm—a literary structure where ideas mirror one another. The first and last lines (A and A) concern salvation and God's unbreakable faithfulness. The middle lines (B and B) concern reward. When we endure, we will reign—we receive the victor's crown, like the athlete; the firstfruits, like the farmer; the commendation of our commanding officer, like the soldier.

When we disown Christ, we forfeit those rewards. But even in our faithlessness, God remains faithful to His character—He cannot deny Himself. Our salvation stands secure in His character, not in our performance.

**The reward is worth it:** long-term faithfulness, the salvation of the elect, eternal glory.

## The Race Is Yours (Summary)

Two thousand years ago, the race began. Jesus appeared, died, rose, and entrusted His message to faithful disciples. They spoke and wrote, entrusting these things to reliable men. Eighty generations have passed since then—eighty 100-metre laps. The baton has been passed hand to hand through the centuries.

And now it is in your hands.

Don't slack off. Train hard. Be singleminded. Don't be distracted by the competing claims of comfort and ease. For the sake of the elect—those who will follow you, your children, your children's children—**don't drop the baton. Pass the gospel forward.**
