## What is Grit?

Over a hundred years ago, Francis Galton studied eminent figures across politics, sport, art, music, and science. He concluded that high achievement stems from a combination of ability, zeal, and the capacity for hard labor. When he shared this with his cousin Charles Darwin, Darwin replied:

> You have made a convert of an opponent in one sense, for I have always maintained that, excepting fools, men did not differ much in intellect, only in zeal and hard work.

Zeal and hard work—or as psychologist Dr. Angela Lee Duckworth calls it, *grit*.

Duckworth developed the Grit Scale to measure perseverance beyond IQ. She tested it on Ivy League undergraduates, West Point cadets, and Scripps National Spelling Bee finalists. Her research revealed consistent patterns:

- More determined undergraduates achieved higher GPAs
- West Point cadets with more grit were more likely to complete their first summer
- "Grittier" spellers outranked less tenacious competitors
- Grittier individuals had higher education levels and fewer career changes

Remarkably, grit showed no correlation with IQ. Duckworth concluded: "Perseverance may be as, if not more, important as intelligence in determining a person's success."

Actor Will Smith captures this beautifully:

> I've never really viewed myself as particularly talented. I've viewed myself as slightly above average in talent. And where I excel is ridiculous, sickening, work ethic. The only thing that is distinctly different about me is that I am not afraid to die on a treadmill. You might have more talent than me, you might be smarter than me, but if we get on a treadmill together, there are two things: you are getting off first or I am going to die. It's really that simple.

## God Disciplines His Children (12:4-11)

The writer of Hebrews calls us to persevere in our struggle against sin. Yet he reminds us: we have not yet resisted to the point of shedding our blood. Our suffering, though real, pales compared to Christ's sacrifice.

> In your struggle against sin, you have not yet resisted to the point of shedding your blood. And have you completely forgotten this word of encouragement that addresses you as a father addresses his son? (12:4-5)

The passage quotes Proverbs, reminding us that discipline flows from a father's love:

> Because the Lord disciplines the one he loves, and he chastens everyone he accepts as his son. Endure hardship as discipline; God is treating you as his children. For what children are not disciplined by their father? (12:6-7)

### Discipline Shapes Character

Every good parent disciplines their children. We recognize this in the everyday struggles of childhood—chores, homework, exercise, school attendance, tutoring. And the consequences: correction, loss of privileges, grounding. Without discipline, children become spoiled; communities suffer social evil. A father's absence is a root of dysfunction.

If we respect our earthly fathers for their discipline, how much more should we submit to our heavenly Father?

> We have all had human fathers who disciplined us and we respected them for it. How much more should we submit to the Father of spirits and live! (12:9)

The contrast is crucial: human fathers discipline according to their limited understanding, but God—with infinite knowledge—disciplines us for our good and for our holiness.

> They disciplined us for a little while as they thought best; but God disciplines us for our profit, in order that we may share in his holiness. No discipline seems pleasant at the time, but painful. Later, however, it produces a harvest of righteousness and peace for those who have been trained by it. (12:10-11)

The temporal versus the eternal: Our earthly fathers' discipline lasts a season and may be imperfect. God's discipline, though painful in the moment, produces eternal fruit—righteousness, peace, and a share in his holiness.

### Why Do We Suffer?

Suffering arises from multiple sources. Sometimes it results from sin—personal choices that create consequences, or the cumulative greed and brokenness of a fallen world. But there is a complementary truth: God uses all hardship as training for the Christian. 

Every struggle becomes an opportunity to:
- Grow in holiness and godliness
- Understand the love of Christ more deeply
- Appreciate the grace given to us
- Serve others with compassion

Consider Nick Vujicic. His hardship shaped who he became—not just another privileged child, but a man of extraordinary purpose and faith.

## Be Disciplined (12:12-17)

> Therefore, strengthen your feeble arms and weak knees. Make level paths for your feet, so that the lame may not be disabled, but rather healed. (12:12-13)

This is not merely physical exhortation—it's spiritual. Strengthen your spiritual arms to stand for God. Walk a straight path. The word "disabled" carries the sense of being turned aside from the truth. We see this warning throughout Scripture: some turn aside to fruitless discussion (1 Timothy 1:6), others turn away to follow Satan (1 Timothy 5:15), and still others turn aside to myths and false teaching (2 Timothy 4:4).

### Don't Fail in Faith

> Make every effort to live in peace with everyone and to be holy; without holiness no one will see the Lord. (12:14)

Peace isn't mere absence of conflict—it's a positive pursuit of right relationships. Holiness means being set apart for God's purposes. Both require active effort.

> See to it that no one falls short of the grace of God and that no bitter root grows up to cause trouble and defile many. (12:15)

A single person's bitterness can poison an entire community. We must guard against it—in ourselves and others.

> See that no one is sexually immoral, or is godless like Esau, who for a single meal sold his inheritance rights as the oldest son. Afterward, when he wanted to inherit this blessing, he was rejected. Even though he sought the blessing with tears, he could not change what he had done. (12:16-17)

Esau traded his eternal inheritance for temporary satisfaction. Some swaps—like Esau's—exchange good for evil and cannot be undone. This is why perseverance matters: some choices have consequences we cannot reverse.

## You Have Come to Mount Zion (12:18-24)

The writer contrasts two mountains, two covenants, two experiences of God's presence.

### The Old Covenant: Mount Sinai

> You have not come to a mountain that can be touched and that is burning with fire; to darkness, gloom and storm; to a trumpet blast or to such a voice speaking words that those who heard it begged that no further word be spoken to them, because they could not bear what was commanded: If even an animal touches the mountain, it must be stoned to death. The sight was so terrifying that Moses said, I am trembling with fear. (12:18-21)

Mount Sinai was terrifying—a place of law, judgment, and death. Even Moses trembled.

### The New Covenant: Mount Zion

> But you have come to Mount Zion, to the city of the living God, the heavenly Jerusalem. You have come to thousands upon thousands of angels in joyful assembly, to the church of the firstborn, whose names are written in heaven. You have come to God, the Judge of all, to the spirits of the righteous made perfect, to Jesus the mediator of a new covenant, and to the sprinkled blood that speaks a better word than the blood of Abel. (12:22-24)

The New Covenant brings us into joy, assembly, perfection, and better mediation. Where Abel's blood cried out for vengeance (Genesis 4:10), Jesus's blood speaks a better word—mercy and redemption.

God no longer speaks from an external, untouchable mountain. He speaks through Jesus, and his word penetrates to the marrow of our beings (see Hebrews 4:12).

## Do Not Refuse the Speaker (12:25-29)

> See to it that you do not refuse him who speaks. If they did not escape when they refused him who warned them on earth, how much less will we, if we turn away from him who warns us from heaven? (12:25)

The stakes are higher under the New Covenant. Those who rejected God's warning through Moses faced consequences. How much more serious to reject the one who speaks from heaven?

> At that time his voice shook the earth, but now he has promised, Once more I will shake not only the earth but also the heavens. The words once more indicate the removing of what can be shaken—that is, created things—so that what cannot be shaken may remain. Therefore, since we are receiving a kingdom that cannot be shaken, let us be thankful, and so worship God acceptably with reverence and awe, for our God is a consuming fire. (12:26-29)

God's final shaking will remove all that is temporary and created, leaving only what is eternal and unchangeable. We inherit a kingdom that cannot be shaken. This should produce gratitude, reverence, and awe—not irreverence.

## Application: Church as Training Ground

### Why Discipline in the Church?

Church discipline—accountability, commitment, volunteer service—prepares us for life itself. A high school student learning to prioritize studies discovers skills for university. A university student learning self-discipline discovers skills for work. Similarly, church commitments teach us:

- **Prioritization**: Learning to put spiritual things first, even when other pressures mount
- **Commitment**: Showing up when it's inconvenient
- **Service**: Caring for one another practically (preparing food, serving)
- **Perseverance**: Sticking with it when motivation fades

For young families, these disciplines shape not just individuals but *households*—determining whether a family will merely exist or thrive as a Christian family with specific values and habits.

### The Formative Years Matter

Before age 25, our brains retain remarkable elasticity. Good habits formed now last a lifetime. Young students and young families are in formative seasons. The disciplines we practice now—showing up to church, studying Scripture, serving—shape the people we become.

## Persevere: Because Now Is the Time

### On Ordinary Faithfulness

> 80% of success in life is showing up. — Woody Allen

We often measure life by dramatic moments—Kodak moments, conferences, memorable experiences. But the richest things in life are made of ordinary moments: unplanned conversations, small touches, everyday decisions. A child asks a question about the sermon on the drive home; faith takes root. Parents engage in the mundane rhythms of family life; relationships deepen.

Memorable experiences cannot compensate for shallow understanding built over years of absence. But ordinary faithfulness—week in and week out—produces sustained growth and deep roots.

The movement mentality keeps us restless. We want inspiring conferences, our favorite leaders, anonymity. But an actual church—ordinary, familiar, requiring submission and presence—is where we truly grow.

We need to recover sound practices that deepen us and our children in the new creation God is building. Perhaps when we discover the opportunities of the ordinary, we will be radical after all.

### On Finishing the Race

John Stephen Akhwari competed in the 1968 Olympic Marathon in Mexico City. Approximately 19 kilometers into the 42-kilometer race, he fell badly, wounding his knee and dislocating it. Medical staff urged him to withdraw. But he continued—walking, limping, slowly making his way around the track.

When the sun had set and only a few thousand remained in the stadium, Akhwari finally crossed the finish line. When asked why he ignored the advice to quit, he replied:

> My country did not send me 10,000 miles just to start the race; they sent me to finish the race.

Of the 75 who started that marathon, 18 withdrew. No one remembers the gold medalist's name. But we honor Akhwari because he finished in the most difficult circumstances.

> You are in the race of your life.

## Appendix: Waiting Well

The perseverance of the saints is nothing else but the patience of God—and it may require our patience as well.

### Waiting at Work

> Make it your ambition to lead a quiet life, to mind your own business, and to work with your hands. (1 Thessalonians 4:11)

Work is for provision, not for status or careerism. We work with our hands, our minds, our whole selves—but we work unto the Lord, not unto men.

**Practical wisdom for work:**
- Be aware of reality—old habits from university can resurface
- Balance stress and rest (Saturday mornings matter for your brain)
- Understand your personal rhythm and sync it with family and friends
- Care for your health through exercise and good eating habits
- Account for life circumstances without overcommitting
- Resist middle-class aspiration (the drive to get more, better, fulfill your potential)
- Beware of using travel or the next high as an escape from meaninglessness

### Waiting at Home

> Work in quietness and submission to those in authority over you. (paraphrased from 1 Thessalonians 4:11, 1 Timothy 2:2, 1 Peter 3:1-4)

Home is not a hotel (temporary, impersonal) but a hostel (communal, shared). Whether you're living with parents or roommates, the disciplines of quiet living and submission matter. This is a long-term investment—we're talking about relationships that span decades, affecting grandchildren yet unborn.

**Practical wisdom for home:**
- Embrace quietness and submission to household life
- Remember it's a very long-term commitment
- Appreciate the shelter and care provided (as you might, looking back, appreciate your mother's love)
- Consider whether moving out is a genuine option or would compound family hurt

### Waiting with Friends

Friendship takes many forms—some seasonal, some lifelong, some brief. Rather than pressuring friendships into fixed categories, let them be what they are. Keep in touch with your long-term friends. The church is a good place for friendship, though not the only place.

> Make it your ambition to increase your love for one another. (paraphrased from 1 Thessalonians 3:12, 4:9)

In all these contexts—work, home, friendship—we ask: Are we living for success in the now-and-not-yet? Or are we patient, faithful, ordinary, letting God shape us through the regular rhythms of grace?
