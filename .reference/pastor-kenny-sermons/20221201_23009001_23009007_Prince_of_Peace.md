# Isaiah 9:1-7

**Title:** Prince of Peace
**Date:** 25 December 2022
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Rejoice Because Jesus Has Come

The passage opens with a stunning contrast. In Isaiah 8:22, the previous chapter ends with darkness and gloom overwhelming the land. Yet here, God speaks through the prophet Isaiah with a promise: there will be no more gloom.

This is particularly meaningful for us. After years of lockdown and fear during the pandemic, we finally gather freely to celebrate Christmas. The ancient Israelites faced a similar darkness. The tribes of Zebulun and Naphtali had been conquered by Assyria and were called "Galilee of the nations"—a land diluted with foreign captives so no dominant group could challenge Assyrian rule.

> Nevertheless, there will be no more gloom for those who were in distress. In the past he humbled the land of Zebulun and the land of Naphtali, but in the future he will honor Galilee of the nations, by the Way of the Sea, beyond the Jordan. (Isaiah 9:1)

> The people walking in darkness have seen a great light; on those living in the land of deep darkness a light has dawned. (Isaiah 9:2)

The imagery is stark: from gloom to glory, from humiliation to honor, from deep darkness to great light. Imagine sitting in darkness at dawn—your eyes see nothing. Then, in minutes, the sky blazes bright and you must shield your eyes. The transformation is drastic.

### Application

Christmas reminds us of the greatest turning point in history. Jesus came into the world. Matthew tells us Jesus moved to Capernaum, in the land of Zebulun and Naphtali, fulfilling Isaiah's prophecy exactly. From that moment, Jesus preached: "Repent, for the kingdom of heaven has come near."

This changed everything—our relationship with God:
- The kingdom of God has drawn near to us
- Through repentance, Jesus offers forgiveness

This change transforms how we relate to each other:
- We treat one another with respect and dignity as bearers of God's image
- We love our neighbor as ourselves
- We steward God's created world together

Christmas is a season for renewal—a time for reconciliation, for families to release the past and begin again.

## Rejoice Because Jesus Has Forgiven Us

The rejoicing deepens. We celebrate because we have won the war.

> You have enlarged the nation and increased their joy; they rejoice before you as people rejoice at the harvest, as warriors rejoice when dividing the plunder. (Isaiah 9:3)

The word "joy" appears three times in this verse, painting a picture of abundance. A farmer rejoices at harvest after battling sun, wind, and rain—his struggle has ended in victory. A soldier divides the plunder after defeating enemies and conquering fear. Both images capture the joy of triumph.

> For as in the day of Midian's defeat, you have shattered the yoke that burdens them, the bar across their shoulders, the rod of their oppressor. (Isaiah 9:4)

In Judges, Gideon defeated the entire Midianite army with just 300 soldiers—a picture of liberation from oppression. The battle was won.

### Application

We rejoice because our war with God is finished. We are freed from sin. We are reaping the harvest of righteousness.

Consider what happened this Christmas in Australia specifically. The war against COVID has been won. The virus retreats, growing weaker as more gain immunity and grow stronger. Government restrictions and lockdowns are over. Supply chains improve daily. There is abundance again—mangoes are plentiful, ham is plentiful, children receive gifts in abundance.

The war is over. Rejoice.

## Rejoice Because Jesus Leads Us

> For to us a child is born, to us a son is given, and the government will be on his shoulders. And he will be called Wonderful Counselor, Mighty God, Everlasting Father, Prince of Peace. (Isaiah 9:6)

This child is Jesus. Luke 2:11 declares: "Today there has been born for you a Savior, who is Christ the Lord."

Yet this child does not remain a child. The word "son" carries the meaning of "heir." Jesus grows to inherit rulership of the world. Notice the contrast with verse 4—Israel bore a yoke of oppression on their shoulders, while government rests on Jesus's shoulders. Governance is a burden. It can either crush people through oppression or ennoble leaders who bear responsibility for their people's welfare.

Jesus bore this burden sacrificially. His shoulders hung on a cross so that ours might be relieved of the yoke that crushes us.

### The Titles of the King

These names read like royal titles. Consider King Charles III, who carries multiple titles: Head of Australia, Order of the Garter, Defender of the Faith. Jesus surpasses all earthly rulers. He is:

- **Wonderful Counselor** (wisdom)—He guides us with perfect understanding
- **Mighty God** (power)—He possesses ultimate authority
- **Everlasting Father** (protection)—He cares for us eternally
- **Prince of Peace**—He uniquely brings peace

> Of the greatness of his government and peace there will be no end. He will reign on David's throne and over his kingdom, establishing and upholding it with justice and righteousness from now on and forever. The zeal of the LORD Almighty will accomplish this. (Isaiah 9:7)

Jesus offers something no earthly leader can: true peace. Not the stalemate of suppression, but the peace of victory—peace over death and sin, peace through reconciliation, peace found in trusting almighty God, peace in our hearts.

During lockdown, I discovered an unexpected gift: amid the fear, there was quietness. Empty streets. No traffic. No rush. Shops closed. No hurry. Jesus offers that profound peace—not from defeat or surrender, but from the triumph of His kingdom established forever.

## Closing Reflection

As 2022 ends much like 2020 began, with wars, inflation, and suffering, Christmas comes as the Lord's Day to remind us:

The gloom will end. The light will shine. There will be peace.

All of this—our transformation, our freedom, our leadership—because a child came unto us.

Merry Christmas and a Happy New Year.
