# Matthew 1:18-25

**Title:** The Birth of Hope

**Date:** December 17, 2011

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Story Behind Names

Every name carries significance. Some names are merely frivolous—in 2008, about 2,500 babies in China were named "Olympic," and some parents choose fruit names like Apple or Cherry. But more often, names represent something far deeper: they tell stories of history and express hopes for the future.

Names can commemorate history: a child named Winston after Winston Churchill, or named for a biblical character we admire, or carrying forward a grandparent's legacy. Names can also embody hope—some Asian parents give their daughters names like "Ka Heng" (meaning "add a brother"), expressing their desires for their families. One book about Asian Christians features a chapter titled "They Called Me Harvard," capturing how names often reflect the hopes and dreams parents hold for their children.

My own Chinese name means "peace." My mother was pregnant with me while my grandmother was seriously ill, so my parents gave me this name in hope that my arrival would bring a season of peace to our family.

The birth of Jesus brings together both history and hope through the remarkable names given to him.

## Jesus: The Historical Lineage

Matthew begins his gospel with genealogy—a list of forty names representing forty generations stretching back to Abraham. While it might seem an odd way to start a story, there is profound purpose here. This genealogy demonstrates that Jesus is not a random figure, but part of a continuous family line all the way from Abraham's time. Through Jesus, God's ancient promises to Abraham—land, people, and blessings—are finally answered. The number forty itself echoes Israel's forty years waiting in the wilderness for the promised land. The wait is over. Jesus has arrived.

This genealogical connection gives us confidence that Jesus is not mythology or fairy tale. He is a real historical person with real parents, grandparents, and great-grandparents. We could compare this to the fairy tales we know—do we really know Cinderella's grandparents? Did Santa Claus have a genealogy? These figures exist in legend and myth. Jesus exists in history.

## Jesus: The Savior

> She will give birth to a son, and you are to give him the name Jesus, because he will save his people from their sins. (Matthew 1:21)

The name Jesus comes from the Hebrew *Yoshua*, which means "Yahweh saves"—similar to the name Isaiah, which means "Yahweh's salvation." Many names are functional: Teddy means "wealthy guardian," Quentin means "fifth," Jason means "healer," Henry means "ruler of the home." But while we might bear names whose meanings we don't fully live up to—I'm not sure how handsome I actually am, despite the meaning of Kenny—Jesus takes his name to its absolute extreme. He fulfills his purpose completely by voluntarily laying down his life on the cross to save us from sin.

Inherent in the name Jesus is the very reason for his birth: to save us from our sins, to endure the agony and pain of the cross on our behalf.

Let me illustrate this with a story. I have two cousins in Malaysia—sisters. The older sister was dying from a blood disease and desperately needed a bone marrow transplant. When doctors couldn't find a suitable donor, my second cousin was conceived specifically to be that matching donor. The younger sister endured a painful procedure, but both sisters survived and are healthy today. In a real sense, my little cousin was born to save her sister's life.

Jesus was born to save—not just one person, but all of us. Not for one life, but for eternity. This Christmas, don't think of a cute baby in a manger. Think of a Savior born for the specific purpose of transplanting a new heart into your defective, sinful heart. And in doing so, he bears the agony of death itself.

## Christ: The King

> All this took place to fulfill what the Lord had said through the prophet. (Matthew 1:22)

Jesus is also called the Christ. This is not a surname—it is the Greek term for the Hebrew word *Messiah*, meaning "the anointed one." In ancient Israel, oil was poured upon leaders—kings like David, and priests of Israel—to establish their authority. This anointing was how Israel's kings were formally recognized, not with a crown but with oil. When we call Jesus "the Christ," we are saying Jesus is the anointed one. He is the King, in the line of David.

But this is not the end of the story. The baby in the manger grows to become an adult King, sitting at the right hand of God. We must follow him and obey him.

## Immanuel: God With Us

> The virgin will be with child and will give birth to a son, and they will call him Immanuel—which means, "God with us." (Matthew 1:23)

For those who are Christian, Jesus has yet another name. At that first Christmas, Jesus was one person among 150 million others on earth. If you had been there and asked Mary's permission, you could have touched him, held him, been with him. God became present with us through Jesus. He is not distant or unreachable. He is Emmanuel—God with us.

So we see that Jesus is historical, the one who saves us, the King who rules over us, and God who is with us.

## Your Name: Christian

Whatever your name is, you bear another name: Christian. You are "Christ-ian"—one who follows Christ.

> However, if you suffer as a Christian, do not be ashamed, but praise God that you bear that name. (1 Peter 4:16)

Do not be ashamed of being Christian, even when it's not popular in your class or among your friends. Rather, bear this name with pride. In fact, there is much we can do to let others know that we carry the name of Christ:

- **Invite others to Christian groups.** By inviting friends to Soul Purpose, you're letting them know about a Christian community and that you are a Christian.

- **Be open about your faith in conversation.** If our friends don't know our names, wouldn't it be strange? Likewise, if being Christian is part of our identity, shouldn't our friends know this about us?

- **Express your faith naturally.** You might say "God bless you" when someone sneezes, or "Praise God" when they share good news, or "I'll pray for you tonight" when they face difficulty. These small expressions reveal what we believe.

- **Use visible reminders of your faith.** Even something as simple as a Bible verse on your folder or a fish sticker on your pencil case can open conversations about who you follow.

The name Christian is the most wonderful name to bear. It is filled with hope: Christ has died for us, and we will be rescued on the day of judgment. Christ is with us and rules over us with grace and compassion. This name represents the weight of history—those who have gone before us in faith. And it represents the weight of hope as we serve God today.

Live out your name.
