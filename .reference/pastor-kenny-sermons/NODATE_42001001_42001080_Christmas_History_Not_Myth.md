# Luke 1:1-80

**Title:** Christmas: History, Not Myth  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Separating Fact from Christmas Fiction

We live surrounded by Christmas mythology. Christmas trees, turkey, snow, Santa, elves—these are all myths that have accumulated around the holiday. Even some of our most beloved traditions carry false assumptions: that Jesus was born on December 25th, that he was a silent baby ("no crying he makes," as we sing in "Away in a Manger"), or that three wise kings visited him in the manger.

When we let these myths dominate our understanding of Christmas, we risk treating the entire Christmas story—and with it, the whole of Scripture—like a fairy tale rather than history. But this is precisely what Luke sets out to counter in his Gospel.

Over the coming weeks, we'll examine the Christmas story as Luke presents it: not as myth, but as genuine history.

## History, Not Myth: Luke's Opening Claim

> Many have undertaken to compile a narrative about the events that have been fulfilled among us, just as the original eyewitnesses and servants of the word handed them down to us. It also seemed good to me, since I have carefully investigated everything from the very first, to write to you in an orderly sequence, most honorable Theophilus, so that you may know the certainty of the things about which you have been instructed. (Luke 1:1-4)

Luke opens his Gospel with an explicitly historical claim. He tells us that **many** have already compiled narratives of these events, drawing from **original eyewitnesses**. Luke himself has **carefully investigated everything from the very first**—not hearsay, but careful historical research. His purpose is clear: to provide **certainty** about the events his reader has been taught.

This is the language of history, not mythology. Notice several important markers:

- **Multiple sources and eyewitnesses**: The events of Christmas were not a private conspiracy but public events witnessed by many. The crucifixion itself was a public execution in Jerusalem, observed by crowds.

- **Careful investigation**: Luke was a physician and historian who did his homework. He didn't simply compile loose recollections; he investigated thoroughly.

- **Written for a specific person**: This gospel is addressed to "Theophilus," a real individual. It's not a vague spiritual tale but a historical account meant to be read and verified.

- **Emphasis on certainty**: Luke wants his reader to **know** the certainty of these things. That's the concern of historians, not myth-makers.

Compare this to Cinderella: in modern retellings, we have many characters and elaborate settings, yet not a single real name, place, or historical marker. Why? Because it's a fairy tale—specificity doesn't matter. But when a narrative is filled with names, places, and historical details (as Luke's immediately becomes with King Herod), we recognize it as history.

## The Deeper Story: Pre-History and Promise

Luke doesn't begin his account with Jesus. Instead, he starts with Zechariah and Elizabeth, the parents of John the Baptist. This seems like a strange way to begin a book about Jesus, but it reveals Luke's deeper purpose.

> In the days of King Herod of Judea, there was a priest of Abijah's division named Zechariah. His wife was from the daughters of Aaron, and her name was Elizabeth. Both were righteous in God's sight, living without blame according to all the commands and requirements of the Lord. But they had no children because Elizabeth could not conceive, and both of them were well along in years. (Luke 1:5-7)

Jesus's story doesn't begin at his birth. It stretches back through the entire Old Testament—to the promises of God given to Abraham, to the institution of priesthood and temple worship, to the hope for a redeemer who would come to restore what was broken.

Notice the parallel Luke deliberately creates: Like Abraham and Sarah in Genesis 12—both elderly and barren—Zechariah and Elizabeth are described as "well along in years" with no children. This is Luke's way of signaling that God is about to do something miraculous, something consistent with his pattern of fulfilling impossible promises. We should immediately recognize from these circumstances that God is at work.

When Zechariah is chosen by lot to burn incense in the sanctuary, an angel appears to him:

> Do not be afraid, Zechariah, because your prayer has been heard. Your wife Elizabeth will bear you a son, and you will name him John. There will be joy and delight for you, and many will rejoice at his birth. For he will be great in the sight of the Lord and will never drink wine or beer. He will be filled with the Holy Spirit while still in his mother's womb. He will turn many of the sons of Israel to the Lord their God. And he will go before Him in the spirit and power of Elijah, to turn the hearts of fathers to their children, and the disobedient to the understanding of the righteous, to make ready for the Lord a prepared people. (Luke 1:13-17)

## John: The Bridge from Promise to Fulfillment

John the Baptist—his name means "Yahweh is Gracious"—represents the culmination of the Old Testament. Every detail about him echoes Scripture:

- **He embodies Old Testament holiness**: Never drinking wine or beer, filled with the Holy Spirit from the womb—these mark him as set apart for God's service, like the Nazirites of old.

- **He carries Elijah's mantle**: The reference to him going "in the spirit and power of Elijah" connects him to one of the greatest figures in Israel's history. Elijah was a prophet who turned the nation's heart back to God; John comes to do the same.

- **He prepares the way**: His baptism in the Jordan wasn't simply a ritual cleansing. It was preparation—making ready for the Lord a people prepared to receive him.

This is what John's ministry was about: preparing hearts and minds for the coming of Jesus. He represents the final moment of Old Testament anticipation.

## Jesus: Greater Than All Preparation

But Jesus surpasses everything that came before:

- **Greater than Elijah or John**: Unlike John, Jesus doesn't need abstinence from wine to make him holy. He possesses his own inherent holiness. Where John merely baptizes with water, Jesus baptizes with the Holy Spirit and with fire.

- **The source, not the servant**: John's entire purpose is to prepare the way for someone greater. Jesus doesn't need preparation; he is the Lord himself.

- **He transforms everything**: Jesus doesn't just turn hearts toward righteousness—he makes things holy by his touch and presence. He is not preparing for God to arrive; he is God arriving.

This is the Christmas story: not a mythical tale of magic and sentiment, but the arrival of God himself into human history. It is the culmination of thousands of years of promise and preparation.

## Preparing for Christmas

As we approach Christmas, we typically prepare with shopping, decorations, and feasts. But Luke's opening challenges us to deeper preparation: preparation of heart for the one who has come and who will come again.

John's message remains relevant for us: to prepare means to turn our hearts toward Christ, to make our lives a place where he can dwell, to be "a prepared people" for his arrival.

The Christmas story is not myth. It is history—God's irruption into our world, announced through signs and wonders, witnessed by eyewitnesses, handed down through careful testimony, meant to give us certainty about the one in whom all of Scripture's promises find their fulfillment.
