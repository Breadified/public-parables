# Ecclesiastes 4:4-12

**Title:** True Satisfaction: Work?
**Date:** 23 August 2018
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction

We continue our study of Ecclesiastes exploring the question: Does work truly satisfy? When we consider that approximately half of our productive lifetime is spent working, we must ask whether we can find genuine satisfaction in our labor. King Solomon, who built vast projects, expanded vineyards, established trade networks, and amassed great wealth, gives us his answer: the work itself—motivated by envy and competition—is meaningless, a chasing after wind.

## One Is Better Than Two

> Then I saw that all toil and all skill in work come from a man's envy of his neighbor. This also is vanity and a striving after wind. (Ecclesiastes 4:4)

Solomon identifies three attitudes toward work: the lazy, the crazy, and the daily.

### The Lazy

> The fool folds his hands and eats his own flesh. (Ecclesiastes 4:5)

Folded hands represent idleness—the refusal to work. Scripture warns that laziness leads to serious consequences:

> The sluggard's craving will be the death of him, because his hands refuse to work. (Proverbs 21:25)

Like Newton's first law of motion, a lazy person tends to remain at rest, unable to overcome inertia. This isn't merely disliking work; it's the inability to hold down a job, to submit assignments on time, or to take responsibility for one's actions. The medieval church rightly listed sloth among the seven deadly sins because it leads to destruction.

In a subsistence economy, refusing to work means starvation. Paul writes:

> If a man will not work, he shall not eat. (2 Thessalonians 3:10)

Yet we're called to productive work:

> He who has been stealing must steal no longer, but must work, doing something useful with his own hands, that he may have something to share with those in need. (Ephesians 4:28)

Work—even humble work—has dignity. Whether it's a well-paid position or wiping floors at a fast-food restaurant, useful work with your hands is honorable. A practical tip: get dressed for work even on days when you have none. This simple act combats the inertia of laziness.

### The Crazy

> Better is a handful of quietness than two hands full of toil and a striving after wind. (Ecclesiastes 4:6)

The opposite extreme is just as destructive. While the lazy fold their hands, others grab with both hands—working excessively out of greed and envy.

In Japan, this problem is so severe it has a name: *karoshi*—death from overwork. The government reports 30,000 victims annually. Studies confirm that working twelve or more hours a day, six or seven days a week, year after year, causes severe physical and mental deterioration. Many professions in Australia approach similar extremes.

Some seasons demand intense work—new graduates establishing themselves, or unusual circumstances. But habitually overworking for status, for competitive advantage over neighbors, or to maintain a false image is meaningless. Consider the examples: nations compete to build the tallest buildings for mere bragging rights. China built entire empty cities so local councils would look good on paper. When we overwork to impress others, everyone loses—both the overworker and their competitor grow stressed while chasing an endless game neither can win.

The Japanese workforce exemplifies this tragic irony: they have sufficient savings and resources, yet work long hours not for necessity but for appearance. It's about maintaining an image at the office, about who they appear to be. This produces no extra profit and no satisfaction.

### The Daily

> Better is a handful of quietness than two hands full of toil and a striving after wind. (Ecclesiastes 4:6)

Instead of folded hands (laziness) or two grasping hands (greed), Solomon commends having one handful—representing contentment. One hand full, the other hand saying, "Enough. No thank you."

The word "quietness" suggests peace and tranquility:

> Make it your ambition to lead a quiet life, to mind your own business and to work with your hands, just as we told you, so that your daily life may win the respect of outsiders and so that you will not be dependent on anybody. (1 Thessalonians 4:11-12)

This is fundamentally different from working for meaning, significance, status, or gain. We work for daily needs—our own and others'. We provide for layers of relationship: family, church, friends, charity, and society. We work in obedience to God.

### Choosing One Over Two

The question becomes: can we be content with one instead of always wanting two?

This doesn't mean wanting two incomes is inherently wrong. Many families genuinely need dual incomes—immigrant families building from nothing, families in difficult seasons, or households where part-time work is manageable. These are legitimate circumstances.

But consider how a generation ago, when neighbors moved into bigger houses or renovated, envy drove others to take on larger debts for larger homes. To service that debt, both parents began working. Then everyone else saw their neighbors do this, envied them, and did the same. Now all houses doubled in price because every family works double income, and we're back to square one—except now both parents work.

With this comes extra toil, extra stress, fractured families. Children lose their mothers' presence at home. Childcare is contracted to strangers. Exhausted families become chaotic. Weekends fill with guilt-driven activities, leaving no time for church. Neighborhoods become less safe.

Is it worth sacrificing these things for material status? For looking good in front of neighbors? For your children to have two languages instead of one, two hobbies instead of one, two rooms to watch television in?

**To young women:** You're pressured to return to work partly through the envy of men—they have careers and corporate lunches and suits, so why shouldn't you? But consider: there's a season for raising a family. While childcare may not feel fulfilling or significant by worldly standards, it matters deeply to God and to those you serve.

**To young men:** Make it easier for your future wives by not making such a profound identity claim around your work. When asked "What do you do?" most answer with their occupation—defining themselves by career. But ask yourself: what do I really do? The answer should be: I provide the daily. I bring home the bacon. I provide daily necessities for my family and community.

Christians don't have careers; we have jobs. Once we understand work's purpose rather than work's status, occupational labels become meaningless. You can provide for your family as a lawyer, a doctor, a garbage collector, a plumber, a nurse, or a carpenter. You won't be labeled by profession in heaven, so why be preoccupied with those labels now—especially at the expense of relationships?

## Two Is Better Than One

> Again, I saw vanity under the sun: one person who has no other, either son or brother, yet there is no end to all his toil, and his eyes are never satisfied with riches. (Ecclesiastes 4:7-8)

The workaholic stands alone. He sees his bank account rise—no satisfaction. He watches buildings rise from the ground—no satisfaction. His corporate position climbs—no satisfaction. His portfolio and superannuation grow—no satisfaction. Yet he never asks the fundamental question:

> For whom am I toiling and depriving myself of pleasure? (Ecclesiastes 4:8)

This isn't an argument for self-indulgence or working less to play more. It's asking: why am I depriving my soul of good? Why am I abandoning relationships?

Work itself is good when it serves others. Obedience to God in different seasons is good. Fearing the Lord is good. But when one hand holds wealth while the other hand is empty of relationships, something crucial is lost.

> Two are better than one, because they have a good reward for their toil. For if they fall, one will lift up his fellow. But woe to him who is alone when he falls and has not another to lift him up! Again, if two lie together, they keep warm, but how can one keep warm alone? And though a man might prevail against one who is alone, two will withstand him. (Ecclesiastes 4:9-12)

Companionship provides what wealth never can:
- When you fall, someone lifts you up
- When rejected, someone consoles you
- When cold, someone provides warmth
- When attacked, someone stands with you

Isn't family and friendship better than wealth? Yet in our pursuit of status and riches, we've lost this truth.

### The Loneliness of Modern Life

We've built sophisticated isolation into our very homes. Automatic garage doors allow us to drive directly into our houses, avoiding neighbors. Advanced security systems lock us away from community. Our homes are larger than ever, yet we fill them with separate rooms—bigger closets, private gyms, entertainment spaces—but no common gathering places. Every child has their own room in double-story houses; there's no need to interact.

Public gathering spaces have declined. Coffee shops feel unsafe for conversation. Sit-down restaurants give way to fast food. We substitute real friends with Facebook connections, watching what others are doing. We sit exhausted in entertainment rooms watching reruns of *Friends* instead of actually having friends. We browse social media at work watching *How I Met Your Mother* instead of investing time to actually meet people.

For whom are you toiling? Is it worth sacrificing relationships for promises the world can never deliver? It's a wild goose chase without a goose—a rat race where only the winners are rats. It is meaningless.

### The Threefold Cord

> A threefold cord is not quickly broken. (Ecclesiastes 4:12)

The text shifts from the language of ones and twos to suddenly introduce three. It's not just that two is better than one, but three is even better. In fact, four, five, six, seven, eight—we, all of us together, are better than one.

This is what we're attempting at our community: a gathering of Christians and seekers for each other and for those not yet with us. Friends are essential. Growing up, I had limited friendships in high school—only one pair of going-out clothes. But at church, I found friends. I was welcomed, cared for, belonged somewhere. The conversations were meaningful.

Relationships aren't perfect. They can be limited and sometimes even feel meaningless. But we have an opportunity today to welcome each other, to encourage each other in Christ, and to invite others to join us. It's a time to be content, not to work more or study more, but to invest in relationships.

Remember: Christ died for his friends—us—to make us into one family. Jesus has brought us together:

> But now in Christ Jesus you who once were far away have been brought near by the blood of Christ. For he himself is our peace, who has made the two groups one and has destroyed the barrier, the dividing wall of hostility. (Ephesians 2:13-14)

We have both relationship with God and relationship with each other.

## Conclusion

One is better than two. Two is better than one. It's a busy time, but consider committing to gathering with friends to discuss what truly matters in life. Find your community. Invest in relationships. Work to provide, not to compete. This is the whole duty of humanity:

> Fear God and keep his commandments, for this is the whole duty of man. For God will bring every deed into judgment, with every secret thing, whether good or evil. (Ecclesiastes 12:13-14)
