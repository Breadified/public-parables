# Luke 18:18-30

**Title:** Riches Found: How to be Rich in Faith

**Date:** 19 May 2012, 14 December 2019

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## The One Thing Missing (18:18-25)

A wealthy ruler approaches Jesus with a question that echoes across generations: "Good teacher, what must I do to inherit eternal life?" This isn't idle curiosity. He's witnessed the crowds following Jesus, heard of miracles performed, perhaps confronted his own mortality. It's the same question that rises in all of us when we contemplate life beyond this world—when we've experienced loss, when we realize our days are numbered.

Jesus responds by listing the commandments—not the first four about loving God, but the second table: do not commit adultery, do not murder, do not steal, do not give false testimony, honor your father and mother. It's the same answer given to another lawyer in Luke 10:25-28: "Do this and you will live."

The ruler's reply is striking: "All these I have kept since I was a boy." From his bar mitzvah onward, he's been consistent. And looking at that list, we can relate. Most of us in comfortable suburbs could honestly say the same. We haven't murdered anyone, we haven't robbed a bank, we haven't committed adultery—at least not in the strictest legal sense. We're generally nice people from nice families. We could tick off these commandments too.

Yet Jesus' evaluation surprises: "You still lack one thing."

In contrast to ALL the commandments the ruler has kept, Jesus identifies a single missing piece. Sell everything you have and give to the poor, and you will have treasure in heaven. Then come, follow me.

There's a literary play at work here: he kept ALL, he lacks ONE, so he must sell ALL. It's a call to radical reorientation—to give up the thing that has become his master.

The result is devastating: "When he heard this, he became very sad, because he was very wealthy." The exceeding wealth corresponds to exceeding grief. A man turns away from Jesus to return to his riches, having failed the one important thing.

What was this one thing? Looking at the commandments Jesus quoted, he notably omitted the tenth: "Do not covet." The greed commandment. The insatiable desire for more and more. Jesus exposes that the rich man's real god isn't money itself—it's the love of money, the refusal to surrender control of his wealth.

> "How hard it is for the rich to enter the kingdom of God! Indeed, it is easier for a camel to go through the eye of a needle than for someone who is rich to enter the kingdom of God."

This isn't a riddle about a small gate or a mountain pass. Jesus uses this image to say the impossible. The problem with wealth is not wealth itself—it's that the rich do not need God. When your resources are abundant, you can insulate yourself from dependence on Him. There's an empirical correlation we can observe: the wealthiest suburbs in any city tend to be the least religious. The poorer neighborhoods depend more openly on God. As communities prosper, the spiritual hunger diminishes.

## No One Is Good (18:26-27)

The disciples hear this and ask the question that emerges naturally: "Who then can be saved?" If even the virtuous, wealthy ruler can't enter the kingdom, what hope is there for anyone?

This brings us back to Jesus' strange opening question: "Why do you call me good? No one is good—except God alone."

Some use this verse to argue Jesus claims he's neither good nor God. But that misreads it. Jesus is asking a testing question—the same kind of question he asks Peter: "Who do you say I am?" He's not claiming amnesia. He's probing the ruler's understanding of goodness itself.

Moreover, Jesus elsewhere calls himself good—"I am the good shepherd. The good shepherd lays down his life for the sheep." By claiming to be the only good one, Jesus is identifying himself with God.

The deeper truth emerges in Romans 3:10-12: "There is no one righteous, not even one; there is no one who understands; there is no one who seeks God. All have turned away, they have together become worthless; there is no one who does good, not even one."

The law was meant to bring life. The Ten Commandments summarize God's law—the first four about loving God, the rest about loving neighbor. Yet we cannot follow it. Particularly, we fail at the first four: we do not love God first, we create idols before Him, we misuse His name, we break His Sabbath.

God is calling this ruler—and all of us—to reorder our allegiances. Give up that idol of money. That career. That dream. That relationship. Put Me first. Love God first. And the ruler walks away because of this one thing—one vulnerability, like Achilles' heel, that proves fatal.

**The Application:** What is your one thing? The part of life you do not want God to touch?

Is it relationships—that boyfriend or girlfriend worth almost giving up God for? Is it success—the relentless drive for more, "just a little more," willing to sacrifice Sundays for it? Is it studies, consuming all your time and energy? Is it careerism, so tied to your identity that you compromise ethics, relationships, and spiritual health to climb another rung?

What is that one thing you never want to surrender? God, in His wonderful humor, will challenge you precisely there—not out of cruelty, but to free you from it.

## To Gain All (18:27-30)

Yet there is hope: "What is impossible with man is possible with God."

It's impossible for us to please God on our own. But God the Son can satisfy the Father's wrath. Christ who gave all—He became poor so that we might become rich in heaven.

Jesus predicts what's coming: "We are going up to Jerusalem, and everything that is written by the prophets about the Son of Man will be fulfilled. He will be delivered over to the Gentiles. They will mock him, insult him and spit on him; they will flog him and kill him. On the third day he will rise again."

He is the ruler who gave up all the trappings of wealth and came to earth as a lowly carpenter. He surrendered His claims, His rights, His dignity. On the cross, He gave up life itself. And He gave His love to everyone, making salvation possible for all.

Because of this, Peter can declare: "We have left all we had to follow you!"

And Jesus responds with a promise that reframes everything:

> "Truly I tell you, no one who has left home or wife or brothers or sisters or parents or children for the sake of the kingdom of God will fail to receive many times as much in this age, and in the age to come eternal life."

It wasn't surrender for nothing. The promise includes:
- **Eternal life** in the age to come
- **Treasure in heaven** (verse 22)
- **Many times as much in this age**—now, in the present

> "But seek his kingdom, and these things will be given to you as well." (Luke 12:31)

Christians are people who hold this world loosely—its wealth, its delights—because they know the real treasure is in heaven. You signed up to follow Jesus precisely because whatever the world offers pales compared to what awaits.

This should make generosity natural. We shouldn't withhold from others because we fear parting with our money. The decision to give might depend on circumstances and wisdom, but never on fear or covetousness.

Yet don't be paralyzed by "sell all you have." This isn't a command to give away every penny immediately. It's an acknowledgment that periodically, after accumulating, we give it away. We do it readily for holidays, for houses, for expensive purchases—we can also do it for gospel reasons: missionaries liquidifying to enter ministry, Christians being generous, putting God's kingdom first.

**Start small.** Sell the rubbish in your garage. Liquidate that enormous collection of unnecessary clothes and shoes. If you can't give up rubbish, it will be nearly impossible to give up treasure.

---

## Conclusion

It came down to one thing. Why couldn't God let this one thing go to the keeper?

God isn't being nasty, picky, or bored. He's inoculating you—not just dipping your whole body, but ensuring that heel is covered too, so you won't remain vulnerable there. That one thing won't become a stumbling block. That one thing won't enslave you with regrets. That one thing won't waste your time or leave you discovering too late that it doesn't satisfy.

That one thing won't be your Achilles' heel.

**God is doing you a favor.**

Friends, what is that one thing stopping you from following God fully? Put it away. You think it's important, but it isn't. God has better things in store for you.

The ambition must change. Not to be rich in the world's sense, but to be rich in God.

Give up the one thing. To gain all.
