# Acts 11:19-26

**Title:** The Church in Crisis: When Persecution Plants Seeds
**Date:** 1 December 2013

## The Spread Through Persecution

> Now those who had been scattered by the persecution that broke out when Stephen was killed traveled as far as Phoenicia, Cyprus and Antioch, spreading the word only among Jews. (Acts 11:19)

When Stephen was martyred in chapter 7, it triggered a chain reaction. The mob mentality that killed him sparked a mass exodus—Christians scattered in all directions, fleeing for their lives. This was not cowardice; it was wisdom. As long as they weren't abandoning those who needed help, sometimes the most faithful thing is to remove yourself from a situation where you can no longer be effective.

Yet something remarkable happened: wherever they ran, they preached. Their displacement became the vehicle for spreading the gospel.

### A Pattern Throughout History

This pattern repeats throughout church history. The Reformers were persecuted by Rome, yet the Reformation spread. The Puritans fled England for America and planted thriving churches. The Anglicans split from Rome under persecution. The Quakers were persecuted by the Puritans. The Baptists by the Reformers.

There's a troubling tendency—whenever any group gains power, it persecutes minorities. This is not a godly thing; it's a human thing. Churches are not immune to these intrigues. But God uses even this darkness for His purposes. When the persecuted moved, they planted new churches and they grew.

Sometimes we become too comfortable in the status quo. We need a push—and nothing pushes quite like the desperate necessity of change, whether that comes through persecution or suffering. It's like the night before exams when we finally get serious, or when our mother finally loses her cool and we actually listen.

### A Present Challenge

In crisis, we're tempted toward action that costs *others* but not ourselves. We hear calls for economic shutdown, but ask: Who's making those calls? Are they restaurant owners? Waitresses? Tourist operators? Baggage handlers? Or are they people comfortable enough to advocate for sacrifice they won't personally bear?

Yet there's a better action available to all of us, regardless of whether we view this crisis as primarily a health or economic issue: **the gospel of hope**.

If we truly believe people will die without hope, then what are we doing about it? This is their last chance to repent. Death might not be as far away as we think. When we're stripped of our health, our business, our finances—when we're on our knees—we're in the perfect position to pray. To call upon our mighty God, to confess our sin, and to ask for His salvation.

What a great opportunity to speak of the hope that only Jesus provides.

## Growth Comes Through Godly Character

Urgency creates action, but the initial group faced a blind spot:

> And great that they took new opportunities. But nothing about growing? (v.19)
>
> They spread the word only among the Jews.

Antioch was a Gentile city in ancient Syria—the third-largest city in the Roman Empire. Yet the Jewish Christians only reached Jews. The once-persecuted became exclusive. Old hostilities between Jews and Gentiles persisted.

Then something shifted:

> Some of them, however, men from Cyprus and Cyrene, went to Antioch and began to speak to Greeks also, telling them the good news about the Lord Jesus. The Lord's hand was with them, and a great number of people believed and turned to the Lord. (Acts 11:20-21)

### Why the Different Results?

The distinction is significant. When Jews reached Jews, there was no mention of conversion. But when these men from Cyprus and Cyrene reached Greeks, people "turned to the Lord"—a phrase used throughout Acts for Gentile conversion (1 Thessalonians 1:9, Acts 14:15).

Jews didn't need to turn from idols (forbidden by the first commandment); they only needed to believe Jesus was Messiah (Acts 2:36-38). But Gentiles faced a deeper spiritual reorientation—turning from false gods to the living God.

The Jewish Christians faced an unconscious barrier: they saw Christianity as essentially Jewish. How could Gentiles believe without first becoming Jews? How could they follow Jesus without adopting Jewish culture, rituals, and practices?

But the gospel transcends culture. It doesn't eliminate culture, but you don't have to assimilate to the dominant culture in order to believe in Jesus. It's like a Chinese Sikh—culturally strange because we've linked identity to ethnicity. But allegiance to Jesus isn't about race; it's about the Lord.

### Barnabas: Character Matters

> News of this reached the church in Jerusalem, and they sent Barnabas to Antioch. When he arrived and saw what the grace of God had done, he was glad and encouraged them all to remain true to the Lord with all their hearts. He was a good man, full of the Holy Spirit and faith, and a great number of people were brought to the Lord. (Acts 11:22-24)

The Jerusalem leaders were shocked—a non-Jewish church? But Barnabas didn't arrive with suspicion or correction. He arrived, saw God's work, and was glad. Luke describes him as "full of the Holy Spirit and faith"—a phrase Luke uses to describe character, not supernatural power. Stephen, Barnabas, and the disciples were described as "full of the Spirit" (Acts 6:5, 7:55). Dorcas was "full of good works" (Acts 9:36). Elymas was "full of deceit" (Acts 13:10).

It describes *who they are*.

Barnabas was a godly man who spent his time in Gentile culture, teaching from God's Word—not Jewish culture, not Gentile culture, but Christian culture.

### A New Identity

> Then Barnabas went to Tarsus to look for Saul, and when he found him, he brought him to Antioch. So for a whole year Barnabas and Saul met with the church and taught great numbers of people. The disciples were called Christians first at Antioch. (Acts 11:25-26)

The term "Christian" was new. It was a designation for this new group of people who had no name before. Before this, the followers of Jesus would have been seen as another Jewish sect—Essenes, Sadducees, Pharisees, followers of the Way. But now there was a name for a community that transcended ethnic lines.

It was probably derogatory at first, like "Puritan," "Quaker," or "Protestant." But it became the identity for a new people united not by race or culture, but by allegiance to Christ.

## For Us Today

The world outside may be crazy—panic, hoarding, fear. But it cannot be crazy in here. We need godly people. We need you.

We need Christians who don't panic in times of trouble, but remain calm and collected, knowing their days are numbered. We need people who fear the loss of their soul more than the loss of their lives. Who fear God's judgment more than running out of supplies. Who look out for the interests of others and speak God's Word into the culture of fear around them.

We have no building. No massive gatherings. No band to sing inspirational songs. But we have the Word of God. We have each other. We have Christ and we have Christians—godly Christians who will go out and preach the Word.

What a great opportunity. What a great time.

If you've found this gathering useful, invite someone else. We're in multiple locations, and if you can't make it in person, we're only a Zoom away.

Whoever knows how long this shutdown lasts, but in two weeks or two months, we'll be a stronger church because we're full of the Spirit.
