# James 3:13-18

**Title:** Heavenly Wisdom: Knowing and Showing It

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## What Is Wisdom?

The world throws the word "wisdom" around constantly—associating it with good decisions, maturity, intelligence, academic success, or wealth. But what does true wisdom actually mean? The common image is the old sage sitting on a mountain, dispensing profound insights. Yet James shows us something different: wisdom is revealed not in what we claim to know, but in how we live. As James 3:13 tells us, the wise person "shows it by his good life, by deeds done in the humility that comes from wisdom."

The readers of James weren't particularly wise, and their behavior proved it. That's why James writes that if anyone lacks wisdom, he should ask for it from God—"the father of the heavenly lights"—who "gives generously to all without finding fault" (James 1:5). This isn't about becoming cleverer or raising our IQ. Heavenly wisdom is something far more profound: it is the knowledge of the gospel.

## Wisdom from Heaven: The Gospel

Consider what Paul writes in 1 Corinthians 1:22-24:

> Jews demand miraculous signs and Greeks look for wisdom. But we preach Christ crucified: a stumbling block to Jews and foolishness to Gentiles, but to those whom God has called, both Jews and Greeks, Christ the power of God and the wisdom of God.

And again in 1 Corinthians 1:29-30:

> It is because of him that you are in Christ Jesus, who has become for us wisdom from God.

Real wisdom is God revealing himself to humanity. God has revealed himself supremely in his Son, Jesus Christ. The wisdom of the world operates as if God is not real, and therefore it operates in an unreal world—a world of false priorities and ultimate emptiness.

Consider how different philosophies play by different rules. Materialism says: "He who has the most toys at the end wins." But for those who know the gospel, this is foolish—we know that possessions perish. Hedonism says: "He who has the most fun and pleasure wins." But for those with the gospel, we understand that pleasure is temporary, while heaven is eternal.

The gospel reframes everything. If we believe it, we are truly wise, because in Christ we have access to the wisest of all. Wisdom is the knowledge of what God has accomplished for us in Christ.

Yet the gospel demands something of us: we must forsake the non-Christian, worldly views of reality and begin seeing our world through the lens of the gospel. We must change our minds, replacing false perceptions with reality. This is transformation—not conforming to the pattern of the world (Romans 12:2), but being renewed by the study of his Word and by prayer.

When we ask God for wisdom (James 1:5), we're not asking to become cleverer or more intuitive. We're asking to know "the hope to which he has called you, and to work out how great it really is" (Ephesians 1:17-23; 3:16-19).

## Wisdom Must Be Shown

But here's the crucial point: wisdom isn't merely intellectual knowledge. It flows through into our actions. If you suddenly realize you're playing soccer instead of rugby, you don't keep trying to score tries, do you? You change your game to match reality.

This is why James 1:22 is so important:

> Do not merely listen to the word, and so deceive yourselves. Do what it says!

Throughout James, different teachers claim to be wise. But how do we identify who truly is? By looking at their good life and humble deeds done in wisdom (James 3:13). It's not enough to claim wisdom—it must be demonstrated. Homer Simpson singing "I am smart, S-M-A-R-T" is clearly anything but smart. Wisdom is profoundly practical.

## Two Kinds of Wisdom

James presents a stark contrast between two kinds of wisdom:

**Earthly, Unspiritual Wisdom**
- Origins: earthly, un-spiritual, devilish
- Characteristics: bitter envy, selfish ambition, jealousy
- Result: disorder, every evil thing

What causes quarrels and disorder? Our own selfishness—the decision that our needs matter more than others'. It flows from envy, jealousy, and the prioritization of self.

**Heavenly Wisdom**
- Origins: from above, heavenly
- Characteristics: pure, peace-loving, considerate, submissive, full of mercy and good fruit, impartial and sincere
- Result: good life, harvest of righteousness

When we possess heavenly wisdom—the gospel—our actions will reflect it. This isn't mystical; it's practical:

- **Pure:** How do you act with your boyfriend or girlfriend when others are watching and when they're not? Christ died to give us holy lives. What will you do with that?

- **Peace-loving:** Will you gossip, slander, quarrel, or will you pursue peace?

- **Considerate:** Will you put others' needs before your own? Share your food at recess? Christ sacrificed his life for you—undeserving as you are. How will you respond to those weaker than you?

- **Submissive:** Will you submit to the authority of parents, teachers, and leaders? If Jesus is ruler over the universe, how can you refuse to submit to his authority and to those he has entrusted with authority?

- **Full of mercy and good fruit:** Will you look out for the weak and vulnerable?

- **Impartial and sincere:** Will you treat people fairly, or only befriend those who are popular?

## Living Out Heavenly Wisdom

For those who remain unwise, still playing the wrong game—whatever you build will ultimately come to nothing. A try scored in soccer is not a goal. If you lack wisdom, ask for it from the God who is generous to all.

But if you're in Christ, the will of God is known: that everything in the world come under Christ's authority. Your task is to act appropriately to that reality. This isn't mystical guidance—it's objective reasoning about the implications of the gospel in your life.

The Bible plays a crucial role here. As we study Scripture, we become more aware of what Christ has done for us. When we appreciate his work with grateful and humble hearts, we naturally make decisions that align with that reality.

## The Challenge

Who is wise and understanding among you? Show it by your good life, by deeds done in the humility that comes from wisdom. We'll see, won't we?
