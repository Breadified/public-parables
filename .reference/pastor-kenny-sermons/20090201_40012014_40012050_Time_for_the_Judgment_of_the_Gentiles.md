# Matthew 12:14-50

**Title:** Time for the Judgment of the Gentiles
**Date:** 22 February 2009
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Context

Jesus has faced mounting opposition from the Pharisees, who plotted to kill him. This passage marks a significant turning point: Jesus moves into a new phase of his mission—opening the kingdom to the Gentiles. His response reveals both the nature of God's judgment and the spiritual blindness of those who refuse to receive his mercy.

---

## Part A: The Judging Spirit (Matthew 12:14-23)

### The Prophet's Promise

When the Pharisees plotted against him, Jesus withdrew and continued healing. Matthew connects this to Isaiah's prophecy about God's chosen servant:

> Here is my servant whom I have chosen,
> the one I love, in whom I delight;
> I will put my Spirit on him,
> and he will proclaim justice to the nations.
> He will not quarrel or cry out;
> no one will hear his voice in the streets.
> A bruised reed he will not break,
> and a smoldering wick he will not snuff out,
> till he leads justice to victory.
> In his name the nations will put their hope.

The key phrase is "justice to the nations"—literally, judgment to the Gentiles. This fulfills Isaiah's ancient promise that the gospel, first given to the Jews, would extend to include all people.

### A Spirit of Judgment on the Gentiles

Jesus is anointed with the Spirit to bring God's judgment—but not judgment of condemnation. The Hebrew word for "nations" (used here and in Matthew 10:5, "do not go to the Gentiles") refers to the Gentiles. Isaiah promises that God will make the Messiah "a light for the Gentiles, to open eyes that are blind, to free captives from prison and to release from the dungeon those who sit in darkness."

This judgment is merciful and restorative.

### A Spirit of Hope for the Gentiles

God's dealings with the Gentiles in history had been stern—think of Egypt's destruction at the Red Sea, its army "snuffed out like a wick." Yet notice the language: the wick is "smoldering," not completely extinguished. There remains hope.

Even Egypt, the traditional Gentile enemy of Israel, receives the promise of mercy. The bruised reed will not be broken. The smoldering wick will not be snuffed out. This is not judgment unto destruction, but judgment unto victory and salvation. "In his name, the nations will put their hope."

### The Illustration: Healing the Blind and Mute

Immediately after this teaching, Jesus demonstrates his compassion:

> Then they brought him a demon-possessed man who was blind and mute, and Jesus healed him, so that he could both talk and see. All the people were astonished and said, "Could this be the Son of David?"

This miracle symbolizes what Jesus does for the Gentiles. Unlike other healings in Matthew where individuals had single afflictions, this man carries the full weight of spiritual blindness and deafness—plus demonic possession. The Gentiles are described throughout Scripture as deaf and blind, lacking the revelation, law, covenant, and promises given to Israel. They are ignorant of God's truth.

Yet Jesus opens their eyes and unstops their ears. He brings them from darkness into light.

## Part B: The Judgmental Spirit (Matthew 12:24-45)

### The Pharisees' Problem

The Pharisees respond to this miracle with two accusations:

> When the Pharisees heard this, they said, "It is only by Beelzebub, the prince of demons, that this fellow drives out demons."

And later:

> Some of the Pharisees and teachers of the law said to him, "Teacher, we want to see a miraculous sign from you."

The Pharisees possess what Jesus will condemn—a judgmental, prejudicial spirit. They cannot bear to see the weak, the outsider, the Gentile admitted into God's kingdom. Where Jesus embodies a spirit of salvation and mercy toward the Gentiles, the Pharisees embody a spirit against that salvation. They are unmerciful to the outsider, and in that unmercifulnessthey condemn themselves.

### The Unforgivable Sin

> Every sin and blasphemy will be forgiven men, but the blasphemy against the Holy Spirit will not be forgiven. Anyone who speaks a word against the Son of Man will be forgiven, but anyone who speaks against the Holy Spirit will not be forgiven, either in this age or in the age to come.

This is not merely about swearing at God. It describes a spirit of unforgiveness—a refusal to acknowledge God's mercy and salvation. The Pharisees, in their hard hearts, reject the Holy Spirit's work of extending grace to the Gentiles. They condemn themselves through their own lack of mercy.

Jesus teaches later (in Matthew 18) that if we cannot forgive, we will not be forgiven. The Pharisees' unmerciful hearts seal their own judgment.

### Condemned by Their Own Actions

> Every kingdom divided against itself will be ruined, and every city or household divided against itself will not stand. If Satan drives out Satan, he is divided against himself. How then can his kingdom stand? But if I drive out demons by the Spirit of God, then the kingdom of God has come upon you.

The logic is simple: a kingdom divided against itself cannot stand. The Pharisees attack one of their own—Jesus, an Israelite—in their refusal to accept God's broader plan of salvation. They fail to see that a stronger power has arrived. They are not gathered with Jesus; they scatter. Their household is divided, and it will not stand.

### Condemned by Their Own Words

> Make a tree good and its fruit will be good, or make a tree bad and its fruit will be bad, for a tree is recognized by its fruit. You brood of vipers, how can you who are evil say anything good? For out of the overflow of the heart the mouth speaks. The good man brings good things out of the good stored up in him, and the evil man brings evil things out of the evil stored up in him. But I tell you that men will have to give account on the day of judgment for every careless word they have spoken. For by your words you will be acquitted, and by your words you will be condemned.

The Pharisees' words reveal their evil hearts. They think of themselves as clean, as God's chosen people. But they are worse than the very Gentiles they despise:

> When an evil spirit comes out of a man, it goes through arid places seeking rest and does not find it. Then it says, "I will return to the house I left." When it arrives, it finds the house unoccupied, swept clean and put in order. Then it goes and takes with it seven other spirits more wicked than itself, and they go in and live there. And the final condition of that man is worse than the first. That is how it will be with this wicked generation.

A clean exterior without a merciful heart is spiritually dangerous. The Pharisees are emptied of God's grace, and darkness rushes in.

### Condemned by the Gentiles

The Pharisees face a final humiliation: they will be condemned not by Jesus alone, but by the very Gentiles they despised.

**The Men of Nineveh:**

> A wicked and adulterous generation asks for a miraculous sign! But none will be given it except the sign of the prophet Jonah. For as Jonah was three days and three nights in the belly of a huge fish, so the Son of Man will be three days and three nights in the heart of the earth. The men of Nineveh will stand up at the judgment with this generation and condemn it; for they repented at the preaching of Jonah, and now one greater than Jonah is here.

Jonah fled from God's call to preach to Assyria, the Gentile nation. He did not want to save them. When they repented anyway, Jonah resented God's mercy:

> O Lord, is this not what I said when I was still at home? That is why I was so quick to flee to Tarshish. I knew that you are a gracious and compassionate God, slow to anger and abounding in love, a God who relents from sending calamity. Now, take away my life, for it is better for me to die than to live.

The Pharisees, like Jonah, lack the love of God in their hearts. They do not possess a merciful heart. Yet the Ninevites—mere Gentiles—repented at Jonah's preaching. How much more should this generation repent at the preaching of One greater than Jonah?

**The Queen of the South:**

> The Queen of the South will rise at the judgment with this generation and condemn it; for she came from the ends of the earth to listen to Solomon's wisdom, and now one greater than Solomon is here.

A foreign queen traveled vast distances to receive wisdom. Yet the Jewish leaders refuse to recognize One greater than Solomon standing before them. The Gentile woman will condemn them through her own eagerness.

### The Real Problem

The Pharisees have no room in their theology for the weak, the alien, the outsider. They cannot conceive of God's love extending beyond those they deem worthy. This is not a small failure—it is spiritual death. Without mercy, without the willingness to make room for the outsider, they seal their own condemnation.

## Part C: A New Basis for Judgment (Matthew 12:46-50)

### The New Family

> While Jesus was still talking to the crowd, his mother and brothers stood outside, wanting to speak to him. Someone told him, "Your mother and brothers are standing outside, wanting to speak to you." He replied to him, "Who is my mother, and who are my brothers?" Pointing to his disciples, he said, "Here are my mother and my brothers. For whoever does the will of my Father in heaven is my brother and sister and mother."

In one radical statement, Jesus redefines family, belonging, and judgment itself. It is no longer about being born Jewish or Gentile, privileged or disadvantaged, leader or follower. The only criterion is this: **Do you do the will of my Father?**

Jew or Gentile, strong or weak, high-status or low-status—none of it matters. What matters is whether you align yourself with God's will, which is mercy, healing, and inclusion of all who believe.

### Living Amidst Opposition

Jesus teaches and acts this way while facing active opposition. He does not quarrel. He does not cry out in the streets. His method is quiet, merciful, unbroken toward the bruised reed. Yet his message is revolutionary: God's kingdom has come, and it is wider and more merciful than anyone imagined.

### A Call for Our Time

The question echoes to us: Where do we draw the lines? Are we merciful to the weak, the outsider, the one society has written off? Or do we harbor a spirit like the Pharisees—excluding those we deem unworthy of God's grace?

Do we welcome the unlikely? The broken? The one from the wrong background? Or do we find reasons to keep them at arm's length?

Jesus's answer is clear: **Whoever does the will of my Father is family.** There is no other criterion. In Christ, all other distinctions fade. What remains is the simple, radical call to mercy and obedience to God's will.
