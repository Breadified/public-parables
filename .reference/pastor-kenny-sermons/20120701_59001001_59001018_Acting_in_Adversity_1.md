# James 1:1-18

**Title:** Acting in Adversity
**Date:** 18 July 2012
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Introduction: Understanding Trials

Have you ever faced a situation where your trust in Jesus and God's goodness was tested? Not necessarily through persecution, but through the ordinary trials of life—unemployment, a difficult boss, chronic sickness, family problems, relationship struggles, bullying at school, or the death of a loved one.

These trials come in many forms, and we often wonder why God allows them. Early in my time at Bible college, a friend and his wife were expecting twins. The babies were due to be induced on Saturday at 1pm, but at noon his wife began bleeding profusely. They rushed to the hospital, but the babies were stillborn—they suffocated from a lack of oxygen. An hour before they were to be born, they died. At the funeral, my friend described it this way: "A bandit came into my home, stole my babies, and left a puddle of blood behind." His faith in God's goodness was being severely tested. This is the question James addresses: How do we respond to trials, suffering, and tragedy?

---

## 1. God's Testing Brings Life (1:2-12)

### Enjoy Your Trials (v. 2)

James writes something that seems almost harsh:

> Consider it pure joy, my brothers, whenever you face trials of many kinds.

This seems contradictory. How can we be joyful while experiencing disappointment, frustration, and sadness? Yet it is possible to hold two states simultaneously. We can acknowledge our pain while maintaining joy for the following reasons:

### Trials Produce Perseverance (v. 3-4)

> The testing of your faith develops perseverance. Perseverance must finish its work so that you may be mature and complete, not lacking anything.

Trials are not random. They produce **perseverance**—the ability to trust God and obey Him regardless of circumstances. This perseverance is essential because it leads to maturity and completeness in Christ. We wish there were another way—that God could supernaturally transform us without pain. But God has chosen this method.

Consider how testing works in other areas of life. We test batteries to determine which ones last longest. We test gold by fire to verify its genuineness. Testing separates the genuine from the counterfeit. Similarly, trials reveal the true character of our faith.

**Example:** At the gym, the most tempting time to stop exercising is when it starts to hurt. Yet that exact moment is when old muscle burns away and new, stronger muscle develops. That's precisely why you're at the gym. If you don't endure, you don't complete the goal.

Similarly, when taking antibiotics, if you don't persevere through the full course, the medication doesn't achieve its purpose and the illness returns. We need to cultivate resilience in ourselves and in our children.

### Seek Wisdom in Your Trials (v. 5)

> If any of you lacks wisdom, he should ask God, who gives generously to all without finding fault, and it will be given to him.

This wisdom is the maturity and completeness we need to navigate our trials. God gives generously without finding fault—He doesn't shame us for asking.

### Trials Distinguish the Blessed from the Fading (v. 9-11)

> The brother in humble circumstances ought to take pride in his high position. But the one who is rich should take pride in his low position, because he will pass away like a wild flower.

The poor person facing trials should be proud because, if they persevere and continue doing right, they will become mature and complete. No wonder James can say they should rejoice!

The rich person, by contrast, is untested. They live in comfort and independence, needing no one. But:

> The sun rises with scorching heat and withers the plant; its blossom falls and its beauty is destroyed. In the same way, the rich man will fade away even while he goes about his business.

**Example:** Astronauts in space experience weightlessness. Without gravity's resistance, their muscles never have to work. When they return to earth after a long time in orbit, they collapse. Without resistance, their muscles have atrophied. In contrast, those who face resistance yet persevere develop strength.

The tested life, though difficult, builds genuine strength. The untested life, though comfortable, leads to spiritual atrophy.

### Trials Lead to Life (v. 12)

> Blessed is the man who perseveres under trial, because when he has stood the test, he will receive the crown of life that God has promised to those who love him.

The "crown of life" is a Greek symbol of victory—like the laurels given to Olympic champions. For Christians, victory means eternal life itself. When we persevere through testing, we receive the crown of life—salvation, heaven, eternal fellowship with God.

As Peter reminds us: "The God of all grace, after you have suffered a little while, will himself restore you and make you strong, firm and steadfast."

### Application: Don't Give Up the Crown

Consider Joni Eareckson Tada, who became a quadriplegic after a diving accident decades ago. She suffers from chronic pain and was recently diagnosed with breast cancer. Yet she writes:

> Only God knows why I was paralyzed. Maybe He knew I'd be ultimately happier serving Him. If I were still on my feet, it's hard to say how things have gone. I probably would have drifted through life—marriage, maybe even divorce—dissatisfied and disillusioned. When I was in high school, I reacted to life selfishly and never built on any long-lasting values, and almost always at the expense of others.

Trials reveal our inadequacies and show us where we need growth. As John Calvin observed:

> If we are given gold, would we not test it to determine its value? If we doubted its genuineness, we would test it by fire. So God does with us. The fire of affliction reveals the quality of our faith.

Yet we struggle with steadfastness. The long, dull, monotonous years—whether of prosperity or adversity—are where souls wear away. As C.S. Lewis wrote in *The Screwtape Letters*:

> The long, dull, monotonous years of middle-aged prosperity or middle-aged adversity are excellent campaigning weather. You see, it is so hard for these creatures to persevere. The routine of adversity, the gradual decay of youthful loves and youthful hopes, the quest despair of ever overcoming the chronic temptations—all this provides admirable opportunities of wearing out a soul by attrition.

Many of us in comfortable places like Australia face a different challenge. Our exchange rates have risen. We subcontract our responsibilities. We satisfy our desires instantly through apps and credit cards. Life is easier, and therein lies a problem: without challenge, we may not mature spiritually.

We should be thankful for God's blessings, but we must also cultivate endurance. Perhaps we should practice delayed gratification—observe Lent, exercise regularly, serve in challenging contexts, give away our wealth and live simply. God desires a resilient people who remain steadfast to Him when times are tough.

We must also encourage one another. Church exists to help us persevere through hardship. Sometimes people in trials don't need solutions; they need to know someone cares and will listen. And we must teach our children resilience. In our eagerness to protect them from failure, we often shield them from healthy struggle. When they don't want to study, we hire tutors. When exams approach, we excuse them from chores. But this teaches them that someone else will solve their problems. One day they'll face a problem we cannot fix, and they'll give up—perhaps even give up on Jesus.

Let us be a tested people. On that final day, there are only two options: those who persevered and received the crown of life, or those who faded away in the scorching heat.

## 2. Do Not Test God (1:6-8, 13-17)

While God's testing of us leads to life, the opposite is true: testing God leads to death.

### How We Test God (v. 6-8)

> When he asks, he must believe and not doubt, because he who doubts is like a wave of the sea, blown and tossed by the wind. That man should not think he will receive anything from the Lord; he is a double-minded man, unstable in all he does.

When we ask God for wisdom, we must ask in faith—believing that God will provide what is truly good for us. "Doubting" here doesn't mean asking honest questions about Scripture or God's actions. Rather, it means being suspicious of God's intentions—thinking He's out to get us, or that He is evil. Such a person is like the waves of the sea, double-minded and unstable, wavering between two loyalties.

But here's the problem: you can't ask God for wisdom and then immediately say the test is too hard. You can't ask for help and then doubt God's goodness. In doing so, you are actually **testing God**—and testing God brings death.

### How We Blame God and Sin (v. 13-15)

> When tempted, no one should say, "God is tempting me." For God cannot be tempted by evil, nor does he tempt anyone; but each one is tempted when, by his own evil desire, he is dragged away and enticed. Then, after desire has conceived, it gives birth to sin; and sin, when it is full-grown, gives birth to death.

When faced with testing, people often blame God. They act like Bart Simpson: "My arm is going like this already; it's not my fault if you hit it." But this thinking leads to death.

Notice the progression:
- **Before (God tests us):** Trials → Perseverance → Maturity/Wisdom → Life
- **Now (We test God):** Temptation → Desire → Sin → Death

We use trials as excuses for lowered standards:
- "Not my fault I'm poor—I'll be stingy and selfish"
- "Not my fault I have a lousy job—I'll cheat the company"
- "Not my fault I'm lonely—I'll marry a non-Christian"
- "Not my fault movies are expensive—I'll download them illegally"
- "Not my fault my parents won't let me skip church—I'll stop being Christian"
- "Not my fault I'm sick—I'll cheat on exams"

These desires come from within us, not from God. And testing can never justify sin. If you sin, you've already failed in perseverance.

### God's Nature: Only Good (v. 16-17)

> Don't be deceived, my dear brothers. Every good and perfect gift is from above, coming down from the Father of the heavenly lights, who does not change like shifting shadows.

God is not evil—we are. Sin comes not from external sources but from within us. God does not entrap people in their sins. God is good. He is consistent and not sneaky. He doesn't decide one day to be merciful and another to be cruel. He's not playing games with us. He never changes like shifting shadows.

You cannot test God because God's goodness has already been tested and proven.

## 3. God Has Already Been Tested (1:18)

> He chose to give us birth through the word of truth, that we might be a kind of firstfruits of all he created.

By His own initiative, God sent His Son to redeem us from sin. He has made us the firstfruits of His creation. Instead of a birth leading to death (as in v. 15), we are born to life through the gospel.

> God demonstrates his love for us in this, while we were still sinners, Christ died for us. (Romans 5:8)

We will always fail our trials—even as we try not to. But Jesus not only suffered as we do; He actually persevered to the very end. He cried, "My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?" Yet He obediently remained on the cross to die. Jesus never wavered in where His loyalties lay. He never doubted God's goodness. He endured every test through obedience.

This is the God we must trust, the God we must persevere for, regardless of what circumstances life holds.

As Lewis wrote in *The Screwtape Letters*:

> It is during such trough periods, much more than during the peak periods, that [the creature] is growing into the sort of creature He wants it to be. Do not be deceived, Wormwood, our cause is never more in danger than when a human, no longer desiring but still intending to do our Enemy's will, looks round upon a universe from which every trace of Him seems to have vanished, and asks why he has been forsaken—and still obeys.

---

## Conclusion

Friends, there are two responses to trials and temptations. One leads to joy, perseverance, wisdom, and life. The other leads to sin and death. The choice is ours.

Do not be deceived. Blessed is the man who remains steadfast under trial. Persevere, for the crown of life awaits those who love Him.
