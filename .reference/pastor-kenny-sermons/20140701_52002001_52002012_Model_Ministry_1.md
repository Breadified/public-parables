# 1 Thessalonians 2:1-12

**Title:** Model Ministry
**Date:** 13 July 2014
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction: Ministry as Service

Ministry means service. The question Paul addresses in this passage is fundamental: **How should ministry be exercised?** This is particularly relevant to us today as many people are suspicious of the church and its motives.

Paul has come to Thessalonica to tell the people about Jesus. But his methods are being questioned. Chapter 2 is Paul's defense of how he conducted his ministry—a defense that remains deeply relevant to how we exercise our ministries today.

> For you know, brothers and sisters, that our visit to you was not without results. We had previously suffered and been treated outrageously in Philippi, as you know, but with the help of our God we dared to tell you his gospel in the face of strong opposition. (1 Thessalonians 2:1-2)

Paul presents **three models of ministry** based on his relationship with the Thessalonians.

---

## 1. Like an Infant: Innocent and Truthful (2:3-6a)

> Instead, we were like young children among you. (2:7)

Different Bible versions translate this verse differently. Some say "gentle," others say "infants." The difference lies in a single Greek letter—the difference between *hēpios* (gentle) and *nēpios* (infants). Scholars determine that the best ancient manuscripts support "infants," reflecting Paul's intent to describe his own innocence and integrity.

### A. Paul Had No Evil Agenda

> For the appeal we make does not spring from error or impure motives, nor are we trying to trick you. On the contrary, we speak as those approved by God to be entrusted with the gospel. We are not trying to please people but God, who tests our hearts. You know we never used flattery, nor did we put on a mask to cover up greed—God is our witness. (2:3-5)

Paul is defending himself against accusations. Like an innocent infant, he had no hidden, malicious motives. This doesn't mean we can't have multiple good motivations—it's normal and healthy to have several innocent agendas at once. When we invite friends to church, we genuinely want fellowship *and* we want to share the gospel. Both motivations are good.

What Paul condemns is something far darker: **intentional deception for evil gain**—like a bait-and-switch religious organization that uses false pretenses to draw people in.

Paul's approach is refreshingly honest. As believers, we should openly declare our Christian faith. We should naturally invite our friends to church. We should tell them about Jesus without hiding our intentions. We have **no reason to conceal the truth** because we don't believe in deceiving people. Our motives don't require disguising.

### B. Paul Was Not a People-Pleaser

The hallmark of a people-pleaser is flattery—telling people what they want to hear rather than what is true. Watch a skilled salesman: he'll flatter you, tell you how wonderful you are, and suggest his product will make you even better. But he'll never tell you the hard truth.

Like an infant, Paul was a truth-teller. Infants don't flatter their parents—they tell you exactly what they think, whether it's convenient or not.

So when Paul addresses the Thessalonians, he speaks difficult truths: "You are sinners. Turn from your idols and serve the living and true God!" Sometimes at church we address controversial topics. That should be the case. We should refute error. We must never be afraid of hard truth simply because it might offend or because powerful people might get angry.

The better question is not: "Will this hurt someone's feelings?" The better question is: **"Is this true? What truths are we dealing with? If this isn't the truth, what is?"**

### C. Paul Was Not Seeking Ego or Praise

> We never were looking for praise from people, not from you or anyone else, even though as apostles of Christ we could have asserted our authority. (2:6)

Paul had legitimate authority as an apostle. He could have demanded respect, created a hierarchy, insisted on being treated differently. He could have set himself apart, created a "green room" mentality—a special place for important people with their own food, privileges, and special treatment.

But Paul rejected this. An infant doesn't seek Instagram likes or personal adoration. An infant wants the toy, not the acclaim. Similarly, Paul's ministry wasn't driven by the desire for recognition, titles, or career advancement.

This confronts modern ministry culture directly. Today, "full-time ministry" and "ministry apprentice" roles can carry a perception of spiritual hierarchy. Even secular culture praises these roles: "You're following your dreams! You're so sacrificial! What a wonderful person you are!" This can subtly create a sense that some people are in a different, more important spiritual class.

But Paul's model rejects this entirely. **There is no green room here.** When we read Scripture together, we ALL read it. When we pray, we ALL pray together. We ALL participate equally.

The real test is this: **Are we serving because we want acknowledgement and titles, or are we serving out of love?** When we do the right but unpopular thing, we won't get praise. We might not gain anything. And that's okay.

## 2. Like a Mother: Caring and Life-Sharing (2:7b-8)

> Just as a nursing mother cares for her children, so we cared for you. Because we loved you so much, we were delighted to share with you not only the gospel of God but our lives as well. (2:7b-8)

If an infant represents innocence, a **mother represents care and vulnerability**—the willingness to share your very life with those you're serving.

### A. A Mother Cares

A mother's care is distinctive. Children run to their mother when hurt because they know they'll receive tenderness and comfort. A mother's affection is soft and nurturing. She is present, attentive, and responsive to her children's needs.

Paul mirrors this: he cared for the Thessalonians and loved them deeply. His ministry wasn't transactional; it was relational.

### B. A Mother Shares Her Life

A mother literally shares her body with her child—first in the womb, then through breastfeeding. She shares her wisdom, her experience, her presence. From gestation through infancy, a mother is intimately involved in shaping her child's life and emotional foundation.

This is attachment theory at work: a mother provides the emotional security that allows her child to develop healthily, to love others, and to establish patterns for life.

**Paul set this trajectory for the Thessalonians.** He didn't just teach them doctrine; he lived alongside them:

> You know how we lived among you for your sake. (1 Thessalonians 1:5)

This authenticity gave power to his message. The Thessalonians could see whether Paul actually believed what he taught. They could observe his integrity. His shared life validated his words.

This principle still applies today. A salesman telling you a product is great while he uses a competitor's product undermines his own message. Similarly, when we share the gospel, people ask: Does this message actually transform the person teaching it? We must be willing to share our lives, not just our doctrine.

### C. Ministry Modeled at This Church

Our church aspires to this model. We are all ministers—servants of one another. We:

- **Care practically**: University students advise HSC students. Members share job opportunities with the unemployed. We help one another navigate life's challenges.
- **Pray for one another**: We intercede for those who are sick or struggling.
- **Share our lives**: We gather regularly not just to study Scripture but to know each other deeply. Trust takes time, but as we share more of ourselves, we become better equipped to minister to one another.

The principle is simple: **The more time we invest, the more we share ourselves, the better we can minister to others and receive ministry from them.** This requires commitment to gathering together, to being present, to opening our lives.

To those new to this church: we invite you to join this family of God. We want to tell you more about Jesus. We want to share not just the gospel but our lives as well. This is Paul's vision in action.

## 3. Like a Father: Teaching and Encouraging (2:9-12)

> For you know that we dealt with each of you as a father deals with his own children, encouraging, comforting and urging you to live lives worthy of God, who calls you into his kingdom and glory. (2:11-12)

If mothers represent nurturing care, **fathers in the ancient world represented education and example**. A father taught his children how to work, how to earn a living, how to solve problems, how to behave socially, and how to walk with God.

### A. Paul's Fatherly Discipline and Teaching

Paul's fatherly methods involved three elements:
- **Encouraging**: Not merely cheerleading, but also gentle rebuke and discipline
- **Comforting**: Emotional support during hardship
- **Urging**: Calling them to live worthy of their calling

Paul emphasizes his own integrity:

> You are witnesses, and so is God, of how holy, righteous and blameless we were among you who believed. (2:10)

This echoes the principle found in Hebrews:

> Endure hardship as discipline; God is treating you as his children. For what children are not disciplined by their father? If you are not disciplined—and everyone undergoes discipline—then you are not legitimate, not true sons and daughters at all. (Hebrews 12:7-8)

A father's discipline is an expression of love and legitimate care.

### B. Paul's Sacrifice and Hard Work

> Surely you remember, brothers and sisters, our toil and hardship; we worked night and day in order not to be a burden to anyone while we preached the gospel of God to you. (2:9)

Paul was a tentmaker by trade. He worked night and day, not merely to support himself, but to avoid being a financial burden on the young church. This was costly sacrifice on behalf of people he loved.

When Paul couldn't visit them, he sent Timothy to check on them. His concern was constant and genuine.

> For what is our hope, our joy, or the crown in which we will glory in the presence of our Lord Jesus when he comes? Is it not you? Indeed, you are our glory and joy. (2:19-20)

Paul's reward wasn't an ulterior motive—it was a good one. His hope lay in seeing the Thessalonians mature in Christ. **This is the power of the changed life.** As Paul's life was transformed by Christ, he testified to that reality. The Thessalonians, hearing his words and witnessing his life, imitated him. They too were changed. They became a model for other believers, including us today.

### C. Fatherly Ministry Today

Many of you are working hard for the Lord. Some serve the youth—not just teaching them Scripture on Sundays, but sharing your lives, attending their musicals, showing them what Christian living looks like. Others share the gospel with international students and then go beyond: you join them for karaoke, you share meals, you open your lives.

This is fatherly ministry—the kind that shapes trajectories and sets patterns for life.

Your reward will transcend any earthly recognition: **you will receive a crown greater than any jewel-encrusted headpiece.**

## The Model Ministry: Living It Out

This is the ideal ministry we are called to emulate: **innocent as infants, caring as a mother, hardworking as a father.** We will fall short. We are human. But when we stray, we pick ourselves up and head in the right direction again.

The core conviction is this: **A ministry that shares not just the gospel but also our lives.**

The coronation ceremony that opened this sermon is old, ceremonial, and formal. But it powerfully reminds us that authority is not about self-aggrandizement. The king is anointed by bishops who symbolize that God—not the king's cleverness or wealth—has placed him in that position. The ceremony humbles rather than exalts.

Similarly, our ministries must be characterized by:
- **Innocence**: No hidden agendas, no deception, truthfulness even when it's costly
- **Care**: Genuine love for those we serve, willingness to be vulnerable and present
- **Hard work**: Sacrifice and dedication rooted in love, not pride or recognition-seeking

We serve the King of Kings. Let us serve with integrity, love, and tireless devotion to His kingdom.
