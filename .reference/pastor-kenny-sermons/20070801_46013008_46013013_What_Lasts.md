# 1 Corinthians 13:8-13

**Title:** What Lasts?  
**Date:** 13 August 2007  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction: What Truly Lasts?

Consider what surrounds us: a doughnut lasts a day; pizza, maybe a few days; clothes, a season or a couple of years; homework, until the next year; your house, perhaps your lifetime or your children's lifetime; grand structures like the Opera House or Harbour Bridge, maybe 150 years.

Everything around us has an expiry date.

But this question matters for how we invest ourselves. Generally, the longer something lasts, the more effort we're willing to invest in it. We invest heavily in HSC because we believe its effects will endure for years. We spend more time on housing decisions than on lunch. Our choices are shaped by how long we think their consequences will persist.

So what truly lasts? Let's look at what Paul tells us in 1 Corinthians 13:8.

## 1. The Motivation of Love Never Passes Away

> **1 Corinthians 13:8** Love never fails. But where there are prophecies, they will cease; where there are tongues, they will be stilled; where there is knowledge, it will pass away.

Paul makes a striking contrast. The Greek word for "fail" literally means to fall to one's ruin, to be destroyed, to come to an end. Love never experiences this—it never ceases, never is stilled, never passes away.

### The Corinthian Controversy

In the Corinthian church, different spiritual gifts were creating tension. Some thought prophecy was most important; others championed speaking in tongues; still others valued knowledge and learning. Each person with their particular gift seemed to think theirs mattered most—the preacher, the translator, the learned teacher. Who was right?

Paul's answer cuts through the argument: prophecies will cease, tongues will be stilled, knowledge will pass away. Notice the contrast—these things have expiry dates. But love? Love never fails.

### Function vs. Motivation

This isn't primarily about which function is more important. It's about something deeper: the motivation underlying those functions. Paul is saying that regardless of what task we perform—preaching, teaching, translating—the love motivating us is what truly endures. The function is temporary; the motivation is eternal.

### Why Does Love Last Forever?

Earlier in chapter 13, Paul had already made love's supremacy clear:

> **1 Corinthians 13:1-3** If I speak in the tongues of men and of angels, but have not love, I am only a resounding gong or a clanging cymbal. If I have the gift of prophecy and can fathom all mysteries and all knowledge, and if I have a faith that can move mountains, but have not love, I am nothing. If I give all I possess to the poor and surrender my body to the flames, but have not love, I gain nothing.

Love is the most excellent way. Love for others as an underlying motivation—not self-centered ambition, not careerism, not the pursuit of recognition—is what God values eternally.

### Application: Where Is Your Motivation?

Consider your work. Maybe you produce spreadsheets, or build infrastructure, or care for the sick. Not all jobs are satisfying. Not all are well-paid. Not all bring recognition. But if done with love—if your underlying motivation is to benefit others and serve their wellbeing—then that work, however ordinary, is as valuable as any grand spiritual function.

Our Father sees that faithful motivation. He is pleased.

But this doesn't mean manufacturing feelings. Rather, look for opportunities for love. If your job's nature doesn't naturally express love toward others, seek out those around you who do need it. Love makes everything negotiable—moving a table, canceling lunch, choosing to use or not use a projector for the sake of others' benefit. Love shifts our perspective from what we want to what serves others.

Even in church—there are many tasks and functions. That's fine. But do them out of love, knowing they will help someone. Seek opportunities to love. This is doing things for the kingdom of God, not for our own preferences or self-respect.

## 2. The Remnants of the Ages

> **1 Corinthians 13:9-13** For we know in part and we prophesy in part, but when perfection comes, the imperfect disappears. When I was a child, I talked like a child, I thought like a child, I reasoned like a child. When I became a man, I put childish ways behind me. Now we see but a poor reflection as in a mirror; then we shall see face to face. Now I know in part; then I shall know fully, even as I am fully known. And now these three remain: faith, hope and love. But the greatest of these is love.

Paul repeats his principle: our knowledge is partial, our prophecy is partial. But when perfection comes—when we stand before God in wholeness—the incompleteness disappears.

### Two Illustrations of Partiality

**First, childhood to adulthood.** A child's knowledge is fragmentary, reasoning incomplete. But when adulthood comes, the childish ways are left behind. The partial gives way to the complete.

**Second, mirror to face-to-face.** In Paul's day, mirrors gave poor reflections—missing details, incomplete impressions. What you see in a mirror is never the whole picture. But when we stand face to face before God, there is clarity, completeness. The incompleteness is gone.

### What This Means

Paul isn't making a philosophical point about epistemology or stages of maturity. He's emphasizing the supremacy of love. All our deeds are partial. All our functions are temporary. Our knowledge passes. Our prophecies cease. But the motivation of love? It endures.

### The Judgment Day Perspective

Think ahead to judgment day. An accountant's spreadsheets—burned up. A builder's bridge—melted down. A doctor's patients—eventually they all die. The only function that truly lasts is the conversion of souls. That is eternal fruit.

Even those who dedicate their lives to gospel ministry need this reminder: the hours you spend telling people the Bible—that will last. The administrative tasks you do—those won't. Some work generates eternal consequences; some doesn't. Paul will address this next in chapter 14, where he urges us to pursue the gifts that build people up toward faith. But the foundation of it all is love.

### Application: What Will You Invest In?

At the end of the day, it's not really about what you do. If love is your motivation, it's good. You'll make many imperfect decisions—choosing between good things, trying to discern which function will best love others. You won't always get it right. But with love as your underlying motivation, you remain faithful to what endures.

Ask yourself: What will you invest in? What function, carried out in love, will generate eternal consequences? What will further God's kingdom?

That's the question for next week.
