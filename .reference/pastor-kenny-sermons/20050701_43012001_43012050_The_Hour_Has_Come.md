# John 12:1-50

**Title:** The Hour Has Come
**Date:** 17 July 2005
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## The Book of Signs Comes to a Close

We've been reading through John for six months now, and tonight marks the ending of what's called the **Book of Signs**—chapters 1-12, where John records Jesus' miracles and signs designed to demonstrate that he is indeed the Christ, the king Israel had been waiting for.

These signs weren't mere displays of power. The feeding of the five thousand shows Jesus as the better Moses, offering better bread. The resurrection of Lazarus points not just to supernatural power, but to the Christ who will usher in the age of resurrection. Each sign carries theological weight, revealing who Jesus is to those with eyes to see.

The critical question John leaves us with is this: **Have these signs and miracles actually worked?** Have they led people to believe? As we look at chapter 12, we'll see the varied—and often disappointing—responses to Jesus' hour of glorification.

---

## Just Another Passover (11:55ff)

Chapter 12 opens with the third and final Passover mentioned in John's Gospel. Two years have passed since Jesus first walked freely in Jerusalem at the Passover in chapter 2. Now, everything has changed. There is a warrant for his arrest.

As always, Jerusalem fills with pilgrims from around the world coming to purify themselves and atone for their sins. They've heard the latest news: a man who can raise the dead. They've come not just for the feast, but hoping to catch a glimpse of this miracle-worker, Jesus.

Six days before Passover, Jesus emerges from hiding and returns to Bethany, just three kilometers from Jerusalem. There, Lazarus hosts a dinner in Jesus' honor—the least he can offer the one who raised him from the dead. Martha serves while Mary sits at Jesus' feet, anointing them with expensive perfume, wiping them with her own hair.

Judas objects. What a waste of money! Why not sell this perfume and give the proceeds to the poor?

Jesus' response is sharp and revealing.

### Preparation for the Hour (v.7-8)

> Jesus said, "Leave her alone, so that she may keep it for the day of my burial. The poor you always have with you, but you do not always have me."

Jesus is preparing for death, and Mary—whether consciously or not—is preparing him for his burial. In Jewish tradition, spices and perfumes were applied to the body before wrapping it in linen. Mary has sacrificed great wealth in her gratitude for her brother's resurrection, anointing Jesus' travel-weary feet as an act of devotion.

But perhaps she senses something darker. The authorities want Jesus dead. Knowing they have a warrant for his arrest, she may realize this could be the last time she can show him honor.

Judas, meanwhile, can only think of money. He misses the point entirely. Jesus reminds him: the poor will always be here. There will always be opportunities to help them. But Jesus will not always be present. The countdown to his death has begun.

The danger is real. In Jerusalem, large crowds gather to see Jesus—so large that the authorities fear an uprising. They don't just want Jesus dead; they want to kill Lazarus too. A living, breathing resurrection is inconvenient evidence. It's like a spy thriller: eliminate the target, eliminate the witnesses, destroy all evidence.

## The Hour Has Come (v.23)

> And Jesus answered them, "The hour has come for the Son of Man to be glorified."

For the entire first half of John's Gospel, there's been a constant refrain: **the hour has not yet come.**

- At the wedding in Cana (2:4), Jesus tells his mother, "My hour has not yet come."
- To the Samaritan woman (4:21-24), he speaks of an hour coming when worship will transcend geography.
- Multiple times (7:30, 8:20), John notes that authorities could not arrest Jesus because "his hour had not yet come."

But now the situation changes. The hour arrives—not because of the authorities, not because of popular pressure, but because of something unexpected: **the arrival of Greeks.**

### The Trigger: The Greeks (v.20-22)

Among the Passover pilgrims were some Greeks—Gentiles, not ethnic Greeks necessarily, but people from the pagan world coming to worship at the Jewish feast. They were likely converts or God-fearers, circumcised and observant of Jewish practice. Yet they couldn't enter the temple proper; they were confined to the Court of the Gentiles. Under Judaism, salvation was necessarily limited to Israel.

But now the Gentiles are here, asking to see Jesus.

This moment signals the fulfillment of Jesus' mission. He came to save the whole world (John 3:16) without ethnic distinction. His work concerns all humanity—those lost and in rebellion against God. And as the chief priests bitterly note, "the whole world has gone after him."

John's irony is delicious: the authorities meant it as hyperbole, a complaint about the crowds in Jerusalem. But John knows it's literally true. The hour has come because the world—Jew and Gentile alike—is finally ready to see Jesus for who he is.

## Glorification Through Death

Here we encounter a profound paradox. Jesus announces that the hour has come **for the Son of Man to be glorified.** To be glorified means to be exalted, promoted, raised up—given power, honor, and praise.

Yet Jesus is being prepared for death. How can these things possibly reconcile?

### The Triumphal Entry (v.12-15)

On the surface, there is glory. As Jesus approaches Jerusalem, crowds take palm fronds and rush out to meet him, crying "Hosanna! Blessed is he who comes in the name of the Lord, even the king of Israel!"

This is the fulfillment of Zechariah's prophecy: the king returning to his city in triumph. It's like a conquering general returning home through a ticker-tape parade, an Olympic champion receiving the keys to the city, crowds on both sides cheering and waving flags.

Yet look closer. Jesus comes on a donkey, not a warhorse. He comes as a servant, not a warrior. This is a humble entry, not a glorious one. The one being hailed as king arrives as one prepared for burial.

How do we reconcile these contradictions?

### The Answer: Glory Via Death (v.24-26)

> If a grain of wheat falls to the ground and dies, it remains only a single seed. But if it dies, it produces many seeds.

Jesus uses the image of a seed. A farmer doesn't store seeds safely away in a storeroom where they'll remain forever unchanged. Instead, he plants the seed in moist soil where it dies—it breaks apart and degrades. But that very death, that breaking apart, is what allows new life to emerge. A shoot springs forth, becomes a plant, produces many fruits and many more seeds.

This is the pattern: **life comes through death. Glory comes through death.**

Jesus will indeed be exalted and raised up—but when? When he is lifted up on the cross for all the world to see (v.32-33). This is the death that awaits him. He must die so that others might live. Through his death, Jesus claims all glory.

Listen to the Father's response to Jesus' acceptance of this hour: his voice thunders from heaven in approval (v.27-29). This is a foretaste of the glory that comes from God the Father.

But this has one crucial implication for those who follow Jesus.

### The Application: Death Now, Glory Later (v.25-26)

> Whoever loves his life loses it, and whoever hates his life in this world will keep it for eternal life. If anyone serves me, he must follow me; and where I am, there will my servant be also. If anyone serves me, the Father will honor him.

Those who follow Jesus must follow the same path. Where the master goes, there goes the servant. The choice before us is simple but profound: **When do you want your glory—now or later?**

**Glory now** looks appealing. Christian life is hard. It's difficult to persevere through suffering without visible success. So we're tempted to look for glory now—signs of power and prestige that prove Christianity works.

We want our faith to produce successful Christian businesspeople, political influence, material wealth. We want to point to impressive ministries and say, "Look, this is what faith produces." We're drawn to Christian conventions with beautiful speakers, magazines full of success stories and answered prayers, churches announcing numerical growth.

We want it to be cool to be Christian, culturally acceptable, socially advantageous.

But this is hollow glory—and often not even real. It doesn't reflect the actual difficulty of faithful Christian life.

**Glory later**, by contrast, looks like this: **death now.**

Not martyrdom in the violent sense, but long, slow dying. Listen to Peter:

> The God of all grace, who called you to his eternal glory in Christ, after you have suffered a little while, will himself restore you and make you strong, firm and steadfast.

What does this slow death look like today? It's the person who quietly keeps trying to bring friends to faith, year after year. It's the person who prepares a special meal for the Bible study group with no expectation of acknowledgment. It's the background figure who compiles the song sheets—nothing flashy, but the work gets done. It's the one who stays after church on a cold night when everyone else has left, washing the dishes.

This is what slow death looks like: giving up your life gradually for the sake of others.

Now, this doesn't mean we can't enjoy the good gifts God gives—beauty, popularity, security, personality. If you're naturally likeable, that's wonderful. Don't wear sackcloth as penance for being blessed. But here's the key: **use those gifts for others.**

If people naturally like you and follow you, don't just retreat into your comfortable circle. After talks and meals, don't only hang with your gang. Talk to the people sitting alone—the ones who gave their week to attend an event where they know no one. Invite them along to your next gathering. Your social ease becomes a tool for welcoming the lonely.

The principle applies everywhere: glory only comes via death. For Jesus, and for his followers.

## Three Responses to the Hour

By the end of the Book of Signs, John makes clear that the overwhelming response to Jesus was **unbelief.** Even his own disciples don't understand (v.16). But the crowd's reactions break down into three categories.

### A. Those Who See but Do Not Believe (v.37)

> Though he had done so many signs before them, they still did not believe in him.

Jesus performed miracle after miracle—yet they refused to believe. They saw with their own eyes but failed to see with the eyes of faith. This is a recurring theme in John.

In chapter 2, many believed when they saw Jesus' miraculous signs, yet Jesus didn't entrust himself to them—he knew what was in the human heart.

In chapter 6, crowds believed after the feeding miracle, but the moment Jesus began teaching, they said, "This is a hard saying; who can listen to it?" and walked away.

Even in chapter 12, crowds come because they witnessed Lazarus' resurrection, but when Jesus starts talking about his death, they object: "The Christ isn't supposed to die!" (v.34)

The pattern is clear: **signs create a shallow belief that evaporates when Jesus speaks the hard truth.**

There's a lesson in this for us. Many today say, "If only God would perform a miracle, I'd believe." But God has performed miracles throughout history, and it didn't automatically produce faith. And if you're looking for signs, the real problem becomes apparent when you actually get them—because then the words are what trip you up.

Think of it this way: imagine a large road sign pointing toward Carlingford Village. What's its purpose? Not to be admired for its glazing or pole, but to direct you to where you need to go. In the same way, Jesus' miracles were meant to point to something beyond themselves—to his authority, his identity, his mission.

The Pharisees, actually, believed in the signs. What they questioned was Jesus' authority and the manner of his claims. Jesus nearly had them—if only he would stop talking, they might have stayed on board! But he didn't. The signs brought them close, but the words revealed what was really in their hearts.

**The application is simple: don't look for signs. Listen to the words.**

### B. Those Who Believe but Fear (v.42-43)

> Nevertheless, many (even of the authorities) believed in him, but for fear of the Pharisees they did not confess it, so that they would not be put out of the synagogue; for they loved the glory that comes from man more than the glory that comes from God.

This second group actually believes. They accept Jesus' words. But they keep silent. They don't openly declare their faith.

Why? Fear. Fear of the Pharisees, who held power and privilege. Being in with the ruling class meant cushy positions and social advantage. Fear of being cast out of the synagogue meant losing community, facing shame and ostracism—and in a culture where community is everything, this was devastating. The synagogue wasn't just a place of worship; it was the basis of identity and belonging. To be cut off from it was to be cut off from life itself.

This fear is genuine, but John diagnoses the real problem: **they loved the praise of men more than the praise of God.** They wanted to maintain their standing with the powers that be rather than risk everything for truth.

John's judgment is blunt but fair. What they really loved was the glory—the honor, status, and acceptance—that comes from human approval.

**The application hits close to home.** How often do we stay silent when we should speak? I know the feeling well. Recently, I was at afternoon tea with friends from church. It would have been natural and appropriate to suggest we pray before leaving. But I said nothing. Why? Fear of being the intense one, the spiritualizer, the party pooper. Fear that they'd think me weird and not want to spend time with me anymore.

But what about our calling to exhort and rebuke one another in love? Not going around confronting everyone, but speaking truth to those we have genuine relationships with—asking whether their outside-church lives match their inside-church commitments? This is hard because we fear being labeled puritans. We fear losing friendships. We fear that asking tough questions will make time together feel too intense.

Peer pressure doesn't disappear after adolescence; we just disguise it better. The pressure to seek human glory—the approval and praise of others—rather than God's approval is constant. We need special encouragement, especially those of us who are naturally liked or respected. If you have social influence, you can stretch further than most. People listen to you. Your presence draws others. You have the opportunity to make a social difference. But don't use that influence only to stay comfortable with your own crowd.

Yet Jesus offers comfort: "Fear not, the King is here. Forgiveness is yours."

### C. Those Who Hear and Believe (v.44-50)

Strikingly, John records no actual third group responding faithfully in this scene. By the end of the Book of Signs, unbelief dominates. Even the disciples don't fully understand.

Instead, Jesus describes the people he expects and welcomes—the third category that *should* exist.

> Whoever believes in me, believes not in me but in him who sent me. And whoever sees me sees him who sent me. I have come into the world as light, so that whoever believes in me may not remain in darkness.

> If anyone hears my words and does not keep them, I do not judge him; for I did not come to judge the world but to save the world. The one who rejects me and does not receive my words has a judge; the word that I have spoken will judge him on the last day.

> I have not spoken on my own authority, but the Father who sent me has himself given me a commandment—what to say and what to speak. And I know that his commandment is eternal life. What I say therefore, I say as the Father has told me.

Who truly believes? **Those who hear the words and keep them.** Those who recognize that Jesus speaks with the authority of God the Father. Those who understand that his words carry eternal life itself.

This is what belief means in John: not just intellectual assent to who Jesus is, but receiving his words as God's authoritative speech. The words are sufficient. You don't need signs once you have the words.

In John 20:29, Jesus says, "Blessed are those who have not seen and yet believe"—a statement of approval for those who come after the resurrection, who never witnessed the signs firsthand. It's like knowing the way to Carlingford Village—once you know where it is, you don't need the road sign anymore.

This is consistent throughout John. The Gospel's summary statement (20:31) says: "These are written so that you may believe that Jesus is the Christ, the Son of God, and that by believing you may have life in his name." The words—from the Father to the Son to the disciples to us—carry that life-giving power.

The paradigm for belief is **hearing, not sight.** (This is easier for those of us who can read—we have the advantage of approaching Scripture as literature, carefully and thoroughly, not jumping around like reading a mystery novel. We can read the Gospel accounts the way they deserve to be read, absorbing them deeply.)

Barthimaeus the blind man believed through hearing (chapter 9). Those who truly see are those who hear. At the end of the Book of Signs, the emphasis is clear: the signs are pointers, nothing more. They're directions to truth from heaven. But **the words are where decision happens.** Turn from signs to words. If you believe the words, the signs become secondary.

**The application is direct: trust the words, not the signs. Hear, don't demand to see.**

Since the signs point to Jesus' words, listen to those words carefully. Read them regularly. Read them the way you'd read literature—not skimming, not jumping around, but absorbing them fully. Eat up his words. Let them nourish you.

The warning that follows is sobering. These words have been written down. They will judge us in the end. It's like a road sign pointing left—if you turn right instead, you'll crash into the Bunnings warehouse. On the last day, there's no point complaining. The words speak for themselves.

## Looking Forward to the Final Passover

We are heading toward not just another Passover, but the final Passover. In Jesus, the prophecies of a saving king come true.

Notice how John's pace changes dramatically. Everything from chapters 1-12 spans nearly two years. But what comes next—chapters 13-21, Jesus' final days—will compress those events into less than two weeks. Time slows down. Jesus steps away from public ministry and spends intense, private time with his disciples. There is so much to teach them, so little time left.

The hour has come. Death and suffering loom ahead. Yet paradoxically, this is the Book of Glory.

Through this final Passover, through death and suffering, salvation opens to all. Jew or Greek or Asian—no distinction remains. We don't need to exchange currency to believe in God. The barrier between insider and outsider collapses at the cross.

**Don't be like the crowds of old who saw but refused to believe.** Don't demand signs before you'll commit yourself. Instead, hear these words—the words of God the Father spoken through the Son, preserved for us in Scripture—and **believe.**
