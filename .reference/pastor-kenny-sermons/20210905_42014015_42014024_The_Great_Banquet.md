## A Good Party

Think about the best party you've ever been to—not a dance party or a LAN party, but one where real conversations happened. You made genuine friends, discussed interesting things, and didn't want to leave because of the company. And importantly, there was abundant, quality food.

Perhaps it was a birthday party. I remember mine at twelve—my mum cooked noodles, fried chicken, and grass jelly drink, then we played with toys with friends. Or a church BBQ, wasting away a lazy afternoon with close friends. Maybe a fancy restaurant lunch downtown, or a wedding feast where you celebrated your best friend, dressed up, remembering the past while creating new memories. Or simply husband and wife sharing cheese together. Or Lunar New Year with Malaysian relatives.

The Bible takes food and fellowship seriously. The words "feasting" and "banqueting" appear about 180 times throughout Scripture. The Bible is about partying—celebrating together.

## A Great Banquet

> Jesus replied: "A certain man was preparing a great banquet and invited many guests. At the time of the banquet he sent his servant to tell those who had been invited, 'Come, for everything is now ready.'" (Luke 14:16-17)

This man is wealthy, with servants attending him. He's throwing a mega-party—the Greek word for "great" is *mega*. In those times, a banquet was a lavish event with rack of lamb and other fine meats, spices, sauces, fine bread. Wine. Reclining. Servants serving. Think of Heston Blumenthal preparing ancient recipes with meticulous detail. Days of preparation—every servant working, the master checking every detail, cutlery and bowls polished and gleaming, tablecloths, enough food and drink, pillows fluffed for reclining.

When everything is ready, the servant announces it. In that culture, there were no clocks, so the servant would come personally, probably escorting guests door to door. It's like a good date—the girl gets picked up for the meal.

**The Key Ingredients for a Good Party: Generous Food and People**

Look at what Jesus taught just before this parable:

> When you give a luncheon or dinner, do not invite your friends, your brothers or relatives, or your rich neighbors; if you do, they may invite you back and so you will be repaid. But when you give a banquet, invite the poor, the crippled, the lame, the blind, and you will be blessed. Although they cannot repay you, you will be repaid at the resurrection of the righteous. (Luke 14:12-14)

The Bible teaches us to be hospitable:

> Share with God's people who are in need. Practice hospitality. (Romans 12:13)
>
> Above all, love each other deeply, because love covers over a multitude of sins. Offer hospitality to one another without grumbling. (1 Peter 4:8-9)
>
> Keep on loving each other as brothers. Do not forget to entertain strangers, for by so doing some people have entertained angels without knowing it. (Hebrews 13:1-2)

This is so important it's a requirement for church leaders:

> The overseer must be above reproach, hospitable, able to teach. (1 Timothy 3:2)

**How We Fall Short**

Yet our modern parties often look nothing like this. We are not generous with food. It costs to feed people, so we're tempted to be stingy. We don't shout people. We ask them to bring their own meat. We invite people to pay for themselves—and sometimes even pay for us. At weddings, we skip the reception meal entirely, spending money on photos and exotic honeymoons instead, leaving guests hungry and thirsty.

I wonder if we could be different. What if we ran parties the old way—the family cooks something simple and everyone shares in the celebration? Perhaps as the host I provide the meat, and others help with salads and drinks. Or if it's too expensive, skip the party and just have a simple gathering with cake. The birthday isn't about you—it's your opportunity to serve others.

When you next have a party, be generous not just with food but with your guest list. Invite people who can't repay you. Invite the unpopular, those who'd never normally get invited. At your wedding, fight for a table or two for recent acquaintances. At a twenty-first, save spots for future friends. 

I think church should be like this—a party with a generous menu and a generous guest list. To newcomers: you've come to the right place; we hope you'll have a nice time. To regulars: welcome the stranger, the new person. But something sad happens.

## The Excuses

> They all alike began to make excuses. The first said, 'I have just bought a field, and I must go and see it. Please excuse me.' (Luke 14:18-19)

This is lame. Whether in the Middle East or Australia, no one buys a field without examining it thoroughly first. You'd know about the springs, wells, stone walls, trees, and paths long before purchasing. And even if true, go tomorrow—the land isn't going anywhere. You've already bought it; it's too late to inspect anyway.

> Another said, 'I have just bought five yoke of oxen, and I'm on my way to try them out. Please excuse me.' (Luke 14:19)

Equally lame. Yes, oxen needed to be tested to see if they'd work together as a team—like a test drive before purchase. But you already bought them. This excuse, like the first, is insulting.

> Still another said, 'I just got married, so I can't come.' (Luke 14:20)

If he married that very day, in a tightly-knit Middle Eastern community, everyone would know about the wedding. The master would have been invited. Meals would've been prepared beforehand. Unlikely no one knew in advance. More probably, this newly-wed just wants to stay home. Perhaps he'd rather stay home and watch TV with his wife, eating takeaway pizza.

All lame excuses—simply rude and disrespectful.

**Understanding the Offense**

In that culture, when giving a dinner, you invited a set number. Those accepting became counted. You prepared the meal accordingly. Animals were slaughtered. In the absence of refrigeration, food would spoil. No refunds, no takeaway boxes. The invited guests were duty-bound to attend. These men disrespected the master.

**The Modern Parallel**

You organize a fixed seating dinner—a wedding or twenty-first. You're charged a small fortune per seat because you're trying to be generous. Then someone last minute says they can't come. An emergency is fine, but a dumb excuse? I've organized many church events and heard every excuse in the book: "I slept in" (the party's in the evening). "I forgot to do my homework" (are you still in primary school?). "My relatives are over." "It's too hot." "It's too cold." My favorite: "I thought it was cancelled."

My pet peeve is the Facebook "maybe" button. Do you know what that means? "Yes, unless something better comes along." What does that tell the host? "I'll come if nothing better appears, but I reserve the right to cancel if I find a better friend." That's saying to the host: you've planned ahead, incurred all the costs, and I might dismiss you if I find someone better. Would the host ever do that to you? Say yes, keep your night free, then cancel because a better friend appeared?

Look, some parties don't require RSVPs—just whoever can make it. But when you're invited and your response matters, it's not just about the food. It's about relationship. Dumping the party isn't dumping food; it's dumping your friends because someone "better" came up.

Church and small groups are like a small-scale party—some food and fellowship with friends. Don't dump your friends. If earthly parties matter this much, what about if God holds a party? That's what Jesus is talking about here—something far more important than a birthday party.

## The Kingdom of Heaven

> Blessed is the man who will eat at the feast in the kingdom of God. (Luke 14:15)

The kingdom of God is heaven. Heaven has many images—a great city, a place of friendship, where God dwells, where many peoples gather. But another picture is a feast. Isaiah, written 500 years before Jesus, describes it:

> On this mountain the Lord Almighty will prepare a feast of rich food for all peoples, a banquet of aged wine—the best of meats and the finest of wines. On this mountain he will destroy the shroud that enfolds all peoples, the sheet that covers all nations. He will swallow up death forever. The Sovereign Lord will wipe away the tears from all faces; he will remove the disgrace of his people from all the earth. The Lord has spoken. (Isaiah 25:6-8)

When Isaiah wrote, Israel was in war—losing. Being invaded. Isaiah wrote to a hungry, thirsty people in the midst of famine. The feast meant more than just food and people. It symbolized peace—fighting was over. A meal between enemies. It promised security and an end to hunger, to sadness and despair, to estrangement. Particularly, it predicted the end of war with God.

That war began long ago when Adam and Eve ate what they shouldn't have—the first meal that divided us from God. We've all done this. In our meals, by our lack of generosity, our aggression, our rudeness and unfaithfulness. Violent words over the dinner table making mothers cry. Bitter words. Lack of love for each other. Eating without thankfulness, as if food sustains us rather than God. Lack of gratitude to God.

But it was another meal that saved us. When Jesus came, he ate—not with the popular, powerful, and rich, but with sinners and the outcast. He forgave them. He accepted them. At his last meal before death—the Passover—he told his disciples his body would be broken like bread, his blood poured out like wine. Jesus gave his life to save strangers, to bring us back to God.

So the promise is this great banquet in the future—the best party of the universe.

**Generous Food**

Isaiah 55 speaks of food that satisfies, that costs no money. No fast food. No junk food. Eaten with a clear conscience. Whatever food you think is good, heaven is better.

**Generous Guest List**

Not just food but people—love, no agenda. The sense of contentment, surrounded by friends. A sense of being together, all the time.

**God Will Be There**

The symbol of peace between us and God. An end to bushfires, to arsonists, to limited good intentions that end in disaster. An end to death. We get to eat with God.

## The Judgment

> I tell you, not one of those men who were invited will get a taste of my banquet. (Luke 14:24)

The master's anger is at those invited who wouldn't come. He gives them what they want—to stay out of heaven. This happened historically with the ancient Jews who rejected Jesus.

**If You're Not a Christian, What Excuses Remain?**

Science and Christianity? False debate. Christianity and science are friends. The war is between scientism and faith.

Miracles? If your science is limited to high school, okay. But go further—high school science breaks down. There's no such thing as the scientific method guaranteeing truth by repetition. Quantum physics shows that at the sub-particle level, miracles happen constantly. You can't locate an electron; it disappears and reappears. Schrödinger's Cat. Einstein called it "spooky action at a distance."

Family disapproval? I'm not saying rebel if you're under eighteen and dependent on them. But parents disapprove of many things we still decide to do if they matter enough. You'd find a way if you met the girl of your dreams.

Not 100% sure? We don't make decisions on absolute certainty. We decide on probability. This bus probably won't crash. This driver probably isn't drunk. This meal probably isn't poisoned. We trust with reasonable probability. Is it more probable than not that Jesus is who he says he is?

Too many changes required? Commitment matters for good, true things.

Still trying to find out more? That's valid. But are you actually trying?

To find out more: come to church. Have an honest conversation with a Christian. Tell a friend who brought you, one of the leaders, or myself. We'll show you the next steps.

## God's Generous Response

> The servant came back and reported this to his master. Then the owner of the house became angry and ordered his servant, 'Go out quickly into the streets and alleys of the town and bring in the poor, the crippled, the blind and the lame.' The servant said, 'Sir, what you ordered has been done, but there is still room.' Then the master told his servant, 'Go out to the roads and country lanes and make them come in, so that my house will be full.' (Luke 14:21-23)

The invited guests refuse to come. What does the host do? He can't have a feast without guests. So he invites the unworthy, the poor, crippled, blind, and lame—the undesirables. Those not normally invited to banquets. Those who couldn't test oxen in fields. Those who didn't normally marry. But there's still room, so he brings in more.

Friends, God invites us to heaven. To eat with God. Being a Christian is an invitation to a party.

If you're a Christian, be joyful that we're going to this banquet.

If you're not yet a Christian, we don't want you to miss out. Give it a go and say yes. Don't just go home without telling anyone. Tell the friend who brought you, one of the leaders, or myself, and we'll show you the next steps.
