# 1 Samuel 16:1-23

**Title:** The Spirit on David
**Date:** 4 June 2017
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## An Unlikely Leader

Queen Elizabeth was not born to be queen. When her grandfather King George V died in 1936, her uncle Edward VIII assumed the throne. But Edward VIII chose love over duty—he abdicated to marry Wallis Simpson, an American divorcee, because the rules forbade a divorced consort. As a result, Elizabeth's father George VI took the throne, and when he died, she became queen. She was not meant to be queen.

Today we encounter another unlikely leader: a young shepherd boy named David.

## The Spirit Comes Upon David (1-13)

The Lord sent Samuel to Jesse of Bethlehem with a specific mission: "I have chosen one of his sons to be king." This marked the beginning of the end for Saul's reign. The Lord had already rejected him as king over Israel, and Samuel grieved this loss. Yet Samuel feared for his own life—if Saul learned of this anointing mission, he would kill the prophet.

The Lord gave Samuel a cover story: go to Bethlehem and sacrifice to the Lord. Invite Jesse and his sons to the sacrifice. "I will show you what to do. You are to anoint for me the one I indicate."

When Samuel arrived in Bethlehem, the elders trembled at his presence. But once the sacrifice began, Samuel's eyes fell upon Eliab, Jesse's firstborn. Samuel thought surely this was the Lord's anointed—Eliab was tall, impressive, and strong like a warrior. He fit every outward criterion for kingship.

But God interrupted Samuel's judgment: "Do not consider his appearance or his height, for I have rejected him. The Lord does not look at the things people look at. People look at the outward appearance, but the Lord looks at the heart."

Samuel had not learned his lesson from Saul. He was still measuring by height and appearance—the very things that had led Israel astray before.

One by one, Jesse presented his sons to Samuel: Abinadab, then Shammah, then five more. But to each one, Samuel heard the same word: "The Lord has not chosen this one." Finally Samuel asked Jesse, "Are these all the sons you have?"

Jesse answered, "There is still the youngest. He is tending the sheep."

"Send for him," Samuel said. "We will not sit down until he arrives."

When David was brought in, the text describes him in striking detail: "He was ruddy, glowing with health, with a fine appearance and handsome features." At first this seems contradictory—the Lord just said He doesn't look at appearance, yet immediately we're given David's physical description. The Hebrew reveals something more nuanced. "Ruddy" literally means red like Edom—David had probably been exerting himself, flushed from rushing back from the sheep. "Fine appearance" speaks of beautiful eyes. "Handsome features" could mean good vision or keen sight.

David emerges in this passage not as the mighty warrior we expect, but as something entirely different: a ruddy-faced boy with bright eyes. He's probably the type who would start the chess club and join the school band, not captain the sports team. An unlikely choice. A nerd, perhaps.

Yet this is precisely God's point. He chooses based not on outward strength or impressive stature, but on the heart—according to His own purposes and intentions. The Hebrew of verse 7 is clear: "For the Lord sees not as man sees: man looks according to the eyes, but the Lord according to the heart."

When Scripture says God chooses someone "after His own heart," it doesn't mean they have a naturally good heart. David's later life reveals this truth starkly—he would commit adultery, murder, theft, and corruption. Rather, "according to his heart" means according to God's own will, purposes, and intentions. God will shape this unlikely nerd into a warrior and a king.

Then the Lord said to Samuel: "Rise and anoint him; this is the one."

Samuel took his horn of oil and anointed David in the presence of his brothers. And from that moment, "the Spirit of the Lord came powerfully upon David."

Later, when David entered Saul's service, the servants would describe him in transformed terms: "I have seen a son of Jesse of Bethlehem who knows how to play the lyre. He is a brave man and a warrior. He speaks well and is a fine-looking man. And the Lord is with him." The Spirit had changed everything.

### Application for Us

God's perspective spans eternity. He showed the shepherds of Bethlehem, nearly a thousand years after David's anointing, His eternal viewpoint. They came to see a tiny, weak, helpless baby born in obscurity. Yet they departed knowing the truth the angels proclaimed: "Glory to God in the highest, and on earth peace among those with whom he is pleased." They saw the King not according to human eyes, but as God sees—Jesus the King, the Savior, the Messiah.

And God will do the same with you. Before the creation of the world, He chose us in Christ to be holy and blameless in His sight. When God picked you, Christian, He did not look at your heart—at all the weakness, failure, and confusion within. Instead, in Christ, you were chosen according to His predestined plan and His purpose. You are marked with the seal of the Holy Spirit as a deposit guaranteeing your inheritance.

Your life has a purpose: "For we are God's handiwork, created in Christ Jesus to do good works, which God prepared in advance for us to do."

What is the purpose of your life? To do the good works He prepared for you in advance. In you, God is going to transform this world and the lives within it. You are going to be part of God's plan to turn this place around.

You think you're weak. You think you're poor. You think you have no self-control. You think you're inadequate. You think you have no power. You think you're just a nerd.

Stop asking, "What can I do?" That's the wrong question. Ask instead: "What can God do through me?"

## The Spirit Departs from Saul (14-23)

While the Spirit of the Lord came upon David, something terrible happened to Saul. "Now the Spirit of the Lord had departed from Saul, and an evil spirit from the Lord tormented him."

Saul's attendants recognized his torment and offered a remedy: "Let our lord command his servants to search for someone who can play the lyre. He will play when the evil spirit from God comes on you, and you will feel better."

Saul agreed, and his servants found David—the very young man God had chosen to replace him. They brought David to Saul, and Saul was delighted. David became his armor-bearer, and Saul requested that Jesse allow David to remain in his service permanently.

Whenever the evil spirit tormented Saul, David would take up his lyre and play. Relief would come to Saul; he would feel better, and the evil spirit would depart. The word for relief here means to feel good (tov), and the spirit would turn aside (sur) from him.

### Understanding God Sending Evil Spirits

The text speaks of God sending evil spirits. This language appears throughout Scripture: the spirit of falsehood in 1 Kings 22, the spirit of jealousy in Numbers 5:14, the evil spirit between Abimelech and the Shechemites in Judges 9:23-24, and similar language in 2 Kings 19:7, Isaiah 9:13-14, Isaiah 29:9-10, and Hosea 4:12 and 5:4.

This raises an objection: How can God, who is holy and good, send evil? The answer lies in God's eternal nature. God predestines all things and sends both good and evil without becoming evil Himself. He is the only God—there is no cosmic dualism. In the realm of eternity, God predestines all things because He is sovereign. Yet He remains absolutely good. When He permits evil or uses it for His purposes, it is always for a righteous end. God can accomplish good through what would be sin for us to do intentionally. This is the prerogative of the Almighty alone.

### What God Values: Justice and Mercy

Throughout Scripture, God reveals what truly matters to Him. Through the prophet Amos, He cries out against empty religious performance:

> "I hate, I despise your feasts, and I take no delight in your solemn assemblies. Even though you offer me your burnt offerings and grain offerings, I will not accept them. Take away from me the noise of your songs; to the melody of your harps I will not listen. But let justice roll down like waters, and righteousness like an ever-flowing stream."

In the Psalms, David himself understood this. His heart's desire was not ceremonial correctness but obedience rooted in love:

> "In sacrifice and offering you have not delighted, but you have given me an open ear. I have come to do your will, O God; your law is within my heart."

And in his deepest repentance, David confessed:

> "For you will not delight in sacrifice, or I would give it. The sacrifices of God are a broken spirit; a broken and contrite heart, O God, you will not despise."

Jesus Himself emphasized this principle repeatedly. "Go and learn what this means," He told the Pharisees. "I desire mercy, and not sacrifice." When the Pharisees condemned innocent disciples for gathering grain on the Sabbath, He repeated the same truth: "If you had known what this means, 'I desire mercy, and not sacrifice,' you would not have condemned the guiltless."

The weightier matters of God's law are justice, mercy, and faithfulness—not the minutiae of ritual observance. These are what truly matter to the heart of God.
