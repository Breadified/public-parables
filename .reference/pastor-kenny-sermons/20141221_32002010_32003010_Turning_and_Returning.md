## Introduction: A Week of Changed Minds

It's been a significant week in Australia. In Cairns, we heard of a tragedy—the death of eight children. In Sydney, many witnessed a hostage crisis and loss of life. These events remind us how fleeting life truly is and how dramatically circumstances can change in an instant.

One story stands out: David Johnson, an employee at the Lindt café, was scheduled to work that fateful Monday. But because he'd done extensive overtime the previous weekend, his boss gave him the day off. A simple change of mind—a rescheduled shift—saved his life.

Today we'll explore another story where changing one's mind brings salvation. We'll look at three remarkable turnarounds: Jonah repents, the Ninevites repent, and God repents.

## Jonah's Reluctant Obedience (2:10-3:4)

### The Prophet Who Ran

We've been reading through Jonah these past weeks. God commanded Jonah to preach repentance to Nineveh, but Jonah fled. Last chapter, he prayed to God for mercy while trapped in the fish's belly, and God answered:

> And the Lord commanded the fish, and it vomited Jonah onto dry land.

God repeats His command:

> Then the word of the Lord came to Jonah a second time: "Go to the great city of Nineveh and proclaim to it the message I give you." Jonah obeyed the word of the Lord and went to Nineveh. Now Nineveh was a very large city; it took three days to go through it.

### Understanding Nineveh's Greatness

The phrase "great city to God" (in the Septuagint and Jonah 4:11) reveals something important. Nineveh was indeed impressive—situated on a high mound near the Tigris River, with turreted walls, wide paved streets, gardens, parks, palaces, and temples. It was one of the wonders of the ancient world.

The "three days" likely didn't mean the city's actual width (no ancient city was 60 miles across), but rather that it would take three days to complete the prophetic work—one day visiting, one day in the markets, another meeting nobility.

### Jonah's Halfhearted Message

> Jonah began by going a day's journey into the city, proclaiming, "Forty more days and Nineveh will be overthrown."

This message is strikingly minimal—just five Hebrew words. There's no introduction like "Thus says the Lord," no offer of hope, no mention of Yahweh. The word "overthrown" echoes what God did to Sodom and Gomorrah, invoking divine judgment.

Yet something is deeply wrong here. Jonah was told to preach for three days' worth of work, but he only completed one day. He's procrastinating, dragging his feet. Notice the signs of his insincerity:

- In 1:9, he evaded the sailors' direct questions about responsibility
- He announced solutions without confessing his own evil
- Even his prayer of thanksgiving lacked genuine confession

Most tellingly, at the end of chapter 2, Jonah vowed to make sacrifices and visit the holy Temple in Jerusalem. He was planning to flee to Jerusalem—not stay and fulfill his mission in Nineveh. That's why God had to command him a second time.

### The Pattern of Procrastination

Jonah is the reluctant prophet, giving himself and Nineveh "forty more days"—time they don't actually have. We recognize this pattern in ourselves, don't we?

We procrastinate on studying, finding endless distractions—a quick game, a trip to the fridge, sharpening pencils—anything to delay the inevitable. We tell ourselves: "Forty more days. I'll do it later."

The same happens with household repairs: that leaky roof, the cluttered garage. Delay. Procrastinate. Forty days more.

**And just like Jonah, we procrastinate about telling others about Jesus.** We drag our feet as though it's the most difficult thing in the world.

### Why We Hesitate

Fear often paralyzes us—fear of rejection, fear of what others might do. But remember from Jonah 1: Yahweh is the God of land, sea, and heaven. Nothing is beyond His control or sight. Fear God, and there is no one else to fear.

Perhaps it's simpler than fear—pure procrastination and laziness. You've thought of friends you'd love to invite to church, but that New Year's resolution from last year has quietly vanished. A whole year has passed. "I've been busy," you tell yourself.

But we can procrastinate no longer. Tie up those loose ends. Now is the time—especially during the break coming up.

### Making Time for What Matters

Some will say: "But I'm busy over summer with holidays and parties I've already planned." We fill our schedules so completely that important things get crowded out. We add holiday after holiday, event after event, until nothing else fits.

Here's the truth: **holidays aren't breaks—they're another thing we add to our schedule.** We can't keep fitting things in. Something has to go. And too often, it's evangelism.

It takes time to tell people about Jesus. Real time. Genuine relationship-building. Three days' worth of work. Don't give up after one day.

It takes time to cultivate relationships, to build trust, to invite people thoughtfully. Your friends are busy too. If you struggle to prioritize church, how much harder must it be for them? They need advance notice, time to prepare. Thank you to those visiting with us today—thank you for carving time out of your busyness.

### Seizing the Moment

But friends, summer is the ideal time. You'll be meeting people—parties, dinners, catch-ups. This is a different crowd from work, where deeper conversations flow more naturally.

It's Christmas. The whole season is about Jesus—the very Christ in Christmas. Even secular celebrations like Carols in the Domain feature singing about Jesus. Nearly every shopping center displays a nativity scene. The culture itself will help you. This is the best time.

Bring up Jesus. Invite them to church. Invite them to Easyway—perhaps that January event.

The fate of a city hung in the balance. Will you procrastinate, or will you do the work of an evangelist?

## Nineveh's Genuine Repentance (3:5-9)

### A City Transformed

Despite Jonah's reluctance—and we certainly shouldn't emulate him—God used even his meager effort to accomplish His will.

> The Ninevites believed God. A fast was proclaimed, and all of them, from the greatest to the least, put on sackcloth.

Notice: they believed in God, not in Jonah.

Repentance means a change of mind—turning from one direction to go another. From not believing to believing. From not trusting to trusting.

### The King's Humility

When the message reached the king of Nineveh:

> He rose from his throne, took off his royal robes, covered himself with sackcloth and sat down in the dust.

The great king of this great city rose from his throne—symbolically relinquishing power—and recognized that God alone is truly king. He exchanged his finest robes for the coarsest sackcloth. He sat in dust.

The king's proclamation was absolute:

> Do not let people or animals, herds or flocks, taste anything; do not let them eat or drink. But let people and animals be covered with sackcloth. Let everyone call urgently on God. Let them give up their evil ways and their violence. Who knows? God may yet relent and with compassion turn from his fierce anger so that we will not perish.

### The Depth of True Repentance

In ancient times, fasting, sackcloth, and dust were the visible signs of genuine repentance—as seen in Nehemiah 9:1 and Job 2:12.

The Ninevites repented sincerely. Their actions weren't mere words or social media gestures. We live in an age of slacktivism—hashtag activism where commitment amounts to clicking a button or performing a stunt.

Real repentance demands years of consistency. True solutions require steady effort, not emotional roller coasters. The steady hand of transformation. The possibility of an entire city changing.

It has happened before. Whether Nineveh, Rome, London, or cities in Africa—kings and cities have moved from child sacrifice, debauchery, and pagan worship to follow the true and living God.

It can happen again.

## God's Compassionate Relenting (3:10)

### The Greatest Miracle

While it's remarkable that an entire city repented, **the greatest miracle is that God repented.**

> When God saw what they did and how they turned from their evil ways, he relented and did not bring on them the destruction he had threatened.

"Relented" uses the same word as the Ninevites' repentance—a turning from one action to another.

### God's Sovereignity and Compassion

God didn't have to do this. When a king meets a peasant on the road, who gives way?

Yet God has made this promise—recorded in Jeremiah 18:7-8:

> If at any time I announce that a nation or kingdom is to be uprooted, torn down and destroyed, and if that nation I warned repents of its evil, then I will relent and not inflict on it the disaster I had planned.

While God is sovereign—His will predestined, without change in His eternal nature—from our human perspective, God changes His mind. This is His gracious promise.

### Grace Greater Than Our Sin

Maybe you've done something you believe is unforgivable. No one could forgive you. You can't even forgive yourself. No god could forgive you.

But God promises to change His mind if you change yours.

He can do this—not because He is fickle, but because His one and only Son came into the world two thousand years ago and died for your sins. **That unforgivable sin? He has paid for it.** You can go free.

Repent and turn from your evil ways.

### More Certain Than the Ninevites

Unlike the Ninevites who could only hope—"Who knows?"—we have certainty:

> If you declare with your mouth, "Jesus is Lord," and believe in your heart that God raised him from the dead, you will be saved. (Romans 10:9)

The Ninevites repented in uncertainty. We repent knowing that Jesus has ensured God's forgiveness. We know that God will relent and with compassion turn from His fierce anger so that we will not perish.

## Returning Home

### The Hostages' Escape

The hostages at the café understood something crucial: staying meant death. The gunman had threatened something would happen the next day; he carried what appeared to be explosives. When an opportunity came—an unsupervised walk to the toilet—they noticed an emergency button. They pressed it, turned, and ran for their lives. They took the opportunity to escape and returned.

### Our Return to God

Friends, we have all walked far away from God. We've gotten ourselves into a mess. But we can change our mind and return. God promises to change His mind.

If you haven't returned to God, perhaps today is the day to do so. Life is fleeting. Make peace with your Maker before it's too late.
