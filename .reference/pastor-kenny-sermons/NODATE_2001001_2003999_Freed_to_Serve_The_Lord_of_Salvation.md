# Exodus 1-3
**Title:** Freed to Serve - The Lord of Salvation
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Part 1: A Progress Report (Exodus 1:6-7)

The book opens with a stocktake of the nation of God. What has happened to the promise God made to Abraham?

> These are the names of the sons of Israel who went to Egypt with Jacob, each with his family: Reuben, Simeon, Levi and Judah; Issachar, Zebulun and Benjamin; Dan and Naphtali; Gad and Asher. The descendants of Jacob numbered seventy in all; Joseph was already in Egypt. Now Joseph and all his brothers and all that generation died, but the Israelites were fruitful and multiplied greatly and became exceedingly numerous, so that the land was filled with them.

The promise of blessing is being fulfilled. Israel began with seventy people and has grown to fill the land. Notice the language used: "fruitful," "multiply," and "fill the earth"—the same words God spoke to Adam and Eve in Genesis. These words are repeated throughout the chapter, emphasizing the multiplication:

- Exodus 1:9 - "too numerous"
- Exodus 1:10 - "numerous"  
- Exodus 1:12 - "multiplied"
- Exodus 1:20 - "increased and became even more numerous"

Despite the Pharaoh's opposition—despite attempts to kill them—the people grew. Those who helped them were blessed. This is "Big Australia" in action: like feral rabbits, the nation multiplied.

### Israel's Definition of Blessing

Look at Psalm 128 and Psalm 127. Israel understood blessing in concrete, generational terms:

> Blessed are all who fear the Lord, who walk in his ways. You will eat the fruit of your labor; blessings and prosperity will be yours. Your wife will be like a fruitful vine within your house; your sons will be like olive shoots around your table.

> Like arrows in the hands of a warrior are sons born in one's youth. Blessed is the man whose quiver is full of them.

Children are a blessing. Sons and daughters are arrows in the hands of a warrior. The creation mandate to be fruitful and multiply still applies because marriage still applies. How many children should a family have? As many as possible—a full quiver. How many people should the church have? As many as possible.

## Part 2: Progress Stalled (Exodus 1:10, 22; 2:22)

Yet the chapter ends with an ominous threat: active killing, genocide. Three times, Pharaoh attempts to destroy the Hebrew boys. Three times, God's providence provides escape.

**First attempt:** The midwives. Pharaoh commands them to kill Hebrew boys at birth, but the midwives feared God and would not obey. They told Pharaoh that Hebrew women were vigorous and gave birth before the midwives arrived. God blessed the midwives for their faithfulness.

**Second attempt:** Pharaoh orders all Hebrew boys thrown into the Nile. But Moses' mother hides him for three months. When she can hide him no longer, she places him in a papyrus basket and sets it among the reeds. His sister watches from a distance.

> Then Pharaoh's daughter went down to the Nile to bathe, and her attendants were walking along the river bank. She saw the basket among the reeds and sent her slave girl to get it. She opened it and saw the baby. He was crying, and she felt sorry for him. "This is one of the Hebrew babies," she said.

The sister offers to find a Hebrew nurse, and Pharaoh's daughter agrees. Moses' own mother nurses him and is paid for it. When he is older, he becomes her son. She names him Moses, saying, "I drew him out of the water."

**Third attempt:** When Moses grows up, he sees an Egyptian beating a Hebrew. His heart stirs—he wants to be a deliverer. He kills the Egyptian and hides him in the sand. But the next day, he discovers his deed is known. When another Hebrew confronts him, asking who made him a judge, Moses realizes his approach won't work.

> When Pharaoh heard of this, he tried to kill Moses, but Moses fled from Pharaoh and went to live in Midian, where he sat down by a well.

Moses finds refuge with Jethro, a priest of Midian. He rescues Jethro's daughters from shepherds and waters their flock. Jethro welcomes him, gives him his daughter Zipporah in marriage, and Moses settles into a quiet life in Midian. He names his son Gershom: "I have become an alien in a foreign land."

Here is where progress truly stalls. No matter how many people Israel has, they remain slaves in a foreign land. The problem is not the people—the problem is the land. They are aliens, and in that foreign land, they will always be slaves.

But hope lies elsewhere.

## Part 3: The God Who Hears and Saves (Exodus 2:23-3:22)

Forty years pass. The Pharaoh who knew Joseph dies. The people's groan rises to heaven.

> During that long period, the king of Egypt died. The Israelites groaned in their slavery and cried out, and their cry for help because of their slavery went up to God. God heard their groaning and he remembered his covenant with Abraham, with Isaac and with Jacob. So God looked on the Israelites and was concerned about them.

God hears. This is consistent throughout the narrative:

- The princess hears the baby's crying and feels compassion; she draws Moses out of the water.
- Moses hears the cries of Reuel's daughters being driven away; he rescues them and draws water for their flock.
- God hears the cry of His people bound in slavery; He will draw them from the waters of judgment to a land flowing with milk and honey—the first time this description appears.

Moses is tending Jethro's flock when he comes to Horeb, the mountain of God. There the angel of the Lord appears to him in flames of fire within a bush that burns but is not consumed. Curious, Moses approaches to see this wonder.

> When the Lord saw that he had gone over to look, God called to him from within the bush, "Moses! Moses!" And Moses said, "Here I am."

God speaks. He calls Moses by name—twice—and Moses responds. This is the pattern of encounter with the true God.

> "Do not come any closer," God said. "Take off your sandals, for the place where you are standing is holy ground." Then he said, "I am the God of your father, the God of Abraham, the God of Isaac and the God of Jacob." At this, Moses hid his face, because he was afraid to look at God.

The Lord speaks of His nature: He is the God of the patriarchs. He is the God who made promises to Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob. And He now reveals His intention:

> "I have indeed seen the misery of my people in Egypt. I have heard them crying out because of their slave drivers, and I am concerned about their suffering. So I have come down to rescue them from the hand of the Egyptians and to bring them up out of that land into a good and spacious land, a land flowing with milk and honey."

God has seen. God has heard. God is concerned. God will rescue. This is the God of salvation.

### The Name of God

When God commissions Moses to go to Pharaoh and bring the people out, Moses asks the crucial question:

> "Suppose I go to the Israelites and say to them, 'The God of your fathers has sent me to you,' and they ask me, 'What is his name?' Then what shall I tell them?"

God answers:

> God said to Moses, "I AM WHO I AM. This is what you are to say to the Israelites: 'I AM has sent me to you.'"

In ancient cultures, names were deeply significant. They revealed identity and character. God's name—the Tetragrammaton, the sacred name revealed here as "I AM"—speaks of His eternal, self-existent nature. He is who He is. He depends on nothing and no one. His name is His deeds. That which is transcendent can be named only by what it does.

God tells Moses:

> "Say to the Israelites, 'The Lord, the God of your fathers—the God of Abraham, the God of Isaac and the God of Jacob—has sent me to you.' This is my name forever, the name by which I am to be remembered from generation to generation."

The God who hears will save. The God who saves is the Lord—Yahweh. This is who He is. This is how He makes Himself known: through action, through deliverance, through covenant faithfulness.

### A Prediction of Salvation Events

God unfolds His plan:

> "Go, assemble the elders of Israel and say to them, 'The Lord, the God of your fathers—the God of Abraham, Isaac and Jacob—appeared to me and said: I have watched over you and have seen what has been done to you in Egypt. And I have promised to bring you up out of your misery in Egypt into the land of the Canaanites, Hittites, Amorites, Perizzites, Hivites and Jebusites—a land flowing with milk and honey.'

> "The elders of Israel will listen to you. Then you and the elders are to go to the king of Egypt and say to him, 'The Lord, the God of the Hebrews, has met with us. Let us take a three-day journey into the desert to offer sacrifices to the Lord our God.' But I know that the king of Egypt will not let you go unless a mighty hand compels him. So I will stretch out my hand and strike the Egyptians with all the wonders that I will perform among them. After that, he will let you go.

> "And I will make the Egyptians favorably disposed toward this people, so that when you leave you will not go empty-handed. Every woman is to ask her neighbor and any woman living in her house for articles of silver and gold and for clothing, which you will put on your sons and daughters. And so you will plunder the Egyptians."

The pattern is clear: God will act with mighty works. Pharaoh will resist. God's hand will be stretched out in judgment. The people will be freed. And the nations will know that the Lord is God.

## Part 4: Moses the Chicken (Exodus 3:11-4:17)

But now we meet the deliverer himself—and he is full of excuses.

**Excuse #1:**

> But Moses said to God, "Who am I, that I should go to Pharaoh and bring the Israelites out of Egypt?"

God answers:

> And God said, "I will be with you. And this will be the sign to you that it is I who have sent you: When you have brought the people out of Egypt, you will worship God on this mountain."

Notice: God doesn't address Moses' self-doubt directly. Instead, He promises His presence. The sign won't be something immediate—the sign will be the accomplished fact of Israel's deliverance and worship on this very mountain.

**Excuse #2:**

> Moses said to God, "Suppose I go to the Israelites and say to them, 'The God of your fathers has sent me to you,' and they ask me, 'What is his name?' Then what shall I tell them?"

God provides the name—"I AM WHO I AM"—and the assurance that this God's faithfulness to Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob will be evident through what He does.

**Excuse #3:**

> Moses answered, "What if they do not believe me or listen to me and say, 'The Lord did not appear to you'?"

God gives him three miraculous signs: a staff that becomes a snake, a hand that becomes leprous and is healed, and water from the Nile that becomes blood. With these signs, the people will believe.

**Excuse #4:**

> Moses said to the Lord, "O Lord, I have never been eloquent, neither in the past nor since you have spoken to your servant. I am slow of speech and tongue."

God responds:

> The Lord said to him, "Who gave man his mouth? Who makes him deaf or mute? Who gives him sight or makes him blind? Is it not I, the Lord? Now go; I will help you speak and will teach you what to say."

**Excuse #5—The Heart of the Matter:**

> But Moses said, "O Lord, please send someone else to do it."

This is the final excuse, the real one. Moses doesn't want to go.

> Then the Lord's anger burned against Moses and he said, "What about your brother, Aaron the Levite? I know he can speak well. He is already on his way to meet you, and his heart will be glad when he sees you. You shall speak to him and put words in his mouth; I will help both of you speak and will teach you what to do. He will speak to the people for you, and it will be as if he were your mouth and as if you were God to him. But take this staff in your hand so you can perform miraculous signs with it."

God's anger is kindled. The excuses have worn thin. Yet in His mercy, He provides Aaron as Moses' spokesman. Moses is given the staff of power, the means by which God will work wonders.

### Implicit Excuses

Moses returns to Jethro:

> "Let me go back to my own people in Egypt to see if any of them are still alive."
> Jethro said, "Go, and I wish you well."

There's comfort in the approval of a mentor. But there's also an excuse in it—needing permission, needing validation.

Then God speaks:

> "Go back to Egypt, for all the men who wanted to kill you are dead."

The danger has passed. Yet how easy it would be to find new reasons to delay, new reasons to stay comfortable in Midian.

### The Pattern of Excuses

We all do this. We say our prayers and our devotions:

- "I'm not worthy" (false humility)
- "I don't have the right gifts" (self-doubt)
- "What if people don't believe?" (lack of faith)
- "I'm not eloquent" (insecurity)
- "Send someone else" (avoidance)

We'll fly to the ends of the earth for a holiday but hesitate to go across town for God's work. We use our time carefully for entertainment but find no time for mission. We deceive ourselves with ease.

How serious are we about following Jesus? That seriousness shows in our choices. How we spend Saturday nights reveals how serious we are about Sunday morning. How we spend our leisure time, our money, our energy—these reveal our true priorities. The church must call each other to accountability. Small groups and one-to-one friendships are where this happens, where we lovingly call each other's bluff.

### God's Anger at Excuses

As Moses finally agrees and begins his journey, God's anger at his hesitation becomes apparent. God speaks to Aaron: "Go to meet Moses in the desert."

Then, shockingly:

> At a lodging place on the way, the Lord met Moses and was about to kill him. But Zipporah took a flint knife, cut off her son's foreskin and touched Moses' feet with it. "Surely you are a bridegroom of blood to me," she said. So the Lord let him alone.

Moses has been commissioned to lead God's people, yet he has not circumcised his own son—the sign of the covenant. His own house is not in order. Zipporah, a Gentile woman, acts. She performs the covenant sign, and God's judgment is averted.

Later, Moses meets Aaron:

> The Lord said to Aaron, "Go into the desert to meet Moses." So he met Moses at the mountain of God and kissed him. Then Moses told Aaron everything the Lord had sent him to say, and also about all the miraculous signs he had commanded him to perform.

Together they gather the elders of Israel:

> Moses and Aaron brought together all the elders of the Israelites, and Aaron told them everything the Lord had said to Moses. He also performed the signs before the people, and they believed. And when they heard that the Lord was concerned about them and had seen their misery, they bowed down and worshiped.

The people believed. The God who heard has sent His messenger. The God who saves will act.

---

**Application:**

Jesus is called Joshua—He saves. That is who God is. This is the action that defines Him throughout Scripture and history. He is the God who hears the cry of His people, who sees their misery, who comes down to rescue them. He is the God of salvation.
