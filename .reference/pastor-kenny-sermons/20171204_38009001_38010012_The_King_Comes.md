# Zechariah 9:1-10:12

**Title:** The King Comes

**Date:** December 4, 2017

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Three Certainties in God's Plan

### 1. God Will Defeat His Enemies (9:1-12, 10:10-12)

Christians worldwide face a difficult season. Every year, thousands of believers are martyred by extremist groups—often more than anywhere else in the world combined. We also encounter persecution closer to home: through cultural pressures, governmental restrictions, and personal opposition from family, friends, and colleagues.

Yet Zechariah opens with an announcement that should encourage us: God will fight and defeat his enemies, bringing hope in the midst of persecution.

#### The Prophecy Against Israel's Enemies

> A prophecy: The word of the Lord is against the land of Hadrak and will come to rest on Damascus—for the eyes of all people and all the tribes of Israel are on the Lord—and on Hamath too, which borders on it, and on Tyre and Sidon, though they are very skillful.

The nations listed—Hadrak, Damascus, Hamath, Tyre, and Sidon—were enemies surrounding Israel. God announces judgment against them, declaring that all nations would witness His power.

#### Tyre: A Case Study in Judgment

Tyre exemplifies the point. This city was uniquely fortified—consisting of both a mainland settlement and an island fortress in the Mediterranean. Its wealth was legendary: merchants had amassed silver like dust and gold like street dirt. Protected by water and commerce, Tyre seemed invulnerable. Even the great Nebuchadnezzar II, after a thirteen-year siege, eventually gave up without conquering it completely.

Yet Tyre had treated Israel cruelly, selling Judean people to the Greeks and celebrating Jerusalem's downfall:

> Because Tyre has said against Jerusalem, "Aha! The gateway of the peoples has been broken! Everything will come my way, and I will become rich now that she is devastated." (Ezekiel 26:2)

God's response was clear:

> But the Lord will take away her possessions and destroy her power on the sea, and she will be consumed by fire.

What seemed impossible came to pass. In 332 BC, Alexander the Great besieged Tyre. When the proud city killed his ambassadors, Alexander responded decisively. He destroyed the old city, used its rubble to build a causeway across the channel to the island fortress, and conquered what empires before him could not. The once-mighty island city fell.

#### The Subjugation of the Philistines

Similar judgment came upon the Philistine cities:

> Ashkelon will see it and fear; Gaza will writhe in agony, and Ekron too, for her hope will wither. Gaza will lose her king and Ashkelon will be deserted. A mongrel people will occupy Ashdod, and I will put an end to the pride of the Philistines.

More remarkably, the remaining Philistines would be absorbed into Judah:

> I will take the blood from their mouths, the forbidden food from between their teeth. Those who are left will belong to our God and become a clan in Judah, and Ekron will be like the Jebusites.

This echoes King David's conquest of Jerusalem from the Jebusites—an enemy absorbed and transformed into part of God's people.

#### Extension to Other Enemies

The pattern continues as Zechariah expands the scope:

> I will bring them back from Egypt and gather them from Assyria. I will bring them to Gilead and Lebanon, and there will not be room enough for them. They will pass through the sea of trouble; the surging sea will be subdued and all the depths of the Nile will dry up. Assyria's pride will be brought down and Egypt's scepter will pass away. I will strengthen them in the Lord and in his name they will live securely.

#### Application for Today

As Christians, persecution should not surprise us:

> We know that we are children of God, and that the whole world is under the control of the evil one. (1 John 5:19)

The world belongs to the evil one, so it naturally opposes God's children. Our enemies are not merely flesh and blood—they are sin, the world system, the devil, and all forces arrayed against righteousness. Yet we have hope:

> The reason the Son of God appeared was to destroy the devil's work. (1 John 3:8)

We pray for our enemies' repentance, and we also pray that God will come and vindicate His name if they refuse to turn. God will ultimately free His people:

> Because of the blood of my covenant with you, I will free your prisoners from the waterless pit. Return to your fortress, you prisoners of hope; even now I announce that I will restore twice as much to you.

The "waterless pit" speaks of dungeons and despair, but God's covenant secures our freedom. The name of the Lord is a strong tower; the righteous run to it and are safe (Proverbs 18:10). Restoration will come—whether in coming years, in a generation, or in the life to come—but it will come.

### 2. God Will Use Israel as His Instrument (9:13-17, 10:3-7)

God defeats His enemies not just by direct intervention, but by using Israel as His instrument of war. Just as He used Alexander the Great historically, He will employ Israel with powerful imagery:

> I will bend Judah as I bend my bow and fill it with Ephraim. I will rouse your sons, Zion, against your sons, Greece, and make you like a warrior's sword.

Judah (the southern kingdom) becomes the bow; Ephraim (the northern kingdom) becomes the arrows. Northern and southern kingdoms work in concert as instruments of God's purpose.

The imagery of war continues throughout:

> My anger burns against the shepherds, and I will punish the leaders; for the Lord Almighty will care for his flock, the people of Judah, and make them like a proud horse in battle. From Judah will come the cornerstone, from him the tent peg, from him the battle bow, from him every ruler. Together they will be like warriors in battle trampling their enemy into the mud of the streets. They will fight because the Lord is with them, and they will put the enemy horsemen to shame.

God promises restoration with compassion:

> I will strengthen Judah and save the tribes of Joseph. I will restore them because I have compassion on them. They will be as though I had not rejected them, for I am the Lord their God and I will answer them. The Ephraimites will become like warriors, and their hearts will be glad as with wine. Their children will see it and be joyful; their hearts will rejoice in the Lord.

#### A Sobering Historical Reality

Yet here lies a difficult truth: despite all this language about arrows, bows, and warriors, ancient Israel never won another significant war. They experienced minor skirmishes and small victories, but then faced successive domination by Persians, Greeks, and Romans. The temple was destroyed in 70 AD; the people scattered. While the temple was rebuilt, Jews and Christians were prohibited from it. Successive empires—Byzantine, Muslim (with devastating massacres in the sixth century), Crusader, Ottoman—ruled the land. The Jewish people themselves did not return to that land until the twentieth century.

This teaches us something crucial: **God's ultimate warrior is not us.** Neither ancient Israel nor modern believers are "good enough" to be His final instruments. We are called to be righteous instruments:

> Do not offer any parts of yourselves to sin as weapons for unrighteousness. But as those who are alive from the dead, offer yourselves to God, and all the parts of yourselves to God as weapons for righteousness. (Romans 6:13)

We put on the full armor of God—truth, righteousness, the gospel of peace, faith, salvation, and the word of God (Ephesians 6:10-17)—following the example set for us. But ultimate victory belongs to Christ alone.

### 3. A Time of Peace Predicted (9:8-10, 10:8-9)

God uses one Israelite—the true Messiah—to bring lasting peace:

> Rejoice greatly, Daughter Zion! Shout, Daughter Jerusalem! See, your king comes to you, righteous and victorious, lowly and riding on a donkey, on a colt, the foal of a donkey.

#### The Significance of the Donkey

A donkey may seem an unusual choice for a king, yet it carries deep meaning in Hebrew culture:

1. **The National Animal**: Israelites were forbidden from importing warhorses (Egyptian enemies used them). Donkeys suited Israel's mountainous, rocky terrain perfectly.

2. **A Sign of Wealth and Rulership**: In Israel's history, the donkey was the elite's transport. Judges 10:4 notes that a judge with thirty sons "rode thirty donkeys" and "controlled thirty towns in Gilead." Judges 12:14 similarly records a leader with "seventy donkeys" who "led Israel eight years."

#### The Promise of Peace

This king enters Jerusalem to proclaim not war but peace:

> I will take away the chariots from Ephraim and the warhorses from Jerusalem, and the battle bow will be broken. He will proclaim peace to the nations. His rule will extend from sea to sea and from the River to the ends of the earth.

"Sea to sea" means from the Mediterranean to the Dead Sea. "The River" refers to the Euphrates—the territory once promised to Abraham and achieved under Solomon's reign (1 Kings 4:21).

Most significantly, weapons are removed:

> I will signal for them and gather them in. Surely I will redeem them; they will be as numerous as before. Though I scatter them among the peoples, yet in distant lands they will remember me. They and their children will survive, and they will return.

#### Two Types of Peace

The text distinguishes between two kinds of peace:

- **A Truce**: A temporary cessation of hostilities between enemies who remain fundamentally opposed
- **Real Peace**: When one army defeats the other, evil is vanquished, and lasting harmony prevails

Zechariah speaks of real peace—the kind where God has defeated evil entirely. This is what we long for, not mere truces.

#### Jesus: God's Ultimate Weapon

Jesus came riding a donkey and disarmed the powers and authorities through the cross:

> Having disarmed the powers and authorities, he made a public spectacle of them, triumphing over them by the cross. (Colossians 2:15)

On that cross, He defeated sin, death, and Satan. As the balance between good and evil swings throughout history, remember that Christ has already won. The ultimate victory is secure.

## Christmas: The Arrival of Peace

The sermon was preached during Advent, emphasizing that Jesus came at Christmas over two thousand years ago. Yet the full realization of this peace still awaits us.

**Advent Prayer (Second Week):**

> Merciful God, who sent your messengers the prophets to preach repentance and prepare the way for our salvation: Give us grace to heed their warnings and forsake our sins, that we may greet with joy the coming of Jesus Christ our Redeemer, who lives and reigns with you and the Holy Spirit, one God, now and for ever. Amen.

#### The Christmas Truce of 1914

History offers a poignant reminder. During World War I, five months into fighting with a million lives already lost, something remarkable happened on Christmas Eve. Soldiers ceased fire. Someone erected a tree; troops began singing "Silent Night." On Christmas Day, hundreds of enemies met in no man's land, exchanged gifts, played soccer together—a brief glimpse of peace amid carnage.

But it lasted only hours. Fighting resumed within twenty-four hours, and the war continued until forty million more had died.

#### Hope in the Darkness

Yet this brief truce points to something deeper. As our world teeters on the edge of another war, we pray for the peace only Jesus brings. On that silent night of Advent, we remember: **Peace is coming.**

Christmas celebrates the arrival of the King who will one day establish peace that never ends. Until that final consummation, we live as those who have already encountered the Prince of Peace, secure in the victory Christ has already won.
