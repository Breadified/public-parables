# Ephesians 5:21-33

**Title:** Marriage, Submission, and Leadership  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## The Reality of Submission

The word "submit" makes us deeply uncomfortable. When we hear it today, our minds often jump to disturbing images: slavery, exploitation, abuse—people forced into subjugation against their will. These are rightfully abhorrent, and no Christian endorses such things.

Yet here we have Bonnie, an intelligent and educated woman, voluntarily choosing to submit to her husband Michael. This isn't coercion; it's a conscious decision rooted in Christian conviction. This is what Scripture calls us to in marriage.

> Wives, submit to your husbands as to the Lord. For the husband is the head of the wife as Christ is the head of the church, his body, of which he is the Savior. (Ephesians 5:22-23)

The comparison is staggering. Just as the church follows Christ unconditionally, so wives are called to submit to their husbands unconditionally:

> Now as the church submits to Christ, so also wives should submit to their husbands in everything. (Ephesians 5:24)

This seems outdated in our modern world, yet it makes profound sense when we understand the biblical vision of marriage.

---

## Reason One: Creating Space for Leadership

Notice something striking in this passage: there's one sentence for wives on submission, but an entire paragraph of instruction for husbands. Why? Because submission is part of a larger picture of how marriage is meant to function.

> Husbands, love your wives, just as Christ loved the church and gave himself up for her. (Ephesians 5:25)

This is not tyrannical leadership. This is sacrificial love—the standard Jesus himself set.

**What does Christ's love for the church look like?**

- Jesus protected his beloved
- Jesus fought for his beloved
- Jesus sacrificed his life on the cross to save his beloved
- Jesus rose from the dead and continues to protect the church

Husbands are called to model this same self-giving love:

> In this same way, husbands ought to love their wives as their own bodies. He who loves his wife loves himself. After all, no one ever hated his own body, but he feeds and cares for it. (Ephesians 5:28-29)

**This means husbands take responsibility in every dimension:**

- **Physically:** Standing between danger and family, willing to sacrifice for their safety
- **Materially:** Providing food, shelter, and financial care—even if it means taking a humble job beneath their qualifications
- **Emotionally:** Being the first to initiate reconciliation and repair when conflict arises, even if it costs them pride
- **Spiritually:** Guiding the family toward holiness, ensuring as much as possible that their wives flourish spiritually

> To make her holy, cleansing her by the washing with water through the word, and to present her to himself as a radiant church, without stain or wrinkle or any other blemish, but holy and blameless. (Ephesians 5:26-27)

**The crucial insight:** A husband cannot lead without his wife's willing submission. Leadership requires followers. No leader can function if his followers are dragging their feet, complaining behind his back, or refusing to follow. By agreeing to submit, Bonnie gives Michael the space and trust he needs to lead well. Her submission enables his sacrifice.

Notice the beautiful symmetry of the vows: the groom promises first—to die for his bride, to love her sacrificially. Only after hearing this promise does the bride agree to follow. The man initiates; she accepts. And thus begins a sacred dance.

## Reason Two: A Tiebreaker for Irreconcilable Differences

You might reasonably ask: Why can't we just mutually submit to one another? Why can't couples take turns, vote, or seek outside counsel?

Of course you can do these things in most situations. Part of a husband's love is graciously giving way for the sake of harmony. A wise husband might let his wife choose where to eat, what to watch, how to spend an evening—not because he must, but because he loves her.

But God's instruction on submission isn't given for situations where couples can negotiate and compromise. It's given for moments of *irreconcilable differences*—when both parties have legitimate reasons for their position, but they genuinely cannot both get what they want.

**Imagine this scenario:** One spouse wants to live in Brisbane; the other wants Melbourne. Both have excellent reasons. Neither is wrong. By definition, they cannot both be satisfied.

What happens then?

- **Divorce?** Under modern family law, irreconcilable differences mean you can dissolve the marriage. But what if marriage is meant to be for life—a covenant, not a contract?
- **Voting?** In a democracy of two, you're back where you started.
- **Asking parents?** That seems to undo the whole purpose of independence.

If you believe marriage is stronger than your whims and feelings, if you believe marital love is worth fighting for, then you need a decision-maker when the vote is split.

God, in his wisdom, has already structured this. He could have reversed it—made wives the final decision-makers—but either way, someone has to give way. God's design ensures that couples never *have* to divorce over an irreconcilable difference. There is always a tiebreaker. There is always a way forward.

**This is a gift.** God sees this couple standing before him, deeply in love, making lifelong vows. He says, "I will help you. I will structure your marriage so that when you face impossible disagreements, you won't have to split apart. You'll have a way through."

God wants to preserve their love. He doesn't want to see them separated by geography, by incompatible dreams, by deadlock. He wants to see them together—united, thriving, building a life together.

---

## The Deeper Mystery

But here's the profound truth: this instruction about submission and leadership in marriage points to something infinitely greater.

> This is a profound mystery—but I am talking about Christ and the church. (Ephesians 5:32)

God is calling each of us to submit—like a wife to her husband—to Christ, who is our perfect head. But this is not submission to a tyrant. It is submission to one who has proved his love by dying for us, who protects us, who guides us toward fullness of life in this world and the next.

If this vision of marriage resonates with you, then consider: God has a whole heap of other instructions and wisdom that make sense of our world. The structure he offers isn't oppressive; it's liberating. It's designed to help us flourish.

---

**To Michael and Bonnie:** Remember these truths when you face your own irreconcilable differences. Lean on them. Live them out. And know that the God who loves the church with a sacrificial, eternal love walks with you both.
