# Job 42:1-17

**Title:** The End of Suffering
**Date:** August 2, 2013
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Why Suffering?

Pain and suffering have always been part of human history. Rain falls on both the good and the bad, the righteous and unrighteous. Earthquakes, floods, and tornadoes respect no nations, tribes, or colours.

We experience perfect and defective births, timely and untimely deaths, joyful and sad partings. All of us ride the roller coaster of emotion, passion, and seasons.

When human hearts cry to God for deliverance and nothing comes—when silence fills the void—the deepest questions emerge: Why God? Why me? Why now? Why suffering?

Like the Sarah McLachlan cover of "Dear God," suffering becomes one of the biggest objections to trusting God. It can feel like God is either cruel or doesn't care.

Over the past month, we've examined why suffering exists. The Book of Job offers answers to those objections. Now we come to the end of those questions and answers.

## The End of Questions

After over forty long chapters of wrestling, Job finally reaches an end to his questioning.

> Job 42:1-3 Then Job replied to the LORD: "I know that you can do all things; no plan of yours can be thwarted. You asked, 'Who is this that obscures my counsel without knowledge?' Surely I spoke of things I did not understand, things too wonderful for me to know."

The answer is simple: God knows what he's doing.

Job confesses what he cannot know and what is beyond his understanding. The world's suffering is too wonderful—too complex—for him to comprehend.

> Job 42:4-5 "You said, 'Listen now, and I will speak; I will question you, and you shall answer me.' My ears had heard of you but now my eyes have seen you."

Job has moved from intellectual knowledge to direct encounter. From hearing about God to seeing God.

But verse 6 is puzzling:

> Job 42:6 Therefore I despise myself and repent in dust and ashes.

This seems wrong. Job is presented as a righteous man—the whole point of the book is that he's innocent. God himself declares it:

> Job 1:8 "Have you considered my servant Job? There is no one on earth like him; he is blameless and upright, a man who fears God and shuns evil."

Job's righteousness is the very foundation of God's argument against Satan. If Job is repenting of sin, that argument collapses.

A better translation: Job is not repenting *of* his righteousness, but *from* dust and ashes—the position of mourning.

> Job 30:19 He throws me into the mud, and I am reduced to dust and ashes.

Job has been sitting in mourning, lamenting his suffering, crying out for God to answer him. And now, having encountered God, he is ready to move on from that place of grief.

This is the end of mourning—like a widow finally stepping out of her black clothes after a season of loss. Job had sat long enough in the dust. It was time to stop dwelling in that darkness and begin trusting again.

## The Start of Trusting

Suffering cannot be the excuse for everything. It cannot excuse:

- Ignoring God, whether the suffering is in the world or in yourself
- Continuing to doubt God; hardship and bitterness cannot have the final word
- Stopping your productive service to God
- Excusing your sins

Yes, there is a time for mourning. But there is also a time to move on, to repent from the ashes and dust, and to persevere and trust God.

Job was in the middle of a test. Behind heaven's curtain, God had claimed to Satan that Job was righteous. Satan said he only feared God because of blessing. "Take away the hedge protecting him," Satan said, "and he will curse you."

But Job didn't curse God. He passed the test. So it was time to move on.

We are called to persevere through suffering, not to wallow in it. We are not to escape through sinning or through taking our own life. God uses hardship as discipline to strengthen us and prepare us.

> Hebrews 12:4-6 In your struggle against sin, you have not yet resisted to the point of shedding your blood. And you have forgotten that word of encouragement that addresses you as sons: "...do not lose heart when he rebukes you, because the Lord disciplines those he loves, and he punishes everyone he accepts as a son."

> Hebrews 12:7, 10-11 Endure hardship as discipline...God disciplines us for our good, that we may share in his holiness. No discipline seems pleasant at the time, but painful. Later on, however, it produces a harvest of righteousness and peace for those who have been trained by it.

Just as children need discipline to grow strong, we need hardship to develop resilience. Decluttering skills are learned in a small house. Patience is learned when you have children. Perseverance is learned when you want what you cannot have.

If you can't stop cursing God when you accidentally hit your thumb, how will you keep faith when you lose your arm? If you can maintain your commitment through small obstacles—exams, headaches, hardship—you develop the strength to endure when real suffering comes.

Build your resilience now through the little things. Your commitment to church and Bible study even when exams are stressful. Your giving even when you have less. These aren't burdens to avoid; they're building blocks for your faith.

## God Has Spoken

Why was Job ready to move on? Because God has spoken.

Throughout the Book of Job, Job's deepest cry has been for an audience with God:

> Job 13:3 "But I desire to speak to the Almighty and to argue my case with God."

> Job 19:27 "I myself will see him with my own eyes—I, and not another. How my heart yearns within me!"

Job wasn't primarily asking for restoration of his wealth or family. He was asking for God to break his silence.

And though God doesn't specifically explain that Job's suffering was a test, God does speak. He speaks out of the storm:

> Job 40:6-10 "Then the LORD spoke to Job out of the storm: 'Brace yourself like a man; I will question you, and you shall answer me. Would you discredit my justice? Would you condemn me to justify yourself? Do you have an arm like God's, and can your voice thunder like his?'"

God reminds Job that he is still in charge—of creation, of history, of everything. And when God speaks, Job's faith grows, and he is ready to move on.

We might ask "why suffering?" and find scientific answers. Why cancer? Because cells mutate and grow out of control. Why did I eat burnt food or sit in the sun too much? But these explanations, however accurate, still leave the deepest question unanswered: Why?

The world, in its ultimate nature, is silent about this question. Karma offers no answer. Random selection is unfulfilling. Superstition is foolish.

But in this vacuum, God has spoken. He came as Jesus two thousand years ago and addressed suffering directly:

> Luke 13:1-3 "Now there were some present at that time who told Jesus about the Galileans whose blood Pilate had mixed with their sacrifices. Jesus answered, 'Do you think that these Galileans were worse sinners than all the other Galileans because they suffered this way? I tell you, no! But unless you repent, you too will all perish.'"

When confronted with tragic suffering, Jesus didn't say it was punishment for sin. He said: Repent. Get right with God.

> Luke 13:4-5 "Or those eighteen who died when the tower in Siloam fell on them—do you think they were more guilty than all the others living in Jerusalem? I tell you, no! But unless you repent, you too will all perish."

Again: no specific explanation for suffering. Just a call to repent and trust.

Not karma. But grace. God's ultimate word to us in response to the suffering in the world is: Repent and trust me.

As C.S. Lewis wrote:

> "God whispers to us in our pleasures, speaks to us in our conscience, but shouts in our pains: It is His megaphone to rouse a deaf world."

God has spoken to us through his word and through Jesus. Jesus came not just to speak but to act. He got on the cross and paid the penalty of sin. He suffered so that our suffering might one day end—so that our suffering will be dealt with permanently.

> Romans 8:18-21 "I consider that our present sufferings are not worth comparing with the glory that will be revealed in us. The creation waits in eager expectation for the sons of God to be revealed. For the creation was subjected to frustration, not by its own choice, but by the will of the one who subjected it, in hope that the creation itself will be liberated from its bondage to decay and brought into the glorious freedom of the children of God."

## The End of Suffering

After his encounter with God, things returned much as they were before. But more significantly, Job's posture changed.

**Job goes back to petitioning for others.** Before, he interceded for his children's sin. Now he intercedes for his friends:

> Job 42:8-9 "So now take seven bulls and seven rams and go to my servant Job and sacrifice a burnt offering for yourselves. My servant Job will pray for you, and I will accept his prayer and not deal with you according to your folly. You have not spoken of me what is right, as my servant Job has. So Eliphaz the Temanite, Bildad the Shuhite and Zophar the Naamathite did what the LORD told them; and the LORD accepted Job's prayer."

**Job eats with others again.** He is no longer an outcast:

> Job 42:11 "All his brothers and sisters and everyone who had known him before came and ate with him in his house. They comforted and consoled him over all the trouble the LORD had brought upon him, and each one gave him a piece of silver and a gold ring."

**Job's wealth is restored—doubled:**

> Job 42:10, 12 "After Job had prayed for his friends, the LORD made him prosperous again and gave him twice as much as he had before...The LORD blessed the latter part of Job's life more than the first."

His animals are doubled: fourteen thousand sheep, six thousand camels, a thousand yoke of oxen and a thousand donkeys.

**His family is restored and increased:**

> Job 42:13-15 "He also had seven sons and three daughters. The first daughter he named Jemimah, the second Keziah and the third Keren-Happuch. Nowhere in all the land were there found women as beautiful as Job's daughters, and their father granted them an inheritance along with their brothers."

The fact that his daughters inherit is extraordinary. In that culture, daughters typically didn't inherit because families couldn't afford to split estates. Inheritance went to the male heir to preserve the family's wealth and power. But when there's enough to bless everyone—when abundance overflows—then even the daughters can inherit. This detail signals Job's overwhelming wealth.

**His years are doubled:**

> Job 42:16 "After this, Job lived a hundred and forty years; he saw his children and their children to the fourth generation."

Psalm 90:10 tells us seventy years is a typical lifespan. Job lived twice that—long enough to see his great-grandchildren.

All these blessings exceed what Job lost. They are not rewards for his virtue, but gestures of grace. Job received undeserved suffering and now receives undeserved blessing.

Yet even in this blessing, there remains incompleteness:

> Job 42:17 "And so he died, old and full of years."

For all the doubling—all the restoration and increase—Job still aged, got sick, and died. He had to leave everything behind.

The true end of suffering for those in Christ comes not in this life, but in the life to come. Job's earthly restoration points to a greater inheritance awaiting believers—a crown waiting at the end of life for those who persevere.

> James 5:7-11 "Be patient, then, brothers, until the Lord's coming...Brothers, as an example of patience in the face of suffering, take the prophets who spoke in the name of the Lord. As you know, we consider blessed those who have persevered. You have heard of Job's perseverance and have seen what the Lord finally brought about. The Lord is full of compassion and mercy."

We must plant these truths in our hearts now, building resilience through small acts of faith and perseverance. When real trouble strikes—when you face cancer, persecution, family crisis, or the loss of everything you hold dear—you won't be in a place to hear sermons or read theology. You need to know this now:

1. God is in charge of both good and evil
2. We are called to endure, not to escape
3. God shows us grace, not karma
4. God has spoken a word of comfort to us
5. There is an end to suffering for those in Christ

Let us strengthen our feeble arms and weak knees now, building the resilience we will need when the storms come.
