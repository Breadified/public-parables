# Isaiah 7:1-17

**Title:** Signs of Immanuel  
**Date:** 18 December 2022  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Crisis: Civil War Among God's People (vv. 1-2)

When Ahaz son of Jotham became king of Judah, King Rezin of Aram and Pekah son of Remaliah, king of Israel, marched up to fight against Jerusalem. Yet they could not overpower the city.

> Now the house of David was told, "Aram has allied itself with Ephraim"; so the hearts of Ahaz and his people were shaken, as the trees of the forest are shaken by the wind.

The crisis facing Judah was devastating not simply because of external enemies, but because it represented something far worse: civil war among God's own people. Both Israel and Judah worshipped the same God, yet they were now joining with Gentile nations against their own blood relatives.

Pekah was a troubling figure—a military captain who had assassinated his predecessor, King Pekahiah, to seize the throne. Rezin ruled Aram (Syria), a pagan nation. Together, they represented a dangerous alliance bent on destroying Judah.

**A Word for Today**

This pattern repeats itself in our own time. Christians often find themselves attacking fellow believers more fiercely than they engage the pagan world. Social media has amplified this tendency—believers become "famous" for attacking other Christians, labeling them "not progressive enough" or "not conservative enough."

Yet when we are a minority, perhaps it is not the time to wage war among ourselves. By all means, maintain our distinctions. By all means, debate privately. But we must not fight in front of the pagans:

> "For since there is jealousy and quarreling among you, are you not worldly?" (1 Corinthians 3:3)

> "The very fact that you have lawsuits among you means you have been completely defeated already. Why not rather be wronged? Why not rather be cheated?" (1 Corinthians 6:7)

As a minority group, the body of Christ must remember: Christians are the only ones who invest time and resources into churches. Pagan groups support secular causes. We have a unique responsibility to support the family of believers.

## The Call to Faith: Stand Firm (vv. 3-12)

The Lord commanded Isaiah to meet Ahaz at the aqueduct of the Upper Pool with a message of reassurance:

> "Be careful, keep calm and don't be afraid. Do not lose heart because of these two smoldering stubs of firewood—because of the fierce anger of Rezin and Aram and of the son of Remaliah."

The imagery here is crucial. Rezin and Pekah are described as "smoldering stubs of firewood"—pieces of kindling that were once powerful but have now burned down to ash. They may still smoke and emit some heat, but they pose no real danger. The best response? Leave them alone, and they will extinguish themselves.

> "Aram, Ephraim and Remaliah's son have plotted your ruin, saying, 'Let us invade Judah; let us tear it apart and divide it among ourselves, and make the son of Tabeel king over it.'"

Yet God declares:

> "It will not take place, it will not happen, for the head of Aram is Damascus, and the head of Damascus is only Rezin. Within sixty-five years Ephraim will be too shattered to be a people. The head of Ephraim is Samaria, and the head of Samaria is only Remaliah's son."

God emphasizes the fragility of these enemies. Each kingdom's power rests on a single city; each city's power rests on a single man. This is not a stable foundation. History confirms this: both Rezin and Pekah were soon deposed or assassinated. Like the Ukraine-Russia conflict, which seems to depend entirely on whether Putin or Zelensky remains in power, empires built on personality rather than principle are inherently unstable.

**The Temptation to Act**

When anxiety strikes, we feel compelled to "do something." Yet sometimes the cure proves worse than the disease. Sometimes the problem resolves naturally if we simply wait. This was true for Rezin and Pekah—the smoldering would have faded on its own.

But Ahaz did not trust God. Instead, despite Isaiah's invitation to ask for a sign of God's faithfulness, he refused:

> "Ask the Lord your God for a sign, whether in the deepest depths or in the highest heights." But Ahaz said, "I will not ask; I will not put the Lord to the test."

On the surface, this sounds pious. He was quoting Deuteronomy 6:16—don't test the Lord. Yet his refusal was not obedience; it was unbelief. He had already made his decision. He would not trust God to protect Judah as He had promised to David's descendants.

Instead, Ahaz sent messengers to Tiglath-Pileser, king of Assyria:

> "I am your servant and vassal. Come up and save me out of the hand of the king of Aram and of the king of Israel, who are attacking me."

This was the fatal error. Judah, called to be a light to the Gentiles, surrendered its independence and became a vassal state. Ahaz traded his faith in God for trust in a pagan superpower.

**A Word for Today**

Christians are rapidly becoming a minority in Western culture. The temptation is real: cling to power by compromising with the world, by becoming its vassal, trusting it will save us. We face pressure from secular culture and sometimes even attacks from other believers.

Yet we cannot compromise our core values, even if it means standing alone. As Paul writes:

> "Do not be yoked together with unbelievers. For what do righteousness and wickedness have in common? Or what fellowship can light have with darkness?" (2 Corinthians 6:14-16)

We can do business with the world. We can have limited partnerships. But wholesale allegiance? That crosses the line. Better to stand alone—even on a remote farm with your family—than to surrender your faith for the world's protection.

This applies to marriage, to business partnerships, to every major commitment. The call is clear: stand firm in faith in God, or stand not at all.

## The Judgment: God's Sign (vv. 13-17)

Having rejected God's offer of assurance, Ahaz now receives judgment instead of comfort:

> Then Isaiah said, "Hear now, you house of David! Is it not enough to try the patience of humans? Will you try the patience of my God also? Therefore the Lord himself will give you a sign: The virgin will conceive and give birth to a son, and will call him Immanuel."

The word "virgin" has sparked centuries of debate. In Hebrew, the term can mean either "young woman" or "virgin." But the Septuagint—the Greek translation of the Hebrew scriptures, the Bible of Jesus' time—explicitly translates it as "virgin." Early Christians quoted this passage so frequently that the Jewish community eventually returned to the Hebrew Masoretic text to challenge the interpretation.

The sign is called "Immanuel"—"God with us." This is not a sign of comfort but of judgment. For Ahaz, who refused to believe that God would be with him to protect his kingdom, the birth of this child becomes an indictment of his unbelief.

> "He will be eating curds and honey when he knows enough to reject the wrong and choose the right, for before the boy knows enough to reject the wrong and choose the right, the land of the two kings you dread will be laid waste."

The immediate fulfillment came rapidly. Tiglath-Pileser attacked Damascus and deported its inhabitants to Kir. Rezin was executed. Ephraim was reduced to a "rump state," with most of its territory absorbed into the Assyrian Empire. Pekah was assassinated by his successor, Hoshea.

But Judah's judgment was more severe and long-lasting:

> "The Lord will bring on you and on your people and on the house of your father a time unlike any since Ephraim broke away from Judah—he will bring the king of Assyria."

The very alliance Ahaz sought for protection became his doom. Judah would suffer under Assyrian domination far longer than either Aram or Israel.

## Living as a Faithful Minority

The closing message is deeply relevant for Christians today. Minority groups survive through mutual loyalty—through employing one another, supporting one another, identifying the one who might escape poverty and lifting everyone together. Migration, education, advancement—these become communal victories.

Christians are no longer in the majority. We must learn to think and act like a wise minority. The Bible has prepared us for this. Our survival depends on cultivating fierce loyalty to one another as brothers and sisters in Christ.

The temptation to abandon our faith for worldly power is ever present. But the lesson of Ahaz is clear: faith in God, even if it means standing alone, is incomparably better than the security the world offers. Our true ally is not any earthly power, but the God who promises to be Immanuel—God with us.
