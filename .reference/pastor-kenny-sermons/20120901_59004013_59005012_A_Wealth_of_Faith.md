# James 4:13–5:12

**Title:** A Wealth of Faith  
**Date:** 16 September 2012  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Faith in Wealth: An Introduction

When I was young growing up in Malaysia, my ambition wasn't to be a doctor, lawyer, or businessman. It was simply to be an office worker. Malaysia is intensely hot, and air-conditioned offices seemed like the pinnacle of comfort and success. That dream drove much of my early life.

Years later, when I finally graduated from university, I was proud to work in a large, air-conditioned office. I wore a suit, carried a briefcase, had my own desk, and enjoyed free lunches and umbrellas. My proudest moment came when the company sent me to Melbourne for work-related training. I could tell my friends, "Tomorrow I'm flying to Melbourne"—though I must admit, that was my first and last work-related flight!

This sermon explores what James teaches about wealth, success, and where our true faith should rest. The passage moves through four portraits: the proud, the planner, the prosperous, and the patient.

---

## 1. The Proud (James 4:13–14, 16)

### The Portrait of Success

> Now listen, you who say, "Today or tomorrow we will go to this or that city, spend a year there, carry on business and make money." (4:13)

James describes a successful merchant—a man who travels between cities conducting business. He's independent, strategic, intelligent, wealthy, and accustomed to his plans working out. On the surface, this looks admirable. We aspire to be independent, to earn good incomes, to afford homes and vacations.

But James identifies a critical problem: the attitude that accompanies these plans.

### The Problem: Taking God for Granted

> Why, you do not even know what will happen tomorrow. What is your life? You are a mist that appears for a little while and then vanishes. (4:14)

James reminds us that we cannot guarantee tomorrow. The world does not revolve around us. Our plans are limited by our knowledge, and that knowledge is severely constrained. Our lives are like mist—appearing briefly, then dissolving into nothing, leaving nothing behind.

This echoes the parable of the rich fool in Luke 12:

> "But God said to him, 'You fool! This very night your life will be demanded from you. Then who will get what you have prepared for yourself?'" (Luke 12:20)

### Success Breeds Blindness to God

The successful person often forgets a crucial truth: *everything he has is a gift from God's grace*.

- He travels safely because God keeps the roads secure.
- He profits because God hasn't sent economic depression to that city.
- He stays alive to work because God sustains his breath.
- He can plan at all because God gave him a mind.
- He can execute plans because God gives him life itself.

Yet he acknowledges none of this. He boasts in his schemes as though he alone created them.

> As it is, you boast in your arrogant schemes. All such boasting is evil. (4:16)

There is no reason to boast in anything we possess or accomplish. Everything comes by God's grace. We live and act only because of God's will.

### Keeping Perspective

The universe is vast. We are specks upon a speck of a planet. If you become successful, don't boast—show thankfulness to God for what He has given you.

Consider this: I grew up in Malaysia with bleak circumstances, poor schools, and little prospect of education. I dreamed of air-conditioning. Twenty-four years later, I live in Australia. The dollar is strong. I'm educated. My parents, once modestly educated, now jet-set around the world.

Why? One circumstance: my mother was a nurse, and the Australian government needed that skill. Who would have thought being a nurse could transport a family to Australia? Through what appear to be fortunate accidents, everything traces back to God.

Think about your own life:
- The lucky break your parents had in coming to Australia
- Living in one of the safest suburbs in Sydney, in the safest country in the world
- Perhaps passing one selective schools test that gave you access to excellent education
- All of this seems "lucky," but it isn't luck—it's God's grace

There is no boasting. There is no dependence on self alone. All good comes from God.

## 2. The Planner (James 4:15, 17)

### Planning Is Not Wrong

This doesn't mean we should never make plans. Planning is part of our stewardship. Even the apostle Paul made plans:

> After all this had happened, Paul decided to go to Jerusalem, passing through Macedonia and Achaia. "After I have been there," he said, "I must visit Rome also." He sent two of his helpers, Timothy and Erastus, to Macedonia, while he stayed in the province of Asia a little longer. (Acts 19:21–22)

But there are two ways to understand verse 15:

### Option One: Prayer for God's Blessing (After Planning)

> Instead, you ought to say, "If it is the Lord's will, we will live and do this or that." (4:15)

The first reading treats this as asking God to bless our already-made plans. We decide what to do, then pray, "Lord, please bless these plans." There's nothing wrong with this—it shows trust. But it risks making God a rubber stamp for decisions we've already made in our own wisdom.

Consider how we typically plan:
- Get into a selective school, hire the best tutors, study relentlessly in years 11–12, achieve a state ranking, gain a high ATAR, pursue law or commerce
- Build work experience, secure internships, land a graduate position, work for years, relocate to London or Hong Kong
- At 40, with a certain superannuation balance, retire early and travel

For our children, we plan: selective or private school, excellent tutoring, relentless pressure in years 11–12, high ATAR, professional university degree in medicine or law, high-paying graduate position, mortgage, and room for us to retire with them.

These plans are thorough—but where is God in them? Where is room for Him to redirect? Where is space for His will instead of ours? Where are His mission and His definition of success?

### Option Two: Discerning God's Will First

The second reading asks: What IS the Lord's will? We must ask God first, then plan accordingly.

God has revealed His will in Scripture. He calls us back to Jesus and the gospel. He tells us not to be envious, to care for widows, to do good works. And crucially:

> If anyone, then, knows the good they ought to do and doesn't do it, it is sin for them. (4:17)

We should plan to do good. We should plan to do God's will.

We meticulously plan syllabuses and careers, yet do we plan whom to help this week? We set goals for how many textbook chapters to read during study break, yet do we set goals for reading God's Word? We decide which university course to pursue, but have we considered Bible study? We plan summer holidays overseas, but has an overseas mission ever crossed our minds? We aspire to career success, yet has full-time ministry work never seemed possible for us?

When we ask what God wants *first*, our decisions change.

### The Real Cost of Misaligned Priorities

Don't become so desperate for a high ATAR that you abandon your Christian walk. If you study Monday through Friday, attend tutoring all day Saturday, and spend Saturday night on social media, you'll be mentally exhausted by Sunday. When you arrive at church—if you go—you're too drained to think about God or support your friends.

This applies to all of life's plans. If you keep scheduling parties or music lessons on Sundays, or taking holidays on Sundays (because flights are cheaper), you'll miss church, Sunday school, or youth group. You'll feel less part of the community, making you less likely to return. This is not God's will.

I recently heard that one young woman moved her tutoring to a different day so she could attend church. Yes! That's the right priority.

Do God's will and plans first, then arrange everything else around them.

### A Word to Parents

You must decide what you truly want for your children. Pushing them toward a high ATAR at the expense of their Christian life is misguided. If they study all week and attend tutoring all day Saturday, they'll be exhausted on Sunday. If they miss church because of exhaustion, they'll feel disconnected from the faith community and less likely to return.

If you only plan their academics and ignore their spiritual wellbeing, they learn that studying matters more than God. If you schedule parties or music lessons on Sundays, or book holidays on Sundays to save money, they'll miss church and youth group. They'll feel less part of the family of faith, making them reluctant to return.

**You must choose which hills you're willing to die on—and some hills, once you leave them, don't come back.**

### When Work and Faith Conflict

If your career, however successful, requires constant travel that jeopardizes your Christian life, consider taking a lower-paying job, living more modestly, and spending more time with your family. Wouldn't that be the Lord's will?

If you must travel for business or vacation, minimize the damage to your faith. Don't travel when church is scheduled. Sunday flights are cheaper, but is that the Lord's will? Have you truly considered the impact on your church when you spend weeks planning the perfect holiday yet fail to think about God?

If you must travel, catch the airport bus instead of asking someone to drive you—that way you're not keeping them from church. But "that's expensive!" Yes—you planned everything else and didn't factor in the cost to others?

When we put God's priorities first, everything else follows. This shows we understand that worldly success won't save us. We stop placing our faith in careers, degrees, or superannuation plans. Your life won't be ruined without a university degree or a substantial bank account.

In the end, prioritizing the world and its wealth isn't worth it. Look at James's condemnation of the rich.

## 3. The Prosperous (James 5:1–6)

### The Coming Judgment

> Now listen, you rich people, weep and wail because of the misery that is coming on you. Your wealth has rotted, and moths have eaten your clothes. Your gold and silver are corroded. Their corrosion will testify against you and eat your flesh like fire. You have hoarded wealth in the last days. (5:1–3)

The rich have not done God's will. They've hoarded wealth for themselves like the rich fool, keeping it all locked away. But when the door opens—what do we find?

- Riches have rotted
- Clothes are eaten by moths
- Gold and silver are corroded

All of it is *unused*. Sitting there. All the good these riches could have purchased, all the suffering they could have relieved—wasted.

### The Sin of Exploitation

The rich were supposed to use their wealth to fulfill their responsibilities:

> Look! The wages you failed to pay the workers who mowed your fields are crying out against you. The cries of the harvesters have reached the ears of the Lord Almighty. (5:4)

They have cheated workers—stolen from them—and in doing so, they have effectively killed them.

> You have condemned and murdered the innocent one, who was not opposing you. (5:6)

In the first century, workers received their daily wages each day so they could buy food for their families that very evening. If you withheld their pay, there was no food, no sustenance, no survival. Without social security nets, unpaid workers simply died.

In our time, this principle applies when we strip penalty rates for Sunday work. This destroys family life and church life. *Get out of Sunday work.*

### The Judgment Stands

> You have lived on earth in luxury and self-indulgence. You have fattened yourselves in the day of slaughter. (5:5)

Like turkeys at Christmas or ducks being fattened for pâté, the rich keep hoarding, consuming, fattening themselves—all for the day of judgment. Their corrosion will testify against them.

## 4. The Patient (James 5:7–11)

### A Call to Persevere in Doing Good

Given the seriousness of what James has just described, we must work out the good. We must use the gifts and resources God has given us to serve others. As James says elsewhere, faith without deeds is dead (2:18).

The rich have responsibilities. The poor are just like us, except for circumstances of grace. It's tempting to think we can treat the poor differently—as though they're lazy, unimportant, inferior to us because we've "worked hard" for success. But to think this is to forget God's special grace and favor toward us.

Doing God's will costs:
- You'll be poorer because you've given wealth away
- You'll have less to spend because you've committed to the church
- You might lose friendships as you pursue a humbler lifestyle (popularity is a function of fashion, which is a function of money)
- You'll be personally humbled; you'll lose the opportunity to boast
- You'll have less security

As you make these changes, you may wonder if you've missed out. You may question whether the cost was too high. If you feel that way, hear James's encouragement:

> Be patient, then, brothers and sisters, until the Lord's coming. See how the farmer waits for the land to yield its valuable crop, patiently waiting for the autumn and spring rains. You too, be patient and stand firm, because the Lord's coming is near. (5:7–8)

God is coming soon. He will examine our lives to see if they produce fruits of good works. When He sees that fruit, He will say, "Well done, good and faithful servant."

> Don't grumble against one another, brothers and sisters, or you will be judged. The Judge is standing at the door! (5:9)

> Brothers and sisters, as an example of patience in the face of suffering, take the prophets who spoke in the name of the Lord. As you know, we count as blessed those who have persevered. You have heard of Job's perseverance and have seen what the Lord finally brought about. The Lord is full of compassion and mercy. (5:10–11)

### Two Reasons to Trust Patience

**First, you're not actually missing out.** Studies on materialism show that satisfaction levels remain the same whether people are wealthy or modest. Yet depression rises as wealth increases. Happiness isn't about things or experiences money buys. It's not about power.

**Second, security doesn't come from money.** God confounds our well-laid plans and insurance policies anyway. Kerry Packer, Australia's richest man, died of a heart attack and took nothing with him. He is no fool who gives up what he can never have for what he can never lose.

Be patient because judgment day is coming soon. The day when wrong will be made right, when inequality will be corrected, when those who've fattened themselves will be judged and find themselves thirsty in fire. Judgment day and death are the great equalizers—but which side will you be on?

Hear the words of comfort from James:

> Listen, my dear brothers: Has not God chosen those who are poor in the eyes of the world to be rich in faith and to inherit the kingdom he promised those who love him? (2:5)

## A Wealth of Faith

**Be rich in your faith.**

We follow a Savior who chose to be poor for our sake so that we might be rich. He calls us to do the same for others.

Years ago, I struggled with whether to leave office work and serve a church. I feared the lack of security—what if I became ill and needed money? I'd carefully planned my long-term future, and this change threatened it.

But I came to understand two things:

First, no worldly plan I'd made was protected from God. Whether I'm a rich accountant or a less wealthy pastor, if God wants me to be sick, it makes no difference. I am but a mist that vanishes.

Second, no clever plan of mine could ultimately protect me. I must do God's will first and seek His kingdom first.

If God can bring material success from a humble Malaysian nurse, He can sustain whatever calling He places before you.

Don't let your plans stop you from doing good.

Jesus modeled this in the Garden of Gethsemane. He didn't proceed with His own life and see how God fit in. He surrendered His will to the Father's.
