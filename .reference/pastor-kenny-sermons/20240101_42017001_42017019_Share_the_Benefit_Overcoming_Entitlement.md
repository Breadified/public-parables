## Introduction: First World Problems

A few years ago, the term "First World Problems" became popular—describing the trivial complaints of those who take luxury for granted. Examples include: "I forgot my earphones," "My gardener is mowing too early," "There are too many seeds in my watermelon," "My treadmill is broken so I have to run outside."

We create these problems because we take for granted what is actually a luxury to the majority of the world. But here's the real danger: this same entitlement seeps into our spiritual lives. We forget that God does not owe us anything, and yet we have owed him everything.

This sermon series, "Share the Benefit," is about looking after our neighbour. Last week we studied Zaccheus and learned about empathy. Today, let's understand what can stop us from acting on that empathy: **entitlement**.

---

## I. God Does Not Owe Us (Luke 17:7-10)

Jesus tells a story about a master and his servant. The servant has had a hard day—perhaps plowing fields, turning over soil to bring fresh nutrients to the surface, or shepherding sheep across miles of land, protecting them from predators, feeding and milking them. After sweat, muscle pain, blisters, and hunger under the Middle Eastern sun, the servant naturally wants to sit down and eat.

But here's what the master says:

> "Will he not rather say, 'Prepare my supper, get yourself ready and wait on me while I eat and drink; after that you may eat and drink'?" (Luke 17:8)

The work isn't finished. The master hasn't had his dinner yet. So the servant must continue serving.

> "Would he thank the servant because he did what he was told to do? So you also, when you have done everything you were told to do, should say, 'We are unworthy servants; we have only done our duty.'" (Luke 17:9-10)

**The servant has merely done his duty.** He deserves no thanks, no gratitude, no commendation. He plowed, he served, he cooked—this is simply his job. There is no favour here, no extra mile worthy of praise.

This strikes us because we've become accustomed to thanking people for doing exactly what they're supposed to do. But think about it: if you hand in an assignment to your lecturer, would they say, "Thank you so much for giving me an assignment—you've done me a great favour"? No. You've simply done what you were supposed to do.

The issue isn't the master's character. The issue is the **servant's attitude of entitlement**—a sense that he deserves rest, gratitude, and recognition for doing his duty.

### The Gospel Perspective

Now consider our relationship with God. We have been rescued and redeemed. Ephesians 2 tells us:

> "But because of his great love for us, God, who is rich in mercy, made us alive with Christ even when we were dead in transgressions—it is by grace you have been saved. And God raised us up with Christ and seated us with him in the heavenly realms in Christ Jesus... in order that in the coming ages he might show the incomparable riches of his grace, expressed in his kindness to us in Christ Jesus." (Ephesians 2:4-7)

We were dead in sin. There was nothing we could do to raise ourselves. But Jesus, out of love and mercy and grace, raised us up. We are now of infinite worth because Christ paid the infinite price for us.

**How, then, can we feel entitled to anything God gives us?**

We are but unworthy servants. What we do, we do out of duty. It's our response to a gracious God.

## II. Rather, We Owe God (Luke 17:1-6, 11-19)

Because we have been forgiven much, we now owe generosity to others.

### The Scandal of Unforgiveness (Luke 17:3-4)

> "If your brother sins, rebuke him, and if he repents, forgive him. If he sins against you seven times in a day, and seven times comes back to you and says, 'I repent,' forgive him." (Luke 17:3-4)

We think we're entitled to hold a grudge. But we who have been forgiven must forgive others. "Seven times" means unlimited—not because forgiveness is easy, but because we forgive much because we have been forgiven much.

When we forgive, we're not being heroes. "Look at me—I'm so kind and generous." No. We're simply doing our duty.

### The Scandal of Withholding Grace (Luke 17:1-2)

> "Things that cause people to sin are bound to come, but woe to that person through whom they come. It would be better for him to be thrown into the sea with a millstone tied around his neck than for him to cause one of these little ones to sin." (Luke 17:1-2)

Why such harsh language? To "cause one of these little ones to sin" means to prevent them from accessing forgiveness—to keep them from Jesus. When we withhold the gospel from someone right in front of us—in our classroom, at our workplace, in our backyard—we cause them to remain in their sin.

We think we're entitled to keep the good news to ourselves. But we still have a duty on this earth: to tell our friends about Jesus, to support the church that exists to tell people about Jesus.

### The Real Problem Isn't Faith (Luke 17:5-6)

When the disciples heard Jesus' call for such radical generosity and forgiveness, they said:

> "The apostles said to the Lord, 'Increase our faith!'" (Luke 17:5)

> "He replied, 'If you have faith as small as a mustard seed, you can say to this mulberry tree, "Be uprooted and planted in the sea," and it will obey you.'" (Luke 17:6)

**The problem is not faith.** The disciples had enough faith. The problem is that they—like us—feel entitled. We excuse ourselves: "I don't have enough money. I don't have the gift of evangelism. I don't have enough faith." These are excuses born from entitlement.

### Being Thankful (Luke 17:11-19)

The passage about the ten lepers beautifully illustrates the problem:

> "Now on his way to Jerusalem, Jesus traveled along the border between Samaria and Galilee. As he was going into a village, ten men who had leprosy met him. They stood at a distance and called out in a loud voice, 'Jesus, Master, have pity on us!' When he saw them, he said, 'Go, show yourselves to the priests.' And as they went, they were cleansed. One of them, when he saw he was healed, came back, praising God in a loud voice. He threw himself at Jesus' feet and thanked him—and he was a Samaritan." (Luke 17:11-16)

> "Jesus asked, 'Were not all ten cleansed? Where are the other nine? Has no one returned to give praise to God except this foreigner?' Then he said to him, 'Rise and go; your faith has made you well.'" (Luke 17:17-19)

All ten were healed. But only one—a Samaritan, an outsider—came back to thank Jesus. The other nine simply walked away, feeling entitled to their healing.

Notice what Jesus says to the one who returned: "Your faith has made you well." Not just physically well, but whole. Why? Because **gratitude is the mark of true faith**.

Why should Christians be generous? Not to earn heaven—there's no way to work our way to salvation. But out of **gratefulness**. When we remember that we were lost but are now found, that we were on the other side of a great chasm desperate to reach God, and God mercifully pulled us out—when we remember this, we are moved to generosity.

## III. Last World Problems

We are a spoilt generation. We create our own version of "First World Problems" while the world is in dire need:

- "I can't be generous because I need to buy yet another phone."
- "I can't commit to mission work—I'm trying to book cheap flights to Korea during my holidays in Hong Kong."
- "I can't help with this ministry. I'm skiing in Canada."
- "I only get four months off for university holidays—there's so much to do."
- "I'd hang out with this lonely person, but I don't like the food."
- "If I bring the kids to Sunday school this week, they'll miss part of a birthday party."

But these are not First World problems. They are **Last World problems**—the problems of a world that has forgotten its duty, that has fallen into entitlement, that has stopped serving.

**Let us have no talk of entitlements and no more excuses.** We have a duty. Let's do it together.

---

## Conclusion: The Pattern of Duty

Jesus is the person who did his duty and more. He did it out of love for you and me. We are generous because we are thankful. Grace motivates us to serve, not obligation.

Remember: the work is never finished. There is always someone to forgive, someone to reach, someone to serve. The danger is thinking we've "done our Christian bit"—that we packed up the chairs at church and now we're done, or we led a mission trip and deserve a year off, or we worked hard on one event and can rest for the rest of the year.

But John Chapman, a beloved Christian worker in Sydney, didn't retire. Until his dying day, he continued to travel and speak. He didn't rest playing golf or traveling overseas. He **died serving the Lord**. He did his duty and now he is at rest.

God wants us to be generous—not as an option, but as our duty. Not because we're trying to earn anything, but because we've been given everything. Grace has made us grateful. Let that gratitude move us to serve.
