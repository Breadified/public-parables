# 1 Peter 1:12–2:3

**Title:** Privileged Power

**Date:** 16 October 2011

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Introduction: Understanding Privilege

The word "privilege" carries heavy weight in our culture today. It suggests unfair advantage, unearned power held by one group over another. In recent years, particularly in social media and educational circles, the concept of "white privilege" has become prominent—the idea that people of European descent benefit from systemic advantages based solely on their race.

While these discussions swirl around us, Peter's letter invites us to consider a far deeper kind of privilege—one that belongs to every Christian regardless of ethnicity or background. As we approach Christmas, we'll explore what it means to be spiritually privileged and how that privilege transforms the way we live.

---

## Part One: The Privilege of Knowledge

### The Prophets' Long Search

> Concerning this salvation, the prophets, who spoke of the grace that was to come to you, searched intently and with the greatest care, trying to find out the time and circumstances to which the Spirit of Christ in them was pointing when he predicted the sufferings of the Messiah and the glories that would follow. It was revealed to them that they were not serving themselves but you, when they spoke of the things that have now been told you by those who have preached the gospel to you by the Holy Spirit sent from heaven. Even angels long to look into these things. (1 Peter 1:10-12)

Imagine a detective story spanning thousands of years. Hundreds of prophets worked across centuries like detectives investigating the same grand mystery: Who is the Messiah? Each contributed clues, drew closer to the answer, yet never saw the final revelation. They predicted his virgin birth, his birthplace in Bethlehem, his pierced side, the thirty pieces of silver—all the details. But they never experienced the climax themselves.

Think of it like an Agatha Christie novel where multiple investigators work on the case throughout history, each one bringing clarity, but none discovering the ending. Even the angels longed to understand these things more fully.

Then Jesus came. All the clues converged. The mystery was solved.

### We Have the Answer

And here's our privilege: **we have read the final chapter.** We know how the story ends. We know the Messiah's name. We know he came, suffered, died, rose again, and will return. We possess knowledge that:

- The greatest prophets searched for but never found
- Angels desire to comprehend more deeply
- Millions throughout history longed to understand
- Only we—the believers in Jesus—can fully grasp

This is extraordinary privilege. It's like winning the lottery when you picked a horse at 55-to-1 odds—you knew something no one else did. We know the culmination of all history. We understand the purpose of human existence and the path to salvation.

This knowledge is not meant to make us proud but to humble us with gratitude and move us to share it with others who remain in ignorance.

## Part Two: The Privilege of Holiness

### Called to Be Set Apart

> Therefore, with minds that are alert and fully sober, set your hope on the grace to be brought to you when Jesus Christ is revealed. As obedient children, do not conform to the evil desires you had when you lived in ignorance. But just as he who called you is holy, so be holy in all you do; for it is written: "Be holy, because I am holy." Since you call on a Father who judges each person's work impartially, live out your time as foreigners here in reverent fear. (1 Peter 1:13-17)

Because we now possess true knowledge—minds alert and fully sober—we can no longer live as we did in ignorance. Peter calls us to holiness, which simply means being "set apart" and "different."

Consider what it's like to be visibly foreign in a culture not your own. When I first arrived in Australia thirty years ago, shops closed at 5 p.m. except Thursdays. There was one Chinese takeaway. For Asian groceries, you had to travel to Blacktown or Parramatta. When walking through shopping centers as a foreigner, people would stare. Yet when I encountered fellow Asians, we'd nod in silent recognition—we belonged to a different world.

Now imagine being born in Australia, a third-generation citizen with perfect English, yet people still stare at you as though you're from another planet. That's the spiritual reality Peter describes. We live on earth, yet we're called to live so differently that people wonder what foreign country we're from.

### Living as Contrarians

We are to be people of contrast. We don't assimilate to the native culture around us. As younger believers especially face peer pressure and the desire to fit in, we must resist. For older believers, this may come more naturally—we've weathered social pressure longer. But for all of us, the call is the same: **live differently because you were born different.**

### The Strategic Shortcut

Here's a practical observation: as our culture increasingly distances itself from Christian roots, the world's values and God's values grow increasingly opposed. What once required careful theological discernment now becomes simpler. Listen to what the mainstream media, public education, politicians, and popular culture proclaim on moral, economic, philosophical, environmental, and sexual issues—and do the exact opposite.

I say this without bitterness but with clarity: across nearly every major issue, the world's position stands at odds with biblical truth. When in doubt about an issue you haven't studied, identify what the secular consensus advocates, then take the opposite position. You may not understand why yet, but odds strongly favor you aligning with Scripture's perspective.

### The Cost of Being Different

This privilege comes with a cost. Being holy—being set apart—means standing alone sometimes. It means resisting the current. It means accepting that others may mock or reject you. But we do this knowing we serve a Judge who measures all work impartially, who sees our faithful obedience even when the world doesn't acknowledge it.

## Part Three: The Privilege of Suffering

### Rejoicing Through Trials

> In all this you greatly rejoice, though now for a little while you may have had to suffer grief in all kinds of trials. These have come so that the proven genuineness of your faith—of greater worth than gold, which perishes even though refined by fire—may result in praise, glory and honor when Jesus Christ is revealed. (1 Peter 1:6-7)

Peter's first readers lived under the Roman Empire. Despite Rome's power and security, these early Christians faced sporadic persecution under successive emperors. They had no legal protection, no birthright of safety. Yet notice what Peter says: "In all this you greatly rejoice."

This seems paradoxical. How can we be both sad and joyful simultaneously? The answer lies in perspective. We can grieve today's trials while celebrating their eternal significance. We sorrow in the present but rejoice for tomorrow, knowing that suffering purifies our faith like fire refines gold.

### Faith Without Sight

Many struggle with not experiencing God tangibly—not seeing Jesus, not witnessing miracles, not feeling a supernatural presence. Peter addresses this directly:

> Though you have not seen him, you love him; and even though you do not see him now, you believe in him and are filled with an inexpressible and glorious joy, for you are receiving the end result of your faith, the salvation of your souls. (1 Peter 1:8-9)

Not seeing God is the normal Christian experience, not the exception. Throughout history, faithful believers loved Jesus without seeing him. They believed without sensory confirmation. Yet they experienced inexpressible, glorious joy—not because of what they felt, but because of what they received: salvation itself.

### Serving as a Privilege

In healthy cultures, citizens understand service to their nation as a privilege. Soldiers speak of "the privilege of serving my country." This reflects a biblical perspective: our suffering, our trials, our willingness to be different and stand apart—all of these are privileges. We're privileged to:

- Be on trial for our faith
- Go door-to-door sharing the gospel
- Face persecution for Christ's name
- Serve others through our sacrifice

This knowledge we possess isn't meant to be hoarded. It's meant to be shared so others might escape ignorance. We call others into this same contrarian life—to set themselves apart from the world's moral corruption and live visibly different.

## A Word on Privilege and Culture

### The Misuse of a Term

The idea of "white privilege" requires biblical evaluation. Certainly, advantages exist based on geography, history, and culture. English-speaking nations, built over centuries by people of European descent, naturally reflect their values and preferences. There's nothing inherently wrong with this—every culture shapes its society according to its own values.

If Australia had been built by a different culture, it would reflect those values instead. No one calls it unfair that Mandarin proves essential in China, or that Asian families demonstrate educational advantages rooted in Confucian values emphasizing learning and family stability. These advantages stem from cultural inheritance, not unfair conspiracy.

### The Real Issue

What concerns me more deeply is the direction of the conversation. The term "privilege" as currently used carries shame and accusation rather than gratitude and responsibility. Yet Australia—built on British and Christian foundations—has demonstrated remarkable generosity with its advantages:

- It opened its borders to the world, becoming one of the least homogenous nations on earth
- Its citizens fought wars far from home when no national interest required it (Malaysia, Singapore, Vietnam, two World Wars)
- It established laws, economics, and institutions based on Christian principles of justice and service

These aren't signs of greed but of Christian values—hospitality, sacrifice, other-centeredness—lived out by a people shaped by 1,500 years of Christian influence.

### The Decline We Should Lament

Yet today, white Australians increasingly suffer:

- War veterans are predominantly white
- Homelessness affects primarily white Australians
- Suicide rates among white males exceed other groups
- Birth rates among European Australians have fallen below replacement
- Divorce among older white women creates widespread hardship

These aren't signs of privilege but of decline. As Lee Kuan Yew warned, without its Christian moorings, Australia risks becoming "the poor white trash of Asia."

### A Call to Responsibility

To white Australians: Stop apologizing for your heritage. Return to your Christian roots. Reclaim your identity not with arrogance but with humble service.

To Asian and other minority Christians: We who possess this gospel knowledge must embrace greater responsibility for this nation. We must see decent white Australians not as enemies to be blamed but as mentors and allies in upholding Christian values. We should aspire to leadership roles in government, military, and society. This is our privilege and duty—to take our place alongside those who built this nation and help preserve what's worth preserving.

We must reject the division the world offers and embrace unity in serving something greater than ourselves.

## Part Four: Living as Aliens

### Born of an Imperishable Word

> For you know that it was not with perishable things such as silver or gold that you were redeemed from the empty way of life handed down to you from your ancestors, but with the precious blood of Christ, a lamb without blemish or defect. He was chosen before the creation of the world, but was revealed in these last times for your sake. Through him you believe in God, who raised him from the dead and glorified him, and so your faith and hope are in God. (1 Peter 1:18-21)

Peter reminds us of a profound truth: **we are alien-born.** Not born of this earth but of something eternal—the blood of Christ and the living, enduring word of God.

> For you have been born again, not of perishable seed, but of imperishable, through the living and enduring word of God. For, "All people are like grass, and all their glory is like the flowers of the field; the grass withers and the flowers fall, but the word of the Lord endures forever." And this is the word that was preached to you. (1 Peter 1:23-25)

Everything in this world is temporary—wealth, status, physical beauty, human glory. All fade like grass and flowers. But the word by which we were born endures forever. It sustained the prophets. It will sustain us. It will outlast every earthly kingdom.

### The Word Was, Is, and Always Will Be

The prophets spoke this word in ages past, and it proved true—vindicated by Jesus. This same word lives and speaks to us in the present through Scripture. And this word will continue standing forever into the future, long after every earthly empire crumbles.

Yet we face two attacks on this eternal word:

**Attack One: Denying What the Bible Says**

Modern culture employs a three-step strategy:
1. Proclaim ignorance: "The Bible is so old. Who really knows what this passage means?"
2. Appeal to other authorities: "Studies show..." "My friend who is gay is nice..." "The latest research suggests..."
3. Assert certainty about a new position: "We now know the Bible was wrong about this. Let's overturn centuries of teaching."

The gay marriage debate exemplifies this perfectly. When we face biblical passages we find culturally uncomfortable, we must remember: *Scripture interprets Scripture, not culture. When in doubt, stick to the conservative reading.*

**Attack Two: Adding to What the Bible Says**

While others deny Scripture, some take the opposite approach—filling in gaps with extra-biblical revelations: dreams, visions, "words of knowledge," treasure hunts guided by prophetic impressions.

I understand the desire. Scripture doesn't tell us which university course to pursue, whom to marry, whether to buy an iPad. There are legitimate gaps. But we must resist the temptation to fill them with alleged divine words we cannot verify.

How do we know if a voice we hear is truly God rather than our own desire or peer pressure? The Bible already provides what we need. If it doesn't answer a particular question, perhaps we're not meant to know the answer, or meant to work it out through wise discernment and prayer.

### The Discipline of Scripture

Rather than seeking new words, we need:

- Better study habits and comprehension skills
- A more humble heart willing to hear what God actually says
- A repentant heart that recognizes when we manipulate Scripture to justify our preferred behavior
- Willingness to read the Bible with others—your church leaders actively seek opportunities to study Scripture with people

The Bible is like a precious passport we carry through foreign lands. We're not in heaven; we're in foreign territory. Keep your passport close—physically in church and Bible studies, mentally through regular reading, and spiritually through obedient application.

Some practical suggestions:
- Bring your physical Bible to church gatherings (not just your phone)
- Read in context and take handwritten notes
- Bring a pen and notebook to track your thoughts
- Even bring your Bible to school when possible
- Meet regularly with others to study Scripture

## Part Five: The Character of Aliens

### Do Not Assimilate

> Therefore, prepare your minds for action; be self-controlled; set your hope fully on the grace to be given you when Jesus Christ is revealed. As obedient children, do not conform to the evil desires you had when you lived in ignorance. But just as he who called you is holy, so be holy in all you do; for it is written: "Be holy, because I am holy." (1 Peter 1:13-15)

Aliens don't belong to the earth, so we shouldn't act like the natives. This applies across every area of life:

- We choose our entertainment, reading, and media differently
- We conduct our relationships—romantic, professional, financial—by different standards
- We don't marry those who don't share our faith, not from racial bias but from spiritual necessity
- We dress, speak, and present ourselves with different priorities

Assimilation is hardest for the young. When I was young, my sister and I—only a year apart—took different paths. I remained somewhat strange, hanging with Asian peers, keeping my accent. She assimilated fully—Australian friends, no accent. For young people especially, peer pressure makes fitting in nearly everything. But we must help them see: you're born of different DNA. Act accordingly.

### Work Hard in a Foreign Land

> Since you call on a Father who judges each person's work impartially, live out your time as foreigners here in reverent fear. (1 Peter 1:17)

We live as strangers here, not at home. This calls us to work diligently—not to amass wealth for ourselves but to advance God's kingdom and serve others generously. We're temporary residents in a foreign land, accountable to a Father who judges all work fairly and impartially.

### Hunger for Spiritual Nourishment

> Therefore, rid yourselves of all malice and all deceit, hypocrisy, envy, and slander of every kind. Like newborn babies, crave pure spiritual milk, so that by it you may grow up in your salvation, now that you have tasted that the Lord is good. (1 Peter 2:1-3)

Recently, the world's 7 billionth baby was born. What do all newborns do? They cry for milk—urgently, persistently, without shame.

We who are born again should mirror this hunger. We've tasted that the Lord is good. That taste should create an insatiable appetite for more. This hunger is satisfied through God's living, enduring, imperishable word.

Yet it requires we rid ourselves of malice, deceit, hypocrisy, envy, and slander. These toxins poison our ability to nourish ourselves spiritually. Clean these out. Then feed hungrily on Scripture. There's nothing else that can satisfy the spiritual hunger of the reborn.

---

## Conclusion: The Privilege and Power of Being Alien-Born

We are privileged. Extraordinarily so. We know salvation's answer. We're called to live visibly different lives. We've tasted the Lord's goodness and carry His word—the only eternal thing in a dying world.

This is no reason for shame. It's no reason to apologize. It's a reason to:

- Steward our knowledge with humility and share it generously
- Live set apart with courage, not arrogance
- Serve our communities sacrificially, not selfishly
- Build alliances across cultures united by gospel values
- Hunger continually for deeper understanding of Scripture
- Accept suffering and opposition as the privilege it truly is

We are aliens—born not of this world but of Christ's blood and God's eternal word. Let us live as such, with joy, holiness, and service.
