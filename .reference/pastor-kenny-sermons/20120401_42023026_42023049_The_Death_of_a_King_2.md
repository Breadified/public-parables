# Luke 23:26-49

**Title:** The Death of a King
**Date:** April 1, 2012
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## The King's Dilemma

If you are a king, you enjoy tremendous privileges. You are treated well—surrounded by countless attendants, given the finest food and education, provided with the best of everything this world offers. But we turn now to read about another king.

> **Luke 23:38** An inscription was above Him: THIS IS THE KING OF THE JEWS.

What we find is starkly different from what we'd expect. Jesus is on the cross.

### The Brutality of Crucifixion

> **Luke 23:32-34** Two other criminals were also led away to be executed with Him. When they arrived at the place called The Skull, they crucified Him there, along with the criminals, one on the right and one on the left. And they divided His clothes and cast lots.

Crucifixion was one of history's cruelest forms of execution. The prisoner was first publicly humiliated by being stripped naked. His hands were then nailed to the horizontal wooden beam and his feet to the vertical pole. The cross was hoisted upright and dropped into a socket in the ground. A peg provided some support, but the victim would hang there helplessly—exposed to excruciating physical pain, public mockery, the burning heat of day and the freezing cold of night. The torture could last for days.

After a mock trial, Jesus was sentenced to death. But His crucifixion raises a profound question.

### The Mockery of Kingship

> **Luke 23:35-37** The people stood watching, and even the leaders kept scoffing: "He saved others; let Him save Himself if this is God's Messiah, the Chosen One!" The soldiers also mocked Him. They came offering Him sour wine and said, "If You are the King of the Jews, save Yourself!"

And one of the criminals joined in:

> **Luke 23:39** "Aren't You the Messiah? Save Yourself and us!"

The expectation is clear: if you are truly a king, save yourself. The word "Messiah" means "king" in Hebrew—so they're taunting Him with the very promise His kingdom was supposed to fulfill.

### Jesus Could Have Saved Himself

Jesus possessed incredible power. He could have called upon angels for protection. As the Son of God, He had authority over all creation—power to control the elements themselves. He could have made the nails simply fall away like Wolverine's wounds healing, or conjured a storm to scatter His enemies. Imagine the drama: a superhero rising from the cross, flying before thousands of witnesses. Everyone would have believed. Problem solved, right?

But He didn't.

### The Real Problem Isn't Belief

Why didn't Jesus save Himself with a spectacular display of divine power?

Because the crowd misunderstood what the fundamental problem was. They thought the problem was that people didn't believe in God—as if God were lonely, desperately needing us to believe in Him so He wouldn't feel rejected.

But that's not the problem at all.

The real problem is **sin and the forgiveness of sin.**

Rather than God needing love from us, it is we who desperately need love from God.

## The Criminals Face Reality

One of the criminals hanging beside Jesus had a moment of clarity:

> **Luke 23:41** "We are punished justly, because we're getting back what we deserve for the things we did, but this man has done nothing wrong."

This criminal spoke a universal truth. They are facing punishment for their sins—they are being punished justly for what they've done. And here's the hard truth this represents for all of us:

If we truly believe in justice for everyone, then all of us will face judgment for everything we have done.

### We All Deserve Punishment

If we're honest with ourselves, we know we have done wrong. What we've said to our parents in anger. What we've said about our friends behind their backs. The words we've written online in moments of rage. The things we've watched that we shouldn't have. The theft—even small ones—we've justified in our minds.

We try to move the goalposts, telling ourselves our sin isn't so bad. Or we compare ourselves to others: "At least I'm not like them." But if we stop moving the goalposts, we have to face it—we will break under the weight of true justice.

We deserve punishment.

### The Puzzle of Substitution

Yet here is the central puzzle of the cross: Why is Jesus being punished?

> **Luke 23:41** "We are punished justly, because we're getting back what we deserve for the things we did, but this man has done nothing wrong."

The man next to Him has committed crimes. Jesus has committed none. Yet they are undergoing the same judgment. The same torture. The same death.

**What's going on?**

Jesus is taking the punishment **for us.**

The criminal had asked, "Save Yourself and us!" But that's not how it works. It's not "save yourself AND us." It's "save yourself OR us."

Think of it like this: in the movie *Titanic*, there was only one spot on the floating door. Either Jack or someone else. In *The Hunger Games*, it was either one sister or the other. Both couldn't survive. Someone had to make a choice.

Jesus made that choice. He could not save Himself *because sin had to be punished. Justice had to be done.* To save Himself would have meant leaving us to face that punishment—and we cannot endure it. We would be destroyed.

So He made a choice: **He did not save Himself. He saved us.**

### Love Pays the Price

It is love that pays the debt of sin so that we can be forgiven. Only one Man was truly innocent—sinless from eternity. Only God Himself could pay this price.

And because Jesus did this, the words of the second criminal become the model of true faith:

> **Luke 23:42** "Jesus, remember me when You come into Your kingdom!"

Notice what he's doing: he's acknowledging that **Jesus is King.** He's confessing his own guilt and calling out for help. This is the repentance God desires—not good works that we can boast about, but the humble recognition that we don't deserve to be remembered, and the plea for mercy.

And Jesus' answer is immediate and absolute:

> **Luke 23:43** "I assure you: Today you will be with Me in paradise."

Paradise—the garden, the place of God's presence. Not because of what the thief did. Not because of his good deeds. But because it was **already done for him.**

## The King Resurrected

> **Luke 23:50-56** There was a good and righteous man named Joseph, a member of the Sanhedrin, who had not agreed with their plan and action. He was from Arimathea, a Judean town, and was looking forward to the kingdom of God. He approached Pilate and asked for Jesus' body. Taking it down, he wrapped it in fine linen and placed it in a tomb cut into the rock, where no one had ever been placed. It was preparation day, and the Sabbath was about to begin. The women who had come with Him from Galilee followed along and observed the tomb and how His body was placed. Then they returned and prepared spices and perfumes. And they rested on the Sabbath according to the commandment.

The resurrection account breaks into the narrative with stunning clarity:

> **Luke 24:1-8** On the first day of the week, very early in the morning, they came to the tomb, bringing the spices they had prepared. They found the stone rolled away from the tomb. They went in but did not find the body of the Lord Jesus. While they were perplexed about this, suddenly two men stood by them in dazzling clothes. So the women were terrified and bowed down to the ground. "Why are you looking for the living among the dead?" asked the men. "He is not here, but He has been resurrected! Remember how He spoke to you when He was still in Galilee, saying, 'The Son of Man must be betrayed into the hands of sinful men, be crucified, and rise on the third day'?" And they remembered His words.

The women reported this to the Eleven and the other disciples, but their words seemed like nonsense. They did not believe. Peter, however, ran to the tomb and saw the empty linen cloths for himself. He went home amazed at what had happened.

## The King Teaches

The resurrected Jesus encounters two disciples on the road to Emmaus:

> **Luke 24:13-24** That same day two of them were on their way to a village called Emmaus, which was about seven miles from Jerusalem. Together they were discussing everything that had taken place. And while they were discussing and arguing, Jesus Himself came near and began to walk along with them. But they were prevented from recognizing Him. Then He asked them, "What is this dispute that you're having with each other as you are walking?" And they stopped walking and looked discouraged. The one named Cleopas answered Him, "Are You the only visitor in Jerusalem who doesn't know the things that happened there in these days?" "What things?" He asked them. So they said to Him, "The things concerning Jesus the Nazarene, who was a Prophet powerful in action and speech before God and all the people, and how our chief priests and leaders handed Him over to be sentenced to death, and they crucified Him. But we were hoping that He was the One who was about to redeem Israel. Besides all this, it's the third day since these things happened. Moreover, some women from our group astounded us. They arrived early at the tomb, and when they didn't find His body, they came and reported that they had seen a vision of angels who said He was alive. Some of those who were with us went to the tomb and found it just as the women had said, but they didn't see Him."

### The Scriptures Reinterpreted

Jesus responds with gentle rebuke:

> **Luke 24:25-27** "How unwise and slow you are to believe in your hearts all that the prophets have spoken! Didn't the Messiah have to suffer these things and enter into His glory?" Then beginning with Moses and all the Prophets, He interpreted for them the things concerning Himself in all the Scriptures.

Jesus walked them through the entire Old Testament, showing them that His suffering was never a contradiction to His identity as the Messiah—it was the very fulfillment of what the prophets had written. The cross was not a failure but the climax of God's redemptive plan.

### Recognition in the Breaking of Bread

> **Luke 24:28-31** They came near the village where they were going, and He gave the impression that He was going farther. But they urged Him: "Stay with us, because it's almost evening, and now the day is almost over." So He went in to stay with them. It was as He reclined at the table with them that He took the bread, blessed and broke it, and gave it to them. Then their eyes were opened, and they recognized Him, but He disappeared from their sight.

In that moment of breaking bread—so reminiscent of the Last Supper—they recognized Him. And as suddenly as they understood, He vanished.

> **Luke 24:32** "Weren't our hearts ablaze within us while He was talking with us on the road and explaining the Scriptures to us?"

They felt it—the burning in their hearts as Jesus opened the Scriptures to them. Understanding and recognizing Jesus came not through a spectacular appearance, but through the patient teaching of Scripture.

### Appearances and Peace

Jesus then appears to the disciples in Jerusalem:

> **Luke 24:36-43** "And as they were saying these things, He Himself stood among them. He said to them, 'Peace to you!' But they were startled and terrified and thought they were seeing a ghost. 'Why are you troubled?' He asked them. 'And why do doubts arise in your hearts? Look at My hands and My feet, that it is I Myself! Touch Me and see, because a ghost does not have flesh and bones as you can see I have.' Having said this, He showed them His hands and feet. But while they still were amazed and unbelieving because of their joy, He asked them, 'Do you have anything here to eat?' So they gave Him a piece of broiled fish, and He took it and ate in their presence."

Jesus offers them His hands and feet—the marks of crucifixion. He eats fish before them to demonstrate that He is truly, physically risen. Not a ghost. Not a vision. The same Jesus who died is now alive.

### The Mission Defined

> **Luke 24:44-49** "Then He told them, 'These are My words that I spoke to you while I was still with you—that everything written about Me in the Law of Moses, the Prophets, and the Psalms must be fulfilled.' Then He opened their minds to understand the Scriptures. He also said to them, 'This is what is written: The Messiah would suffer and rise from the dead the third day, and repentance for forgiveness of sins would be proclaimed in His name to all the nations, beginning at Jerusalem. You are witnesses of these things. And look, I am sending you what My Father promised. As for you, stay in the city until you are empowered from on high.'"

Jesus gives them their mission: proclaim repentance and forgiveness of sins to all nations, beginning in Jerusalem. But they are not to go alone. They must wait for the empowerment of the Holy Spirit—"what My Father promised."

> **Luke 24:50-53** "Then He led them out as far as Bethany, and lifting up His hands He blessed them. And while He was blessing them, He left them and was carried up into heaven. After worshiping Him, they returned to Jerusalem with great joy. And they were continually in the temple complex praising God."

He ascends with a blessing. They return to Jerusalem not in despair, but in great joy, waiting for the Spirit's power to carry out the greatest mission ever entrusted to human hands.
