# Luke 10:25-37

**Title:** Love Your Neighbour
**Date:** 11 February 2024 (ARISE)
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Everybody Needs Good Neighbours

The Australian television show *Neighbours*, which ran from 1985 to 2022, featured the memorable lyric: "It's not much of a life without good neighbours." This simple truth resonates deeply. Good neighbours enrich our lives immeasurably—they offer practical help, companionship, and genuine care. Yet the opposite is equally true: poor neighbourly relationships make life miserable, keeping us perpetually on edge rather than at peace in our own homes.

Jesus teaches us today about what it means to truly be a neighbour, and more importantly, to recognise in Him the ultimate neighbour we all desperately need.

---

## 1. Love Your Neighbour (25-28)

### The Initial Question

> 10:25 On one occasion an expert in the law stood up to test Jesus. "Teacher," he asked, "what must I do to inherit eternal life?"

An expert in the law—a professional scholar and scribe of God's laws found in Scripture—approached Jesus with a question designed to test Him. The word "test" (Greek: *peirazō*) carries the weight of temptation, the same word used when Satan tempted Jesus (Luke 4:12). Yet beneath the testing lay a genuine question about how to secure eternal life.

Jesus responded brilliantly by turning the question back:

> 26 "What is written in the Law?" he replied. "How do you read it?"

Essentially, Jesus was saying: "You're the expert—surely you know this already."

### The Summary of the Law

> 27 He answered, "'Love the Lord your God with all your heart and with all your soul and with all your strength and with all your mind'; and, 'Love your neighbor as yourself.'"
>
> 28 "You have answered correctly," Jesus replied. "Do this and you will live."

The lawyer correctly summarised all of God's commands into two foundational principles. This summary is echoed throughout Scripture (Matthew 22:37-40, Romans 13:8-10). All the Law and the Prophets hang upon these two commands.

### Love God (Deuteronomy 6:4-5)

> Hear, O Israel: The Lord our God, the Lord is one. Love the Lord your God with all your heart and with all your soul and with all your strength.

God had rescued Israel from slavery in Egypt. In response, His people were called to love Him completely—with every faculty of their being. This wasn't arbitrary: it was the grateful response of those who had been liberated by divine grace.

### Love Your Neighbour (Leviticus 19:17-18)

> 'Do not hate a fellow Israelite in your heart. Rebuke your neighbor frankly so you will not share in their guilt. 'Do not seek revenge or bear a grudge against anyone among your people, but love your neighbor as yourself. I am the Lord.

When God delivered Israel from the oppressive world of slavery—where survival was brutal, cutthroat, and ruled by fear—He called His people to be radically different. They were to recognise that each person bore God's image and had been rescued by God's hand. Israel was to be a nation marked by love: "I love God" is demonstrated by "I love you."

The phrase "as yourself" sets the standard. We naturally care for our own welfare; we should extend that same care to others. Consider the implications:

- Do you want others to commit adultery against you? Then don't commit adultery.
- Do you want others to murder you or your family? Then don't murder.
- Do you want others to steal from you? Then don't steal.
- Do you want others to envy you, curse you, or think ill of you? Then don't covet.
- Do you want others to lie against you? Then don't bear false witness.

We are to put ourselves in the other person's shoes. What do I consider loving? Apply that standard to how I treat others.

Think practically: if you love spending money on yourself for discretionary purchases, imagine spending equally on others. If you buy yourself a treat, buy one for someone else. We complain about those who litter—let us be people who don't litter out of love for others. We're annoyed by unloving hooligans—let us train our children to be responsible citizens. We dislike neighbours who leave shopping trolleys on our street—let us return our trolleys. If you want good neighbours, you must be a good neighbour.

> "Do this and you will live."

To live this way in the present—with good neighbours—and to live in the future in eternal life with God requires both loving God and loving neighbour. Yet here lies the hidden crisis.

## 2. Who Is My Neighbour? (29-37)

### The Lawyer's Defence

> 29 But he wanted to justify himself, so he asked Jesus, "And who is my neighbor?"

The lawyer, wanting to vindicate himself, pushed back. He asked for boundaries: who exactly is my neighbour? Looking to Leviticus 19:17, where God commands love "among your people," the lawyer could argue that "neighbour" meant Israelites—family, friends, those within his circle. This narrow interpretation would make the command manageable, achievable.

### The Good Samaritan

Jesus answered, not with a definition, but with a parable of extravagant love.

> 30 In reply Jesus said: "A man was going down from Jerusalem to Jericho, when he was attacked by robbers. They stripped him of his clothes, beat him and went away, leaving him half dead.

The journey from Jerusalem (one of Israel's highest points) to Jericho (one of the world's lowest cities, below sea level) was treacherous—a long descent through desolate terrain. On this dangerous road, the man was ambushed. The robbers stripped him of his valuable clothing—in ancient times, garments represented significant wealth. They beat him brutally and left him for dead.

### The Religious Response

> 31 A priest happened to be going down the same road, and when he saw the man, he passed by on the other side. 32 So too, a Levite, when he came to the place and saw him, passed by on the other side.

A priest and a Levite—both religious professionals serving in the temple—encountered the wounded man. Both "saw him." Both chose to pass by on the other side.

Why? Under Mosaic law, touching a dead person or someone with a disease would render one ceremonially unclean (Leviticus 5:3). The consequence was severe: they would need to travel back to Jerusalem, await the allotted time, and offer sacrifices to be restored to ritual purity. So the calculation was straightforward: help this stranger and face inconvenience, danger (the robbers might still be nearby), and ritual defilement—or continue home, rest comfortably, and avoid complication. They chose comfort over compassion.

### The Samaritan's Compassion

> 33 But a Samaritan, as he traveled, came where the man was; and when he saw him, he took pity on him.

Here enters a shocking figure. Samaritans were of mixed ancestry—originally Israelites but ethnically corrupted in Jewish eyes. The two peoples despised each other. The traditional enmity is captured in an ancient saying: "He that eats the bread of the Samaritans is like one that eats the flesh of swine." Yet this Samaritan—this sworn enemy of the Jews—had compassion.

> 34 He went to him and bandaged his wounds, pouring on oil and wine. Then he put the man on his own donkey, brought him to an inn and took care of him.

The Samaritan didn't merely acknowledge the wounded man; he acted with costly sacrifice. He bandaged the wounds with oil and wine—substances with cleansing and soothing properties. He surrendered his own donkey, forcing himself to walk. He brought the man to shelter and nursed him through the night.

> 35 The next day he took out two denarii and gave them to the innkeeper. 'Look after him,' he said, 'and when I return, I will reimburse you for any extra expense you may have.'

Most remarkably, the Samaritan left money—two days' wages—with the innkeeper and promised to pay any additional costs upon his return. He offered open-ended financial responsibility for a stranger, an enemy, someone he would likely never see again.

### The Reversal of the Question

> 36 "Which of these three do you think was a neighbor to the man who fell into the hands of robbers?"
>
> 37 The expert in the law replied, "The one who had mercy on him." Jesus told him, "Go and do likewise."

Notice the brilliant reversal. The lawyer had asked, "Who is my neighbour?"—seeking to identify those he was obligated to love. Jesus reframed the question: "Who *was* a neighbour to the man?"—shifting the focus from identity to character and action.

The lawyer's original question allowed for boundaries and excuses. Jesus' question demanded self-examination: *Are you a neighbour? Will you be a neighbour?*

### Understanding Neighbour

A neighbour is not defined by family ties, shared history, or tribal identity. A neighbour is someone we encounter—someone near us, someone in proximity. The word itself carries this meaning: "near-bour"—near to you.

Neighbours are everywhere:
- On a road where an accident has just occurred—we can stop safely, offer help, call emergency services.
- Outside a home where someone struggles with shopping—we can offer assistance.
- At work when someone spills coffee—we can help clean and offer fresh refreshment.
- On a plane with crying children—we can offer compassion and help.

The barrier isn't ability; it's cost. We fear losing comfort, time, money, or status. Yet the Samaritan demonstrates that these costs pale in comparison to the value of human life and dignity.

Churches and communities become genuine places of belonging not through perfect doctrinal alignment but through practical love. When you're in a new class and all your friends are elsewhere, you have neighbours by proximity—the opportunity to make new friends through consistent care and presence. At church, many are strangers linked only by name tags; yet they are your neighbours. Help carry chairs, fill water, clean bins, introduce yourselves. With time and love, neighbours become friends.

## 3. Love the Lord Your God (36-37)

### The Uncomfortable Truth

Jesus' parable sets an impossibly high standard. The Samaritan descended from Jerusalem to the low point, showed extraordinary compassion to an enemy, sheltered him, nursed him, and committed himself to unlimited financial responsibility. This is what loving your neighbour looks like.

Yet the initial question remains unanswered in a satisfying way: *What must I do to inherit eternal life?*

The lawyer sought a path of works—a checklist of actions that, once completed, would secure eternal life. But the parable exposes the fatal flaw in this approach: the standard is beyond human reach.

> Romans 3:20 Therefore no one will be declared righteous in his sight by observing the law; rather, through the law we become conscious of sin.

We fail the law. We pass by the suffering. We calculate costs instead of showing compassion. We love our own more readily than strangers. We cannot fulfil the law to God's standard—not fully, not consistently, not with the whole-hearted devotion it demands.

### Jesus as the True Neighbour

The parable points beyond itself to Someone Else—Someone who will accomplish what we cannot.

**Jesus is the Neighbour to humanity.**

Just as the Samaritan descended to help the suffering man, Jesus descended from the heavens to the lowest point—to earth, to shame, to death. He showed compassion not to friends but to enemies. He did not "pass by on the other side" but stopped and gave everything.

- He was beaten, opposed, vilified, cursed, framed, and betrayed (Luke 23:16).
- He was stripped of His clothing (Luke 23:34).
- Unlike the half-dead traveller, Jesus was fully dead—He died in our place.
- He bandaged our wounds through His sacrifice; we have an anointing from Jesus (1 John 2:20, 27; 2 Corinthians 1:21).
- He carries our burdens and lifts our anxieties from us (Philippians 4:6).
- He shelters and provides for us with unlimited grace.
- He gives us not just temporary help but eternal life—an inheritance we cannot earn, only receive.

Jesus fulfilled the impossible requirement of the law. He alone loved God with all His heart, soul, mind, and strength. He alone loved His neighbour—indeed, all His enemies—as Himself.

This is why "love your neighbour" isn't merely another moral commandment. It's a call to recognise and trust in the One who perfectly fulfilled the law on our behalf—the true and perfect Neighbour, Jesus Christ Himself.

### The Nature of Inheritance

There is deep theology here. An inheritance cannot be "done"—it cannot be earned or achieved. By definition, an inheritance is received, a gift given by grace. You cannot work for an inheritance; it is given to you.

Jesus says: You cannot do your way to eternal life through obedience to the law. But I have done it. I have fulfilled the law perfectly. Therefore, you can receive eternal life—not as something you've earned, but as an inheritance, a gift of grace through faith in Me.

The law reveals our need. Jesus provides the answer.

## Everybody Needs *the* Good Neighbour

We live in a society blessed with good neighbours—people shaped by Judeo-Christian values, people who embody the Ten Commandments in practical ways. Such neighbours are a genuine gift.

But we need more than good neighbours; we need *the* Good Neighbour—the One who has already done for us what we cannot do for ourselves.

**Love the Lord your God with all your heart and with all your soul and with all your mind.**

This is an unusual response to God. Unlike other religions that emphasise slavish obedience, God desires love—mutual, reciprocal love. After rescuing Israel from slavery, God didn't demand servitude; He offered a covenant more like marriage than a master-servant relationship.

The master-slave mindset breeds anxiety: What must I do? Have I done enough? It's never finished. But Jesus says: The work is finished. I have done it. Inherit eternal life—receive it as a gift.

**Love your neighbour as yourself.**

As you experience God's love and grace, let it overflow into love for others. The two commands are inseparable.

Do not cross to the other side. When you see Jesus—in His Word, in His offer of grace, in the testimony of what He's done—don't brush Him off. Don't delay: "I'll think about Him when exams are over. I'll consider Him when university is finished. I'll listen when work is complete. I'll serve Him when I'm free."

Don't cross to the other side. He has done everything for you. He deserves your hearing, your trust, your love, and your service.
