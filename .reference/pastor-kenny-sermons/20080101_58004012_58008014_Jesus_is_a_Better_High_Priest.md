# Hebrews 4:12-8:14

**Title:** Jesus is a Better High Priest
**Date:** 18 January 2008
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Can I Trust Him?

In business, we often ask two fundamental questions. The Western approach emphasizes contract and law: Is the contract good? Can I trust the legal system to enforce it? The Asian approach emphasizes relationship and networks: Can I trust this person? Do we have family connections?

Both matter. Without enforceability, agreements become too easy to break. Without trust, no law can truly protect you. We see this in financial dealings, the choice of tutors, even something as simple as buying on eBay.

But when it comes to Jesus and doing business with God, we must ask the same questions: Is the contract good? Can we trust Him? Can we actually do business with God?

The writer of Hebrews gives us a resounding yes. In Hebrews 4:14-16, he assures us:

> Therefore, since we have a great high priest who has gone through the heavens, Jesus the Son of God, let us hold firmly to the faith we profess. For we do not have a high priest who is unable to sympathize with our weaknesses, but we have one who has been tempted in every way, just as we are--yet was without sin. Let us then approach the throne of grace with confidence, so that we may receive mercy and find grace to help us in our time of need.

Yes, you can trust Him. But why? The writer's answer is striking: because He is a high priest.

## The Priesthood of Jesus (4:14-16)

For those new to Scripture, the concept of Jesus as a priest might seem unusual. Outside the book of Hebrews, it's rarely mentioned explicitly. We see hints elsewhere—references to sacrifice, to Jesus praying for His disciples, to Him serving as an advocate—but the word "priest" doesn't explicitly label Jesus in most of the New Testament.

Yet Hebrews makes this its central theme. In fact, the entire book builds toward understanding Jesus' priesthood. We've already encountered two previews:

> For this reason he had to be made like his brothers in every way, in order that he might become a merciful and faithful high priest in service to God. (Hebrews 2:17)

> Therefore, holy brothers, who share in the heavenly calling, fix your thoughts on Jesus, the apostle and high priest whom we confess. (Hebrews 3:1)

Before we can understand what makes Jesus a "better" priest, we need to understand what the Old Testament priesthood looked like.

## 1. The Priesthood of Levi

The Old Testament describes the Levitical priesthood in detail, especially in the book of Leviticus. There we find elaborate laws and regulations for approaching God—offerings, food laws, cleanliness requirements—and the specific role of priests in mediating between God and the people.

What made a high priest qualified for this role? The writer summarizes in Hebrews 5:1:

> Every high priest is selected from among men and is appointed to represent them in matters related to God, to offer gifts and sacrifices for sins. He is able to deal gently with those who are ignorant and are going astray, since he himself is subject to weakness.

### i. Priests Were Representatives

A priest's fundamental qualification was that he came from among the people. He had to represent them. This made sense: he knew their weaknesses because he shared them. He could sympathize and deal gently with their failures.

Think of it like representation in government. Your member of parliament should live in your suburb, understand your community, share your concerns. A representative out of touch with those they represent cannot truly represent them.

But here's the fatal flaw in this arrangement: the same weakness that qualified him to represent the people also disqualified him from helping them. Verse 3 reveals the problem:

> This is why he has to offer sacrifices for his own sins, as well as for the sins of the people.

The priest himself was trapped in sin. He was trying to cleanse others while needing cleansing himself. It's like a blind opthamologist trying to surgically remove a cataract from a patient's eye. You appreciate their qualifications and their care, but ultimately, they cannot help you.

### ii. Priests Were Appointed

The second requirement was that priests had to be appointed by God. No one could simply take this honor upon themselves:

> No one takes this honor upon himself; he must be called by God, just as Aaron was. (Hebrews 5:4)

### iii. Jesus Fits Both Criteria—But Represents Something Better

Jesus meets both qualifications for priesthood. First, He was appointed by God:

> So Christ also did not take upon himself the glory of becoming a high priest. But God said to him, "You are my Son; today I have become your Father." And he says in another place, "You are a priest forever, in the order of Melchizedek." (Hebrews 5:5-6)

Second, Jesus was a true representative. In Hebrews 5:7, we see Him offering up prayers and petitions with loud cries and tears. These prayers weren't merely for Himself—asking that the cup of wrath be removed. Rather, He was interceding for us, calling out to God on our behalf. And verse 9 clarifies the result: He became the source of eternal salvation for all who obey Him.

> During the days of Jesus' life on earth, he offered up prayers and petitions with loud cries and tears to the one who could save him from death, and he was heard because of his reverent submission. Although he was a son, he learned obedience from what he suffered and, once made perfect, he became the source of eternal salvation for all who obey him and was designated by God to be high priest in the order of Melchizedek. (Hebrews 5:7-10)

Here's what makes Jesus different: He represents us without being trapped in our problems. He is our brother. He can sympathize with our weaknesses without being enslaved to sin.

Yet there's something puzzling here. The writer just explained that Jesus meets all the criteria of the Levitical priesthood, then immediately says He's a priest in the order of Melchizedek, not Levi. Why?

Because the Levitical system was incomplete. Hebrews 7:18-19 tells us:

> The former regulation is set aside because it was weak and useless (for the law made nothing perfect), and a better hope is introduced, by which we draw near to God.

The era of the Levitical priesthood has ended. Even though 1 Peter speaks of Christians as a nation of priests, and Romans speaks of offering sacrifices, these aren't calls to reinstitute the old priesthood. They're using the imagery of dedication and holiness.

Today, Anglican ministers are not priests. I am not a priest. The Lord's Supper is not a sacrifice. We don't have a special person in a special place offering special things to God. Medieval Christianity confused the building (thinking it was a temple), the meal (thinking it was a sacrifice), and the minister (thinking he was a priest mediating between people and God). But that's not how it works.

The Lord's Supper is an ancient tradition—a way to remember Jesus and look out for one another based on His words at His final meal. I'm a teacher, not a priest. For the Levitical priesthood is finished.

## Warning: Hard Teaching Ahead (5:11-6:12)

At this point, the writer pauses. He knows he's about to introduce something difficult, and he wants to prepare his readers for maturity. He gives them a gentle warning:

> We have much to say about this, but it is hard to explain because you are slow to learn. In fact, though by this time you ought to be teachers, you need someone to teach you the elementary truths of God's word all over again. You need milk, not solid food! Anyone who lives on milk, being still an infant, is not acquainted with the teaching about righteousness. But solid food is for the mature, who by constant use have trained themselves to distinguish good from evil. (Hebrews 5:11-14)

He calls them to maturity:

> Therefore let us leave the elementary teachings about Christ and go on to maturity. (Hebrews 6:1)

What does maturity look like? It means moving beyond the basics—repentance, faith, baptism, resurrection, and judgment—to deeper understanding. It means training yourself to discern good from evil through constant practice with God's word.

The writer wants to push his readers forward. He wants the congregation to grow in spiritual maturity through deeper knowledge of Scripture. Shallow learning produces shallow Christians. As he says, sermonettes produce Christianettes.

This is why he now introduces Melchizedek—not to confuse, but to mature his readers into a fuller understanding of who Jesus is.

## 2. The Priesthood of Melchizedek

Melchizedek appears suddenly in Genesis 14. He's a mysterious figure—a king and priest who blesses Abraham, receives a tenth of his plunder, and then vanishes from history. Why would the writer of Hebrews reach back to this obscure figure?

Because God's blessing of Abraham through Melchizedek reveals a priesthood that existed before the Levitical system and will continue after it.

### i. God Promised to Bless Abraham Through Melchizedek

The writer takes us back to God's covenant with Abraham:

> When God made his promise to Abraham, since there was no one greater for him to swear by, he swore by himself, saying, "I will surely bless you and give you many descendants." And so after waiting patiently, Abraham received what was promised. (Hebrews 6:13-15)

Then, in Hebrews 7:1-3, the writer explains how this promise was mediated:

> This Melchizedek was king of Salem and priest of God Most High. He met Abraham returning from the defeat of the kings and blessed him, and Abraham gave him a tenth of everything. First, his name means "king of righteousness"; then also, "king of Salem" means "king of peace." Without father or mother, without genealogy, without beginning of days or end of life, like the Son of God he remains a priest forever.

Notice the titles: King of Righteousness, King of Peace. Notice his permanence: unlike mortal priests, he has no genealogy, no beginning or end of days. He remains a priest forever.

What's more, Melchizedek was greater than Abraham—and therefore greater than Levi, who descended from Abraham:

> Just think how great he was: Even the patriarch Abraham gave him a tenth of the plunder! Now the law requires the descendants of Levi who become priests to collect a tenth from the people--that is, their brothers--even though their brothers are descended from Abraham. This man, however, did not trace his descent from Levi, yet he collected a tenth from Abraham and blessed him who had the promises. And without doubt the lesser person is blessed by the greater. (Hebrews 7:4-7)

The point is stunning: Melchizedek represents a priesthood older and greater than the Levitical system. God blessed Abraham through Melchizedek, not through Levi. And since Levi descended from Abraham, Levi himself paid homage to Melchizedek through his ancestor.

So why was a different priest needed? Because:

> If perfection could have been attained through the Levitical priesthood (for on the basis of it the law was given to the people), why was there still need for another priest to come--one in the order of Melchizedek, not in the order of Aaron? For when there is a change of the priesthood, there must also be a change of the law. He of whom these things are said belonged to a different tribe, and no one from that tribe has ever served at the altar. For it is clear that our Lord descended from Judah, and in regard to that tribe Moses said nothing about priests. (Hebrews 7:11-14)

Jesus came from Judah, not Levi. He didn't inherit priesthood through genealogy. Instead:

> He has become a priest not on the basis of a regulation as to his ancestry but on the basis of the power of an indestructible life. For it is declared: "You are a priest forever, in the order of Melchizedek." (Hebrews 7:16-17)

His priesthood rests not on a legal regulation, but on the power of an indestructible life.

### ii. God Swore an Oath to David

Beyond the promise to Abraham, God made an additional oath regarding the Messiah. This oath secured the arrangement:

> Men swear by someone greater than themselves, and the oath confirms what is said and puts an end to all argument. Because God wanted to make the unchanging nature of his purpose very clear to the heirs of what was promised, he confirmed it with an oath. (Hebrews 6:16-17)

What is this oath? It comes from Psalm 110:4, and the writer quotes it:

> And it was not without an oath! Others became priests without any oath, but he became a priest with an oath when God said to him: "The Lord has sworn and will not change his mind: 'You are a priest forever.'" (Hebrews 7:20-21)

Because of this oath, Jesus becomes the guarantee of a better covenant.

### Why This Matters: Real Hope

Both the promise to Abraham and the oath to David are unchangeable. And because God cannot lie, we who have fled to take hold of the hope offered to us have solid ground beneath our feet:

> God did this so that, by two unchangeable things (the promise and the oath) in which it is impossible for God to lie, we who have fled to take hold of the hope offered to us may be greatly encouraged. We have this hope as an anchor for the soul, firm and secure. It enters the inner sanctuary behind the curtain, where Jesus, who went before us, has entered on our behalf. He has become a high priest forever, in the order of Melchizedek. (Hebrews 6:18-20)

If God promised to bless the nations through Melchizedek, and if God swore an oath to place His blessing through Melchizedek, and if Jesus is the Melchizedek priest, then we have unshakeable hope. We can trust Him.

## The Permanence of Jesus' Priesthood

Because Jesus lives forever, His priesthood never ends:

> Now there have been many of those priests, since death prevented them from continuing in office; but because Jesus lives forever, he has a permanent priesthood. Therefore he is able to save completely those who come to God through him, because he always lives to intercede for them. (Hebrews 7:23-25)

Unlike the Levitical priests who offered sacrifices daily—first for their own sins, then for the people's sins—Jesus offered one perfect sacrifice for all time:

> Such a high priest meets our need--one who is holy, blameless, pure, set apart from sinners, exalted above the heavens. Unlike the other high priests, he does not need to offer sacrifices day after day, first for his own sins, and then for the sins of the people. He sacrificed for their sins once for all when he offered himself. For the law appoints as high priests men who are weak; but the oath, which came after the law, appointed the Son, who has been made perfect forever. (Hebrews 7:26-28)

## The Main Point

> The point of what we are saying is this: We do have such a high priest, who sat down at the right hand of the throne of the Majesty in heaven, and who serves in the sanctuary, the true tabernacle set up by the Lord, not by man. (Hebrews 8:1-2)

Jesus serves in the true sanctuary—the heavenly one—of which the earthly tabernacle was only a shadow:

> Every high priest is appointed to offer both gifts and sacrifices, and so it was necessary for this one also to have something to offer. If he were on earth, he would not be a priest, for there are already men who offer the gifts prescribed by the law. They serve at a sanctuary that is a copy and shadow of what is in heaven. This is why Moses was warned when he was about to build the tabernacle: "See to it that you make everything according to the pattern shown you on the mountain." But the ministry Jesus has received is as superior to theirs as the covenant of which he is mediator is superior to the old one, and it is founded on better promises. (Hebrews 8:3-6)

## The New Covenant

With Jesus as our high priest, a new covenant replaces the old:

> For if there had been nothing wrong with that first covenant, no place would have been sought for another. But God found fault with the people and said: "The time is coming, declares the Lord, when I will make a new covenant with the house of Israel and with the house of Judah. It will not be like the covenant I made with their forefathers when I took them by the hand to lead them out of Egypt, because they did not remain faithful to my covenant, and I turned away from them, declares the Lord. This is the covenant I will make with the house of Israel after that time, declares the Lord. I will put my laws in their minds and write them on their hearts. I will be their God, and they will be my people. No longer will a man teach his neighbor, or a man his brother, saying, 'Know the Lord,' because they will all know me, from the least of them to the greatest. For I will forgive their wickedness and will remember their sins no more." (Hebrews 8:7-12)

By calling this covenant "new," the old one becomes obsolete. What is obsolete and aging will soon disappear.

## Why Can You Trust Jesus?

I began with a question: Can we trust Jesus? How do we know we can do business with God?

The answer lies in His priesthood. We have a man representing us in the heavens. We have someone on the inside. It's like having a trusted friend as a surgeon during your cancer operation—someone who understands the system from within and advocates for you.

Both the Western emphasis on contract and the Asian emphasis on relationship find their answer in Jesus:

- **The contractual side** is covered. God made promises and swore oaths. God does not lie. His word is unchangeable.
- **The relational side** is covered. We have a man inside—our brother, our representative—who genuinely sympathizes with our weaknesses.

We have exactly what we need: a high priest forever, in the order of Melchizedek. You can trust Him to bring you to God. You can trust Him completely.
