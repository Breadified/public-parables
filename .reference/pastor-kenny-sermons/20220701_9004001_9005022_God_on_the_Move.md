# 1 Samuel 4:1-5:22

**Title:** God on the Move

**Date:** 3 July 2022

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Everyone on the Move

It's holiday season, and people are moving everywhere—some heading to the snow, others traveling overseas. The world itself seems to be in motion. The latest census results reveal Australia is changing too, with new populations arriving. But more importantly, today we discover how God is also on the move.

We've been reading through 1 Samuel, a long and dense book. The narrative has zoomed out from Samuel's birth to encompass all of Israel, and we're about to see what happens when God's people try to domesticate Him.

---

## Moving God: The Battle at Aphek (4:1-22)

> Now the Israelites went out to fight against the Philistines. The Israelites camped at Ebenezer, and the Philistines at Aphek. The Philistines deployed their forces to meet Israel, and as the battle spread, Israel was defeated by the Philistines, who killed about four thousand of them on the battlefield.

This was the time of the judges—a period when the twelve tribes were scattered and decentralized. Each tribe had been given the task of cleansing the land, populating it, and conquering it. Israel's mission was to drive out evil.

After their initial defeat, the elders asked themselves the right question: "Why did the Lord bring defeat on us today before the Philistines?" But then they came up with their own solution rather than truly wrestling with the answer.

> When the soldiers returned to camp, the elders of Israel asked, "Why did the Lord bring defeat on us today before the Philistines? Let us bring the ark of the Lord's covenant from Shiloh, so that he may go with us and save us from the hand of our enemies."

Their logic was sound—or so they thought. The Ark of the Covenant, constructed during the Exodus, was the place where God dwelled. If God was in the Ark, and they carried the Ark into battle, then surely God would fight for them and defeat their enemies.

But this reasoning reveals a dangerous assumption: that they could force God to be on their side. They believed if they possessed the Ark, God would be obligated to grant them victory.

> When the ark of the Lord's covenant came into the camp, all Israel raised such a great shout that the ground shook.

The moral boost was immediate. But something remarkable happened—even the Philistines understood the significance of what they'd heard.

> Hearing the uproar, the Philistines asked, "What's all this shouting in the Hebrew camp?" When they learned that the ark of the Lord had come into the camp, the Philistines were afraid. "A god has come into the camp," they said. "Oh no! Nothing like this has happened before. We're doomed! Who will deliver us from the hand of these mighty gods? They are the gods who struck the Egyptians with all kinds of plagues in the wilderness."

The Philistines knew the reputation of Israel's God—the God who had devastated Egypt with plagues. Even they recognized this as a moment when an overwhelming force had entered the battle.

> So the Philistines fought, and the Israelites were defeated and every man fled to his tent. The slaughter was very great; Israel lost thirty thousand foot soldiers. The ark of God was captured, and Eli's two sons, Hophni and Phinehas, died.

The result was devastation beyond expectation. After losing four thousand soldiers in the first battle, Israel now suffered thirty thousand casualties—nearly ten times as many. The Ark was captured. The elders' plan had catastrophically failed.

### Why Did This Happen?

The Israelites never properly answered the question they themselves posed. The real answer lies in their spiritual condition. Judges 2:18-19 reveals the pattern of their age:

> Whenever the Lord raised up a judge for them, he was with the judge and saved them out of the hands of their enemies as long as the judge lived; for the Lord relented because of their groaning under those who oppressed and afflicted them. But when the judge died, the people returned to ways even more corrupt than those of their ancestors.

The problem was sin. And 1 Samuel 2:30 reminds us of God's covenant principle: "For those who honor me I will honor."

Here's the critical insight: when Israel sinned, the conflict was no longer between God and their enemies. Israel itself had become the enemy. God cannot be coerced into fighting on the side of the wicked. God stands on His own side. When His people turn from Him, He remains opposed to their rebellion, regardless of what religious symbols they carry into battle.

They attempted to domesticate God—to put Him in a jar, bind Him to a formula, treat Him like a talisman to carry into their own wars. But God cannot be domesticated. He is not obligated to bless disobedience.

### The Messenger Brings News (4:12-22)

> A Benjamite ran from the battle line and went to Shiloh with his clothes torn and dust on his head. When he arrived, there was Eli sitting on his chair by the side of the road, watching, because his heart feared for the ark of God.

The messenger's appearance—torn clothes and dust—signaled both the devastation of battle and the mourning that would follow. Eli, now ninety-eight years old and blind, waited anxiously for news.

> "I have just come from the battle line; I fled from it this very day," the man said. "What happened, my son?"

> The man who brought the news replied, "Israel fled before the Philistines, and the army has suffered great losses. Also your two sons, Hophni and Phinehas, are dead, and the ark of God has been captured."

When Eli heard that the Ark had been captured, he fell backward off his chair and broke his neck. He died—an old man, and he was heavy. Significantly, Scripture tells us that Eli had become heavy by fattening himself on the offerings meant for God (2:29). His physical heaviness reflected his spiritual condition. He had led Israel for forty years, yet in the end, he fell and died when he heard that God's glory had departed.

But the tragedy continued:

> His daughter-in-law, the wife of Phinehas, was pregnant and near the time of delivery. When she heard the news that the ark of God had been captured and that her father-in-law and her husband were dead, she went into labor and gave birth, but was overcome by her labor pains. As she was dying, the women attending her said, "Don't despair; you have given birth to a son." But she did not respond or pay any attention.

> She named the boy Ichabod, saying, "The Glory has departed from Israel"—because of the capture of the ark of God and the deaths of her father-in-law and her husband.

The word "glory" here carries profound weight. In Hebrew, it connects to the concept of heaviness—the presence, weightiness, honor, and importance of God. It suggests the magnificent weight of God's throne room, filled with His presence. But now, with her dying breath, she declared that this glory had departed from Israel.

This wasn't merely political defeat or military loss. It was spiritual abandonment—the ultimate judgment. God had departed from the house of Eli, fulfilling the prophecy He had spoken in 1 Samuel 2:27-36. The deaths of Eli's sons, Eli himself, and now the mother bearing witness to judgment—all of it unfolded as God had declared.

For believers, this is a serious warning: to resist God's will, to attempt to domesticate Him for our own purposes, invites His departure. As Jesus said in Matthew 7:22-23, "Many will say to Me on that day, 'Lord, Lord, did we not prophesy in Your name, and in Your name drive out demons and perform many miracles?' Then I will tell them plainly, 'I never knew you; depart from Me, you workers of lawlessness!'"

### The Temptation to Domesticate God

We must resist the temptation to domesticate God—to contain Him within our plans and make Him serve our vision for victory. This doesn't mean trying to control His movement or force His hand to bless our disobedience.

Consider how many people throughout history have made this mistake. Nations invoke God's name for their wars. We create formulas and systems, thinking if we just do this right, God must act on our behalf. But God is not bound by our logic or our religious practices.

Even in modern conflicts, some declare that God is on their nation's side. But God is not on Russia's side. God is not on Ukraine's side. These are wars of powerful men. God stands apart from human ambition and violence. His will is sovereignty—mysterious and sometimes inscrutable. But His revealed will is clear: He destroys evil. We live in a fallen world where innocent people suffer and the wicked sometimes prosper. But ultimately, God will judge righteousness and wickedness.

The deepest error is to fashion God into an idol—a powerless statue we can carry with us wherever we go. When we move houses, we bring Him along. When we travel overseas, we slip Him into our luggage. But this reduces the living God to mere material, stripped of His infinite power and authority.

## God on the Move: The Ark Among the Philistines (5:1-12)

After capturing the Ark, the Philistines took it to Ashdod and placed it in the temple of Dagon, one of the Canaanite gods.

> After the Philistines had captured the ark of God, they took it from Ebenezer to Ashdod. Then they carried the ark into Dagon's temple and set it beside Dagon.

Dagon appears to have been a non-fish deity (though some confuse the name with the Hebrew word *dag*, meaning fish). The Philistines were confident: they had captured Israel's God and placed Him alongside their own god in their temple. "You are my god now," they seemed to declare.

> When the people of Ashdod rose early the next day, there was Dagon, fallen on his face on the ground before the ark of the Lord! They took Dagon and put him back in his place.

The next morning brought a shock. Dagon had fallen on his face before the Ark. They restored him, thinking perhaps it was an accident.

> But the following morning when they rose, there was Dagon, fallen on his face on the ground before the ark of the Lord! His head and hands had been broken off and were lying on the threshold; only his body remained.

This time, Dagon couldn't stand. His head and hands were severed. Only his torso remained. The Philistines had to establish a custom: from that day forward, neither the priests of Dagon nor anyone else entering his temple would step on the threshold, out of superstitious reverence for what had happened there.

Dagon becomes a picture of domesticated gods. He cannot move. He must be lifted up by his worshippers, only to fall again. He cannot protect himself or even stand. Dagon is powerless.

By contrast, consider idols in religions throughout history. The fastest-growing religion in Australia is Hinduism, which has expanded by over fifty percent. It encompasses thousands of gods. Yet these are ultimately powerless statues—pieces of material that cannot act, cannot move, cannot save.

### The God Who Moves

Our God is entirely different. He is not confined. He is not bound to one place like Dagon. He moves.

> The Lord's hand was heavy on the people of Ashdod and its vicinity; he brought devastation on them and afflicted them with tumors. When the people of Ashdod saw what was happening, they said, "The ark of the god of Israel must not stay here with us, because his hand is heavy on us and on Dagon our god."

The word "heavy" here refers to God's glory—His presence and power pressing down upon them with judgment. Though God's glory had departed from Israel, His hand now rested heavily upon the Philistines.

> So they called together all the rulers of the Philistines and asked them, "What shall we do with the ark of the god of Israel?" They answered, "Have the ark of the god of Israel moved to Gath." So they moved the ark of the God of Israel.

> But after they had moved it, the Lord's hand was against that city, throwing it into a great panic. He afflicted the people of the city, both young and old, with an outbreak of tumors. So they sent the ark of God to Ekron.

The Ark moved from city to city—Ashdod, then Gath, then Ekron. Wherever it went, God's judgment followed. The Philistines couldn't escape it. They couldn't contain it. They couldn't negotiate with it. God moved, and His power went with Him.

> As the ark of God was entering Ekron, the people of Ekron cried out, "They have brought the ark of the god of Israel around to us to kill us and our people." So they called together all the rulers of the Philistines and said, "Send the ark of the god of Israel away; let it go back to its own place, or it will kill us and our people." For death had filled the city with panic; God's hand was very heavy on it.

Finally, they sent it back to Israel. And Scripture later tells us of the guilt offerings they sent with it—gold tumors and gold rats, one for each Philistine city—a desperate attempt to appease the God they could not control.

God is a God who moves and initiates. In the Exodus, the angel of God moved before the camp of Israel, then relocated behind them to protect them from Pharaoh's pursuing army. The pillar of cloud went before them by day, and the pillar of fire led them by night. Even when God restricted Himself to the Ark of the Covenant, He did not stop moving.

> The Lord was going before them in a pillar of cloud by day to lead them on the way, and in a pillar of fire by night to give them light, that they might travel by day and by night.

God does not need any human army to accomplish His will. He is quite capable of managing His own affairs and advancing His purposes. The Philistines learned this the hard way.

### Who Moves What?

Here is the profound reversal: God doesn't move because we move. We move because God moves first. As Paul wrote in Acts 17:28:

> For in him we live and move and have our being.

Our very capacity to move, to act, to exist comes from God's being. We are His offspring, sustained by His power.

God's greatest movement from our perspective was Jesus coming to earth. The Son of God descended from heaven not to do His own will but the will of the Father who sent Him. He came to save us, to accomplish redemption on our behalf.

> For I have come down from heaven not to do my will but to do the will of him who sent me.

We are called to move around God's movements, not the other way around. We adjust ourselves to His will. This is where many of us stumble: we fit God around our lives rather than fitting our lives around God's will.

It seems logical that God, being constant and unchanging, would remain while we change. But the error is thinking that means we can simply do what we want and God will remain available to us, like a portable idol we carry wherever we go. That's the Israelites' mistake repeated in a different form.

Instead, we are called to be a people becoming increasingly like Jesus. Given that we began as sinners with a long way to go, we must keep moving, keep following His will. We cannot domesticate God. We must follow where He leads.
