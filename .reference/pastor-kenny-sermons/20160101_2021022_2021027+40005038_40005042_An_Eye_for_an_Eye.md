**Title:** An Eye for an Eye

**Date:** 10 January 2016

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction

The phrase "an eye for an eye" has become infamous in popular culture as a justification for revenge and bloodthirsty retaliation. Yet this biblical principle, properly understood, stands as one of the most important humanitarian advances in human history. Today we examine what this law actually means, how Jesus fulfills and reinterprets it, and what it reveals about God's justice and mercy.

---

## Justice is Retributive

### The Foundation of Accountability

> 22 "If people are fighting and hit a pregnant woman and she gives birth prematurely but there is no serious injury, the offender must be fined whatever the woman's husband demands and the court allows.
>
> 23 But if there is serious injury, you are to take life for life, 24 eye for eye, tooth for tooth, hand for hand, foot for foot, 25 burn for burn, wound for wound, bruise for bruise.
>
> 26 "An owner who hits a male or female slave in the eye and destroys it must let the slave go free to compensate for the eye. 27 And an owner who knocks out the tooth of a male or female slave must let the slave go free to compensate for the tooth.

The essence of "an eye for an eye" is simple: when a crime creates a moral injury, a debt is created, and that debt must be repaid. Justice is retributive—a measured response to wrongdoing.

We understand this intuitively. If someone purposefully scratches your car, should they simply get away with it? If a neighbor steals your child's bicycle, are you content to let it go? No. A moral injury demands accountability.

Without retributive justice, might makes right. The powerful decide what is just, and the weak have no protection. Justice must be retributive, or society descends into lawlessness.

### Proportional, Not Excessive

A crucial feature of this law: the punishment must be *proportional* to the crime. The law specifies eye for eye, tooth for tooth—not two eyes for an eye, not eye for a tooth, not hand for a foot.

**This exactness serves two purposes:**

**First, it prevents escalating cycles of vengeance.** In ancient Palestine, if someone stole one sheep, the "manly" response was to kill five of his cattle. If someone damaged your crops, you might burn down his entire field. The eye-for-an-eye principle stopped this escalation cold: the punishment must match the harm, no more.

Even today, we see societies without proportional justice descend into barbarism. In some cultures, drawing a cartoon costs people their lives. In others, a daughter who rejects an arranged marriage faces an honor killing. In Iran, a man blinded another with acid; the state responded by gouging out his eyes while he slept. This is the horror that results when proportionality disappears.

**Second, proportionality is merciful to the offender.** When someone wrongs us, emotion fills us. We want to "teach them a lesson." But the law restricts excessive vengeance. You may feel like demanding ten times repayment for a one-dollar theft, but the law says: no, only one dollar. The perpetrator receives mercy through the limits placed on justice.

### Justice for All, Without Exception

> 26 "An owner who hits a male or female slave in the eye and destroys it must let the slave go free to compensate for the eye. 27 And an owner who knocks out the tooth of a male or female slave must let the slave go free to compensate for the tooth.

Notice the radical implication: even though there is a power disparity between master and slave, justice is equally available. The slave has the same protection as the master. This is equality before the law—a revolutionary concept in the ancient world.

Justice cannot be corrupted by wealth, status, or popularity. It is blind to these things. In countries not ruled by Hebrew law, the rich and corrupt escape consequence while the poor face harsh punishment. The Western legal system, shaped by biblical principles, remains one of the few where honest business can flourish because justice is theoretically available to all.

## The Original Intent: Mercy, Not Vengeance

### How the Law Was Misused

Into a context where justice had become distorted, Jesus speaks in Matthew 5:

> "You have heard that it was said, 'Eye for eye, and tooth for tooth.' But I tell you, do not resist an evil person. If anyone slaps you on the right cheek, turn to them the other cheek also. And if anyone wants to sue you and take your shirt, hand over your coat as well. If anyone forces you to go one mile, go with them two miles. Give to the one who asks you, and do not turn away from the one who wants to borrow from you."

Jesus is not contradicting the law but restoring its original purpose. The Pharisees had weaponized "an eye for an eye" to justify personal revenge. They had forgotten that the law's purpose was to limit retribution, not encourage it.

When Jesus says "do not resist," he likely does not mean "never resist evil." Scripture elsewhere commands us to resist the devil, to be on guard against evil, to put on the armor of God. Rather, Jesus means: do not resist evil *by evil means*.

If we interpret "an eye for an eye" literally and personally—if someone rapes you, rape them back; if someone kills your child accidentally, kill theirs—we create not justice but barbarism. We drag society into depravity.

### When to Pursue Justice, When to Let Go

Justice on earth is always an approximation. How do you calculate the full value of a life, of lost opportunities, of trauma? Even material damages are difficult to assess precisely. A car hit in an accident may be repaired, but it will never be quite the same.

There is wisdom in distinguishing between major and minor offenses:

**Major injustices demand justice.** Real crimes—murder, theft, assault—require court intervention. These cannot simply be ignored.

**Minor offenses may be released.** Daily life between sinners involves constant small injuries: insults, offensive jokes, accidental bumps, scratched cars, broken items. If we litigate every offense, it becomes impossible to live together. We would end up doing exactly what "an eye for an eye" was meant to prevent—keeping score, calculating every wrong, waiting for our moment of revenge.

The examples Jesus gives point to personal slights:
- A backhanded slap (an insult, not serious injury)
- A lawsuit over your shirt (a minor material loss)
- Forced labor for a mile (oppressive, but temporary)

In these situations, there is wisdom in letting go. But this is personal mercy, not abandonment of justice. Real crimes still demand response.

## God's Justice and God's Mercy

### The Nature of Divine Retribution

God, as creator and lawgiver, expects his laws to be obeyed. These laws are not arbitrary but designed for the good of humanity. Since each human is made in God's image, a crime between humans is also a crime against God.

For God to be just, he must punish proportionally. What is the punishment for crime against your creator—the one who gave you everything, whom you collectively rejected and killed? Not merely breaking a rule, but rebellion itself.

The punishment is eternal death—infinite punishment because the crime is infinitely evil. Life for a life.

> **Genesis 18:25:** "Shall not the Judge of all the earth deal justly?"

### Jesus as the Fulfillment

Yet Jesus came and did something extraordinary. He fulfilled the law's demand for retribution on himself.

In Matthew 5, Jesus describes what it means not to resist by evil means—to turn the other cheek, give up your coat, walk the extra mile. Then in Matthew 26-27, he *lives out* these very words:

- He spoke of being slapped; he was struck by the Sanhedrin and Roman guards, yet did not retaliate.
- He spoke of having his clothes taken; his garments were divided by lot at the crucifixion.
- He spoke of being forced to walk a mile; he walked to Golgotha carrying his cross.
- When Peter drew a sword to defend him, Jesus said: "Put your sword back in its place, for all who draw the sword will die by the sword." He chose not to resist by evil means.

God's justice is retributive—a debt must be paid. But God took that debt upon himself through Jesus. The eye that was bashed in was his, not ours. The hand that was nailed to the cross was his, not ours. The side that was pierced was his, not ours.

The debt we created through our crimes against God and against our fellow humans was paid completely, equitably, and infinitely by his death on the cross.

## Justice and Mercy in Society

### The Danger of Forgetting Proportionality

Modern society has drifted far from biblical wisdom about proportional justice. We have moved in two dangerous directions:

**We ignore retribution for real crimes:**
- Corporations steal millions through fraud while individuals go unpunished due to legal technicalities
- Criminal justice systems treat offenders softly while law-abiding citizens pay to house them in relative comfort
- Violent criminals receive light sentences while minor offenses go unpunished

**We impose disproportionate punishment for minor infractions:**
- New York threatens fines of $250,000 for misusing pronouns
- Refusing to bake a cake for an event one disagrees with brings $135,000 in damages
- Cyclists fined $400 for road violations—equating a 10kg bicycle to a 2,000kg vehicle
- A bad pickup line earns a $10,000 fine and bans from employment

This is the error of the Pharisees: majoring on the minors and minoring on the majors. They tithed their herbs but plotted to kill Jesus. Today's societies police trivial speech while ignoring systemic injustice.

### The Path Forward

Society needs wisdom: proportional retribution for real crimes, personal mercy for minor wrongs, and a legal system that actually serves justice rather than political agendas.

But personally, we have been given something better. Jesus has fulfilled retributive justice on our behalf. We no longer face the penalty we deserve.

Therefore: the old law said "an eye for an eye." The new message is "turn the other cheek"—not because justice doesn't matter, but because Jesus has already paid the price. We are free to extend the same mercy we have received.

## Application

The Sermon on the Mount should be read not as legalistic rules but as wisdom reflecting the principle of love. Through Christ, we are called to mercy rather than vengeance, to compassion rather than payback.

This does not mean:
- We cannot be police officers or enforce laws (love sometimes requires protecting the innocent)
- We must be pacifists or never defend ourselves (love sometimes requires saying "no" to evil)
- We ignore real injustice (love sometimes demands justice)

It means we apply these principles with wisdom and love:

- In some situations, self-sacrifice is called for. In others, it would be wrong to allow abuse.
- Sometimes we enforce our rights; sometimes we release them. The principle is love, not rigid rules.
- Major crimes deserve major justice. Minor slights can be forgiven.

We pick and choose—not viciously or arbitrarily, but according to the principle of love that undergirds all Scripture.

For we have been shown mercy beyond measure. In response, we extend mercy to others, not because they deserve it, but because we have first received it from God.
