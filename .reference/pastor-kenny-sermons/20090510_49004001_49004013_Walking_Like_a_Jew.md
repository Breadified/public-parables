# Ephesians 4:1-13

**Title:** Walking Like a Jew  
**Date:** May 10, 2009  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Main Idea

The reconciliation between Jews and Gentiles has profound implications for how Asian churches—and all believers—must learn to walk together in unity.

## 1. Called to One Hope

### The Foundation of Unity

> There is one body and one Spirit—just as you were called to one hope when you were called—one Lord, one faith, one baptism; one God and Father of all, who is over all and through all and in all. (Ephesians 4:4-6)

Through Christ's reconciliation, Jews and Gentiles are now united as one body and one Spirit. They share:
- One Lord
- One faith  
- One baptism (the same experience of repentance)
- One God and Father
- One hope in Jesus and the coming resurrection

This means both Jews and Gentiles now live under the same gospel and have received the same calling.

### What True Unity Actually Is

True biblical unity is fundamentally spiritual, not organizational. We must resist the temptation to equate gathering together with demonstrating unity. The Olympic Games, the United Nations, and even the World Council of Churches represent human attempts at unity, but they fall short of what Scripture describes.

We gather for many good reasons—to read God's Word together, to know one another more deeply, to encourage one another in perseverance. These activities may result in strengthened relationships, but unity itself is not achieved by proximity.

**The crucial point:** If people in a room do not share the one Lord, one faith, and one hope, there is no true unity, no matter how much time they spend together. Conversely, believers separated by geography but united in Christ's resurrection hope are genuinely one.

Consider Andy Clark at Moore College, Rob Horne in Germany, or others scattered across Asia—we are one with them not because we gather, but because we share the same resurrection hope and align our purpose with God's: seeing people saved. That alignment with God's purposes is what creates genuine unity.

### A Cultural Challenge for Asian Churches

This presents a particular challenge for those of us from shame cultures. We often struggle to disagree openly; instead, we maintain an appearance of unity while harboring resentment. When conflict arises between two people, rather than addressing it directly, a "cold war" develops. Only when something finally explodes do they speak, and then the accusation comes: "You should have known."

Think about our own families. False unity breeds festering tension. It's far healthier to argue openly, discuss our differences, and work them out together. We don't gather merely to show unity; we gather to ensure that all of us arrive together at the future heavenly unity, when we will be united with Christ.

## 2. The Two-Stage Order of Salvation

### Why This Order?

Some have asked: If God planned for Gentiles to receive the same inheritance, why not reach out to them from the beginning? Why the long setup with Israel, followed by two thousand years before expanding to the nations? Why did God not simply extend the gospel equally to everyone at once?

Paul addresses this through an extended meditation on Psalm 68:

> But to each one of us grace has been given as Christ apportioned it. This is why it says: "When he ascended on high, he led captives in his train and gave gifts to men." (Ephesians 4:7-8)

### Christ Apportions Gifts

When God ascends to His sanctuary, the vision in Psalm 68:24-27 unfolds: singers lead the procession, musicians follow, and the tribes of Israel gather to praise God. But the crucial detail appears in Psalm 68:18: "When you ascended on high, you led captives in your train; you received gifts from men...that you, O LORD God, might dwell there."

Christ came to give gifts to the rebellious—to the nations and Gentiles—so that they might also dwell in God's house. Psalm 68:29-32 expands this vision: kings will bring gifts to the temple, and nations will submit to God singing His praises.

**What gifts did Christ give when He came?** Paul tells us:

> It was he who gave some to be apostles, some to be prophets, some to be evangelists, and some to be pastors and teachers, to prepare God's people for works of service, so that the body of Christ may be built up until we all reach unity in the faith and in the knowledge of the Son of God and become mature, attaining to the whole measure of the fullness of Christ. (Ephesians 4:11-13)

### The Jewish Leadership and Gentile Inclusion

Traditionally, we've understood this as a progression: apostles and prophets (in New Testament times) gave way to evangelists, pastors, and teachers (in our present age). But I see something deeper here.

The apostles, prophets, evangelists, and pastors/teachers represent one group—the godly Jewish leaders of the past. Their purpose was to make the Jewish saints (God's people) a beacon to the Gentile world. Why did God structure it this way?

**First**, God works through people, not apparitions. He chose to fulfill His purposes through human leadership and relationships.

**Second**, God consistently starts small. He promised Abraham blessing for all nations (Genesis 12), beginning with one family.

**Third**, God deliberately chose Israel not because of their power but because of their weakness. He selected a tiny, insignificant nation to demonstrate His power and bring glory through them.

**Fourth**, the Old Testament focuses entirely on Israel—on Canaan as the homeland of the Jews. Though other great civilizations were flourishing (China had established its borders, introduced Confucianism, and was building the Great Wall by the first century), Scripture concentrates on Israel as God's instrument to fulfill His promises.

**Fifth**, when we examine Israel's history, we discover God's focus narrows even further—not on Israel collectively but on the one Israelite. Israel as a nation ultimately failed. They did not declare God's praises to the nations. Despite their advantages, they became corrupt, selfish, and served idols. So hope rested not on Israel but on the one true Israelite: Jesus.

**Sixth**, this explains Ephesians 4:13: "Until we all reach unity in the faith and in the knowledge of the Son of God and become mature, attaining to the whole measure of the fullness of Christ."

**Seventh**, two thousand years of Israel's history set the stage for Jesus. This is not favoritism; it is biblical theology: Jesus first, then to everyone. He is the final Apostle (the One sent), the final Prophet, the final Evangelist (the Gospel Proclaimer), the final Pastor and Teacher (the Chief Shepherd and Rabbi). He is the ultimate Jew.

Now both Jew and Gentile depend on Him alone. And this dependence transforms everything.

## 3. Therefore, Walk With Each Other

> As a prisoner for the Lord, then, I urge you to live a life worthy of the calling you have received. Be completely humble and gentle; be patient, bearing with one another in love. Make every effort to keep the unity of the Spirit through the bond of peace. (Ephesians 4:1-3)

Paul calls the Gentiles to walk in a manner worthy of their calling—worthy of their invitation to join the people of God alongside the Jews. This walk is characterized by:
- Complete humility and gentleness
- Patience
- Bearing with one another in love
- Maintaining the bond of peace (not the false peace of avoided conflict, but the peace of reconciled former enemies)

There is no room for envy or a superiority complex. Both Jews and Gentiles stand equally in need of grace. Together, they are worthless apart from Christ (Ephesians 2:6), yet together they are saved under the one Jesus.

### Real-World Applications

Consider the great rivalries that divide our world: Liberal versus Labor, Hindu and Tamil Sri Lankans, Mainland China and Taiwan. Now imagine enemies brought together—forced to learn to walk alongside one another. This was the early church's first challenge: Jews and Gentiles learning to share meals, navigate cultural differences, and overcome centuries of hostility.

For Asian churches and fellow believers today, this means:

- If you tend toward criticism, cultivate patience
- Learn to address issues openly rather than allowing cold wars to fester
- Practice genuine humility and gentleness toward Christians of other races, denominations, and traditions
- Extend the same grace that welcomes you to others who believe

## 4. Therefore, Walk Like a True Jew

> So I tell you this, and insist on it in the Lord, that you must no longer live as the Gentiles do, in the futility of their thinking. They are darkened in their understanding and separated from the life of God because of the ignorance that is in them due to the hardening of their hearts. Having lost all sensitivity, they have given themselves over to sensuality so as to indulge in every kind of impurity, with a continual lust for more. (Ephesians 4:17-19)

Paul offers a stark portrait of pagan Gentile life:
- Futile thinking
- Darkened understanding
- Separated from the life of God
- Ignorant and hard-hearted
- Lost all sensitivity to God
- Given over to sensuality and impurity
- Enslaved to perpetual lust

In the first century, to be called a "Gentile" was a slur—implying uncouthness, lack of sophistication, even barbarism.

### The Call to a New Way

> You, however, did not come to know Christ that way. Surely you heard of him and were taught in him in accordance with the truth that is in Jesus. You were taught, with regard to your former way of life, to put off your old self, which is being corrupted by its deceitful desires; to be made new in the attitude of your minds; and to put on the new self, created to be like God in true righteousness and holiness. Therefore each of you must put off falsehood and speak truthfully to his neighbor, for we are all members of one body. (Ephesians 4:20-25)

### Two Types of Christian Struggles

Among believers, I observe two patterns of struggle. Some come from Christian homes—raised in the faith—and their temptations tend toward pride, forgetfulness, and ungratefulness. They struggle with arrogance and a lack of acceptance toward outsiders.

But I want to address the second group: those who came to Christ from non-Christian families, who didn't grow up in Christian environments. If you've lived a lifestyle of drunkenness, crude speech, sexual immorality, given yourself to sensuality and deceitful desires, gambling, or cheating—God forgives you. But that lifestyle must end.

### A Hard Call to Former Ways

You can no longer get drunk. You might have one or two beers with non-Christian friends to maintain relationship, but not to the point of losing your senses. If your friends cannot respect your desire to follow Christ, you need to find new friends who will.

I understand the appeal: drinking lowers inhibitions, facilitates conversation, enables "deep and meaningful" moments. But there are healthier ways to build community and connection. Find a counselor, develop new communication skills, but don't use alcohol to escape or connect.

Kings Cross—with its bottles and violence—represents the pagan lifestyle you've left behind. You might be tempted to dabble at the edges, but those are murky waters. Stay away.

That former way of life—the drunkenness, the nightclubbing, the sleeping around—that's the pagan lifestyle. You've been called out of it. It's a hard call, but it's your calling.

This doesn't mean I'm singling you out for criticism. Those raised in Christian comfort have their own struggles—arrogance, self-righteousness, spiritual pride. To everyone: **Live like the true Jew. Live like Jesus.** You've been called to it.
