# Mark 2:23-3:6

**Title:** Rules for Life 2
**Date:** 25 April 2021
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Strange Rules

Jesus teaches about the Sabbath by challenging how we understand and apply God's law. The Pharisees accuse Jesus's disciples of breaking the Sabbath, but their interpretation is far too narrow. To understand this passage, we must ask: What is truly unlawful? What does Jesus mean when he refers to David? And what is the purpose of the Sabbath itself?

## The Sabbath Day

### Understanding the Commandment

The fourth commandment established the Sabbath as sacred rest:

> Exodus 20:8-11: "Remember the Sabbath day by keeping it holy. Six days you shall labor and do all your work, but the seventh day is a sabbath to the Lord your God. On it you shall not do any work, neither you, nor your son or daughter, nor your male or female servant, nor your animals, nor any foreigner residing in your towns. For in six days the Lord made the heavens and the earth, the sea, and all that is in them, but he rested on the seventh day. Therefore the Lord blessed the Sabbath day and made it holy."

The Jewish Sabbath runs from sunset Friday to sunset Saturday. This law carried serious weight:

> Exodus 31:14-15: "Observe the Sabbath, because it is holy to you. Anyone who desecrates it is to be put to death; those who do any work on that day must be cut off from their people. For six days work is to be done, but the seventh day is a day of sabbath rest, holy to the Lord. Whoever does any work on the Sabbath day is to be put to death."

### What Counts as Work?

The Bible never explicitly defines what activities constitute "work." However, various passages give us examples of forbidden activities:

- No going out or traveling (Exodus 16:29)
- No gathering wood (Numbers 15:32-34)
- No lighting fires for cooking (Exodus 35:3)
- No carrying loads (Jeremiah 17:22)
- No commercial activity or selling (Amos 8:5; Nehemiah 13:15-22)
- No fighting (1 Maccabees 2:29-38)

Even during critical times like harvest and plowing seasons, rest was required:

> Exodus 34:21: "Six days you shall labor, but on the seventh day you shall rest; even during the plowing season and harvest you must rest."

The pattern suggests a focus on economic activity—getting food, selling food, and cooking food. The Sabbath restricted the most essential survival activities.

### Why the Sabbath Mattered

**The Creation Connection:** God rested on the seventh day, establishing a rhythm that reminds us work is not our ultimate purpose.

**The Slavery Motif:** In Egypt, the Israelites worked continuously—24/7, every day. The Sabbath represented liberation from this oppressive cycle. It was a sign that they were no longer enslaved to Pharaoh, but could rest under God's care.

**The Trust Factor:** The Sabbath required faith. Imagine fields ready for harvest, a storm coming, and you cannot work. You must trust that God will provide. This taught the Israelites to depend on God rather than their own labor.

**Community and Generosity:** The Sabbath created space for others to glean and work. It reminded the wealthy that life is not only about maximizing production and personal gain. Greed jeopardizes community.

### The Sabbath Today

In our modern context, we face relentless pressure: 24/7/365 demands on our time. Career advancement, education, security—there is never enough. We cannot finish all our homework, all our work, all our housework. The Sabbath whispers a radical truth: *You cannot finish everything, and that is okay.*

When we shut down, turn off our computers, and put down our tools—even for a few hours—we trust God with running the world. We live for something beyond economic activity: for God, for relationships, for remembering who we are.

In previous generations, the rhythm was built in. No Sunday trading. No weeknight commerce. The nation paused together, and everyone rested. Now we must choose discipline. A day of rest trains us to prioritize what truly matters.

## Reading the Law as Intended

### The Disciples' Actions

> Mark 2:23-24: "One Sabbath Jesus was going through the grainfields, and as his disciples walked along, they began to pick some heads of grain. The Pharisees said to him, 'Look, why are they doing what is unlawful on the Sabbath?'"

The Pharisees accused Jesus's disciples of working on the Sabbath. But were they truly breaking the law?

### The Law of Gleaning

God had actually provided for this situation:

> Leviticus 19:9-10: "When you reap the harvest of your land, do not reap to the very edges of your field or gather the gleanings of your harvest. Do not go over your vineyard a second time or pick up the grapes that have fallen. Leave them for the poor and the foreigner."

> Deuteronomy 23:25: "If you enter your neighbor's grainfield, you may pick kernels with your hands, but you must not put a sickle to their standing grain."

The disciples were not stealing. They were gleaning—a legal right for the poor and hungry. They walked along the path picking grain for their own sustenance, exactly as the law permitted. Therefore, they acted within the law.

The real offense, according to the Pharisees' phrase "on the Sabbath," was not *what* they were doing but *when* they were doing it. However, this too reveals a misreading of Scripture.

### Jesus's Response: The David Example

> Mark 2:25-26: "He answered, 'Have you never read what David did when he and his companions were hungry and in need? In the days of Abiathar the high priest, he entered the house of God and ate the consecrated bread, which is lawful only for priests to eat. And he also gave some to his companions.'"

Jesus refers to 1 Samuel 21:1-6, when David fled from King Saul and received bread from the high priest Ahimelech. (Jesus mentions "Abiathar," likely referring to the era when Abiathar served faithfully as high priest for many years, as opposed to Ahimelech who was killed shortly after this episode.)

The showbread—the "bread of the Presence"—was sacred and reserved exclusively for priests:

> Leviticus 24:8-9: "This bread is to be set out before the Lord regularly, Sabbath after Sabbath, on behalf of the Israelites, as a lasting covenant. It belongs to Aaron and his sons, who are to eat it in the sanctuary area, because it is a most holy part of their perpetual share of the food offerings presented to the Lord."

Yet Ahimelech gave David the bread. David ate it and gave some to his companions. David was not a priest, so technically he consumed what was forbidden to him. Why was this permitted?

### How to Read Laws Rightly

The answer lies in understanding how to interpret God's law. When Scripture says something "belongs to priests," it establishes the primary stewardship, not an absolute prohibition. Consider similar statements:

- When we say "the serving team will help with lunch," we do not mean *only* the serving team can help and no one else should assist. That would be reading the rule too narrowly.
- When a mother says "I will cook dinner tonight," it does not mean the family cannot help her. 
- When the law says "eye for an eye," many read it as permission for revenge. But it actually sets a *maximum limit*, restraining us from excessive retaliation. It means: do not take two eyes for one eye.

Similarly, the showbread was "kept for priests" but priests had discretion over it. They could give it away to others in need. The law established what the bread was primarily for, not what it *only* could be used for.

Moreover, David's status added weight. He was God's anointed king—already set apart as a leader of God's people. The principle was clear: *human need takes precedence.*

> 1 Samuel 21:4-6: "But the priest answered David, 'I don't have any ordinary bread on hand; however, there is some consecrated bread here—provided the men have kept themselves from women.' David replied, 'Indeed women have been kept from us, as usual whenever I set out. The men's bodies are holy even on missions that are not holy. How much more so today!' So the priest gave him the consecrated bread, since there was no bread there except the bread of the Presence that had been removed from before the Lord and replaced by hot bread on the day it was taken away."

David himself argued that his men were in a state of holiness—consecrated and prepared for their mission. The bread, freshly replaced on the table, was technically *common bread* though it had been consecrated. The priest saw the justice in feeding the hungry, even when it meant giving what was normally reserved for priests.

### The Principle

Jesus's point is crucial: When you read God's law as it is *intended*—with its full moral context—you see that it is always lawful to do good. The law was made for humanity's benefit, not to trap us in legalism. Life and mercy trump ritual.

## Everyday a Sabbath

### Doing Good on the Sabbath

> Mark 3:1-4: "Another time Jesus went into the synagogue, and a man with a shriveled hand was there. Some of them were looking for a reason to accuse Jesus, so they watched him closely to see if he would heal him on the Sabbath. Jesus said to the man with the shriveled hand, 'Stand up in front of everyone.' Then Jesus asked them, 'Which is lawful on the Sabbath: to do good or to do evil, to save life or to kill?' But they remained silent."

Again, the Pharisees sought to accuse Jesus of breaking the Sabbath. Jesus responded with a piercing question: Is it lawful to do good or to do evil on the Sabbath? To save life or to kill?

The answer is self-evident. Even the Pharisees acknowledged the principle:

> Matthew 12:11-12: "If any of you has a sheep and it falls into a pit on the Sabbath, will you not take hold of it and lift it out? How much more valuable is a person than a sheep! Therefore it is lawful to do good on the Sabbath."

The Pharisees themselves would rescue a sheep on the Sabbath. Circumcision happened on the eighth day, even if it fell on the Sabbath. Life transcends ritual.

If ordinary people can do good on the Sabbath—and the Pharisees themselves acknowledged this—then surely Jesus, who is Lord of the Sabbath, can do good.

### The Healing and the Response

> Mark 3:5-6: "He looked around at them in anger and, deeply distressed at their stubborn hearts, said to the man, 'Stretch out your hand.' He stretched it out, and his hand was completely restored. Then the Pharisees went out and began to plot with the Herodians how they might kill Jesus."

Jesus healed the man. His hand was restored completely. But the Pharisees' response was revealing: they plotted to kill Jesus—on the Sabbath.

In their anger at what they deemed "Sabbath violation," they:
- Called good evil (the healing)
- Called evil good (their plot to murder)
- Broke their own moral code (you shall not kill)
- Abandoned their loyalties (allying with Herodians, the very Roman-sympathizers they opposed)
- Violated the law they claimed to protect

They failed to see what the Sabbath truly meant. Their legalism blinded them to mercy.

### Every Day a Sabbath

The law, read rightly, reveals something profound: It is lawful to do good on the Sabbath. By extension, it is lawful—indeed, it is *required*—to do good every day.

> Hebrews 4:3: "Now we who have believed enter that rest."

The earthly Sabbath was a sign pointing to ultimate rest in God—our salvation. In Christ, we enter that rest. Every day becomes an opportunity to honor God and do good. The early church understood this, meeting daily and living in the reality of perpetual Sabbath rest.

> Hebrews 3:12-13: "See to it, brothers and sisters, that none of you has a sinful, unbelieving heart that turns away from the living God. But encourage one another daily, as long as it is called 'Today,' so that none of you may be hardened by sin's deceitfulness."

We gather on this day to do good. We pause in the week. We encourage one another. We practice the discipline of the Sabbath principle—rest, community, devotion to God.

### Living This Out

We are blessed with privileges previous generations did not have. We have cheap energy, electricity that extends our productive hours. We have flexible work arrangements. We have weekends built into our culture. We have the luxury—and the responsibility—to do good.

This means:
- Do not sin every day. Do not simply do as we please for self-gratification.
- Prioritize what matters: God, relationships, others' welfare.
- Rest, knowing God sustains the world.
- Encourage others daily in the faith.
- Practice mercy, healing, restoration—the true work of the Sabbath.

The withered hand was healed. The stubborn hearts remain difficult. But the call stands clear:

**Do only good. Everyday.**
