# 1 Corinthians 13:1-13

**Title:** The Common Ministry: Love

**Date:** 24 January 2010

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Introduction: Good But Useless

We often treasure trivia—facts that seem important but rarely serve us in life. Similarly, the exercise of our spiritual gifts can be good, yet ultimately useless without the right foundation.

Last week we learned that all Christians possess the Spirit of God within them. This is what it means to be spiritual—the exercise of our gifts. But Paul asks us a crucial question: how can these gifts, seemingly so valuable, become profitless? The answer lies in understanding what Paul calls "the most excellent way" in 1 Corinthians 13.

---

## Part 1: Gifts Are Useless Without Love (v.1-3)

> If I speak in the tongues of men and of angels, but have not love, I am only a resounding gong or a clanging cymbal. If I have the gift of prophecy and can fathom all mysteries and all knowledge, and if I have a faith that can move mountains, but have not love, I am nothing. If I give all I possess to the poor and surrender my body to the flames, but have not love, I gain nothing.

Paul presents an arresting equation: **Gifts × Love = Profit**

Without love, our talents become mere noise. Your time and effort may be extraordinary, but if they lack love as their animating force, they profit nothing. An orchestra without a conductor, no matter how skilled each musician, produces only chaos. Love provides the essential ingredient that transforms good works into truly meaningful ministry.

### What Is Love? (v.4-7)

> Love is patient, love is kind. It does not envy, it does not boast, it is not proud. It is not rude, it is not self-seeking, it is not easily angered, it keeps no record of wrongs. Love does not delight in evil but rejoices with the truth. It always protects, always trusts, always hopes, always perseveres.

This is the love we must bring to our service of one another. Love provides proper focus for our gifts and talents. Love is the standard by which we judge our activity.

When you read this list, you see Jesus. He is patient and kind. He does not envy or boast. He is not rude or self-seeking. He does not grow angry or keep records of our wrongs. He delights in truth and protects, trusts, hopes, and perseveres with us. We practice this love not because we have learned it in isolation, but because He has loved us first.

### The Body of Christ Comes Before Personal Gifting

Jesus taught us in Matthew 25 that when we care for "the least of these," we care for Him. Whatever we do—or fail to do—for others, we do for Christ Himself.

Therefore, we must act out of genuine concern for others. The crucial test is this: **Does it exhibit thoughtful love, or is it the unrestrained exercise of our gifts?** Nothing at church should be done without concern for the body of Christ. The body is more important than the exercise of any individual's gifts.

### Love Applied: Practical Examples

**1 Corinthians 8—The Stumbling Brother**

Paul addresses a community divided over food sacrificed to idols. Those with knowledge understood that idols are nothing; there is only one God. Yet Paul writes:

> Be careful, however, that the exercise of your freedom does not become a stumbling block to the weak. When you sin against your brothers in this way and wound their weak conscience, you sin against Christ. Therefore, if what I eat causes my brother to fall into sin, I will never eat meat again, so that I will not cause him to fall.

Knowledge puffs up; love builds up. Our freedom must be restrained by concern for those still struggling with conscience. This is love in action—choosing not to exercise what we rightly can do, for the sake of another's spiritual growth.

**1 Corinthians 11—The Lord's Meal**

When the church gathers for communion, Paul urges: "When you come together to eat, wait for each other." Do not rush ahead in self-interest. Do not ignore the hungry among you. The meal is corporate; it belongs to the body, not to individuals.

### Objection: "Why Would God Give Me This Gift?"

Some ask: If God gave me this ability, surely I should use it. Why would He give it otherwise?

Consider this analogy. A high school student earns an excellent academic score. Does this single mark determine their path? Not necessarily. It is a tool. The question is not "What can I do?" but "What should I do with what I can do?" Your ATAR might enable medicine, law, arts, teaching, or politics—or dropping out to pursue your own studies. The tool itself is neutral; your purpose determines its use.

Similarly, gifts are tools. The question is not merely "Do I have this ability?" but "Does this gift serve love?" A skill used selfishly is wasted. A lesser skill used lovingly bears fruit.

### Objection: "I Have No Other Gifts to Exercise"

This objection misses the point entirely. We all possess multiple gifts, and there will always be a need to love.

Look around. Ask yourself: Who needs care? Who needs to hear the gospel? Who needs encouragement? Who needs someone to listen?

If you can speak, you have the gift of greeting and conversation. If you have legs, you can walk toward someone in need. If you have memory, you can remember others' burdens and intercede for them throughout the week. If you have eyes, you can notice who sits alone or who leaves quickly out of loneliness. If you have arms, you can carry chairs, pull in the garbage bin, or offer a hug. If you have hands, you can write a note or send an SMS saying, "Keep following Jesus."

Do you have a driver's license? You can give someone a lift. Do you have internet access? You can email about youth group or reach out to someone you haven't seen. Do you have a wallet and income? You can give generously. Do you have a house? You can host a dinner. Do you have a mobile phone? You can pray.

These are not trivial. They are all gifts from God. Stop saying you have no gifts. Open your mind to the possibilities. They all express love.

### Reframing: Opportunities, Not Just Gifts

I prefer to shift our language from "gifts" to "opportunities." This reflects how Paul himself speaks in 1 Corinthians 7 regarding singleness and marriage—as opportunities, not limited talents.

The problem with many church seminars on spiritual gifts is that they start in the wrong place: "What am I good at, and what looks impressive?" This invariably leads to a focus on upfront roles—leading worship, singing, playing instruments—as though God had only a few dozen gifts and they all belong at the front. We celebrate the glamorous and overlook the essential.

But the gift of taking out the garbage is no less spiritual than the gift of prophecy. Every single one is given by God. The question is not "What am I naturally skilled at?" but "Where is there a need, and how can I meet it with love?"

**Make the decision to love first. The gifts will follow.**

## Part 2: Love Is More Important Than Gifts (v.8)

> Love never fails. But where there are prophecies, they will cease; where there are tongues, they will be stilled; where there is knowledge, it will pass away.

### Love Endures; Gifts Are Temporary

One day these gifts will end. Prophecies will cease. Tongues will fall silent. Knowledge will pass away. When we reach perfection—when we see face to face and know fully as we are fully known—the partial gifts we now exercise will disappear.

But love never fails. Love is the one fruit that lasts eternally.

### Love as Our Primary Motivation When Things Grow Difficult

Service begins with enthusiasm. Volunteering feels meaningful and purposeful. But one day the work grows difficult. The excitement fades. For musicians, the songs become routine. For Bible leaders, teaching becomes predictable. For those who serve in morning tea, coffee becomes a chore. For those who pack up chairs, the work feels thankless.

When that day comes, what will sustain you? If your motivation was fun, resume-building, friendship, self-esteem, or skill development—these will evaporate. Service is hard work. A resume may take a beating if you choose a less glamorous path for the sake of love. Self-esteem dips when you absorb criticism for trying to serve others. It is not cool to be alone packing chairs.

**Will you still serve then? Out of love?**

This is the danger young people face in the church. It is easy to join a group; young people move in packs. But your loyalty—you who have the Spirit of Christ—must be to Jesus first, not to your peer group. Because of this, your loyalty extends to serving the entire body of Christ, not merely those in your circle. You will care for individuals when your group abandons them.

*If you find yourself burning out in ministry, or if you realize you are serving out of obligation rather than love, please speak with the pastoral leadership. Genuine service flows from genuine love.*

### Love as Lasting Fruit

> When I was a child, I talked like a child, I thought like a child, I reasoned like a child. When I became a man, I put childish ways behind me. Now we see but a poor reflection as in a mirror; then we shall see face to face. Now I know in part; then I shall know fully, even as I am fully known. And now these three remain: faith, hope and love. But the greatest of these is love.

One day, SMAC will close its doors. You will forget what you learned here. People will not remember your deeds. But love will count. If you served with genuine concern for others, if you were not self-seeking, if you lived out the character of love—that will matter eternally.

True spirituality is love. Love is the evidence of true spirituality. God knows your motivation, regardless of whether the ministry appears successful or grows large. When is a gift truly a gift? When it is exercised in love. Without love, the greatest talent is merely noise.

## The Best Trumps the Good

We face a tyranny of choice. So many good things clamor for our time and attention. So many people are gifted. So many opportunities exist.

A friend once told me: "The good is the enemy of the best."

We must choose. We must limit our options. We must do the best thing, not merely the good thing. The best thing is love.

Do not trivialize your opportunities. Make them count through love.
