# Acts 19:1-20:1

**Title:** Paul's Ministry in Ephesus: Power, Opposition, and Persuasion
**Date:** 28 June 2015
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Foundation: Complete Conversion

Paul arrived in Ephesus and discovered some believers who had only received John's baptism. He asked them a crucial question: "Did you receive the Holy Spirit when you believed?" Their answer revealed a critical gap in their understanding—they had not even heard that the Holy Spirit existed.

> "John's baptism was a baptism of repentance. He told the people to believe in the one coming after him, that is, in Jesus."

Once they understood the full Gospel, they were baptized in the name of the Lord Jesus. When Paul placed his hands on them, the Holy Spirit came upon them, and they spoke in tongues and prophesied. This was genuine conversion—faith in Jesus completed what had been begun through John's preparatory message.

## Two Years of Persistent Reasoning

Paul's work in Ephesus unfolded in distinct phases. For three months, he entered the synagogue and reasoned persuasively about the kingdom of God. Some listened, but others became obstinate and refused to believe, publicly maligning "the Way." Rather than force engagement with hostile ears, Paul wisely separated himself and the disciples.

He then spent two years engaged in daily discussions in the lecture hall of Tyrannus. This extended period was remarkably fruitful—all the Jews and Greeks throughout the province of Asia heard the word of the Lord. Paul was not building church buildings or establishing institutions. His activity was **persuasion through reasoning**: marshalling evidence about Jesus and the resurrection, appealing to minds, not emotions.

## Power Made Manifest

God worked through Paul in extraordinary ways:

> "God did extraordinary miracles through Paul, so that even handkerchiefs and aprons that had touched him were taken to the sick, and their illnesses were cured and the evil spirits left them."

The divine power validated the apostolic message. God gave Paul an authority that extended even through material objects. This was God's way of authenticating the Gospel proclaimed.

However, some Jewish exorcists attempted to harness this power for themselves. The seven sons of Sceva invoked the name of Jesus over demon-possessed people, saying, "In the name of Jesus whom Paul preaches, I command you to come out." But spiritual authority cannot be borrowed or manipulated. When the demon spoke, it revealed the hollowness of their presumption: "Jesus I know, and Paul I know about, but who are you?" The possessed man overpowered all seven of them, beating them so severely they fled naked and bleeding.

## The Fear of God and the Burning of Scrolls

When this incident became known throughout Ephesus, fear seized both Jews and Greeks. The name of the Lord Jesus was held in high honor—not as a magical formula, but as the true source of authority.

Many who had believed came forward and openly confessed what they had been doing—their secret practices, their hidden sins. This was genuine repentance, not external compliance. Even more striking, those who had practiced sorcery brought their scrolls together and burned them publicly. The economic value was immense: fifty thousand drachmas. This represented not mere superstition abandoned, but a costly realignment of allegiance. People were choosing the kingdom of God over financial security and cultural status.

## The Riot: Faith Confronts Commerce

After this spiritual harvest, Paul decided to journey toward Jerusalem, planning ultimately to reach Rome. But his departure was delayed by a violent disturbance.

Demetrius, a silversmith who crafted silver shrines of Artemis, recognized the economic threat posed by Paul's preaching. He gathered fellow craftsmen and appealed to their pocketbooks: "You know, my friends, that we receive a good income from this business." Paul's message—that gods made by human hands are no gods at all—struck at the heart of their livelihood. Demetrius framed the issue in religious terms (the goddess's honor would be robbed of her majesty), but the real concern was **income from superstition**.

He stirred up the city, and a mob formed. Gaius and Aristarchus, Paul's traveling companions, were seized and dragged into the theater. Paul wanted to address the crowd, but the disciples wisely restrained him. Even some city officials, friends of Paul, begged him not to venture into the theater. The mob was an unthinking crowd—most didn't even know why they were there. For two hours they chanted, "Great is Artemis of the Ephesians!"

The city clerk finally quieted the assembly and applied reason to the chaos. He pointed out that Paul and his companions had neither robbed temples nor blasphemed the goddess. If Demetrius and the craftsmen had a grievance, the courts were open. This was a matter for legal process, not mob violence. His appeal to order and lawful procedure succeeded; the assembly was dismissed.

## The Challenge of Reasoning in a Hostile World

Throughout this passage, Kenny wrestled with a profound question: How do we reach people who are closed to reasoning?

The challenge is genuine. As David Hume famously sneered, "It is easier to believe that men made the belief than that the dead man rose." The rational objection to Christianity's central claim—resurrection—can feel insurmountable to the skeptical mind.

Yet Paul's method was consistent: **reasoning, discussion, marshalling evidence**. He approached people as rational agents capable of understanding. His activity was persuasion, not coercion. He appealed to minds, not manipulated emotions through sentimentality or fear.

True knowledge requires three elements:
- **Belief** (what you hold to be true)
- **Fact** (the objective reality)  
- **Justification** (rational grounds for connecting belief to fact)

Paul was a true empiricist. He didn't retreat into pure reason (like Descartes or Kant) or pure emotion. He included historical evidence—the resurrection of Jesus as a datable, observable event. He marshalled the testimony of witnesses, the transformation of disciples from fear to boldness, the miraculous confirmation of his message.

## The Power of Honest Inquiry

There is a vast difference between an unthinking mob and honest inquirers. Kenny's heart was for those genuinely seeking understanding. For those willing to engage seriously with Scripture, misunderstandings can be cleared up. For those honest in their doubt—whether agnostic or simply unsure—there remains hope.

The harder question concerns those who close their minds. It's easy to dismiss Christianity through arrogance and ignorance, to blame "hypocritical Christians" or claim "I'm too busy for God." But God's patience has limits. He has "overlooked" the times of ignorance, but judgment is coming. Repentance remains possible as long as breath remains.

## Application

Paul modeled something vital: The Gospel advances not primarily through institutions or sentiment, but through the power of God validated in the real world, proclaimed by reasoning servants, and received by honest hearts. He reasoned daily for two years. He accepted opposition without bitterness. He fled when the mob came, not in cowardice but in wisdom. And the word of the Lord spread widely and grew in power.

Human Insight: Are you willing to reason about Jesus? Will you examine the historical evidence? Or will you join the unthinking mob, convinced by emotion and economic interest rather than truth?
