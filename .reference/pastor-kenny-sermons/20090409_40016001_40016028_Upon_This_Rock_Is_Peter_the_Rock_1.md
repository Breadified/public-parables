# Matthew 16:1-28

**Title:** Upon This Rock: Is Peter the Rock?
**Date:** April 9, 2009
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Question: Who Is the Rock?

In Matthew 16:18, Jesus makes a striking declaration to Simon Peter: "You are Peter, and on this rock I will build my church." The Greek text reveals an intentional wordplay that has sparked centuries of theological debate. Jesus calls Simon by the name *petros* (stone), then speaks of building His church upon *petra* (rock). The critical question is: How closely are these two words meant to be connected? Is Jesus referring to Peter himself, or to something else entirely?

## Four Major Interpretations

### 1. Peter as the Foundation (Roman Catholic View)

The Roman Catholic position argues that *petros* and *petra* are essentially the same word, and therefore Jesus is explicitly identifying Peter as the rock upon which the church is built. This leads to the conclusion that Peter was the first pope and founder of the earthly church, with his successors inheriting his authority through apostolic succession. The keys given to Peter represent his power to bind and loose—to regulate church membership through excommunication.

However, this interpretation faces significant challenges. There is no New Testament evidence that Peter served as the head of the church in Rome, nor is there any indication of apostolic succession. As Reformed Christians, we acknowledge only one pope: Christ Himself.

### 2. The Rock Is Peter's Confession

Responding to the Catholic position, Protestant scholars have drawn a distinction between the two Greek words. In classical Attic Greek, *petros* refers to a movable stone or pebble, while *petra* denotes an immovable rock, boulder, or cliff. If this distinction holds, they argue, then Peter cannot be "the rock"—the pebble cannot be confused with the massive foundation stone.

This leads to an alternative reading: the rock upon which Jesus builds His church is Peter's confession itself. The foundation is not a person but a truth—the declaration that "Jesus is the Christ." This interpretation gains support from Matthew Henry, who wrote, "Now, says Christ, this is that great truth upon which I will build my church." Without this confession, the church collapses. A survey of ancient commentators found that forty-four out of eighty-five held this view.

### 3. The Rock Is Christ Himself

Others argue that the rock refers not to Peter or his confession, but to Christ Himself. Several biblical considerations support this:

The Old Testament is replete with references to God as Rock. Throughout the Hebrew scriptures, "rock" is always used as a symbol for God, never for man. It appears in Deuteronomy, the Psalms, and Isaiah as a designation for God or the Messiah.

Jesus Himself claims to be the rock of Psalm 118, identifying this Old Testament stone with His own person (Matthew 21:42). Paul echoes this in 1 Corinthians 10:4, explicitly identifying Jesus as the rock. In Ephesians 2:20, Paul teaches that the foundation of the church is "the apostles and prophets"—understood to mean their inspired teaching *about Christ*, not their persons or offices.

Most tellingly, Peter himself never claims to be the rock or foundation. Instead, he identifies Christ as the "chief cornerstone" from Psalm 118:22 (1 Peter 2:4-8; Acts 4:10-11).

### 4. Peter as the Rock Reconsidered

Yet there remains a compelling difficulty with options two and three: if the rock is not Peter, why must we look back to verse 16 for its meaning when *petros* appears just words before in verse 18a? Furthermore, if Peter is not the rock, why does Jesus give *him* the keys and the authority to bind and loose?

Recent scholarship has reconsidered whether the semantic distinction between *petros* and *petra* truly applies in Hellenistic Koine Greek, the language of Matthew's gospel. These words may have been synonymous in common usage, or the distinction may be grammatically irrelevant if Jesus originally spoke in Aramaic—where only one word exists for stone, pebble, and rock: *Kepha* (Cephas). When Matthew translated into Greek, he may have used *petros* as the closest masculine form, since *petra* is feminine and could not serve as a proper name.

## The Linguistic and Structural Case for Peter

**Semantic Argument:** Modern lexicons, including the authoritative Bauer-Danker-Arndt-Gingrich dictionary, list distinct meanings for *petros* and *petra*. Yet numerous exceptions exist in Attic Greek where *petros* refers to larger stones. More importantly, if Jesus spoke Aramaic, the Greek wordplay may be Matthew's creative translation of an Aramaic pun that only works in Aramaic.

**Structural Argument:** The passage exhibits a clear three-fold structure (Matthew 16:17-19), with each section containing two parallel statements connected by conjunctions:

> Verse 17: Blessed are you, Simon son of Jonah—*for* this was not revealed to you by man, *but* by my Father in heaven.
> 
> Verse 18: And I tell you that you are Peter—*and* on this rock I will build my church, *and* the gates of Hades will not overcome it.
> 
> Verse 19: I will give you the keys of the kingdom of heaven—*and* whatever you bind on earth will be bound in heaven, *and* whatever you loose on earth will be loosed in heaven.

This parallel structure suggests that verse 18b refers back to verse 18a: the rock is Peter.

**Grammatical Argument:** The use of the demonstrative *taute* ("this very") combined with *kai* ("and") creates a structure that binds the two rock references together grammatically, making them refer to the same entity.

## What Peter's Special Role Actually Means

All the evidence points to this conclusion: **Peter is the rock that Jesus refers to.** Yet this requires careful qualification to avoid Roman Catholic conclusions unsupported by Scripture.

Peter holds a uniquely significant position in the early church. He is consistently listed first among the disciples, features as the leading character in numerous Gospel narratives, and serves as the apostles' primary spokesperson. He shows boldness in following Jesus, fights for Him, and receives repeated special commissions. The first half of Acts portrays Peter guiding the choosing of Matthias, preaching to the Jews, and later bringing the gospel to the Gentiles through Cornelius. The evidence overwhelmingly suggests Jesus gave Peter a special responsibility.

However, we must avoid a critical error: reading Peter's first-century role directly onto our own situation or onto a supposed papal succession in Rome. Peter's position was historically unique and unrepeatable. The keys given to Peter—representing his authority to teach and regulate who enters God's kingdom—are actually given to the entire church of believers in Matthew 18. The other disciples also receive binding and loosing authority.

Furthermore, the alternative interpretations need not be rejected entirely. They can be incorporated into a fuller understanding:

**Peter and the Keys:** The keys Jesus gives Peter symbolize his role as a teacher. His authority is teaching authority—apostolic teaching. The Pharisees had "shut the kingdom of heaven in people's faces" by their poor teaching (Matthew 23:13). Peter, by contrast, teaches rightly about Jesus as the Christ, opening the kingdom through gospel proclamation.

**Peter and Confession:** The apostolic teaching that Peter delivers is grounded in the confession Jesus draws from him: "You are the Messiah, the Son of the living God." This confession undergirds everything Peter preaches in Acts. The rock on which the church is built is thus both Peter *and* the confession bound up in his role as apostle.

**The Double Meaning:** Many early church fathers—both Latin and Greek writers like St. John Chrysostom—held that the "foundation rock" applies simultaneously to Peter personally *and* to his confession of faith symbolically, with the promise extending more broadly to all twelve apostles and the church at large. This "double meaning" interpretation remains present in Catholic theology. Jesus, who frequently employed wordplay to create single-word allegories, likely intended this richness of meaning.

Reformed churches rightly insist that if Peter alone were the foundation, it would contradict Ephesians 2:20, which states that "the church's foundation...are the apostles and prophets." The most faithful reading is that Jesus uses a play on words with Peter's name to teach that the confession Peter has just made—the truth that Jesus is the Christ—is the rock on which the church is built.

## Peter's Legacy Without the Papacy

We must honor Peter as a foundational apostle. Many churches throughout history have borne his name. But he was never the first pope, nor was he Roman Catholic. His own epistles reveal no teaching about a Roman hierarchy. Instead, he proclaims that all Christians are "a royal priesthood" (1 Peter 2:9). Even modern Catholic historians acknowledge that there were no popes and no single bishop of Rome in the first century. As Francis A. Sullivan writes in *From Apostles to Bishops*: the threefold structure of one bishop among presbyters and deacons in each church does not appear in the New Testament, and the church of Rome was led by a group of presbyters for at least part of the second century.

Peter received a very special responsibility from Jesus in light of his faithful confession. But that responsibility was his alone, historically grounded, and not transferable through institutional succession.
