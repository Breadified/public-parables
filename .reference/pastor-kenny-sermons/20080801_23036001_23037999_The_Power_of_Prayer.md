# Isaiah 36-37

**Title:** The Power of Prayer  
**Date:** 10 August 2008  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Main Point

Depend on Yahweh's truth rather than the half-truths of the world.

---

## Introduction: The Danger of Half-Truths

Half-truths are deceptive statements that contain some element of truth but conceal the fuller picture. They appear in every area of life—politics, advertising, media—all designed to mislead while maintaining a veneer of truthfulness.

The problem with half-truths is that they're harder to defend against than outright lies. An advertiser might truthfully claim "nine out of ten doctors recommend our product," while omitting that they only surveyed ten doctors, nine of whom work for the company. The truth can deceive just as effectively as falsehood when it's incomplete.

This is precisely the challenge Hezekiah faced in Isaiah 36-37: distinguishing between Assyria's compelling half-truths and God's complete truth.

---

## Historical Context: The Threat of Assyria

After 35 chapters of warnings and judgments, Isaiah now turns to narrative. The northern Kingdom of Israel has already fallen to Assyria—its cities conquered, its people dispersed and assimilated into foreign lands. The question now hangs over Judah: will the southern kingdom meet the same fate?

It's 701 BC. King Sennacherib of Assyria has conquered 46 cities of Judah, including the heavily fortified city of Lachish. Now his sights are set on Jerusalem—the last great prize. King Hezekiah must decide: on whom will Judah depend?

---

## The Field Commander's First Challenge

### The Setting

Sennacherib sends his field commander to demand Jerusalem's surrender. The commander meets Judah's officials at a symbolic location—the aqueduct of the Upper Pool—the same place where King Ahaz had faced a crisis of faith generations earlier.

> "This is what the great king, the king of Assyria, says: On what are you basing this confidence of yours? You say you have strategy and military strength—but you speak only empty words. On whom are you depending, that you rebel against me?" (Isaiah 36:4-5)

The field commander's logic is stark: Jerusalem has no real military strength, and therefore, no reason to resist.

### Reason #1: Egypt Is Unreliable

The field commander's first argument attacks Judah's natural ally:

> "Look now, you are depending on Egypt, that splintered reed of a staff, which pierces a man's hand and wounds him if he leans on it! Such is Pharaoh king of Egypt to all who depend on him." (Isaiah 36:6)

This is actually true. Egypt looks powerful from a distance, but it's hollow—like a reed staff that splinters and wounds those who lean on it. Sennacherib has just routed the Egyptian army. They're in no position to rescue Jerusalem.

### Reason #2: Yahweh Is on Assyria's Side

The commander makes a bolder claim:

> "Furthermore, have I come to attack and destroy this land without the LORD? The LORD himself told me to march against this country and destroy it." (Isaiah 36:10)

This contains a grain of truth. Isaiah has indeed described Assyria as "the rod of my anger and the club of my wrath" (Isaiah 10:5). God has committed to using Assyria to punish Judah. But the commander misses the fuller truth: God will also judge Assyria after using it.

### Judah's Response: Fearful Silence

The Judean officials ask the commander to speak in Aramaic rather than Hebrew—afraid that the common soldiers and people on the walls will hear these frightening truths:

> "Please speak to your servants in Aramaic, since we understand it. Don't speak to us in Hebrew in the hearing of the people on the wall." (Isaiah 36:11)

Their fear makes sense. Everything the commander said was partially true, and they knew it.

---

## The Field Commander's Public Challenge

Undeterred, the commander addresses the people directly, his message brutal:

> "Was it only to your master and you that my master sent me to say these things, and not to the men sitting on the wall—who, like you, will have to eat their own filth and drink their own urine?" (Isaiah 36:12)

A siege by the Assyrian army would mean years of starvation. He's offering them at least the dignity of foreknowledge.

### Reason #1: Hezekiah Cannot Deliver You

> "Do not let Hezekiah deceive you. He cannot deliver you! Do not let Hezekiah persuade you to trust in the LORD when he says, 'The LORD will surely deliver us; this city will not be given into the hand of the king of Assyria.'" (Isaiah 36:14-15)

The commander shifts focus to Hezekiah himself. His argument: Don't trust Hezekiah's promises. He's deceiving you. He cannot save you.

This is true. Hezekiah is merely a man—a king of a small nation facing the first worldwide empire in history.

### Reason #2: Trust Sennacherib Instead

But the commander offers an alternative:

> "Make peace with me and come out to me. Then every one of you will eat from his own vine and fig tree and drink water from his own cistern, until I come and take you to a land like your own—a land of grain and new wine, a land of bread and vineyards." (Isaiah 36:16-17)

Sennacherib positions himself as a deliverer—a second Moses, offering another promised land. The imagery of vines, fig trees, cisterns, grain, and wine represents the deepest longing of the Israelite people: security, abundance, peace, and contentment.

To prove his point, Sennacherib offers empirical evidence:

> "Has the god of any nation ever delivered his land from the hand of the king of Assyria? Where are the gods of Hamath and Arpad? Where are the gods of Sepharvaim? Have they rescued Samaria from my hand? Who of all the gods of these countries has been able to save his land from me? How then can the LORD deliver Jerusalem from my hand?" (Isaiah 36:18-20)

The pattern is clear: every nation's gods have fallen before Assyria. Why should Judah's God be different?

Again, this contains truth. But it's a half-truth. What Sennacherib omits is that the gods of these nations are mere wood and stone—fashioned by human hands. Yahweh is in an entirely different category.

Furthermore, Sennacherib's promise of a new land conceals a darker reality: the people won't go together. They'll be dispersed across the empire, their cultural identity deliberately diluted. The goal is to eliminate all distinctives—no more Jews, only Assyrians.

---

## Hezekiah's Response: Prayer and Trust

In the face of complete defeat, Hezekiah does something remarkable. He tears off his clothes, puts on sackcloth, and goes to the temple of Yahweh. He summons the prophet Isaiah:

> "This is what Hezekiah says: This day is a day of distress and rebuke and disgrace, as when children come to the point of birth and there is no strength to deliver them. It may be that the LORD your God will hear the words of the field commander, whom his master, the king of Assyria, has sent to ridicule the living God, and that he will rebuke him for the words the LORD your God has heard. Therefore pray for the remnant that still survives." (Isaiah 37:3-4)

Hezekiah's words reveal the desperation: a nation in labor with no strength to deliver itself. The kingdom of Israel is gone. Judah is reduced to a remnant—46 cities conquered, only Jerusalem remains.

### Isaiah's Word: Do Not Be Afraid

Isaiah brings God's reply:

> "Do not be afraid of what you have heard—those words with which the underlings of the king of Assyria have blasphemed me. Listen! I am going to put a spirit in him so that when he hears a certain report, he will return to his own country, and there I will have him cut down with the sword." (Isaiah 37:6-7)

God's word is clear: Sennacherib will leave Jerusalem and return to his own land, where Yahweh will destroy him.

---

## Sennacherib's Letter: A Direct Attack on God

Before this happens, Sennacherib sends a letter—a final, comprehensive challenge. This time, his attack on Yahweh is direct:

> "Do not let the god you depend on deceive you when he says, 'Jerusalem will not be handed over to the king of Assyria.' Surely you have heard what the kings of Assyria have done to all the countries, destroying them completely. And will you be delivered?" (Isaiah 37:10-11)

Sennacherib lists the gods he has defeated—the gods of Mesopotamia and Syria. The message is unmistakable: your God is just another idol I'll crush.

---

## Hezekiah's Prayer: Based on God's Character

Now we reach the heart of the passage. Hezekiah takes the letter to the temple and spreads it before the LORD:

> "O LORD Almighty, God of Israel, enthroned between the cherubim, you alone are God over all the kingdoms of the earth. You have made heaven and earth." (Isaiah 37:16)

Hezekiah's prayer recognizes the crucial distinction: Sennacherib has defeated gods made of wood and stone—human creations. But Yahweh is the God who *made* humanity, who created heaven and earth.

> "It is true, O LORD, that the Assyrian kings have laid waste all these peoples and their lands. They have thrown their gods into the fire and destroyed them, for they were not gods but only wood and stone, fashioned by human hands." (Isaiah 37:18-19)

It's like someone winning a children's athletic competition and then claiming to be the world champion. You must compare like with like. Yahweh is not in the same category as these idols.

And so Hezekiah prays with righteous confidence:

> "Now, O LORD our God, deliver us from his hand, so that all kingdoms on earth may know that you alone, O LORD, are God." (Isaiah 37:20)

---

## Yahweh's Reply: Complete Vindication

God's response comes in several parts:

### Jerusalem Will Mock Assyria

> "The Virgin Daughter of Zion despises and mocks you. The Daughter of Jerusalem tosses her head as you flee." (Isaiah 37:22)

### Assyria's Crime: Arrogance

> "By your messengers you have heaped insults on the Lord. And you have said, 'With my many chariots I have ascended the heights of the mountains, the utmost heights of Lebanon. I have cut down its tallest cedars, the choicest of its pines. I have reached its remotest heights, the finest of its forests. I have dug wells in foreign lands and drunk the water there. With the soles of my feet I have dried up all the streams of Egypt.'" (Isaiah 37:24-25)

Sennacherib claims credit for his conquests, as though his power is his own. But God corrects him:

> "Have you not heard? Long ago I ordained it. In days of old I planned it; now I have brought it to pass, that you have turned fortified cities into piles of stone." (Isaiah 37:26)

All of it—every conquest, every victory—was part of God's plan, not Sennacherib's achievement.

### Assyria Will Not Conquer Jerusalem

God promises a sign to Hezekiah:

> "This year you will eat what grows by itself, and the second year what springs from that. But in the third year sow and reap, plant vineyards and eat their fruit. Once more a remnant of the house of Judah will take root below and bear fruit above. For out of Jerusalem will come a remnant, and out of Mount Zion a band of survivors. The zeal of the LORD Almighty will accomplish this." (Isaiah 37:30-32)

And the direct command:

> "Therefore this is what the LORD says concerning the king of Assyria: 'He will not enter this city or shoot an arrow here. He will not come before it with shield or build a siege ramp against it. By the way that he came he will return; he will not enter this city,' declares the LORD." (Isaiah 37:33-34)

### Sennacherib's Fate

The irony is devastating:

> "Then the angel of the LORD went out and put to death a hundred and eighty-five thousand men in the Assyrian camp... So Sennacherib king of Assyria broke camp and withdrew. He returned to Nineveh and stayed there. One day, while he was worshiping in the temple of his god Nisroch, his sons Adrammelech and Sharezer cut him down with the sword." (Isaiah 37:36-38)

Sennacherib, who mocked God's power to protect, is murdered in the temple of his own god by his own sons. The one who claimed Yahweh was impotent is cut down by his own flesh and blood.

## Application: On Whom Are You Depending?

The field commander's original question still echoes across the centuries: "On whom are you depending?"

In 2008, we face the same choice as Hezekiah did in 701 BC. We're tempted by modern half-truths that pull us away from dependence on God.

### The HSC: The Half-Truth of Success

Consider how the HSC is promoted: "Do well on this exam and you're set for life. You'll get into a good course, secure a good career, and everything will work out."

This contains truth. The HSC matters. But it's only half the truth.

Some students give everything to this one exam—tutoring for every subject, abandoning chores for two years, neglecting relationships, giving up extracurricular activities, isolating themselves in their rooms, reading nothing that's not on the syllabus. The sacrifice suggests that all of life's purpose depends on this single test.

But this is unsustainable and unrealistic:

- **The lifestyle doesn't transfer:** If you burn yourself out studying for the HSC, what happens at university? In your career? In marriage? Will you take tutoring forever?

- **Rote learning produces ignorance:** The more education becomes about memorization, the less educated people actually become. We stop learning *how to think*.

- **You might lose what matters most:** I know of a year 12 student who gave up Christianity for a year to focus on his studies—no Bible, no church, no youth group. He did well on the HSC. But he got so comfortable without God that he never returned to faith. He became dependent on his studies instead of depending on God.

### Romantic Relationships: The Half-Truth of Passion

"That special person you really like—find true love and you'll be happy forever."

This also contains truth. Romantic relationships are good. It's wonderful that many of you are finding future spouses. And yes, passion matters.

But it's only half the truth. The complete truth is harder: **marriage is difficult. Raising a family is difficult. Finding common ground beyond physical attraction is difficult.**

Growing up on Australian soaps and Hollywood fairy tales, we're fed a half-truth: that love conquers all, that passion is enough. But consider this reality: if you marry someone who doesn't share your faith in Jesus, what are you building on?

Perhaps they identify as Christian. Perhaps they don't. Either way, if they don't have Jesus as their foundation, what happens when:

- They want your children raised differently?
- You disagree about how to spend money?
- Life gets hard and you need spiritual resources to endure?

At best, you're mere acquaintances flowing through life on superficial compatibility. At worst, you're both hypocrites, pretending you have more in common than you do.

One survey found that Australian couples with a regular partner had sex an average of 1.84 times per week. That's twice a week—leaving 10,000+ waking hours for everything else. If passionate attraction is all you share, that's going to be a profoundly boring marriage.

Yet our selection of a spouse is based almost entirely on how they look and how much passion we feel. It's the same half-truth that tempted Judah: "Trust in what you can see and feel, not in what you cannot."

### Real Estate: The Half-Truth of Security

"Buy a quarter-acre house with four bedrooms and a study. Own your home. Build your dream. This is the Australian way."

Again, there's truth here. You need shelter. Over time, ownership is often cheaper than renting. But it's only half the story:

- **Houses are growing while families are shrinking:** The average family size is 2.6 people, yet the average house size keeps expanding.

- **Ownership becomes obsession:** A house isn't just shelter; it becomes an investment to pay off as quickly as possible. Then you buy another. The cycle never ends.

- **The pursuit corrupts integrity:** Some will compromise their values to own property—working themselves to death, neglecting family duties (under the guise of "doing it for the kids"), cheating on taxes to make one more mortgage payment.

- **The rat race starts young:** The path to a "good" house requires a "good" job, which requires a "good" degree from a "good" university, which requires a "good" HSC score, which requires the "best" school, which requires the "best" primary school, which requires getting into the "best" OC class at age 7. Childhood is lost before it begins.

The "Great Australian Dream"—born from Menzies' post-war desire to combat socialism through home ownership—has become a half-truth that drives people to sacrifice everything.

### The Pattern: Half-Truths Everywhere

It's deeply human to think this way. We all desire security. Success in this world does provide some security. But the danger is losing perspective—forgetting what we're sacrificing to chase these dreams.

I'm not saying to do your worst or to stop working hard. Hard work has its place. But we must keep proper perspective: **What are we actually giving up to pursue these dreams? Is it worth the cost?**

---

## The Counter-Truth: A Different Kind of Security

In this room are people who have decided differently. They've chosen not to follow the half-truths of the world:

- They've decided that life isn't about wealth, happiness, or sex, but about obedience to Jesus Christ.
- They've decided that life isn't about securing themselves, but about serving others.
- They've decided that life isn't about accumulation, but about contentment.
- They've decided that a good spouse is measured by godliness, not appearance or personality.

To the world, these people look like losers. But Jesus asked a penetrating question:

> "What good is it for a man to gain the whole world, yet forfeit his soul?" (Matthew 16:26)

### The True Promised Land

As we trace through Scripture, we discover that the promised land isn't a plot of dirt in the Hills District—or anywhere on earth. The real promised land is kept in the future: a new heaven and earth where sin no longer reigns, where tears no longer flow, where righteousness dwells, and where God lives physically with His people.

That's the truth we must live by. Not the half-truths of Sennacherib, but the complete truth of Yahweh.

---

## Conclusion: A Strategy of Prayerful Dependence

As you go about your week, remember Hezekiah's choice. When faced with Assyria's compelling half-truths, he chose to depend on Yahweh's complete truth.

Prayer is the outward expression of this dependence. It's our continual acknowledgment that we need God to sustain and save us.

When you're in trouble and convicted of your own sinfulness and lack of dependence, call on your Father in heaven. Ask His forgiveness. Ask Him to sustain you as you wait for His Son to return.

Pray according to what you know of God's character:

- Pray that He will give you a future land, not a temporary one, so you won't be disappointed if earthly dreams don't materialize.
- Pray that the Father will protect you from Satan's deceptions and the world's half-truths.
- Pray that you'll have the wisdom and courage to depend on His truth, even when the world's promises sound more compelling.

This is the lesson of Isaiah 36-37: **Depend on Yahweh's truth rather than the half-truths of the world.**
