# 2 Samuel 13:1–22

**Title:** Downfall Dynasty  
**Date:** 21 October 2023  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction

We continue our study through 2 Samuel, examining one of Scripture's most difficult passages. This passage depicts the unfolding consequences of David's sin—not merely personal consequences, but a wide-ranging downfall that affects his entire family and eventually his kingdom. Last week we studied David and Bathsheba's adultery. Now we see how that sin triggers a cascade of destruction through David's household, beginning with his family.

This is a multi-generational problem, much like the ongoing conflicts we see in our world today.

## Part 1: Incest (verses 1–9)

> In the course of time, Amnon son of David fell in love with Tamar, the beautiful sister of Absalom son of David.

**Amnon** was David's oldest son and his assumed heir, born to David's second wife Ahinoam of Jezreel during David's reign in Judah. **Tamar** was a daughter of King David, sister of Absalom, and known for her beauty. Her mother was Maacah, David's third wife.

The phrase "fell in love" translates the Hebrew word *ahav* (love), but here it carries the sense of lustful desire—he chose her with intent to possess.

> Amnon became so obsessed with his sister Tamar that he made himself ill. She was a virgin, and it seemed impossible for him to do anything to her.

The law explicitly forbade such relationships:

> "Do not have sexual relations with your sister, either your father's daughter or your mother's daughter, whether she was born in the same home or elsewhere." (Leviticus 18:9)

Beyond the spiritual prohibition, incest carries biological consequences—genetic diseases result from such unions. More significantly, sexual relationships between family members create a dangerous conflict of interest. A guardian's fiduciary duty toward those under his protection cannot coexist with sexual exploitation; you cannot produce victims from your own family while holding authority over them.

### Bad Advice

> Now Amnon had an adviser named Jonadab son of Shimeah, David's brother. Jonadab was a very shrewd man. He asked Amnon, "Why do you, the king's son, look so haggard morning after morning? Won't you tell me?"

Jonadab, another member of the royal family, was known for his cunning. He notices Amnon's deteriorating appearance and presses him for the reason.

> Amnon said to him, "I'm in love with Tamar, my brother Absalom's sister."

The painful reminder—"my brother's sister"—emphasizes the wrongness of his desire.

> "Go to bed and pretend to be ill," Jonadab said. "When your father comes to see you, say to him, 'I would like my sister Tamar to come and give me something to eat. Let her prepare the food in my sight so I may watch her and then eat it from her hand.'"

This advice is deeply troubling. How does watching her cook remedy his condition? Why would a shrewd man encourage such a plan? He seems to have misunderstood—or perhaps intentionally ignored—the real problem. The scheme shows cunning in manipulating David through false concern for his health, but it leads to catastrophe.

## Part 2: Rape (verses 9–18)

David accepts the request and sends Tamar to care for his sick son. She obeys, preparing special bread for him in his presence.

> Then she took the pan and served him the bread, but he refused to eat.

> "Send everyone out of here," Amnon said. So everyone left him. Then Amnon said to Tamar, "Bring the food here into my bedroom so I may eat from your hand." And Tamar took the bread she had prepared and brought it to her brother Amnon in his bedroom.

Once they're alone:

> But when she took it to him to eat, he grabbed her and said, "Come to bed with me, my sister." "No, my brother!" she said to him.

The narrator emphasizes their kinship—"my sister," "my brother"—to underscore the violation.

> "Don't force me! Such a thing should not be done in Israel! Don't do this wicked thing. What about me? Where could I get rid of my disgrace? And what about you? You would be like one of the fools in Israel. Please speak to the king; he will not keep me from being married to you."

Tamar's response is complex. She protests the wrongness of the act, invoking Israel's moral law. Yet her final statement—that the king would permit their marriage—is puzzling. Was this a delaying tactic, or did she genuinely believe it possible?

The historical reality is sobering: incest was common in ancient royal families seeking to preserve bloodlines. King Tut married his sister. Cleopatra married her brother. Hawaiian and Siamese royalty practiced it. Even in Israel's history, precedent existed. Abraham married his half-sister (Genesis 20:12). David himself may have had an incestuous relationship with Abigail, his wife (1 Samuel 25:42), who shared a name with his sister (1 Chronicles 2:16). And we might ask: whom did Adam and Eve's children marry?

But now the violation escalates:

> But he refused to listen to her, and since he was stronger than she, he raped her.

The law pronounced severe consequences for such acts. Under Deuteronomy 22, if a man raped a betrothed virgin, both were to be stoned. If he raped a virgin in the countryside where she could not be heard, only the man was executed—it was considered equivalent to murder. But if he raped an unattached virgin like Tamar, he had to pay her father a bride price and marry her, forfeiting the right to divorce:

> "If a man happens to meet a virgin who is not pledged to be married and rapes her and they are discovered, he shall pay her father fifty shekels of silver. He must marry the young woman, for he has violated her. He can never divorce her as long as he lives." (Deuteronomy 22:28–29)

This law seems harsh to modern sensibilities, but it served a purpose: it made the rapist responsible for the woman's welfare and the child born from the assault. One evil does not nullify another. Bad things happen—the question is how we live with their consequences. Rather than seeking to reset reality like a video game, we must accept responsibility and move forward through the difficulty.

Had Amnon married Tamar as the law required, he would have borne lifelong responsibility. But then comes the twist:

> Then Amnon hated her with intense hatred. In fact, he hated her more than he had loved her. Amnon said to her, "Get up and get out!" "No!" she said to him. "Sending me away would be a greater wrong than what you have already done to me." But he refused to listen to her. He called his personal servant and said, "Get this woman out of my sight and bolt the door after her." So his servant put her out and bolted the door after her.

The reversal is stark. His "love" transforms to violent hatred. He doesn't merely order her out—he has his servant forcibly remove her and bolt the door, denying her even basic human dignity. 

Amnon is now guilty of two grave sins: rape and abandonment. He refuses responsibility for what he has done.

## Part 3: Avoiding Responsibility (verses 18–22)

> She was wearing an ornate robe, for this was the kind of garment the virgin daughters of the king wore. Tamar put ashes on her head and tore the ornate robe she was wearing. She put her hands on her head and went away, weeping aloud as she went.

Tamar's visible mourning—ashes, torn clothing, loud weeping—announces her desolation to all who see her. She does not hide her violation.

> Her brother Absalom said to her, "Has that Amnon, your brother, been with you? Be quiet for now, my sister; he is your brother. Don't take this thing to heart." And Tamar lived in her brother Absalom's house, a desolate woman.

Absalom's response is puzzling. What does fraternity have to do with silence? Perhaps he means that she should refrain from demanding marriage, since union with a brother would be incestuous. Yet his command to silence and his instruction not to grieve suggest something more troubling—he appears to be telling her to accept the violation without response.

> And Absalom never said a word to Amnon, either good or bad; he hated Amnon because he had disgraced his sister Tamar.

Absalom says nothing to Amnon—no rebuke, no confrontation. His silence masks a growing hatred that will fester for two years.

> When King David heard all this, he was furious.

David's anger is kindled, but significantly—he takes no action. He neither punishes Amnon nor comforts Tamar. No consequence follows.

### The Pattern of Avoidance

This scene echoes the previous chapter. Just as David sought to avoid responsibility for his adultery with Bathsheba by having her husband Uriah killed, Amnon now avoids responsibility by casting Tamar away. The sin spirals downward.

Kenny teaches that this generational pattern is not primarily genetic but ideological—a passing down of mindset and character. If a father steals, his children will steal. If he lies, they will lie. If he finds loopholes and avoids responsibility, will they not do the same? They reflect their parents.

Yet there is a complicating factor: David did repent of his sin (as we saw last chapter). Sometimes in overcompensating for past guilt, we swing to the other extreme. How can David lecture his son about sexual ethics when he himself has failed? We fear being hypocritical. In our contemporary culture, we celebrate "integrity" and avoid seeming contradictory, so we withdraw from guiding our children morally.

But this misunderstands parenting. Our role is not to guide them to our standard—which may be flawed—but to God's standard, which is objective and unchanging. We may confess our failures while calling them to obedience: "Do what I say, not what I do. Follow Jesus, not me."

David's failure to act creates a vacuum that Absalom will fill with revenge.

## Part 4: Murder and Dynasty's Fall (verses 23–39)

Two years pass in silence and brewing resentment.

> Two years later, when Absalom's sheepshearers were at Baal Hazor near the border of Ephraim, he invited all the king's sons to come there.

Absalom carefully orchestrates an invitation to a festive gathering. When David declines to attend, Absalom presses him to send Amnon:

> Then Absalom said, "If not, please let my brother Amnon come with us." The king asked him, "Why should he go with you?" But Absalom urged him, so he sent with him Amnon and the rest of the king's sons.

> Absalom ordered his men, "Listen! When Amnon is in high spirits from drinking wine and I say to you, 'Strike Amnon down,' then kill him. Don't be afraid. Haven't I given you this order? Be strong and brave." So Absalom's men did to Amnon what Absalom had ordered.

The murder is deliberate and premeditated. Absalom uses hospitality as a cover for violence—the same hospitality Tamar extended when she prepared food. It echoes the betrayal of Judas.

The news reaches David in exaggerated form:

> While they were on their way, the report came to David: "Absalom has struck down all the king's sons; not one of them is left." The king stood up, tore his clothes and lay down on the ground; and all his attendants stood by with their clothes torn.

But Jonadab, the same shrewd adviser who engineered Amnon's sin, appears again:

> But Jonadab son of Shimeah, David's brother, said, "My lord should not think that they killed all the princes; only Amnon is dead. This has been Absalom's express intention ever since the day Amnon raped his sister Tamar."

Only Amnon is dead. David's anguish gives way to relief, then to mourning as his sons return:

> As he finished speaking, the king's sons came in, wailing loudly. The king, too, and all his attendants wept very bitterly. Absalom fled and went to Talmai son of Ammihud, the king of Geshur. But King David mourned many days for his son. After Absalom fled and went to Geshur, he stayed there three years. And King David longed to go to Absalom, for he was consoled concerning Amnon's death.

David's family—his dynasty—disintegrates. The sword foretold in God's judgment (2 Samuel 12:10) begins its work.

Note the irony: Amnon's punishment by death exceeds what the law prescribed. The law did not mandate execution for rape or even incest with a sibling. Absalom's murder—however justified it might feel—is itself a crime that demands blood.

The cycle repeats: David's generation witnessed sexual sin followed by murder (his adultery with Bathsheba, then the death of Uriah). Now Amnon's generation repeats the pattern—sexual sin followed by murder. The curse spoken over David's house is working itself out through his children.

> "The fathers ate sour grapes, and the children's teeth are set on edge." (Jeremiah 31:28–29)

## Application: Taking Responsibility

The overarching message of this passage is simple yet difficult: we must accept responsibility for our sins. God will work through that responsibility toward restoration and redemption.

We see this truth illustrated in the contrasts throughout:
- Amnon refuses responsibility → he is murdered
- Absalom avoids responsibility through silence → he becomes a murderer
- David avoids responsibility through inaction → his family collapses

By contrast, when David repented (last chapter), God promised him grace. The way forward in Scripture is never to hide, justify, or pass our problems to others. It is to acknowledge what we have done, accept the consequences, and trust God's redemptive work even in the midst of difficulty.

This is particularly relevant when we face the results of our own poor choices or the sins of those before us. We cannot reset reality. We cannot pretend the problem didn't happen. We cannot shift blame. We must look honestly at what has occurred and say, "I will take responsibility for my part and move forward."
