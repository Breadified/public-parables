# 1 Peter 3:18–4:6

**Title:** Waiting for Christmas

**Date:** 24 December 2018

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Waiting for Jesus Before Jesus

The opening of this passage contains one of Scripture's most puzzling moments: Christ preaching to imprisoned spirits. Peter begins with the cross and its redemptive purpose, then moves into mysterious territory.

> For Christ also suffered once for sins, the righteous for the unrighteous, to bring you to God. He was put to death in the body but made alive in the Spirit. After being made alive, he went and made proclamation to the imprisoned spirits—to those who were disobedient long ago when God waited patiently in the days of Noah while the ark was being built.

Throughout church history, theologians have wrestled with what "preaching to spirits in prison" means. Some have proposed purgatory—a state between heaven and hell where sins can be purged away. But this contradicts the sufficiency of Christ's death. If Christ has truly brought us to God through His death, there should be nothing more to pay.

Others suggest fallen angels, yet this lacks textual support. Still others interpret it as addressing people alive during Jesus' earthly ministry who have since died. But the most compelling interpretation connects this to the Old Testament faithful—those who died before Christ came.

### The Promise to the Dead

When we look at Isaiah 61, we find language of preaching to captives and prisoners:

> The Spirit of the Sovereign LORD is on me, because the LORD has anointed me to preach good news to the poor. He has sent me to bind up the brokenhearted, to proclaim freedom for the captives and release from darkness for the prisoners.

But who are these captives and prisoners? Not criminals requiring justice, but something far more profound—death itself. The Old Testament speaks of Sheol or Hades, the place of the dead. It's not torment like hell (Gehenna), but rather the prison of death itself.

The Psalms repeatedly express this reality:

> But God will redeem my soul from the power of Sheol, for He will receive me. (Psalm 49:15)

> You have delivered my soul from the depths of Sheol. (Psalm 86:13)

> When you ascended on high, you took many captives; you received gifts from people, even from the rebellious—that you, Lord God, might dwell there. (Psalm 68:18)

### The Hope for Old Testament Saints

Peter addresses believers facing exile and persecution by reminding them: God's promises to the ancient Israelites were fulfilled. Those Old Testament saints who died before Christ came were not left behind. Christ's preaching to them—whether spiritually or in fulfillment of Isaiah's promise—proclaims that their hope has come true.

The Apostles' Creed captures this: "He descended into hell" (or the place of the dead). Christ didn't abandon those who had waited for Him in faith.

### The Application: Faith Across the Ages

This raises the Sunday school question many have asked: What happens to people who died before Jesus came? They never heard His name, never read the gospel. How can they be saved?

The answer is profound: **Christ remembers them.** Old Testament believers had faith in the future Messiah, just as we have faith in the past work of Christ. They looked forward; we look back. The direction differs, but the object is the same—Jesus Christ.

Being born in a different era doesn't disqualify you from salvation. Faith in Christ is effective for those born before His incarnation just as it will be for those born after His return.

### God's Faithfulness in Judgment

The reference to Noah isn't random. Isaiah spoke to the Jewish exiles about Noah:

> In a surge of anger I hid my face from you for a moment, but with everlasting kindness I will have compassion on you, says the LORD your Redeemer. To me this is like the days of Noah, when I swore that the waters of Noah would never again cover the earth. So now I have sworn not to be angry with you, never to rebuke you again. (Isaiah 54:8-9)

God judged the world in Noah's time, yet He carefully preserved eight people. The waters that destroyed the wicked became waters of salvation for the righteous. God is patient in judgment—He doesn't simply destroy everything. He judges carefully, preserving His people.

This is God's character: patient, faithful to His promises, working salvation even in the midst of judgment.

## Waiting for Jesus After Jesus

Now Peter calls Christians to patience in their own age. Christ's suffering becomes the model for ours.

> Therefore, since Christ suffered in his body, arm yourselves also with the same attitude, because whoever suffers in the body is done with sin. As a result, they do not live the rest of their earthly lives for evil human desires, but rather for the will of God.

### The Impatient Life

Peter then describes the pagan lifestyle—the way of those who cannot wait:

> For you have spent enough time in the past doing what pagans choose to do—living in debauchery, lust, drunkenness, orgies, carousing and detestable idolatry. They are surprised that you do not join them in their reckless, wild living, and they heap abuse on you.

This is the portrait of impatience. No delayed gratification. Living only for immediate passions, the moment, desires. Reckless and wild, thinking only of self, unconcerned with long-term consequences or the needs of others. This is what happens when we refuse to wait.

Contrast this with self-control—the ability to wait. Consider what waiting produces:

- Sex within marriage, not before
- Assets before expenses
- Investment before the house
- Work before holiday
- Discipline before pleasure

Waiting is difficult. It requires faith that something better is coming. Like a child learning to open Christmas presents one at a time—delayed gratification teaches us to wait for what truly matters.

### The Patient Life

We who know Christ are those who wait patiently. We don't join them in their reckless living. And they are surprised by this.

> But they will have to give account to him who is ready to judge the living and the dead. (1 Peter 4:5)

This is the crucial truth: judgment is coming.

### The Sufficiency of Christ's Death

Let's return to the foundation Peter laid:

> For Christ also suffered once for sins, the righteous for the unrighteous, to bring you to God. (1 Peter 3:18)

This is the Christmas proclamation—salvation came. Christ died, the righteous for the unrighteous, to accomplish one purpose: **to bring you to God.**

This ancient truth comes from Isaiah 53, where the prophet foresaw the suffering servant:

> But he was pierced for our transgressions, he was crushed for our iniquities; the punishment that brought us peace was upon him, and by his wounds we are healed. We all, like sheep, have gone astray, each of us has turned to his own way; and the LORD has laid on him the iniquity of us all. (Isaiah 53:5-6)

### Baptism and Identification with Christ's Death

Peter connects this to baptism:

> This water symbolizes baptism that now saves you also—not the removal of dirt from the body but the pledge of a clear conscience toward God. It saves you by the resurrection of Jesus Christ, who has gone into heaven and is at God's right hand—with angels, authorities and powers in submission to him. (1 Peter 3:21-22)

Baptism isn't about the water itself or the physical act of cleansing. It's about **identifying yourself with Christ's death**. Like Noah, who took his stand with God despite ridicule, baptism says: "I am on God's side."

When you join in His death through baptism, you join in His resurrection. He has gone into heaven and sits at God's right hand. Your identity is changed. Death is no longer the end—resurrection follows.

This truth transforms both past and future. Consider a changed Facebook profile picture that says "FORGIVEN"—it alters how all past entries appear and colors all future ones. So Christ's resurrection changes everything about us, backward and forward.

### Our Waiting Now

Yet we still wait. The community can be ugly toward Christians. In my anxiety, I wonder: what's taking so long? But Christ preaches good news to us who are prisoners—prisoners to sin, slaves of our passions and lusts.

> No longer slaves to sin. To bring you to God.

But the second coming will close the offer of salvation. It will be a time of judgment. This will be the last Christmas.

## Waiting for the Last Christmas

> There will be a last Christmas. When he visits us once more not to bring salvation but to bring judgment.

We are approaching the 2018th anniversary of the first Christmas. This is our time to get right with God, to sort out whether our final journey leads to heaven or to eternal death.

For those who might not yet know Jesus personally: I invite you to get to know Him better. This season of grace is real, but it is not endless. One day, the offer closes and the judgment comes.

Peter preaches a message of hope rooted in hard truths. Christ came once to save. He will come again to judge. Between these two Christmases, we wait—with patience, with faith, with the certain hope that God keeps His promises to all who trust in Him.
