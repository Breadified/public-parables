## Introduction: What Is Grit?

Over a century ago, Francis Galton studied eminent figures across politics, sport, art, music, and science to understand why some people succeed while others fail. He concluded that high achievement comes from a combination of three things: ability, zeal, and the capacity for hard labor. His cousin Charles Darwin affirmed this insight: "Excepting fools, men did not differ much in intellect, only in zeal and hard work."

More recently, Dr. Angela Lee Duckworth, a psychology professor at the University of Pennsylvania, coined the term "grit" to describe what she observed as a primary school teacher: that IQ was not the best predictor of student success. Instead, it was perseverance—the ability to stick with something difficult until completion.

Duckworth's research across Ivy League students, West Point cadets, and National Spelling Bee finalists consistently showed that grit outpredicted intelligence. Grittier students earned higher GPAs, cadets were more likely to complete their training, and spellers with more perseverance won competitions. She concluded that perseverance may be as important as—if not more important than—intelligence in determining success.

Actor Will Smith captures this perfectly: "I've never really viewed myself as particularly talented. I've viewed myself as slightly above average in talent. And where I excel is ridiculous, sickening work ethic. I am not afraid to die on a treadmill. You might have more talent than me, you might be smarter than me, but if we get on a treadmill together, there are two things: you are getting off first, or I am gonna die."

This capacity for discipline and perseverance begins at home, in our relationship with our fathers.

## Part 1: Fathers on Earth (5-8)

### The Importance of Paternal Discipline

The author of Hebrews compares God's discipline to the discipline of earthly fathers:

> My son, do not make light of the Lord's discipline, and do not lose heart when he rebukes you, because the Lord disciplines the one he loves, and he chastens everyone he accepts as his son. (Proverbs 3)

A loving father disciplines his children. From childhood, we experience this discipline in countless small ways: learning to use a spoon, eating vegetables, waking on time, doing mathematics, exercising, attending school, doing chores, attending classes. Sometimes discipline involves pain: the naughty corner, scolding, physical correction. As we grow older, the methods change—no pocket money, being grounded, losing car privileges.

These disciplines may not feel pleasant in the moment, but they flow from love. A good father disciplines because he wants his child to flourish. Do we want to be people who cannot use utensils, cannot wake on time, cannot count, cannot care for themselves? Discipline is what every loving parent provides.

This personal discipline teaches character. My own father was not given to sentimental displays of affection, but I respect him deeply for the discipline he instilled in me. When school carnivals were compulsory, he refused to write excuses that were dishonest, even though other parents did. He had traveled 7,000 kilometers from his home country and changed his job so that our family could be educated here—he was not going to allow us to waste that sacrifice through dishonesty. That single act of holding us to a standard taught me honesty and commitment. I learned to show up to school, to church, to work, to my obligations, regardless of how I felt. This discipline set me up well for so many things in life.

### The Consequence of Absent Discipline

The opposite picture is the illegitimate child—the neglected child. Scripture warns:

> If you are not disciplined—and everyone undergoes discipline—then you are not legitimate, not true sons and daughters at all.

The neglected child, the child without a present and involved father, lacks the training necessary for human flourishing. This is not merely a biblical concern; it is a social reality documented extensively in modern research.

The fundamental predictor of child poverty in wealthy nations is not inequality or capitalism or overpopulation, but the absence of a stable, married mother and father. When fathers are absent, children suffer measurably. Senator Daniel Moynihan documented this reality in 1965, warning that the decline of the two-parent family was fueling poverty, unemployment, and crime. His warnings have only grown more urgent. In 1965, the out-of-wedlock birth rate among white Americans was 3 percent; today it exceeds 29 percent. Among Black Americans, it has risen from 25 percent to 72 percent. Among Hispanics, it stands at 53 percent.

When half to three-quarters of children are born to single mothers, we inevitably see widespread poverty, disenfranchisement, lack of training, unemployment, gang involvement, and the cycle perpetuating itself across generations. President Obama himself acknowledged this reality: "We've got single moms out here, they're heroic what they're doing, and we're so proud of them. But at the same time, I wish I had had a father who was around and involved."

A child can also be legitimate—have two parents—yet still be undisciplined. Such a child is called "spoiled," which literally means ruined, good for nothing. Discipline requires not just the presence of a father, but his active, loving engagement.

### Application: Train Your Children Now

Parents, do not neglect your children. Train them while they are young, before habits are set. When children are young, each compromise you make exponentially multiplies: skip brushing teeth one night, and the next night the battle is seven times harder. Some seasons may require your presence at home rather than full work schedules. Schools alone cannot do this work.

As Proverbs teaches:

> Train up a child in the way he should go; even when he is old he will not depart from it.

### Application: Young People, Be Grateful

For those still living at home, you may not appreciate your parents' discipline now. But in the normal scheme of things—setting aside abuse or neglect—you will eventually thank them. Discipline is part of their love for you. As verse 9 says, "we respected them for it."

## Part 2: Father in Heaven (9-11)

### The Greater Discipline of God

If we respect and appreciate our earthly fathers for their discipline, how much more should we value the discipline of our heavenly Father?

> Moreover, we have all had human fathers who disciplined us and we respected them for it. How much more should we submit to the Father of spirits and live!

The contrast here is striking. Our earthly fathers disciplined us as they thought best, according to their limited understanding. But God, with infinite knowledge and wisdom, disciplines us for our eternal good:

> They disciplined us for a little while as they thought best; but God disciplines us for our good, in order that we may share in his holiness. No discipline seems pleasant at the time, but painful. Later on, however, it produces a harvest of righteousness and peace for those who have been trained by it.

Our earthly fathers acted on what seemed right to them, for a short time. God acts with perfect knowledge for our eternal benefit. Their discipline brought temporary discomfort; God's discipline produces a harvest of righteousness and peace.

### Understanding Suffering in Light of God's Fatherhood

We must reset our expectations about suffering. When hardship comes, many of us become embittered toward God, asking, "Why am I suffering?"

One answer is that suffering results from sin—from the brokenness of our world since Adam's fall. The natural state of creation is disorder; we live in a world groaning for liberation. But Scripture offers a complementary answer: God uses our present sufferings as training for us as Christians. Pain is weakness leaving the body.

> I consider that our present sufferings are not worth comparing with the glory that will be revealed in us. (Romans 8:18)

> And we know that in all things God works for the good of those who love him, who have been called according to his purpose. (Romans 8:28)

> No temptation has overtaken you except what is common to mankind. And God is faithful; he will not let you be tempted beyond what you can bear. But when you are tempted, he will also provide a way out so that you can endure it. (1 Corinthians 10:13)

Rather than view challenges in life negatively, we must remember that God is our Father, acting in love. Under the safety net of heaven:
- All hardships are chances to become holy and godly
- All trials are opportunities to understand the love of Christ more deeply
- All difficulties help us appreciate our lives on earth
- All struggles train us to serve others better

**John Piper's Example:** The famous pastor was diagnosed with cancer and wrote an article titled "Don't Waste Your Cancer." Some found this controversial, but his point was profound: cancer itself is not a gift, but he transformed his trouble into a gift by seeing it as an opportunity to trust God more deeply, to deepen his relationship with Him, to seek comfort from God rather than odds, to think seriously about death, to take sin more seriously as his appetite for pride, greed, lust, and laziness diminished, and to resolve to cherish Christ and witness to others. Piper saw that cancer was not a disability but a training opportunity.

**Nick Vujicic's Example:** Nick was born in Australia with a rare condition called tetra-amelia syndrome—he has no arms and no legs. At age eight, he struggled with God: "Why did you take my arms and legs away? Why not give me what everybody has?" But rather than remain bitter and self-pitying, Nick reconciled with God and became thankful for what he did have. With that understanding, he submitted himself to God's will. Instead of asking God for a miracle, he became a miracle to others. He has a cheerful disposition, does everything at 100 percent, is married, became a father, jumps out of airplanes, and swims. Because of his condition, God has used him to bring over 200,000 people to Christ around the world. Nick says: "What would you rather? I would rather have no arms and no legs temporarily on earth in order to reach someone else for Christ and spend eternity with them."

Nick's hardship made him who he is. He could have been just another spoiled child, but God trained him for service. As the Psalmist writes:

> A father to the fatherless, a defender of widows, is God in his holy dwelling. (Psalm 68:5)

Not all fathers are good. But we all have one perfectly good Father.

## Part 3: Be Disciplined (12:12-13)

### Strengthen Yourself Spiritually

> Therefore, strengthen your feeble arms and weak knees. Make level paths for your feet, so that the lame may not be disabled, but rather healed.

This famous verse is a favorite among gym enthusiasts, but it speaks to spiritual arms and spiritual knees. The author is calling us to stand firm, to be ready for God. Walk a straight path. Do not turn aside from the truth to false teaching, as Scripture warns elsewhere:

> Some have deviated from these and turned aside to fruitless discussion. (1 Timothy 1:6)

> Some have already turned away to follow Satan. (1 Timothy 5:15)

The call is clear: exercise daily and weekly with God's Word. Cultivate habits of Bible reading and prayer. Think and speak about the things of God. Attend church regularly. Just as abdominal crunches build core muscles, these disciplines strengthen your spiritual life and keep your paths straight.

### The Crisis of Our Generation

Yet we must be honest: our generation struggles profoundly with discipline. For Gen Y (born between 1982 and 1999), this has reached a crisis point. Youth unemployment approaches 40 percent, not primarily due to lack of jobs, but because employers find that young people cannot stick with commitments. We arrive late to work or not at all. We don't show up to job interviews. We post our absences on Facebook instead of calling. We resign by text message. When difficulties arise, we blame the government and demand entitlements.

This extends to basic life skills—we lack driving licenses, don't finish university courses we start, cannot cook or iron a shirt. We exhibit little of the grit previous generations possessed. Gen Y is becoming Generation Y Bother to Show Up.

This lack of discipline flows into every area of life:
- **In relationships:** Breaking promises about being present or on time shows a lack of love. Half our marriages end in divorce.
- **In our Christian life:** God becomes just another optional button on the Facebook of life—something we attend to when convenient.

I am concerned about this pattern. Perhaps this is why our church emphasizes certain habits: attending regularly, serving consistently and diligently, letting others know if we cannot attend, apologizing with good reason. From the outside, it may look legalistic and joyless. But we are trying to train in discipline—to keep the promises we make, to show up to our commitments.

One place to learn discipline is at home. Another is here at church.

### The Church as a Place of Voluntary Discipline

Yes, church is voluntary. We are saved by grace. Showing up doesn't make you a Christian. But it certainly helps. We welcome newcomers warmly—please come back whenever you can. But for those who have committed to this church family, think of your commitment like that of State Emergency Service volunteers. SES workers are unpaid; they have regular jobs. They volunteer freely, but once they sign up, they commit to regular training, to responding to emergencies, to putting community ahead of personal comfort. They drop their regular work when there is a brush fire threatening lives. That is discipline. Not "I'll show up when I feel like it. It's too hot, I'm going to the beach." They signed up to be committed, and it requires sacrifice.

We do the same here—not fighting brush fires, but warning of the fires of hell. If middle-aged people can set aside time to fight physical fires, surely those convinced of the need for others to know Christ can commit to church with equal seriousness.

As Woody Allen observed, "80% of success in life is showing up."

### What We Are Seeing—and What We Need

I am encouraged by improvements we are seeing:
- A growing number of you have committed to regular attendance and service, giving up precious time to serve one another and the community
- Young people are cooking lunch for us today—I know of no other church where young people serve the parents rather than vice versa
- Some of you are working to get your driving licenses
- Others are slowly but surely gaining employment and working hard

We do this not merely out of love for those who organize these things, not merely out of love for those depending on you, not merely for our own benefit (though we do become more reliable and employable). We do this for our spiritual life.

Consider this progression:
- If we cannot prioritize the things of God when we are students, how will we do so when we are working, when a boss is chasing us and pressure mounts?
- If we cannot prioritize God while working, how will we do so when we have the pressures of a young family—a spouse to please, children getting sick, a mortgage to pay?
- If we cannot do it as a student, as a young worker, as a parent—after a lifetime cultivating different habits—why would you ever think you'll change when the kids move out or in retirement? You will spend time on golf, not on God.

**Friends, NOW IS THE TIME TO GET DISCIPLINE.**

For HSC students, I know this is difficult timing, but prioritize the things of God. For year 11 students, train yourselves now. For young families, these are formative years. What habits will you establish? Will yours be merely a family, or a Christian family with certain values, habits, and disciplines?

Good habits formed now will last a lifetime.

## Conclusion: Finish the Race

Angela Lee Duckworth says: "I don't think anyone's figured out how to make people smarter, but these other qualities of grit may be teachable. If it's important for you to become one of the best people in your field, you are going to have to stick with it when it's hard."

Consider the story of Abebe Akhwari, who competed in the 1968 Olympic Marathon in Mexico City. About 19 kilometers into the 42-kilometer race, he fell badly. His knee was wounded, his shoulder struck the pavement hard, his leg bled, his knee appeared dislocated. Medical staff urged him to withdraw. But Akhwari continued—walking, limping, running slowly. He finished last among the 57 who completed the race, more than an hour after the winner crossed the finish line.

By then, the sun had set and only a few thousand people remained in the stadium. But as he finally crossed the finish line, a cheer arose from the small crowd. A television crew, diverted from a medal ceremony, captured this courageous man's agonizing finish.

When asked why he ignored the advice to quit, Akhwari replied: "My country did not send me 5,000 miles just to start the race; they sent me to finish the race."

Of the 75 who started that Olympic marathon, 18 others pulled out. No one remembers the gold medal winner's name. But we remember and honor Akhwari because he finished the race in the most difficult circumstances.

**You are in the race of your life. Finish it. Persevere.**
