# Luke 9:1-21

**Title:** A Party that Reveals Everything
**Date:** January 2003
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## What Makes a Party Successful?

We begin this new year with a simple question: How do you judge the success of a party? Is it the people who show up? The quality and quantity of food?

I once organized a New Year's Eve party with a friend. We had everything prepared—food, board games, videos. But when the evening came, only the three of us showed up. Then even that one other person left for the fireworks. Just my friend, myself, and Jesus remained.

Today's passage shows us a very different kind of party—one that tells us something profound about Jesus and what it means to follow him.

---

## A Successful Gathering

> **Luke 9:14-17** "About five thousand men were there." But he said to his disciples, "Have them sit down in groups of about fifty each." The disciples did so, and everyone sat down. Taking the five loaves and the two fish and looking up to heaven, he gave thanks and broke them. Then he gave them to the disciples to distribute to the people. They all ate and were satisfied, and the disciples picked up twelve basketfuls of broken pieces that were left over.

This passage describes what looks like an ideal party. It succeeds for at least two reasons.

### First: The Crowd Showed Up

Over five thousand people came—and if we count families, the number was far larger. How did they arrive at this remote place?

> **Luke 9:1-2** "When Jesus had called the Twelve together, he gave them power and authority to drive out all demons and to cure diseases, and he sent them out to proclaim the kingdom of God and to heal the sick."

The disciples had gone village to village proclaiming good news and healing people. Their message had spread, creating anticipation and hunger for more of Jesus. When Jesus withdrew to Bethsaida, seeking a quiet place, the crowds pursued him anyway.

> **Luke 9:10-11** "When the apostles returned, they reported to Jesus what they had done. Then he took them with him and they withdrew by themselves to a town called Bethsaida, but the crowds learned about it and followed him. He welcomed them and spoke to them about the kingdom of God, and healed those who needed healing."

Imagine that scene—waves of people arriving, following Jesus into this remote place. And what did Jesus do? He welcomed them. As always, he showed grace to those who sought him.

### Second: They Were Satisfied

Late in the afternoon, the disciples realized the practical problem:

> **Luke 9:12** "Late in the afternoon the Twelve came to him and said, 'Send the crowd away so they can go to the surrounding villages and countryside and find food and lodging, because we are in a remote place here.'"

We're in the middle of nowhere. The people are hungry. What's the solution?

> **Luke 9:13** "He replied, 'You give them something to eat.' They answered, 'We have only five loaves of bread and two fish—unless we go and buy food for all this crowd.'"

And then Jesus performed a miracle. The disciples didn't produce hidden supplies or make a "catering mistake." Jesus took those five loaves and two fish, gave thanks, broke them, and fed over five thousand people. And not just fed them—they were satisfied.

This echoes something profound from Israel's history. In the Exodus, God provided manna to a hungry people in the wilderness. There, too, people complained about their provisions until God sent a miracle. But here, Jesus provides not just bread but fish—abundance beyond what was asked.

When Jesus organizes a gathering and provides for his people, he doesn't do things halfway. His provision is complete.

### The Deeper Meaning: Eternal Satisfaction

> **Application:** But what does this teach us about true satisfaction?

Jesus doesn't offer food for a day. He offers a picture of food for eternity. You can get sick of even the finest seafood eventually. There's only so much any meal can satisfy. But the spiritual hunger that Jesus addresses—the deep human longing for meaning, purpose, completion—that's different.

I think of Mark Zuckerberg. Despite his billions, his successful company, his perfect family, he once said there must be something more. His vast wealth hasn't satisfied him. His remarkable achievements haven't satisfied him. Even the people closest to him haven't satisfied him. There's an ache in the human soul that only Jesus can fill.

Perhaps one day Mark Zuckerberg will discover what millions have found: that Jesus truly satisfies.

---

## The Tension: A Party That Divides

But here's where the passage reveals something unexpected. Look closely at verse 17:

> **Luke 9:17** "They all ate and were satisfied, and the disciples picked up twelve basketfuls of broken pieces that were left over."

Twelve basketfuls of leftovers.

There are two ways to read this. The first is simple: Jesus over-catered. But that doesn't align with how God worked. The manna in the desert satisfied and was complete—enough and nothing wasted. God's miracles aren't mistakes.

The second interpretation is more troubling: perhaps not enough people came. The food remained because the crowd was smaller than intended. And if that's true, we need to ask why.

The answer comes immediately after:

> **Luke 9:18-21** "Once when Jesus was praying in private and his disciples were with him, he asked them, 'Who do the crowds say I am?' They replied, 'Some say John the Baptist; others say Elijah; and still others, that one of the prophets of long ago has come back to life.' But what about you? he asked. 'Who do you say I am?' Peter answered, 'God's Messiah.' Jesus strictly warned them not to tell this to anyone. And he said, 'The Son of Man must suffer many things and be rejected by the elders, the chief priests and the teachers of the law, and he must be killed and on the third day be raised to life.'"

The crowds had divided opinions about Jesus. Some thought he was John the Baptist resurrected. Others thought Elijah had returned. But very few understood who he truly was. And even fewer would accept what he was about to tell them.

### The Party Killer

Then came the words that would thin the crowd:

> **Luke 9:23** "Then he said to them all: 'Whoever wants to be my disciple must deny themselves and take up their cross daily and follow me.'"

To be a Christian means to deny yourself and carry your cross daily. To take up your cross means to die. Daily means to die slowly, progressively. To follow Jesus means to die the way he died—with sacrificial love for others' wellbeing.

Who signs up for that? It's like an unpopular invitation. Who wants to be associated with a "loser," especially if it costs everything?

The way is narrow. Being Christian is hard. Not everyone came to this party. Not everyone will respond to Jesus. Many will hear the call to discipleship and walk away.

Yet here's the profound truth: this is our purpose. Our motivation isn't self-preservation but following Jesus' example. There's a deep sense of purpose in pouring out your life for something greater than yourself.

---

## Hope Beyond the Party

But the passage doesn't end in defeat. There's an optimistic turn:

> **Luke 9:24-26** "For whoever wants to save their life will lose it, but whoever loses their life for me will save it. What good is it for someone to gain the whole world, and yet lose or forfeit their very self? Whoever is ashamed of me and my words, the Son of Man will be ashamed of them when he comes in his glory and in the glory of the Father and of the holy angels."

This is only bad if we think short-term. Long-term, there's a different calculation entirely. When Jesus comes in his glory—surrounded by the Father's glory and the holy angels—that's the great party. That's the feast we're heading toward.

The twelve basketfuls take on new meaning too. If we remember the Exodus pattern with its twelve tribes, perhaps those baskets represent something hopeful: there's room for more. Lots more. A new people. Our God is generous. All are welcome. There is more than enough to satisfy.

---

## The Mission Ahead

As we start this new year together, let's remember our purpose. We're not here merely for our own spiritual satisfaction. We're learning about Jesus so we can tell others. Our purpose isn't self-focused but outward-focused—sharing the gospel with our children, our parents, our families, our friends, our colleagues, our strangers.

We're not five thousand gathered in the wilderness. I'm not Jesus, and this food didn't miraculously appear (it came from the hard work of dedicated people). But the hope is the same. Jesus is the one who truly satisfies his people, and he wants more to experience his sacrifice and grace.

As we gather in this lovely part of God's creation, longing for his kingdom to come, may we be invigorated. May we renew ourselves to carry out this mission together this year.
