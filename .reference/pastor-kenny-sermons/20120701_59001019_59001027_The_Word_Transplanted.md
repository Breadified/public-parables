# James 1:19-27

**Title:** The Word Transplanted  
**Date:** 29 July 2012  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## The Problem: Wasted Knowledge

I graduated from university about thirteen years ago, but I must confess it was largely a waste. I spent too much time in Chinatown and the newly built Market City, chased after too many parties, and studied law that proved entirely theoretical. The first time I actually entered a courtroom was when I was called for jury duty this year! My degree was wasted knowledge.

But knowledge of law pales in comparison to knowledge of God. We must treat biblical knowledge differently. James doesn't challenge us merely to know the theory of God—he challenges us to actually *live it out*. This is the heart of his message in this passage.

---

## 1. The Word Planted (v. 21)

> Therefore, get rid of all moral filth and the evil that is so prevalent and humbly accept the word planted in you, which can save you. (James 1:21)

The word James speaks of is the gospel—the good news of Jesus' death and resurrection. As Christians, this word is already planted within us. By faith, it becomes an integral part of who we are.

Consider a patient who receives a heart transplant. That new heart is not an accessory or temporary addition; it becomes the very center of their being, the source of their life. Similarly, God's word is transplanted into us—it is not external knowledge we can ignore or discard. This echoes the beautiful promise God made through Jeremiah:

> "This is the covenant I will make with the house of Israel after that time," declares the LORD. "I will put my law in their minds and write it on their hearts." (Jeremiah 31:33)

God promised His people a heart transplant—a transformation so fundamental that His word would be written on the very core of their being.

---

## 2. Get Rid of All Moral Filth (v. 21)

But a new heart demands new habits. James tells us to rid ourselves of all moral filth and the evil that is so prevalent. Just as a heart transplant patient must exercise and abandon fatty foods that once filled their diet, so we must abandon the spiritual poison that surrounds us.

Many of us don't truly believe that evil is everywhere. We tell ourselves that people are fundamentally good, that perhaps only a few aberrations exist at the margins of society. But Scripture tells a different story.

Consider the tragedy of Anders Breivik's massacre in Norway on July 22, 2011. He killed 78 people—eight in a bombing, then 69 more at a youth camp, many of them teenagers. The youngest victim was 14 years old. Some were lured out of hiding by someone posing as a police officer; others died jumping from cliffs to escape. One person called it "the deadliest massacre ever committed by a sole gunman." This is prevalent evil.

The press scrambled to make sense of it. "He must be mad," they said. "No sane person could commit such horrors. We are all born good, aren't we? Surely such evil requires insanity." But Breivik insisted he wasn't mad. Psychologists confirmed he was fully functioning, aware of his actions, and had planned this meticulously for three years. The Bible offers a clearer diagnosis: He is not mad—he is simply bad.

We resist this answer because it implicates us. We think like Confucius: "People at birth are naturally good. Their natures are similar; their habits become different." But Confucius was wrong. We are born naturally inclined toward evil, and that terrifies us because it means we too are capable of great wickedness.

Just days after this incident, an American armed himself with assault weapons and opened fire in a movie theater showing *The Dark Knight Rises*, killing 12 people. The youngest victim was six years old. The evil isn't confined to the shooter alone—why do democratic societies insist on the right to carry assault weapons? Why is a six-year-old awake at midnight watching a violence-rated film? We are all complicit in the evil that pervades our world.

This is precisely why we desperately need God's word—a word that tells us we are forgiven of our crimes, a word that gives us hope that we are slowly being transformed into the likeness of Jesus, the gracious and righteous Son of God.

## 3. Listen with Humility (v. 19-20)

> My dear brothers, take note of this: Everyone should be quick to listen, slow to speak and slow to become angry, for man's anger does not bring about the righteous life that God desires. (James 1:19-20)

What does anger have to do with listening? James is likely addressing our defensive reaction when we hear hard truths about ourselves. When God's word cuts us personally—as it inevitably does—we often respond with anger rather than openness.

> For the word of God is living and active. Sharper than any double-edged sword, it penetrates even to dividing soul and spirit, joints and marrow; it judges the thoughts and attitudes of the heart. Nothing in all creation is hidden from God's sight. Everything is uncovered and laid bare before the eyes of him to whom we must give account. (Hebrews 4:12-13)

When we feel exposed and judged by Scripture, we often resort to what I call the "sports strategy"—attack is the best form of defense. We raise our voices, show our temper, and defend ourselves fiercely. We do anything but listen. Yet our anger does not produce the righteousness God requires.

Instead of anger and proud arrogance, God wants us to listen with humility and teachability. As Isaiah says:

> "This is the one I esteem: he who is humble and contrite in spirit, and trembles at my word." (Isaiah 66:2)

Over the coming weeks, James will challenge us with many hard truths. Don't be defensive. Don't let pride wall you off from God's correction. Receive His criticism humbly, knowing that it flows from His love for us and His desire to change us.

---

## 4. Do the Word (v. 22-25)

> Do not merely listen to the word, and so deceive yourselves. Do what it says. (James 1:22)

Here is the crux of the matter: We excel at pretending to listen while doing nothing. Our children do this with us—they nod their heads, assure us they've heard, and then forget everything we said. We do the same with God.

James offers a humbling analogy:

> Anyone who listens to the word but does not do what it says is like a man who looks at his face in a mirror and, after looking at himself, goes away and immediately forgets what he looks like. (James 1:23-24)

Picture someone constantly checking the mirror—"Did I comb my hair? What about my nose hair?"—so distracted and forgetful they don't remember their own face. They forget they have a new heart. They forget the word is implanted in them. They forget who they are in Christ.

But look at verse 25:

> But the man who looks intently into the perfect law that gives freedom, and continues to do this, not forgetting what he has heard, but doing it—he will be blessed in what he does. (James 1:25)

Don't forget the gospel that has changed you. God's word is the perfect law that gives us freedom—not license to sin, but freedom to do good and live righteous lives. Continue to follow God. He promises blessing—the same happiness we discussed last week—to those who truly do His word.

Trials are precisely this kind of opportunity. They take our theory and put it into action. Some people know all the facts about solving a homicide but have never lived through one—wasted knowledge. I know everything about solving homicides from television, but I've never actually done it—wasted knowledge. We cannot afford to let our knowledge of God remain theoretical. It must become lived reality.

---

## 5. Pure Religion: Love in Action (v. 26-27)

> If anyone considers himself religious and yet does not keep a tight rein on his tongue, he deceives himself and his religion is worthless. Religion that God our Father accepts as pure and faultless is this: to look after orphans and widows in their distress and to keep oneself from being polluted by the world. (James 1:26-27)

This is where we truly do the word: Pure religion is loving the vulnerable. In biblical times, orphans and widows had no safety net, no social welfare system. Even today, widows in our church are vulnerable—they need friends, support, and community. Some among us are wives whose husbands work far away in Hong Kong, leaving them to navigate life alone. We have young people in our congregation whose parents have abandoned them here, and they need food, discipline, and transport.

You might expect James to define "pure religion" as prayer, fasting, or elaborate rituals. But he redefines it entirely as love for one another. As Jesus said:

> By this all men will know that you are my disciples, if you love one another. (John 13:35)

If you claim to be religious, let it be seen in how you love each other. Let the world see how radically different you are from its rampant selfishness and wickedness.

The Christian must not be separated from the new heart God has transplanted within us. God's word lives in us. We have been changed. Now let us listen to it. Let us do it. Let us love one another in ways that bear witness to the transformation Christ has wrought in us.
