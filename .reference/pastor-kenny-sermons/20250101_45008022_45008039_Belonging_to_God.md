# Romans 8:22-39

**Title:** Belonging to God
**Date:** 26 January 2025
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Groaning (vv. 22-27)

The creation itself reflects the pain we experience in our daily lives. Paul writes:

> We know that the whole creation has been groaning as in the pains of childbirth right up to the present time. (Romans 8:22)

The Greek word for "groaning" expresses deep pain—a sound uttered when words fail us. It's the cry of someone in profound suffering, like the oppressed Israelites in Egypt who groaned under slavery with no one to hear them except God. When words are useless and no one will listen, we can still groan.

This groaning is universal. We groan inwardly as we wait for the redemption of our bodies—not just physical pain, but spiritual and emotional anguish. Memories, regrets, intrusive thoughts, depression, the frustration of doing what we don't want and failing to do what we want. We groan because our bodies are not yet redeemed, still subject to pain and decay.

Paul frames this using the image of childbirth—the curse of Genesis playing out in humanity. Women groan in the pain of bearing and raising children. Men groan under the burden of work, becoming like slaves. We compound this suffering by resenting one another's roles rather than understanding that both are equally difficult.

### Preparing for Suffering

Being an adult means accepting that suffering is part of life. No parent will rescue us from hard work, criticism, heartbreak, or pain. We will face disease, failure, loneliness, and the simple grinding slowness of time itself. Even our instant-generation expectations reveal our groaning—when the internet goes down for a day, we barely survive. We groan in impatience.

The temptation is escapism—seeking distraction from pain through various means. But using breaks and rest to avoid our responsibilities to God and one another misuses these gifts.

### Instead, We Wait

Yet waiting is what hope truly means:

> For in this hope we were saved. But hope that is seen is no hope at all. Who hopes for what they already have? But if we hope for what we do not yet have, we wait for it patiently. (Romans 8:24-25)

Delayed gratification deepens joy. When we finally receive what we've waited for, it tastes twice as sweet.

### Instead, We Pray

When our suffering overwhelms us, we turn our groans into prayer. There may be no words—only groans—but God understands:

> In the same way, the Spirit helps us in our weakness. We do not know what we ought to pray for, but the Spirit himself intercedes for us through wordless groans. And he who searches our hearts knows the mind of the Spirit, because the Spirit intercedes for God's people in accordance with the will of God. (Romans 8:26-27)

When the problem is too great for words, we can be still and place our matter before God. The year ahead will be harder than the summer break—like stepping into a heatwave. We will be groaning. But we are not alone in that groaning.

## Glory (vv. 28-39)

### We Groan with Purpose

Our groaning is not meaningless or random. God is at work:

> And we know that in all things God works for the good of those who love him, who have been called according to his purpose. (Romans 8:28)

God's purpose is always good. It's not mindless chaos—the random accidents an atheist would claim characterize existence. Rather, God is finishing the task He began. Everything that happens serves His purpose: to strengthen us, teach us, comfort us, discipline us, create self-awareness, and make us into new people fit for Him.

### The Order of Salvation

> For those God foreknew he also predestined to be conformed to the image of his Son, that he might be the firstborn among many brothers and sisters. And those he predestined, he also called; those he called, he also justified; those he justified, he also glorified. (Romans 8:29-30)

God is creating a new family in the image of His Son—a new Adam. The logic flows from foreknowledge to predestination to calling (our conversion) to justification to glorification. While theologians call this the *ordo salutis* (order of salvation), we shouldn't get too hung up on the precise sequence. The parts overlap—foreknowledge and predestination sound similar, predestination appears twice, and some elements like faith and sanctification aren't explicitly listed. These may occur simultaneously at the moment of conversion rather than in strict sequence.

What matters is that everyone agrees on the endpoint: **Those he justified, he glorified.**

### Understanding Glory

The Hebrew word for glory means to be "heavy" or "weighty"—like precious gold, worthy of honor. In the temple, God's presence was heavy, filled with incense and smoke. When Jesus became incarnate, God was revealed as both weighty in holiness and full of grace and truth.

At the last trumpet, when Jesus returns, we will undergo instant transformation. The perishable will put on the imperishable. Yet even now, in a mysterious way, we behold God's glory and are being transformed from one degree of glory to another. Some things are likely to get better.

### Promises in This Life and the Next

There are biblical promises for our current life. Through sanctification and living according to God's design, where work is rewarded and rule of law is upheld, we can experience His blessing: mortgages paid off, growing wisdom with experience, children who eventually mature, medical breakthroughs, a growing church, freedom from addiction.

But there are no guarantees. The world is fallen, and false hopes are dangerous.

What is guaranteed is glory in heaven. Those He justifies, He glorifies. There will be no more sin, no more enemies, no wrath, no separation from God. We will be holy, immortal beings enjoying eternity in God's presence. This is certain because of what Jesus already accomplished.

### The Logic of God's Faithfulness

> What, then, shall we say in response to these things? If God is for us, who can be against us? He who did not spare his own Son, but gave him up for us all—how will he not also, along with him, graciously give us all things? (Romans 8:31-32)

We know God's promises are true because we see them fulfilled in Jesus. He didn't spare His own Son from death on the cross—the hardest thing. If He accomplished that, why would He withhold anything easier, like giving us heaven? If your parents would give you a million dollars, would they refuse you a dime?

### Nothing Can Separate Us

> Who will bring any charge against those whom God has chosen? It is God who justifies. Who then is the one who condemns? No one. Christ Jesus who died—more than that, who was raised to life—is at the right hand of God and is also interceding for us. Who shall separate us from the love of Christ? Shall trouble or hardship or persecution or famine or nakedness or danger or sword? No, in all these things we are more than conquerors through him who loved us. For I am convinced that neither death nor life, neither angels nor demons, neither the present nor the future, nor any powers, neither height nor depth, nor anything else in all creation, will be able to separate us from the love of God that is in Christ Jesus our Lord. (Romans 8:33-39)

Nothing can separate us from God's love. Not trouble, hardship, persecution, famine, danger, or death. Not even our own failures and stupidity.

We are protected. Inoculated. Our inheritance is kept safe in heaven where nothing rusts, fridges keep working, backaches don't exist, roofs don't leak. No more tears, no more pain. We will have new, sinless bodies.

That is where we are going. Glory.
