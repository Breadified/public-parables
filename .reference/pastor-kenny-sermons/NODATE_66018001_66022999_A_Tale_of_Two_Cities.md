## The Culmination of God's Plan

The final chapters of Revelation paint a breathtaking picture of divine victory and restoration. As we survey this sweeping narrative, we encounter three climactic movements:

- **Revelation 18**: The fall of spiritual Babylon—the world system opposed to God
- **Revelation 19**: Jesus is victorious over evil (Hallelujah!)
- **Revelation 20**: Jesus is victorious over Satan and brings final judgment

## Vision of the New Age (Revelation 21:1-8)

Revelation 21:1-8 introduces a comprehensive vision of the new age, which is then unpacked in greater detail through the remainder of the passage. The structure reveals how one vision is told multiple times from different angles, each emphasizing a particular facet of God's restoration:

| Verse | Corresponds to | Focus |
|-------|---|---|
| 21:2 | 21:10-21 | God's city |
| 21:3 | 21:22-27 | God's dwelling |
| 21:4-5a | 22:1-3 | God's word renewed |
| 21:5b | 22:6-10 | God's word validated |
| 21:6a | 22:11-15 | God's work completed |
| 21:6b-7 | 22:16-17 | God's final blessing |
| 21:8 | 22:18-19 | God's final curse |

## The New Jerusalem Revealed (Revelation 21:10-21)

In Revelation 4 and 5, we encountered a vision where everything centered on the Lamb—the slain, risen, exalted Jesus. Now, in chapter 21, we see a stunning development: the city of God itself becomes the focal point, prepared as a bride for the Lamb (21:9-10). This echoes Paul's vision in Ephesians 5:26-27, where the church is presented as a radiant, holy bride.

The city's characteristics reveal profound truths:

**Light and Glory**: The city requires no sun or moon because the glory of God illuminates it, and the Lamb is its lamp. The source of all light and life is the presence of God Himself.

**Gates and Walls**: The gates and walls speak of both security and access. The city is protected, yet its gates are always open—suggesting a place of perfect safety where the redeemed dwell eternally with God.

**Beauty and Splendor**: The city's beauty is displayed through its precious stones, its gold like pure glass, and its architectural perfection. Every detail reflects the character and majesty of God.

## Access and Exclusion (Revelation 21:22-27)

One of the most striking features of the heavenly Jerusalem is the absence of a temple. Why is there no temple? Because the barrier between God and His people is forever removed. God Himself and the Lamb are the temple—direct, unmediated presence with the Divine.

Entry into the city is determined by one criterion: those whose names are written in the Lamb's book of life. Conversely, those who practice falsehood, embrace idolatry, or persist in rebellion find themselves outside. This underscores a critical truth: our eternal destination is determined by our relationship to Christ.

## The River of Life (Revelation 22:1-5)

The river of living water flows from the throne of God and of the Lamb through the city's street. This image recalls the springs of living water that appear throughout Scripture—in Eden's garden, in Ezekiel's vision of the restored temple, and in Jesus's promise to the woman at the well. All these threads converge here: the ultimate fulfillment of God's promise to quench the spiritual thirst of His people.

The tree of life—forbidden to humanity after the Fall—now stands accessible again. The curse is lifted. Healing flows from its leaves. God's people see His face and bear His name. They reign with Him forever.

## A Call to Obedience (Revelation 22:6-10)

An obedient study of the Bible should lead us to reverent worship and faithful living. These words are trustworthy and true. They deserve our careful attention and wholehearted response.

## The Finality of Repentance (Revelation 22:11-15)

These verses address a sobering reality: there comes a point where opportunities to repent are no longer available. The one who is righteous continues in righteousness. The one who is filthy remains filthy. The trajectory of our choices becomes fixed. This is not arbitrary punishment but the natural consequence of persistent rejection of God's grace.

Yet even here, the gospel shines through: the invitation remains open to all who thirst, all who hunger for righteousness, all who will come.

## The Davidic Promise (Revelation 22:16-17)

How can Jesus be both the Root and the Offspring of David? This paradox resolves in Jesus's divine-human nature. As God, He is David's Root—the source and sustainer of all things, including David himself. As the incarnate Son, He is David's Offspring—His descendant, fulfilling the covenant promise. Jesus transcends time; He is both before and after, both source and fulfillment.

The Spirit and the bride echo the same invitation: "Come." The gospel's final word is not judgment but welcome.

## The Seal of Scripture (Revelation 22:18-19)

The curse pronounced upon those who add to or subtract from these words is brought about by the nature of the act itself: to tamper with God's revelation is to presume authority over God. Such presumption carries its own penalty—the loss of the blessings promised in the book.

## A Tale of Two Cities: The Gospel Demonstrated

Revelation is ultimately a tale of two cities. Babylon—the city of man, built on rebellion, greed, and idolatry—falls into ruin. Jerusalem—the city of God, built on holiness, redemption, and divine love—stands eternal and glorious. One represents the world's rebellion; the other represents God's restoration.

The gospel is woven through this narrative: Christ's victory is not merely forensic (declaring us righteous) but cosmic and eternal. He reconciles all things to Himself. He restores what was broken. He welcomes sinners into communion with the Holy God. The tale of two cities shows us that the choices we make—toward God or away from Him—have eternal weight.
