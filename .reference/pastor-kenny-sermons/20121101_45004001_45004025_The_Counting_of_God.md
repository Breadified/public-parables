# Romans 4:1–25

**Title:** The Counting of God
**Date:** 25 November 2012
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction: What Counts?

Why do accountants seem to lead happy lives? Because they achieve balance. Today we're asking: What counts when it comes to God? What doesn't count?

Last week we learned that righteousness is possible without the law. This week we explore further: faith is what God counts as righteousness.

## 1. Abraham's Faith Counts, Works Do Not (vv. 1–5)

> What does Scripture say? "Abraham believed God, and it was credited to him as righteousness." (Romans 4:3)

The word "counted" is accounting language—to reckon, credit, determine by mathematical process. "Believed" is the same word as faith. It means trust.

Notice the distinction: faith and righteousness both lack verbs in English, so we use "believed" and "justified" as substitutes. But they all mean the same thing. The temptation is to think "believe" refers to ordinary trust while "faith" refers to something special. Not so. They're identical.

### Righteousness and Justice

The Greek root connects justice, righteous, and justified:
- **Justice** = righteous
- **Justify** = to make righteous
- **Justification** = the process of becoming righteous

It's court language. Before a judge: innocent (righteous) versus guilty. Abraham's faith was counted as righteousness—he stood innocent before God.

This stands in contrast to righteousness from works.

> If, in fact, Abraham was justified by works, he had something to boast about—but not before God. (Romans 4:2)

We learned from Romans 3:20 that no one will be declared righteous in God's sight by works of the law. Rather, through the law we become conscious of sin.

> However, to the one who does not work but trusts God who justifies the ungodly, their faith is credited as righteousness. (Romans 4:5)

### Why Faith Is Not a Work

Faith feels like work because we do it. I choose to believe. I'm making a decision. But this is only a trick of language. Faith is not my work—it's faith *in someone else's work*. By definition, faith is always outward-looking. It's a **transitive verb**—a verb that requires an object.

You cannot say "I have faith." You must say "I have faith in..." Similarly, you cannot simply "believe." You must "believe in..."

In verse 5, Abraham trusts *God*, and therefore his faith is not his own work—it relies on God's power to save.

### An Illustration: The Rip Current

Imagine you're caught in a rip current, thrashing and struggling. A lifesaver arrives and says, "Let go. Stop fighting. Trust me." You calm down and allow the rescuer to work. Did you save yourself? Do you boast on the beach, "Thank goodness I was wise enough to calm down"? No. The lifesaver saved you. Your act of trust—your refusal to fight—wasn't your salvation. Your faith in the lifesaver was.

Similarly, faith in God isn't your righteousness. God's work is. Your faith simply stops fighting and trusts.

### Application: Jesus Says DONE

Consider what the religions of the world offer: Buddhism's Four Noble Truths, Islam's Five Pillars, secular asceticism with its endless rules—do this, don't do that, count calories, punish yourself at the gym. It's exhausting. Being watched constantly to see if you're doing the right thing.

As 1 Timothy 4:3 warns, false teachers forbid marriage and demand abstention from foods God created for thanksgiving. They confuse the sign with the attraction itself—like Instagram replacing the confessional booth.

But amidst all these "do, do, dos," **Jesus says DONE**.

Abraham's faith was counted as righteousness. His works were not counted. Faith in Jesus is counted as righteousness. This is good news.

## 2. David's Faith Counts, Sin Is Not Counted (vv. 6–8)

> David says the same thing when he speaks of the blessedness of the one to whom God credits righteousness apart from works: "Blessed are those whose transgressions are forgiven, whose sins are covered. Blessed is the one whose sin the Lord will never count against them." (Romans 4:6–8, quoting Psalm 32:1–2)

Paul quotes the Psalms to show another Old Testament witness. David speaks of being blessed—happy, favored—because transgressions are forgiven and sins are covered. Most importantly: **the Lord will not count his sin**.

This psalm, traditionally attributed to David, was written after he committed murder and adultery with Bathsheba. How did forgiveness come?

> Then I acknowledged my sin to you and did not cover up my iniquity. I said, "I will confess my transgressions to the Lord." And you forgave the guilt of my sin. (Psalm 32:5)

David's path to forgiveness was through faith—an act of trust.

### The Nature of Confession as Faith

Faith means acknowledging, not covering, confessing. It's an act of vulnerability—throwing everything out there and trusting the other person won't use it against you. It's trusting that correction, even if harsh, comes for your good.

David didn't try to earn righteousness through works. He didn't rely on the law. He acknowledged his guilt, and God forgave him. His sin is no longer counted against him. Instead, faith is counted as righteousness.

### Forgiveness at a Cost

But here's what matters: **this forgiveness came at a cost**. The nature of forgiveness is that someone else pays. Sins don't simply disappear like magic. Someone always pays. Where there's a credit, there's a debit.

Sometimes accountants understand what God has done better than anyone—they know about balance sheets and payment.

### An Illustration: The Policeman and Hayden Carlo

In Texas, near Christmas, a policeman stopped a driver named Hayden Carlo for an outdated registration. Carlo explained he couldn't afford the registration fee—it was either feed his family or renew the tag. Justice demanded the fine. The policeman had to issue it.

But then the officer did something extraordinary. He slipped a $100 bill into the ticket. Carlo said: "I opened it up and there's a hundred dollar bill. I broke down in my car." That money was enough to pay the fine *and* update the registration for both his car and his wife's car.

**This is what God has done for us.**

He must punish. That's justice. But He provides a way to avoid that punishment. God sends His "$100 bill"—Jesus—which is enough to pay the debt of our sins and start us on a new life. Someone has paid so we can go free.

God is now offering a **recount of your life**. Sin no longer counts against you. It's a chance to start over.

### Application

Confess to God. You will be pleasantly surprised at His reaction. If you confess, He forgives (1 John 1:9).

As we approach the last Sunday of the year with Holy Communion, consider going to a local shopping center and inviting someone to share a meal—an opportunity to reflect together on forgiveness and new beginnings.

## 3. The Uncircumcised's Faith Counts, Circumcision Does Not (vv. 9–12)

> Is this blessedness only for the circumcised, or also for the uncircumcised? We have been saying that Abraham's faith was credited to him as righteousness. Under what circumstances was it credited? Was it after he was circumcised, or before? It was not after, but before! (Romans 4:9–10)

Paul asks: Is this blessing exclusive to the Jews (the circumcised)?

### The Timeline of Abraham's Righteousness

Every male Jewish baby is circumcised on the eighth day—a command from God:

> This is my covenant with you and your descendants after you: Every male among you shall be circumcised. You are to undergo circumcision, and it will be the sign of the covenant between me and you. (Genesis 17:10–11)

Circumcision was designed to distinguish Israel from all other nations. It was so central to Jewish identity that ethnicity itself became tied to the law. When you think "law," you think "Jew."

But when was Abraham declared righteous? We must look back two chapters:

> He took him outside and said, "Look up at the sky and count the stars—if indeed you can count them." Then he said to him, "So shall your offspring be." Abram believed the Lord, and he credited it to him as righteousness. (Genesis 15:5–6)

**Abraham was declared righteous before circumcision.**

> And he received circumcision as a sign, a seal of the righteousness that he had by faith while he was still uncircumcised. (Romans 4:11)

### Circumcision as Sign, Not Source

Circumcision is an outward sign—like a wedding ring. The ring doesn't make you married; it signifies that you are. If you're unmarried and wear a ring on your fourth finger, it doesn't make you married. And if you're married but don't have a ring, you're still married! (Don't tell my wife.)

The sign points back to faith. It's not the source of righteousness.

> So then, he is the father of all who believe but have not been circumcised, in order that righteousness might be credited to them. And he is then also the father of the circumcised who not only are circumcised but who also follow in the footsteps of the faith that our father Abraham had before he was circumcised. (Romans 4:11–12)

Abraham is father to two groups: those who believe without circumcision, and those who are circumcised *and* follow Abraham's faith. Both share the same foundation—faith.

### The Law as Sign

The law functions like circumcision—it's a sign pointing somewhere. Israel had the Law when no other nation did: the Ten Commandments, the sacrificial system, food laws, circumcision. These marked them as God's people.

But in observing the law, the Israelites sometimes got caught up in mere observance and forgot what the law was about. They **remembered the sign but didn't visit the attraction**.

**The law has always been about faith.** It points to faith. This is why Paul says:

> Where, then, is boasting? It is excluded. Because of what law? The law that requires works? No, because of the law that requires faith. (Romans 3:27)

This phrase—"the law of faith"—is striking. It suggests that law and faith aren't enemies but partners. The law leads to faith. They help each other.

> Do we, then, nullify the law by this faith? Not at all! Rather, we uphold the law. (Romans 3:31)

And as Jesus Himself said:

> Do not think that I have come to abolish the Law or the Prophets; I have not come to abolish them but to fulfill them. (Matthew 5:17)

When you follow the law properly, it leads you to its fulfillment. It leads you to Christ. It leads you to Abraham's faith. The law comes *after* faith, not before. First faith, then obedience.

### Application

Keep reading the Bible. It's all about Jesus. The law points to faith. Faith points to Christ.

## 4. Too Many to Count (vv. 13–25)

> It was not through the law that Abraham and his offspring received the promise that he would be heir of the world, but through the righteousness that comes by faith. (Romans 4:13)

The promise extends beyond law to the entire world. Abraham becomes the father of many nations—his descendants are too many to count, just as God promised.

### Abraham's Faith Against All Hope

> Against all hope, Abraham in hope believed and so became the father of many nations, just as it had been said to him, "So shall your offspring be." Without weakening in his faith, he faced the fact that his body was as good as dead—since he was about a hundred years old—and that Sarah's womb was also dead. Yet he did not waver through unbelief regarding the promise of God, but was strengthened in his faith and gave glory to God, being fully persuaded that God had power to do what he had promised. (Romans 4:18–21)

Abraham's faith reveals something crucial: it grows, it perseveres through impossibility, it trusts God's promises even when circumstances contradict them. He didn't weaken. He didn't waver. Instead, he grew strong in faith and glorified God.

> This is why "it was credited to him as righteousness." (Romans 4:22)

### Written for Us

> The words "it was credited to him" were written not for him alone, but also for us, to whom God will credit righteousness—for us who believe in him who raised Jesus our Lord from the dead. He was delivered over to death for our sins and was raised to life for our justification. (Romans 4:23–25)

This promise is for everyone who believes. As many as the stars—too many to count.

If you believe in God who raised Jesus from the dead, it will be credited to you as righteousness. You can write your name in: "_____ believed God, and it was credited to him/her as righteousness."

Grow strong in your faith. Summer is coming—use it to strengthen your trust in God.

---

## Summary: What God Counts

- **Works are not counted**
- **Sin is not counted** (when confessed in faith)
- **Circumcision is not counted** (the sign points to faith, not itself)
- **Faith is counted** as righteousness

And through faith, righteousness extends to the world—to all who believe, whether Jews or Gentiles. God is the God of all peoples. He justifies the circumcised and uncircumcised alike, through the same faith in Jesus Christ, who paid our debt and rose to justify us.
