# Galatians 3:1-18

**Title:** Faith Over Works: The Bewitchment of False Gospel
**Date:** 25 July 2021
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## You Fools!

Paul opens with striking directness:

> You foolish Galatians! Who has bewitched you?

The term "foolish" here carries affection rather than contempt. It's the language of a pastor speaking to beloved children who have strayed. Jesus used the same word when rebuking the disciples on the Emmaus road (Luke 24:25), and Paul applies it to the Galatian believers in Titus 3:3—not as an insult, but as a wake-up call delivered with love.

Something has captivated the Galatians. They've been "bewitched" by false teachers promoting the circumcision party—those claiming that Gentile converts must become Jewish before truly becoming Christian. These agitators have brought false guilt, insisting that the Gentiles must undergo circumcision and follow food laws. The Galatians, who once embraced Christ freely, have been seduced into spiritual bondage.

---

## Work or Spirit: Appeal to Experience (3:2-4)

Paul's first argument strikes at the heart of the matter. He appeals not to theory, but to the Galatians' own lived experience:

> Did you receive the Spirit by the works of the law, or by believing what you heard?

Think about your own conversion. When you became a Christian, what drew you? Was it circumcision? Food laws? The avoidance of pork? No. The Galatians came to faith by hearing about Jesus and responding in faith.

Paul repeats the question to drive the point home:

> Are you so foolish? After beginning by means of the Spirit, are you now trying to finish by means of the flesh?

The logic is devastating. You started with the Spirit—with God's transformative power—so why would you now depend on flesh, on human effort, on external rituals?

Then Paul appeals to their suffering:

> Have you experienced so much in vain—if it really was in vain?

These Gentile converts have borne real cost for their faith. They may have been disowned by families, hunted by authorities, stripped of wealth, and ridiculed in their communities. All of that suffering was undergirded by a vision of Christ crucified—not by the circumcision badge or the food they ate.

> Before your very eyes Jesus Christ was clearly portrayed as crucified.

The cross is the sign of their faith, not circumcision. The cross means Christ died. The cross means nothing can be accomplished by human works alone. Don't look down at your circumstances or your guilt. Look up at the Christ who paid the price.

---

## Work or Spirit: Faith in Christ (3:5)

Paul asks again:

> Does God give you his Spirit and work miracles among you by the works of the law, or by your believing what you heard?

What moved you to transformation? Was it because you stopped eating pork? Or did you see something far greater—your sin, Christ's forgiveness, your sin crucified with Jesus, and freedom? That vision of the cross produced faith. And faith—trusting in Christ's work rather than your own—is how you became a Christian.

The Galatians came to Christ because they saw Jesus. They saw their desperate need. They saw His substitutionary death. They saw themselves set free. And they believed.

---

## Abraham: The Founder's Example (3:6-16)

Paul's second argument turns to the patriarch himself—Abraham, the father of the Jewish people, the founder figure invoked by those demanding circumcision.

> Abraham believed God, and it was credited to him as righteousness.

Abraham obtained righteousness not through circumcision, but through faith in God's promises. This is crucial: faith is not a work. Faith is the opposite of works. When we speak of justification by faith, we're saying "it's not about me, it's about trusting something beyond myself." Faith means receiving what has already been accomplished, like accepting a gift.

Even Abraham—the patriarch whom the circumcision party claims as their authority—came to God through faith, not through rituals. The law came later, 430 years after Abraham's covenant with God:

> The law, introduced 430 years later, does not set aside the covenant previously established by God and thus do away with the promise.

A covenant is a formalized promise. God's promise to Abraham was based on grace—pure, unearned favor. Adding the law later doesn't annul the original promise. It's like the Constitution and subsequent regulations; the later does not override the earlier.

The promise itself pointed to Christ:

> The promises were spoken to Abraham and to his seed. Scripture does not say "and to seeds," meaning many people, but "and to your seed," meaning one person, who is Christ.

Therefore, if righteousness comes through faith in God's promise, that principle applies universally:

> Understand, then, that those who have faith are children of Abraham.

This includes Gentiles. The original plan was always for all nations:

> Scripture foresaw that God would justify the Gentiles by faith, and announced the gospel in advance to Abraham: "All nations will be blessed through you." So those who rely on faith are blessed along with Abraham, the man of faith.

---

## The Law's Curse and Christ's Redemption (3:10-14)

Paul now presents the contrast. If someone relies on the works of the law, they place themselves under a curse:

> For all who rely on the works of the law are under a curse, as it is written: "Cursed is everyone who does not continue to do everything written in the Book of the Law."

The key word is "everything." Not circumcision alone. Not a few select laws. *Everything*. The standard is absolute obedience to the entire law. The Pharisees and circumcision party set the bar just below what they could achieve, judging others by a lower standard they themselves had created. But Paul insists: if you're going to claim salvation by law-keeping, then be consistent. Keep *everything*.

This is why the law exposes our bankruptcy:

> Clearly no one who relies on the law is justified before God, because "the righteous will live by faith."

The law demands what it cannot give. It reveals sin but cannot cure it. It pronounces judgment but offers no salvation.

But here is the gospel's glory:

> Christ redeemed us from the curse of the law by becoming a curse for us, for it is written: "Cursed is everyone who is hung on a pole."

Jesus took the curse we deserved. He stood in our place. The cross became the instrument of our redemption—the very symbol of the law's judgment became the means of our deliverance.

This is justification: being treated as if we had never sinned, because the punishment fell on Another. We are guilty, but the Father sees us covered by Christ's righteousness.

And the result? The blessing promised to Abraham now flows to all who believe:

> He redeemed us in order that the blessing given to Abraham might come to the Gentiles through Christ Jesus, so that by faith we might receive the promise of the Spirit.

---

## The Law's True Role (3:19-29)

Paul anticipates a question: If faith is the answer, why was the law given at all?

> The law, introduced 430 years later, was added because of transgressions until the Seed to whom the promise referred had come.

The law was a temporary guardian, not a permanent master. It exposed our sinfulness until Christ came:

> Before the coming of this faith, we were held in custody under the law, locked up until the faith that was to come would be revealed. So the law was our guardian until Christ came that we might be justified by faith. Now that this faith has come, we are no longer under a guardian.

The law had a pedagogical role—to show us our need, to drive us to Christ. But once faith has come, we have entered a new relationship entirely.

And the final word unites all believers:

> In Christ Jesus you are all children of God through faith. For all of you who were baptized into Christ have clothed yourselves with Christ. There is neither Jew nor Gentile, neither slave nor free, nor is there male and female, for you are all one in Christ Jesus. If you belong to Christ, then you are Abraham's seed, and heirs according to the promise.

In Christ, all human categories dissolve. Racial identity, social status, gender—none of these determine standing before God. All who trust Christ inherit the promise given to Abraham. All are justified. All are sons and daughters.

This is the scandal and the glory of the gospel: it offers what human effort can never achieve, received not through striving but through faith, granted not to the morally superior but to the spiritually bankrupt who trust in Another's finished work.
