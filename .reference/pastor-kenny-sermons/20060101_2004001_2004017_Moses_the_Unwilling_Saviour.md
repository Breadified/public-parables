# Exodus 4:1-17

**Title:** Moses the Unwilling Saviour
**Date:** 22 January 2006
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## The Pattern of Excuses

We all know what it means to avoid something we don't want to do. We make excuses—little delays, hoping the person asking will eventually forget or give up. It's not quite saying "no," but it's not really saying "yes" either.

Moses does exactly this. God has called him to go to Pharaoh and lead the Israelites out of slavery in Egypt. But Moses is unwilling, and he comes up with excuse after excuse.

> "I have indeed seen the misery of my people in Egypt. I have heard them crying out because of their slave drivers, and I am concerned about their suffering. So I have come down to rescue them from the hand of the Egyptians and to bring them up out of that land into a good and spacious land, a land flowing with milk and honey" (Exodus 3:7-9).

Yet Moses resists. What follows are four excuses, each met with God's patient provision—and yet Moses persists in his reluctance.

---

## First Excuse: "Who Am I?"

> "But Moses said to God, 'Who am I, that I should go to Pharaoh and bring the Israelites out of Egypt?'" (Exodus 3:11)

Moses begins with false humility. "I'm not qualified. I'm not good enough." But this is an excuse, not genuine inadequacy. Moses was raised in Pharaoh's household. He had connections, education, and standing. He was actually the best person for the job. If anyone could approach Egypt's ruler, it would be him.

God's answer is simple and profound:

> "I will be with you. And this will be the sign to you that it is I who have sent you: When you have brought the people out of Egypt, you will worship God on this mountain." (Exodus 3:12)

God doesn't argue about Moses's worth. He offers something better: His presence.

---

## Second Excuse: "Who Are You?"

But Moses isn't satisfied. He presents a second objection:

> "Suppose I go to the Israelites and say to them, 'The God of your fathers has sent me to you,' and they ask me, 'What is his name?' Then what shall I tell them?" (Exodus 3:13)

Now Moses shifts the problem to God. The Israelites won't believe him without proof. What credibility does he have? If he tells them about a burning bush and a voice from heaven, they'll think he's lost his mind.

God responds by revealing His name—a name that had never before been disclosed:

> "I AM WHO I AM. This is what you are to say to the Israelites: 'I AM has sent me to you.'" (Exodus 3:14)

But God doesn't stop there. He reinforces His identity as the God of Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob—ancestors the Israelites deeply respected. God also reiterates the promise to deliver them from Egypt and even mentions that they will leave with compensation from their captors.

---

## Third Excuse: "They Won't Believe Me"

Still unsatisfied, Moses presents yet another objection:

> "What if they do not believe me or listen to me and say, 'The LORD did not appear to you'?" (Exodus 4:1)

So God provides not just one, but three miraculous signs:

**The Staff Becomes a Snake:**
> "What is that in your hand?" the LORD asked. "A staff," Moses replied. The LORD said, "Throw it on the ground." It became a snake, and Moses ran from it. Then the LORD said, "Reach out your hand and take it by the tail." Moses took hold of the snake and it turned back into a staff in his hand. (Exodus 4:2-4)

**The Hand Becomes Leprous, Then Healed:**
> "Put your hand inside your cloak." When Moses withdrew it, his hand was leprous, like snow. "Now put it back into your cloak," he said. When Moses took it out, it was restored, like the rest of his flesh. (Exodus 4:6-7)

**Water Turns to Blood:**
> "If they do not believe you or pay attention to the first miraculous sign, they may believe the second. But if they do not believe these two signs or listen to you, take some water from the Nile and pour it on the dry ground. The water you take from the river will become blood on the ground." (Exodus 4:8-9)

These signs were given specifically so the Israelite elders would believe Moses's message. One would have been impressive; three is overwhelming.

---

## Fourth Excuse: "I Can't Speak"

After God has answered every objection with patient provision, Moses offers his final excuse:

> "O Lord, I have never been eloquent, neither in the past nor since you have spoken to your servant. I am slow of speech and tongue." (Exodus 4:10)

This excuse reveals something important: it's almost laughable. Moses has now progressed through "Who am I?" to "Who are you?" to "They won't believe me." Now he claims he can't speak. But this is absurd for a simple reason—God has already given him everything he needs:

- God's presence
- God's name
- God's words (already provided in 3:14-17)
- Three miraculous signs that require no speech at all

Moses is fixating on himself when God has made it clear this isn't about Moses at all. God reminds him:

> "Who gave man his mouth? Who makes him deaf or mute? Who gives him sight or makes him blind? Is it not I, the LORD?" (Exodus 4:11)

If God is with him, Moses lacks nothing.

---

## The Real Reason: Unwillingness

Despite all of God's provision, Moses's true heart is finally revealed:

> "But Moses said, 'O Lord, please send someone else to do it.'" (Exodus 4:13)

After running through every excuse—about himself, about God, about the people, about his abilities—Moses finally admits the truth: *He simply doesn't want to go.* All the previous objections were delays, procrastinations, time-wasting tactics. If he'd been honest from the start, at least God could have worked with that candor.

Instead of obedience, Moses offers reluctance. Instead of trust, he offers doubt. And God, in His patience exhausted, grows angry and assigns Aaron, Moses's brother, to be his spokesman. Moses eventually goes, but only because he's been worn down, not because he's been convinced.

---

## The Contrast: Two Saviours

Moses the unwilling saviour leaves us with a troubling picture. This is his character—a man who hesitates, who makes excuses, who avoids obedience. Such a mediator offers little hope.

But we live under a different covenant, saved by a different Saviour.

Consider Jesus in His final hours, facing not Pharaoh but death itself:

> "They went to a place called Gethsemane, and Jesus said to his disciples, 'Sit here while I pray.' He took Peter, James and John along with him, and he began to be deeply distressed and troubled. 'My soul is overwhelmed with sorrow to the point of death,' he said to them. 'Stay here and keep watch.'
>
> Going a little farther, he fell to the ground and prayed that if possible the hour might pass from him. 'Abba, Father,' he said, 'everything is possible for you. Take this cup from me. Yet not what I will, but what you will.'" (Mark 14:32-36)

Jesus doesn't hide His dread. He is "deeply distressed," His soul "overwhelmed with sorrow to the point of death." He asks His Father if there's any other way. He doesn't want to suffer.

But notice His response: Not a series of excuses. Not reluctance masquerading as piety. Instead, absolute obedience born of love: *"Yet not what I will, but what you will."*

Where Moses procrastinates, Jesus submits. Where Moses makes excuses, Jesus surrenders. Where Moses is unwilling, Jesus is willing—willing to die for people who don't deserve it, willing to bear sins He didn't commit, willing to pay a price He need never have paid.

---

## The Call to Us

This contrast illuminates our own condition. We too live under slavery—not to Pharaoh, but to sin and its judgment. And we too are offered salvation, not through an unwilling mediator, but through a willing Saviour.

Our response need not be complicated. We need only be willing to be saved. We need only trust Him.

Yet life after that will test us as it tested Moses. We too will face opportunities to obey God and will be tempted to make excuses. When God calls us to love one another, we'll hesitate. When He calls us to lay down selfish desires, we'll find reasons not to. When He demands faithfulness in small things—our words, our commitments, our appointments—we'll struggle.

But here is the grace that Moses did not have: We are not saved by our obedience. We are saved by Jesus's obedience. And the Holy Spirit who indwells us empowers us, through weakness and failure, to grow toward the willing surrender that Jesus modeled.

Thank God for Jesus, the willing Saviour who has saved us from our sins. In Him, we find not just rescue from judgment, but transformation from reluctance into willingness.
