# Jonah 1:1-16

**Title:** The Fear of God
**Date:** 24 July 2015
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Call and the Flight

> The word of the Lord came to Jonah son of Amittai: "Go to the great city of Nineveh and preach against it, because its wickedness has come up before me." But Jonah ran away from the Lord and headed for Tarshish. He went down to Joppa, where he found a ship bound for that port. After paying the fare, he went aboard and sailed for Tarshish to flee from the Lord.

Jonah faced a terrifying assignment. Nineveh, located near present-day Mosul in northern Iraq, was the capital of the Assyrian Empire—home to one of history's most brutally wicked civilizations. The Assyrians were renowned for unimaginable cruelty: they dismembered victims and left them to die slowly on poles, created pillars of enemy skulls at their gates, and conducted mass public torture sessions to demoralize their opponents. They were masters of terror, using systematic violence as both a weapon and a tactic.

God was calling Jonah to proclaim judgment against these barbarous people. Jonah's response was immediate: he ran. Rather than obey God's command, he boarded a ship sailing in the opposite direction—toward Tarshish, as far from Nineveh as he could get. In his fear of the Ninevites, Jonah turned away from God himself.

## The Storm and False Gods

> Then the Lord sent a great wind on the sea, and such a violent storm arose that the ship threatened to break up. All the sailors were afraid and each cried out to his own god. And they threw the cargo into the sea to lighten the ship.

But Yahweh would not be escaped so easily. He hurled a storm at the ship, so violent that the vessel itself seemed about to splinter. The pagan sailors, terrified by the tempest, cried out to their gods—the gods of the wind, the gods of the water, the gods of the sea. In their desperation, they threw cargo overboard, attempting to appease the gods of the sea, hoping that their sacrifices would restore calm.

These were sincere men, genuinely seeking help from the only sources of security they knew. Yet their sincerity was futile. Their gods were not real, and no amount of cargo tossed into the sea could satisfy forces that did not exist. Being sincerely wrong provides no salvation.

> The captain went to him and said, "How can you sleep? Get up and call on your god! Maybe he will take notice of us so that we will not perish."

Even the captain appeals to Jonah—yet the prophet remains indifferent, asleep below deck while men above cry out for divine mercy.

## Two Different Fears

> But Jonah had gone below deck, where he lay down and fell into a deep sleep.

Jonah's behavior is striking. While the sailors panic and pray, Jonah sleeps. He shows no fear of the storm, no concern for the sailors, no desperate prayer to God. He seems almost apathetic—as though he could not be bothered with God, with the fate of these men, or even with his own life.

Later, when the sailors ask what they should do to calm the storm, Jonah offers a response that reveals his reckless resignation:

> "Pick me up and throw me into the sea," he replied, "and it will become calm. I know that it is my fault that this great storm has come upon you."

Yet when questioned about who he is, Jonah makes a striking confession:

> He answered, "I am a Hebrew and I worship the Lord, the God of heaven, who made the sea and the dry land."

The word "worship" here literally means "I fear the Lord." Jonah claims to fear God—the God of heaven, the God who made the sea and the dry land. He acknowledges the sovereignty of the one who controls all three realms: sky, earth, and ocean.

But his actions tell a different story. If Jonah truly feared God, why did he run from God's command? If he truly feared the God of the dry land, he should not have attempted to flee by ship. If he truly feared the God of the heavens, he should not have expected to escape the one who commands the winds. If he truly feared the God of the sea, he should not have imagined the ocean could hide him.

Jonah's words contradict his deeds. He says he fears God, but his behavior reveals that he fears the Ninevites more.

## The Sailors Learn True Fear

When the sailors learned who Jonah was and what he had done, their reaction was profound:

> This terrified them and they asked, "What have you done?" (They knew he was running away from the Lord, because he had already told them so.)

The magnitude of the sea had already terrified them. But now they grasped something far greater: the one who created and controlled the sea. If the sea itself could inspire such terror, what of the one greater than the sea? The sailors began to fear God.

Their subsequent actions demonstrate this transformation:

> Instead, the men did their best to row back to land. But they could not, for the sea grew even wilder than before. Then they cried out to the Lord, "Please, Lord, do not let us die for taking this man's life. Do not hold us accountable for killing an innocent man, for you, Lord, have done as you pleased."

The sailors tried to save Jonah. They strove mightily to row back to shore rather than throw him overboard. Only when all other options failed did they reluctantly cast him into the sea:

> Then they took Jonah and threw him overboard, and the raging sea grew calm.

The contrast is instructive: Jonah, who claimed to fear God, did nothing. The sailors, pagan worshipers who initially cried out to false gods, learned to fear the true God through the very storm that Jonah had caused. And their fear moved them to action—compassion, mercy, and ultimately, a reverent acceptance of God's will.

> At this the men greatly feared the Lord, and they offered a sacrifice to the Lord and made vows to him.

## A Question for Today

So here is the challenge: Do you truly fear God?

A test of whether we fear God is whether our actions match our words. Are we like Jonah—claiming to fear God while allowing other fears to override that allegiance? Or are we like the sailors—letting the reality of God transform our choices and direct our lives?

Consider the Great Commission that Jesus gave to his followers:

> Therefore go and make disciples of all nations, baptizing them in the name of the Father and of the Son and of the Holy Spirit, and teaching them to obey everything I have commanded you. And surely I am with you always, to the very end of the age.

We have been given a command, just as Jonah was. We are to speak to others about the goodness of God, the mercy purchased by his Son, and the assurance his Spirit brings. Yet this command frightens us. We fear losing our popularity. We fear being associated with strange elements of Christian culture. We fear the cost in time, money, and convenience. We fear rejection and disappointment. We fear that our efforts might seem futile.

But consider: Is God truly smaller than these fears?

Our God is bigger than embarrassment. Our God is bigger than persecution. If your friend is God, can you really be unpopular? If you belong to the one who created all things, are you truly missing out? If you choose to follow God, will he truly abandon you?

There are Christians in Mosul and throughout the world today who face genuine persecution for their faith. They have chosen to fear God more than they fear the violence of those around them. They are willing to suffer as witnesses to their persecutors, trusting that their God is greater than their circumstances.

Words and sticks and stones cannot touch what truly matters. Your inheritance is secure in heaven. God is the God of sea, air, and land—he is sovereign over all things. Let that reality reshape what you fear, and let your fear of him eclipse all other terrors.
