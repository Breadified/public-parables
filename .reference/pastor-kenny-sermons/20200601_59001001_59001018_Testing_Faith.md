# James 1:1-18

**Title:** Testing Faith  
**Date:** 7 June 2020  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction

We live in a time of testing. Trials come to all of us—some through global upheaval, others through personal difficulty. And when they arrive, we ask God hard questions: Why? Where are you? This disrupts our faith. But Scripture has answers for us, and the Book of James speaks directly to this struggle.

James opens with two profound truths: God tests us for our good, and we must never test God in return.

---

## Part One: Embrace Your Trials

### Why We Can Rejoice in Suffering

James begins with a startling command: "Consider it pure joy, my brothers and sisters, whenever you face trials of many kinds" (James 1:2).

This isn't some mystical Christian experience or detached inner peace. It's genuine happiness. The Greek word *chara* means to rejoice. And yes, this seems impossible—even macabre. Yet it's what Scripture teaches.

Here's the paradox of human emotion: we are complex creatures capable of holding opposite feelings simultaneously. A Christian funeral combines grief for earthly loss with joy over eternal hope. We can feel disappointment and happiness at the same time.

Why can we genuinely rejoice in trials? Two reasons.

### Reason One: Trials Produce Perseverance

**"Because you know that the testing of your faith produces perseverance."** (James 1:3)

When our faith is tested, it builds *endurance*—the ability to keep trusting God no matter what, to keep obeying Him regardless of circumstances.

We might wish God could simply supernaturally transform us into His image. Perhaps a pill? A shortcut? But God's wisdom has determined that perseverance—genuine spiritual strength—comes through testing. This is how He shapes us.

The most valuable things in life are those that have persevered:

- **A degree** brings lasting value, unlike fleeting pleasures
- **Gold** doesn't corrode; we choose it for wedding bands because it endures through time
- **A brick house** withstands hail and storm
- **Genuine friendships** are forged through ups and downs, not shallow moments
- **Our character** is built through adversity we refused to abandon

This principle extends to physical training. Athletes know that pain in the gym is actually the moment of growth—when muscle fibers tear microscopically to rebuild stronger. The temptation to stop is strongest precisely when the body is improving most. Stopping at that moment wastes everything that came before.

Similarly, when trials hurt most deeply, that's when God is doing His most transformative work.

### The Ultimate Result: The Crown of Life

**"Blessed is the one who perseveres under trial because, having stood the test, that person will receive the crown of life that the Lord has promised to those who love him."** (James 1:12)

After endurance, after standing the test, comes blessing—happiness.

The crown is a Greek symbol of victory. In ancient Olympics, wreaths were awarded not just to first-place finishers, but to all who completed the race—despite pulled hamstrings, despite disabilities, despite pain. They merely had to finish.

God's crown of life is eternal victory: salvation, heaven, everlasting peace with our Creator. This is the reward awaiting those who persevere.

**"Let perseverance finish its work so that you may be mature and complete, not lacking anything."** (James 1:4)

Through trials, we gain maturity. We become complete. We lack nothing essential.

**"If any of you lacks wisdom, you should ask God, who gives generously to all without finding fault, and it will be given to you."** (James 1:5)

Wisdom is the endpoint. The end goal of Christian maturity is wise, complete, whole people who reflect Jesus Himself. And this requires discomfort.

### Practical Wisdom for Daily Perseverance

Many of us drop everything when trials come—including church attendance, prayer, Christian fellowship, personal disciplines. This makes no sense.

Consider the gym again. When exercise begins to hurt, stopping seems merciful. But that pain signals exactly where growth happens. Stop at that moment, and you've wasted your effort. The pain is when it works.

Trials are never the time to abandon Christian practice. Instead:

- **Keep coming to church** even during exams
- **Maintain fellowship** when life is difficult
- **Honor your obligations** when they feel overwhelming
- **Teach your children perseverance** by not protecting them from every hardship

When we shield our children from difficulty—hiring tutors instead of letting them struggle, excusing chores during stressful seasons, solving every problem—we teach them to *avoid* hardship, not overcome it. We teach them that someone else will fix their problems. Then one day they face suffering no money can solve, and they abandon Jesus entirely.

**Instead, fix your eyes on the goal.** The HSC is hard, but you persevere because the reward justifies the struggle. Athletes train hard because it's worth it. God promises you a crown—make that worth persevering for.

### A Culture That Resists Perseverance

We live in a culture that abhors hardship. We engineer our lives to avoid difficulty, pain, and genuine work. We live in comfort, yet we have epidemic rates of depression, anxiety, and suicide. We're unprepared for the trials life will inevitably bring.

When marriages become difficult, we abandon them. When faith becomes costly, we walk away. When real testing comes—the kind that reveals who we truly are—we fail.

### Reason Two: God Loves You Through Trials

Trials aren't just means to an end. They're evidence of God's love.

**"Believers in humble circumstances ought to take pride in their high position."** (James 1:9)

The person facing trials—often poor, struggling, marginalized—stands in a high position before God. Why? Because trials are the mark of God's discipline, and discipline is what love looks like.

Consider parenting. Disciplining a child is painful. You set boundaries. You say no to endless sweets. You require good habits. You correct disrespect. There are tears. But that discipline creates a healthy, well-behaved, capable adult. To merely indulge every whim under the guise of love is actually unloving. It abandons the child to become selfish, rude, and unprepared for life.

God's discipline works the same way. He cares too much about you to leave you unchanged.

The writer of Hebrews explains this beautifully:

**"Endure hardship as discipline; God is treating you as his children. For what children are not disciplined by their father? ... God disciplines us for our good, in order that we may share in his holiness. No discipline seems pleasant at the time, but painful. Later on, however, it produces a harvest of righteousness and peace for those who have been trained by it."** (Hebrews 12:7-11)

#### The Story of Joni Eareckson Tada

Consider Joni Eareckson Tada. As a teenager in 1967, she loved riding horses, hiking, tennis, and swimming. One summer day, she dived into a river, misjudged the depth, struck a sandbank, and broke her neck. She became a quadriplegic—paralyzed from the shoulders down.

Her rehabilitation was brutal. She experienced anger, depression, suicidal thoughts, and deep doubts about God's goodness. For decades afterward, she endured chronic pain. In her sixties, she fought breast cancer.

If anyone had reason to feel life was unfair, it was Joni. Yet here's what she says:

*"Only God knows why I was paralyzed. If I were still on my feet, it's hard to say how things would have gone. I probably would have drifted through life—marriage, maybe even divorce—dissatisfied and disillusioned. When I was in high school, I reacted to life selfishly and never built on any long-lasting values, almost always at others' expense. Maybe He knew I'd be ultimately happier serving Him."*

Her suffering transformed her into someone radically more useful to God's kingdom than she would have been on her feet.

### The Hidden Danger of Comfort

**"But the rich should take pride in their humiliation—since they will pass away like a wild flower. For the sun rises with scorching heat and withers the plant; its blossom falls and its beauty is destroyed. In the same way, the rich will fade away even while they go about their business."** (James 1:10-11)

The truly disadvantaged aren't those facing trials. They're the untested.

An untested person is like a wildflower that blooms perfectly in ideal conditions—soft, weak, with shallow roots. When the sun rises with scorching heat, it withers. It has no inner strength, no resilience, no developed character to draw upon. These come only through testing.

The rich and comfortable—those of us who've never suffered real hardship—are most vulnerable. We've cruised through life:

- Good schools, good universities
- Safe neighborhoods
- Instant gratification at our fingertips
- One click buys what we want; one swipe of a card purchases our desires
- We subcontract our struggles—hire tutors, pay for convenience, avoid discomfort
- Generally an upward curve: good school, university, job, house, family, investments

But in a flash, everything can collapse. Then where will we be?

### Choosing Strategic Discomfort

If life hasn't disciplined you, you must discipline yourself. Not through self-harm, but through intentional difficulty.

**Push yourself:**

- Join a gym and embrace the pain of growth
- Practice delayed gratification—skip dessert because you can
- Take the stairs instead of the escalator
- Create friction in your comfort

More importantly, leverage your privilege strategically. If you have advantage, use it:

**As priests**, look at your community's needs. What can you do? Push yourself as God's ambassador.

**As parents**, don't wait for the "perfect" time. Decide based on what's right, not what's easy. Yes, children are costly and challenging—that's the point.

**As stewards**, live in a house one room smaller than your peers so you can give more away. Force yourself toward simplicity.

You can do these things because you have privilege. With that privilege comes responsibility—not guilt, but opportunity. The question isn't whether you have advantage. The question is what you do with it.

It takes generations to build lasting change. One generation builds; another generation takes over; another generation transforms it further. Real perseverance means playing the long game, refusing victim mentality, and using whatever advantage you have to lift others up.

## Part Two: Do Not Test God

While God testing us leads to life, the reverse isn't true. We cannot test God and expect blessing.

### How We Test God

**"But when you ask, you must believe and not doubt, because the one who doubts is like a wave of the sea, blown and tossed by the wind. That person should not expect to receive anything from the Lord. Such a person is double-minded and unstable in all they do."** (James 1:6-8)

To test God is to doubt His goodness when trials come.

Note: doubting here doesn't mean asking questions. "What does this passage mean?" or "Why did God do that?"—these are honest inquiries. Rather, testing God means suspicion of His character. It means thinking, "God is out to get me" or "God is cruel." It means wavering between two loyalties—claiming to trust God while secretly believing He's against you.

When you ask God for wisdom, you must ask in faith, believing He will provide what's good for you. But then to turn around and say "This test is too hard" or "I doubt God's goodness" is to test Him. You're saying, "I asked for wisdom, but I actually suspect you're evil." How can you expect wisdom while secretly distrusting God's intentions?

Testing God brings death.

### The Pathway to Death

**"When tempted, no one should say, 'God is tempting me.' For God cannot be tempted by evil, nor does he tempt anyone; but each person is tempted when they are dragged away by their own evil desire and enticed. Then, after desire has conceived, it gives birth to sin; and sin, when it is full-grown, gives birth to death."** (James 1:13-15)

When facing trials, many blame God. They reason: "This is God's doing, so whatever evil comes out of this is His fault." This thinking is lethal.

The pathway is clear:

**Temptation → Desire → Sin → Death**

Compare it to God's pathway:

**Testing → Perseverance → Maturity → Life**

God is not evil. We are. Sin doesn't come from outside—it emerges from within us. God doesn't entrap us in sin; He's entirely good.

**"Don't be deceived, my dear brothers and sisters. Every good and perfect gift is from above, coming down from the Father of the heavenly lights, who does not change like shifting shadows."** (James 1:16-17)

God is consistent. He doesn't alternate between mercy and cruelty. He's not playing games. He's not double-minded.

### How We Blame God Today

We use trials as excuses for lower moral standards:

- "I'm poor, so I'll be stingy and selfish."
- "I'm underpaid, so I'll cheat my employer."
- "I'm lonely, so I'll marry a non-Christian instead of waiting."
- "Movies are expensive, so I'll steal them."
- "My parents won't let me attend church, so I'll abandon Christianity."
- "I'm sick, so I'll cheat on exams."

These desires originate in us, not God. Testing never justifies sin. If you sin, you've already failed in perseverance.

**Refuse victim mentality.** Don't fall into blaming circumstances for your choices. Instead, see life as a trial to be persevered through, not an excuse for compromise.

## Part Three: God Has Already Proven His Goodness

### The Ultimate Test Already Passed

Yet there's no reason to doubt God's goodness—because He has already undergone testing for us.

**"He chose to give us birth through the word of truth, that we might be a kind of firstfruits of all he created."** (James 1:18)

By His own initiative, God sent His Son to redeem us from sin. He's made us firstfruits of His creation—not destined for death like sin naturally produces, but born into life through the gospel.

God's goodness has been proven beyond question:

**"God demonstrates his love for us in this: While we were still sinners, Christ died for us."** (Romans 5:8)

Jesus didn't just suffer as we suffer. He persevered perfectly to the very end. He cried out, "My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?"—yet He remained obedient on that cross. He never wavered in loyalty. He never doubted God's goodness or intentions. He underwent every test and endured through complete obedience.

**"For we do not have a high priest who is unable to sympathize with our weaknesses, but we have one who has been tempted in every way, just as we are—yet was without sin. Let us then approach the throne of grace with confidence, so that we may receive mercy and find grace to help us in our time of need."** (Hebrews 4:15-16)

Jesus suffered. Jesus was tempted. Jesus knows your struggle intimately. And He persevered perfectly so we could trust Him completely.

This is the God you're called to trust, regardless of circumstances. Do not be deceived. Blessed is the person who remains steadfast under trial.

---

## Conclusion: Look to the End

Trials are unavoidable if you want a life worth having. Pain is temporary. The good news is that testing has an end—a glorious one.

One day, you will receive the crown of life. There will be no more tears, no more sadness. You will enjoy God forever.

But that's the future. Today, persevere. Be ready to endure. Let the pain do its work.

Be a tested people. When that final day comes, there will be only two kinds of people: those who persevered and received the crown of life, and those who faded away in the heat.

Choose to be among the tested, the strong, the faithful.

**Persevere.**
