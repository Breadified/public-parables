# Matthew 5:38-48

**Title:** Loving Like the Father  
**Date:** September 7, 2008  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Law: Eye for Eye (v.38)

We encounter one of the most recognizable commandments in all of Scripture: "Eye for eye, and tooth for tooth." This principle appears throughout the Old Testament, establishing a framework for justice in ancient Israel.

> "You have heard that it was said, 'Eye for eye, and tooth for tooth.'" (Matthew 5:38)

### The Origin of the Law

This law emerges from multiple Old Testament contexts, each revealing its original purpose:

**Exodus 21:22-25** (context of injury):
> "If men who are fighting hit a pregnant woman and she gives birth prematurely but there is no serious injury, the offender must be fined... But if there is serious injury, you are to take life for life, eye for eye, tooth for tooth, hand for hand, foot for foot, burn for burn, wound for wound, bruise for bruise."

**Leviticus 24:17-21** (context of justice):
> "If anyone takes the life of a human being, he must be put to death... If anyone injures his neighbor, whatever he has done must be done to him: fracture for fracture, eye for eye, tooth for tooth."

**Deuteronomy 19:16-21** (context of the law courts):
This passage applies the principle when a witness brings false testimony, ensuring the punishment matches the intended harm.

### Why This Law Was Good

The law of proportional justice served several vital purposes:

- **Promotes fairness**: It ensures equal retribution. When someone wrongs another, justice demands accountability at a proportional level—not arbitrary punishment.
- **Prevents escalation**: Most importantly, it functions as a *limiting* principle. Without such boundaries, personal vengeance spirals into blood feuds and tribal warfare. If someone injures you, you might kill them; their family kills your son; you kill their son plus others—and the cycle never ends. This law says: *no more than equal damage*. It was a mercy to the offender, preventing the endless cycles of retaliation we see in our world.

### How the Law Was Corrupted

Over time, the Jewish oral tradition twisted this law from a *limiting* principle into a *maximizing* one. The Pharisees began asking: "How much revenge may I legally extract?" The law became about personal vengeance rather than justice. They missed the deeper principle—that justice must be tempered with mercy.

## Jesus' Reinterpretation: Non-Resistance and Generosity (v.39-42)

Jesus radically reframes this law, not by abolishing it but by restoring its original intention:

> "But I tell you, Do not resist an evil person. If someone strikes you on the right cheek, turn to him the other also. And if someone wants to sue you and take your tunic, let him have your cloak as well. If someone forces you to go one mile, go with him two miles. Give to the one who asks you, and do not turn away from the one who wants to borrow from you." (Matthew 5:39-42)

Jesus illustrates his principle through four concrete examples:

1. **Physical assault**: Turn the other cheek
2. **Legal injustice**: Surrender more than demanded
3. **Forced service**: Go beyond what's required
4. **Financial requests**: Give generously

### Why This Shift Is Necessary

The narrow, legalistic reading of "eye for eye" fails in practice:

- **It doesn't restore what's lost**: Knocking out the offender's tooth doesn't give you back your tooth. Money might compensate for property, but not for human dignity or suffering.
- **It fuels endless cycles**: Even limited retaliation can perpetuate violence. The vicious cycle continues.
- **Mercy is essential**: The Old Testament teaches us that God's justice far exceeds human justice. We cannot measure retribution accurately, and our fallen nature distorts even fair punishment into cruelty.

Jesus is calling us back to the *heart* of the law: not vengeance, but *mercy*. Not payback, but *love*.

## Understanding Christian Relationship to Old Testament Law

Before we can apply these teachings, we must understand how Old Testament law relates to us as Christians. The primary question is: Are we required to follow these commands literally?

Several interpretations exist:

**The Literal Approach**: Some argue the Sermon on the Mount demands radical discipleship—we should follow these teachings exactly as written. Yet this proves impossible. If we literally cut off our hand for every sin, none of us would survive a week.

**The Interim Ethic View**: Others suggest these teachings apply only to Jesus' immediate context, when the end of the world seemed imminent. Just as we might make radical decisions during a final exam period (all-nighters, no entertainment), Jesus' disciples lived radically because they believed the end was near. Once that eschatological urgency faded, the teachings became irrelevant.

**The Impossible Standard View**: This interpretation sees the Sermon as revealing God's impossible standard, designed to drive us to grace. Since we cannot fulfill it, the teachings are meant only to expose our need for Christ, not to guide daily living.

**The Wisdom Approach**: However, there's a more compelling reading. Jesus himself declares in Matthew 5:17:

> "Do not think that I have come to abolish the Law or the Prophets; I have not come to abolish them but to fulfill them."

### Christological Fulfillment

The six reinterpretations in the Sermon ("You have heard... but I tell you") all find their answer in *Jesus himself*. He is the fulfillment of the law. These commands describe what Jesus did:

- **Non-resistance**: When arrested by Pilate and the Jewish mob, Jesus went willingly and could have called twelve legions of angels but refused.
- **Turning the other cheek**: The high priests and Sanhedrin spit in his face and struck him with their fists; he did not retaliate. Roman guards flogged him and struck him repeatedly; he remained silent.
- **Surrendering clothing**: His garments were divided and distributed by lot at the crucifixion.
- **Going the extra mile**: He walked to Golgotha bearing his cross.
- **Giving to those who ask**: He gave his life for those who persecuted him.

Jesus is the complete embodiment of this reinterpretation. Having fulfilled the law perfectly, the Mosaic system no longer binds us. Instead, these teachings now function as *wisdom*—guidance for living according to the greater law of *love*.

## Application: Love as the Governing Principle

If the Sermon is wisdom rather than law, it grants us flexibility to apply its principles according to *love*. The governing principle is not vengeance or payback, but *mercy*.

Consider how this plays out:

**"Do not resist an evil person"** does not mean we cannot be police officers or defend the vulnerable. It means that in situations requiring wisdom, we choose the loving response. Sometimes love demands that we fight injustice; sometimes love demands that we surrender our rights to break a cycle of conflict.

**Enforcing rights** is sometimes wise; sometimes it is not. If someone steals from you, love might demand you pursue justice to protect others. If someone wrongs you financially, love might demand you forgive. The principle is love, discerned through wisdom—not an arbitrary rule.

**Generosity** follows the same pattern. When a beggar asks, love might mean giving them a meal. But wisdom asks: What actually helps this person? What does genuine love require in this moment?

The command to "pick and choose" is not vicious hypocrisy—it is *wisdom*. We apply the spirit of the law (mercy and love) rather than the letter, guided by the principle that animates all of Scripture: *love your neighbor as yourself* (Galatians 5:14).

## The Law: Love Your Enemy (v.43-47)

The second great reinterpretation concerns our enemies:

> "You have heard that it was said, 'Love your neighbor and hate your enemy.' But I tell you: Love your enemies and pray for those who persecute you, that you may be sons of your Father in heaven. He causes his sun to rise on the evil and the good, and sends rain on the righteous and the unrighteous. If you love those who love you, what reward will you get? Are not even the tax collectors doing that? And if you greet only your brothers, what are you doing more than others? Do not even pagans do that?" (Matthew 5:43-47)

### The Distortion of the Original Law

Leviticus 19:18 commands us to love our neighbor, but nowhere does it command us to hate our enemies. The Pharisees created this requirement by defining "neighbor" narrowly—only those within their community. Everyone else became an enemy to be opposed.

Jesus fundamentally redefines what it means to be a son or daughter of God. We become his children not through ethnic identity or legal obedience, but through *functional sonship*—by loving as he loves.

### The Two Arguments for Loving Enemies

**First, we reflect the Father's character**: God causes his sun to rise on both evil and good people; he sends rain on the righteous and unrighteous alike. His love is unconditional and universal. To be God's child means to love without calculating who "deserves" it.

**Second, loving only those who love us is self-interest**, not virtue. Tax collectors do this. Pagans do this. Anyone driven by survival instinct loves their allies. The question Jesus poses cuts to the heart of discipleship: *"What are you doing more than others?"*

This echoes the earlier teaching about salt and light. How are we different? How does our love transcend mere self-preservation?

### Jesus as the Fulfillment

Jesus perfectly embodied this teaching. He loved his enemies—not in sentimental feeling, but in concrete action. He spent time with tax collectors and prostitutes. He healed the servants of those who opposed him. Most radically, he prayed for forgiveness even as his enemies crucified him.

## The Call to Perfection (v.48)

> "Be perfect, therefore, as your heavenly Father is perfect." (Matthew 5:48)

The word translated "perfect" carries the sense of *completion* or *fulfillment*. Jesus calls us not to sinless perfection (impossible for fallen humans) but to *wholehearted devotion*—following not from legalistic obligation but from transformed hearts.

This is the maximalist approach: pursue the *spirit* of God's law with full commitment, not the bare minimum of legalistic compliance. We follow Jesus, who fulfilled the law completely, knowing that in him we are counted as righteous.

The path forward is wisdom guided by love—the principle that animates all of Scripture and the character of our heavenly Father.
