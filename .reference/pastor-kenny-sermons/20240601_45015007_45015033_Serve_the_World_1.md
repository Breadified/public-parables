## Opening: A Time of Grief

Our church has been hit by a wave of grief recently. We prayed for Elaine's mother last Sunday after her heart attack, and sadly she passed away that Monday night. But she isn't the only one. In just the last fortnight, several families connected to our church have experienced loss—a grandfather, a grandmother, a mother-in-law, a sister-in-law, an uncle.

## Part One: Gentiles to Glorify God (Romans 15:8-14)

### The Surprising Revelation

For centuries, the Gentiles existed on the margins of God's people. In the temple system, they occupied the outer courtyard—present but separate, not full participants in the covenant. Some thought they would forever remain second-class citizens, requiring circumcision and conversion to Judaism before accessing God's promises.

But Paul reveals something revolutionary in this passage. Christ came as a servant to the Jews to confirm the promises made to the patriarchs. Yet the good news extends beyond Israel:

> "The Gentiles might glorify God on behalf of his mercy" (Romans 15:9)

Paul supports this claim with a series of Old Testament passages that had always been there, prophesying this very moment:

> "Therefore I will praise you among the Gentiles; I will sing the praises of your name." (2 Samuel 22:50, Psalm 18:49)

> "Rejoice, you Gentiles, with his people." (Deuteronomy 32:43)

> "Praise the Lord, all you Gentiles; let all the peoples extol him." (Psalm 117:1)

> "The Root of Jesse will spring up, one who will arise to rule over the nations; in him the Gentiles will hope." (Isaiah 11:10)

These weren't songs of victory sung over conquered peoples. Rather, they invited the Gentiles to join Israel in chorus, together glorifying the one true God.

### The Mystery Revealed

Here lies the mystery of the gospel that Paul emphasizes:

> "Now to him who is able to establish you in accordance with my gospel, the message I proclaim about Jesus Christ, in keeping with the revelation of the mystery hidden for long ages past, but now revealed and made known through the prophetic writings by the command of the eternal God, so that all the Gentiles might come to the obedience that comes from faith." (Romans 16:25-27)

> "This mystery is that through the gospel the Gentiles are heirs together with Israel, members together of one body, and sharers together in the promise in Christ Jesus." (Ephesians 3:6)

> "There is neither Jew nor Gentile, neither slave nor free, nor is there male and female, for you are all one in Christ Jesus." (Galatians 3:28)

This doesn't mean everyone becomes the same. Rather, it means standing before God on equal footing. The mystery is not that Gentiles are included—they always were in God's plan. The mystery is that they are included as **heirs**, not as servants. They don't need to become Jewish first.

### An Illustration: The Roman Household

Think of a Roman household of Paul's time. It included not just the family members but also servants, maids, and laborers—all living together under one roof. Jewish people perhaps imagined that relationship with God would work similarly: Jews as the landowners inside the temple, Gentiles as servants in the outer courtyard, all together serving and glorifying God.

But the mystery reveals something radically different. The Gentiles are not second-class citizens. They are **children of God in their own right**.

### Application to Modern Racism

This ancient debate between Jew and Gentile remains relevant today in our discussions about racism and equality. The issue isn't between Jew and Gentile anymore, but between Gentile and Gentile—between different ethnic groups.

Consider the Australian Voice referendum from last year. I understand the desire to care for Indigenous Australians who face real disadvantages. But the solution to racism isn't **reverse** racism against other races. If we want to help the marginalized, we should help all marginalized people. If we want to help the poor, we should help all poor people—regardless of their ethnicity.

Why mark specific boxes by race rather than by need? Why not simply identify "the voiceless," "the dispossessed," or "those affected by racism"? Homelessness affects people of all colors. Poverty affects people of all colors.

As we move away from Christianity, we lose the Christian foundation for racial equality. The mystery of Christ has been revealed—yet sometimes we act as though we want to hide it again.

## Part Two: Leading the Gentiles (Romans 15:15-23)

### Paul's Unique Role

Paul sees himself with a particular calling:

> "The grace God gave me to be a minister of Christ Jesus to the Gentiles. He gave me the priestly duty of proclaiming the gospel of God, so that the Gentiles might become an offering acceptable to God, sanctified by the Holy Spirit." (Romans 15:16)

Notice the priestly language. Paul is not a priest in the Old Testament sense, but he describes his apostolic work using temple imagery. Just as a priest presents offerings to God, Paul's role is to present the Gentiles as an offering—sanctified and holy.

This authority came directly from Jesus:

> "But the Lord said to Ananias, 'Go! This man is my chosen instrument to proclaim my name to the Gentiles and their kings and to the people of Israel.'" (Acts 9:15)

It was also confirmed through the early church leadership:

> "Then Paul and Barnabas answered them boldly: 'We had to speak the word of God to you first. Since you reject it and do not consider yourselves worthy of eternal life, we now turn to the Gentiles. For this is what the Lord has commanded us: "I have made you a light for the Gentiles, that you may bring salvation to the ends of the earth."'" (Acts 13:46-47, quoting Isaiah 49:6)

Paul understood himself as fulfilling the suffering servant prophecy, continuing Jesus' work to bring the gospel to the nations.

### Paul's Ambitious Vision

What was Paul's ambition?

> "It has always been my ambition to preach the gospel where Christ was not known, so that I would not be building on someone else's foundation." (Romans 15:20)

He had covered enormous territory:

> "From Jerusalem all the way around to Illyricum, I have fully proclaimed the gospel of Christ." (Romans 15:19)

Paul focused on pioneering new territories rather than building on foundations others had already laid. This wasn't merely a missionary strategy; it reflected his unique apostolic calling.

But why go to Rome, which already had an established church? Some scholars debate Paul's exact meaning, but his primary focus remained on "unreached" territories—places where no one had yet proclaimed Christ.

> "Those who were not told about him will see, and those who have not heard will understand." (Isaiah 52:15)

Gospel proclamation is **labor-intensive**. It's not about building enough churches in enough cities and assuming people will simply come. It requires people—youth leaders, Sunday school teachers, Scripture teachers, ordinary believers talking with their neighbors and colleagues. The real question is local: Do my neighbors know Christ? Are there children in my sphere of influence who haven't heard the gospel? If so, that's unreached territory.

As strange as it sounds, we import unbelievers by the thousands each week in cities like Sydney. The work is never complete until every person has encountered the good news.

Paul's unique role was to lay foundations in new regions, fulfilling Old Testament prophecies about the gospel reaching the nations. As John Stott noted, Paul was emphasizing his apostolic authority and the particular task God had given him. As N.T. Wright observed, Paul saw his mission as fulfilling Old Testament prophecies—bringing light to the nations.

### An Illustration: The Partner and the Associate

Imagine a senior partner in a law or accounting firm. She walks in, does the high-level work—maybe an hour, maybe a day. Then she leaves it to the junior associate to dot the i's and cross the t's for the next months. On the charge sheet: Partner, 1 hour at $2,000. Associate, 200+ hours at $200/hour.

Jesus came and invested three incredible years of ministry. Paul spent the next thirty years executing that vision, bringing the gospel to the ends of the civilized world. By the time Paul planned to reach Rome, you could say he had completed most of the project.

Some early sources suggest he may have continued to Spain after his time in Rome. Regardless, the account we have ends in Rome—the center of the civilized world. From Rome, the gospel would spread via Roman roads to the ends of the earth, eventually reaching even Australia.

The gospel to the world has been theologically completed.

## Part Three: Service of the Gentiles (Romans 15:25-33)

### An Unexpected Journey

Before heading to Spain, Paul took a detour to Jerusalem—a dangerous one:

> "Now, however, I am on my way to Jerusalem in the service of the Lord's people there. For Macedonia and Achaia were pleased to make a contribution for the poor among the Lord's people in Jerusalem. They were pleased to do it, and indeed they owe it to them. For if the Gentiles have shared in the Jews' spiritual blessings, they owe it to the Jews to share with them their material blessings. So after I have completed this task and have made sure that they have received this contribution, I will go to Spain and visit you on the way." (Romans 15:25-28)

This seems like mere itinerary. But Paul's description is strange. He willingly risked his life to deliver a gift when others could have taken it. He traveled with a large group of church representatives, making an expensive and complex journey. Why?

### The Eschatological Meaning

Some see something symbolic happening here—more than a simple charitable donation.

In Old Testament prophecy, the nations are depicted flowing to Mount Zion with gifts, repenting and serving the God of Israel:

> "At that time gifts will be brought to the Lord Almighty from a people tall and smooth-skinned, from a people feared far and wide... the gifts will be brought to Mount Zion, the place of the Name of the Lord Almighty." (Isaiah 18:7)

> "The children of your oppressors will come bowing before you; all who despise you will bow down at your feet." (Isaiah 60:14)

A Roman military triumph was a spectacular procession through the city gates—generals, captured leaders, slaves, and treasure. The conquered were displayed as subjects of Rome.

Now imagine Paul's vision: The Jews had largely rejected the gospel. But Paul wasn't deterred. He would lead a procession of Gentiles into Jerusalem—not as slaves or subjects, but as those offering their service to God. Through this act, Paul hoped to signal to the Jewish people that the end times had come. The Gentiles are here. Jesus is the Messiah. Perhaps jealousy would stir some Jews to believe.

> "I urge you, brothers and sisters, by our Lord Jesus Christ and by the love of the Spirit, to join me in my struggle by praying to God for me. Pray that I may be kept safe from the unbelievers in Judea and that the contribution I take to Jerusalem may be favorably received by the Lord's people there." (Romans 15:30-31)

### Application: We Are That Offering

The application isn't primarily about missionary work or fundraising. It's about **what we are**:

> "Therefore, I urge you, brothers and sisters, in view of God's mercy, to offer your bodies as a living sacrifice, holy and pleasing to God—this is your true and proper worship." (Romans 12:1)

We are the priestly sacrifice presented to the world.

**What is your ambition?** In light of God's mercy, what drives you?

The end times are here. Death surrounds us. We are playing for keeps. Our choices to follow God—or not to—will be permanent, unable to be reversed for eternity.

So we proclaim the gospel. We build the infrastructure needed to sustain gospel proclamation. We have children so we have future gospel proclaimers. We serve here, anywhere. We set aside earthly desires.

Your ambition isn't to visit foreign countries or keep up with your neighbors' materialism and experiences. Your ambition is beyond this world. It is heavenly.

We desire God to return and everyone to be prepared for his return.
