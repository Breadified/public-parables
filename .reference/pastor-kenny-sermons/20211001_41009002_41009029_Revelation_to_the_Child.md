# Mark 9:2-29

**Title:** Revelation to the Child  
**Date:** 31 October 2021

## Listen (9:2-8)

> *After six days Jesus took Peter, James and John with him and led them up a high mountain, where they were all alone. There he was transfigured before them. His clothes became dazzling white, whiter than anyone in the world could bleach them.*

The word "transfigured" means to morph or change—a complete transformation. Mark includes a remarkable detail: Jesus' clothes, normally grey and brown from constant travel, became brilliantly white. The evangelist emphasizes that no amount of earthly bleach could achieve such whiteness. This is glory beyond the natural world.

> *And there appeared before them Elijah and Moses, who were talking with Jesus.*

This appearance carries profound significance. Moses represents the Law—he brought the ten plagues, split the Red Sea, and led Israel to the Promised Land. Elijah represents the Prophets—he called down fire from heaven and was taken up to heaven in a chariot of fire. Together, they embody the Old Testament itself.

Yet Jesus stands apart. The appearance of these two giants of faith alongside Jesus signals a critical moment: the handing of the baton of revelation from the Law and Prophets to Jesus himself.

> *Then a cloud appeared and covered them, and a voice came from the cloud: "This is my Son, whom I love. Listen to him."*

The cloud recalls Exodus 24:15, where the cloud covered Mount Sinai and the glory of the Lord settled there—the very presence of God. When Moses descended from that mountain after encountering God's presence, his face was radiant with glory (Exodus 34:29-30). 

Jesus embodies this same divine radiance. As Hebrews 1:3 declares, "The Son is the radiance of God's glory and the exact representation of his being, sustaining all things by his powerful word." While Moses was faithful as a servant (Hebrews 3:5), Jesus is the Son—greater than any figure in Israel's history.

The Father's command is unmistakable: **Listen to him.** He can be trusted. He can be believed. He is not deceiving you.

> *Suddenly, when they looked around, they no longer saw anyone with them except Jesus.*

## Faith (9:9-29)

### The Descent and Confusion

> *As they were coming down the mountain, Jesus gave them orders not to tell anyone what they had seen until the Son of Man had risen from the dead. They kept the matter to themselves, discussing what "rising from the dead" meant.*

The disciples descend from the mountain top experience, bewildered by Jesus' cryptic words about resurrection. They don't yet comprehend his meaning.

### The Reality Below

> *When they came to the other disciples, they saw a large crowd around them and the teachers of the law arguing with them.*

What a stark contrast to the mountain's glory. At the base awaits a scene of conflict and need. The disciples encounter a boy possessed by a spirit that has robbed him of speech—an unclean spirit that throws him to the ground, causes him to foam at the mouth, gnash his teeth, and become rigid.

> *A man in the crowd answered, "Teacher, I brought you my son, who is possessed by a spirit that has robbed him of speech. Whenever it seizes him, it throws him to the ground. He foams at the mouth, gnashes his teeth and becomes rigid. I asked your disciples to drive out the spirit, but they could not."*

The father's faith wavers. He adds the crucial words:

> *"But if you can do anything, take pity on us and help us."*

His doubt is palpable—*if* you can. He has tried everything. His child's suffering has worn him down.

### The Call to Belief

> *Jesus asked, "Everything is possible for one who believes."*

Jesus reframes the question entirely. The issue is not his power—it is the father's faith. Every thing is possible for the one who **believes**.

> *When Jesus saw that a crowd was running to the scene, he rebuked the impure spirit. "You deaf and mute spirit," he said, "I command you, come out of him and never enter him again."*

Notice Jesus calls the spirit "deaf and mute." This echoes the Exodus motif throughout. When Moses descended from Mount Sinai with the Ten Commandments, he found the people had fashioned a golden calf—an idol that cannot speak or hear. Idols are deaf and mute, as Psalm 115:4-7 describes: they have mouths but do not speak, eyes but do not see, ears but do not hear.

The parallel is striking. Just as Elijah confronted the prophets of Baal at Mount Carmel, calling on a silent, unresponsive god (1 Kings 18:26-29), this demon represents spiritual deafness and muteness—the inability to hear God's voice.

But our God is not deaf and mute. He has spoken. He speaks through his Word. He listens to our prayers.

> *The spirit shrieked, convulsed him violently and came out. The boy looked so much like a corpse that many said, "He's dead." But Jesus took him by the hand and lifted him to his feet, and he stood up.*

Jesus raises him from apparent death to new life. This is what salvation looks like—deliverance from spiritual death to life in God's kingdom.

### Prayer: Faith Made Visible

> *After Jesus had gone indoors, his disciples asked him privately, "Why couldn't we drive it out?"*

They had been given power to cast out demons, yet they failed. Jesus explains:

> *"This kind can come out only by prayer."*

The shift from "belief" to "prayer" is intentional. Belief is theoretical; prayer is observable action—faith made tangible in words. Prayer is belief expressed through speech, through vulnerability, through admission that we cannot do this ourselves.

Why do we pray? We acknowledge that we are insignificant compared to God, that we are sinful, that we cannot redeem ourselves. We must trust in Jesus. Faith in God must be expressed through words: "Lord, I am not able to do this myself, but I trust in your ability."

Elijah understood this. Before God sent fire from heaven on Mount Carmel, Elijah prayed: "Lord, the God of Abraham, Isaac and Israel, let it be known today that you are God in Israel and that I am your servant and have done all these things at your command. Answer me, Lord, answer me, so these people will know that you, Lord, are God, and that you are turning their hearts back again" (1 Kings 18:36-37).

Prayer is not mere meditation or internal reflection—though these have their place. Prayer is speaking to God, engaging in dialogue, committing ourselves to relationship rather than fleeting thoughts about him. When we pray aloud or speak our faith in words, we bind ourselves to God in covenant relationship.

The father of the afflicted boy understood this:

> *"I do believe; help me overcome my unbelief!"*

His prayer combines faith with honest acknowledgment of his struggle. He speaks his belief and his doubt simultaneously—and Jesus honors that prayer.

## Jesus Is the Ultimate Revealer (Application)

### Listen to Him

The passage opens with the Father's command: **Listen to him.** This is the call of reformation—back to the Bible.

During the Reformation, the Church returned to Scripture itself. Martin Luther translated the Bible from Hebrew and Greek into the vernacular, essentially inventing the German language in the process. The Gutenberg printing press made Bibles available to common people. The movement was `ad fontes`—"back to the sources"—recovering what had been lost in the Dark Ages.

But there was a deeper principle at work. During the medieval period, people relied on special priests and authorities who claimed unique access to God's revelation. Knowledge came through intermediaries. But the Reformation declared that every believer could understand Scripture. Even the ploughboy could interpret God's Word.

Why? Because Jesus is the one who was in the presence of God and received divine revelation. We don't need to be that radiant person—we couldn't survive it anyway. Moses barely survived receiving just ten direct words from God. All we need do is **listen to him** through his Word.

### Read the Bible

- Read Scripture at home, consistently
- Interpret the Old Testament through the lens of the New
- Understand that the Law (Moses) and Prophets (Elijah) all point to Jesus

### Pray With Commitment

Prayer is not optional luxury or last resort—it is the first port of call in all matters, whether big or small. When facing something risky, during anxious times, as part of daily routine, before meals. Prayer expresses that we are not deaf and mute, but people who listen to God's words and speak our faith to him.

Then, while we act wisely according to our best judgment, we let time pass and see how God answers, trusting that he has handled our circumstances well.

### Why This Matters

Jesus comes to save us from judgment, rescuing us from ignorance, from spiritual deafness and muteness, from being dead in sin. He lifts us up by the hand and raises us to our feet, just as he did the boy at the mountain's base.

As our church restarts and rebuilds after loss of habit during lockdown, we return to basics: **Bible and prayer.** We listen to Jesus. We speak to our Father in faith. And in both listening and speaking, we experience the presence of God and the reality of his kingdom.
