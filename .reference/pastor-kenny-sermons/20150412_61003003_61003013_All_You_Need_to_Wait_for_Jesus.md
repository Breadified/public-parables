# 2 Peter 3:3-13

**Title:** All You Need to Wait for Jesus
**Date:** April 12, 2015
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Don't Be Wrong About Time

We live in a world that seems to stretch endlessly before us. Life appears long—we have time for education, careers, experiences, travel. Yet Peter challenges us to reconsider our understanding of time entirely. The core issue isn't whether time feels long or short, but whether we're thinking about time correctly.

### Time Feels Long (v. 4)

On the surface, time appears to extend indefinitely. We're living longer than ever—the average lifespan in developed countries now exceeds 80 years. We fit more experiences into our lives: multiple degrees, career changes, international travel, musical training. By our reckoning, time is abundant.

The **scoffers** Peter describes exploit this illusion. They're the mockers mentioned in Psalm 2 who ridicule God's sovereignty. Their argument is simple and seemingly logical: "Where is this 'coming' he promised? Ever since our ancestors died, everything goes on as it has since the beginning of creation." Generations have passed. Jesus hasn't returned. The world runs like clockwork—the same weather cycles, the same seasons, the same patterns repeating endlessly.

This perspective has deep roots. Ancient Eastern religions developed an understanding of time as cyclical—an infinite wheel of death and rebirth, repeating endlessly. For some, this cycle becomes exhausting, driving them to seek Nirvana, a state of non-existence. Even today, after 2,000 years since Peter wrote these words, the waiting feels interminable.

For those of us consciously waiting for Christ's return, this creates a peculiar vulnerability. We stand like someone at a bus stop waiting for a bus that never comes. People pass by wondering what we're doing. Entire demographics cycle through—morning commuters, afternoon mothers with prams, evening schoolchildren. When the same people from the morning shift pass us again on the evening bus, they stare. We feel foolish.

This frustration cuts deepest for those who suffer. The chronically ill—not well enough to enjoy life, not sick enough to die quickly. Christians in the Middle East and Africa facing persecution. For them, the question becomes unbearable: When will perfection come? When will this pain end? The temptation is real: He isn't coming. I should lose hope. Perhaps time is cyclical after all.

Yet here is Peter's first correction: though we wait, we wait not for long. For time is, in fact, short.

## Time Is Short (v. 8)

Peter offers us a radically different perspective on time. He doesn't deny that time feels long; instead, he recalibrates our understanding.

### God's Perception of Time

> With the Lord a day is like a thousand years, and a thousand years are like a day.

This isn't a mathematical conversion rate for calculating divine transactions. Rather, Peter is jolting our sense of perspective. From God's eternal vantage point, the entire span of human history—including the nearly 2,000 years since Christ's ascension—is minuscule. When Peter wrote this letter around 60 AD, only thirty years had passed since the resurrection. In God's reckoning, that's less than 0.03 of a day. Roughly an hour.

To grasp this, consider your own experience. To a young child, a year in school feels endless. But to a parent watching their child grow, those years pass in a blink. To God, who exists from eternity, all of human time is similarly compressed. This is the "grand scheme"—God's perspective, not ours. He is creating for himself a people eager to do good, a holy people. And in the scope of His eternal plan, the time we're waiting is nothing.

### We Live in a High-Risk Zone

But time is also short in another sense: we are in a danger zone.

> The day of the Lord will come like a thief. The heavens will disappear with a roar; the elements will be destroyed by fire, and the earth and everything done in it will be laid bare.

Consider a soldier deployed to war. Technically, his life expectancy might be eighty years. But he operates under constant risk. The possibility of sudden death is real and present. Because of this, soldiers write letters as if each one might be their last. They understand viscerally what we often forget: time is short when you're in a high-risk zone.

Similarly, we live under the promised return of Christ. It could happen at any moment. God has demonstrated this pattern before. In Noah's time, scoffers mocked, and the world was deluged and destroyed. Peter reminds us:

> By God's word the heavens came into being and the earth was formed out of water and by water. By these waters also the world of that time was deluged and destroyed. By the same word the present heavens and earth are reserved for fire.

The world was created by God's word. Just as easily, by His word it can return to formlessness. What was created can be uncreated. This isn't speculation—it's a pattern God has already demonstrated. When that moment comes, it will arrive unexpectedly. Without warning. The earth and everything in it will be laid bare.

### Time Ends

Time is also short because it has an endpoint. Unlike the cyclical worldview of Eastern religions, Christianity presents time as linear: it has a beginning, a middle, and an end. There is coming judgment.

This judgment is total and absolute. It cannot judge one nation and spare another. It cannot condemn the child rapist but overlook ordinary sins. Every person, every building, every structure will face that day. And it will be final—there is no court of appeal, no second chance to recant, no opportunity to restore what judgment has destroyed.

Yet here is the mercy: God's justice would demand immediate judgment, but His patience holds back that day. His desire is to see more people saved. He is waiting for that last person whom He has elected to come to faith. The only reason judgment hasn't come is so that more might repent and believe.

## Now Is the Time of Salvation (v. 9)

> The Lord is not slow in keeping his promise, as some understand slowness. He is patient with you, not wanting anyone to perish, but everyone to come to repentance.

God's patience is not laziness or indifference. It is patience born from love—a desire to see people saved. The very thing that frustrates us (the delay of Christ's return) reveals God's heart for the lost. He is extending the invitation. He is waiting for you.

If you are not yet a Christian, consider this: you may be the one person God is still waiting for. The suffering of the world, the injustice, the cries of Christians everywhere—all would end if you would turn and believe. God's judgment waits because of you. Repent now.

## What This Demands of Us

Peter's question pierces directly to the heart: "Since everything will be destroyed in this way, what kind of people ought you to be?"

### Live Holy and Godly Lives

> You ought to live holy and godly lives as you look forward to the day of God and speed its coming.

We have all we need for this. There are no more excuses. Peter has spent his entire letter establishing that God has given us everything required for a godly life.

This means:
- **Stop sinning.** Put away that sin today. Not tomorrow. Not after one more time. Today. Whether it's a compromising relationship, destructive online habits, or habitual patterns—address them now.
- **Start serving.** Go meet the lonely person at youth group. Do ministry. Engage in active love, not theoretical kindness.
- **Start learning.** Build your knowledge of Scripture so you won't be deceived by false teachers and so you can help others find their way to truth.
- **Be intentional.** The core principle isn't time management—it's priority management. Manage your actions. Manage your priorities. Be godly and holy. Time will fit around that.

### Speed Its Coming

How do we "speed the day's coming"? By proclaiming the gospel. If God is waiting for one last person to come to faith, let's bring them in. Let's go tell someone about Jesus. Let's not put off the Great Commission.

This is the only reason we're still here. Not for your first kiss or your marriage or parachuting or seeing Europe. We exist because God wants one more person saved. That's why He hasn't brought judgment yet. So proclaim it.

If you lack confidence, equip yourself. That's what church community is for. But don't delay. You don't know how much longer you have.

## The Application to Your Life

What will you do with the time you've been given? This question applies to everyone:

**High school students:** Congratulations on your ATARs. But university isn't what life is ultimately about. Time is short. We're in a high-risk zone. Change your priorities around.

**University students:** When choosing your course, ask yourself:
- Will this course pull you away from Christianity? If it demands so much that you have no time for church, Bible study, or Christian community, it's the wrong course.
- Can you proclaim the gospel within it? Commerce students have freedom in their schedules—use that for gospel work, not just for part-time jobs.
- Will it lead to a job where you can live godly? If the only path forward requires 12-hour days, weekend work, compromising ethics, or abandoning family and Christian friends, why start?
- Nursing and teaching are among the most demanding and helpful professions in society. Consider fields where you can genuinely serve.

**All of us:** The fundamental tension is this: time is long enough that if Jesus doesn't return in your lifetime, you'll still need to eat, work, and build a life. So shower. Go to university if it serves your kingdom purposes. Have a career. But don't therefore think you can do anything. Don't pursue luxury for its own sake. Don't chase status or wealth or experiences as your primary goal.

As you look forward to Christ's return, make every effort to be found spotless, blameless, and at peace with Him. Be on your guard so you're not carried away by the error of lawless people and fall from your secure position. Grow in the grace and knowledge of our Lord Jesus Christ.

## The Grand Perspective

We have all we need to wait for Jesus. We know the time. And knowing how long the wait is—or rather, how short it is in God's reckoning—we can endure.

In the blink of an eye, this world will end. God the judge will be here. When He looks at your life and asks for an accounting of your time on earth, what will you say? That you studied well? That you earned good money? That you achieved a high ATAR?

Wouldn't it be terrific to show Him love and godliness born out of adversity and perseverance? Wouldn't it be wonderful to have lived in a way that sped His day's coming? To hear Him say: "Well done, good and faithful servant"?

That's something worth investing the time of your life into.
