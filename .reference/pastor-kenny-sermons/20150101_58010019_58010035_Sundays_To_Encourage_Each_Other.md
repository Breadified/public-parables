# Hebrews 10:19-35

**Title:** Sundays: To Encourage Each Other
**Date:** 24 January 2015
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## 1. Approaching God (19-23)

Welcome back! Today we're exploring how to approach God and, just as importantly, how to approach one another as His people.

### The Most Holy Place

> Therefore, brothers and sisters, since we have confidence to enter the Most Holy Place by the blood of Jesus, by a new and living way opened for us through the curtain, that is, his body, and since we have a great priest over the house of God... (Hebrews 10:19-21)

To understand this passage, we need to grasp the layout of the ancient Jewish temple. There were three main spaces, each with increasing levels of sacredness—separated by a thick curtain:

- **The outer court**: where Gentiles could gather
- **The temple proper**: accessible to Jewish worshippers
- **The inner sanctuary (Holy of Holies)**: the most sacred place, entered only by the high priest

Think of it like your own home. The downstairs is open to everyone. The upstairs is reserved for family and close friends. But your parents' bedroom—that's forbidden to all but them. It holds your most precious possessions: passports, valuables, irreplaceable documents. It's the most holy of holies.

In the temple, this innermost chamber held Israel's most sacred treasures: the tablets of the Ten Commandments, relics like the bronze serpent, and the Ark of the Covenant—the ancient box upon which God's presence was said to rest. To enter the Most Holy Place was literally to enter God's bedroom, to stand in His presence.

### The Danger of God's Presence

But here's the problem: **no one could withstand the presence of God.**

The holiness of God is not a soft, welcoming thing. It's dangerous. When Moses saw merely the back of God, he aged visibly. When Uzzah touched the Ark, he died instantly. When God's presence came to the Philistines, they fell sick. Standing before the living God without protection meant death.

So the temple system required layer upon layer of protection. Constant sacrifices were made—their aroma providing temporary covering for sin, much like wearing protective gear before entering a nuclear reactor. And only one person could enter this place: the high priest, a man specially chosen by God.

Even then, he entered only once a year. And legend tells us he wore a rope around his waist—in case he died in God's presence and they needed to retrieve his body.

### Jesus Changes Everything

But now—**everything changes.**

> Since we have confidence to enter the Most Holy Place by the blood of Jesus... (Hebrews 10:19)

Jesus died on the cross so we don't have to die eternal death. His blood was shed. His body was crucified. He took the penalty of sin upon Himself so sinners could be saved.

The sacrifices of bulls and goats provided only temporary covering. Their aroma masked the stench of sin for a little while, but it never lasted. What we needed was a better sacrifice. We needed Jesus.

### Drawing Near With Confidence

> Let us draw near to God with a sincere heart and with the full assurance that faith brings, having our hearts sprinkled to cleanse us from a guilty conscience and having our bodies washed with pure water. (Hebrews 10:22)

We now have confidence—what the text calls "full assurance"—to approach God's presence. This is the incredible privilege of Christianity.

Imagine if Taylor Swift held a concert, and beyond the public seating, there was backstage access—a place not advertised, where you could walk right up and meet her. That access would be priceless. But here's what Jesus offers us: direct access to God Himself. Just walk in. "Hey, God."

This is fundamentally different from every other religion. In most religions, God is far away. You must perform countless rituals and jump through endless bureaucratic hoops to reach Him. Even atheists—whose god is government—can't approach the top; there's always another layer of bureaucracy.

But in Jesus, something remarkable happens: **God approaches us, and we can approach God.**

### How to Draw Near

How do we actually draw near to this holy God? James gives us the answer:

> Come near to God and he will come near to you. Wash your hands, you sinners, and purify your hearts, you double-minded. Grieve, mourn and wail. Change your laughter to mourning and your joy to gloom. Humble yourselves before the Lord, and he will lift you up. (James 4:8-10)

To draw near means **to humble yourself**. Verse 9's language of grieving and mourning isn't about despair—it's about understanding the weight of our sin. We must recognize that we're sinners with no right to stand in God's presence. We come with no claims, no entitlements.

We approach **on God's terms, not our own.**

When you visit Taylor Swift backstage, you go on her terms. You don't demand anything. You don't complain about the setup. You show gratitude for being allowed in at all.

This is how we approach God: with genuine humility, with a grateful heart, with no sense of entitlement. When we pray, we pray with thanksgiving—not expecting answers, not claiming rights, but asking with reverence. When we ask God questions, we ask in humility, submitting to His ultimate authority.

> Hold unswervingly to the hope we profess, for he who promised is faithful. (Hebrews 10:23)

We hold fast to our hope in Jesus, not because we've earned it, but because **God Himself is faithful.** He keeps His promises.

## 2. Approaching Each Other (24-35)

Having established how we approach God, Paul now addresses something equally vital: **how we approach one another.**

### Consider One Another

> And let us consider how we may spur one another on toward love and good deeds, not giving up meeting together, as some are in the habit of doing, but encouraging one another—and all the more as you see the Day approaching. (Hebrews 10:24-25)

Notice the phrase "one another" appears three times:

1. **"Spur one another"** (v. 24)
2. **"Meeting together"** (v. 25)
3. **"Encourage one another"** (v. 25)

"Let us consider" means to think seriously—with the same level of commitment we give to thinking about Christ Himself. This is serious business:

> Therefore, holy brothers and sisters, who share in the heavenly calling, fix your thoughts on Jesus, whom we acknowledge as our apostle and high priest. (Hebrews 3:1)

**"Spur one another"** carries an interesting double meaning. The word can be negative—as when Paul and Barnabas had such a sharp disagreement that they parted company. Yet later, Paul reconciled with Barnabas and John Mark. Sometimes the sharp edge of truth—like a spur—is what's needed.

> Speaking the truth in love, we will grow to become in every respect the mature body of him who is the head, that is, Christ. (Ephesians 4:15)

We spur one another toward **love and good deeds**—calling each other to the works God has prepared for us to do:

> For we are God's handiwork, created in Christ Jesus to do good works, which God prepared in advance for us to do. (Ephesians 2:10)

### The Importance of Meeting Together

> Not giving up meeting together, as some are in the habit of doing, but encouraging one another—and all the more as you see the Day approaching. (Hebrews 10:25)

"Encouraging one another" literally means "calling alongside"—standing with one another in the struggle against sin.

Here's the critical insight: **you cannot do these things alone.**

- You can't encourage yourself.
- You can't spur yourself on.
- You cannot do "together" if you're not together.
- You cannot "one another" without another.

It's like saying you can applaud with one hand. It's logically impossible.

### Why Sundays Matter

Many people view church as optional—just another item on a consumer menu. But consider: **If church is primarily about food, you'd be better off eating at home.** The food is better. You can eat in peace without a care in the world.

**If church is primarily about listening to a sermon, you can stream better ones at home.** There are world-class theologians and brilliant speakers on YouTube available anytime.

**If church is primarily about music, you have better options.** Your Spotify playlist, your home stereo with surround sound—you can get Taylor Swift-level production quality without leaving your couch.

But **if Sundays are about each other**, that's something you cannot duplicate at home.

Sundays aren't about consuming a product. They're about **participating together as a family.**

- Reading Scripture together, being of one mind, wrestling with God's Word—then discussing it over lunch and throughout the week.
- Laughing together.
- Singing together—not performing perfectly like a concert, but being on the same beat, hearing each other's voices, marching to the same drum, strengthening each other for action.

**We approach not as consumers but as participants with one another.**

That's why Sunday at 12pm matters. That's why the habit of meeting together deserves priority and importance.

### The Problem of Excuses

Life always brings obstacles. There will be assignments, birthday parties, exams, house chores, holidays. Once-off emergencies and important things come up. Some things can't be helped—that's just life.

But there's a difference between exceptions and habits. Your teeth can survive one night without brushing, but too many exceptions and decay sets in. You can skip the gym once, but if skipping becomes your habit, you'll never get fit. Do you enroll your child in piano lessons and then skip whenever something comes up?

The excuses we make to miss church—"I don't feel like it," "I might have a headache," "I have a birthday party or dentist appointment"—we would never make these excuses to our boss or to someone we're dating. Why do we make them to the people of God, for whom Christ died?

**Here's the challenge**: if you're new to our church, consider joining us regularly. If you've been coming once a month, stretch it to once a fortnight. If you come fortnightly, why not weekly? If you've committed to weekly but you're slacking, ask yourself what's holding you back.

### Preparing for Hardship

> If we deliberately keep on sinning after we have received the knowledge of the truth, no sacrifice for sins is left, but only a fearful expectation of judgment and of raging fire that will consume the enemies of God. (Hebrews 10:26-27)

The Hebrews faced persecution and were tempted to abandon Christ. The solution wasn't found in private faith alone. It was found in drawing near to Christ and drawing near to each other.

> Remember those earlier days after you had received the light, when you endured in a great conflict full of suffering. Sometimes you were publicly exposed to insult and persecution; at other times you stood side by side with those who were so treated. You suffered along with those in prison and joyfully accepted the confiscation of your property, because you knew that you yourselves had better and lasting possessions. (Hebrews 10:32-34)

Notice the phrases: **"stood side by side"** and **"suffered along with those in prison."**

The Hebrews survived persecution because they were together. They supported one another. And because of that togetherness:

> So do not throw away your confidence; it will be richly rewarded. (Hebrews 10:35)

Don't take each other for granted. When the day of sickness, suffering, or persecution comes—and it will come—it's far easier to withstand if someone is by your side.

**We prepare for that day the same way a sports team prepares: through huddles, through togetherness, through reminding each other of our hope.**

And notice:

> Do not give up meeting together... and all the more as you see the Day approaching. (Hebrews 10:25)

What is "the Day"? In Hebrews, it often refers to "Today"—the present moment, the everyday struggle against sin's deceitfulness:

> But encourage one another daily, as long as it is called "Today," so that none of you may be hardened by sin's deceitfulness. (Hebrews 3:13)

So don't miss Sunday. We've organized it. We've cooked lunch. We've gotten dressed—some of us have even put on a decent shirt. Don't take it for granted.

**Take the opportunity to encourage one another. Be there. Bring a friend. See you next Sunday.**
