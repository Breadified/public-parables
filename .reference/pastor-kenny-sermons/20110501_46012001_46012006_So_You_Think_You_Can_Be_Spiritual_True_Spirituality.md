# 1 Corinthians 12:1-6

**Title:** So You Think You Can Be Spiritual? True Spirituality

**Date:** 28 May 2011

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## What Is True Spirituality?

The Corinthian church faced a crucial question: What does it mean to be truly spiritual? This wasn't merely academic—it was deeply personal. We all carry our own conceptions of spirituality. For some, it's the yoga instructor transcending dental medication through meditation. For others, it's the contemplative monk in a quiet monastery, the television evangelist performing miracles, or the missionary who left everything to serve overseas. Maybe it's the faithful church attendee who participates in every event, the person with disciplined quiet times, or someone waking at 5 AM in the cold to pray. Many equate spirituality with emotionalism—the person who is passionate, spontaneous, loud, and fervent in worship.

The Corinthians struggled with these very questions, and Paul's response to them speaks directly to us today.

## The Setting: Corinth's Spiritual Crisis

Corinth was a wealthy, multicultural harbor city—a place of tremendous trading, abundance, and influence. But with prosperity came moral chaos: sexual immorality, idolatry, greed, and hedonism saturated the city. It was remarkable that a church existed there at all, let alone one planted by Paul himself (Acts 18).

Yet the church was struggling severely. Paul tells them directly: "Brothers, I could not address you as spiritual but as worldly—mere infants in Christ" (1 Corinthians 3:1). The Corinthian Christians reflected too closely the pagan culture around them. Their church was plagued by sexual immorality, bitter divisions, idolatry, selfishness, and greed so severe that some members went hungry. In reaction, a few went to the opposite extreme—strict asceticism—teaching that the body and all physical pleasure were evil.

Desperate for clarity, the Corinthians sent Paul a letter asking about spirituality. His response addresses the very heart of what true spirituality actually is.

## Part 1: Understanding "The Spirituals"

When Paul writes in 1 Corinthians 12:1, "Now about the spirituals," he's using a Greek term that simply means "spirituals"—not "spiritual gifts" as many translations suggest. This distinction matters profoundly.

The original Greek uses an adjective without an explicit noun, leaving us to determine what Paul means. Two main interpretations exist, and understanding which one Paul intends completely changes our understanding of spirituality.

### Option A: Spiritual Gifts?

The most popular interpretation suggests "spirituals" refers to spiritual gifts. This seems reasonable at first—after all, the Corinthians did have issues with gifts and abilities in their church.

However, this view has serious problems:

**The terminology doesn't match.** The phrase "spiritual gifts" (plural) doesn't actually exist anywhere in the New Testament. "Spiritual gift" (singular) appears only once—Romans 1:11—where Paul refers to imparting "a spiritual gift" to the Roman church. Later we discover this "gift" is simply the gospel, not some special ability.

**The separation is striking.** Throughout 1 Corinthians, when Paul discusses gifts (chapters 1, 7, 12:27-30), the Spirit is rarely mentioned. When he discusses the Spirit's work (chapter 2), gifts are virtually absent. In fact, Paul can tell the Corinthians they lack no gifts (1:7) while simultaneously saying they are not spiritual (3:1). If "spirituals" meant gifts, Paul would be contradicting himself.

The New Testament makes a surprisingly weak connection between the Spirit and gifts. This contrasts sharply with popular Christian teaching, where discussion of gifts is always paired with emphasis on the Spirit. The reality is more complex and more beautiful.

### A Necessary Correction: Who Actually Gives Gifts?

Here's something crucial: The Godhead—not exclusively the Holy Spirit—distributes gifts. While verse 7 speaks of "the manifestation of the Spirit" and verse 11 says the Spirit "divides them to each one," look at verse 6: "There are different kinds of working, but the same God works all of them in all men."

Throughout Scripture, God the Father, Jesus, and the Holy Spirit all give gifts. The Father gives (Hebrews 2:4; 2 Timothy 1:6; 1 Peter 4:10-11). Jesus gives (Ephesians 4:8). The Spirit gives (1 Corinthians 12:11). It's the unified work of the Trinity, not a solo performance by the Holy Spirit.

This matters because popular Christian culture has made the Holy Spirit sound like Santa Claus of the Trinity—the gift-giver—while the Father seems stern and the Son is the hero. But this is more pagan than Christian. The Spirit is not an impersonal supernatural force dispensing empowerment. The Godhead works together in perfect unity.

### Option B: Spiritual Truths (The Better Reading)

The interpretation I find more persuasive—and one that's been new to my own study—is that "spirituals" refers to spiritual truths, specifically the communication of spiritual truths to one another.

To understand what Paul means, look back to 1 Corinthians 2, where he discusses the Spirit extensively. There he contrasts the spirit of the world with the Spirit of God:

**The world's approach:**
- Eloquence and superior wisdom
- Wise and persuasive words
- Human wisdom
- The wisdom of this age

**God's approach:**
- Focus on Jesus Christ and him crucified
- Demonstration of Spirit and power
- God's power, not human reasoning
- God's secret wisdom, now revealed through his Spirit
- Words taught by the Spirit

The spiritual truths are the gospel itself—knowledge of Christ, understanding what God has freely given us, the message of wisdom that comes from God. When Paul speaks of "spirituals" in 1 Corinthians 12-14, he's talking about the communication of these gospel truths to one another.

This interpretation aligns perfectly with 1 Corinthians 9:11: "If we have sown spiritual seed among you, is it too much if we reap a material harvest from you?" The spiritual seed is the gospel message sown among them. Speaking spiritual truths means proclaiming and discussing the gospel.

**The essence:** When Paul or the Corinthians speak of "the spirituals," they're discussing spiritual truths—the gospel proclaimed, the truth spoken, the word of God shared among God's people.

## The Meaning of "Pneuma" (Spirit)

To understand spirituality, we must understand the word itself. In Greek, *pneuma* literally means wind or breath. Think of pneumonia (disease of the lungs), pneumatic tires (inflated tires), or pneumatology (study of the lungs). The Hebrew equivalent is *ruach*.

Throughout Scripture, God's spirit is his breath—his words, his life-giving force:

- Genesis 8:1: "God sent a wind on earth"
- Amos 4:13: "He who forms the mountains, creates the wind"
- Hebrews 1:7: "He makes his angels winds"
- 2 Thessalonians 2:8: "The Lord Jesus will overthrow the lawless one with the breath of his mouth"
- Genesis 2:7: "God breathed into his nostrils the breath of life"

In every case, God or Jesus is doing the breathing. The breath of God is the word of God. The spirit of God is the speaking God. Breath, wind, words, and life are interconnected—all flowing from the breath of the Almighty who speaks creation into being.

## Part 2: Three Marks of True Spirituality

### Mark 1: Don't Be Ignorant of Spiritual Truths

"Now about the spirituals, brothers, I do not want you to be ignorant" (12:1).

Too often, spirituality is equated with ignorance—tarot cards, horoscopes, psychics, empty meditation that blanks the mind of thought. This anti-intellectual spirituality pervades late-night television where charlatans ask for money with promises of divine blessing. Emotionalism gets mistaken for spirituality: "If you're spontaneous and don't think critically, you're more spiritual!"

But Christian spirituality is the opposite. It's rooted in knowledge—real, transformative knowledge. Paul wants us to know, to understand, to study. Emotions are good and beautiful and helpful, but they're not signs of spirituality. Ignorance is not spirituality.

This becomes clear in verse 2: "You know that when you were pagans, somehow or other you were influenced and led astray to mute idols." As pagans, they followed dumb idols—pieces of wood or silver that couldn't speak, couldn't respond, couldn't reveal truth. By contrast, the God of the Spirit is the speaking God, the revealing God, the God who communicates knowledge to his people.

**Application:**
- Non-Christians, know Jesus. Explore Christianity through courses like Christianity Explored. The non-Christian world is often ignorant of what Christians actually believe.
- Christians, keep growing in knowledge. It leads to godliness. Don't slack off in Bible study. Bring your Bible to church and write in it—it should be marked up with notes and insights. The goal is to learn spiritual truths and let them transform you.

### Mark 2: Confess the Lordship of Christ

"Therefore I tell you that no one who is speaking by the Spirit of God says, 'Jesus be cursed,' and no one can say, 'Jesus is Lord,' except by the Holy Spirit" (12:3).

If you're looking for a sign of spirituality, don't look for emotion. Look for this: the confession that Jesus is Lord.

This isn't a formula to chant mindlessly. It's the wholehearted commitment to the gospel—the acknowledgment that Jesus Christ died, rose again, and reigns as Lord of the universe. To say "Jesus is Lord" means submitting your life to his authority.

The acid test of whether someone is truly spiritual is whether they're under the Lordship of Christ. This doesn't mean Christians live perfectly—we all stumble and make mistakes. But the direction of our lives should be toward obedience. Piety and emotionalism don't compensate for refusing to follow Jesus. The person who attends church on Sunday but cheats on Monday, who prays at 7 AM but shows no real change in their character—they haven't truly confessed Christ's Lordship, no matter how spiritual they appear.

**Application:**
True spirituality means allowing Christ to be Lord over every area of life—your relationships, your work, your choices, your money. It's not about perfect performance; it's about genuine submission.

### Mark 3: The Gospel Is the Ultimate Spiritual Truth and Power

"There are different kinds of gifts, but the same Spirit. There are different kinds of service, but the same Lord. There are different kinds of working, but the same God works all of them in all men" (12:4-6).

Here Paul makes a stunning point: All Christians, regardless of their abilities or personalities, share something far more important than any gift—they share the same Spirit, the same Lord, the same God. More profoundly, all Christians share the same foundational spiritual truth: the gospel.

Whether you're emotionally expressive or quiet and contemplative, whether you're gifted in speaking or serving, whether you're an extrovert or introvert—you possess the supreme gift of all: eternal life through Christ. You've been saved by the same gospel. You're loved by the same God. You're under the same Lord.

In recent years, I've heard prominent Christian leaders suggest that some churches (particularly in Sydney) only believe in "the Father, Son, and Holy Bible." The implication is harsh: these churches don't have the Spirit, so they're not truly Christian. But this critique misses something vital.

First, the Holy Bible is intimately connected to the Holy Spirit—the breath of God. Is the word of God not "the sword of the Spirit"? If you want the Spirit, you'll find him in the word of God.

Second, and more fundamentally, this criticism often boils down to: "They don't have the same emotionalism I have." But emotionalism is not spirituality. Emotion is wonderful—our feelings matter—but being passionate or excited doesn't make you more spiritual. This logic echoes an old heresy: the idea of a "second baptism" where the real spiritual power comes after your initial conversion. But that's not biblical. When you first trusted Christ and surrendered to him, the Spirit came into you. That's your baptism. That's your access to God's power.

This kind of thinking raises the barrier to Christianity higher than it actually is. It makes people wonder if they're "really" Christian unless they have certain emotional experiences. Meanwhile, it obscures the most amazing gift of all—the gift that unites all believers: eternal life in Christ.

## What True Spirituality Actually Looks Like

Here's what I want you to see: True spirituality doesn't require dramatic displays. It doesn't demand loud worship or hand-raising or tears. It's not found in formality or emotionalism.

True spirituality is found whenever God's people gather to speak spiritual truths to one another—when we proclaim the gospel, when we remind each other of Christ's Lordship, when we share how the gospel has changed us. It's when we open God's word, have it read and proclaimed, and discuss how its truths apply to our lives. It's when we're bonded together not by personality or preferences, but by our common faith in the same Lord, served by the same Spirit, recipients of the same grace.

That's as spiritual as it gets.

## A Challenge for Us

As we gather together, I want to challenge you to speak spiritual truths to one another. Tell each other how the Lordship of Christ has affected your life. Share how God has helped you through difficulties. Ask each other: "How did God help you survive this season?" Talk about how you've been challenged by Scripture, how you've seen God work, how the gospel is changing you.

So you think you can be spiritual? Yes—you absolutely can. Start by knowing the truth of the gospel. Confess Christ as Lord. And then, speak those spiritual truths to one another. That's where true spirituality flourishes.
