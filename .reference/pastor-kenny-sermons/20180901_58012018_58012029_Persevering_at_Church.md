# Hebrews 12:18-29

**Title:** Persevering at Church  
**Date:** 9 September 2018  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Fiery Mountain: Mount Sinai

This week a volcano erupted somewhere in the world. It's a reminder that at any moment, there are approximately fifteen active volcanoes around the globe. Today we come to another fiery mountain—not in the natural world, but in Scripture.

The Letter to the Hebrews calls us to persevere in our faith despite hardship and discouragement. One challenge we face is that we naturally wish to experience God physically, to feel His presence tangibly. This leads us to Hebrews 12:18, where the author describes Mount Sinai—a fiery mountain from Israel's history.

> You have not come to a mountain that can be touched and that is burning with fire; to darkness, gloom and storm; to a trumpet blast or to such a voice speaking words that those who heard it begged that no further word be spoken to them, because they could not bear what was commanded: If even an animal touches the mountain, it must be stoned to death. (Hebrews 12:18-20)

This describes the scene at Mount Sinai approximately 1,500 years before Jesus. After Israel's escape from Egyptian slavery, they arrived at this mountain where God would speak the Law to them directly.

### The Preparation and the Manifestation

God instructed Moses to prepare the people for this encounter:

> The Lord said to Moses, "Go to the people and consecrate them today and tomorrow. Have them wash their clothes and be ready by the third day, because on that day the Lord will come down on Mount Sinai in the sight of all the people. Put limits for the people around the mountain and tell them, 'Be careful that you do not approach the mountain or touch the foot of it. Whoever touches the mountain is to be put to death. They are to be stoned or shot with arrows; not a hand is to be laid on them. No person or animal shall be permitted to live.'" (Exodus 19:10-13)

This wasn't arbitrary harshness. It reflected a profound spiritual reality: **God and sin do not mix**. 

Consider how we react when something repulsive—dirt, filth, anything unclean—touches our eye or our food. We recoil with revulsion and act decisively to remove it. This illustrates God's relationship to sin. He is not indifferent to it; He is repulsed by it. The holiness of God and the corruption of sin are utterly incompatible. It matters not whether the sin is great or small—the principle is the same. What level of contamination would you tolerate in your food? None. Neither can God.

When the third day arrived, the manifestation was terrible:

> On the morning of the third day there was thunder and lightning, with a thick cloud over the mountain, and a very loud trumpet blast. Everyone in the camp trembled. Mount Sinai was covered with smoke, because the Lord descended on it in fire. The smoke billowed up from it like smoke from a furnace, and the whole mountain trembled violently. As the sound of the trumpet grew louder and louder, Moses spoke and the voice of God answered him. (Exodus 19:16-19)

Even Moses, God's friend who spoke with Him face to face, responded with terror:

> The sight was so terrifying that Moses said, "I am trembling with fear." (Hebrews 12:21)

### The Problem with Seeking God Today

We live in an age that prizes experience above all else. We no longer chase material possessions as previous generations did—we chase experiences. We collect selfies, Instagram photos, memories of trips and adventures. **Experience has become our new idol.**

There are entire Christian denominations whose primary distinction is their claim that they can bring you into God's presence, that they can take you to the mountaintop experience. But here lies a profound problem: when God was tangible and approachable in Scripture, He was actually terrifying and unapproachable. If you truly experienced God's presence as it was revealed at Sinai, you would face a volcano of divine power. You would tremble with fear as Moses did.

The earthly experience of God is not what our modern minds imagine it to be. An unmediated encounter with God's holiness would be your death.

### Faith Without Feelings

This teaches us something essential: **our faith cannot be built on experiences or feelings.**

When I first became a Christian as a teenager, I experienced a remarkable convergence of circumstances. I gained new friends, I fell in love, I was accepted to a prestigious school—all at once. My hormones magnified the already intense spiritual highs I was feeling. That transcendent, mountaintop experience was unique to that season of life and will never be repeated.

But I cannot equate the absence of that feeling with God's absence or unreality. Feelings are temporary and unreliable. Facts, however, remain constant.

The fact that God exists and is holy does not depend on whether I feel it to be true. The fact that He loves you does not depend on whether you feel loved today. When suffering comes, when joy seems distant, when you lack the "zing" of early faith—these are not indicators that God has abandoned you. They are simply the natural rhythms of a human life lived in faith.

**Facts don't care about your feelings.** But God does care about your faithfulness.

## The Better Mountain: Mount Zion

Having described what we have not come to, the author now describes what we have come to:

> But you have come to Mount Zion, to the city of the living God, the heavenly Jerusalem. You have come to thousands upon thousands of angels in joyful assembly, to the church of the firstborn, whose names are written in heaven. You have come to God, the Judge of all, to the spirits of the righteous made perfect, to Jesus the mediator of a new covenant, and to the sprinkled blood that speaks a better word than the blood of Abel. (Hebrews 12:22-24)

This is not a physical, earthly mountain. It is heavenly, invisible, yet more real and enduring than Mount Sinai ever was. And in this reality, we encounter **a better word**—the word spoken through Jesus Christ.

### The Superior Revelation

The author connects this to an earlier passage:

> In the past God spoke to our ancestors through the prophets at many times and in various ways, but in these last days he has spoken to us by his Son, whom he appointed heir of all things, and through whom also he made the universe. (Hebrews 1:1-2)

Abel was the first death recorded in Scripture—a victim of sin. His blood cried out from the ground for justice. But Jesus offers a final death—His own—that ends all deaths. His blood speaks a better word than Abel's blood ever could, because it speaks not of accusation but of redemption.

This word is now alive within us who live by faith:

> For the word of God is alive and active. Sharper than any double-edged sword, it penetrates even to dividing soul and spirit, joints and marrow; it judges the thoughts and attitudes of the heart. (Hebrews 4:12)

Mount Sinai's revelation was external—the Law written on stone tablets, thundering from a mountain. Mount Zion's revelation is internal—the word of Christ cutting to the very marrow of our being, transforming us from the inside out.

## The Call to Perseverance

> See to it that you do not refuse him who speaks. If they did not escape when they refused him who warned them on earth, how much less will we, if we turn away from him who warns us from heaven? (Hebrews 12:25)

The logic is a classic "how much more" argument. If rejecting God's word on earth brought judgment, how much more terrible will be the judgment for rejecting His word from heaven—the word spoken through His Son?

Christ has come. He has revealed everything. He has died for us. To turn away from Him is to spurn the greatest gift ever offered. It is like a beloved child rejecting a father's sacrifice and throwing it back in his face.

### The Character of True Discipleship

To be a disciple is to have discipline. The Christian life is not a collection of emotions or peak experiences—it is a commitment to righteousness, holiness, and obedience. This requires grit. It demands:

- **Self-control** in the face of temptation
- **Generosity** when the world teaches hoarding
- **Compassion** when indifference is easier
- **Service** when we're exhausted
- **Faithfulness** when circumstances test us

Yet our generation struggles profoundly with discipline. We live in an age of unprecedented comfort, yet we exhibit little of the grit our predecessors possessed. We change jobs impulsively, abandon relationships at the first difficulty, break commitments casually. This extends even to basic responsibilities—many cannot commit to finishing a university degree or maintaining a single job.

### Discipline in Every Area

The author of Hebrews addresses this directly in chapter 13, calling the church to practical perseverance:

> Keep on loving one another as brothers and sisters. Do not forget to show hospitality to strangers. Continue to remember those in prison as if you were together with them, and those who are mistreated as if you yourselves were suffering. (Hebrews 13:1-3)

> Marriage should be honored by all, and the marriage bed kept pure, for God will judge the adulterer and all the sexually immoral. Keep your lives free from the love of money and be content with what you have, because God has said, "Never will I leave you; never will I forsake you." (Hebrews 13:4-5)

These are not suggestions for the exceptionally spiritual. They are the basic requirements of Christian faithfulness. And they all demand discipline—the willingness to keep loving when hurt, to keep giving when it's costly, to keep your marriage vows when attraction fades, to be content when comparison tempts you to want more.

Yet we are not very good at discipline. Half of our marriages end in divorce. We post our workplace absences on social media instead of calling our employers. We resign via text message. When faced with difficulty, we whine and blame others rather than persevere.

This unfaithfulness flows into every area of life—our relationships, our work, our spiritual commitments. We treat God as just another optional option in the Facebook of life.

### The Story of Akhwari

There is a story that reminds us what perseverance truly looks like.

In the 1968 Olympic Marathon in Mexico City, a runner named Akhwari competed. Approximately nineteen kilometers into the forty-two kilometer race, he was jostled by other runners and fell hard. His knee was apparently dislocated, his shoulder badly scraped, and his leg was bleeding. Medical staff urged him to withdraw from the race.

But Akhwari continued. He walked and limped and ran by turns, pushing forward in pain. He finished last among the fifty-seven competitors who completed the race. The winner had finished in 2:20:26. Akhwari crossed the line well over an hour later, in 3:25:27, as the sun had set and only a few thousand spectators remained in the stadium.

When asked later why he ignored the advice to pull out and continued running, Akhwari said simply:

> My country did not send me 5,000 miles just to start the race; they sent me to finish the race.

Of the seventy-five runners who started that marathon, eighteen others did pull out. Remarkably, no one remembers the name of the gold medal winner that year. But the world remembers Akhwari. We honor him not for victory, but for finishing the race in the most difficult circumstances.

**This is what we are called to do. To finish. To persevere.**

## The Promise to Those Who Persevere

> Therefore, since we are receiving a kingdom that cannot be shaken, let us be thankful, and so worship God acceptably with reverence and awe, for our God is a consuming fire. (Hebrews 12:28-29)

We do not come to a mountain that can be touched, that shakes and trembles. We come to a kingdom that **cannot be shaken**—eternal, unmovable, more real than anything our senses perceive.

And to encourage us in perseverance, God reminds us:

> Never will I leave you; never will I forsake you. So we say with confidence, "The Lord is my helper; I will not be afraid. What can mere mortals do to me?" (Hebrews 13:5-6)

When you feel abandoned, when the world presses hard, when your strength fails—remember that God has made you this promise. He will not leave. He will not forsake. This is the word that speaks better than all other words.

Finish the race. Persevere at church. Persevere in your home. Persevere in your faithfulness to Christ.

For the kingdom we are receiving cannot be shaken.
