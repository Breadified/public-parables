# Luke 1:1-38

**Title:** Christmas Begins Before Jesus

## Introduction: Understanding Christmas Rightly

Many people confuse Christmas with its cultural trappings—Santa, shopping, trees, reindeer, and festive celebrations. No wonder Jesus himself is sometimes dismissed as merely part of a mythical story. But the true Christmas narrative is far more profound: it is the fulfillment of God's ancient promise to bring blessing where there was only barrenness.

Over the next four weeks, we'll examine the Christmas story directly from Luke's Gospel. Luke begins his account by telling us exactly what he's doing: compiling an orderly narrative of the things that have been accomplished, drawing from eyewitnesses and ministers of the word, so that we might have certainty about what we've been taught (1:1-4). This is history grounded in reality, not a fairy tale.

Interestingly, Jesus's story doesn't begin with his birth, nor with his parents' love story. Instead, Luke begins with Herod, and with an old, childless couple named Zechariah and Elizabeth. This is where Christmas truly begins.

## Part One: John the Baptist—The Forerunner

### An Old Childless Woman (1:5-10)

Luke sets the story firmly in history: "In the days of Herod." This is not "once upon a time," but a specific historical moment. Herod was a puppet king appointed by the Roman emperor Augustus to rule over Judea and Jerusalem. He maintained his power through murder and assassination of any rival.

Zechariah was a priest—specifically, an Aaronic priest, a descendant of Aaron, Moses's brother. This was a privileged position. While common Levites managed the temple's practical operations, only Aaronic priests could enter the Holy Place itself to perform sacrifices and prayers. Zechariah belonged to one of twenty-four priestly divisions, and his turn for temple duty was like winning a lottery—a once-in-a-lifetime privilege.

Elizabeth also came from excellent lineage: she was a daughter of Aaron, from that same privileged priestly line. Together, they had the kind of pedigree that would have made their parents proud—something like coming from distinguished, well-established families. But more than their ancestry, verse 6 tells us they were "righteous before God, walking blamelessly in all the commandments and statutes of the Lord." They were deeply devout, living out their faith in every way.

We would expect such a couple to be blessed abundantly by God. Yet verse 7 contains a devastating word: "But they had no child, because Elizabeth was barren, and both were advanced in years." 

### The Reproach of Childlessness

In that culture, childlessness was not merely a personal disappointment—it was a curse. There was no one to carry on their lineage, no one to inherit their standing, no one to care for them in old age. The Old Testament echoes with this desperation: Abraham and Sarah, barren yet promised descendants "as the dust of the earth" and "as the stars of the sky" (Genesis 12:2; 15:5). Hannah, childless and despised by her husband's other wife, wept before God in the temple until he granted her Samuel (1 Samuel 1).

In Hebrew thinking, children weren't luxuries—they were assets, arrows in your quiver (Psalm 127:5). The more children you had, the wealthier and more blessed you were. Childlessness, by contrast, was considered a sign of God's judgment. Deuteronomy 28:18 lists it among the curses that follow disobedience: "Cursed shall be the fruit of your womb." Those without children were seen as living under God's displeasure.

This is a sharp contrast to our modern thinking, where children are often viewed as financial burdens. But in Scripture's worldview, children are blessings, and the refusal to have them—whether through abortion, materialistic calculation, or selfish comfort—represents a fundamental misunderstanding of what God values.

Because of this, Zechariah and Elizabeth would have endured constant whispers: "They must be hiding some sin. They look righteous on the outside, but God clearly judges them." The couple bore the reproach of their people, even though they were truly righteous. What would God do about this injustice?

### The Angel Gabriel Appears (1:8-13)

One day, Zechariah was on duty at the temple, chosen by lot to enter the Holy Place—a singular honor. There, at the altar of incense, an angel of the Lord suddenly appeared. Zechariah was terrified, troubled with fear.

But the angel's message was clear: "Do not be afraid, Zechariah, because your prayer has been heard." His prayers for a child had been answered. His wife Elizabeth would bear a son, and they were to name him John.

### The Task of John: A New Elijah (1:14-17)

This wasn't just good news for the couple. Many would rejoice at John's birth because he would be great before the Lord. In fact, John would become the last and greatest prophet in the Old Testament lineage—not because of his character alone, but because of his timing. While the Law and the Prophets had preached about a coming kingdom, John would actually *see* it (Luke 16:16).

The angel specified that John would be a Nazirite—a person separated from normal life for God's purposes, which is why he would drink no wine or strong drink. More significantly, he would be filled with the Holy Spirit from his mother's womb.

And here is John's commission, his task:

- He will turn the children of Israel to the Lord their God
- He will have the spirit and power of Elijah
- He will turn the hearts of fathers to their children
- He will turn the disobedient to the wisdom of the righteous
- He will make ready for the Lord a people prepared

This language echoes the final prophecy of the Old Testament, given by Malachi 400 years earlier:

> See, I will send you the prophet Elijah before that great and dreadful day of the Lord comes. He will turn the hearts of the fathers to their children, and the hearts of the children to their fathers; or else I will come and strike the land with a curse. (Malachi 4:5-6)

The Jews had waited four centuries for this promise. Just before God would set things right, He said He would send someone to call Israel to repentance, to reconcile broken families, and to prepare the way. John's task, in essence, is to clean house—like frantically tidying up before your parents come home from vacation. He is the forerunner, preparing a people for someone greater than himself.

### Zechariah's Doubt and Silence (1:18-25)

Yet Zechariah responds with doubt: "How shall I know this?" This is surprising for several reasons. First, he's standing in the Holy Place, just beyond the veil that separates him from God's presence. Of all places to encounter a divine being, this is it. Second, as a priest with access to the Law and Prophets, Zechariah should have known the stories of Abraham and Hannah—he should have known God's power. Third, these were his own prayers being answered. How could he ask for proof?

The angel's response is simple: "I am Gabriel, who stands in the presence of God. I was sent to bring you this good news." In other words, "Here I am. I'm standing right in front of you. What more do you need?"

Because of his unbelief, Zechariah is struck mute—unable to speak. Yet the angel's promise is fulfilled: Elizabeth conceives and hides herself for five months. When Zechariah emerges and finally can write down what has happened, God has already begun removing the reproach his wife suffered among the people.

## Part Two: Jesus—The True King

### A Young Childless Woman (1:26-27)

Some time later, the angel Gabriel is sent to another childless woman. But this one is young, unmarried, and a virgin. Her name is Mary, and she lives in Nazareth. She is betrothed to Joseph, who is descended from the house of David—the royal line of Israel's greatest king.

The parallel is striking: like Elizabeth, Mary is childless. But where Elizabeth's barrenness comes from age and infertility, Mary's comes from virginity. Yet she too is about to receive an impossible promise.

### Gabriel Appears to Mary (1:28-31)

Gabriel appears to Mary with the greeting, "Hail, favored one!" Mary is greatly troubled, trying to discern what kind of greeting this might be. Unlike Zechariah's immediate fear, Mary's reaction is more contemplative—she's trying to understand.

The angel reassures her: "Do not be afraid, Mary, for you have found favor with God. Behold, you will conceive in your womb and bear a son, and you shall call his name Jesus."

Jesus means "Savior" (from the Hebrew Joshua, the same name as the great military leader who brought Israel into the promised land). But this Jesus will be the ultimate Savior.

### The Task of Jesus: A New David (1:31-33)

The angel continues with Jesus's commission:

- He will be great
- He will be called the Son of the Most High
- God will give him the throne of his forefather David
- He will reign over the house of Jacob forever
- His kingdom will have no end

This echoes the covenant God made with David through the prophet Nathan (2 Samuel 7:12-16). God promised David that from his line would come one whose kingdom would be eternal, who would be disciplined but never abandoned, whose throne would be established forever.

Throughout Israel's history, this promise seemed to fail. Solomon's line produced wicked kings. The kingdom was conquered and the people exiled. Herod—who now rules—is not even from David's line and rules unjustly as a Roman puppet. But now, finally, the true king is coming: Jesus, the one who will reign not temporarily, but eternally.

### Mary's Question and the Angel's Explanation (1:34-35)

Mary asks, "How will this be, since I am a virgin?" Perhaps she's wondering if she should consummate her betrothal quickly. Or perhaps she's simply asking how this is physically possible.

The angel explains: "The Holy Spirit will come upon you, and the power of the Most High will overshadow you." This is not describing a physical relationship with the Holy Spirit, but rather that the Spirit himself will be the agent of this conception. With John, the Spirit filled him while he was in the womb. But with Jesus, the Spirit will cause the conception itself. He is greater than John. He is the Lord coming home.

### Mary's Faith (1:36-38)

The angel adds that Elizabeth, her relative, has also conceived in her old age—nothing is impossible with God. And Mary responds with remarkable faith:

> Behold, I am the servant of the Lord; let it be to me according to your word.

This young, possibly uneducated girl acknowledges God's will completely. Rather than doubt like Zechariah, she believes. She yields to God's purpose.

## Application: Christmas in Context

The Christmas story doesn't begin with Jesus's birth or even with Mary and Joseph. It begins with the fulfillment of Old Testament promises. It begins with John, the last prophet, who comes 400 years after Malachi's final word. It begins with the convergence of Daniel's prophecy (that the time of Israel's punishment is ending and redemption is coming), the promise of 2 Samuel 7 (that David's line will produce an eternal king), and Malachi's promise (that Elijah will return to prepare the way).

Christmas is not a vacuum. It is not a commercial holiday invented by modern culture. The angels are not mere special effects. The virgin birth is not a controversial plot device. These realities exist because they fulfill what God promised centuries before.

The message is this: God is keeping His word. The new Elijah, John, will come with power to turn hearts back to God, to call Israel to repentance, and to prepare the way for one greater than himself. The new David, Jesus, will come not as a temporary king, but as an eternal Savior. He will deal with sin and rebellion once and for all. He will not allow the curse to run loose—instead, He will choose to save His people.

We wait in eager anticipation for the rest of this story: the removal of reproach, not among people, but the removal of God's wrath from His people. That is the true story of Christmas.
