# 2 Samuel 7:1-29

**Title:** Where is God?

**Date:** 11 September 2023

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Part 1: David's Misguided Plan (verses 1-9)

After years of warfare and struggle, King David finally had rest. He had settled into his magnificent cedar palace in Jerusalem—a striking contrast to the years he spent as a nomad. Comfortable and prosperous, David felt the weight of an inconsistency: he was dwelling in a house of cedar (permanent, strong, heavy wood that lasts centuries), while the ark of God—God's dwelling place among His people—remained in a tent with curtains (temporary, fragile fabric easily destroyed).

David shared this observation with Nathan the prophet: "Here I am, living in a house of cedar, while the ark of God remains in a tent." His solution seemed logical and generous. He wanted to build God a permanent, proper dwelling place. Nathan initially agreed, encouraging David: "Whatever you have in mind, go ahead and do it, for the Lord is with you."

But that same night, God had a different word for Nathan.

### God's Response: A Rebuke to Misunderstanding

God's answer came as a question: "Are you the one to build me a house to dwell in?" This wasn't a gentle refusal wrapped in embarrassment. It was a correction—a rebuke to David's fundamental misunderstanding of God's nature.

God reminded David of His history with Israel: "I have not dwelt in a house from the day I brought the Israelites up out of Egypt to this day." Throughout Israel's entire history, God had never needed or wanted a permanent, fixed location. Instead, God had moved with His people, dwelling in a tent with handles—designed for movement, not settlement.

This reflects a crucial principle found in Deuteronomy: God explicitly prohibited the permanent localization of worship. Unlike the pagan gods of surrounding nations—gods who were immobilized in temples and tied to specific regions—the God of Israel is a God of movement and freedom. God cannot be domesticated or contained.

> "I have been moving from place to place with a tent as my dwelling. Wherever I have moved with all the Israelites, did I ever say to any of their rulers whom I commanded to shepherd my people Israel, 'Why have you not built me a house of cedar?'"

God is Spirit. As Jesus later taught, "The wind blows wherever it pleases. You hear its sound, but you cannot tell where it comes from or where it is going." God is not controlled by human structures or institutions. He is transcendent and free.

This also reflects the deeper reality: God's nature makes Him universally present. As Isaiah would later declare, "Heaven is my throne, and the earth is my footstool. Where is the house you will build for me? Where will my resting place be?"

### The Real Problem

What was David really trying to do? Perhaps unconsciously, he was attempting to domesticate God—to place Him next to his own palace as a kind of cosmic insurance. "If I house God here, God will be invested in my security." But our God is a missionary God, not a pet to be controlled or tamed.

We see this same impulse in our culture today. We try to make God relevant to our evolving standards. We tell God He must keep up with the times. We treat Him like an accessory—a source of inspiration when we need it, like wisdom from a fortune cookie. Or we reduce Him to a hobby, a little bit of companionship for when we're lonely, something we put away when inconvenient, like a dog we board when we go on holiday.

God is not a hobby or a pet. He is more like your boss—someone who cannot be domesticated but rather does the domesticating. Your friends see your God as your pet, or as your Boss? The sovereign ruler of heaven and earth? The one who uses the entire earth as His footstool while we clean the dust?

## Part 2: God Builds a House for David (verses 10-29)

Instead of David building a house for God, God announced He would build a house for David. This reversal carries profound significance.

> "The Lord himself will establish a house for you. When your days are over and you rest with your ancestors, I will raise up your offspring to succeed you, your own flesh and blood, and I will establish his kingdom. He is the one who will build a house for my Name, and I will establish the throne of his kingdom forever."

Notice the repeated refrain: **I will**. God takes all the initiative.

- "I will make your name great, like the names of the greatest men on earth."
- "I will provide a place for my people Israel and will plant them so that they can have a home of their own."
- "I will also give you rest from all your enemies."

God is in control—always has been, always will be. David is not the king; he is a pawn in God's hands. God has been orchestrating David's entire life: "I took you from the pasture, from tending the flock, and appointed you ruler over my people Israel. I have been with you wherever you have gone, and I have cut off all your enemies from before you."

The promise of a "house" for David is not about a building of cedar. It is about dynasty, lineage, and an heir. God would raise up an offspring—a descendant—to succeed David. This offspring would build the temple that David could not build (due to his hands being stained with blood from warfare). But more importantly, this promise pointed to something far greater.

### The Ultimate Fulfillment: Jesus

In the short term, Solomon fulfilled this by building the temple. But the ultimate object of this prophecy is Jesus Christ, the final offspring of David's line.

When the angel Gabriel came to Mary, he announced: "You will conceive and give birth to a son, and you are to call him Jesus. He will be great and will be called the Son of the Most High. The Lord God will give him the throne of his father David, and he will reign over Jacob's descendants forever; his kingdom will never end."

Jesus is the house God builds. Not a structure of cedar with a postcode, but a living, eternal kingdom. Jesus Himself claimed this truth: "Destroy this temple, and in three days I will raise it up." He was speaking of the temple of His body.

God's covenant with David finds its fulfillment in Christ: "I will be his father, and he will be my son." Jesus is the one through whom God's blessing flows eternally. While Jesus suffered the punishment meant for our sins—struck by the rod of men, flogged, beaten—His love was never taken away. His throne is established forever.

As Paul would later affirm in Acts: "God raised [Jesus] from the dead so that he will never be subject to decay. As God has said, 'I will give you the holy and sure blessings promised to David.'"

## Application: Understanding God's Nature

The central lesson is this: **God cannot be domesticated.** We live in a culture constantly attempting to reshape God to fit our preferences. We evolve biologically, sexually, and ethically, then demand that God evolve with us. We want Him to be relevant on our terms.

But God is Spirit—free, transcendent, and sovereign. He is not bound by our structures, our institutions, or our expectations. This should humble us and humble our pride.

Those who trust in Jesus participate in God's eternal blessings. Death is not the end for those in Christ. We too await the full rest that comes when Jesus returns—the completion of God's redemptive work. Until then, we live in the tension between the "already" of salvation secured in Christ and the "not yet" of His future return.

**The question for us:** Do you see God as your pet—something to be managed and controlled? Or as your Boss—the sovereign Lord before whom you bow? Your answer determines everything about how you live.
