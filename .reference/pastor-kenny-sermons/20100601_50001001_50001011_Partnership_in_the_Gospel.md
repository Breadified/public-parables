## The Nature of Partnership

Partnerships shape our world. We see them everywhere—in business (Ernst & Young), music (Gilbert and Sullivan, Alain Boublil and Claude-Michel Schönberg), sports (Michael Jordan and Scottie Pippen), and entertainment. What makes these partnerships so powerful? What defines true partnership? Over the coming weeks, we'll explore not just any partnership, but partnership in Christ—partnership rooted in the gospel.

The partnership of Michael Jordan and Scottie Pippen illustrates this beautifully. Playing for the Chicago Bulls in the 90s, they won six titles together. They moved with such synchronization that they could execute alley-oops without even looking at each other. They knew exactly where the other person would be. Their success came from years of training together, learning the same plays, the same offensive and defensive strategies. They had common ground.

## Paul's Thanksgiving (Philippians 1:3-8)

Paul begins his letter to the Philippians with gratitude. This is significant—after his opening greetings, Paul immediately expresses his joy and thanksgiving for them:

> I thank my God every time I remember you. In all my prayers for all of you, I always pray with joy because of your partnership in the gospel from the first day until now. (Philippians 1:3-5)

### Partnership in the Gospel

Paul is grateful for the Philippians' **partnership in the gospel**. They share the same good news of salvation—the gospel of Christ. This gospel, as Paul will later explain, centers on Christ's humiliation and exaltation:

> Who, being in very nature God, did not consider equality with God something to be grasped, but made himself nothing, taking the very nature of a servant, being made in human likeness. And being found in appearance as a man, he humbled himself and became obedient to death—even death on a cross! Therefore God exalted him to the highest place and gave him the name that is above every name. (Philippians 2:6-9)

Jesus did all this to take upon himself the penalty of sin that should fall on us. The gospel declares that we obtain Christ's righteousness by faith in him:

> And be found in him, not having a righteousness of my own that comes from the law, but that which is through faith in Christ—the righteousness that comes from God and is by faith. (Philippians 3:9)

This gospel has been known among the Philippians from the beginning. When Paul first visited Philippi (as recorded in Acts 16), he proclaimed this message of salvation. The city was a Roman colony in Macedonia, run by Roman law and Roman administrators. Into this pagan city, Paul entered and found his first convert—a Gentile woman named Lydia, a dealer in purple cloth who worshiped God. Her household was baptized, and she welcomed Paul and his companions into her home.

Later, another remarkable conversion occurred. Paul and Silas were imprisoned, yet at midnight they sang hymns to God. An earthquake shook the prison, and the Philippian jailer, thinking the prisoners had escaped, prepared to take his own life. But Paul called out, "We are still here!" The jailer brought them out and asked the most important question: "What must I do to be saved?" Paul and Silas replied, "Believe in the Lord Jesus, and you will be saved—you and your household." That night, the jailer and his entire family believed and were baptized.

**Application:** Do you believe this gospel? Where does your righteousness come from? Paul's righteousness comes from Christ, not from his own merit. Some may hesitate, saying, "I believe now, but I might not in the future, so how can I say I truly believe?" This overthinks the matter. Commit to Christ now, and that commitment will sustain you into the future. Our generation struggles with certainty because we want to keep our options open. But close those options. Commit. Like marriage—yes, many marriages fail in our time, but the solution is not to avoid marriage; it is to stay committed. The Philippians were precisely this kind of people: loyal, faithful through thick and thin, both to Paul and to God. This is why Paul rejoices in them with such joy.

### Partnership in God's Gift

Paul's second reason for thanksgiving is the Philippians' **material support**. Partnership in the gospel is not just faith in Christ together; it is also active cooperation in spreading the gospel. Paul states:

> It is right for me to feel this way about all of you, since I have you in my heart; for whether I am in chains or defending and confirming the gospel, all of you share in God's grace with me. (Philippians 1:7)

The word translated "share" carries the sense of being Paul's partners in grace—partners in God's gift. Paul traveled through Asia and Europe planting new churches and supporting existing ones. He needed resources. Then he found himself in a Roman prison, awaiting trial and possibly execution. Through it all, the Philippians stood with him. They provided physical help and material support. When Paul says, "I thank my God every time I remember you," part of that gratitude springs from their generosity.

But here is something wonderful: the "good work" God is carrying forward is not just God's internal work in their hearts. It is also the fruit of their generosity—the material support that enables Paul to continue proclaiming the gospel. That proclamation generates fruit, which generates more fruit. Even today, as we read Paul's letters, we are transformed by them. The original generosity of the Philippians continues to bear fruit, right up until Christ returns.

**Application:** This brings tremendous joy and comfort. The work is still bearing fruit among you! Whatever labor we undertake for the Lord—teaching Sunday school, sharing the Bible with friends, challenging and rebuking one another in love—has the potential to bear fruit far beyond what we can see. The money given to support ministry, if invested wisely and faithfully, can raise up new generations of Christian leaders who will pass on the gospel. It is a gift that keeps on giving. Your work in the Lord is not in vain.

## Paul's Prayer for the Philippians (Philippians 1:9-11)

Given such a people—united in faith, faithful in partnership—what does Paul pray for them? Not health. Not safety. Not wealth. Rather:

> This is my prayer: that your love may abound more and more in knowledge and depth of insight, so that you may be able to discern what is best and may be pure and blameless until the day of Christ, filled with the fruit of righteousness that comes through Jesus Christ—to the glory and praise of God. (Philippians 1:9-11)

### Two Essential Dimensions: Godliness and Knowledge

**Godliness:** Paul prays that their love may abound more and more. This love extends to one another, to Paul himself, and to their city. But to love rightly requires understanding.

**Knowledge:** Knowledge shows us how to love properly. Without it, our love becomes misdirected. A medicine is good, but unless applied in the right circumstances to the right person, it can cause harm. Knowledge, combined with depth of insight (wisdom and discernment), ensures that our love is applied wisely.

### The Purpose: Discernment and Purity

The goal of this growing love in knowledge and discernment is clear:

> So that you may be able to discern what is best, in order that you may be pure and blameless in view of the day of Christ.

They may distinguish the truly important issues in their lives together and act on those distinctions. Paul wants them fully prepared for Christ's return—pure and blameless, refined by love expressed with wisdom.

Young people often have high acuity but low perspective—like a driver with good eyesight but no experience reading the road. Life presents situations far beyond what we can anticipate. You will find yourselves in difficult circumstances—relationships, ethics, work decisions—where parents and pastors cannot be present. You must make godly decisions. Gain knowledge now. It is not for today; it is for the future.

**Application:** How do we gain knowledge? Through reading, through courses, through serious engagement with God's Word. This is not a dumb person's religion. Doing works does not get us to heaven; knowledge of the Son does. For university students, MYC offers a week of intensive Bible training. Come to borrow books. For all of you, engage with Scripture personally. If self-discipline is difficult, join a Bible study group, a campus group, a work group, a mothers' group. Sometimes we grow more easily together.

But knowledge alone is not enough. We must also cultivate godliness—love that flows in the right direction. This requires us to challenge and rebuke one another gently and truthfully. If someone is chronically late, not honoring others' time, we should challenge that politely. If language is coarse or conduct is slack, loving one another means speaking truth. But this cannot rest on leaders alone—it makes them look like the moral police. All of us must encourage and challenge one another. Otherwise, our church becomes merely a club with Christian sentiment and songs, a dead institution like so many others. We are aiming to be like the Philippians: a people whose love abounds more and more in knowledge and discernment.

This is certainly worth praying for. We pray for exams and health, but do we pray for wisdom? For knowledge of Scripture? For discernment?

## Closing Prayer

Father, I thank you for this fellowship of believers. I thank you for our partnership from the first day I met them until now. Thank you for the fruit of righteousness that comes through Jesus Christ's blood.

Because of that, I pray that our love may abound more and more in knowledge and depth of insight, so that we may be able to discern what is best, in order that we may be pure and blameless in view of the day of Christ. Amen.
