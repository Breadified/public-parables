**Title:** Model Ministry

## Introduction: Recognizing False Models

We live in a world of deceptive schemes. Pyramid schemes promise wealth with no skill or effort required—"Work from home and earn $5,000 a month!" The promise is simple: recruit others, and you'll get rich. But the system is designed to collapse. Only those at the top profit, while everyone below eventually finds no one left to recruit.

These schemes prey on the greedy, the desperate, and the unemployed. They're scams because they produce nothing of real value. The only way to make money is by recruiting new people who pay upfront costs. It's deception masquerading as opportunity.

I learned this the hard way once. An advertisement promised free carpet cleaning for one room. Being the bargain-hunter I am, I took them up on it—planning to offer my largest room. Three suited salesmen arrived. After cleaning one corner, they launched into a high-pressure sales pitch for a vacuum cleaner costing over $1,000. They claimed they wanted to "help" my family avoid dirt and asthma. It took an hour to convince them I wasn't interested. The "free" service was just bait for a sales trap.

As Christians, we also promote something—the good news of Jesus Christ. But how should we do it? What ministry model should we adopt? In a world suspicious of manipulation and ulterior motives, how do we share our faith authentically?

Paul, one of Jesus's first followers, provides the answer in his letter to the Thessalonians.

---

## Paul's Defense of His Methods

When Paul came to Thessalonica to proclaim Jesus, he faced opposition and suspicion. In 1 Thessalonians 2, Paul defends his methods—not to boast, but to show the Thessalonians (and us) how genuine ministry looks.

**1 Thessalonians 2:1-2**
> You know, brothers and sisters, that our visit to you was not without results. We had previously suffered and been treated outrageously in Philippi, as you know, but with the help of our God we dared to tell you his gospel in the face of strong opposition.

Paul's defense reveals three aspects of his ministry: he acted like an **innocent baby**, cared like a **loving mother**, and taught like a **faithful father**.

---

## 1. Like a Baby: Innocent and Beyond Reproach (2:3-7a)

**1 Thessalonians 2:3-7**
> For the appeal we make does not spring from error or impure motives, nor are we trying to trick you. On the contrary, we speak as those approved by God to be entrusted with the gospel. We are not trying to please people but God, who tests our hearts. You know we never used flattery, nor did we put on a mask to cover up greed—God is our witness... Instead, we were like young children [infants] among you.

The Greek word here is *nepioi* (infants), not gentle. This distinction matters. Paul is describing his innocence and lack of deception—the purity of an infant's motives.

Compare this to 1 Corinthians 14:20: "In regard to evil be infants, but in your thinking be adults." Paul was defending himself against accusations that his ministry involved hidden agendas.

### What Paul *Rejected*:
- **Error or impure motives** (v. 3): His intentions were sound
- **Deception** (v. 3): He wasn't trying to trick anyone
- **Greed** (v. 5): No hidden agenda to exploit them financially
- **People-pleasing** (v. 4-5): He spoke truth, not flattery
- **Masks** (v. 5): No pretense covering ulterior motives

Salesmen use flattery as a tactic: "You have such a beautiful face—buy this product and look even better!" But Paul did the opposite. He told the truth: "Turn from your idols and serve the living God."

Infants, like innocent babies, don't deceive. They have no ulterior motives. They don't flatter—they cry when something's wrong and laugh when they're happy. They tell you exactly what they think.

### Application: Innocent Ministry

In our ministry to others, we should be innocent and transparent. This doesn't mean we can't have multiple motives—that's normal. When a colleague asks how you are, you might want both friendship and a favor. When you invite someone to church, you genuinely want their company *and* want to share the gospel. Multiple motives aren't the problem.

The problem is *concealed* motives—deliberately hiding the truth for personal gain.

For Christians, this should be straightforward. We don't believe in hiding the truth anyway. We openly tell people we're Christians. We openly invite friends to church. We openly want to tell them about Jesus. This isn't deception; this is honest intention.

At church, we should be equally clear. We gather to read God's Word and learn from it. If someone asks, "Why do you talk about the Bible so much?" they should never be surprised. This is who we are.

We should be careful, though, not to sugarcoat biblical truths. I used to say people are "imperfect" rather than "sinners"—it sounds more positive and gets universal agreement. But not everyone believes they're a sinner. "Sinner" implies we've done something *wrong* against God. We must be honest about our true condition before God, even if it's uncomfortable.

If you're not yet a Christian, approach our invitations with confidence. We're not manipulating you when we ask to meet for coffee—we simply want to spend time with you. When we ask how you are, we're genuinely asking. Our invitation to church isn't control; it's genuine care for someone we haven't seen in a while.

## 2. Like a Mother: Caring and Sharing Life (2:7b-9)

**1 Thessalonians 2:7b-9**
> Just as a nursing mother cares for her children, so we cared for you. Because we loved you so much, we were delighted to share with you not only the gospel of God but our lives as well. Surely you remember, brothers and sisters, our toil and hardship; we worked night and day in order not to be a burden to anyone while we preached the gospel of God to you.

A mother cares. She shares her life completely with her children—from pregnancy through birth, nursing, and early childhood. The gift of a mother's presence and time creates the emotional foundation a child carries for life. This is what attachment theory reveals: a mother's consistent care shapes a child's ability to form healthy relationships.

Paul did this for the Thessalonians. He:
- **Cared deeply** for them
- **Loved them** sacrificially
- **Shared not just words, but his life**
- **Worked tirelessly** (night and day as a tentmaker) so they wouldn't bear his financial burden

This is how Paul proved his message was real. In 1 Thessalonians 1:5, he reminds them:

**1 Thessalonians 1:5**
> Our gospel came to you not simply with words but also with power, with the Holy Spirit and deep conviction. You know how we lived among you for your sake.

The gospel came with power through Paul's transformed life. When he shared his life openly, the Thessalonians could see whether he genuinely believed what he taught. There was no hypocrisy, no mask—just authentic faith demonstrated through his actions.

This is crucial: **authenticity gives power to the message.** If a car salesman recommends Toyota but drives a Honda, you wonder about his sincerity. Similarly, why should anyone believe the gospel if it has no visible impact on the person proclaiming it?

This is why overseas sermons through podcasts, while helpful, have limits—you can't see how the preacher actually lives. You can't assess their authenticity.

### Application: Caring and Sharing at Church

At our church, we aim to care for each other by sharing our lives. This happens in tangible ways:

- University students mentoring younger students during study days
- Members sharing practical advice (car maintenance, job opportunities)
- Building community through consistent presence

This requires **time**. We encourage everyone to come on Sundays—not as legalism, but as a practical way to share life together. When we're busy, having one set time each week to gather over God's Word creates continuity and deepens relationships.

The more time we invest, the more we understand each other's needs, and the better we can care for one another. It's a virtuous cycle: more time leads to more sharing, which leads to deeper care, which deepens sharing further.

Six years ago, our church tried to host a New Year's Eve party. Two people came. Now we're so large that when we meet at restaurants, we can barely fit—and people refuse to split into multiple tables because they don't want anyone left out. This is Paul's model in action: sharing not just the gospel, but our lives.

Today's church lunch is a perfect example. Our senior high school girls are caring for us by preparing food. Wednesday night gatherings give university students additional opportunities to connect.

If you're new to our church, we invite you into this family of God. We want to tell you more about Jesus. We want to share not just the gospel, but our lives with you as well.

## 3. Like a Father: Teaching and Discipline (2:9-12)

**1 Thessalonians 2:10-12**
> You are witnesses, and so is God, of how holy, righteous and blameless we were among you who believed. For you know that we dealt with each of you as a father deals with his own children, encouraging, comforting and urging you to live lives worthy of God, who calls you into his kingdom and glory.

In the ancient world, a father's primary role was education. He taught his children how to work, how to earn a living, how to solve problems, how to behave socially, and how to walk with God. A father's encouragement included both affirmation and discipline.

This is what Hebrews explains:

**Hebrews 12:7-9**
> Endure hardship as discipline; God is treating you as his children. For what children are not disciplined by their father? If you are not disciplined—and everyone undergoes discipline—then you are not legitimate, not true sons and daughters at all. Moreover, we have all had human fathers who disciplined us and we respected them for it. How much more should we submit to the Father of spirits and live!

Paul's fatherly role was to:
- **Encourage**: Affirm believers in their faith
- **Comfort**: Support them through hardship
- **Urge** (or testify): Call them to live worthy of God

This encouragement wasn't only positive affirmation. It included rebuke when necessary—like a spur, which can hurt briefly but redirects the horse. As Hebrews 10 urges, we should "spur one another on" as the day of Christ approaches.

Notice verse 10: Paul reminded them that *they* were witnesses to how "holy, righteous and blameless" he was among them. He didn't just teach with words; he taught by **example**. His changed life testified to the power of the gospel.

**Hebrews 12:10**
> They disciplined us for a little while as they thought best; but God disciplines us for our good, in order that we may share in his holiness.

This is the power of the changed life. As Paul's life was transformed by the gospel, he testified to the Thessalonians. They heard his words and saw his holy life. They imitated him. The Thessalonians themselves changed and became models for other churches—a chain of transformation that reaches us today.

### Application: Teaching Like a Father

We do this in our church as well, though in a more limited sense. We encourage one another, comfort each other through suffering, and gently rebuke when someone strays from God's path. Our lives should demonstrate the gospel's power.

Paul spoke with authority—approved by God to proclaim the gospel, with God himself testing his heart and serving as witness. This wasn't arrogance; it was accountability. He knew he answered to God for how he shepherded the Thessalonians.

---

## Conclusion: The Model We Must Follow

Paul's ministry model is the one we must emulate. It's a ministry that:
- Operates with **innocent motives** (like a baby)
- Shares **life and love** (like a mother)
- Teaches by **example and discipline** (like a father)

This is a ministry that shares not just the gospel, but our entire selves.

Come to church. Share in our lunch today. Invest time in God's Word and in one another. This is how the gospel moves from words to power—through the transformed lives of God's people.
