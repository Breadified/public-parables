# Matthew 6:25-34

**Title:** Worry and the Good Life

**Date:** November 11, 2018

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Opening: The Real Issue with Worry

Jesus gives us a command that may seem strange at first: "Do not worry." This isn't saying we should do nothing or make careless decisions. Rather, he's teaching us that worry itself—the anxious preoccupation with future circumstances—is not the path to the good life. The right approach is to do what's right, make wise decisions, and then trust God with the results.

It's worth asking ourselves: What worries us today?

For some, it's the basics—food and clothing. For others, it's deeper: school performance and career prospects, relationship status, house prices and financial security, threats to our environment and safety, our appearance and how others perceive us, finding a marriage partner. We live in an age of unprecedented anxiety. Research shows that roughly 1 in 14 young people aged 4-17 experience anxiety disorders.

But before we address these worries, we need to understand Jesus's perspective. He presents **four reasons why we should not worry**.

---

## Reason 1: Life Is More Important Than Its Provisions

> Therefore I tell you, do not worry about your life, what you will eat or drink; or about your body, what you will wear. Is not life more than food, and the body more than clothes? (Matthew 6:25)

The key insight here is understanding what Jesus means by "life." In Greek, the word is *psyche*—your soul, your very self. Yes, food is necessary for survival, but your life is not merely the sum of what you consume. You are not a machine where inputs automatically produce outputs. We are creatures of immeasurable worth.

This connects to a profound truth Jesus teaches elsewhere:

> For whoever wants to save their life will lose it, but whoever loses their life for me will find it. (Matthew 16:25)

Our value doesn't come from what we eat or wear. We are infinitely more precious than our material provisions.

Consider the birds:

> Look at the birds of the air; they do not sow or reap or store away in barns, and yet your heavenly Father feeds them. Are you not much more valuable than they? (Matthew 6:26)

If God provides for the birds—creatures that cannot plan, plant, or harvest—how much more will he care for us, his image-bearers? Martin Luther once reflected on this with beautiful simplicity: *"I prefer to be in the Lord's kitchen. He has made heaven and earth, and he himself is the cook and the host. Every day he feeds and nourishes innumerable birds out of his hand."*

God sustains the entire food cycle. The grain comes from the sun, worms from the soil, insects from creation itself. All of it ultimately flows from God's hand. And if he feeds the birds, the chickens, the ducks, surely he will feed us.

The principle is clear: **it is better to have your soul and be starving than to have food without your soul.** Life—true, meaningful, spiritual life—is worth infinitely more than food or clothing.

---

## Reason 2: Worry Is Not Productive

> Can any one of you by worrying add a single hour to your life? (Matthew 6:27)

Here Jesus presents a simple, devastating truth: **worry accomplishes nothing.** We cannot by mental effort or anxious concern change what we cannot control. Consider the examples:

- Once an exam is finished or an assignment submitted, worrying about the marks cannot change them. It cannot reverse the past.
- The housing market is the housing market. Rising interest rates, government policy, economic cycles—these are beyond your control. Worrying about them changes nothing.
- If you become ill, worrying cannot stop a virus or slow cancer cells. We have no mental power to alter the course of disease or aging.
- Even regarding global challenges like climate change, what can individual worry accomplish?

Most of our worries involve things we cannot change. And here's the remarkable thing: **most worries never come true**. Studies show this consistently. We create anxious narratives in our minds about futures that never materialize.

Even worse, worry is often self-fulfilling. And in many cases, chronic worry actually *reduces* our lifespan. Research shows that people frayed by distress—losing sleep over worries, struggling to concentrate—are about 20% more likely to die over a 10-year period compared to those without such symptoms.

So worry doesn't add to our life; it subtracts from it.

---

## Reason 3: Worry Reflects a Lack of Trust in God

> So do not worry, saying, "What shall we eat?" or "What shall we drink?" or "What shall we wear?" For the pagans run after all these things, and your heavenly Father knows that you need them. (Matthew 6:31-32)

Jesus draws a crucial distinction here. He's not saying food and clothing are unimportant—he explicitly affirms that "your heavenly Father knows that you need them." Rather, he's saying that **obsessing over these things is the way of those who don't know God**.

### The Pattern of a Godless Culture

In cultures that reject God's authority, we see something remarkable: a shift in what's considered morally binding. When sexual morality becomes unmoored from transcendent truth, society doesn't simply become amoral everywhere. Instead, that moral energy often redirects toward food.

We see this today. In many progressive circles, sexual ethics are treated as entirely personal—there are no binding rules. Yet simultaneously, food ethics become increasingly rigid. Monthly studies dictate what's acceptable: butter is evil, then butter is good again. New dietary systems proliferate—keto, paleo, vegan, intermittent fasting—each with strict rules and adherents who enforce them with zealous conviction.

We've created elaborate systems of control around food:

- Different diets with their strict protocols
- Vegetarianism and veganism as moral commitments
- Food fetishism and "food porn"
- Precise specifications even for coffee orders
- Government taxes on sugared drinks and meat
- Guilt over ethical sourcing (organic, cage-free, sustainable, fair-trade)

This is what happens when food becomes your moral absolute. As one observer put it, we've become "gluttons in bed but prudes at dinner."

Paul addresses this pattern:

> If you have died with Christ to the spiritual forces of the world, why do you submit to its regulations: "Do not handle, do not taste, do not touch!"? (Colossians 2:20-21)

### The Deeper Issue: Appearance Worship

When you strip away transcendent meaning, you're left with appearance. And appearance becomes everything. We obsess over how food looks ("food porn"), how our bodies look, what fashion communicates about us.

This drives an entire exploitative industry. Consumers see digitally altered images of impossibly thin models and think, "I want to look like that." But no one actually looks like that—not even the models. It's all Photoshop. Yet girls starve themselves, induce vomiting, and destroy their bodies chasing a fabricated ideal. Meanwhile, the media and fashion industry profit from this destruction.

The apostle Peter offers a corrective:

> Do not let your adorning be external—the braiding of hair and the putting on of gold jewelry, or the clothing you wear—but let your adorning be the hidden person of the heart with the imperishable beauty of a gentle and quiet spirit, which in God's sight is very precious. (1 Peter 3:3-4)

Similarly, Timothy instructs:

> Women should adorn themselves in respectable apparel, with modesty and self-control, not with braided hair and gold or pearls or costly attire, but with what is proper for women who profess godliness—with good works. (1 Timothy 2:9-10)

And Samuel's rebuke to Israel captures the principle:

> The Lord said to Samuel, "Do not look on his appearance or on the height of his stature... For the Lord sees not as man sees: man looks on the outward appearance, but the Lord looks on the heart." (1 Samuel 16:7)

### Why This Matters for Our Worry

When we worry obsessively about clothes and appearance, we're betraying something: we're living like spiritual orphans. We're behaving as though God doesn't exist, as though we have no father watching over us.

**Worry is fundamentally a statement of unbelief.** It says: "God, either you don't care (you're wicked) or you can't help (you're weak). Either way, I can't trust you."

The etymology is telling. The word "worry" comes from Old English *wyrgan*—to strangle, to seize by the throat and tear. Worry strangles us. And Jesus connects this to spiritual fruitlessness:

> The seed falling among the thorns refers to someone who hears the word, but the worries of this life and the deceitfulness of wealth choke the word, making it unfruitful. (Matthew 13:22)

Worry—along with wealth—chokes out the word of God in our hearts. It strangles spiritual fruit.

### Clothes and God's Provision

Yet Jesus also speaks specifically about clothing, and there's profound comfort here. He points us to God's care demonstrated throughout Scripture:

When God cast Adam and Eve out into the wilderness, he didn't do it coldly. First, he clothed them:

> The Lord God made garments of skin for Adam and his wife, and clothed them. (Genesis 3:21)

Even in judgment, God's love is expressed through provision. During the wilderness wanderings, God demonstrated his faithfulness in an extraordinary way:

> I have led you forty years in the wilderness; your clothes have not worn out on you, and your sandal has not worn out on your foot. (Deuteronomy 29:5)

Forty years. The same clothes. The same sandals. Miraculous provision.

Jesus then draws a comparison:

> If that is how God clothes the grass of the field, which is here today and tomorrow is thrown into the fire, will he not much more clothe you—you of little faith? (Matthew 6:30)

Notice that phrase: "you of little faith." There's gentleness here, but also a direct challenge. When we worry about clothing and appearance, Jesus is essentially asking: "Why do you trust God's provision for temporary flowers but not for yourselves?"

The parable of the rich man and Lazarus illustrates this principle powerfully. The rich man was clothed in purple and fine linen, yet in eternity, his clothing meant nothing. What matters is not what you wore, but who you were.

## Reason 4: Seek First the Kingdom of God

> But seek first his kingdom and his righteousness, and all these things will be given to you as well. Therefore do not worry about tomorrow, for tomorrow will worry about itself. Each day has enough trouble of its own. (Matthew 6:33-34)

Jesus pivots here from the negative (don't worry) to the positive: **there's something better to occupy your mind and heart.** The good life doesn't come from obsessing over provision; it comes from prioritizing God's kingdom.

### We Are Built for More

Humans differ from animals in a significant way. A giraffe on the savanna lives in what researchers call an "immediate-return environment." Its daily choices—what to eat, where to sleep, how to avoid predators—have immediate consequences. The giraffe lives in the present or near future only.

But humans live in what's called a "delayed-return environment." Our choices today bear fruit tomorrow, next year, in five generations. We can understand how present decisions ripple across decades. We can think strategically about legacy, about what we're building toward.

This capacity for foresight is a gift, but it can become a curse if we're thinking only about temporal things. The question Jesus poses is this: **On what timeline are you focused? What are you really building toward?**

Are you building toward financial security, physical attractiveness, professional status? These are the "pagans" things—the things people pursue when they have no ultimate purpose.

Or are you building toward the kingdom of God? That's where your treasure should be, because that's where it cannot be destroyed.

### The Right Worry

Here's something important: the word "worry" in the Bible isn't always negative. There's a kind of concern—a proper concern—that Jesus affirms.

> I have no one else like him, who will show genuine concern for your welfare. (Philippians 2:20)

> So that there should be no division in the body, but that its parts should have equal concern for each other. (1 Corinthians 12:25)

This is *merimna*—a concern for what matters. We should have concern for our brothers and sisters. Concern for those drifting away from faith. Concern for the things of God. But worry about food and clothes? That's the wrong direction.

### The Response: Prayer, Not Worry

The antidote to anxious worry is this:

> Do not be anxious about anything, but in every situation, by prayer and petition, with thanksgiving, present your requests to God. (Philippians 4:6)

Notice the sequence: instead of worry, bring your requests to God in prayer. Instead of anxiety, respond with thanksgiving. This is the posture of trust.

### The Ultimate Assurance

Jesus's logic builds to this: **If you seek first God's kingdom and righteousness, all these other things will be added to you.** Not through your anxious striving, but through God's faithful provision.

And here's why this is possible: because Jesus has already won the victory. When he came the first time, he took the punishment for sin upon the cross for all who would follow him rather than chase after the world. So when he returns as judge, those who belong to him need not fear condemnation.

Food and clothing will be secured in heaven—not through your worry, but through his promise.

---

## Living This Out

Jesus isn't dismissing real problems or real concerns. Your worries are real. Your struggles are real. You will face genuine hardship. The command to "not worry" isn't about denying reality; it's about changing where you place your trust and focus.

On Remembrance Day—100 years after the Armistice—we remember soldiers who faced unimaginable circumstances. Yet notice: they didn't worry about the cut of their uniform or the quality of their rations. They had a purpose. They stood against evil. They lived with resolve and courage because they were oriented toward something larger than themselves.

Meanwhile, we worry about lattes matching our shirt, about school marks, about whether we're fashionable enough. The contrast is sobering.

What if we reoriented our minds? What if, instead of asking "What shall I eat? What shall I wear?" we asked: "How can I seek God's kingdom? What does his righteousness look like in my life today?"

That's the invitation Jesus extends. Not a life of careless indifference, but a life of purposeful trust. Not the anxious scrambling of those who have no father, but the quiet confidence of those who know they're loved and provided for by a heavenly Father who knows exactly what they need.

This is the good life. Not freedom from problems, but freedom from the strangling grip of worry. And that freedom comes when we stop running after the things pagans chase, and start seeking first the kingdom of God.
