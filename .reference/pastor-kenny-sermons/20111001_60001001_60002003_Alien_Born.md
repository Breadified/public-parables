# 1 Peter 1:1-2:3

**Title:** Alien Born
**Date:** 16 October 2011
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Birth Rights

Being born in a certain land confers certain privileges. I was born in Malaysia in Ipoh, a former mining town, and I can claim connection to the birthplace of actress Michelle Yeoh. Different nations grant different birthright privileges—in the US, only those born there can become President; in Hong Kong, those born in the territory receive automatic citizenship regardless of their parents' status, a principle so powerful that mainland Chinese mothers came in such numbers to give birth there that births to non-citizen parents increased twenty-five times in five years.

The greatest privilege of birth is citizenship itself. Today's passage addresses two things: first, the privilege Christians possess through their spiritual birth, and second, where that birth originates. We'll explore the consequences of this birthright as well.

## 1. Christians Are Born Into Living Hope

> Praise be to the God and Father of our Lord Jesus Christ! In his great mercy he has given us new birth into a living hope through the resurrection of Jesus Christ from the dead. (1 Peter 1:3)

### An Inheritance (v. 4)

In Scripture, inheritance language refers primarily to **land**, not money or blessing. This traces back to God's promise to Abraham around 2000 BC in Genesis 12—a promise of great land, a great people, and great blessing. Throughout history, this inheritance remained central: Moses was commanded to lead the Israelites into their inheritance in the promised land; the Levites were told they had no inheritance, meaning they were landless (set apart to serve the temple, not given territory); David and Solomon achieved the greatest territorial expansion; yet Israel ultimately lost this inheritance in exile to Babylon.

But with Jesus, everything changes. Peter writes to people with no land and no privilege—strangers scattered throughout Pontus, Galatia, Cappadocia, Asia, and Bithynia. They are aliens, foreigners, displaced people (what we might call "FOBs"—fresh off the boat). In the mighty Roman Empire where Roman citizenship granted tremendous security and privilege, these Christians had no birthright. Instead, they faced sporadic suffering and persecution, with Rome itself described as "the new Babylon" where they were exiled.

In this context, Peter reassures them: you have an inheritance! You have a land! Though we are landless in this world, we have inherited a heavenly land.

### Protected (v. 4-5)

> Into an inheritance that can never perish, spoil or fade—kept in heaven for you, who through faith are shielded by God's power until the coming of the salvation that is ready to be revealed in the last time. (1 Peter 1:4-5)

This inheritance is safe from every threat. There is no inflation to erode its value, no thief to steal it, no UV rays to fade it, no obsolescence to make it worthless. It is free from the effects of global warming, sun, rain, earthquakes, and floods. Unlike the inheritance of Bill Gates's children, which their father has pledged to give away to charity, our inheritance from our Father will be protected and given to us.

But how is this possible? We are born of earth, foreigners to heaven. Yet through Jesus Christ, we are alien born—born again into a heavenly citizenship.

### A Hope (v. 9)

> For you are receiving the goal of your faith, the salvation of your souls. (1 Peter 1:9)

This inheritance is still to come—a heavenly land, not a patch of dirt in Sydney or the Middle East, but a city descending from heaven as revealed in Revelation 21. Abraham himself never possessed Canaan, yet he purchased a piece of that land as a burial site for himself and Sarah—his "lay-by" on the promise. Joseph, too, refused to remain in Egypt's grandest pyramids and insisted his bones be carried to the promised land, for the resurrection of the dead would take place where God's kingdom is established.

The fact that our inheritance lies in the future does not diminish our certainty. The promise is so sure that we can speak of our salvation as already possessed. Just as I call my house "my house" even though the bank technically holds the title deed—no one calls it "the bank's house"—we can speak with certainty of our spiritual future. If we can claim ownership through future assurance even in times of economic uncertainty and job insecurity, we can certainly be sure of our eternal inheritance.

Some of us doubt our salvation. In hard times, we may not emotionally feel God's joy. But notice what these early Christians experienced:

> In this you greatly rejoice, though now for a little while you may have had to suffer grief in all kinds of trials. These have come so that your faith—of greater worth than gold, which perishes even though refined by fire—may be proved genuine and may result in praise, glory and honor when Jesus Christ is revealed. (1 Peter 1:6-7)

It is entirely possible to be sad and happy simultaneously—sad today because of present trouble, joyful because of tomorrow's salvation.

Others of us are frustrated that we haven't seen or experienced God directly. Yet this is the normal Christian experience:

> Though you have not seen him, you love him; and even though you do not see him now, you believe in him and are filled with an inexpressible and glorious joy, for you are receiving the goal of your faith, the salvation of your souls. (1 Peter 1:8-9)

I have not seen Him, yet I love Him. I have not seen Him, yet I believe in Him. I have not seen Him, yet I am filled with inexpressible and glorious joy. This is not a lack of faith—it is the normal shape of Christian faith.

## 2. Christians Are Born From the Living Word

> For you have been born again, not of perishable seed, but of imperishable, through the living and enduring word of God. (1 Peter 1:23)

Our birth is altogether different from natural birth. We are aliens, born of the living word of God. The DNA of our birth is **imperishable, not perishable; living, not dead; enduring, not temporary; heavenly**.

This DNA matters profoundly because the circumstances of your birth determine your privilege. If you were born of perishable things—silver, gold, material wealth—what is handed down is an empty way of life. But if you are born of Christ:

> You were redeemed not with perishable things such as silver or gold, but with the precious blood of Christ, a lamb without blemish or defect. He was chosen before the creation of the world, but was revealed in these last times for your sake. Through him you believe in God, who raised him from the dead and glorified him, and so your faith and hope are in God. (1 Peter 1:18-21)

Under Roman law, nationality was determined by blood (the principle of *jus sanguinis*). In Italy today, you can be a citizen if your ancestors were Italian, even if you've never visited Italy. You inherit rights through blood. Similarly, you are born by the precious blood of Christ. You are born from an immortal source. You are born by the word of the gospel. Born to a new citizenship—heaven.

We each came to faith in different ways. Some experienced a spiritual awakening. Some grew up in Christian families. Some joined friends in Scripture class at school. Some were convinced by apologetic arguments. But regardless of how we entered the faith, the real way we are born is through the word of the gospel. We believe the great news: Jesus came to earth, lived a godly life, was undeservedly crucified to pay for our ungodly lives, and rose to new life, promising the same resurrection to all who believe.

Have you been born of the gospel?

### A Living Word in the Past (v. 10-12)

> Concerning this salvation, the prophets, who spoke of the grace that was to come to you, searched intently and with the greatest care, trying to find out the time and circumstances to which the Spirit of Christ in them was pointing when he predicted the sufferings of Christ and the glories that would follow. It was revealed to them that they were not serving themselves but you, when they spoke of the things that have now been told you by those who have preached the gospel to you by the Holy Spirit sent from heaven. Even angels long to look into these things. (1 Peter 1:10-12)

The prophets of old spoke with the Spirit of Christ pointing through their words. They didn't know the name of Jesus, yet what they spoke pointed to Him—His sufferings, our redemption through His blood on the cross, His resurrection. Their words were always relevant, always pointing forward to God's redemptive plan in history. Even the angels long to understand these things.

### A Living Word in the Present (v. 18-20)

The word is living in the present for us. We have been born again—a different birth altogether. When I watch the film *Alien*, I'm struck by how strange that birth is: an egg produces a larvae ("facehugger") that implants an alien baby in a human host, which then bursts through the chest as a "chestburster." We, too, have a very strange birth! We are aliens, born of the living word of God.

If you were born of perishable things, you inherited an empty way of life. But:

> You were redeemed not with perishable things such as silver or gold, but with the precious blood of Christ, a lamb without blemish or defect. He was chosen before the creation of the world, but was revealed in these last times for your sake. (1 Peter 1:18-20)

The destiny of Christ becomes our destiny. The word is **imperishable, living, enduring, heavenly**. Belonging to this word, we too become imperishable. Death no longer holds us. We are resurrected.

### A Living Word in the Future (v. 24-25)

> All men are like grass, and all their glory is like the flowers of the field; the grass withers and the flowers fall, but the word of the Lord stands forever. (1 Peter 1:24-25)

The word will continue living into the future. Do we believe the word of the Lord stands forever? This belief faces two attacks.

**Attack One: Pressure to move forward.** Culture urges us to abandon "old" interpretations. The pattern is familiar: first, claim ignorance ("the Bible is unclear, who knows what this says?"); second, appeal to non-biblical authorities (early church fathers, Calvin, modern research, contemporary culture); third, in the resulting confusion, claim to have "the answer" from these non-biblical sources and insist we abandon traditional readings for new ones.

Consider the gay marriage debate. The pattern repeats: proclaim ignorance ("ancient Bible laws are outdated"), cite authorities ("studies show 10% of people are gay, therefore it must be okay"; "I have a gay friend who is nice, so gay acts must be permissible"), then claim certainty and demand we restructure marriage and church leadership.

A few rules for reading Scripture:
- **Scripture is interpreted by Scripture, not by other authorities**
- **When in doubt, stick to the conservative reading**

**Attack Two: Filling the gaps.** While some deny what the Bible says, others add to what it hasn't said. The Bible doesn't tell us which subjects to study, which university course to take, whether to marry someone, or whether to buy an iPad. We're tempted to fill these gaps with extra words from God—visions, dreams, voices.

But we must be careful. How do we know these voices are truly God and not merely our own desires or peer pressure? I've heard of "treasure hunts" where small groups pray, receive visions about where to go and whom to find, then evangelistically approach strangers with "revelations." While evangelism matters, we must be cautious about fabricating divine direction.

The Bible is always relevant. If it doesn't address something, perhaps we're simply not meant to know, or meant to figure it out ourselves. Rather than seeking new words from God, we need better comprehension skills, more humble hearts, more repentant hearts willing to recognize when we manipulate truth to justify our sinfulness.

Don't ask for words of knowledge. Ask for wisdom to discern what's already there in the text. Ask for repentance to apply what Scripture says. Ask for strength to keep doing so even when it becomes hard. Find someone to read the Bible with—your leaders are looking for opportunities. The Bible is like our precious passport. We're not in heaven yet; we're in a foreign land. Let's keep it close—physically, mentally, spiritually.

Bring your Bible to Christian gatherings. Open it during the sermon. If you use a phone Bible, that's wonderful, though I encourage the paper version in church and Bible studies for better focus and note-taking. Bring a pen to write notes, or a notebook if you don't want to mark your Bible. Bring your Bible to school. Study it more regularly. Pray that you read it rightly, listening to what it says rather than making it say what you want to hear.

## 3. Living as Aliens (2:1-3)

So we are aliens by birth and aliens by destiny. How then should we live?

### Do Not Assimilate

> Therefore, prepare your minds for action; be self-controlled; set your hope fully on the grace to be given you when Jesus Christ is revealed. As obedient children, do not conform to the evil desires you had when you lived in ignorance. But just as he who called you is holy, so be holy in all you do; for it is written: "Be holy, because I am holy." (1 Peter 1:13-16)

Aliens do not belong to earth, so let us not act like the natives. Holy means "set apart." Do not assimilate to the natives' behavior. My Asian parents came to Australia and told us: don't be like the Australians, speak Cantonese, don't play cricket and rugby. You are different because you are born different.

This applies to marriage too. Don't marry someone who doesn't share your faith. That's not a statement about race but about spiritual incompatibility. Those born again of imperishable, heavenly DNA cannot simply unite with earthly behavior.

### Work Hard

> Since you call on a Father who judges each man's work impartially, live your lives as strangers here in reverent fear. (1 Peter 1:17)

Live as strangers now. This is not home. Work hard for the gospel. Send your spiritual resources homeward, toward the kingdom, not to earthly accumulation.

This is hardest for the young. Youth want to assimilate; peer pressure screams at you about what your friends think. My sister, just a year younger than me, assimilated beautifully—Australian friends, no accent. I remained strange—hung out with Asians, kept my accent. It's easier for the old to resist; parents aren't as concerned about peer pressure. Look at how your parents dress! But for the young, everything revolves around what friends think.

### Crave Pure Spiritual Milk

> Therefore, rid yourselves of all malice and all deceit, hypocrisy, envy, and slander of every kind. Like newborn babies, crave pure spiritual milk, so that by it you may grow up in your salvation, now that you have tasted that the Lord is good. (1 Peter 2:1-3)

Last week, the 7 billionth baby was born, and like all newborns, it cried for milk. We who are born again ought to do the same. This milk is God's word—His living, enduring, imperishable word. Soak it up. Through the written word, you will continue to taste that the Lord is good. There is nothing else that can quench the hunger you have.

---

**Where are you born?** Peter says we are alien born—not of this earth but of the eternal word, born of the blood of Christ. In his great mercy, God has given us new birth into a living hope through the resurrection of Jesus Christ from the dead.
