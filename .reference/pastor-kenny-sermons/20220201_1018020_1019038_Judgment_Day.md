# Genesis 18:20-19:38

**Title:** Judgment Day
**Date:** 27 February 2022
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## The Coming of Judgment (18:20-33)

Abraham has received three divine visitors who announce that judgment is coming to Sodom and Gomorrah. The Lord reveals His intention to destroy these cities, whose sin has become so grievous that their outcry has reached heaven.

> Then the Lord said, "The outcry against Sodom and Gomorrah is so great and their sin so grievous that I will go down and see if what they have done is as bad as the outcry that has reached me. If not, I will know." (Genesis 18:20-21)

Sodom's wickedness was not new. As we learned from Genesis 14, these cities had long been enslaved to evil, yet Lot had made the fateful decision to settle there. Though Abraham had once rescued Lot from captors, Sodom continued its downward spiral into depravity.

### Abraham's Intercession

After the two angels depart for Sodom, Abraham remains standing before God, burdened for the righteous who dwell within the city. His concern is profound: how can judgment fall indiscriminately on both the wicked and the innocent?

> "Will you sweep away the righteous with the wicked? If you are going to destroy Sodom, what about the innocent?" (Genesis 18:23-24)

Abraham then begins a remarkable negotiation with God, approaching with deep reverence and humility. He acknowledges his unworthiness—"though I am nothing but dust and ashes"—yet he persists in his intercession.

The haggling that follows reflects an important truth about prayer: Abraham reduces the threshold from fifty righteous people to forty-five, then to forty, thirty, twenty, and finally ten. With each reduction, God grants the request. Abraham's approach mirrors Eastern bargaining customs, where one must respectfully explore what the vendor is truly willing to accept, leaving room for both parties to maintain dignity.

Abraham stops at ten. Why ten? Perhaps he knows something about Lot's family size.

### Does Prayer Change God's Mind?

This intercession raises a profound theological question: If God is sovereign and knows all things, how can human prayer make any difference?

The answer lies in understanding the relationship between divine sovereignty and human responsibility. God is indeed sovereign—He knows all, determines all, and controls all outcomes. Yet we cannot presume to know His specific plans simply by asserting His sovereignty.

> The secret things belong to the Lord our God, but the things revealed belong to us and to our children forever, that we may follow all the words of this law. (Deuteronomy 29:29)

Just as you cannot know what price a vendor will ultimately accept, we cannot know in advance how God's sovereignty will work itself out in history. This is why prayer matters. Your prayer is God's chosen means of accomplishing His purposes in the world.

We must therefore pray for our loved ones, our parents, our children, and strangers. We must intercede for those caught in conflict—pray that Ukrainians and Russian soldiers would come to know Jesus, that judgment might be delayed through repentance and salvation.

## Judgment Arrives (19:1-25)

The scene shifts to Sodom's gates, where Lot sits as one of the city's elders—a position of respect and responsibility. The city gate was where justice was administered, disputes resolved, and wisdom dispensed. Lot's presence there suggests he has become a man of influence, perhaps using his reputation as Abraham's nephew and his wealth to call the city toward righteousness.

> The two angels arrived at Sodom in the evening, and Lot was sitting in the gateway of the city. When he saw them, he got up to meet them and bowed down with his face to the ground. (Genesis 19:1)

Lot recognizes the significance of these visitors and offers them hospitality—a critical act of protection in a world without public safety or accommodations. In ancient culture, refusing hospitality meant condemning strangers to danger or death. Though the angels initially decline, Lot insists so strongly that they accept his invitation.

> Do not forget to show hospitality to strangers, for by so doing some people have shown hospitality to angels without knowing it. (Hebrews 13:2)

But hospitality alone does not explain Lot's urgency. His insistence conceals a darker necessity.

### The Darkness Emerges

> Before they had gone to bed, all the men from every part of the city of Sodom—both young and old—surrounded the house. They called to Lot, "Where are the men who came to you tonight? Bring them out to us so that we can have sex with them." (Genesis 19:4-5)

The corruption is complete. Not just some men, but all the men—young and old, from every corner of the city—have abandoned all restraint. The sexual perversion that will forever define Sodom in history has become the city's defining appetite.

Lot steps outside to confront the mob, attempting to reason with them as an elder would, calling them away from this wickedness. Yet in a desperate act of protection—one born from the ancient duty of a host to defend his guests—he makes an offer that shocks modern sensibilities:

> Look, I have two daughters who have never slept with a man. Let me bring them out to you, and you can do what you like with them. But don't do anything to these men, for they have come under the protection of my roof. (Genesis 19:8)

We must understand this not as cruelty but as duty. In that culture, when a host accepted guests under his roof, he became absolutely responsible for their safety. Lot faced an impossible choice—the kind a bodyguard for a president might face, or a judge whose daughter is kidnapped to coerce him into freeing a criminal. He was bound by an obligation that superseded his personal wishes. In his mind, the protection of his guests was a sacred trust.

Yet the mob refuses even this. They reject his daughters entirely, turning their hatred upon Lot himself:

> "Get out of our way," they replied. "We'll treat you worse than them." (Genesis 19:9)

Lot, who had tried to be a voice for righteousness in this corrupt city, is now being cancelled. His attempts to call the city to justice, his refusal to condone their wickedness, his very presence as a reminder of accountability—all of it makes him despised. He is about to be overpowered when divine intervention comes:

> But the men inside reached out and pulled Lot back into the house and shut the door. Then they struck the men who were at the door of the house, young and old, with blindness so that they could not find the door. (Genesis 19:10-11)

The angels then reveal their true purpose:

> We are going to destroy this place. The outcry to the Lord against its people is so great that he has sent us to destroy it. (Genesis 19:13)

There is no more time for negotiation. The threshold of ten righteous people has not been met. The corruption is total and absolute.

### The Urgency of Escape

The angels urge Lot to gather his family and flee immediately:

> Do you have anyone else here—sons-in-law, sons or daughters, or anyone else in the city who belongs to you? Get them out of here, because we are about to destroy this place. (Genesis 19:12-13)

Lot goes to his sons-in-law—those pledged to marry his other daughters—and warns them: "Hurry and get out of this place, because the Lord is about to destroy the city!" But they think he is joking.

At dawn, the angels press the urgency further:

> Hurry! Take your wife and your two daughters who are here, or you will be swept away when the city is punished. (Genesis 19:15)

The repeated commands create mounting pressure: "Hurry," "Get them out," "Flee," "Don't look back," "Don't stop anywhere," or "you will be swept away." This is the language of imminent catastrophe—like the alarms sounding in Kiev as troops advance, tanks crossing bridges, explosions drawing near.

> When he hesitated, the men grasped his hand and the hands of his wife and of his two daughters and led them safely out of the city, for the Lord was merciful to them. (Genesis 19:16)

Lot cannot move quickly enough on his own. The angels must physically compel him and his family toward safety. Even then, he lingers:

> Flee for your lives! Don't look back, and don't stop anywhere in the plain! Flee to the mountains or you will be swept away! (Genesis 19:17)

But Lot hesitates still, pleading for a compromise—permission to flee to a small nearby town rather than the mountains. The angel agrees, yet warning: "I cannot do anything until you reach it."

### The Judgment Falls

> Then the Lord rained down burning sulfur on Sodom and Gomorrah—from the Lord out of the heavens. Thus he overthrew those cities and the entire plain, destroying all those living in the cities—and also the vegetation in the land. (Genesis 19:24-25)

But one detail marks the cost of hesitation:

> But Lot's wife looked back, and she became a pillar of salt. (Genesis 19:26)

Perhaps she longed for Sodom. Perhaps she thought of her other daughters who did not escape. Perhaps curiosity overtook obedience. Regardless, the instruction was clear: do not look back. There is nothing there worth seeing.

Abraham, standing where he had interceded, looks down from a distance:

> Abraham got up early the next morning and looked down toward Sodom and Gomorrah, toward all the land of the plain, and he saw dense smoke rising from the land, like smoke from a furnace. (Genesis 19:27-28)

## The Mercy Within Judgment (19:26-29)

Yet even in this narrative of destruction, there is a strange intertwining of judgment and remembrance:

> So when God destroyed the cities of the plain, he remembered Abraham, and he brought Lot out of the catastrophe that overthrew the cities where Lot had lived. (Genesis 19:29)

Destruction and remembrance go hand in hand. God's judgment upon the wicked is inseparable from His mercy toward those who belong to Him. Lot's deliverance is secured by Abraham's intercession and Lot's own righteousness, even in a corrupt city.

> If he condemned the cities of Sodom and Gomorrah to destruction, reducing them to ashes as an example of what is coming on the ungodly; and if he rescued Lot, a righteous man distressed by the depraved conduct of the lawless (for that righteous man, living among them day after day, was tormented in his righteous soul by the lawless deeds he saw and heard); then the Lord knows how to rescue the godly from trials. (2 Peter 2:6-9)

### For Us: Don't Look Back

As Christians, we have left Sodom. We have left sin. Christ has died for us, and judgment has been completed and satisfied in Him. God has declared us righteous—not because of our works, but because of Jesus' blood.

The judgment that was due to us fell upon Jesus instead. We are free.

Why, then, would we ever look back toward our former lives with longing? Those were days of terror and wickedness, of anxiety and self-centeredness, of vices that enslaved us. That is precisely why we followed Christ—why we fled the city of Sodom with the angel.

No matter how difficult the future becomes, we must never be tempted to return. There is nothing there for us. The city is destroyed. The past is ashes. Our only hope lies forward, in the mountains, in the presence of God.

## The Aftermath: A Dark Legacy (19:30-38)

Lot settles in the mountains with his two daughters, still afraid to remain even in the small city of Zoar. Living in a cave, removed from civilization, the family's isolation becomes the backdrop for a tragic moral failure.

The older daughter speaks to her younger sister: "Our father is old, and there is no man around here to give us children—as is the custom all over the earth. Let's get our father to drink wine and then sleep with him and preserve our family line through our father."

What follows is incest—a grave violation of natural law and God's design. On successive nights, both daughters lie with their father while he remains unaware due to intoxication.

> So both of Lot's daughters became pregnant by their father. The older daughter had a son, and she named him Moab; he is the father of the Moabites of today. The younger daughter also had a son, and she named him Ben-Ammi; he is the father of the Ammonites of today. (Genesis 19:36-38)

The righteous man who stood against Sodom's wickedness, who tried to call his city to justice, who was rescued by angels from judgment—this same man becomes perpetrator of sexual sin. His actions produce nations that will later oppose Israel.

The narrative offers no moral justification, no theological explanation. It simply records the fall of a man whose faith, tested by fire and terror, crumbles in the aftermath of survival. Even the righteous are capable of grave moral failure when they lose their moorings in God's law and rely instead on their own reasoning about necessity.

This stands as a sobering reminder: judgment may come, we may be delivered, but deliverance itself is not the completion of our spiritual journey. We must maintain vigilance, cling to God's Word, and never presume that past victories guarantee future faithfulness.
