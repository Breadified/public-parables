## The Challenge of Forgiveness

The September 11 attacks left deep scars that persist across generations. Twenty years after that terrible day, families of the victims still struggle with forgiveness. When asked if they could forgive the perpetrators, most responded with honest pain: "I'm not that good," one grieving mother said. Another, whose daughter was engaged to be married when she died, declared: "There is no way I could ever forgive them."

These are understandable responses to unbearable loss. Yet they frame the central question Jesus addresses in Luke 17:3–4: How do we forgive in a way that reflects maturity and spiritual growth?

---

## The Biblical Process of Reconciliation

> "If your brother or sister sins against you, rebuke them; and if they repent, forgive them." (Luke 17:3)

Jesus outlines a clear, three-step process:

1. **Rebuke** — Address the perceived wrong directly and honestly
2. **Repent** — The offender acknowledges the harm and seeks to change
3. **Forgive** — You release the debt and seek restoration

This is the mature way. Rather than storing up resentment or venting to others, we talk directly. We don't assume we know what another person feels or needs—we ask them. We don't expect them to read our minds either; we tell them what we need and how we want to be treated.

### Forgiveness is Unlimited

> "Even if they sin against you seven times in a day and seven times come back to you saying 'I repent,' you must forgive them." (Luke 17:4)

The number "seven" in Scripture represents completeness and wholeness. This is not a voucher system—like a gym membership with a limited number of visits before you run out. Rather, "seven times" means "as much as it takes." There is no quota on genuine forgiveness.

In Matthew 18:21–22, when Peter asks if he should forgive seven times, Jesus responds: "Not seven times, but seventy-seven times"—emphasizing unlimited forgiveness when the repentance is sincere.

## The Reality of Community Life

We live in community, and community life is messy. We step on each other's ideological toes. We say wrong things. We break promises. We gossip, project suspicion, and sometimes we steal, hoard, and abandon one another. When people interact even more closely—within families—we may swear, hit, lie, and cause real harm.

Yet if the repentance is genuine and sincere, there is no limit, is there?

### What If They Don't Repent?

When someone refuses to acknowledge wrongdoing, we escalate carefully, following the Matthew 18 principles:

> "If your brother or sister sins, go and point out their fault, just between the two of you. If they listen to you, you have won them over. But if they will not listen, take one or two others along, so that 'every matter may be established by the testimony of two or three witnesses.' If they still refuse to listen, tell it to the church; and if they refuse to listen even to the church, treat them as you would a pagan or a tax collector." (Matthew 18:15–17)

This escalation allows the issue to pass to other authorities and voices. Ultimately, we cannot control another person's repentance. There's genuine frustration when people refuse to change, but **adulting requires recognizing that you can only control yourself.**

## Differentiation: The Mark of Maturity

As psychologist Harriet Lerner writes in *Growing Yourself Up*: "The only person we can change and control is our own self."

Mature adulthood involves **differentiation**—the ability to distinguish your thoughts from your feelings. An adult has worked-out principles and doesn't need others to validate what they should feel. They have a "solid self."

Undifferentiated people, by contrast, cannot separate feelings from thoughts. When asked to think logically, they're flooded with emotions and struggle to respond rationally. They haven't developed their own identity; instead, they mimic views they've heard rather than forming their own convictions.

True emotional maturity isn't about being unfeeling. It's about expressing appropriate emotions at the right time—grief at death, joy at celebration.

**Sympathy** means you feel what another person feels. **Empathy** means you understand what they're going through because you've walked a similar path.

### Reacting vs. Responding

Notice how different arguments become when we differentiate. In conflict, undifferentiated people *react*—they lose emotional control and lash out. Mature adults *respond*—they stay present and thoughtful even under pressure.

This brings to mind Ephesians 4:13–15:

> "Until we all reach unity in the faith and in the knowledge of the Son of God and become mature, attaining to the whole measure of the fullness of Christ. Then we will no longer be infants, tossed back and forth by the waves, and blown here and there by every wind of teaching and by the cunning and craftiness of people in their deceitful scheming. Instead, speaking the truth in love, we will grow to become in every respect the mature body of him who is the head, that is, Christ."

No one is perfect. We're all on a journey to keep our emotions and intellect in sync, to maintain our individuality while staying present in our relationships.

The good news? When you model mature responses, you generate mature responses from others. Your differentiated behavior breaks multi-generational patterns. As John Lee writes in *Growing Yourself Back Up*: "When we act like adults, we tell others how we feel and ask them what they feel. We do not make statements about what they should feel, do or say."

## What Adult Forgiveness Looks Like

Adult forgiveness is not childish dismissal—not "whatever" or "I forgot." True forgiveness:

- **Counts the cost** — You understand what was taken from you
- **Absorbs the hurt** — You don't transfer the pain to others
- **Releases the debt** — You no longer hold it against them
- **Values the relationship** — You seek genuine reconciliation
- **Lets go of pride and bitterness** — You choose restoration over retaliation

This kind of forgiveness becomes possible when we've experienced it ourselves.

## The Parable of the Rich Man and Lazarus

> "There was a rich man who was dressed in purple and fine linen and lived in luxury every day. At his gate was laid a beggar named Lazarus, covered with sores and longing to eat what fell from the rich man's table." (Luke 16:19–21)

The "gate" in ancient context was more than an entry point. It was where people met—where enemies encountered, merchants gathered, public judgment was held. It was a place of authority and visibility.

What was the rich man doing at his gate? The prophet Amos describes such scenes:

> "You levy a straw tax on the poor and impose a tax on their grain. There are those who oppress the innocent and take bribes and deprive the poor of justice in the courts." (Amos 5:11–12)

The rich man was hoarding wealth through unjust systems. His stinginess extended not just to money, but to forgiveness itself—a refusal to acknowledge the humanity of those beneath him.

### The Reversal of Fortune

> "The time came when the beggar died and the angels carried him to Abraham's side. The rich man also died and was buried. In Hades, where he was in torment, he looked up and saw Abraham far away, with Lazarus by his side. He called out: 'Father Abraham, have pity on me and send Lazarus to dip the tip of his finger in water and cool my tongue, because I am in agony in this fire.'" (Luke 16:22–24)

This echoes the parable of the unforgiving servant in Matthew 18:23–35. A king forgave his servant an enormous debt—ten thousand bags of gold. But that servant, newly released from his impossible burden, turned around and choked a fellow servant who owed him only a hundred silver coins.

> "The master called the servant in. 'You wicked servant,' he said, 'I canceled all that debt of yours because you begged me to. Shouldn't you have had mercy on your fellow servant just as I had on you?' In anger his master handed him over to the jailers to be tortured." (Matthew 18:32–34)

The point is stark: We receive forgiveness we don't deserve. Christ forgave us all our selfish thoughts, words, and actions through His death. He washed us cleaner than snow. He removed a debt we could never repay.

**If God canceled your debts, how can you not cancel others'?**

## The Warning About Stumbling Blocks

> "Jesus said to his disciples: 'Things that cause people to stumble are bound to come, but woe to anyone through whom they come. It would be better for them to be thrown into the sea with a millstone tied around their neck than to cause one of these little ones to stumble.'" (Luke 17:1–2)

"Little ones" refers not just to children, but to new believers, immature Christians, the weak and vulnerable—anyone in a fragile spiritual place.

To "cause someone to stumble" means to trip them up so they fall away from faith. When a mature believer treats a vulnerable person with contempt or indifference, the damage runs deep. That person thinks, "I'm not good enough to follow Jesus. The best I can do isn't good enough." And they stop.

A millstone was a massive grinding stone, weighing thousands of pounds. Jesus uses this horrific image to emphasize God's fierce protection of the vulnerable. If you're stronger, more mature, or more gifted, don't use that position to look down on others. Instead:

- Care for the weak and fragile
- Use your strength to help others grow
- Don't despise those less experienced
- Remember that God values them deeply

## When Justice Seems Distant

The rich man asks Abraham to send Lazarus back to warn his brothers about the consequences of their greed. Abraham's response is sobering:

> "'They have Moses and the Prophets; let them listen to them.' 'No, father Abraham,' he said, 'but if someone from the dead goes to them, they will repent.' He said to him, 'If they do not listen to Moses and the Prophets, they will not be convinced even if someone rises from the dead.'" (Luke 16:29–31)

This is the apostles' response to Jesus:

> "The apostles said to the Lord, 'Increase our faith!' He replied, 'If you have faith as small as a mustard seed, you can say to this mulberry tree, "Be uprooted and planted in the sea," and it will obey you.'" (Luke 17:5–6)

The lesson: Justice is not always administered on earth. Sometimes the guilty prosper while the innocent suffer. This is why faith—not emotion—must guide us. We trust that God sees and will ultimately judge. Our task is not to execute vengeance, but to mature in our response to injustice.

Adult faith is:
- Not personal
- Not emotional
- Grounded in trust that God will act

## A Prayer

Dear Lord,

We are sorry for the times our behavior and actions do not align with Your Word. We are sorry for how we relate poorly to others, especially within our families.

Please forgive us and change us. Fill us with Your Holy Spirit and help us become more like Your Son, Jesus.

Give us wisdom and patience as we reflect on our place in our family systems and work on changing ourselves. Help us find the strength to choose maturity instead of insecurity, and may it have positive ripple effects for generations, for Your glory's sake.

Amen.
