# Hebrews 12:18-29

**Title:** Persevering at Church  
**Date:** September 9, 2018  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Two Mountains: A Study in Faith

This week, a volcano erupted somewhere in the world. It seems there are about 15 active volcanoes at any given time. Today, we're coming to another fiery mountain—not a physical one, but a spiritual one that speaks to how we persevere in faith.

We've been reading through the latter half of Hebrews, which emphasizes keeping our faith in God and persevering despite hardship. One thing many of us think would help us is the ability to experience God physically. But as we come to verse 18, we discover something surprising about God's nature and how we relate to Him.

### The First Mountain: Tangible But Unapproachable

> You have not come to a mountain that can be touched and that is burning with fire; to darkness, gloom and storm; to a trumpet blast or to such a voice speaking words that those who heard it begged that no further word be spoken to them, because they could not bear what was commanded: "If even an animal touches the mountain, it must be stoned to death." (Hebrews 12:18-20)

This passage refers to Mount Sinai, about 1,500 years before Jesus. When Israel escaped slavery in Egypt, they traveled to Mount Sinai where God would speak to them and give the Law. The description is vivid: thunder, lightning, fire, smoke—perhaps even a volcano. Before God spoke, He told Moses to tell the people:

- Wash yourselves and consecrate yourself
- Put limits around the mountain—do not approach or touch it
- Anyone who touches the mountain will be put to death
- Only when the trumpet sounds may you approach

The reason is profound: **God and sin do not mix.**

Sin repels God in the same way that contamination repels us. Imagine finding a cockroach in your food—you would be repulsed and destroy it completely. That's how God responds to sin. It doesn't matter how small the sin is; it's still sin. Zero tolerance. As a result, while God was tangible at Mount Sinai, He was utterly unapproachable.

> The sight was so terrifying that Moses said, "I am trembling with fear." (Hebrews 12:21)

Here lies a critical problem: **We cannot experience God as He truly is and survive it.** When Moses encountered God directly, he could barely endure it—and Moses was God's friend who saw Him face to face. The earthly experience of encountering the real God is not what we imagine. It would be facing raw, consuming holiness. It would be terrifying.

### The Problem with Seeking Experiences

Many churches today claim they can bring you into God's presence through music, prayer, or special experiences. But the truth is stark: if you really experienced God as He is, you would not survive it. We cannot handle unmediated encounter with the living God.

We live in a post-modern, experiential age. We no longer collect material possessions—we collect experiences. We want Instagram moments, bucket-list trips, and memorable feelings. Experience and feeling have become our new idol. We're no longer materialists; we've become hedonists. We collect selfies instead of stuff, but it's the same emptiness with a different face.

Yet experiences fade. The trip of a lifetime becomes ordinary after a few times. The transcendent feeling of conversion that once seemed eternal quickly becomes a memory. This is the danger: **equating the absence of feeling with the absence of God.**

Many of us remember a time when faith felt real and exciting—new friends, new love, spiritual highs magnified by youth. But that transcendent experience won't return to the same intensity. And here's the critical truth: **you cannot base your faith on feelings.**

> Feelings are just feelings—nothing else. Facts don't care about your feelings. The fact that God exists and is terrifying does not depend on whether you feel that truth.

When you entered the workforce after university, the feeling changed. You had less time, more stress, fewer spiritual highs. But is God further away? No. He is exactly where He has always been. Your feelings shifted, not His presence.

### The Second Mountain: Intangible But Approachable

But you, dear reader, have not come to that mountain. Instead:

> You have come to Mount Zion, to the city of the living God, the heavenly Jerusalem. You have come to thousands upon thousands of angels in joyful assembly, to the church of the firstborn, whose names are written in heaven. You have come to God, the Judge of all, to the spirits of the righteous made perfect, to Jesus the mediator of a new covenant, and to the sprinkled blood that speaks a better word than the blood of Abel. (Hebrews 12:22-24)

This is a completely different mountain—intangible, heavenly, future. This is not an earthly church building full of imperfect people (though it includes us). This is the ultimate church of the firstborn—the church of all God's elect, forever.

The earthly church is pragmatic. We try to help each other, experience some joy, some transcendence, some emotion. But **the earthly church is not perfect, and we must not base our faith solely on it.** We come on Sunday, stay for lunch, go home. We see dimly. We experience dimly. The real church—the heavenly one—is eternal and unshakeable.

Yet here's the miracle: **this heavenly mountain is approachable.**

You have come. You have access. You can walk right up to God. There's no rope, no boundary, no "do not trespass" sign. No one will stop you. Why? Because of the blood of Jesus that speaks a better word than the blood of Abel.

Abel was the first death recorded in Scripture. Jesus is the final death that will end all deaths—the death that covers our sin. God is satisfied with His sacrifice. When God looks at you, He doesn't see you; He sees Christ. You have a free pass. You are cloaked in His righteousness.

### The Weight of Direct Access

This creates an important implication:

> See to it that you do not refuse him who speaks. If they did not escape when they refused him who warned them on earth, how much less will we, if we turn away from him who warns us from heaven? (Hebrews 12:25)

In the old covenant, Moses was the mediator. If people didn't understand God's instructions, they could claim Moses misinterpreted them. But now, we have direct access. We hear Him directly. We can bring our concerns, our questions, our prayers straight to His throne.

**Which means the onus is on us to listen.** We cannot blame anyone else. We have no excuse.

The Transfiguration illustrates this:

> After six days Jesus took with him Peter, James and John...and led them up a high mountain by themselves. There he was transfigured before them...Just then there appeared before them Moses and Elijah, talking with Jesus. Peter said to Jesus, "Lord, it is good for us to be here...While he was still speaking, a bright cloud covered them, and a voice from the cloud said, "This is my Son, whom I love; with him I am well pleased. Listen to him!" (Matthew 17:1-5)

Peter wanted to honor Moses and Elijah equally with Jesus. But God's voice cut through: *Listen to Him.* Not to the Law, not to the Prophets, but to Jesus. He is the final word.

## Living Between the Mountains: Perseverance and Discipline

Now we see dimly; in the future we will see clearly. Now we experience dully; in the future we will experience the full reality. We live in the tension between the promise and its fulfillment. This requires perseverance.

What does perseverance look like? Look at Hebrews 13:

- **Love one another** as brothers and sisters (13:1)
- **Welcome strangers** with hospitality, for you might be entertaining angels unaware (13:2)
- **Remember the suffering**—those in prison, those mistreated (13:3)
- **Honor marriage** and keep it pure (13:4)
- **Be content** with what you have, for God has said, "Never will I leave you; never will I forsake you" (13:5)
- **Serve** and **give generously** (13:16)

All of these require discipline. They require saying no to immediate gratification. They require commitment when it's difficult.

But here's the crisis: our generation struggles with discipline. Youth unemployment is high because employers report that young people won't stick with jobs. We resign by text message instead of speaking face-to-face. We don't show up to work or interviews. When faced with difficulty, we complain and demand entitlements rather than persevering.

This extends to relationships. Half of our marriages end in divorce. How is continually breaking promises and being unreliable a way of loving? To marriage, to work, to faith—we approach it all as optional buttons on the Facebook of life.

### The Character of Faithfulness

> The character of a Christian is faithfulness, commitment, grit.

To be a disciple is to have discipline. Righteousness and holiness require self-control, generosity, compassion, and service. All of these demand tremendous willpower when facing the distractions of the world.

Consider the story of Abebe Bikila in the 1968 Olympic Marathon. About 19 kilometers into the 42-kilometer race, he fell badly, injuring his knee and shoulder. His leg was bleeding, his knee possibly dislocated. Medical staff urged him to withdraw. But Bikila continued—walking, limping, running slowly. He finished last among 57 competitors who completed the race, over an hour after the winner crossed the finish line.

When asked why he didn't quit, Bikila said: *"My country did not send me 5,000 miles just to start the race; they sent me to finish the race."*

Of the 75 runners who started, 18 others pulled out. No one remembers the gold medal winner's name. But we remember and honor Bikila because he finished in the most difficult circumstances.

**It is important to finish the race. To persevere.**

## The Promise and the Call

> Since we are receiving a kingdom that cannot be shaken, let us be thankful, and so worship God acceptably with reverence and awe, for our God is a consuming fire. (Hebrews 12:28-29)

We are receiving a kingdom that cannot be shaken. All earthly things will be removed—only what is eternal remains. We belong to that eternal kingdom. This is not theory; this is promise.

Therefore, listen to Him. Do not refuse the one who speaks from heaven. Do not treat your faith as an optional extra. Love, welcome, remember, honor, serve, give. Persist even when the feeling fades. Finish the race.

For you have come to Mount Zion. You have access. And He will never leave you or forsake you.
