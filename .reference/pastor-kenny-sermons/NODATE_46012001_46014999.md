# 1 Corinthians 12-14

**Passage:** 1 Corinthians 12:1-14:40
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Part 1: The Common Spirit – True Spirituality (12:1-11)

### What Is True Spirituality?

Paul begins by addressing a fundamental question: what does it mean to be truly spiritual? In the Corinthian church, there was confusion about spirituality—some believed it was defined by spectacular gifts, official positions, emotional displays, or belonging to certain groups. But Paul redirects their understanding from the outward to the inward.

> Now about spiritual gifts, brothers, I do not want you to be ignorant. (1 Corinthians 12:1)

The apostle acknowledges that the spirit is back in vogue. When materialism fails to satisfy—when it cannot answer questions of pain, inequality, and personal emptiness—people turn to spiritual pursuits. Yet even as we seek the spiritual, we must understand what true spirituality actually is.

### The Essence of Being Christian

> Therefore I tell you that no one who is speaking by the Spirit of God says, "Jesus be cursed," and no one can say, "Jesus is Lord," except by the Holy Spirit. (1 Corinthians 12:3)

Here Paul makes a stunning claim: the mark of having the Holy Spirit is the ability to confess Jesus as Lord. This is not about extraordinary gifts or supernatural displays. It is the simple, profound declaration that Jesus is Lord—the cry of surrender and allegiance that comes only through the Spirit's work.

This echoes what Paul wrote in Romans: believers cry out "Abba, Father" (Romans 8:15). The same Spirit that worked in Jesus now works in us, enabling us to make this confession. Having the Spirit is the essence of being Christian. We are adequate in Christ simply because we possess His Spirit.

### One Spirit, Diverse Expressions

> There are different kinds of gifts, but the same Spirit. There are different kinds of service, but the same Lord. There are different kinds of working, but the same God works all of them in all men. (1 Corinthians 12:4-6)

Paul emphasizes unity within diversity. While there are many different expressions of the Spirit's work—different gifts, different kinds of service, different ways of working—all flow from the same source. The Spirit is not like Santa Claus distributing various gifts; rather, the Spirit is the common denominator that unites all these different manifestations.

> Now to each one the manifestation of the Spirit is given for the common good. (1 Corinthians 12:7)

This is revolutionary. Every expression of the Spirit—every gift, every ability, every spiritual manifestation—is given for the common good, not for personal glory or self-esteem. The Spirit announces or discloses Himself through us for the sake of the whole community.

### The Gifts Listed

Paul then provides a list of these manifestations:

- **The message of wisdom** – insight into God's truth
- **The message of knowledge** – understanding of spiritual matters
- **Faith** – trust that moves mountains
- **Gifts of healing** – restoration of wholeness
- **Miraculous powers** – demonstrations of God's power
- **Prophecy** – speaking God's truth to His people
- **Distinguishing between spirits** – discernment of what is from God and what is not
- **Speaking in different kinds of tongues** – prayer or proclamation in a language not learned
- **The interpretation of tongues** – the ability to communicate what was spoken in an unknown language

> All these are the work of one and the same Spirit, and he gives them to each one, just as he determines. (1 Corinthians 12:11)

The Spirit distributes these gifts sovereignly, as He determines. Not everyone receives the same gifts. Not everyone is meant to have the same role.

### Who Are the Spirituals?

After establishing that true spirituality is rooted in confessing Jesus as Lord and receiving His Spirit, Paul answers the implicit question: Who are the spirituals?

**The answer is: Christians.** Those who have the Spirit of God. Not the ones with the most dramatic gifts. Not the ones with official titles or organizational positions. Not the ones who raise their hands in displays of piety. Not the ones who join special spiritual groups or follow certain spiritual steps. The spirituals are those who have been given the Spirit of God—which is every believer in Jesus.

This reframes everything. Your spirituality is not measured by what you can do, but by whom you belong to. It is not about performance; it is about possession. Do you have the Spirit? Then you are spiritual. You are adequate.

## Part 2: The Common Body – We Need Each Other (12:12-31)

### The Question: Who Is Most Important?

Who is the most important person in the room? Is it the one up front? The musicians? The person with the most impressive gifts or the most official role? Paul answers with a metaphor that transforms how we understand the Christian community.

### One Body, Many Parts

> The body is a unit, though it is made up of many parts; and though all its parts are many, they form one body. So it is with Christ. For we were all baptized by one Spirit into one body—whether Jews or Greeks, slave or free—and we were all given the one Spirit to drink. (1 Corinthians 12:12-13)

We are baptized by one Spirit into one body. This is not about denominations or organizational structures. This is about the fundamental reality: we are one body in Christ, unified by the Spirit, regardless of our background, ethnicity, or social status.

### The Necessity of Diversity

> Now the body is not made up of one part but of many. If the foot should say, "Because I am not a hand, I do not belong to the body," it would not for that reason cease to be part of the body. And if the ear should say, "Because I am not an eye, I do not belong to the body," it would not for that reason cease to be part of the body. If the whole body were an eye, where would the sense of hearing be? If the whole body were an ear, where would the sense of smell be? (1 Corinthians 12:14-17)

Paul uses the body as a brilliant analogy. Just as a physical body needs many different parts with different functions, the church needs many different people with different roles and abilities. There is no gift, no role, no function that is unimportant.

A foot might look at a hand and think, "I'm not important. I'm not as impressive or visible." But a body with only hands would be useless. Every part—even the ones that seem less impressive—is essential. A person setting out chairs is as necessary as a person leading worship. The person greeting newcomers is as vital as the person teaching.

> But in fact God has arranged the parts in the body, every one of them, just as he wanted them to be. If they were all one part, where would the body be? As it is, there are many parts, but one body. (1 Corinthians 12:18-20)

This is the divine arrangement. God has composed the body intentionally. We each have a role—sometimes formally recognized, sometimes informal and behind the scenes, but always necessary.

### Honoring the Hidden Parts

> The eye cannot say to the hand, "I don't need you!" And the head cannot say to the feet, "I don't need you!" On the contrary, those parts of the body that seem to be weaker are indispensable, and the parts that we think are less honorable we treat with special honor. And the parts that are unpresentable are treated with special modesty, while our presentable parts need no special treatment. But God has combined the members of the body and has given greater honor to the parts that lacked it, so that there should be no division in the body, but that its parts should have equal concern for each other. (1 Corinthians 12:21-25)

Here Paul reverses our natural instincts. We tend to honor the visible, impressive parts. We celebrate the person on stage. But Paul says the weaker, less honorable, unpresentable parts deserve special honor. The parts we usually overlook—the ones doing essential work behind the scenes—are indispensable.

God has intentionally given greater honor to the parts that naturally lack it, ensuring that there is no division in the body, but rather equal concern among all members.

### The Care We Owe Each Other

> If one part suffers, every part suffers with it; if one part is honored, every part rejoices with it. Now you are the body of Christ, and each one of you is a part of it. (1 Corinthians 12:26-27)

When one member of the body suffers, all suffer. When one member is honored, all rejoice. This is not metaphorical sentimentality—it is the reality of what it means to be the body of Christ. We are connected to one another. Your pain is my pain. Your joy is my joy.

How does this work practically? When a newcomer arrives—someone who is the weakest in terms of connection to the community—how do we give them special attention? We might call out their names. We might use cultural practices that make them feel welcomed. Whatever the method, we must ensure that they experience the care and honor due to them as members of the body.

## Part 3: The Common Motivation – Love (12:28-13:13)

### Gifts Without Love Are Nothing

Having established that all members of the body are essential and needed, Paul now raises the question: But what animates all this? What should motivate our service?

> And in the church God has appointed first of all apostles, second prophets, third teachers, then workers of miracles, also those having gifts of healing, those able to help others, those with gifts of administration, and those speaking in different kinds of tongues. Are all apostles? Are all prophets? Are all teachers? Do all work miracles? Do all have gifts of healing? Do all speak in tongues? Do all interpret? But eagerly desire the greater gifts. And now I will show you the most excellent way. (1 Corinthians 12:28-31)

There is an order to the gifts. Some are more foundational (apostles, prophets, teachers). Others are more varied. But then Paul adds a crucial corrective: "Now I will show you the most excellent way." What is this way? It is love.

> If I speak in the tongues of men and of angels, but have not love, I am only a resounding gong or a clanging cymbal. If I have the gift of prophecy and can fathom all mysteries and all knowledge, and if I have a faith that can move mountains, but have not love, I am nothing. If I give all I possess to the poor and surrender my body to the flames, but have not love, I gain nothing. (1 Corinthians 13:1-3)

Imagine possessing every spiritual gift. Imagine speaking in the most beautiful, divine languages. Imagine having prophetic insight, understanding all mysteries, possessing mountain-moving faith. Imagine giving away everything you own and even sacrificing your life. Without love, Paul says, all of this amounts to nothing. It becomes mere noise—a clanging cymbal that draws attention to itself but communicates nothing of value.

### The Nature of Love

> Love is patient, love is kind. It does not envy, it does not boast, it is not proud. It is not rude, it is not self-seeking, it is not easily angered, it keeps no record of wrongs. Love does not delight in evil but rejoices with the truth. It always protects, always trusts, always hopes, always perseveres. (1 Corinthians 13:4-7)

Paul describes love not as a feeling but as a way of being. It is patient with others and kind in action. It does not envy what others have or boast about its own accomplishments. It is not proud or rude. It is not self-seeking—the opposite of the self-centeredness that often masquerades as spirituality.

Love does not fly into anger easily; it does not keep a ledger of wrongs. Instead, it rejoices with the truth—it celebrates what is good and right. And love perseveres. It protects, it trusts, it hopes. Even when circumstances seem hopeless, love continues to believe the best.

### Love Never Fails

> Love never fails. But where there are prophecies, they will cease; where there are tongues, they will be stilled; where there is knowledge, it will pass away. For we know in part and we prophesy in part, but when perfection comes, the imperfect disappears. When I was a child, I talked like a child, I thought like a child, I reasoned like a child. When I became a man, I put childish ways behind me. Now we see but a poor reflection as in a mirror; then we shall see face to face. Now I know in part; then I shall know fully, even as I am fully known. (1 Corinthians 13:8-12)

All the gifts—prophecy, tongues, knowledge—will one day cease. They are temporary, limited expressions of God's truth and power. But love never fails. It is eternal. When we see Jesus face to face, when we enter His presence and know as we are fully known, love will remain. All other things will have passed away, but love will be forever.

> And now these three remain: faith, hope and love. But the greatest of these is love. (1 Corinthians 13:13)

### Love as the Test of True Service

When is a gift not a gift? When it is given without love. When is service not service? When it is motivated by something other than love for others and loyalty to Christ.

Too often we approach service by taking spiritual gifts surveys, learning what we're "good at," and then serving based on our strengths. But Paul suggests a better approach: look at the needs around you. Where is there a gap? Where is someone hurting or lacking? Then fill that need—not because it matches your survey results, but because someone needs help.

This requires love as the restraint on our liberty and gifts. Even if we have the ability to do something, love asks: Is this for the common good? Does this build up others? Or am I serving for my own glory, to build my resume, to be with friends, or to belong to a certain group?

For teenagers especially, there is a temptation to join a ministry because everyone else is doing it, because it's where their friends are, because it makes them feel like they belong. But Paul calls us to a higher loyalty: loyalty first to Christ, then to the individuals in the body. We serve because we love Jesus and we love our brothers and sisters—even when it's inconvenient, even when the group dynamic might push us in a different direction, even when an individual needs help that isn't popular with the crowd.

## Part 4: The Common Desire – Strengthening, Encouragement, and Comfort (14:1-40)

### The Purpose of Spiritual Gifts

> Follow the way of love and eagerly desire spiritual gifts, especially the gift of prophecy. For anyone who speaks in a tongue does not speak to men but to God. Indeed, no one understands him; he utters mysteries with his spirit. But everyone who prophesies speaks to men for their strengthening, encouragement and comfort. He who speaks in a tongue edifies himself, but he who prophesies edifies the church. (1 Corinthians 14:1-4)

After establishing that love is the supreme motivation, Paul now returns to the subject of gifts—not to detract from love, but to clarify their proper purpose. We should eagerly desire spiritual gifts, with a special focus on prophecy (the ability to speak God's truth to build up His people).

Why does Paul emphasize prophecy? Because its purpose is clear: to strengthen, encourage, and comfort others. While speaking in tongues edifies the speaker (strengthens their own spirit), prophecy edifies the church. The distinction is crucial: are we using our gifts for ourselves or for others?

### Intelligibility Matters

> I would like every one of you to speak in tongues, but I would rather have you prophesy. He who prophesies is greater than one who speaks in tongues, unless he interprets, so that the church may be edified. Now, brothers, if I come to you and speak in tongues, what good will I be to you, unless I bring you some revelation or knowledge or prophecy or word of instruction? (1 Corinthians 14:5-6)

Paul uses vivid illustrations to make his point about communication. If a musical instrument—a flute or harp—doesn't play distinct notes, how will anyone know what tune is being played? If a trumpet doesn't sound a clear call to battle, how will soldiers know to prepare?

> Even in the case of lifeless things that make sounds, such as the flute or harp, how will anyone know what tune is being played unless there is a distinction in the notes? Again, if the trumpet does not sound a clear call, who will get ready for battle? So it is with you. Unless you speak intelligible words with your tongue, how will anyone know what you are saying? You will just be speaking into the air. (1 Corinthians 14:7-9)

In other words, communication requires intelligibility. If no one understands what you're saying, you're not communicating anything of value to them—even if it's meaningful to you. This has profound implications for how we use our gifts in community.

> If then I do not grasp the meaning of what someone is saying, I am a foreigner to the speaker, and he is a foreigner to me. So it is with you. Since you are eager to have spiritual gifts, try to excel in gifts that build up the church. (1 Corinthians 14:11-12)

### Prayer with Spirit and Mind

> For this reason anyone who speaks in a tongue should pray that he may interpret what he says. For if I pray in a tongue, my spirit prays, but my mind is unfruitful. So what shall I do? I will pray with my spirit, but I will also pray with my mind; I will sing with my spirit, but I will also sing with my mind. (1 Corinthians 14:13-15)

Paul advocates for a wholeness in our spiritual practice. We should engage both spirit and mind. When we pray, our entire being—emotions, intellect, will—should participate. Both dimensions matter.

> If you are praising God with your spirit, how can one who finds himself among those who do not understand say "Amen" to your thanksgiving, since he does not know what you are saying? You may be giving thanks well enough, but the other man is not edified. (1 Corinthians 14:16-17)

Again, the refrain: what matters is whether it builds up others. If someone cannot understand what you're saying or praying, they cannot participate in it. They cannot echo "Amen." They are excluded. Community requires shared understanding.

### Paul's Own Practice

> I thank God that I speak in tongues more than all of you. But in the church I would rather speak five intelligible words to instruct others than ten thousand words in a tongue. (1 Corinthians 14:18-19)

Paul is not dismissing tongues or suggesting they're not valid. He speaks in tongues more than anyone. But in the gathered community, he prioritizes what builds up the body—clear, understandable instruction.

### Maturity in Thinking

> Brothers, stop thinking like children. In regard to evil be infants, but in your thinking be adults. (1 Corinthians 14:20)

This is a call to spiritual maturity. Be innocent regarding evil—don't pursue it, don't seek to understand its workings—but be adults in how you think about spiritual things. Don't be captivated by the spectacular or the emotionally exciting. Think deeply about what builds up the body.

### Tongues and Prophecy: Signs and Purpose

Paul then quotes from Isaiah to clarify the purpose of tongues:

> "Through men of strange tongues and through the lips of foreigners I will speak to this people, but even then they will not listen to me," says the Lord. (1 Corinthians 14:21)

> Tongues, then, are a sign, not for believers but for unbelievers; prophecy, however, is for believers, not for unbelievers. (1 Corinthians 14:22)

Tongues function as a sign to unbelievers—a marker that something supernatural is happening. But prophecy—clear, understandable proclamation of God's truth—is for building up believers. These gifts have different purposes in community.

> So if the whole church comes together and everyone speaks in tongues, and some who do not understand or some unbelievers come in, will they not say that you are out of your mind? But if an unbeliever or someone who does not understand comes in while everybody is prophesying, he will be convinced by all that he is a sinner and will be judged by all, and the secrets of his heart will be laid bare. So he will fall down and worship God, exclaiming, "God is really among you!" (1 Corinthians 14:23-25)

Imagine an unbeliever walking into a church service where everyone is speaking in unknown tongues. It would look chaotic and incomprehensible—they would think the community is insane. But imagine them entering a church where people are prophesying clearly, speaking God's truth. They would be convicted. They would recognize that God is genuinely present.

### Order in Community Worship

> What then shall we say, brothers? When you come together, everyone has a hymn, or a word of instruction, a revelation, a tongue or an interpretation. All of these must be done for the strengthening of the church. If anyone speaks in a tongue, two—or at the most three—should speak, one at a time, and someone must interpret. If there is no interpreter, the speaker should keep quiet in the church and speak to himself and God. (1 Corinthians 14:26-28)

Paul sets boundaries for corporate worship: no more than two or three people speaking in tongues, one at a time, with interpretation. This is not to quench the Spirit but to ensure that the community is actually edified.

> Two or three prophets should speak, and the others should weigh carefully what is said. And if a revelation comes to someone who is sitting down, the first speaker should stop. For you can all prophesy in turn so that everyone may be instructed and encouraged. The spirits of prophets are subject to the control of prophets. For God is not a God of disorder but of peace. (1 Corinthians 14:29-33)

This is remarkable: the Spirit gives prophets the ability to control when they speak. A prophet is not seized by an uncontrollable force; they can choose to wait their turn. The Spirit is a Spirit of order, not chaos. God is a God of peace.

### The Goal: Instruction and Encouragement

> As in all the congregations of the saints... Therefore, my brothers, be eager to prophesy, and do not forbid speaking in tongues. But everything should be done in a fitting and orderly way. (1 Corinthians 14:33, 39-40)

The central principle that threads through this entire section is this: everything done in the gathered community should serve the purpose of strengthening, encouraging, and comforting the body. This is the lens through which all gifts, all words, all contributions are evaluated.

To summarize the four chapters:

1. **True spirituality** is having the Spirit of Christ—not spectacular gifts or impressive roles
2. **The body needs everyone**—there is no unimportant member, no unnecessary role
3. **Love is the motivation**—all gifts and all service must be animated by love for others and loyalty to Christ
4. **The purpose is community edification**—everything we do should strengthen, encourage, and comfort the body of Christ

## Application: Living Out These Truths

**Who is the most important person in the room?** The answer is: you are. And so is the person next to you. Every member of Christ's body has intrinsic value and indispensable purpose. The question is not "Who is most important?" but "How can I use whatever gifts, abilities, and opportunities I have to serve others and build up the body?"

When you think about how to serve, don't start with a spiritual gifts survey. Start with needs. Look around and ask: Where is there brokenness? Where is someone hurting? Where is a gap that needs to be filled? Then step into that opportunity. Not because it looks good on your resume. Not because all your friends are doing it. Not because you want recognition. But because you love Jesus and you love His people.

And remember: your service doesn't have to be public or impressive to be real. Setting out chairs, greeting newcomers, sending an encouraging text to someone who is struggling—these are not lesser callings. They are the very expression of Christ's love through His body. You are spiritual not because of what you do, but because you belong to Christ. And you serve not because of who you are, but because of whom you serve.
