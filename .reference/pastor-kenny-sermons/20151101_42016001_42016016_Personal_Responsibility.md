# Luke 16:1-16

**Title:** Personal Responsibility

**Date:** 8 November 2015 SMAC

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Taking Responsibility for Yourself

Jesus teaches us through a parable about a manager who has been squandering his master's possessions. This man has not been taking responsibility for his work. When his master calls him to account, the manager realizes he's about to lose his job and faces a desperate situation. Rather than sit idle or complain, he acts quickly and decisively.

> 1 Jesus told his disciples: "There was a rich man whose manager was accused of wasting his possessions. 2 So he called him in and asked him, 'What is this I hear about you? Give an account of your management, because you cannot be manager any longer.'

The manager, knowing he's neither strong enough for manual labor nor proud enough to beg, devises a plan. He begins reducing his master's debtors' bills—giving a 50% discount on one debt and 20% on another. This bold action catches the master's attention.

> 3 "The manager said to himself, 'What shall I do now? My master is taking away my job. I'm not strong enough to dig, and I'm ashamed to beg. 4 I know what I'll do so that, when I lose my job here, people will welcome me into their houses.' 5 So he called in each one of his master's debtors. He asked the first, 'How much do you owe my master?' 6 'Nine hundred gallons of olive oil,' he replied. "The manager told him, 'Take your bill, sit down quickly, and make it four hundred and fifty.' 7 Then he asked the second, 'And how much do you owe?' "'A thousand bushels of wheat,' he replied. "He told him, 'Take your bill and make it eight hundred.' 8 "The master commended the dishonest manager because he had acted shrewdly."

The key point here is that the manager took responsibility. Rather than remaining in his predicament, he recognized his situation and acted upon it with urgency and determination. Whether we view his actions as shrewd business strategy, creative problem-solving, or redemptive action—the crucial lesson is his willingness to take personal responsibility for his circumstances and do something about it.

### Growing into Responsibility

As we mature from childhood to adulthood, we transition fundamentally. We move from dependence to independence, from others caring for us to caring for ourselves and others. This isn't automatic—it requires intentional choice and action. We grow from lying helpless as infants to standing, working, and providing for ourselves. We shift from being fed by others to feeding ourselves and eventually feeding our families.

This same principle applies spiritually and in all areas of life: education, career, relationships, marriage, finances, and personal growth. None of these things happen automatically. You must take responsibility for your job, your education, your financial independence, your studies, your family, your relationships with friends and family. There is no one who will hand these things to you on a platter.

As adults, we face constant excuses—"I'm tired," "It's too far," "Someone else should do this." But the reality is that nothing will be handed to you. Taking responsibility means stepping up even when it's difficult.

## Taking Responsibility for Your Relationship with God

Beyond personal responsibility, Jesus teaches that we must take responsibility for our spiritual relationship with God. This is where the parable becomes deeply spiritual.

> 9 I tell you, use worldly wealth to gain friends for yourselves, so that when it is gone, you will be welcomed into eternal dwellings.

Notice the connection: just as the manager works to gain friends in the earthly realm (verse 4), we are called to use our resources to gain friends eternally. The parallel speaks of eternal dwellings—not just earthly homes, but our standing before God.

> 13 "No one can serve two masters. Either you will hate the one and love the other, or you will be devoted to the one and despise the other. You cannot serve both God and money."

This is our central responsibility as believers. We alone are responsible for our response to God. Just as we cannot rely on others to work our jobs, earn our education, or build our families for us, we cannot rely on others—not our parents, not the church, not our pastors—to build our relationship with God.

We are personally responsible for our own spiritual growth. God deals with us as individuals, not as inheritances. There are no grandchildren of God, only children. This means:

- You are responsible for establishing a Bible reading habit
- You are responsible for organizing your prayer life—a life of dependence on God
- You are responsible for living godly and honoring God in your daily choices
- You are responsible for committing to fellow believers for accountability and growth in service

While the church, pastors, small groups, and teachers can create environments that help us grow, they cannot make us grow. A chef can prepare a magnificent meal, but they cannot make you eat or enjoy it—that remains your choice. The responsibility for your spiritual maturation is yours alone.

> 10 "Whoever can be trusted with very little can also be trusted with much, and whoever is dishonest with very little will also be dishonest with much. 11 So if you have not been trustworthy in handling worldly wealth, who will trust you with true riches? 12 And if you have not been trustworthy with someone else's property, who will give you property of your own?"

Faith is not a noun but a verb—like a muscle that either grows stronger through exercise or atrophies from disuse. There is no middle ground. You either invest in your spiritual growth or you decline. This demands that you make the decision to take responsibility for yourself.

The good news is that God has already initiated friendship with us. Jesus did not hesitate when the time came; He went to the cross to save us. Now, as redeemed children, the time has come for us to take responsibility for our own growth and to stop making excuses we would never make to our employers.

## Taking Responsibility for Others

Finally, Jesus calls us to take responsibility for those around us.

> 15 He said to them, "You are the ones who justify yourselves in the eyes of others, but God knows your hearts. What people value highly is detestable in God's sight. 16 "The Law and the Prophets were proclaimed until John. Since that time, the good news of the kingdom of God is being preached, and everyone is forcing their way into it."

In Jesus' time, the Pharisees—good, believing people committed to living godly lives—held responsibility for bringing the good news of God's forgiveness to the Gentiles. They were meant to be witnesses to God's gracious character. Yet they failed in this responsibility. Instead of drawing others to God, they became obstacles.

> Zechariah 8:23 The Lord of Hosts says this: "In those days, 10 men from nations of every language will grab the robe of a Jewish man tightly, urging: Let us go with you, for we have heard that God is with you."

The expectation was clear: God's people would be so visibly different, so transformed by their faith, that others would desperately want to follow them to God. But Israel failed. They became no different from the nations around them—entangled in the same political troubles, materialism, and idolatry. Because they neglected their responsibility to others, the Gentiles eventually pursued God directly, bypassing the Pharisees altogether. The Pharisees, in their failure to take responsibility for others, found themselves left out of God's kingdom.

This carries a warning for us today. We are not in Israel's position as a special people, but the principle remains: we have a responsibility for those who do not yet know God. We must not waste this opportunity.

### Living Out This Responsibility

Use your resources to make friends. Invite friends to church. Support ministries that reach young people and teach Scripture. But recognize that you cannot rely on institutions or programs alone—ultimately, you must take personal responsibility.

Read the Bible to your children. There is no one more consistent, more loving, or more influential in their lives than you. Children naturally inherit their parents' level of commitment with about 80% the strength. If you are a nominal Christian, your children will tend toward secularism. If you are an average Christian, your children will likely be nominal. If you are active and committed, your children will probably be average. You must model active faith so they will take it upon themselves.

When we reach heaven, we will not regret what we lacked materially. We will regret that we didn't do a little more—that we didn't spend extra time with an unbeliever, that we weren't thoughtful about our friends, that we let the world corrupt our children's thinking instead of setting a clearer example. We may have gained our houses, careers, cars, and holidays, but if our children don't join us in heaven, what will it have profited us?

## Conclusion

The decision to take responsibility marks the transition to maturity. When Kenny got his first car, he faced real costs: petrol, insurance, servicing. He had to decide whether to complain or take responsibility. He remembers clearly—standing on marble, he fell on his head. In that moment, he chose not to throw a tantrum. He chose to grow up. He decided to endure the pain, knowing it would pass, and he moved forward. He hasn't thrown a tantrum since.

That same choice confronts us. Will we take responsibility for ourselves, for our relationship with God, and for those around us? Or will we remain stuck, waiting for someone else to make things happen?

The time is now. The choice is yours.
