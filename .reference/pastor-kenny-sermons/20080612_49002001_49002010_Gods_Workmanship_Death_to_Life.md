# Ephesians 2:1-10

**Title:** God's Workmanship: Death to Life  
**Date:** June 12, 2008  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Paul the Diagnostician

Paul approaches the human condition with the precision of a physician. He begins not with who we can become, but with the honest diagnosis of our spiritual state—where we actually stand before God. This diagnostic clarity is essential because understanding the severity of our condition determines how we appreciate the remedy.

## Dead in Sin (verses 1-3)

Our spiritual death operates on three levels, each revealing a different dimension of our separation from God.

### External Death (verse 2)

We are dead in our **transgressions and sins**—the outward expressions of our rebellion. These are not abstract theological concepts but concrete actions and patterns that reveal our hearts turned away from God.

### Supernatural Death (verse 2)

More fundamentally, we walk according to the **course of this world**. We're caught in a system, an order of things that stands opposed to God's kingdom. Paul identifies this worldly system as being under the influence of **the ruler of the power of the air, the spirit who now works in the sons of disobedience**. This isn't mere social pressure or cultural convention—there's a supernatural reality at work. We're not simply victims of bad circumstances; we're living within a spiritual realm that has declared war on God's authority.

### Internal Death (verse 3)

The deepest level is our **desires of the flesh and of the mind**. Our very inner life—our appetites, our thoughts, our natural inclinations—are bent toward sin. We are, by nature, **children of wrath**. This is not judgment imposed arbitrarily from outside but the natural trajectory of a life oriented away from the source of all goodness. What we desire, we become.

## Alive in Christ (verses 4-9)

Into this condition of death—external, supernatural, internal—God moves with mercy.

> "But God, being rich in mercy, because of the great love with which he loved us, even when we were dead in our transgressions, made us alive together with Christ."

Notice the contrast: **But God**. While we were still dead, while we were still rebels, while we were still enslaved to the desires of the flesh, God acts. His mercy is not earned; it's a gift that flows from His nature and His love for us in Christ.

We are made alive together with Christ. We're not merely forgiven or given another chance—we're brought into union with the risen Christ. We're seated together with Him in the heavenly places. This is not a future hope alone but a present spiritual reality. Our position is already secure in Him.

And this grace is **for the ages to come**—the riches of His kindness toward us in Christ Jesus will continue to unfold throughout eternity. What God has begun in making us alive cannot be undone.

## Death to Life Means Good Works (verse 10)

Here's where grace finds its expression: **For we are his workmanship, created in Christ Jesus for good works, which God prepared beforehand that we should walk in them.**

This is the missionary heart of the gospel. We are God's **poiema**—His masterpiece, His work of art. He hasn't just forgiven us and left us unchanged. He's creating us anew, crafting us in Christ Jesus for a specific purpose: **good works**.

These works are not the ground of our salvation—we're saved by grace through faith, not by works. But they are the fruit of our salvation, the evidence of genuine life. God has actually prepared these works in advance. Our good deeds aren't random acts of kindness; they're part of God's design. We walk in them—they're not burdensome obligations but the natural expression of a life made alive in Christ.

The gospel transforms us from death to life, and that transformation necessarily produces works that reflect the character of Christ and serve the purposes of God's kingdom.
