# 1 Corinthians 13:1-13

**Title:** The Common Ministry: Love
**Date:** 24 January 2010
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Good but Useless

Trivia can be fascinating—fascinating but ultimately useless for 99.9% of our lives. Similarly, the exercise of our spiritual gifts can be genuinely good but profoundly useless if divorced from love.

Last week we learned that the church is the body of Christ, and we are responsible for one another through exercising our individual gifts and talents toward each other. Today, Paul speaks to us about the most excellent way to exercise those gifts: through love.

## Gifts Are Useless Without Love (v. 1-3)

> If I speak in the tongues of men and of angels, but have not love, I am only a resounding gong or a clanging cymbal. If I have the gift of prophecy and can fathom all mysteries and all knowledge, and if I have a faith that can move mountains, but have not love, I am nothing. If I give all I possess to the poor and surrender my body to the flames, but have not love, I gain nothing.

If we were to write this as a mathematical equation, it would be: **Gifts × Love = Profit**

Paul is saying that whatever these gifts are—speaking in tongues, prophecy, understanding mysteries, miraculous faith, sacrificial generosity—they amount to nothing without love. Verse 3 literally uses the word "profit" (Greek: opheleō). It's profitless. Like a company that works incredibly hard for a year and ends with no profit, your talents and effort are ultimately useless if they're not exercised with love.

Think of an orchestra without a conductor. Without love as the unifying principle, even excellent individual performances create only noise—a resounding gong, a clanging cymbal.

The key ingredient is love.

## What Is Love? (v. 4-7)

> Love is patient, love is kind. It does not envy, it does not boast, it is not proud. It is not rude, it is not self-seeking, it is not easily angered, it keeps no record of wrongs. Love does not delight in evil but rejoices with the truth. It always protects, always trusts, always hopes, always perseveres.

This is what we must bring to our service of one another. Love provides the proper focus for our gifts and talents. Love provides the motivation by which we judge our activity.

- **Patience and kindness** address the difficulty of getting along with each other
- **Not envying, boasting, or being proud** counter the poison of individualism
- **Not being rude or self-seeking** guard against self-centeredness
- **Not easily angered, keeping no record of wrongs** enable genuine reconciliation
- **Rejoicing with the truth** means we pursue what is right, not what feels good
- **Always protecting, trusting, hoping, persevering** show us love's steadfast character

Read this list again and you will see Jesus there. We do this not because we have naturally learned to love, but because He has loved us first.

## The Standard of Love: Concern for Christ and His Body

Jesus spoke of this standard in Matthew 25. On the day of judgment, He will separate the sheep from the goats based on how they treated the least among us:

> Whatever you did for one of the least of these brothers of mine, you did for me.

This is not just a personal concern for individuals. This is a concern for Christ Himself. When we love the body of Christ, we show love to Christ.

Therefore, we must now show concern for others. The test is always: **Does this exhibit thoughtful love, or is it the unbridled exercise of my gifts?** God gave us gifts for the common good—not just for ourselves, not even just for our friends. **You must think of loving the whole body, not just yourself.** No ministry operates in isolation. The body is more important than the exercise of your individual gifts.

If you exercise a ministry here—any ministry—ask yourself: Why am I doing this?

## Love Applied: Two Examples from Corinthians

### 1 Corinthians 8: The Stumbling Brother

> Knowledge puffs up, but love builds up. ... Be careful, however, that the exercise of your freedom does not become a stumbling block to the weak. ... When you sin against your brothers in this way and wound their weak conscience, you sin against Christ. Therefore, if what I eat causes my brother to fall into sin, I will never eat meat again, so that I will not cause him to fall.

The Corinthians had knowledge—they understood that idols are nothing, just wood, with no spiritual power. But this knowledge made them arrogant. Some believers had weak consciences; they couldn't eat food sacrificed to idols without feeling defiled. Rather than flaunt their freedom and stumble the weak, Paul says to exercise patience and kindness—to be other-seeking.

### 1 Corinthians 11: The Lord's Meal Together

> When you come together to eat, wait for each other. If anyone is hungry, he should eat at home, so that when you meet together it may not result in judgment.

Here too, the principle is love: when you gather as the body of Christ, consider one another. Don't eat while others go hungry. The standard that governs what you do—what you eat, when you eat it, what clothes you wear, what you say, what beverages you serve, what games you play—is love.

Love supersedes your freedom. Love restrains the exercise of your gifts when necessary.

## Serving at Church: Love First, Gifts Second

In the past, I used to think the key criteria for service could be summarized by the acronym FAT—but I made a mistake. I thought **Fidelity, Ability, Time** came first, and that love would follow. But that's backwards. People may eventually learn to love, but if they don't, then no matter how faithful, able, or available they are—it equals nothing. Useless.

The healthier (though physically unhealthy-sounding) approach is **LOVE-FAT**. Love must come first.

### Why Not to Serve

Before asking why you should serve, let me address why you shouldn't serve:

- It's not so you have a fun new experience
- It's not to boost your resume (especially when serving a church, not a secular organization)
- It's not to hang out with your friends or the "cool" leaders
- It's not to build your self-esteem
- It's not even for your own spiritual training (though this is genuinely godly)

Now, these things *can* happen as byproducts. Service has its joys. When you spend time helping at church, you have something to tell employers. You make friends. You do feel better about yourself. You develop skills, especially people skills. But **these cannot be your primary reason for serving. Not even a close second.**

Like Bryan Adams sings, you have to do it all for love—because all those other reasons don't last. Only love endures.

### Two Objections to This Teaching

**"Why else would God give me this gift?"**

You might ask: If God gave me this ability, shouldn't I use it? Didn't Paul tell us to exercise our gifts? But consider: your ATAR score doesn't dictate your career. It's just a tool. A high ATAR could mean you should study medicine or law—or it could mean you should study arts, or teaching, or politics, or drop out altogether and pursue your own education. The tool itself is neutral. Your *motivation* determines what you do with it.

Gifts are the same. A tool. The question is: what are you trying to accomplish? And if you're driven by love, your gift is never wasted—whether you use it in a highly visible role or a humble one.

**"I have no other gifts to exercise."**

This is false. There will always be a need to love.

Don't know where to serve? First answer: What are your gifts? But second—and more importantly—ask: **What are the love needs around me?** Look around. Who needs care? Who needs to hear the gospel? Who needs encouragement? Who needs someone to listen?

If you're not mute, you have the gift of greeting. We need the gospel to penetrate deep into our hearts through talking to one another. This is hard because it means opening up, sharing what matters, moving beyond small talk about purchases and movies.

You have multiple gifts built into your very being:
- **A mouth**—to speak God's words to each other
- **Legs**—to walk to someone
- **Memory**—to remember others' needs and pray for them
- **Eyes**—to see who's sitting alone or rushing out in fear of isolation
- **Arms**—to help with chairs, garbage, or to offer a hug
- **Hands**—to write a note or send an SMS: "Keep following Jesus"
- **A driver's license**—to give people lifts
- **Internet access**—to email about youth group
- **A wallet**—to give generously
- **A house**—to host a dinner
- **A phone**—to pray

All of these have been given by God. Don't say you have no gifts. Open your mind to opportunities.

I'm increasingly drawn to the language of **opportunities** rather than gifts—because opportunities focus on needs. You see a need, and you fill it. That's how love works. Opportunities are gifts that actually do common good and actually profit.

The problem is we fixate on the glamorous, upfront activities—the 1% of church life. We can't all lead worship or sing or play instruments. Many gift-focused conferences start in the wrong place (what I'm good at and what looks impressive) and inevitably end at the obligatory music, singing, and leadership positions. But no one teaches the gift of taking out the garbage—yet that's service. For whatever it is, it's useless if not accompanied by love. A love seminar would be far more valuable. Gifts will work themselves out.

**Make the decision to love first. Gifts will follow.**

## Love Is More Important Than Gifts (v. 8-13)

> Love never fails. But where there are prophecies, they will cease; where there are tongues, they will be stilled; where there is knowledge, it will pass away.

### Love Is the Fruit That Lasts

One day, the gifts will fail. Prophecies will cease. Tongues will be stilled. Knowledge will pass away. But love never fails.

> When I was a child, I talked like a child, I thought like a child, I reasoned like a child. When I became a man, I put childish ways behind me. Now we see but a poor reflection as in a mirror; then we shall see face to face. Now I know in part; then I shall know fully, even as I am fully known. And now these three remain: faith, hope and love. But the greatest of these is love.

One day, SMAC will disappear. You will forget what you learned. People won't remember your deeds. But love will count—if you did your work out of genuine concern for others, if you weren't self-seeking, if you truly loved.

### Love Is the Primary Motivator When Things Get Hard

Here's the reality: one day, your service will get boring.

To our musicians—it will become tedious. To our Bible study leaders—Christianity is trendy now, and Next Gen is very popular. It's tempting to lead while popular. But what happens when the narrow way becomes unfashionable? Will you lead then?

To those preparing morning tea. To those packing up chairs. To those ushering and welcoming. To those managing the PowerPoint.

All the reasons you initially served—because it was fun, because it looked good on your resume, because it made you feel good about yourself, because it was cool—all of these will eventually fail.

Service won't remain fun because it requires hard work. Your resume will take a beating when, for the sake of love, you quit a lucrative job or take a less glamorous role. Your self-esteem will suffer when you take criticism for trying to act godly and serve others' interests. It certainly won't be cool to be alone, packing up chairs or cleaning up after Bible study.

Will you still serve then? Out of love?

I think of missionary friends. Their preference is to stay home, in comfort. But they're doing what they don't prefer—because of Jesus.

Here's a particular danger for young people in church: it's very appealing to move in packs, to belong to a group. But **your loyalty must first be to the Lord Jesus, not to your peer group.** As a result, your loyalty is to serving the body of Christ, not to peer pressure. You will still care for individuals when the group abandons them.

If you're burning out in a ministry, or you've stopped doing it out of love, or you're only continuing because someone pressured you—please don't do it. Come talk to me or another leader.

## The Best Trumps the Good

We face a tyranny of choices. There are so many good things to do. We have so many talented people. How do we decide?

A friend once said: **The good is the enemy of the best.**

Do the best. Don't settle for good when you can do what's best. Don't just exercise your gift because it's good. Do what flows from love. Don't trivialize your opportunities. Make them count through love.

In the end, what matters isn't how talented we are or how much our ministry grows. What matters is whether it all points toward love. **True spirituality is love.** God knows your motivation—whether your service succeeds or fails. And when is a gift not truly a gift? When it's not offered in love.
