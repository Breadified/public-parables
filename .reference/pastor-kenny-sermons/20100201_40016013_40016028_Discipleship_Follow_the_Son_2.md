# Matthew 16:13-28

**Title:** Discipleship: Follow the Son!  
**Date:** 8 February 2010  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Who Is Jesus?

When Jesus asked his disciples "Who do people say the Son of Man is?" the answers revealed their expectations. Some said John the Baptist—the greatest prophet, so powerful that King Herod feared him. Others said Elijah, who stood alone against 400 Baal worshippers. Still others suggested Jeremiah, a prophet not just to Israel but to the nations. These were the heroes of Israel, and the people expected Jesus to be another great prophet like them.

They weren't entirely wrong. Jesus himself spoke of being a prophet: "A prophet is not without honor except in his own town and in his own home." He was indeed God's messenger, foretelling the future just as the prophets had done.

But Peter saw more. When Jesus asked, "Who do you say I am?" Peter answered with clarity: "You are the Messiah, the Son of the living God."

The word *Messiah* means "Anointed One"—a title, not a name. In Israel, kings were anointed with oil, not crowned. By calling Jesus the Messiah, Peter was saying: You are the King. You are the one the prophets promised. You will rule the whole world, bring justice, save the oppressed, humble the proud, and deliver Israel from Roman oppression.

Jesus received this confession with blessing. He praised Peter—not because flesh and blood had revealed this to him, but because God the Father had opened his eyes. And then Jesus made a remarkable declaration: "On this rock I will build my church, and the gates of Hades will not overcome it."

This was the beginning of something new. God had gathered His people at Mount Sinai, moved them to Jerusalem, scattered them in exile—and now, through Peter's confession of Christ, Jesus was gathering a new people. A worldwide movement built on the foundation of acknowledging Jesus as Lord. All who call upon His name would be part of this gathering.

## The Shocking Truth: The King Must Die

But into this moment of excitement and affirmation, Jesus said something incomprehensible: "I must go to Jerusalem and suffer many things at the hands of the elders, the chief priests and the teachers of the law, and that I must be killed and on the third day be raised to life."

This made no sense. Powerful leaders don't announce their own death. When the great CEOs of the world gain power, they cling to it. Yet here was Jesus—newly acknowledged as king—immediately declaring He would be executed.

Peter couldn't bear it. He took Jesus aside and rebuked Him, saying, "Never, Lord! This shall never happen to you!" Peter was trying to teach his Master how the world actually works.

But Jesus turned and said to Peter, "Get behind me, Satan! You are a stumbling block to me; you do not have in mind the concerns of God, but merely human concerns."

Jesus called Peter an adversary because Peter was opposing the very mission Jesus had come to accomplish. Peter didn't want his Master to die—and perhaps, if we're honest, Peter was also thinking about himself. If the leader dies, who's next? It's like joining an elite navy only to find yourself recruited for a suicide mission. The timing is terrible.

But the real problem Jesus identified goes deeper: Peter was ruled by self-interest. Jesus wanted His followers to be free from that entirely.

## The Call to Radical Discipleship

Jesus then turned to all His disciples with an invitation that echoed His rebuke to Peter: "Whoever wants to be my disciple must deny themselves and take up their cross and follow me."

When you stand in front, you lead. When you stand behind, you follow. "Get behind me" means "come, follow me." And following means denying yourself and carrying your cross.

In the first century, taking up one's cross was not a metaphor—it was a death sentence. Condemned prisoners carried the crossbeam to their execution. So Jesus was calling His disciples to a life of death, a life surrendered entirely to His purposes.

This is what Jesus did for us. We are sinners condemned by the Judge of all the earth to eternal death. But Jesus took that sentence upon Himself instead. He denied Himself and carried His cross so we wouldn't have to. If this is true of our Master, it must be true of those who follow Him.

The Christian life is a call to self-sacrifice. We are not here simply to make money, gain possessions, or enjoy comfortable experiences. We are here to serve others—specifically, to advance the gospel so that others might believe Jesus died for them and secure their eternal life.

But note: this is not the quick, violent death of a kamikaze pilot. It is a slow, deliberate dying to ourselves. It's the gradual laying aside of our own interests for the interests of Christ. And we do it not out of self-centeredness (seeking paradise for ourselves) but out of genuine love for others.

## Realigning Our Ambitions

The problem is that we want to write our own script. We want to die slowly, on our own terms—comfortably. We say things like: "I'll serve, but first let me be rich and successful so people will respect me. Let me be popular first, before I do the unpopular thing. I'll sacrifice when it doesn't hurt."

We create acronyms like WWJD ("What Would Jesus Do?") as though we didn't already know the answer. The truth is, we want Jesus to follow us—to fit into the life we've already planned.

But what if we chose our ambitions differently? Not "I want a comfortable career, a nice house in a nice suburb, nice furniture, a nice car, a nice family, nice holidays, and church on Sundays (except when traveling)—but not so extreme that I'd actually sacrifice anything." Instead, ask yourself: What will it take to change me, my family, my community, my country to turn to Christ? Then act accordingly.

Real discipleship looks like this:

- Witnessing to your family, even if it means giving up social media
- Organizing your life in such a way that you're serving others, not using them
- Pursuing education and skill primarily for gospel work, not comfort
- Taking advantage of opportunities to tell strangers about Jesus
- Living simply so you can give generously
- Leaving home and comfort to serve as a pastor or missionary
- Doing what is needed rather than what is comfortable

## The Promise and the Warning

Jesus offered both a promise and a sobering truth:

> For whoever wants to save their life will lose it, but whoever loses their life for me will find it. What good will it be for someone to gain the whole world, yet forfeit their soul? Or what can anyone give in exchange for their soul?

Death should not be our fear. Poverty should not be our fear. Losing status or lacking the right clothes should not be our fear. The only fear that matters is losing our eternal soul—and if that's resolved by Jesus, then we have nothing to worry about.

Jim Elliott, a young missionary who was martyred in Ecuador, said it perfectly: "He is no fool to give up what he does not have to gain what he cannot lose."

When Japan recruited for the kamikaze force, they looked for "youth, alertness and zeal." They didn't require extensive training or expertise in landing. The Christian movement has similar recruitment criteria: Deny yourself. Follow Jesus. Youth, alertness, zeal. Your life is not necessary; the world's comforts are a luxury.

Jesus concluded with this promise: "The Son of Man is going to come in his Father's glory with his angels, and then he will reward each person according to what they have done."

## A Call to Radical Faith

Are you committed to Jesus? Are you a radical follower of Christ? Will you live a life that is not primarily concerned with how smoothly things land, but one that is centered on serving others and advancing Christ's kingdom?

The invitation stands as it always has: Follow Me.
