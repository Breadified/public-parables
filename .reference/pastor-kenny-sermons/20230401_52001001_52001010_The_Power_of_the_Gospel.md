## The Thessalonians: A Tall Poppy

Australian culture teaches us to cut down the tall poppies—those who stand out or excel. We've inherited this from our convict settlement past and anti-establishment roots, creating an egalitarian society suspicious of titles and prominence.

But the Thessalonian church defies this mentality. They became a model for believers everywhere—a gold standard to imitate, a "type" (in the sense of a mould) that others should fit into.

> And so you became a model to all the believers in Macedonia and Achaia. (1 Thessalonians 1:7)

The Lord's message rang out from them not just locally but everywhere. Their reputation spread across the entire Greek peninsula, perhaps even back to Jerusalem. The word "rang out" suggests a giant bell clanging to the whole world.

### What Made Them Remarkable?

> They tell how you turned to God from idols to serve the living and true God, and to wait for his Son from heaven, whom he raised from the dead—Jesus, who rescues us from the coming wrath. (1 Thessalonians 1:9-10)

They turned from Greek idols—the pantheon of selfish, scandal-ridden gods preoccupied with their own drama—to serve the *living* God. This shift matters profoundly. The ancient gods served themselves. But our God served.

Jesus embodied this radical reversal. While Gentile rulers lord it over others, Jesus came not to be served but to serve, giving his life as a ransom for many. When he walked among us, he washed his disciples' feet. When he left us, he hung on a cross.

The Thessalonians learned this posture of service from their apostle Paul, and Paul learned it from Jesus.

## The Marks of Active Faith

> We continually remember before our God and Father your work produced by faith, your labor prompted by love, and your endurance inspired by hope in our Lord Jesus Christ. (1 Thessalonians 1:3)

The Thessalonians were not passive. They didn't retreat into a room, purchase some advertising, and sit back. They actively lived out their Christian faith, working hard for the gospel, for their families, and for their local community.

### Hard Work and Recognition

Consider MrBeast (Jimmy Donaldson), the most popular YouTube personality with 150+ million subscribers. To become known globally, he invested a decade of extraordinary sacrifice:

- Started at age 13, now 24—ten years of grinding
- Woke up obsessed, worked until 3 AM, crashed when exhausted, then started again
- Dropped out of college to pursue this full-time
- Spent five years in hard work before his first viral video (a 40-hour video counting to 100,000)
- Recently rejected a $1 billion acquisition offer
- Now spends $7-8 million monthly, aiming to be remembered as the greatest YouTuber in history

The principle applies to Christian service: to be known for something requires work and sacrifice. Not in self-promotion, but in serving others. The proclamation of the gospel—in word and deed—demands labor. It starts with our families and churches, but it must extend beyond.

Christians serve even when tired. Christians serve even when they don't want to. Christians serve through distractions. There may be rest, but there is no retirement.

## Model from Paul: Suffering and Endurance

> You know how we lived among you for your sake. You became imitators of us and of the Lord; in spite of severe suffering, you welcomed the message with the joy given by the Holy Spirit. (1 Thessalonians 1:5-6)

The Thessalonians modelled their perseverance after Paul, who in turn modelled his after Jesus. When Paul came to Thessalonica, he endured tremendous persecution.

### The Context of Their Suffering

According to Acts 17, Paul preached in the synagogue on three Sabbaths, reasoning that the Messiah had to suffer and rise from the dead. Some Jews believed, as did many God-fearing Greeks and prominent women. But the jealous Jews stirred up a mob, rushing to Jason's house to drag Paul and Silas before city officials. The persecution was so severe that Paul had to be smuggled out of the city for his safety.

The Thessalonians witnessed this firsthand and chose to follow anyway.

### Paul's Example of Endurance

Paul's life was marked by extraordinary suffering:

> I have worked much harder, been in prison more frequently, been flogged more severely, and been exposed to death again and again. Five times I received from the Jews the forty lashes minus one. Three times I was beaten with rods, once I was pelted with stones, three times I was shipwrecked. I have been constantly on the move. I have been in danger from rivers, from bandits, from my fellow Jews, from Gentiles; in danger in the city, in danger in the country, in danger at sea; and in danger from false believers. I have labored and toiled and have often gone without sleep; I have known hunger and thirst and have often gone without food; I have been cold and naked. (2 Corinthians 11:23-27)

Paul endured all this as a follower of Jesus, who carried his cross and suffered on our behalf.

## The Unique Power of the Gospel

> Our gospel came to you not simply with words, but also with power, with the Holy Spirit and with deep conviction. (1 Thessalonians 1:5)

What is this power? Notice how verse 5 and verse 6 mirror each other:

**Verse 5:** "Our gospel came to you not simply with words, but also with power, with the Holy Spirit and with deep conviction."

**Verse 6:** "In spite of severe suffering, you welcomed the message with the joy given by the Holy Spirit."

The repetition suggests they're describing the same reality. The "power" Paul speaks of isn't magic, miracles, prosperity, or even healings. It's the **power to endure**—formed from deep conviction of God's word by the Holy Spirit.

This seems paradoxical. Suffering suggests weakness and vulnerability. Yet Paul says they received the gospel with power *in spite of* severe suffering. The power lies in genuine belief. Why would anyone suffer for something they knew was false? The Thessalonians believed so deeply that they were willing to invest their own lives into the gospel. These were not mere words of confession—they put their bodies on the line.

### Living with This Power

We're privileged to live in a country where systematic persecution hasn't yet arrived. Be grateful for that protection. Yet we should understand the pattern:

> Everyone who wants to live a godly life in Christ Jesus will be persecuted. (2 Timothy 3:12)

The more godly we become, the more set apart from the world we are, the more the world will hate us and what we stand for. That's not unusual—it's expected.

But we've been given a power. Sometimes we're defeatist: "I'm too sick, too weak, I come from a broken family, I'm too poor." We forget that we can endure and improve. We're not alone. We can build up. I might be weak today, but not tomorrow.

## Models in a World That Rejects Them

Modern culture, in the name of equality, has abandoned the idea of models and standards. To call for an ideal is seen as denigrating minorities. But this misses something vital.

Saying it's *better* to be strong doesn't nullify the worth of those who are weak. Saying it's *better* to have strong families doesn't judge broken families. Saying it's *better* to serve isn't discriminating against the disabled. We all are weak. We all struggle with something.

But models are models. They set high water marks—not to shame those trying, but to raise our eyes from our feet to the skies and see what's possible with God's help. They motivate us to strive for lofty goals. Models are the planets we aim rockets toward, so that even if we fail, we fall among the stars.

The Thessalonians embodied this model mindset: a growth focus asking constantly, *How did they do it? How can I follow? How can I improve?*

## Serving Beyond the Walls

Christian service takes many forms. In church context, we welcome each other and serve one another. But we must look beyond our walls. Who volunteers at soup kitchens? The Salvos? Who teaches Scripture classes? Christians look for opportunities to serve.

Consider World Student Union (WSU) if you're interested in mission work. They operate in Japan, Finland, Vietnam, France, India—wherever international students gather. The ministry provides dinner and conversation, and if natural, shares about church and Jesus. The nations are here. There's no point having lofty goals about reaching distant lands when the world has come to us.

We do well to follow the Thessalonian example—to be models of service, endurance, and faith.
