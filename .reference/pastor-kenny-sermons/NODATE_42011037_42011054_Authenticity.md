# Luke 11:37-54

**Title:** Authenticity
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Problem: Living Authentically in a Fake World

We live in a culture that loves authenticity yet is surrounded by counterfeits. We celebrate authentic food, authentic experiences, authentic people—yet we simultaneously despise fakery in all its forms. We hate fake news, fake people on social media, and the performative dishonesty of those around us.

Jesus calls us to something radically different. In Luke 11, He addresses two groups whose inauthenticity had poisoned their entire approach to God: the Pharisees and the experts in the law. Their failures reveal what Jesus demands of His followers: genuine, undivided hearts aligned with truth.

---

## Part 1: The Legalists (Luke 11:37-44)

### The Invitation and the Offense

> When Jesus had finished speaking, a Pharisee invited him to eat with him; so he went in and reclined at the table. But the Pharisee was surprised when he noticed that Jesus did not first wash before the meal.

An incident occurs that reveals the Pharisee's fundamental misunderstanding of holiness. The Pharisees were a Jewish sect devoted to purity and separation—"separated ones" who wanted to keep their nation distinct and holy unto God. Their intention was admirable, but their execution became toxic.

### The 613 Commandments Problem

The Pharisees had isolated 613 specific commandments from the Torah. To ensure no one could violate them, they developed elaborate additional rules for every conceivable circumstance. For the Sabbath commandment alone, they created 39 additional principles governing what constituted "work." For ritual washing, they developed intricate rules about which water, how far up the wrist, what happens if water flows back—creating a labyrinth of regulations around what should have been simple hygiene.

The washing issue wasn't about cleanliness. It was about ritual purity. Jewish leaders taught that hands, constantly touching a sinful world, needed washing before eating to prevent spiritual contamination. But Jesus saw through their practice to the heart beneath.

### Clean Outside, Dirty Inside

> Then the Lord said to him, "Now then, you Pharisees clean the outside of the cup and dish, but inside you are full of greed and wickedness."

Jesus uses the cup as a metaphor—scrupulously clean on the outside, filthy within. The Pharisees kept the external rules perfectly:

- They tithed down to the smallest herb, giving God's portion of mint, rue, and garden herbs
- They held the most important seats in synagogues
- They received respectful greetings in the marketplace

Yet they were profoundly inconsistent:

> Woe to you Pharisees, because you give God a tenth of your mint, rue and all other kinds of garden herbs, but you neglect justice and the love of God. You should have practiced the latter without leaving the former undone.

They obsessed over a leaf while ignoring justice and mercy. They were unbalanced—their whole lives testified to a fundamental disorder between what they appeared to be and what they actually were.

### God Cares About Your Whole Life

> You foolish people! Did not the one who made the outside make the inside also?

This is the pivot. God doesn't care only about your public performance. He created your entire being—the visible and the invisible, the noticed and the private. He cares whether:

- Your actions align with your words
- Your ethics remain consistent week to week, not swayed by emotion or peer pressure
- You follow both the letter *and* the spirit of God's law
- You balance ceremonial precision with genuine compassion

### The Grave Warning

> Woe to you, because you are like unmarked graves, which people walk over without knowing it.

Using the Pharisees' own framework against them: to touch a grave meant spiritual uncleanness. By being inauthentic, by maintaining a facade of holiness while their hearts harbored greed and wickedness, the Pharisees unknowingly exposed others to true spiritual contamination. They were walking tombs—outwardly respectable, inwardly dead.

## Part 2: The Legislators (Luke 11:45-54)

### The Experts in the Law Respond

> One of the experts in the law answered him, "Teacher, when you say these things, you insult us also."

The scribes—the professional lawyers of Israel—objected. While similar to the Pharisees in their devotion to the law, they held a distinct role: they were the professionals who had dedicated their entire careers to copying, interpreting, and teaching the law. If Pharisees were the practitioners, scribes were the theorists and authorities.

### Burdens Rather Than Freedom

> And you experts in the law, woe to you, because you load people down with burdens they can hardly carry, and you yourselves will not lift one finger to help them.

The scribes had transformed God's law into an impossible weight. Their Sabbath regulations, their tithing requirements, their ceremonial rules—these created a burden that crushed the very people they claimed to shepherd. Worse, they refused to help anyone carry the weight they had created.

### The Nature of Law

Laws, by their nature, must be general. They cannot regulate the microcosm of human behavior. The Bible itself teaches this principle: the Ten Commandments reduce to two—love God, love your neighbor—with case law providing examples and exceptions.

The Pharisees and scribes believed that more laws would produce holiness. If we had enough regulations, they reasoned, people would be generous, kind, and just. But this fundamentally misunderstands what law can accomplish. Laws regulate behavior externally; they cannot transform desire, passion, motivation, or the human heart. The law can guide, but only the revelation of God can make a people genuinely good.

### Killing the Messengers

> Woe to you, because you build tombs for the prophets, and it was your ancestors who killed them. So you testify that you approve of what your ancestors did; they killed the prophets, and you build their tombs.

This is devastating irony. The scribes honored the prophets of old with monuments, yet they rejected those who proclaimed the same message in their own day. They built tombs for dead prophets while silencing living ones. And in Jesus's day, they had already killed John the Baptist and were plotting Jesus's death.

Their hypocrisy was complete: honoring the dead while murdering the living, all while claiming to serve God's law.

### Seizing the Key to Knowledge

> Woe to you experts in the law, because you have taken away the key to knowledge. You yourselves have not entered, and you have hindered those who were entering.

The key to knowledge is Jesus Himself. He is the one who opens access to God, who gives us the knowledge of salvation through forgiveness of sins. But the scribes, in their obsession with rules and regulations, had blocked this access. They had not entered the kingdom themselves, and they actively prevented others from entering.

### The Response

> When Jesus went outside, the Pharisees and the teachers of the law began to oppose him fiercely and to besiege him with questions, waiting to catch him in something he might say.

Rather than repent, they attacked. They sought to trap Him in His words, planning the very thing Jesus had predicted: they would kill the messenger just as their ancestors had killed the prophets.

## The Call to Authenticity

### The Universal Problem

We are all Pharisees and scribes in different measures. We all struggle with inauthenticity. We present one face publicly while harboring different thoughts privately. We perform for acceptance—adopting accepted political discourse, answering questions "correctly" to fit into society, virtue signaling our righteousness while our lives tell a different story.

Consider the hypocrisy of our age: those who lecture about climate change while flying private jets and renting superyachts; politicians who claim to believe in housing affordability while preventing development; influencers who preach one message while living another.

### One Who Was Truly Authentic

But there was one truly good human: the Lord Jesus. Surrounded by the elites of His society—the politicians, the religious authorities, the power brokers of His day—facing tremendous pressure to conform, He refused to compromise. He rebuked them directly and stayed authentic amongst a roomful of fakes.

### What Jesus Demands

Jesus calls us to align our actions with our minds, our public ethics with our private ones. He demands consistency—not perfection, but honesty. He wants us to care about both the rules we keep and the people we neglect. He insists that we cannot separate our outward performance from our inner reality.

As we enter a new season, we have a second chance at authenticity. Let us make sure our actions and our convictions sync up. Let us follow Jesus not as legalists obsessed with rules, not as legislators imposing burdens, but as genuine disciples who have allowed the resurrection life of Christ to transform us from the inside out.

The resurrected Jesus speaks to us still through His Word. The question remains: Will we hear? Will we answer? Will we be authentic?
