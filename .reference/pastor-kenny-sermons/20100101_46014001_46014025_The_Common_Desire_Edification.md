## Why We Gather: The Purpose of Church

Before we dive into this chapter, we need to answer a foundational question: What is the purpose of church? Why do we come together? We might gather for social reasons, because our parents made us, to please someone, or simply because we're bored. But Paul addresses something far more essential.

Over the past weeks, we've been working through the exercise of spiritual gifts—what I call "breath-gifts" or word-speaking gifts from chapter 12. This is the framework Paul builds on:

**We are united by one Spirit → We hear one gospel → We speak and confess Jesus as Lord → Each of us possesses spiritual gifts → These gifts exist for the common good of the body → Love guides how we use them → The result is intelligent edification of the church**

Each gift matters because every member of the body matters. We're not exercising gifts for ourselves alone, but for the collective good of the whole. Love directs and shapes our gifting, motivating us to think not just of our own benefit, but what's right and good for everyone.

Chapter 14 carries this argument forward, focusing specifically on word-speaking gifts and showing us what loving, effective use looks like.

## 1. Desiring Prophecy: The Superior Gift

> Follow the way of love and eagerly desire the greater gifts, especially the gift of prophecy. (1 Corinthians 14:1)

### What Is Prophecy?

The word literally means "to speak before," but this needs unpacking. Prophecy appears about 25 distinct times in the New Testament, and half of those references are right here in 1 Corinthians 11-14. So we're working with limited examples, and interpretations vary widely.

Some understand prophecy as **foretelling**—predicting the future. In the Old Testament, prophets like Jeremiah and Isaiah predicted foreign invasions and exile. One test of a true prophet was whether predictions came to pass. Some argue this continues today through certain Pentecostal movements claiming words of knowledge or revelation ("You'll marry someone this year," "A great job is coming"). But these sound suspiciously like fortune-telling.

Others (cessationists) argue that foretelling doesn't exist today because the canon is closed. If it did exist, it would only be about Christ's return—"Everything is ending; trust me."

But there's a third option: **forthtelling**—speaking forth what God has already said. This means proclaiming God's mind, reminding God's people of His truth and character. Many Old Testament prophets did this: calling Israel back to faithfulness, reminding them of what God had done in Egypt and at Sinai. The New Testament prophets who founded the church through Gospel preaching were forthtellers. By extension, this is what we'd call preaching today.

**My honest assessment:** I doubt that God spontaneously gives new revelations from heaven apart from Scripture—Hebrews 1:1 suggests that era has closed. Some people may be intuitively gifted, but I'm skeptical of claims beyond that. I lean toward understanding prophecy as forthtelling: the intelligent proclamation of the Gospel that calls people back to God's Word.

The beautiful thing is that we don't need to know the precise mechanics of prophecy to understand Paul's main point:

**Prophecy comes from the Spirit (the Gospel) → and results in edification**

## 2. Edification: Building Up God's People

### The Central Metaphor

Paul emphasizes edification throughout chapter 14:

> Everyone who prophesies speaks to people for their strengthening, encouragement and comfort. (verse 3)

> He who prophesies edifies the church. (verse 4)

> Since you are eager for gifts of the Spirit, try to excel in those that build up the church. (verse 12)

Strengthening, edifying, building up—these all translate the same powerful biblical metaphor for what God does with His people.

Jesus said: *"I will build my church, and the gates of Hades will not overcome it."* (Matthew 16:18)

Paul describes the church as *"God's household, built on the foundation of the apostles and prophets, with Christ Jesus himself as the chief cornerstone. In him the whole building is joined together and rises to become a holy temple in the Lord."* (Ephesians 2:19-22)

### How Does God Build?

He does it **through words**. Paul tells us to speak *"the truth in love"* so that *"the whole body, joined and held together by every supporting ligament, grows and builds itself up in love, as each part does its work."* (Ephesians 4:15-16)

Paul's ambition was to *"preach the gospel where Christ was not known, so that I would not be building on someone else's foundation."* (Romans 15:20)

At Pentecost, Peter committed the new believers to *"the word of his grace, which can build you up and give you an inheritance among all those who are sanctified."* (Acts 20:32)

Even in Jeremiah, God says: *"I have put my words in your mouth. See, I appoint you over nations and kingdoms to uproot and tear down, to destroy and overthrow, to build and to plant."* (Jeremiah 1:9-10)

### Application: What Edification Looks Like Today

Prophecy—the intelligent proclamation of God's Gospel—is how we build each other up. This includes:

- Speaking God's Word to one another
- Reading Scripture together
- Speaking truth in love
- Encouraging one another to persevere
- Comforting each other when life doesn't work out
- These are the breath-gifts that build us all up

**The priorities of our church gatherings should reflect this:**

1. **Bible and talk** – My attempt to build you up through God's Word
2. **Intentional fellowship** – Edification through honest, Gospel-centered conversation
3. **Conversations extend beyond Sunday** – Real discipleship happens in ongoing dialogue
4. **Bible and prayer groups** – Come to talk to each other about Jesus
5. **But make it count** – Don't just catch up; get to real things. How's your walk with God? How's your Bible reading? What spiritual struggles are you facing? These are awkward questions, but they matter. Get to the point!
6. **Talk about your decisions** – I think we're far too private about this. What does God want for you? What's the loving, right choice?
7. **Share what you're learning** – Tell me something the Bible taught you this week. Tell me how it changed you. That encourages me. Challenge me toward godliness. When we disagree, let's work it out together, seeking God's truth in love.
8. **Use your words for good** – Speaking must build, never tear down. No gossip, no unnecessary criticism, no discouragement. Be safe with each other. Use your words to strengthen.
9. **Our shared vision** – Hear God's Word together, apply it to our hearts, tell others about it, ask each other about it, discuss it, pray together in thankfulness and for our struggles.
10. **For our young people** – Is this not better than playing handball alone? Stay a bit longer after church. Ask each other about your Christian lives. Pray for one another. Then go play handball—but keep talking about what it means to follow Jesus at school and how difficult that is.
11. **Don't leave early** – The best parts often come later.
12. **If you're afraid to talk** – I know it's hard. But we have to get there somehow, or else we're missing what church is meant to be. It's like going to the gym and never actually lifting the weights.

## 3. The Problem With Tongues: Unintelligibility

Paul contrasts prophecy with speaking in tongues, and the comparison is stark:

> Anyone who speaks in a tongue does not speak to people but to God. No one understands him; he utters mysteries with his spirit. But everyone who prophesies speaks to people for their strengthening, encouragement and comfort. He who speaks in a tongue edifies himself, but he who prophesies edifies the church. (1 Corinthians 14:2-4)

### What Are Tongues?

In Pentecostal and charismatic churches, tongues are often understood as ecstatic utterance—a special, supernatural language. But Acts 2 suggests something different. When the Holy Spirit came at Pentecost:

> They saw what seemed to be tongues of fire that separated and came to rest on each of them. All of them were filled with the Holy Spirit and began to speak in other tongues as the Spirit enabled them. Now there were staying in Jerusalem God-fearing Jews from every nation under heaven. When they heard this sound, a crowd came together in bewilderment, because each one heard them speaking in his own language. (Acts 2:3-6)

The word "tongues" most naturally means **languages**—intelligible languages that people could understand. Peter and the disciples suddenly spoke in the native languages of the pilgrims who heard them.

Whether we interpret tongues as unintelligible utterance or as foreign languages, Paul's critique is the same: **the problem is unintelligibility**.

### Why Tongues Don't Build Up the Church

> Now, brothers, if I come to you and speak in tongues, what good will I be to you, unless I bring you some revelation or knowledge or prophecy or word of instruction? (1 Corinthians 14:6)

Paul illustrates this with music. If a flute or harp makes sounds but with no distinct melody, how will anyone know what tune is being played? If a trumpet doesn't sound a clear call, who will prepare for battle? No one can respond to what they don't understand.

> Unless you speak intelligible words with your tongue, how will anyone know what you are saying? You will just be speaking into the air. (1 Corinthians 14:9)

If I don't understand what someone is saying, we're strangers to each other. There's no communication, no edification, no building up.

The deeper problem: **speaking in unintelligible tongues at church is doing your own thing while ignoring the community.**

If you want to pray in a language others don't understand, that's between you and God—pray alone. Don't do it in a corporate setting where others need to be edified. It's like trying to eat alone at a shared meal, or closing your eyes and disconnecting from the group during corporate worship. As much as it might feel spiritually intimate, you're neglecting the others present.

> For if I pray in a tongue, my spirit prays, but my mind is unfruitful. So what shall I do? I will pray with my spirit, but I will also pray with my mind; I will sing with my spirit, but I will also sing with my mind. If you are praising God with your spirit, how can one who finds himself among those who do not understand say "Amen" to your thanksgiving, since he does not know what you are saying? You may be giving thanks well enough, but the other person is not edified. (1 Corinthians 14:14-17)

Paul's principle is clear: **intelligibility matters more than personal spiritual experience when the church is gathered**.

### The Deeper Problem: Tongues Reverses Pentecost

There's a profound theological issue at stake here. Paul writes:

> Brothers, stop thinking like children. In regard to evil be infants, but in your thinking be adults. (1 Corinthians 14:20)

He then quotes Isaiah:

> Through men of strange tongues and through the lips of foreigners I will speak to this people, but even then they will not listen to me, says the Lord. (1 Corinthians 14:21, quoting Isaiah 28:11)

### The Historical Context

In Isaiah's prophecy, foreign tongues were a sign of **judgment**. God was saying He would send a foreign army to invade Israel—and the appearance of foreign languages would be the sign of defeat. When you heard an enemy speaking in his own tongue, you knew you'd lost the war.

History shows this pattern: the Greeks brought Greek, the Romans brought Latin, the French brought French. Whatever language conquers you determines which language you'll speak. Languages tell you who won the war.

At Babel in Genesis 11, God confused human languages as judgment for human rebellion.

So in Isaiah's context, hearing foreign tongues meant: **You are under God's judgment. The curse of Babel is upon you. You have no access to God's grace.**

### Pentecost as Reversal

But here's the glorious reversal at Pentecost: The Gospel comes in a way that **everyone can understand**, regardless of language or ethnicity.

> A crowd came together in bewilderment, because each one heard them speaking in his own language... "Are not all these men who are speaking Galileans? How is it that each of us hears them in our own native language? Parthians, Medes and Elamites; residents of Mesopotamia, Judea and Cappadocia, Pontus and Asia, Phrygia and Pamphylia, Egypt and the parts of Libya near Cyrene; visitors from Rome; Cretans and Arabs—we hear them declaring the wonders of God in our own tongues!" (Acts 2:6-11)

This is the **reversal of judgment**. Everyone—regardless of their native language or ethnic background—can hear the Gospel in a way they understand. The good news is no longer exclusive; it's accessible to all.

Bibles can be translated. The Gospel reaches every nation. The curse of Babel is undone.

### The Application to Corinth

Paul makes the point sharply:

> So if the whole church comes together and everyone speaks in tongues, and some who do not understand or some unbelievers come in, will they not say that you are out of your mind? (1 Corinthians 14:23)

**If we revert to unintelligible speech, we're reversing Pentecost.** We're making the Gospel exclusive again—accessible only to those who understand our special language or spiritual code. An outsider walks in and thinks the church is crazy. The good news becomes bad news because it's incomprehensible.

But contrast this with what happens when prophecy—intelligible Gospel proclamation—happens:

> But if an unbeliever or someone who does not understand comes in while everybody is prophesying, he will be convinced by all that he is a sinner and will be judged by all, and the secrets of his heart will be laid bare. So he will fall down and worship God, exclaiming, "God is really among you!" (1 Corinthians 14:24-25)

**True Pentecost happens when people hear the truth of the Gospel and repent.** God is really among you because His Word is intelligible, convicting, and transformative.

## The Importance of Intelligibility in Our Meetings

Paul summarizes his argument powerfully:

> I thank God that I speak in tongues more than all of you. But in the church I would rather speak five intelligible words to instruct others than ten thousand words in a tongue. (1 Corinthians 14:18-19)

It's like comparing five cents of actual value to ten thousand cents that are worthless. **Intelligibility is worth far more than impressive-sounding spirituality.**

### Order and Peace

For the Corinthian church, this meant structure:

> When you come together, everyone has a hymn, or a word of instruction, a revelation, a tongue or an interpretation. All of these must be done for the strengthening of the church. If anyone speaks in a tongue, two or at the most three should speak, one at a time, and someone must interpret. If there is no interpreter, the speaker should keep quiet in the church and speak to himself and God. (1 Corinthians 14:26-28)

> God is not a God of disorder but of peace. (1 Corinthians 14:33)

### What Does Intelligibility Look Like for Us?

For Cherrybrook, intelligibility means speaking a language and using cultural expressions that suit **our people** and the **people we're trying to reach**:

- **Language:** English—but more than just words; our whole mode of communication
- **Culture:** Asian. We speak with meta-narratives, common references to food, family, shared experience
- **Young people:** We try to suit them too

This is why combined church services between different congregations are difficult. It's not that we don't desire unity—it's that intelligibility costs something. When we merge services:

- We meet in an unfamiliar location
- The cultural references shift
- The music style changes
- The rhythm feels different

A first-time visitor walks in and thinks, "Who are all these people?" The barriers go up. The Gospel becomes less clear, not more. We're experiencing a temporary reversal of Pentecost.

**For the sake of Pentecost—for the sake of making the Gospel clear and accessible—we keep our gatherings intelligible to our own community.** This isn't sectarianism; it's Gospel stewardship.

## Conclusion: Intelligent Edification

> Therefore, my brothers, be eager to prophesy, and do not forbid speaking in tongues. But everything should be done in a fitting and orderly way. (1 Corinthians 14:39-40)

Paul doesn't forbid tongues. But he reorders priorities. **Prophecy—the intelligent proclamation of the Gospel—is the superior gift** because it builds others up.

Why do we gather? **For the sake of edification. Which means everything must be intelligible. This is what love looks like—order so that understanding happens.**

This year, speak the spirituals to one another:

- In one-to-one conversations
- In emails and notes about God's work in your life
- In Bible studies
- In church

**Build each other up through the intelligent proclamation of God's Word.** That's what the church is for. That's what we're here to do.
