# Colossians 1:15-23

**Title:** Supreme Over All  
**Date:** 1 May 2022  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Image of the Invisible God

> The Son is the image (eikon) of the invisible God, the firstborn over all creation. (1:15)

Jesus is supremely over all creation. The word "image" comes from the Greek *eikon*—literally an icon. Think of an icon on your computer desktop: you cannot access the program without clicking on it. Similarly, we cannot see God, who is spirit and exists in unapproachable light. Yet through Jesus, God made Himself visible and accessible to us.

This is revolutionary. Throughout history, God revealed Himself in shadows and hints: through angels, wind, fire, voices, and the words of prophets. These were incomplete appearances, accommodations to human understanding. But with Jesus, everything changed.

> In the past God spoke to our ancestors through the prophets at many times and in various ways, but in these last days he has spoken to us by his Son... The Son is the radiance of God's glory and the exact representation of his being. (Hebrews 1:1-3)

Jesus is not a partial revelation of God. He is not a counterfeit or mere shadow. Jesus is the complete, exact representation of God's being. All of God's fullness dwells in Him bodily. When Philip asked Jesus to show him the Father, Jesus replied simply: "Anyone who has seen me has seen the Father" (John 14:9).

For our sake, God who is Spirit became flesh so we could hear His voice and feel His love in person. If you had lived in first-century Palestine and touched Jesus, you would have touched God Himself. Yet because Jesus became mortal—localized in a specific time and place—His words and actions are now recorded in Scripture so we can still encounter Him through history. Or we can wait until judgment day, but by then it will be too late.

## Firstborn Over All Creation

> The Son is... the firstborn over all creation. (1:15)

"Firstborn" does not refer to biological birth. God is invisible and pre-biological; chronological sequence does not apply. Rather, *firstborn* denotes rank, authority, and legitimacy.

In Scripture, Israel is called God's "firstborn among the nations" (Exodus 4:22), not because they were chronologically first, but because they held a position of honor. A king's firstborn need not be his first biological son—he could have had earlier children through mistresses or concubines. But the firstborn of the king and queen holds the legitimate right to the throne. It is a title of supremacy.

Jesus is firstborn over the old creation and over the new creation. As the firstborn, He inherits and rules over all that exists or ever could exist. We ourselves are part of this reality: believers are called "the church of the firstborn" (Hebrews 12:23), sharing in His dignity and inheritance.

## Creator and Sustainer of All Things

> For in him all things were created: things in heaven and on earth, visible and invisible, whether thrones or powers or rulers or authorities; all things have been created through him and for him. (1:16)

The text uses merisms—pairs of opposites that together mean "everything": heaven and earth, visible and invisible. Creation was accomplished *through* Him and *for* Him. He is the source and the goal of all existence.

> He is before all things, and in him all things hold together. (1:17)

This describes an unsolved mystery of physics: the universe is held together by four fundamental forces—gravity, electromagnetism, and strong and weak nuclear forces. Each is finely tuned to an extraordinary degree. Increase gravity slightly and the universe collapses; decrease it and no planets form. Jesus is the invisible hand fine-tuning these forces, holding the cosmos in cohesion.

Imagine everything orbiting around Jesus like planets around the sun. Or better yet, picture a black hole—such tremendous mass compressed into such a small volume that space itself curves around it. Everything slowly spirals inward, unable to escape once it crosses a certain threshold. This is Jesus' supremacy: the planets, weather systems, electrons spinning, quantum strings vibrating—all of it revolves around Him.

## Supreme Over Humanity and History

> Whether thrones or powers or rulers or authorities; all things have been created through him and for him. (1:16)

Jesus rules not only over creation but over human government and earthly powers. When elections arrive and we feel forced to choose between imperfect options, remember: God will not allow human rebellion to continue forever. Governments are not outside His orbit. Nor are we.

> And he is the head of the body, the church; he is the beginning and the firstborn from among the dead, so that in everything he might have the supremacy. (1:18)

Jesus has a special concern for His people, the church. He is not merely the ruler of creation but the head of His body. Everywhere He walked during His earthly ministry, people were drawn to Him—like a first-century version of Elon Musk's gravity, drawing everyone into His orbit.

## Reconciliation Through His Blood

> For God was pleased to have all his fullness dwell in him, and through him to reconcile to himself all things, whether things on earth or things in heaven, by making peace through his blood, shed on the cross. (1:19-20)

Jesus' intention is to bring everything back into right relationship with God. He accomplishes this through reconciliation—literally, making peace. But this peace comes at a cost.

Elon Musk wanted to colonize Mars, so he started SpaceX in 2002. He wanted to revolutionize electric cars, so he invested in Tesla. He wanted to transform social media, so he spent $44 billion to purchase Twitter. What's remarkable is that Musk didn't ask the government to do it. He didn't complain or protest. He did it himself, paying the cost from his own wealth.

Jesus loves us with incomparably greater love. He doesn't demand that others reconcile us to God—He does it Himself, paying the price with His own blood. He was willing to become human, to suffer, to die. This is the ultimate expression of love for His enemies.

> Once you were alienated from God and were enemies in your minds because of your evil behavior. But now he has reconciled you by Christ's physical body through death to present you holy in his sight, without blemish and free from accusation. (1:21-22)

We were enemies of God, alienated from Him by our sinful choices. Yet Jesus reached out to us, purchased our reconciliation, and now presents us before the Father as holy and blameless. The debt has been paid.

## Remaining Faithful to the Gospel

> If you continue in your faith, established and firm, and do not move from the hope held out in the gospel... This is the gospel that you heard and that has been proclaimed to every creature under heaven. (1:23)

The word "continue" means to remain, to stay in place. Having been reconciled, we must not abandon course by launching our own rockets in the wrong direction. Our responsibility is to keep the gospel as supreme in our lives—to keep putting Jesus first.

This is difficult. We are naturally drawn toward other forces: the planet of materialism, the star of careerism, the moon of sentimentalism. But the gravitational pull of Christ's love draws us back. We must consciously choose to stay on course.

## Keeping Christ First in All of Life

Keeping Christ first means making Him primary in every area: marriage, finances, work, school, emotions, desires, ambitions, and dreams. All of these are visible or invisible realities that must submit to Christ.

This is why the church emphasizes spiritual discipline—faithful attendance, honoring commitments, studying God's Word. Some ask: Why so strict? Isn't Christianity about grace?

You are right that grace saves us. Christ has already paid for our sins and reconciled us through His blood. Church attendance does not save us. But these disciplines are not ultimately about church—they are about training ourselves to put Christ at the center of everything.

The church is the one place where we can receive honest feedback and accountability on our spiritual lives. At work, no one cares about your walk with God. At school, no one asks. In your room alone, there is no one to correct you. But in community, we can help one another grow.

If we cannot give up a couple hours on a relaxing weekend for God, how will we give our entire lives to Him when we are truly busy? If we cannot study Scripture as students with only two or three classes a day, how will we find time when we have demanding careers? If we cannot give 1% of our income to God, how will we give more? If we cannot remain faithful to small commitments, how will we be faithful to a marriage for fifty years?

> Let us consider how we may spur one another on toward love and good deeds, not giving up meeting together, as some are in the habit of doing, but encouraging one another. (Hebrews 10:24-25)

The church is not a place of guilt and condemnation. It is a place where we humbly acknowledge our failings, call one another to repentance, and assure one another of forgiveness. We are training ourselves—and training the next generation—to live lives orbiting around Christ rather than around ourselves.

Every day, a little of our old, selfish nature dies. The unfaithful part, the greedy part, the corrupt part fades. And a little more of Christ grows in us, bearing fruit in good works.

## Conclusion: Christ Supreme Over All

> Now I rejoice in what I am suffering for you, and I fill up in my flesh what is still lacking in regard to Christ's afflictions, for the sake of his body, which is the church... Christ in you, the hope of glory. (1:24, 27)

The media may not recognize Jesus' supremacy. In Hollywood, His name is used only as profanity. But reality tells a different story: everything is gravitating toward Him. For those rebelling against Him, you will be crushed as the universe collapses around you. But for those who remain faithful to the gospel, who keep Christ first in all things, there is assurance and comfort. You will never be let go.

If Jesus is supreme in all creation, then He must be supreme in our lives.
