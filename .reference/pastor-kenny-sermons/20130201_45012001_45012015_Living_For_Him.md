# Romans 12:1-15

**Title:** Living For Him
**Date:** 24 February 2013
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction: Why We Come to Church

Most people say we come to church to worship God. But what does worship really mean? In modern usage, we often think of it as something religious—prayer, singing, or watching something happen. Yet Paul calls us to a very different kind of worship in Romans 12.

Paul invites us to offer three essential things to God:
1. Our bodies
2. Our minds
3. Our service

This is true spiritual worship.

## 1. Offer Your Bodies (v. 1)

> Therefore, I urge you, brothers, in view of God's mercy, to offer your bodies as living sacrifices, holy and pleasing to God—this is your spiritual act of worship. (Romans 12:1)

### Understanding "In View of God's Mercy"

Throughout Romans, Paul has traced the gospel narrative. In Romans 1, we heard the bad news: we are all condemned because of our sin. By Romans 3, Paul unveiled the good news: a new righteousness from God is available to us. In Romans 5, our assurance of forgiveness is grounded in Christ's death for us while we were still sinners. And in Romans 9, that assurance is deepened—it rests on a God in control who predestined us to mercy out of His grace.

Now, in light of all this mercy, Paul calls us to respond by offering our bodies as sacrifices.

### The Metaphor of Sacrifice

Paul uses Old Testament language here, but as a metaphor for Christian life today. In the Old Testament, worship meant serving in the temple, where bulls and goats were sacrificed. But everything has changed.

Jesus has already sacrificed His body for our sakes. He is the sacrificial lamb of God. So the sacrifices we offer today are different: they are *living* sacrifices. No person or animal dies. Instead, we offer ourselves daily—giving up selfishness, surrendering our own interests for the sake of others.

This is the crucial difference. Dead sacrifices in the Old Testament were offered once. Living sacrifices in the Christian life are offered continuously. Every day, we choose to lay down our lives in small ways—in how we treat others, in what we prioritize, in how we spend our time and energy.

This is the worship God desires. He wants our bodies offered to Him daily.

## 2. Renew Your Mind (v. 2)

> Do not conform any longer to the pattern of this world, but be transformed by the renewing of your mind. Then you will be able to test and approve what God's will is—his good, pleasing and perfect will. (Romans 12:2)

### Transform Your Thinking

We are called to offer our minds to God by transforming how we think. Before coming to Christ, we followed the patterns of the world. Perhaps we were superstitious, following tea leaves, zodiac signs, or feng shui. Perhaps we relied entirely on our feelings and emotions to make decisions. Paul says we must use our minds.

When we renew our thinking, we become able to discern God's will. Notice that Paul calls our worship "spiritual" in verse 1—but the word here means *logical*. God wants our logical, rational, considered, thought-out worship. We are guided by the Word—the Bible—a text that can be studied, memorized, thought about, and read in context.

### Why We Gather to Study Scripture

This is why we read the Bible together. This is why we have Bible study, Sunday school, and sermons at church. Every time we gather, there is an expectation: we will change our minds. We will be transformed by encountering God's Word.

Think about it this way. If you attended a university lecture without bringing a textbook or notes, that would be strange. If you went to an important business meeting without bringing relevant documents, that would be peculiar. Yet how strange it is when we come to church and don't bring our Bibles! When we gather to encounter the living God through His Word, we should come prepared to study, to read, to open Scripture and test and approve God's will for ourselves.

To "approve" God's will doesn't mean to evaluate or judge God. It means to accept, understand, and discern His will—to figure out what God is calling us to do.

### Christianity: A Religion of the Mind

In my experience, some Christians want to be Christians without the mind. They say, "I just want to feel God, and that will be enough. I don't want to think too hard." But this misunderstands Christianity's entire history.

Christianity has always been a religion of the mind. Where Christianity has spread, superstition has subsided and learning has flourished. The monasteries were the last preserves of learning during the Dark Ages. The Reformation drove the development of the printing press. The original universities—Cambridge, Oxford, Harvard, Princeton—began as schools to train church ministers. Nearly every one of the first 123 colleges and universities in the United States has Christian origins.

Even the University of Hong Kong has Christian roots. It began with a School of Medicine founded in 1887 by the London Missionary Society as the Hong Kong College of Medicine for Chinese. Its first graduate in 1892 was Sun Yat-sen. Christians founded Hong Kong University!

In Australia, the first schoolhouse was erected by Rev. Richard Johnson, the colony's first Chaplain, in 1793. The majority of schools in the infant colony were started by clergymen and supported by missionary societies. This reflects a worldwide movement of church-based schools that developed in the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries.

Why all this emphasis on learning? Because Christianity desires truth and knowledge. As knowledge of God grows, so does knowledge in all other areas—medicine, science, languages, and beyond.

### Application: The Long View

You cannot be a mature Christian without engaging your mind. This means persevering with Adult Sunday School, with Bible study, with your own personal reading at home, and with coming to church.

I know that sometimes sermons are boring. Sometimes they run too long; sometimes too short. But I want to assure you that despite all of that, little by little, your mind will be changed. As you expose yourself to reading the Bible, your thoughts and attitudes will transform.

When I was a young boy, I attended many sermons. I don't remember any of them specifically. But I do remember that even though they sometimes felt boring, long, or short, I was changed over time by them. It's like raindrops on a rock. In the short term, it's hard to see whether the drops make any difference. But over a long time, with regularity—week by week, with perseverance in understanding—it does make a difference.

If you have children in Sunday school or youth group, it is important they attend regularly. Week by week, over years, their minds will be changed to follow Christ and conform to His likeness. Treat each Sunday like a week of school. If your child misses one Sunday, it's equivalent to missing five days of school—there are only so many youth group meetings in a year. Let them miss only for genuine emergencies or truly important things, things you would take them out of five days of school for.

Even when they encounter doubts later in life, they will fall back on what they've learned, week in and week out. That foundation matters.

## 3. Serve One Another (vv. 3-8, 9-13)

With a renewed mind and a willingness to offer our bodies, we are now ready to serve one another.

> Just as each of us has one body with many members, and these members do not all have the same function, so in Christ we who are many form one body, and each member belongs to all the others. (Romans 12:4-5)

Each of us has a role to play in the body of Christ. Paul lists the diverse gifts God has given:

> We have different gifts, according to the grace given us. If a man's gift is prophesying, let him use it in proportion to his faith. If it is serving, let him serve; if it is teaching, let him teach; if it is encouraging, let him encourage; if it is contributing to the needs of others, let him give generously; if it is leadership, let him govern diligently; if it is showing mercy, let him do it cheerfully. (Romans 12:6-8)

### How We Serve

There are many ways to serve in our church community. The basic ones include being present, welcoming each other, helping with morning tea, and setting up or packing up equipment. Others emerge as the committee asks from time to time. Beyond our congregation, we can participate in community outreach—at Eastwood Carols, through SMAC's kids club over the summer, or through Anglicare's Toys 'n Tucker program, bringing donations of tinned food or toys.

But service goes deeper than these activities. Paul continues:

> Love must be sincere. Hate what is evil; cling to what is good. Be devoted to one another in brotherly love. Honor one another above yourselves. Never be lacking in zeal, but keep your spiritual fervor, serving the Lord. (Romans 12:9-11)

> Share with God's people who are in need. Practice hospitality. (Romans 12:13)

## Conclusion: True Worship

So why do we come to church? We come to worship God by renewing our minds with His Word and by offering our bodies in service to one another. We are like a family where everyone is committed and involved in serving each other—where we talk to each other, where there is love, where we live out our calling together.

This is the rhythm of all of life, and it is the heart of what we do at church. This is true worship.

For the remainder of this day, let us not take our time together for granted. We are blessed to have each other. So let us love one another. Let us encourage each other with God's Word.
