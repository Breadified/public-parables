# 1 Peter 2:12-25

**Title:** Alien Submission
**Date:** November 6, 2011
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Challenge: How Do Aliens Witness?

Last week we learned that Christians are a chosen people, a royal priesthood, a holy nation, a people belonging to God. We exist to declare the praises of him who called us out of darkness into his wonderful light. This declaration—this proclamation of the gospel—is our evangelistic calling.

But what happens when circumstances make this declaration nearly impossible?

- When governments prohibit the proclamation of God's praises
- When employers prevent us from living out our faith
- When persecution makes witnessing dangerous
- When power imbalances create barriers we cannot overcome

The question becomes: How do we fulfill our calling when the world actively silences us?

### Historical Context: Persecution and Oppression

**China Under Communism**

In 1949, the People's Republic of China was established under Communist ideology. Religion was systematically discouraged by the state. Mao declared missionaries "spiritual aggressors" and expelled them. During the Cultural Revolution (1966-1976), religious expression was effectively banned. Believers were arrested, imprisoned, tortured, and executed for their faith. Bibles were destroyed, churches looted, and Christians subjected to humiliation. Between 1983-1993, thousands of Christians were imprisoned simply for their beliefs.

**Rome Under Nero**

In 64 AD, Emperor Nero blamed Christians for the fire of Rome. He tortured and executed them with unprecedented brutality. Historical accounts describe cities littered with corpses of men and women, young and old together. Nero lined Christians upon stakes in his garden and burned them as living torches for light. The horror was so severe that Christians believed Nero himself was the Antichrist.

### The Core Problem

Aliens with a message face a critical weakness: they exist in the minority and possess no earthly power. How can the powerless make themselves heard in a hostile world?

## 1. Creating Opportunities Through Good Works (v. 12)

Peter offers a surprising solution—not protest, not revolution, not self-immolation, but something far more radical: *live good lives*.

> Live such good lives among the pagans that, though they accuse you of doing wrong, by seeing your good deeds they may glorify God on the day he visits us. (1 Peter 2:12)

### What Does "Good Life" Mean?

It does not mean:
- Pursuing the "Australian Dream" of material comfort and entertainment
- Accumulating wealth and status to prove God's blessing
- Displaying worldly success as evidence of divine favor

Peter clarifies in verse 11:

> Dear friends, I urge you, as aliens and strangers in the world, to abstain from sinful desires, which war against your soul. (1 Peter 2:11)

The good life means **moral distinction**. Christians must continue to be visibly, noticeably different in their values and conduct. Evidence from 1 Peter 4:4 reveals the pagans' complaint:

> They think it strange that you do not plunge with them into the same flood of dissipation, and they heap abuse on you.

Pagans were disturbed by Christian morality. Peter's call is to persist in good deeds despite slander, knowing that pagans will eventually acknowledge the goodness they reject today. On the day of judgment, when Christ returns to visit and oversee all things, the vindication will be complete. Those who dismissed Christian virtue will finally see it clearly.

### Two Results of Living Good Lives

**First: Silencing Ignorant Talk (v. 15)**

> For it is God's will that by doing good you should silence the ignorant talk of foolish men. (1 Peter 2:15)

Good works confound critics and render their slander ineffective. When someone accuses Christians of hypocrisy, the remedy is simple: stop being hypocritical. Be humble. Interact with others genuinely. Let them know you are a Christian. Watch as their ignorant accusations lose their power.

**Second: Creating Conversation Opportunities (1 Peter 3:15)**

> But in your hearts set apart Christ as Lord. Always be prepared to give an answer to everyone who asks you to give the reason for the hope that you have. But do this with gentleness and respect. (1 Peter 3:15)

Over time, as your moral consistency silences critics, something remarkable happens: people begin asking questions. They notice you are different and want to know why. These conversations become opportunities to declare the gospel.

### The Essential Role of Words

Let me be clear: good works alone cannot save anyone. Salvation comes through the gospel—through *words*. The good news is that salvation is **done**, not **do**. People are saved by hearing about Christ, not by observing Christian charity. An unfortunate phrase has circulated: "Always preach the gospel, and if necessary use words." This is backwards. The correct principle is: **Always preach the gospel, and if you can't speak, live good lives to create opportunities to.**

I fear we too often miss opportunities to speak the gospel directly. But some of you have tried and faced rejection. Perhaps your boss holds power over you, making direct conversation awkward. In those situations, use your life to create openings for conversation. Live differently so people ask why.

### Making Yourself Obviously Different

The problem is that we are too much like pagans. We pursue the same Friday night activities. We dress the same. We spend money on the same things. We study the same subjects. We carry the same ambitions. We travel to the same places. What about us would warrant a second look?

We are alien Christians, and our alienness should be obvious.

**Illustration: Travel and Priorities**

My wife attended a wedding in Hong Kong—leaving Friday and returning Monday. Travel is always interesting conversation, but what shocked people was the brevity. In the world's mind, if you're spending that much money, you stay longer. This sparked genuine curiosity: *Why so short?* It opened the door to discuss her priorities—family obligations at home, her values, her freedom from FOMO because of the resurrection to come.

**Illustration: Family Size**

Having four children brings questions because it's so countercultural. People ask why. It's an opportunity to discuss how God values children, how materialism constrains us, whether life is about self or others. Four kids provoke curiosity—imagine five!

**Illustration: Financial Choices**

A successful person who drives an old car and lives in a modest house will shock people. They ask: Why aren't you enjoying the fruits of your success? That's a moment to explain your values.

**Illustration: Kingdom Priorities**

Instead of trick-or-treating, you invite neighbors to church. Or you celebrate the holiday differently—with cute costumes and giving food away. These choices create conversations.

Many of you are already living differently without fully realizing it: prioritizing church, going on summer missions, attending ISCF. These are visible acts of kingdom allegiance. But as people adjust to seeing you this way, you must continue finding new ways to be different.

**Think intentionally:** What happens regularly in your conversations? What do people ask about—what you study, what you watch, what you buy? In those moments, act according to God's values. Be noticeably different.

## 2. Submission to Authority (v. 13-17)

Peter now qualifies what he means by "good lives": we pursue this differently through **submission to authorities**.

This qualification is crucial. The danger in saying "do the right thing" within a pagan government is that we might conclude this means breaking governmental laws. After all, the pagan government opposes us. We are aliens under another law, so why submit to this one?

But Peter is emphatic:

> Submit yourselves for the Lord's sake to every authority instituted among men: whether to the king, as the supreme authority, or to governors, who are sent by him to punish those who do wrong and to commend those who do right. (1 Peter 2:13-14)

### Government Authority Comes From God

Even imperfect authorities are instituted by God. They are sent by the Lord to maintain order—to punish wrongdoing and commend righteousness. This aligns with Paul's teaching in Romans 13:

> Everyone must submit himself to the governing authorities, for there is no authority except that which God has established. The authorities that exist have been established by God. Consequently, he who rebels against the authority is rebelling against what God has instituted. For rulers hold no terror for those who do right, but for those who do wrong. (Romans 13:1-3)

Even imperfect government is preferable to anarchy. We obey the laws of our nation, kingdom, and empire.

### The Exception Question

Immediately, we ask: But what about extreme situations?

- What if you are persecuted?
- What if you live under Nazi Germany?
- What if an authority commands you to sin?
- Is disobedience acceptable if done nonviolently and you accept the punishment?

Scripture records examples of civil disobedience:
- Daniel refused to pray to Nebuchadnezzar
- Daniel's friends refused to worship the gold statue
- Peter and Paul left prison and continued preaching despite prohibition

Honestly, I don't know what to do in such extreme situations. I've spent two weeks thinking about civil disobedience and still can't arrive at a definitive answer. The literature seems to say "submit" while qualifying it with so many exceptions that I wonder if the principle means anything at all. Perhaps it's more fruitful to recognize that neither Peter nor most of us are in such extreme situations. We have great freedom. The principle is: **don't abuse your freedom as cover for rebellion.**

If an authority genuinely compels you to do what God forbids—to kill, to deny Christ entirely—then you cease obedience and accept the consequences. But we should not make doctrine from rare exceptions.

### Freedom in Submission (v. 16)

> Live as free men, but do not use your freedom as a cover-up for evil; live as servants of God. (1 Peter 2:16)

We are freed in Christ. Galatians 5:1 declares:

> It is for freedom that Christ has set us free. Stand firm, then, and do not let yourselves be burdened again by a yoke of slavery. (Galatians 5:1)

Yet this freedom does not exempt us from submitting to earthly authorities. Through Christ's resurrection, we are lords of the earth—yet we submit to pagan rulers. This freedom must never become a pretext for rebellion.

Martin Luther, the German Reformer, captured this paradox beautifully:

> A Christian is perfectly free lord of all, subject to none. A Christian is a perfectly dutiful servant of all, subject to all.

### Show Proper Respect (v. 17)

> Show proper respect to everyone: Love the brotherhood of believers, fear God, honor the king. (1 Peter 2:17)

This hierarchy is significant. We respect all people, love our Christian community with special care, fear God supremely, and honor governmental authority.

### Application to Our Context

In Australia, we have no legitimate excuse for disobedience.

**Illegal Behaviors Disguised as Principle:**
- Students speeding because they're "late for God's work" (Bible study, preaching)
- Workers using creative accounting to avoid taxes
- Pirates downloading music and software
- Occupy movements masking selfish demands as social justice

During school carnivals, we submit to the authority of the NSW Department of Education. If you feel the carnival conflicts with your values, present that reason honestly. Don't fabricate excuses. Understand that school exists not only for academics but for community, sport, friendship, and healthy competition. See teachers differently when you respect their role.

I was once caught breaking a rule myself. It's not something I'm proud of, but it taught me the importance of genuine submission rather than convenient exceptions.

**On Revolution:**
Individual Christians should have no part in overthrowing governments. Revolution causes more damage than living under imperfect rule.

### China's Example: Submission and Flourishing

An article by John Menear addresses the romantic notion of illegal Christian activity in China. Some celebrate smuggling Bibles and organizing underground churches. But this requires serious thought:

- While concerns about state control of large assemblies are legitimate, there are legal alternatives: family networks, one-on-one conversations, Scripture memorization
- Registered churches are not inherently bad; they represent good governance (property ownership, fundraising, public ministry)
- Underground churches, while sometimes necessary, can lack accountability and become secretive
- China operates on a social contract where individual liberty is traded for national harmony; those who threaten that harmony invite state attention

The Christian way remains verse 13:

> Submit yourselves for the Lord's sake to every authority instituted among men.

## 3. Submission to Masters (v. 18-25)

The principle extends to another power relationship:

> Slaves, submit yourselves to your masters with all respect. (1 Peter 2:18)

### Understanding Biblical Slavery

We rightly recoil from slavery today. The 18th and 19th centuries saw unspeakable atrocities: Africans kidnapped by neighbors, shipped across the Atlantic, bought and sold in European and American markets. One captain drowned 133 enslaved people to collect insurance. These horrors are real and unforgivable.

But we must not let that historical darkness distort our reading of biblical slavery, which was fundamentally different.

**Biblical slavery operated under strict guidelines:**
- Slaves could not be abused or kidnapped
- 1 Timothy 1:10 condemns slave trading as equivalent to murder and adultery
- Under Old Testament law, kidnapping a person was punishable by death (Exodus 21:16; Deuteronomy 24:7)

**The sources of biblical slavery:**
- Prisoners of war (Genesis 14:21; Numbers 31:9). In an age without prisons, lifelong bondage was arguably more merciful than execution
- Those in debt or convicted of crime who couldn't pay restitution (Exodus 22:3; 2 Kings 4:1). Without bankruptcy laws, this offered a just solution
- Those who volunteered to escape poverty (Leviticus 25:39-43). Some parents, in desperation, sold children into bondage—cruel by our standards, but an act of mercy for both child and parent

**Biblical slaves were not Roman slaves or 18th-century slaves.** They were not property to be disposed of at will. Instead:
- They could acquire property rights
- They could conduct business on their master's behalf
- They resembled the faithful servant given talents in Matthew 24:14-15, or Daniel in Babylon, or Joseph in Egypt
- They had legal protections: masters who beat slaves to death faced punishment (Exodus 21:20)
- They received rest on the Sabbath (Exodus 23:12)
- Hebrew slaves were freed in the year of Jubilee—maximum seven years of bondage (Leviticus 24:40)

### Peter's Point: Submit Even to the Harsh

Peter writes:

> Submit...not only to those who are good and considerate, but also to those who are harsh. (1 Peter 2:18)

We submit to those unfit to be submitted to. We submit to authority itself, not just to good authorities. We submit to leadership based on *office*, not personality.

Consider: we submit to elected officials not because they are morally upright or competent, but because they hold office. A school principal deserves submission based on their role, not their personal character. This prevents "personality submission"—obeying only those we like, which becomes a cover for rebellion.

Authorities, by nature, make decisions we dislike and coerce us in ways we resist. The office itself—not the person's virtue—commands our submission.

**Why does Peter emphasize harsh masters?** It's easy to submit to good authority. Anyone can do that. Peter wouldn't bother writing if that were the point. Instead:

> For it is commendable if a man bears up under the pain of unjust suffering because he is conscious of God. But how is it to your credit if you receive a beating for doing wrong and endure it? But if you suffer for doing good and you endure it, this is commendable before God. (1 Peter 2:19-20)

The world qualifies submission: "Submit until they offend you, then stop. Submit until it hurts, then resist." But Peter calls for unconditional submission—a posture of the heart, not a strategy with escape clauses.

### The Example of Christ (v. 21-25)

> To this you were called, because Christ suffered for you, leaving you an example, that you should follow in his steps. (1 Peter 2:21)

Listen to what Jesus did:

> He committed no sin, and no deceit was found in his mouth. When they hurled their insults at him, he did not retaliate; when he suffered, he made no threats. Instead, he entrusted himself to him who judges justly. He himself bore our sins in his body on the tree, so that we might die to sins and live for righteousness; by his wounds you have been healed. For you were like sheep going astray, but now you have returned to the Shepherd and Overseer of your souls. (1 Peter 2:22-25)

Notice what Jesus *didn't* do:
- He didn't retaliate, even though he had all power
- He didn't make threats, even though he could have summoned legions of angels
- He didn't exploit the judges' errors or the Sanhedrin's fabricated evidence
- He didn't curse those who spat on him

Instead, he entrusted himself to the one who judges justly. He left vindication to God. He died to sin and rose to righteousness. And through his wounds, we are healed.

I don't hear qualifications in Jesus' example. There are no "buts." He acted as the true servant, and we are called to follow.

### Application: Workplace Bondage

We are not slaves in the Old Testament sense, but many of us experience a kind of bondage:

- Contract workers bound to employers for set terms
- Scholarship recipients sent wherever institutions require
- Those bound by mortgages and family responsibilities
- Those with HECS debt requiring ongoing employment
- Those locked into 24-month phone contracts
- Those in repetitive work with few alternatives

Each represents a form of bondage.

**How should we respond?**

- Do not sin to make up for unfair treatment (don't steal stationery to compensate for unpaid overtime)
- Don't use work time for personal pursuits (social media consumption, while common, amounts to theft of time)
- Sympathize with your bosses; they face pressures too
- Don't scheme to "get back" at unfair employers
- Instead, follow Christ's example: initiate submission and respect

**A critical line:** If a boss demands that you sin—truly commands you to do what God forbids—then the pathway is to escalate through proper channels or to resign. It is not to rebel.

**Creating Opportunities for Gospel Witness at Work:**

By maintaining integrity and submission, we create space to declare God's praises in our workplace. Be radical in your honesty. Don't gossip about colleagues. If you're overpaid for your work, acknowledge it internally. These acts of integrity make you visibly different and open doors for spiritual conversations.

Invite colleagues to experience a different side of you—your home, your values, your faith. Sometimes a different context makes it easier to discuss Jesus.

## Conclusion: The Alien Perspective

For the past 200 years, especially since the Enlightenment, we've celebrated freedom and personal rights. Submission has become a swear word. Any form of deference to authority is viewed with suspicion.

But this wreaks havoc on our understanding of legitimate authority:
- Teachers are no longer authorities; they're guidance counselors
- Parents are no longer authorities; they're friends with money
- Police are no longer authorities; they're personal security guards
- Governments are no longer authorities; they're facilitators of your goals

**But if we don't understand earthly authority, we won't understand God.** If we treat all authority as suspicious and bow only to our own sovereignty, we won't submit to God. If we teach our children to submit to no one but themselves, they will never submit to the King of kings. And we will fail to understand marriage—the subject of next week's teaching.

Meanwhile, let us live such good lives that our distinctness creates opportunities to declare the gospel. Let us submit to authorities—even harsh ones—following our Master's example.

### The Vindication of Submission

**China's Surprising Story:**

There are now 39-41 million Protestant Christians in China. Many belong to legally registered organizations. The strength of evangelical faith—the kind we would recognize theologically—is found in these churches that submitted to the state. Remarkably, rumors suggest the government now consults these churches to understand how Western liberal democracies are rooted in Christianity.

**Peter's Final Testimony:**

During Nero's reign, Peter was persecuted and executed. Yet Peter remained faithful. He requested crucifixion upside-down, saying he was unworthy to die as his Master had. I suspect even his manner of death reflects his submission—he didn't rebel against the state, didn't retaliate, but continued preaching the gospel and living his good life as best he could.

On the last day, his vindication will be complete.
