# Revelation 5

**Title:** The Lion, The Switch and the Throne Room
**Date:** January 12, 2002
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction

How do we gain real power? That's the question that will guide us through Revelation 5 today. I want to start with a movie illustration: Lord of the Rings Part 2. In that film, everyone seeks power through the ring. Some want it for control and domination; others want it for protection and security. But Frodo realizes something crucial—that power corrupts anyone who possesses it, regardless of their intentions. That tension between seeking power and recognizing its corrupting influence frames our exploration of God's throne room today.

Keep your Bible open to Revelation 5 as we work through this passage together. We'll move from the throne room itself, through what I'm calling "the switch"—a shocking transformation—and discover what real power actually looks like.

## The Throne Room

Picture this as a grand cinematic scene. John opens his vision with the heavens displayed in all their glory: twenty-four elders, four living creatures, and God seated on the throne, appearing like jasper and precious gemstones. All honor, glory, and power radiate from that center. The worship fills heaven with voices and songs.

Then the camera pans to a scroll held in God's right hand.

> I saw in the right hand of him who sat on the throne a scroll with writing on both sides and sealed with seven seals. (Revelation 5:1)

This scroll contains God's purposes and plans for the world—comprehensive, complete, written inside and out. But notice: it's sealed. Not with one seal, but seven. These seals will be opened in chapters 6 and 8, each bringing divine judgment. For now, the scroll remains completely protected so that no one can read or access its contents.

Then a mighty angel cries out with a voice that cuts through all the heavenly worship:

> Who is worthy to break the seals and open the scroll? But no one in heaven or on earth or under the earth could open the scroll or even look inside it. (Revelation 5:2-3)

The challenge hangs in the air: Who can do this? Who is worthy? The answer comes back from every corner of creation—no one. Not in heaven, not on earth, not under the earth. No one in all the universe is worthy enough to open God's scroll, to fulfill His plans, or even to catch a glimpse of what lies within.

John's response is profound:

> I wept and wept because no one was found who was worthy to open the scroll or look inside. (Revelation 5:4)

Imagine that anguish. The promise of God's purposes unfulfilled. The hope of blessing denied. Is there no one?

## The Lion—Complete Power

Then one of the elders, clothed in white and representing victory in heaven, speaks comfort to John:

> Do not weep! See, the Lion of the tribe of Judah, the Root of David, has triumphed. He is able to open the scroll and its seven seals. (Revelation 5:5)

Suddenly there's hope. The Lion. 

What does a lion represent? **Power is strength**—the lion is mighty and unmatched. **Power is authority**—the lion rules with wisdom as king of beasts. **Power is security and trust**—you'd deposit your money in a bank displaying a lion logo. **Power is kingship**—even in lands where lions no longer roam, they appear on coats of arms. The lion embodies strength, authority, wisdom, security, and dominion.

Who is this Lion? It's Jesus Christ. While Scripture doesn't use the exact phrase "Lion of the tribe of Judah" until Revelation, John draws on Old Testament imagery. Genesis speaks of Judah as a lion's cub; Ezekiel portrays Israel as a lion; Isaiah points to the Root of Jesse. Revelation weaves together these Old Testament pictures into one coherent vision: God's chosen King is the Lion.

Since King David's reign, Israel had longed for a king of his caliber. Jesus is David's descendant—the one who would conquer foreign invaders, bring peace by crushing enemies, the great and majestic Lion-King to rule the world. As God's chosen representative, the Lion is worthy and able to open the scroll and reveal God's purposes.

## The First Switch—From Lion to Lamb

So John turns around, expecting to see this magnificent Lion. What does he actually see?

> Then I saw a Lamb, looking as if it had been slain, standing in the centre of the throne, encircled by the four living creatures and the elders. (Revelation 5:6)

A lamb. Not even a grown sheep, but a lamb. And slain—wounded, dead, broken.

The switch is almost absurd. It's like imagining America—with all its military might, its missiles, tanks, battleships, stealth bombers, smart bombs, satellite reconnaissance, and nuclear arsenal—sending a helpless baby with a toy into battle. How could weakness possibly conquer?

Jesus could have raised an army. He could have called a legion of angels to fight for Him. The kingdoms of the world would have been His in a moment. He could have come in triumph and victory. Instead, He chose to be slain. When you're dead, you can't do anything—much less conquer or be victorious. Why would the Lion become a powerless lamb?

> Real power is gained by trusting God.

Let me say that again: **Real power is gained by trusting God.**

Jesus in His obedience and trust follows God all the way to the cross. He disarms Himself of everything remotely powerful and goes into the battlefield like a vulnerable infant. Real power is lamb-like—not the world's power, but God's power operating through weakness and trust.

### The Problem with Worldly Power

We find it hard to see this because we naturally understand power through worldly eyes. We look at the "pathetic" Jesus—a slain lamb—and see weakness, not strength. We prefer to trust the world's measurements: wealth, wisdom, strength, status.

Consider how we live. I drive my Toyota Corolla hatchback down Pennant Hills Road regularly. When someone in a basic car or an old vehicle is ahead of me, I have no qualms about revving my engine and passing them. But when it's a luxury car—a BMW convertible—I hesitate. That car intimidates me. The driver seems powerful, and I feel small.

Then one day, I drove a large Verada—champagne colored, with power windows, leather seats, a CD player, and those fancy antennas that rise and lower automatically. Suddenly, I wasn't worried about other drivers' opinions. I felt secure and successful.

That's the trap. Our props—the things we own and display—create the illusion that we're successful. We use them constantly:

- The school we attended, the university course we're studying, the graduate job we'll have
- The latest clothing brand, the newest possessions that mark us as "in"
- Our attendance at the trendy church service, the boyfriend or girlfriend who boosts our social standing
- Our grades, our status, our need to be labeled successful so others will be impressed

Instead of becoming weak lambs, we'd rather build a name for ourselves. But the powerless lamb cuts across all these perceptions and reveals where true power actually comes from: **trusting the powerful God.**

It's hard for us to grasp because we live comfortably and, for most of us, we haven't yet been tested. Only when serious illness comes, when we fail exams, lose our popularity, or experience loss do we truly discover whether we trust God. When we lose everything that makes us feel powerful in this world, when our earthly props crumble—that's when we learn where our trust really lies.

Jesus shows us this without the props. Strip away the mane, silence the roar, file down the teeth and claws, shrink the size, take the life—and what you have is the slain Lamb. Jesus relying not on Himself but on God. And the proof that this way works? That's the second switch.

## The Second Switch—From Slain Lamb to Exalted Power

Here's the remarkable reversal:

> Then I saw a Lamb, looking as if it had been slain, standing in the centre of the throne, encircled by the four living creatures and the elders. He had seven horns and seven eyes, which are the seven spirits of God sent out into all the earth. (Revelation 5:6)

The mortally wounded Lamb is standing. Alive. He stands in the center of the throne—a place of supreme prominence—encircled by the four living creatures and the elders who honor Him. The Lamb has seven horns, signifying complete and perfect power. Seven eyes point to complete and perfect knowledge. He possesses the complete Spirit of God. The lamb is exalted to the highest status.

At once crucified and powerful. This is the absurdity of Christ: death and power together.

> Then I saw a Lamb, looking as if it had been slain, standing in the centre of the throne, encircled by the four living creatures and the elders. (Revelation 5:6)

> He came and took the scroll from the right hand of him who sat on the throne. (Revelation 5:7)

The Lion who triumphed was declared worthy to take the scroll. Yet after this moment, the Lion is never seen or mentioned again in Revelation. Only the Lamb—the one who looked as if it had been slain—takes the scroll and reveals God's purposes.

The Messianic Son of David is God's appointed conqueror, but only through His death. It's not the Lion as Lion who takes the scroll; it's the Lion as the Lamb, looking as if it had been slain. The weak, vulnerable one puts the world's powers in their proper place.

What follows is a cascade of worship:

> When he had taken the scroll, the four living creatures and the twenty-four elders fell down before the Lamb. Each one had a harp and they were holding golden bowls full of incense, which are the prayers of the saints. (Revelation 5:8)

All creation. All authority. Worshipping the Lamb.

> Then I looked and heard the voice of many angels, numbering thousands upon thousands, and ten thousand times ten thousand. They encircled the throne and the living creatures and the elders. In a loud voice they sang: "Worthy is the Lamb, who was slain, to receive power and wealth and wisdom and strength and honor and glory and praise!" (Revelation 5:11-12)

Thousands upon thousands. Ten thousand times ten thousand. More than we can count. In a voice so loud it fills all heaven. And what are they singing? "Worthy is the Lamb who was slain." Not "worthy of our pity," but worthy to receive sevenfold adoration—power, wealth, wisdom, strength, honor, glory, and praise. Complete worship.

And where before the answer to "who can open the scroll?" was utter silence and weeping, now:

> Then I heard every creature in heaven and on earth and under the earth and on the sea, and all that is in them, singing: "To him who sits on the throne and to the Lamb be praise and honor and glory and power, for ever and ever!" The four living creatures said, "Amen," and the elders fell down and worshiped. (Revelation 5:13-14)

Every creature in heaven, on earth, under the earth, and in the sea. All singing the same song to God and to the Lamb. Previously in chapter 4, all glory was given to the one on the throne alone. Now that same honor and glory is shared with the Lamb. Jesus is raised to God's right hand and exalted.

The highest honor and respect belong to God alone. Yet that honor is given to the Lamb because He is God and He is worthy.

Even heaven's theme song has changed. Look at verse 9:

> "You are worthy to take the scroll and to open its seals, because you were slain, and with your blood you purchased men for God from every tribe and language and people and nation. You have made them to be a kingdom and priests to serve our God, and they will reign on the earth." (Revelation 5:9-10)

My mother gets quite upset when our dog jumps on the couch. But here, not only is a farm animal on God's favorite seat, not only has all heavenly attention shifted to the Lamb, but heaven's pre-programmed CD player has been changed. It's a new song. In chapter 4, the song was dedicated to the Creator God. Here, the song is dedicated to the Lamb because He was slain.

God's eternal song is changed. What power it must take to change the song in heaven itself.

## Living as Subjects in God's Kingdom

So the question for us becomes: Is being a lamb worth it? Is trusting God all the way to the cross worth it? 

Based on verses 6-14, the answer is an unequivocal **yes**. The lamb is exalted and powerful.

For us: Is following the way of the lamb worth it? Is giving up my time, my money, my entertainment, my relationships for the kingdom of God worth it? Is being a loser in this world's eyes worth it? Is trusting God all the way through worth my surrender?

When you could be the president of a superpower, is being a vulnerable baby worth it?

Yes. There is real hope and confidence in our weakness and surrender.

Jesus lived on the premise that it is better by far to trust God, to be a loser by the world's standards, to die to self, and to gain heaven. This is how real power works—through trust in God. That fact must reshape how we live in this world.

Why be a slain lamb when you could be a roaring lion? Why give up two weeks of summer for a beach mission? Why trade Tuesday nights for Bible study when you could catch a cheap movie? Why sacrifice half your paycheck for gospel work? Why give up power? Don't you want to have fun? Don't you want the power to enjoy yourself while you have the chance?

Of course we do. Just later.

A friend once told me that greed isn't about wanting more and more—it's about wanting more and more **now**. As Christians, as citizens of heaven, we have everything. We are heirs to all things in earth and heaven. We just have to wait for it. And it is worth waiting for.

Christians aren't suffering for nothing. This isn't a party-pooper theology. There's a real promise of good things in the future that sustains us in the present. When we see heaven like this, when we glimpse the lamb glorified, it gives us reason to release the things of this world. **Power comes in giving up the props of this world and trusting God. Power is lamb-like—powerless by the world's measure, but truly powerful.**

And yes, that's scary. But it's a step of faith. And today we've been given a vision from heaven itself about what awaits us. The exalted lamb means that trusting and relying in God makes perfect sense. It is worth it. A vulnerable baby is worth more than the mighty strength of empires.

## Following the Lamb

Not only is being a lamb worth it—we can actually follow the Lamb.

> You were slain, and with your blood you purchased men for God from every tribe and language and people and nation. You have made them to be a kingdom and priests to serve our God, and they will reign on the earth. (Revelation 5:9-10)

The Lamb was slain to purchase people for God—people in slavery to sin and Satan. God gave the ultimate ransom to buy back His people, and nothing would suffice except the blood of His Son. We no longer belong to Satan.

Now we belong to God. We're His people. But notice: we're not given power to sin; we're given power to serve. We're purchased not to do what we like, but to serve sacrificially. Through Jesus, we see that the way to lion-like power is through becoming a lamb. If you want to be powerful, be the lamb.

Why invest yourself in serving in the unglamorous, unattractive areas? Washing dishes, cleaning tea towels, working the book stall, taking out garbage, talking to socially difficult people—it gets us nowhere. No popularity, no credit. Why serve at the early morning service where no one is awake to see you?

Because the Lamb on the throne shows us two truths: The way of the lamb is worth it, and the way of the lamb is followable. We have been purchased for exactly this purpose.

## Conclusion

How can we have power? Wealth, wisdom, strength, honor? The world offers self-promotion and self-preservation. God offers the way of the lamb—demotion and sacrificial service.

But from the vision of the slain Lamb on the throne, we know this way to real power actually works. The Lamb is exalted. All of it was done for us so that we could follow.

Follow by trusting. Follow by faith in God. Which means powerlessness for us—the kind of powerlessness that is actually the deepest power because it rests entirely on the power of God.

It's like Lord of the Rings: Everyone wants the ring's power. Humans, elves, dwarves, orcs—they all want it. But the path to true power is to give it up. Frodo discovers that this power corrupts and destroys everyone it touches. The ring must be destroyed.

Similarly, all earthly power eventually corrupts. It leads us away from humble obedience to God. The Lamb shows us that giving up power results in real power.

Real power is the Lion trusting God even when it means being a Lamb all the way to the cross. If we want real power—the kind that lasts, that satisfies, that will be honored in heaven itself—it means following the Lamb.

**Real power is lamb-like.**
