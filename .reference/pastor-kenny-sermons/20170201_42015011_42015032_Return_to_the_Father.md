# Luke 15:11-32

**Title:** Return to the Father

**Date:** 26 February 2017

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Adulting: A Generation's Struggle with Responsibility

The word "adulting" has become a millennial anthem—that humorous acknowledgment of entering adulthood. It captures something real about our generation: we recognize the weight of adult responsibilities, even as we sometimes treat them lightly. But this parable Jesus tells us isn't about lighthearted responsibility. It's about two sons, and two very different responses to what it means to be a son.

I have two sons of my own. Joshua, my older son, is reliable—he does extra homework without complaining, volunteers to help. Then there's David, my younger son, a terror who once broke his arm doing something reckless (the first in three generations of our family to do so). Today we hear about two sons in Jesus's parable—an older son and a younger son. No relation to mine, but their story is profoundly our story.

> 11 Jesus continued: "There was a man who had two sons.

---

## Part 1: We Hurt God

### The Demand That Means Contempt

The younger son approaches his father with a shocking request:

> 12 The younger one said to his father, 'Father, give me my share of the estate.'

We don't know what precipitated this. An argument? A conflict over the car? But whatever it was, the son's demand escalates into something unthinkable. In asking for his inheritance now, he's essentially saying: "I wish you were dead." "You are as good as dead to me." "Die already."

In Jewish culture, inheritances belong to the dead. A father could arrange what assets would go to which sons, but they could only take possession after his death. By demanding his share now, the younger son is wishing death upon his father.

Yet here's what the father does, strangely:

> 12 ... So he divided his property between them.

### What a Father Doesn't Do

In that culture, a father had other options. According to Deuteronomy 21:18-21, a stubborn and rebellious son could be brought before the elders and stoned to death. The son who won't obey, who is a glutton and drunkard—he could be executed.

Instead, this father divides his property.

The older son receives two-thirds (the firstborn's double share); the younger receives one-third. In Greek, the text literally says the father "divided his life"—not just his wealth, but his livelihood. Fifty years of work and toil, liquidated in a firesale. Blood and sweat, gone. One-third of his capital, one-third of his business model, divided away.

But most painfully, his son—whom he loved, whom he gave life to, whom he had invested in—is torn from the family. The family is shattered.

How would you feel if you were that father? That's exactly what we do to God.

We are unhappy with the lives God has given us. We are tired of His laws and His way of doing things. We are sick of being told what to do by Him. We are exhausted by guilt. We say, "Go to Hell, God."

### The Death of God in Modern Culture

This echoes Nietzsche's proclamation: "God is dead, and we have killed him." After the Enlightenment, the West realized it no longer needed God. Philosophy showed that governments could be legitimate through reason and consent, not divine right. Morality and order could exist without reference to God. Science revealed a universe governed by natural laws, not divine providence.

This modernist movement concluded: God is dead. We killed Him with our scientific revolution, our desire to understand the world.

And as a society, that's what we've done. Every day, people tell God to go away. We have collectively dismissed Him from the public square, from our institutions, from our personal lives.

But we hurt ourselves in the process.

## Part 2: We Hurt Ourselves

### The Inevitable Collapse

Having obtained his inheritance, the younger son runs away:

> 13 "Not long after that, the younger son got together all he had, set off for a distant country and there squandered his wealth in wild living.

He goes to a distant country—where no one knows him. He can start anew with a new identity, unrestrained by his past, free from the memory of his father. No one will ask, "Aren't you so-and-so's son?" or "Didn't you used to go to church?" Nothing will keep him accountable.

But this distance is more than physical. It's an emotional and spiritual distance between father and son—and it will lead to his near-death.

"Wild living" means prodigal, wasteful, reckless, imprudent, excessive, self-indulgent. The son buys fast cars, pursues girls, throws parties, drinks, gambles, uses drugs, pursues prostitution. He chases worldliness without inhibition. After all, if God is dead, the only restraint is whether you get caught.

But reality always kicks in:

> 14 After he had spent everything, there was a severe famine in that whole country, and he began to be in need.

The wealth runs out. Famine strikes the whole country. He cannot escape consequences, no matter how far he runs.

Desperate and alone, he hires himself out:

> 15 So he went and hired himself out to a citizen of that country, who sent him to his fields to feed pigs. 16 He longed to fill his stomach with the pods that the pigs were eating, but no one gave him anything.

Things are dire when you look at pigs and feel jealous of their food. For a Jewish boy, this is ultimate degradation—pigs were unclean animals. He wanted the worst job in the world to eat the worst things in the world, and he was still hungry. No one gave him anything.

The trajectory is stark: riches to rags, wealth to filth, wine to swine, penthouse to outhouse, everything to nothing.

Consider Charlie Sheen. In 1986, he was a breakthrough actor in *Platoon*. By 2010, at his peak, he was television's highest-paid actor. But then the decline: drugs, prostitution, drink-driving, multiple ex-wives, multiple children, clinic after clinic. In 2011, he famously claimed he didn't need rehab because the AA manual was written for "normal people," not people with "tiger blood" and "Adonis DNA." In 2015, he revealed he was HIV positive.

From the heights to the depths. The consequence of living without restraint.

### The Broader Cost to Society

Our culture celebrates sexual freedom. But researcher J.D. Unwin studied 86 societies across 5,000 years of history. With no Christian convictions and no moral agenda, he concluded: **"Increased sexual constraints always led to increased flourishing of a culture. Conversely, increased sexual freedom always led to the collapse of a culture three generations later."**

Already we see the signs:

- We used to learn Greek in high school; now we need remedial English in university.
- We can no longer agree on basic biological realities.
- Suicide rates are up.
- Mental health crises are up.

And this is in the wealthiest, most educated, most resourced civilization in human history. The contradiction is stark. When we reject God's boundaries, we don't find freedom. We find emptiness.

## Part 3: Come Back

### Coming to His Senses

Something uncommon happens. The son comes to his senses:

> 17 "When he came to his senses, he said, 'How many of my father's hired servants have food to spare, and here I am starving to death!

He rehearses a speech of repentance:

> 18 I will set out and go back to my father and say to him: Father, I have sinned against heaven and against you. 19 I am no longer worthy to be called your son; make me like one of your hired servants.' 20 So he got up and went to his father.

The hired servants—day laborers, not even living on the property—had it better than he did. Even being a servant in his father's house would be better than this. As Psalm 84:10 says: "I would rather be a doorkeeper in the house of my God than dwell in the tents of the wicked."

It's better to be poor in the richest country than rich in the poorest country. Better to suffer under God's care than to have worldly success without Him.

He repents. Genuinely humbled, he returns, expecting judgment:

> 21 "The son said to him, 'Father, I have sinned against heaven and against you. I am no longer worthy to be called your son.'

True humility. True repentance. He expects judgment.

### The Father's Extravagant Welcome

But the father's response shatters all expectation:

> 21 ... "But while he was still a long way off, his father saw him and was filled with compassion for him; he ran to his son, threw his arms around him and kissed him.

Has the father been scanning the horizon every day, waiting for his son? He sees him from a distance and is filled with compassion. He runs—culturally inappropriate for a dignified patriarch. He throws his arms around him and kisses him repeatedly.

The son doesn't even get to finish his prepared speech.

Instead:

> 22 "But the father said to his servants, 'Quick! Bring the best robe and put it on him. Put a ring on his finger and sandals on his feet. 23 Bring the fattened calf and kill it. Let's have a feast and celebrate.

The best robe would be the father's own. The ring is the family signet ring—a symbol of reinstatement to full sonship. Sandals mark him as a free person, not a slave. The father is saying: "No, I will not receive you back as a servant. I receive you only as a son."

The fattened calf was saved for a special occasion—the father kills it for him. He wants the whole village to know. Becoming a Christian is cause for celebration.

> 24 For this son of mine was dead and is alive again; he was lost and is found.' So they began to celebrate.

### The Reason We Can Hope

The law said such a son should be stoned. According to Deuteronomy 21:18-21, the rebellious glutton and drunkard deserved death.

But God the Father is always willing to forgive. The problem is not God. The problem is us.

We call Him dead. Then we're too embarrassed to admit our wrongdoing. We're too proud to seek forgiveness. But if you go to Him and rehearse your repentance, He will run to you and embrace you instead.

How do we know this? Because the same Jesus who tells this parable is the Son who took the punishment for the prodigal son. The judgment didn't disappear. What the son did was criminal—worthy of stoning. But Jesus took that stoning upon Himself.

If you've been stupid, there is a way back to God. Churches are full of people who've fouled up, looking for a second chance. Jesus died so that second chance is real.

## Part 4: The Righteous Son

### The Son Who Works, Who Obeys

Meanwhile, the older son hears the celebration:

> 25 "Meanwhile, the older son was in the field. When he came near the house, he heard music and dancing.

He's been working hard in the field while his brother wastes away in a distant country. He hears the party—music, dancing, cars filling the street.

> 26 So he called one of the servants and asked him what was going on.

Confusion. When did we organize a party?

> 27 'Your brother has come,' he replied, 'and your father has killed the fattened calf because he has him back safe and sound.'

The older brother is not impressed:

> 28 "The older brother became angry and continually refused to go in. So his father went out and pleaded with him.

He refuses to enter. The father must plead with him. Finally, he lets it out:

> 29 But he answered his father, 'Look! All these years I've been slaving for you and never disobeyed your orders. Yet you never gave me even a young goat so I could celebrate with my friends. 30 But when this son of yours who has squandered your property with prostitutes comes home, you kill the fattened calf for him!'

### The Language of Resentment

The language reveals much:

- "Look!"—telling the father off
- "Slaving for you"—as if the father is an employer, not a father; as if He's like those masters who gave the younger son nothing
- "Never gave me even a young goat"—calling the father stingy

This son talks as if the father were no father at all. But that's not true:

> 31 "'My son,' the father said, 'you are always with me, and everything I have is yours. 32 But we had to celebrate and be glad, because this brother of yours was dead and is alive again; he was lost and is found.'"

Always with me. Everything I have is yours. In fact, he has two-thirds—nearly all of it.

### The Deception of Works-Based Love

This older son believes that if he keeps working, keeps doing what he's told, the father will be obligated to love him. He tries to put God in his debt: "If I do A, B, and C, you must do X, Y, and Z."

But the father says: You already have everything. You're always with me. My love is not something you earn. It's something you already possess.

As Romans 4:4-5 says:

> Now to the one who works, wages are not credited as a gift but as an obligation. However, to the one who does not work but trusts God who justifies the ungodly, their faith is credited as righteousness.

The father's inheritance is a gift. God's salvation is mercy, not wages.

### We Are All the Older Son

This week, someone asks: "God should let me into heaven."

Why? Because I've been good. Because I haven't done what the bad son did. Because I work hard.

But it doesn't work like that. For both the bad son and the good son, we fall short of the glory of God. All of God's acts are undeserved mercy.

The father is not a means to an end—not a money provider, not a security provider, not a tool to be used. Yet both sons treat him this way. The younger son demands his wealth. The older son demands recognition for his labor.

Both are using the father.

## Epilogue: The Risk of Living

From Ecclesiastes 9:

> 7 Go, eat your food with gladness, and drink your wine with a joyful heart, for God has already approved what you do. 8 Always be clothed in white, and always anoint your head with oil. 9 Enjoy life with your wife, whom you love, all the days of this meaningless life that God has given you under the sun. 10 Whatever your hand finds to do, do it with all your might, for in the realm of the dead, where you are going, there is neither working nor planning nor knowledge nor wisdom.

Life is risky. Step outside and you might catch a virus. Drive a car and you might have an accident. Eat a cheeseburger and you might have a heart attack. Run in the sun and you might get skin cancer. There is cost to doing something.

But life is also short. We are mortal. There is also cost to not doing something—opportunity cost.

So eat and drink. Enjoy your wife. You cannot keep what you earn. It's a sunk cost.

My friends Brian and Bianca decided to seize the day. Despite restrictions, despite uncertainty about where they could marry, they took the risk. They didn't wait for the next convenient weekend. Because of their mortality, they chose to start their new life together now. They took the risk of starting a new family.

Risk is not something we do later—it wouldn't be risky then. Risk is something we do now.
