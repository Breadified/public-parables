# 1 Corinthians 11:17-34

**Title:** The Lord's Supper
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Problem: Eating Incorrectly

### The Crisis of Disconnected Meals

We live in an age where eating together has become rare. A magazine article on American culture highlighted a troubling reality: one in every five meals is eaten in a car, one in four Americans eats fast food daily, and most families share fewer than five meals together each week. We rush home late, eat on the go, work through dinner—and we've lost something precious in the process.

The research is sobering. Children who don't eat with their families regularly have truancy rates nearly double the global average (30% vs. 15%). Those who eat with parents fewer than twice a week are 40% more likely to be overweight. And children missing family dinners consistently show more trouble with drugs and alcohol, worse academic performance, and weaker family bonds. Meals aren't just about food—they're about belonging.

This same crisis was happening in Corinth.

### The Corinthian Failure

> In the following directives I have no praise for you, for your meetings do more harm than good. In the first place, I hear that when you come together as a church, there are divisions among you, and to some extent I believe it. No doubt there have to be differences among you to show which of you have God's approval. When you come together, it is not the Lord's Supper you eat. (1 Corinthians 11:17-20)

Paul is devastatingly blunt: **"I have no praise for you."** Their meetings do "more harm than good." There are divisions, factions, competing claims to authenticity. But the real problem emerges in what was supposed to be their most sacred meal together.

> For as you eat, each of you goes ahead without waiting for anybody else. One remains hungry, another gets drunk. Don't you have homes to eat and drink in? Or do you despise the church of God and humiliate those who have nothing? What shall I say to you? Shall I praise you for this? Certainly not! (1 Corinthians 11:21-22)

The picture is ugly: the wealthy rushed in, grabbed the best food, ate until they were drunk. The poor sat hungry. Perhaps it was like a potluck where the rich brought elaborate dishes and the poor brought nothing. Or maybe the wealthy simply gorged while others went without. Either way, the result was the same—humiliation of those who had nothing.

I think of my own school days: watching classmates pull out three-course rice and soup while I had a soggy sandwich. At university, they had steak and chips while I made do with rice and gravy. During seminary, they ordered the $25 special while I could only afford the $5 laksa. It was miserable. I couldn't join them for meals. I couldn't participate in that fellowship. Now imagine that happening every single time your church gathered—the core community that should welcome and include you instead making you feel poor, excluded, less-than.

**This is what happens when we stop treating meals as sacred.**

### The Judgment of Selfishness

> Therefore, whoever eats the bread or drinks the cup of the Lord in an unworthy manner will be guilty of sinning against the body and blood of the Lord. A man ought to examine himself before he eats of the bread and drinks of the cup. For anyone who eats and drinks without recognizing the body of the Lord eats and drinks judgment on himself. (1 Corinthians 11:27-29)

When Paul speaks of "sinning against the body and blood of the Lord," he doesn't mean Jesus himself—Jesus is safe in heaven. He means the **corporate body, the church**. The people sitting next to you. The person going hungry. The one being humiliated.

To eat without recognizing the body of the Lord is to eat without seeing your brother, your sister, the hungry one at your table. And this brings judgment—not just personal shame, but actual harm to the community itself.

> That is why many among you are weak and sick, and a number of you have fallen asleep. (1 Corinthians 11:30)

"Fallen asleep" is a euphemism for death. In a subsistent, ancient world where malnutrition was a real threat, imagine what would happen when the strong always ate well and the weak always went hungry. Some would actually die from lack of food. Your brother or sister in Christ would perish because you wouldn't share.

This isn't metaphorical judgment. This is real consequence flowing from real selfishness.

### The Call to Repentance

> But if we judged ourselves, we would not come under judgment. When we are judged by the Lord, we are being disciplined so that we will not be condemned with the world. (1 Corinthians 11:31-32)

Paul calls them to self-examination. Judge your own actions. Repent of your selfishness before God judges it for you. Because God's discipline isn't punishment—it's correction. It's designed to save us from condemnation.

> So then, my brothers, when you come together to eat, wait for each other. If anyone is hungry, he should eat at home, so that when you meet together it may not result in judgment. (1 Corinthians 11:33-34)

The solution is simple: **wait for each other**. Look forward to each other. Expect each other. These gatherings aren't primarily about food—they're about fellowship, community, belonging. If all you care about is feeding yourself, do that at home. But when we gather as the body of Christ, it's about each other.

## The Pattern: Eating Rightly

### Jesus' Model of Service

> For I received from the Lord what I also passed on to you: The Lord Jesus, on the night he was betrayed, took bread, and when he had given thanks, he broke it and said, "This is my body, which is for you; do this in remembrance of me." In the same way, after supper he took the cup, saying, "This cup is the new covenant in my blood; do this, whenever you drink it, in remembrance of me." (1 Corinthians 11:23-25)

In the early church, "breaking bread" was simply how they described eating together—a normal, nourishing meal. Bread was their staple. Wine was everyday drink that prevented illness. This wasn't a token ritual; it was a full meal where Christians gathered daily, before there were Sundays or holidays in the Roman Empire. They met in the evenings after work and ate together.

But notice what Jesus did at His last meal on earth.

**He served.**

He took the bread, broke it, and gave it to others. He poured wine and passed it around. In the ancient world, the host served, but here Jesus was the guest of honor at His own final meal. This was His last chance to indulge, to ask for whatever He wanted. A prisoner facing execution would request caviar and champagne. But Jesus? He served.

All the more remarkable—the very next day, His body would be broken, not just symbolically but physically. Whipped. Crowned with thorns. Pierced with nails. His blood spilt from lashings and wounds. All "for you"—for our sins, for our redemption. The Passover lamb giving everything so we could live.

This was no token meal. This was a preview of ultimate sacrifice—**real service, real blood, real love for His friends.**

### What Gets Passed Down

What did Paul receive from the Lord that he passed on to you? Paul never ate a meal with Jesus in person. So it wasn't a formula or a ritual. What was passed down was something far deeper:

**The other-person-centered love of the Cross.**

Jesus died to serve us by taking our punishment. The Lord passed down to Paul—and to us—a way of eating and drinking. A way of fellowshipping. A way of life centered not on ourselves but on sacrificial love for one another. That's what it means to remember Jesus: to eat as He ate, to serve as He served, to love as He loved.

> For whenever you eat this bread and drink this cup, you proclaim the Lord's death until he comes. (1 Corinthians 11:26)

Every meal is a proclamation. Every shared dish is a witness to the gospel. Every act of service at the table reminds us and each other of Christ's sacrificial love.

## Living It Out: How We Eat

### Serve One Another

Do you serve at meals—making sure everyone has a seat, is comfortable, has food? When you go out for lunch after church, do you save a seat for those running late? Do you take the best piece first, or grab it for someone else?

My grandmother, even in her nineties when she needed all the nutrition she could get, would always take the least desirable piece—the chicken neck, the bony bits. When I asked why, she simply said that's what service looks like.

Do you help wash up afterward? Do you arrive early to prepare? (By the way, if you're looking for a godly spouse—watch how they act at mealtimes. If they're watching TV while everyone else is preparing, they're not ready.)

### Talk Meaningfully

Meals are opportunities to truly know one another and encourage one another. But how often are our conversations just endless random chatter—funny but meaningless morsels of information? Are we actually listening, or just trying to be funny? Are we asking real questions?

After church, instead of "How are you?"—try "What did you learn from the Bible today?" Our whole life shouldn't be just breaking the ice. Where are we breaking *to*?

Pay attention to the rhythm of real conversation. Does it have a thread? Does it go somewhere? Are you understanding who people really are and how the Lord is affecting them? Or are you stuck in performance mode, trying to be clever?

### Pray Together

Meals are opportunities to pray for one another. That's what grace is meant to be—not a rushed formula we mumble before eating, but genuine intercession for those gathered with us. If you're hungry and want to eat, don't force a perfunctory grace. Instead, take time afterward—after you've heard how everyone really is—to pray for one another with real care.

### Remember the Stakes

In many cultures, mealtimes are sacred. The French don't rush meals, even alone—it's considered disrespectful to the food and yourself. In Mexico, families gather in parks and town squares to eat together. In Cambodia, villagers spread colorful mats and bring dishes to share as a potluck.

Research shows that eating together actually changes how people see each other. It reduces perceptions of inequality. People view those of different races, genders, and socioeconomic backgrounds as more equal at the dinner table than in any other social setting.

This is the power of a shared meal.

### The Reality of Our Separation

We are so isolated now. We live far from each other. We work so long. The early Christians saw each other every day. We're attacked by busyness and distance. I'm not sure the solution is moving into one apartment building like the first-century church. But meanwhile, use the opportunity. Don't rush off from church. Stay a little longer. Chat. Encourage. Be encouraged. Pray.

**Don't participate in all the things you shouldn't. Participate in this.**

## The Heart of It All

The meal is important. As important as that last supper Jesus shared with His disciples.

Treat each meal as if it might be your last with those people. My grandmother tried to speak at every meal, to connect, to leave on a good note. At the end of a meal together, thank people. Forgive each other. Come next week—stay for the extended time together.

It hasn't always been this way. When our church met at the Parish Centre, we gathered for the symbolic meal monthly. But as our circumstances changed—moving to the school, time constraints, newcomers finding the ritual unfamiliar—we did it less often. And something else changed too: **you stopped thinking about yourselves and thought about others.** 

Those early days when people made excuses—homework, assignments, wanting to eat proper Asian food—gave way to genuine desire to be together. And now, for many of us, our meals together are the highlight of church, if not the week. The best memories aren't of the food. They're of service, of love, of unity.

**This is what Jesus wants for us.**
