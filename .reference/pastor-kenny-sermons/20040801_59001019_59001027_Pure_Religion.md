# James 1:19-27

**Title:** Pure Religion

**Date:** 29 August 2004

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Introduction: What Is Pure Religion?

In contemporary debates across many faiths, the question of what constitutes "pure" practice keeps arising. In Islam, theologians debate whether true submission requires absolute adherence or permits greater flexibility. Mel Gibson's *Passion* sought to recover "pure" Catholicism through the use of Aramaic and Latin. Within Judaism, certain communities pursue strict observance of Levitical law. Even in Christian circles, some search for "pure Christianity" by isolating only the words of Jesus himself.

But what does James say about pure religion?

Recall from the previous passage that God has created Christians as a kind of firstfruits—people formed by God and for God to do good work. This foundational truth now leads James to define what pure religion actually is. The answer unfolds in three movements: hearing the word, doing the word, and together these constitute pure religion.

## Hearing the Word

**James 1:19-20**

> Know this, my beloved brothers: let every person be quick to hear, slow to speak, slow to anger; for the anger of man does not produce the righteousness that God requires.

### The Connection Between Hearing, Speaking, and Anger

James places these three imperatives together for good reason. They are not separate virtues but interconnected dimensions of the same posture. When we are quick to speak, there is no room for hearing. When we are quick to anger, hearing also ceases. In fact, anger functions as the direct opposite of listening.

When someone becomes quickly angry, that anger produces agitation and defensiveness. Pride rises up as a protective barrier, and the person feels threatened. All of this prevents genuine hearing. Think of the worst argument you've experienced—perhaps trying to persuade your parents about something important. Anger clouds thinking and judgment. Emotions prevent clear reasoning and openness to another perspective.

This kind of anger, James tells us, does not produce the righteousness that God requires. It is incompatible with being a person who truly listens. (Note: while God Himself becomes angry at sin, and we ought to be grieved by sin, the context here concerns human temper and pride.)

**James 1:21**

> Therefore put away all filthiness and rampant wickedness and receive with meekness the implanted word, which is able to save your souls.

### Listen Because It Saves

This verse captures the entire thrust of James's letter. They must listen because this word is able to save their souls. This is the word that speaks of Jesus's death—paying the penalty for our rebellion against God—and of His resurrection, which grants us new birth into a new and obedient life. This is the implanted word, the word of truth referenced in verse 18.

But how are they to receive it? With meekness—with humility and teachability, not with anger or proud arrogance. God seeks people who listen with genuine humility.

This recalls Isaiah 66:2, a passage that speaks to those who think they have it all figured out:

> This is the one to whom I will look: he who is humble and contrite in spirit and trembles at my word.

God esteems the one who is humble, contrite, and meek—the one who receives and listens to His word with reverence.

### The Practice of Listening

A few years ago, while applying to Bible college, I underwent a psychological evaluation. It was one of the best experiences of my life, simply because I encountered someone who listened carefully. The psychologist was slow to speak, never completing my sentences or rushing to judgment, and quick to listen—attentive, asking genuine questions. My defenses came down. I wanted to talk. Even where I disagreed with her assessments, I left that conversation feeling truly heard. How different this is from arguing with your parents!

**Application:** Be quick to hear, slow to speak, slow to anger. This is precisely the attitude God wants us to have toward His word.

What does this mean practically?

**Listening means being slow to find fault with God's word.** Non-Christians often immediately jump to objections: "Isn't the Bible full of errors?" But they haven't read it seriously as adults. Pride prevents openness—we don't want to lose the argument. Even if contradictions appear, we must work through them carefully. We cannot simply discard God's word; we must reconcile it. If you're here tonight and not yet a Christian, I invite you to listen with an open mind about Jesus.

But this challenge extends to Christians too. I'm often frustrated by debates about women's ordination. Yes, Galatians 3 speaks of equality in Christ. But we cannot ignore other passages like 1 Timothy 2. We must listen to all of Scripture and work toward genuine reconciliation, not dismiss parts we find inconvenient by saying, "Paul was writing in a patriarchal society."

**Do not stand over God's word, even if the speaker is poor.** A bad speaker—slow, unfunny, struggling with English—is no excuse for not listening. Even if the preacher mishandles a passage, God's word remains before you. A weak speaker may distract but can never prevent you from hearing God's truth. Instead, be like the person who, after a poor sermon, discussed the passage with me and spoke of how it prompted him to repent of spiritual complacency. That is the model of listening.

**Let us be quick to listen, wanting to obey His word.** For this word is able to save your very soul! It is a gift from the Father of the heavenly lights—God who made the stars, moon, and sun.

## Doing the Word

**James 1:22-24**

> But be doers of the word, and not hearers only, deceiving yourselves. For if anyone is a hearer of the word and not a doer, he is like a man who looks intently at his natural face in a mirror. For he looks at himself and goes away and at once forgets what he was like.

### The Tragedy of Hearing Without Doing

James uses a vivid image: a man gazes intently at his reflection in a mirror, then walks away and immediately forgets what he looked like. How many of us remember what we saw in the mirror this morning? Not much, is it? You look, you acknowledge you look fine, and you move on. It's a fleeting, inconsequential moment.

Notice that the man's gaze is sincere—he looks *intently*. Yet his intention bears no fruit. He is double-minded, like the person described in chapter 1—one who hears and believes but does not act. Such a person is not blessed.

**James 1:25**

> But the one who looks into the perfect law, the law of liberty, and perseveres, being no hearer who forgets but a doer who acts, he will be blessed in his doing.

### The Law of Liberty

In contrast stands the blessed person—the one God approves. This is the doer, the one who acts and perseveres. But what is this "law of liberty"? Liberty and law seem contradictory: liberty implies freedom while law implies restriction. And what does James mean—Jewish dietary laws? The Ten Commandments? Sacrificial laws?

Looking ahead to James 2:8 and 2:12 provides clarity. James writes:

> If you really fulfil the royal law according to the Scripture, You shall love your neighbour as yourself.

And later:

> So speak as those who are to be judged under the law of liberty.

The law of liberty is the law that liberates people to love. It is liberation from wickedness and filthiness into a life of ministry to one another and the world. Or better said: it is the law that liberated people follow and obey.

Consider the scene in *Gladiator* where Russell Crowe is freed from chains. He is liberated, but liberation without purpose is meaningless. Christians are not left in the dark about their liberation. We are freed to love, to serve one another. This is the law of liberty.

Such a person is blessed—the one who keeps doing, persevering to obey God despite trials of many kinds. This person hears the implanted word *and then does it*.

**Application:** Just do it.

As Christians, we are liberated to love and do good works. What is the point of claiming to be a Christian if there is no evidence of it? It may be wonderful to see everyone at church with their Bibles, but it is self-deceiving if you go home and everything is forgotten. What would be the purpose?

What you have learned and been convicted of must be translated into action. How are you loving one another? We need to be real people who genuinely love and care for the welfare of others. The rest of James will give practical examples, but will you actually do them?

Sometimes it's not that we don't *want* to. Many of us hear and are convicted by the Bible's truth and agree that something is the right thing to do. Yet we drag our feet. This is not doing—it is procrastination. If you believe from God's word that something is right, do it. Don't delay.

**Practical examples:**

- **After church:** Talk about what you've heard and what you'll do about it. Encourage one another. Ask each other a week later how you're progressing.

- **Service opportunities:** When a working bee is organized and you're free, commit to it. Don't come up with excuses.

- **Financial giving:** If you're convicted about supporting the church, stop putting it off with credit card excuses. Fill out the form right now.

- **Full-time ministry:** If you're a gifted young person sensing God's call to ministry, don't offer a hundred excuses when asked to consider it. If you believe it's good to pursue, then pursue it.

Don't let God's word die through a thousand good intentions, like New Year's resolutions that fade by January 15th. Being a Christian is not merely hearing but also doing. It's a complete package. Hearing and doing—this is true religion.

## This Is Pure Religion

**James 1:26-27**

> If anyone thinks he is religious and does not bridle his tongue but deceives his heart, this person's religion is worthless. Religion that is pure and undefiled before God and the Father is this: to visit orphans and widows in their affliction, and to keep oneself unstained from the world.

### Redefining Religion

Pure religion is this: to visit orphans and widows in their affliction and to keep oneself unstained from the world. Surprising, isn't it? You might expect James to say prayer, or fasting, or some spiritual discipline. But he doesn't.

I must confess I dislike the word "religion" because it suggests a merit system—earning points with God through good works. That is not Christianity. Jesus has done something for us that we can never repay and never earn. It is pure grace, His gift to us from first to last.

But what does James mean by this term? In his time, for many Jewish Christians, "religion" meant Judaism—maintaining ritual purity and separation from the world through observance of the law. This is what Paul claimed to have pursued with zeal (Acts 26:5).

James, however, redefines religion for the dispersed Christians scattered throughout the world, those mixing with and being influenced by their pagan surroundings. He has heard them say, "I am religious. I have fasted. I observe the laws." And James essentially replies: "If you claim to be religious, then show me. Not through fasting and ritual. Show me through your deeds. Show me how you differ from a world of rampant selfishness and wickedness."

### The Distinction from the World

Christians are not like the world. The distinction the world should see is not their adherence to rituals but their transformation into a new people created to do good works. As Jesus said:

> By this all men will know that you are my disciples, if you love one another. (John 13:35)

The law of liberty differentiates us from the world. Rather than anger, blame, and wickedness, Christians are to visit orphans in affliction—to visit with practical gifts. This is concretely what it means to keep unstained from the world.

Like a furniture package deal, being a Christian comes as a complete package. Practice must follow belief. What we hear to be true must be demonstrated in our actions.

**Application: If you claim to be religious, show it.**

Our God is neither deaf nor silent. Therefore, we are not a deaf and unresponsive people. When we hear God's truth, we must do it. If we don't, we should examine ourselves—either we are not truly hearing, or we don't believe it.

James challenges us on three fronts:

**First, consistency.** Whether you became a Christian recently or decades ago, a life of good deeds is what you've signed up for. Otherwise, you're deceiving yourself into thinking all is spiritually well. Examine yourself: Does your walk match your talk? You say Christ is important—when did you last share the gospel? You say the church is important—are you serving the congregation with full effort?

**Second, practical love for the vulnerable.** Be different from a world of rampant selfishness by actively loving and caring for one another, especially the weakest and neediest. It was wonderful to see some of you contributing to Eddie and Kylie's family a few weeks ago. Continue to look out for them. Consider also the Anglicare food collection upstairs.

**Third, the integration of faith and public life.** Christianity is not a private faith where you keep your opinions to yourself while your actions contradict your beliefs. What you do reflects what you believe to be true. Your faith affects everything—from what coffee you buy to how you handle trials to how you speak to one another tonight to how you treat the weakest among you. There is inward conversion, but also conversion of the hands, the mouth, and the wallet. Everything about a Christian should speak Christian.

## A Word of Grace

Hearing the word and doing the word—this is pure religion.

Don't be discouraged or disillusioned by this calling. We will always struggle to match our deeds with our beliefs. It's very hard, but keep persevering, for the crown of life awaits you. Faith that is untested is not worth much. An unfruitful life is not worth much. A faith that bears no fruit, endures no suffering, and perseveres through no trials is hollow.

But as we watch our deeds fail to match our beliefs, we must look once again to the law of liberty brought about by Christ. Jesus is the one who not only heard but did. He listened to His Father, did not retaliate in anger but obeyed His Father's word. On the cross, Jesus very practically demonstrated His love for us and His obedience. Let us keep trusting Jesus, the author and perfecter of our faith.

After the announcements and green cards, there will be an opportunity to practice what we have heard today—to practice that law of liberty. So let's stay and look for people whom we can serve.
