# John 21:1-25

**Title:** Epilogue: The Post-Easter Challenge

**Date:** 28 April 2019

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## What Comes After Easter?

We often imagine that Easter brings a happy ending—rest, recuperation, recovery from the chocolate eggs. Perhaps we catch up on homework, work on home renovations. But what did Jesus and the disciples actually do in those days following the resurrection?

After Jesus died for our sins on Good Friday and rose from the dead on Easter, He showed Himself to the disciples and others. Yet in this epilogue, we see something troubling: the disciples are regressing. They seem to have forgotten their calling.

## 1. Recollection: When Disciples Forget Their Purpose

### The Regression Begins

> Afterward Jesus appeared again to his disciples, by the Sea of Galilee (Tiberias). (21:1)

This is actually the third appearance of the risen Jesus to His disciples (as noted in 21:14), after He had appeared to more than 500 people during those forty busy days.

The chapter opens by recalling events from John 1—the same disciples, the same location. But notice what Peter does:

> "I'm going out to fish," Simon Peter told them, and they said, "We'll go with you." (21:3)

Jesus had originally called Peter and Andrew from their nets with these words: "Come, follow me, and I will send you out to fish for people." They left their nets to become fishers of men. Now, after everything they've witnessed, they're back to fishing for fish.

The disciples spent the whole night fishing and caught nothing. They had abandoned their calling for their old profession—and it yielded no fruit. When Jesus appears on the shore in the early morning, they don't recognize Him.

> Early in the morning, Jesus stood on the shore, but the disciples did not realize that it was Jesus. (21:4)

### The Subtle Power of Small Choices

> He said, "Throw your net on the right side of the boat and you will find some." When they did, they were unable to haul the net in because of the large number of fish. (21:6)

The difference between the left side and right side of the boat—just a few metres—makes all the difference. A tiny margin changed everything.

This teaches us something profound: the will of God is often determined by mere metres, by mere inches, by a jot or a tittle. Obeying or disobeying the Ten Commandments is the difference between a slight twist of interpretation or motivation. One phrase changed in a law can transform its entire meaning. One dollar stolen or returned makes all the difference in the world.

For Peter, the difference is between being a fisher of men or a fisher of fish.

### Why Does Peter Regress?

This should be a time of triumphalism—Christ has risen! Yet Peter retreats to his former life. Is it fear? Has the reality of faith finally hit the road, and he's found wanting? Perhaps the drama was all too hard, and he simply wanted to return to the simple life.

Christians have been called by the glory and goodness of Christ Himself. Yet like Peter, we face the temptation to regress. We return to our old habits, our former way of life. We want to go back to "fishing."

Sometimes life gets hard. Sometimes the task of service becomes overwhelming. Sometimes we put ourselves out there and get hurt. But regression is not the answer. Do you really love your old life that much? Would you go back to the very things that made you seek Christ in the first place?

> It would have been better for them not to have known the way of righteousness than to have known it and then to turn away from the holy commandment passed on to them. Of them the proverbs are true: "A dog returns to its vomit," and, "A sow that is washed goes back to her wallowing in the mud." (2 Peter 2:21-22)

We see this pattern everywhere: someone left a destructive relationship, found Jesus, repented completely—then returns to another foolish relationship, saying "it's different this time." Someone was burnt out from meaningless work, discovered real purpose in Christ, but after a few years feels the itch to return to the craziness, forgetting the pain and sacrifice involved. Someone left a struggling church seeking better fellowship, found genuine community—then becomes too busy to attend, forgetting why they came in the first place.

You might be regressing without realizing it.

### The Picture of Spiritual Decline

Regression is like an old man who should have matured through life's experiences becoming senile, forgetting the past, regressing into childhood—forgetting how to eat, how to manage basic needs, how to maintain decency. You are regressing.

Or consider a middle-aged husband who has forgotten his vows to his wife. He starts dressing like a young man, buys a gold necklace, pursues a woman the same age as his eldest daughter, purchases a fast car. He wants to eat, drink, and have sex like he's twenty-one again. Muttons dressing as lambs.

The solution is to come back. To progress.

## 2. Recall the Shepherd: Remember Your Confession

Peter is urged to reconsider. Chapter 21 is a recapitulation for Peter of key events throughout John's Gospel—events from chapters 1, 6, 13, and 18. The aim is to help him remember Jesus.

### Remember Who Feeds You

Compare John 6 with John 21:

> When they landed, they saw a fire of burning coals there with fish on it, and some bread. (21:9)

> Jesus came, took the bread and gave it to them, and did the same with the fish. (21:13)

This echoes the feeding of the five thousand and Peter's pivotal confession:

> On hearing it, many of his disciples said, "This is a hard teaching. Who can accept it?" ... From this time many of his disciples turned back and no longer followed him. "You do not want to leave too, do you?" Jesus asked the Twelve. Simon Peter answered him, "Lord, to whom shall we go? You have the words of eternal life. We have come to believe and to know that you are the Holy One of God." (John 6:60-69)

What happened to those promises, Peter? You said Jesus is the one with bread—not just physical food but food from heaven, sustenance for the soul. Where has that conviction gone?

### The Echo of the Last Supper

> Then the disciple whom Jesus loved said to Peter, "It is the Lord!" As soon as Simon Peter heard him say, "It is the Lord," he wrapped his outer garment around him (for he had taken it off) and jumped into the water. (21:7)

Peter's action echoes Jesus in chapter 13:

> Jesus knew that the Father had put all things under his power, and that he had come from God and was returning to God; so he got up from the meal, took off his outer clothing, and wrapped a towel around his waist. After that, he poured water into a basin and began to wash his disciples' feet, drying them with the towel that was wrapped around him. (13:3-5)

Notice the contrast: Jesus removes His outer garment and wraps a towel around His waist to serve. Peter wraps his outer garment around himself. Jesus serves; Peter needs restoration because of his betrayal.

When Jesus asked Peter if he wanted his feet washed, Peter initially refused. Then he asked for a complete washing. This moment by the fire echoes that earlier washing—Peter is seeking forgiveness and restoration by jumping into the water, swimming toward Jesus.

### The Fire: A Call to Remembrance

The charcoal fire itself connects to John 18:

> It was cold, and the servants and officials stood around a fire they had made to keep warm. Peter also was standing with them, warming himself. (18:18)

At that fire, Peter had denied Jesus three times. Now, at another fire, Jesus will restore him three times. This is a recapitulation designed to help Peter remember—remember Jesus, remember your confession, remember hope, remember joy.

> Think of what you were when you were called. Not many of you were wise by human standards; not many were influential; not many were of noble birth. (1 Corinthians 1:26-27)

Remember that the hardest part is over. Remember that Jesus has already done it.

### When Doubt Comes

When you face doubt, go back to Christ. Return to first principles. Go back to church. Come back to fellowship on their terms, not yours. Return to your first love. Remember your baptism. Remember your confession. Remember the Gospel. Go back to the cross. Go back to the resurrection.

Ask yourself: What has really changed that causes me to doubt? Has new evidence emerged against the resurrection? No. Is the proof weaker than when you believed? No. You were convinced then—why isn't it good enough now?

Maybe it's not the information; perhaps you simply didn't expect life to be so hard. But suffering and persecution were predicted for those who follow Christ. You were ready to accept His call. Is life harder than anticipated? Who has shed blood for Christ?

Or is the Christian life simply boring? We live in one of the richest, smartest nations in the world—yet we have the highest suicide rates and don't even know the difference between a boy and a girl. And you can't find purpose in your life? Facing a hungry and starving world, seeing the Gospel's potential impact—don't you think Jesus might be giving you a grand narrative?

Reread the Bible. There's nothing wrong with your initial commitments.

## 3. Call to Discipleship: Feed My Sheep

Peter is now given the chance for restoration:

> When they had finished eating, Jesus said to Simon Peter, "Simon son of John, do you love me more than these?" (21:15)

Notice Jesus calls him "Simon son of John"—reverting to his original name, not "Peter the rock." And "these"—what does Jesus mean? The other disciples? The fish? The tools of fishing? Do you really love fish so much?

> "Yes, Lord," he said, "you know that I love you." Jesus said, "Feed my lambs." (21:15)

> Again Jesus said, "Simon son of John, do you love me?" He answered, "Yes, Lord, you know that I love you." Jesus said, "Take care of my sheep." (21:16)

> The third time he said to him, "Simon son of John, do you love me?" Peter was hurt because Jesus asked him the third time, "Do you love me?" He said, "Lord, you know all things; you know that I love you." Jesus said, "Feed my sheep." (21:17)

The restoration comes through the same command: love others. Serve others. The Greek words vary—feed the lambs (young believers), shepherd the sheep (the flock), feed the sheep (sustain them). But the pattern is consistent: service, care, nurturing.

### The Cost of Following

> Very truly I tell you, when you were younger you dressed yourself and went where you wanted; but when you are old you will stretch out your hands, and someone else will dress you and lead you where you do not want to go. (21:18)

Jesus then predicts Peter's martyrdom—that he will die glorifying God. And with that prediction comes the final command:

> Follow me! (21:19)

### What Does "Feed My Sheep" Mean?

- Feed my sheep through pupil-free days and discipleship opportunities
- Feed my sheep through Sunday School and youth ministry
- Feed my sheep by reading the Bible to your children
- Feed my sheep by looking at a hungry and starving world and finding ways to serve

### The Final Temptation

> Peter turned and saw that the disciple whom Jesus loved was following them. When Peter saw him, he asked, "Lord, what about him?" Jesus answered, "If I want him to remain alive until I return, what is that to you? You must follow me." (21:20-22)

Even after restoration, Peter attempts one final dodge: "What about him?" Someone else can do it. You do it.

But Jesus' answer is clear: "What is that to you? You must follow me."

### The Question Remains Open

The text leaves us without Peter's final response. We're not told what he says. We only know from history that Peter eventually did sacrifice his life for the church and the Gospel. According to tradition, he requested to be crucified upside down so he wouldn't be compared to Christ.

But at the moment of writing, the ending is deliberately ambiguous—because it's not just Peter's decision. It's ours.

## The Call That Awaits

> Jesus did many other things as well. If every one of them were written down, I suppose that even the whole world would not have room for the books that would be written. (21:25)

What comes after Easter? Christ's sacrifice calls us to sacrifice. Christ's service calls us to serve. The brave soldiers of Gallipoli—10% of a small nation voluntarily enlisted without conscription—gave their lives because Christ had given His.

> For Christ's love compels us, because we are convinced that one died for all, and therefore all died. (2 Corinthians 5:14)

The question hangs over us as it hung over Peter: Will you follow me?
