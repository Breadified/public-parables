# Romans 14:1–15:6

**Title:** Serve the Weak  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## When Christians Disagree

Fighting in families is normal, and it's no different in the church family. When Christians disagree about matters that aren't central to salvation, what should we do? Paul addresses this directly in Romans 14, offering three essential principles for how we treat brothers and sisters whose faith differs from ours.

## 1. They Are Accepted by God

> Accept him whose faith is weak, without passing judgment on disputable matters. (Romans 14:1)

All Christians are accepted by God. This is the foundation: if someone has put their trust in Jesus's death and resurrection, God has accepted them. Yet among believers, there are different levels of spiritual maturity.

The tension Paul addresses often emerged between Jewish Christians and Gentile Christians. The Jewish Christians came from centuries of Torah observance—generations who had never eaten pork, who had been trained by their entire culture to regard certain foods as unclean. Even though Jesus declared all foods clean (Mark 7) and Paul himself held this conviction, a Jewish believer couldn't simply override a lifetime of conditioning.

This is like our instinctive reaction to cockroaches. Rationally, they're just insects like any other. But our reflexes cause us to panic. For the Jewish Christian, pork triggered the same visceral response—not from sin, but from deep, ingrained conscience.

> The man who eats everything must not look down on him who does not, and the man who does not eat everything must not condemn the man who does, for God has accepted him. (Romans 14:3)

When Paul says "don't judge," he doesn't mean we should never exercise discernment. Christians are called to distinguish between true and false teaching, to evaluate right and wrong. But "being judgmental" means something different—it means looking down on someone, assuming superiority, implying they're worse than you are.

> Who are you to judge someone else's servant? (Romans 14:4)

We live in a church family with Christians at every stage of maturity. Some have studied Scripture for decades; others are new believers with much to learn. It's natural that younger believers won't know as much, or may take years to accept what they've come to understand. This calls for patience and humility. We're all imperfect—maybe not in this particular area, but certainly in others. Rather than being quick to criticize, we should remember that we stand together as family, all equally sinners needing God's grace.

## 2. They Are Someone Else's Servant

> Who are you to judge someone else's servant? To his own master he stands or falls. And he will stand, for the Lord is able to make him stand. (Romans 14:4)

Each Christian's ultimate accountability is to God, not to us. Everything we do—our eating, our observance of special days—is ultimately done for the Lord.

> He who regards one day as special, does so to the Lord. He who eats meat, eats to the Lord, for he gives thanks to God; and he who abstains, does so to the Lord and gives thanks to God. (Romans 14:6)

> For none of us lives to himself alone and none of us dies to himself alone. If we live, we live to the Lord; and if we die, we die to the Lord. So, whether we live or die, we belong to the Lord. (Romans 14:7–8)

Because everything is ultimately between you and God, your own conscience matters. You must be fully convinced in your own mind about what is right.

> Each one should be fully convinced in his own mind. (Romans 14:5)

If you believe something is wrong, don't do it—regardless of peer pressure or what others around you are doing. This is crucial. When you act against your own conviction, you train yourself to ignore your conscience. You create a dangerous habit: believing something is wrong but doing it anyway. That's spiritually corrosive.

This is why the strong shouldn't tempt the weak to violate their conscience. Once you set that precedent—once you show a believer they can disregard their own convictions—you've done real damage to their spiritual formation.

> Blessed is the man who does not condemn himself by what he approves. But the man who has doubts is condemned if he eats, because his eating is not from faith; and everything that does not come from faith is sin. (Romans 14:22–23)

Yes, you must work out the truth. It's not enough to simply avoid something because you don't like it. You answer to God, not just to church leaders. You'll stand before His judgment seat and give an account of yourself. So have a justifiable case.

This is what the Reformation recovered: the doctrine of Scripture alone. Each person, with help from others, can work out what Scripture plainly teaches. The church offers guidance and persuasion, but ultimately, what you believe is between you and God. No earthly institution can truly force faith.

## 3. Look After the Weak

> Therefore let us stop passing judgment on one another. Instead, make up your mind not to put any stumbling block or obstacle in your brother's way. (Romans 14:13)

If you're spiritually strong, don't harm the faith of others. Don't cause them to stumble, don't distress them, don't destroy the work God is doing in their lives.

> If your brother is distressed because of what you eat, you are no longer acting in love. Do not by your eating destroy your brother for whom Christ died. (Romans 14:15)

> Do not destroy the work of God for the sake of food. All food is clean, but it is wrong for a man to eat anything that causes someone else to stumble. (Romans 14:20–21)

These aren't matters of fundamental right or wrong. Whether someone eats pork or becomes vegetarian is no more morally significant than what pasta they prefer. These disagreements aren't worth the damage they cause.

> For the kingdom of God is not a matter of eating and drinking, but of righteousness, peace and joy in the Holy Spirit. (Romans 14:17)

Instead of insisting on your rights, consider what's best for the other person—what builds them up spiritually.

> Because anyone who serves Christ in this way is pleasing to God and approved by men. Let us therefore make every effort to do what leads to peace and to mutual edification. (Romans 14:18–19)

Notice: serving others in love—that's serving Christ.

> We who are strong ought to bear with the failings of the weak and not to please ourselves. Each of us should please his neighbor for his good, to build him up. (Romans 15:1–2)

> For even Christ did not please himself. (Romans 15:3)

Christ himself didn't insist on his rights. He laid them aside for our sake. That's our model.

> May the God who gives endurance and encouragement give you a spirit of unity among yourselves as you follow Christ Jesus, so that with one heart and mouth you may glorify the God and Father of our Lord Jesus Christ. (Romans 15:5–6)

## Application: The Church Family

This principle applies across many areas. Consider the church's traditional stance against alcohol. We know alcohol's devastating impact—the statistics on addiction in Australia are sobering. Wine itself isn't condemned in Scripture, but we've chosen to exclude it (except communion wine) because we don't know who among us struggles with addiction. Addicts rarely advertise their struggle. So out of love for the weak, we avoid putting temptation before them. This isn't legalism; it's sacrifice.

The problem we often face is that we judge our behavior by what the strong can handle. But Paul calls us to judge by what matters to the weak. On this Mother's Day, as we celebrate family, let's remember our church family. Let's accept one another because Christ died for us all.
