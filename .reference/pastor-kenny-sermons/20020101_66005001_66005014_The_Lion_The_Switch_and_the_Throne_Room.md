# Revelation 5:1-14

**Title:** The Lion, The Switch, and the Throne Room
**Date:** 12 January 2002
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Throne Room

John's vision opens with a grand, cinematic scene panning into God's throne room. There, in the right hand of the One seated on the throne, is a scroll—written on both sides and sealed with seven seals.

> Then I saw in the right hand of him who sat on the throne a scroll with writing on both sides and sealed with seven seals. (Revelation 5:1)

This scroll is comprehensive, full of information. It represents God's vision and plans for the world—His blessings for all creation. To open these seals is to implement His purposes.

But there's a problem.

A mighty angel proclaims in a loud voice: "Who is worthy to break the seals and open the scroll?" The challenge echoes over all heaven—who is good enough? Who can implement God's vision?

> But no one in heaven or on earth or under the earth could open the scroll or even look inside it. (Revelation 5:3)

No one anywhere in this universe is worthy enough. The tragedy is complete. And so John weeps—deep, genuine weeping. He feels the agony and hopelessness, the grief of unfulfilled promises. Is there no one who can do this?

Then comfort comes. One of the elders—one of the victorious ones in heaven—speaks:

> Do not weep! See, the Lion of the tribe of Judah, the Root of David, has triumphed. He is able to open the scroll and its seven seals. (Revelation 5:5)

Hope! The Lion is worthy.

## The Lion

The Lion is powerful. Power means strength—and the Lion is mighty. Power means authority—the Lion rules with wisdom, the King of Beasts. Power means security and trust; you'd place your money in a bank bearing the lion's logo. Across the world, lions appear on coats of arms as symbols of kingship and monarchy, even in lands where they've long been extinct.

The Lion of Judah is Jesus—God's chosen representative, descended from King David. Since David's reign, Israel had longed for a king as great as he. Finally, here He is: the One who would conquer invaders, crush enemies, and bring an era of peace. The majestic, powerful Lion to rule the world.

## The Switch #1: Lion to Lamb

John's eyes light up. He turns to see the worthy Lion. But what does he find?

> Then I saw a Lamb, looking as if it had been slain, standing in the center of the throne, encircled by the four living creatures and the elders. (Revelation 5:6)

Instead of the mighty Lion, John sees... a sheep. Not even a full-grown one—a lamb. And slain.

The powerful Lion switches to become the powerless Lamb.

How can a weak, broken lamb do the job of a king? Why would the Lion choose powerlessness? Jesus could have assembled an army. He could have called a legion of angels to fight for Him. At a stroke, the kingdoms of the world would be His. He could have come victoriously. Instead, He chose to be slain.

Why? Because God's vision is the suffering Christ. God's vision is the gospel—the good news of Jesus dying to save us from sin's penalty. Jesus who disarms Himself of all power and crawls like a baby into the battlefield.

### Our Vision of the Gospel

How do we perceive the gospel today? Often we see it triumphally—prosperity, all problems solved, triumphalistic Christianity. But the gospel appears weak. It doesn't look grand or triumphalistic or aspirational. It looks like:

- Small groups loving each other
- One-to-one connection
- Welcoming the stranger
- Humble, patient service

We judge the gospel through worldly eyes rather than Jesus' eyes. We look at the pathetic Lamb and see no power. We'd rather trust the world's standards—wealth, wisdom, strength—for power.

But our desire for worldly props stops us from seeing true strength. We use the trappings of this world to feel successful: the school we attended, the university course we're studying, the graduate job we'll have. We use clothing brands, the latest technology, giant conferences, and impressive campus groups to make ourselves feel significant. We chase status and labels of success, hoping others will be impressed.

Instead of becoming weak, pathetic lambs, we'd rather make a name for ourselves.

But the powerless Lamb cuts across these perceptions. God's vision is a Lamb.

We must learn to trust a Lamb. It's hard for us, especially when we're young and haven't been tested. Only when we face real suffering—when we're terribly sick, fail exams, lose our popularity, or experience death—do we discover whether we truly trust God. Only when our earthly props give way do we see where our trust really lies.

Jesus is the Lion stripped of all props. Strip away the mane, mute the roar, file down the nails and teeth, shrink the size, and what remains looks very much like a slain Lamb. Jesus, relying not on Himself but on God.

Can we trust God? Can we rely on the powerful God? Yes—and here is proof that it is worth it.

## The Switch #2: Lamb to Powerful

In a split second, the slain Lamb switches again—to become powerful.

> Then I saw a Lamb, looking as if it had been slain, standing in the center of the throne, encircled by the four living creatures and the elders. He had seven horns and seven eyes, which are the seven spirits of God sent out into all the earth. (Revelation 5:6)

This mortally wounded Lamb is standing. He is alive. He stands in a place of great prominence—at the center of the throne, surrounded by all creation's witnesses. He has seven horns, signifying complete power, and seven eyes, pointing to complete knowledge. He possesses the complete Spirit of God. The Lamb is promoted to the highest status.

The Lamb is at once crucified and powerful. Here is the absurdity—weakness and power together. It is this Lamb who comes and takes the scroll from God's right hand.

> He came and took the scroll from the right hand of him who sat on the throne. (Revelation 5:7)

The Lion was said to be worthy to take the scroll. Yet the Lion is never seen or heard from again in Revelation. Only the Lamb—the one who looks as if it had been slain—is worthy to take the scroll. The weak baby rescues the world.

### Heaven Responds

When the Lamb takes the scroll, all creation bows:

> And when he had taken it, the four living creatures and the twenty-four elders fell down before the Lamb. Each one had a harp and they were holding golden bowls full of incense, which are the prayers of the saints. (Revelation 5:8)

Then countless angels join in worship:

> Then I looked and heard the voice of many angels, numbering thousands upon thousands, and ten thousand times ten thousand. They encircled the throne and the living creatures and the elders. In a loud voice they sang: "Worthy is the Lamb, who was slain, to receive power and wealth and wisdom and strength and honor and glory and praise!" (Revelation 5:11-12)

Thousands upon thousands—a number so vast it defies counting. In a voice of deafening praise, they declare the Lamb worthy of sevenfold adoration: power, wealth, wisdom, strength, honor, glory, and praise.

And then—remarkably—every creature in all creation joins the chorus:

> Then I heard every creature in heaven and on earth and under the earth and on the sea, and all that is in them, singing: "To him who sits on the throne and to the Lamb be praise and honor and glory and power, for ever and ever!" (Revelation 5:13)

Earlier, in chapter 4, all glory belonged only to the One on the throne. Now that same glory is given to the Lamb. Jesus is raised to the right hand of God and exalted. The highest honor reserved for God alone is now also given to the Lamb—because He is God and He is worthy.

The Lamb is so powerful that heaven's theme song has changed. A new song is sung:

> You are worthy to take the scroll and to open its seals; because you were slain, and with your blood you purchased men for God from every tribe and language and people and nation. You have made them to be a kingdom and priests to serve our God, and they will reign on the earth. (Revelation 5:9-10)

Not only is a sacrificial animal now on God's throne, not only has heaven's attention turned to the Lamb—but the eternal song itself has changed. This is how worthy the Lamb is. Heaven's music has been rewritten.

## Application: It Is Worth It

Is following the way of the Lamb worth it? Is giving up time, money, possessions, relationships for the sake of God's kingdom worth it? Is being a loser in this world's eyes worth it? Is trusting God completely worth it?

Yes.

Jesus lived on this premise: it is far better to trust God, to be a loser, to die and go to heaven. This vision teaches us how to live. Power comes through trusting God—through being Lamb-like. Power comes from giving up the props of this world.

We are not suffering for nothing. This is not party-pooper theology. There is a real promise of real good things in the future, which sustains us in the present. If we can see heaven like this—if we can see the Lamb glorified—it gives us reason to give up the things of this world. Trusting and relying in God makes sense. It is worth it.

### Serve the World

The Lamb was slain to purchase people for God from every tribe, language, people, and nation. We who have been purchased now live for a new purpose: not to do as we please, but to serve sacrificially. We have been given not the power to sin, but the power to serve. We are priests, called to serve sacrificially.

The way to lion-like power is through lamb-like sacrifice. If you want to be a lion, be the lamb first.

### Serve Each Other

Why engage in the unattractive, boring areas of service—cleaning dishes, washing tea towels, wiping toilets, taking out garbage, talking to socially difficult people? It gets us nowhere. There's no popularity in it, no credit, no recognition. 

Yet there are countless lamb-like jobs behind the scenes at church: packing up, picking up rubbish, bringing in the bin, taking down the sign.

The Lamb on the throne shows us that not only is the way of the Lamb worth it—it is followable. We have been purchased for that very reason.

### The Church's Vision

Our vision for this year remains the same:

1. **To follow the Lamb**
2. **To serve each other**

Through lamb-like, sacrificial service, we become a kingdom. In this way, we reign over the earth—sacrificially serving. This is the vision of the Church.
