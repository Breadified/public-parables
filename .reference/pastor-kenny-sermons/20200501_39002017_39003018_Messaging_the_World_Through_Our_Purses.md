# Malachi 2:17–3:18

**Title:** Messaging the World Through Our Purses  
**Date:** 31 May 2020  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Opening: The Cry for Justice

The ancient Jews had lost hope in the 4th century BC. Released from Babylonian exile and returned to their promised land, they found it occupied by others—stragglers, nomads, and opportunistic peoples who had taken residence in the empty buildings. The towns and fields were no longer theirs. In their small carved-out piece of land, they had to start from scratch, rebuilding the temple while neighbors overcharged for materials, reploughing their fields, rebuilding houses, and digging irrigation systems. Harassed by their neighbors and bullied by the Persian governors, they faced famine. Their cry became desperate: "Where is God?"

In Malachi 2:17, they express this anguish:

> "You have wearied the Lord with your words. How have we wearied him? By saying, 'All who do evil are good in the eyes of the Lord, and he is pleased with them' or 'Where is the God of justice?'"

They asked: If this is God's promised land, why aren't we receiving what has been promised? Is God unfair? What is he doing about protecting his own people?

## 1. God Will Bring Justice

God's answer is clear: He is fair. He is just. So serious about justice is God that he promises to deal with injustice personally. God will come himself.

> **Malachi 3:1** "I will send my messenger, who will prepare the way before me. Then suddenly the Lord you are seeking will come to his temple; the messenger of the covenant, whom you desire, will come," says the Lord Almighty.

### The Messengers

The passage speaks of messengers preparing the way. The first messenger who prepares the way is John the Baptist. Mark 1:1–3 confirms this: "The beginning of the good news about Jesus the Messiah, the Son of God, as it is written in Isaiah the prophet: 'I will send my messenger ahead of you, who will prepare your way—a voice of one calling in the wilderness, "Prepare the way for the Lord, make straight paths for him."'"

John the Baptist called for repentance, baptizing people in a symbol of cleanliness to prepare them for what was coming. And John was himself a Levite—descendant of the tribe set apart for temple service, redeeming a lineage that had gone corrupt.

But his role, crucial as it was, points to something greater. The "messenger of the covenant" refers to Jesus himself. The text speaks of a Levite preparing the way for the Lord—a redemptive echo of Malachi 2:4–6, which describes the covenant with Levi as a covenant of life and peace, calling for reverence and true instruction. John fulfilled this for his time, and then Jesus came.

### The Lord Comes to His Temple

God coming personally to his temple is an act of justice. Malachi 3:5 describes what this looks like:

> "So I will come to put you on trial. I will be quick to testify against sorcerers, adulterers and perjurers, against those who defraud laborers of their wages, who oppress the widows and the fatherless, and deprive the foreigners among you of justice, but do not fear me," says the Lord Almighty.

Jesus did exactly this. He came to the temple and executed judgment:

> **John 2:13–17** "When it was almost time for the Jewish Passover, Jesus went up to Jerusalem. In the temple courts he found people selling cattle, sheep and doves, and others sitting at tables exchanging money. So he made a whip out of cords, and drove all from the temple courts, both sheep and cattle; he scattered the coins of the money changers and overturned their tables. To those who sold doves he said, 'Get these out of here! Stop turning my Father's house into a market!' His disciples remembered that it is written: 'Zeal for your house will consume me.'"

God takes justice seriously. When the boss comes down to check on something personally, you know it's serious—you won't get jerked around.

### Application: God Cares About Justice

God cares deeply about justice, and he cares that we are just in our dealings with others. This applies everywhere: stand against bullies at school, report wrongs to teachers, don't let injustice slide at work, advocate collectively through organizations like the Australian Christian Lobby for the welfare of vulnerable people. Live simply so you have more to share with others.

However, the social justice movement often couches Christian concern in terms of not caring or not loving. That's unfair. The real issue is a conflict about priorities in a world of unlimited problems. There's no solution without cost. Christians care about justice—the question is which injustices to prioritize and how best to address them. That's something to be debated respectfully, not dismissed as uncaring.

## 2. Israel Is Not Ready

But there's a profound problem with calling for justice: when God judges, he judges completely.

> **Malachi 3:2** "But who can endure the day of his coming? Who can stand when he appears? For he will be like a refiner's fire or a launderer's soap."

Fire doesn't discriminate—it kills everything. A refiner's soap washes everything, including your wallet and your notes. Justice affects everyone, not just the guilty.

This is the catch: When you point one finger at injustice, four fingers point back at you. Israel wanted God to judge their enemies—the oppressors, the defrauders, the wicked. But were they themselves free of these sins? No. They were hypocrites, calling for equity with unclean hands.

### The Charge: Robbery

> **Malachi 3:7–8** "Ever since the time of your ancestors you have turned away from my decrees and have not kept them. Return to me, and I will return to you," says the Lord Almighty. But you ask, 'How are we to return?' ... 'But you ask, "How are we robbing you?" In tithes and offerings.'"

The tithe was part of Israel's economic system. The first tithe (Levite tithe) supported the Levites who served at the temple—the entire tribe of Levi, one-twelfth of the nation, needed provision. The second tithe (festival tithe) was for celebrating at the temple. The third tithe (poor tithe) was stored locally and distributed to the poor, widows, orphans, and strangers.

Israel was failing to give these tithes. They were robbing God by withholding what they had vowed to give.

### The Curse

> **Malachi 3:9** "You are under a curse—your whole nation—because you are robbing me."

Here's the dilemma: Israel calls for justice against their enemies, but they themselves are under a curse because they've failed to honor God with what he's asked. They want God to judge others while remaining blind to their own hypocrisy.

Yet God doesn't simply destroy them. He wants to keep his covenant with them. He promises to refine them like gold and silver:

> **Malachi 3:3** "He will sit as a refiner and purifier of silver; he will purify the Levites and refine them like gold and silver. Then the Lord will have men who will bring offerings in righteousness."

They will not be destroyed. They will be made righteous instead.

## 3. The Solution and the Blessed

The solution to Israel's dilemma—needing justice but unable to withstand it—comes through generosity and trust in God.

> **Malachi 3:10–12** "Bring the whole tithe into the storehouse, that there may be food in my house. Test me in this," says the Lord Almighty, "and see if I will not throw open the floodgates of heaven and pour out so much blessing that there will not be room enough to store it. I will prevent pests from devouring your crops, and the vines in your fields will not drop their fruit before it is ripe. Then all the nations will call you blessed, for yours will be a delightful land."

God's call is simple: return to obedience in giving. Trust him. And in return, he will bless them abundantly.

### Those Who Fear the Lord

The passage concludes by contrasting two groups:

> **Malachi 3:13–18** "You have spoken arrogantly against me... You have said, 'It is futile to serve God. What do we gain by carrying out his requirements and going about like mourners before the Lord Almighty? But now we call the arrogant blessed. Certainly evildoers prosper...' Then those who feared the Lord talked with each other, and the Lord listened and heard. A scroll of remembrance was written in his presence concerning those who feared the Lord and honored his name. On the day when I act, they will be my treasured possession. I will spare them, just as a father has compassion and spares his son who serves him. And you will again see the distinction between the righteous and the wicked, between those who serve God and those who do not."

There are those who say it's futile to serve God. They see the wicked prosper and conclude that righteousness doesn't pay. But there are others—the few who feared the Lord—who talk with each other, encourage one another, and honor God's name. God remembers them. On his day of judgment, they will be his treasured possession.

### Generosity as Worship

The old tithe system no longer applies directly to us. There is no Levitical class—Jesus is the true priest. There is no temple—we are the temple. We are not in the Promised Land. But the principle endures: generosity reflects our love and trust in God.

The Macedonian churches modeled this:

> **2 Corinthians 8:1–9** "The grace that God has given the Macedonian churches... In the midst of a very severe trial, their overflowing joy and their extreme poverty welled up in rich generosity. For I testify that they gave as much as they were able, and even beyond their ability. Entirely on their own, they urgently pleaded with us for the privilege of sharing in this service to the Lord's people... They gave themselves first of all to the Lord... For you know the grace of our Lord Jesus Christ, that though he was rich, yet for your sake he became poor, so that you through his poverty might become rich."

Jesus gave not 10%—he gave 100%. His body. His life.

> **2 Corinthians 9:6–7** "Whoever sows sparingly will also reap sparingly, and whoever sows bountifully will also reap bountifully. Each one must give as he has decided in his heart, not reluctantly or under compulsion, for God loves a cheerful giver."

We are commanded to be generous. But unlike the tithe, which was fixed, we have freedom. Not bound to 10% doesn't mean always giving less—sometimes it means giving more.

### Practical Steps

If you're not yet giving, start. Form the habit early. If you're already giving, evaluate whether it reflects God's priority in your life. Place giving at the top of your yearly budget—make it first, before other expenses. Set up automatic giving so it happens without hesitation.

For those with Asian family backgrounds, honor your parents as you're called to do. But remember that God is your Father. Your money must reflect his priorities, not just cultural obligations.

Know this: no one else will give to gospel work. Priority goes to supporting kingdom work, then to other worthy charities. Your giving messages the world about what matters to you.

### The Original Tithe Was Voluntary

Interestingly, the tithe originated as voluntary worship, not obligation:

> **Genesis 14:18–20** "Then Melchizedek king of Salem brought out bread and wine. He was priest of God Most High, and he blessed Abram, saying, 'Blessed be Abram by God Most High, Creator of heaven and earth. And praise be to God Most High, who delivered your enemies into your hand.' Then Abram gave him a tenth of everything."

Abraham's tithe was a freewill offering in response to blessing and gratitude. Jacob's tithe was voluntary (Genesis 28:22). This is the spirit we should recapture—giving not from compulsion but from overflow, from joy in God's goodness.

### The Blessed Are Protected From Judgment

Here lies the heart of the gospel: We approach God with unclean hands not because we've cleaned ourselves up, but because Jesus has taken the judgment away. By having judgment fall on Jesus, God can hear our petitions for justice and grant them.

> **Romans 4:7–8** "Blessed are those whose transgressions are forgiven, whose sins are covered. Blessed is the one whose sin the Lord will never count against them."

This is God's agenda for the world—that those with unclean hands might become friends with him. The gospel creates a new society, a new heaven and new earth where sin, suffering, and injustice are no more. And we participate in that future now through our generous, faithful obedience.

We don't need to go to the stars to find transcendence. We find it in God's presence, in his justice, and in the joy of giving ourselves to his purposes. His hand guides us, and his right hand holds us fast.
