# John 5:1-35

**Title:** The Signs of God: A Willing Jew?
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## An Offer You Can't Refuse

Jesus extends an offer that demands a response: willingness. Like real estate agents offering a sum you cannot refuse, or a shop attendant offering discounted sushi at the day's end, Jesus presents something impossible to turn down. But the question becomes: will you accept it?

This message explores three responses to Jesus in John 5:
1. **A Willing Jew** (1-18) — The paralyzed man who initially accepts
2. **Unwilling Jews** (31-47) — The Jewish leaders who refuse
3. **The Willing Jew** (19-30) — Jesus himself, who perfectly embodies the Father's will

---

## 1. A Willing Jew? (1-18)

### The Setting: Bethesda

> Some time later, Jesus went up to Jerusalem for one of the Jewish festivals. Now there is in Jerusalem near the Sheep Gate a pool, which in Aramaic is called Bethesda and which is surrounded by five covered colonnades.

Jesus arrives not at the temple, but at an ancient spa built on natural springs—likely with legendary healing properties. Archaeologists suggest the structure featured five colonnades with two pools and a supporting center column. It was Spa-radise.

### The Desperate Crowd

> Here a great number of disabled people used to lie—the blind, the lame, the paralyzed.

This was no luxury resort. Near the Sheep Gate (where livestock entered the city), the poor and marginalized sprawled across wet, unhygienic ground on simple mats, seeking relief. The contrast could not be sharper with modern spas.

### One Man's Long Suffering

> One who was there had been an invalid for thirty-eight years.

An invalid—a person with weakness and sickness. Consider what thirty-eight years means: no schooling, no sports, no job, no independence. While Kenny had lived thirty-nine years with travel, relationships, and experiences, this man had lived thirty-eight years paralyzed, wishing every day that his life could be different.

### The Crucial Question

> When Jesus saw him lying there and learned that he had been in this condition for a long time, he asked him, "Do you want to get well?"

The Greek word here is stronger than mere "wish"—it means "will." Jesus asks literally: "Do you *will* to be healed?" It's a question about intention and desire. Do you wish to be whole? To walk independently? To work? To visit loved ones? To care for aging parents?

> Then Jesus said to him, "Get up! Pick up your mat and walk." At once the man was cured; he picked up his mat and walked.

Immediately the healing manifests. The man picks up his mat and walks—a willing Jew! Like the Samaritan woman in chapter 4, this man receives wholeness from Jesus. He recognizes that earthly water—from this pool, from Jacob's well, from purification jars—cannot ultimately heal. Jesus is the true giver of life.

### A Troubling Response

Yet what follows reveals the man's true character:

**First,** when Jesus asks if he wants healing, the man doesn't say yes. Instead:

> "Sir," the invalid replied, "I have no one to help me into the pool when the water is stirred. While I am trying to get in, someone else goes down ahead of me."

He makes excuses and puts words into Jesus' mouth, as though Jesus was defending why he hadn't healed him yet.

**Second,** when Jewish leaders confront him about carrying his mat on the Sabbath, he blames Jesus:

> The day on which this took place was a Sabbath, and so the Jewish leaders said to the man who had been healed, "It is the Sabbath; the law forbids you to carry your mat." But he replied, "The man who made me well said to me, 'Pick up your mat and walk.'"

**Third,** he fails to recognize who healed him:

> So they asked him, "Who is this fellow who told you to pick it up and walk?" The man who was healed had no idea who it was, for Jesus had slipped away into the crowd that was there.

How could someone not know who healed them? He'd spent time with Jesus, yet didn't ask others or discover his identity.

**Fourth,** Jesus' warning is severe:

> Later Jesus found him at the temple and said to him, "See, you are well now. Stop sinning or something worse may happen to you."

A peculiar judgment. What has he been doing? Suspicion hangs over this man.

**Fifth,** most damning of all:

> The man went away and told the Jewish leaders that it was Jesus who had made him well.

He uses his brand new legs—the very gift of healing—to walk directly to the authorities and inform on Jesus. This triggers persecution that ultimately leads to the cross:

> So, because Jesus was doing these things on the Sabbath, the Jewish leaders began to persecute him... For this reason they tried all the more to kill him; not only was he breaking the Sabbath, but he was even calling God his own Father, making himself equal with God.

This unwilling man, who appeared willing at first, becomes the catalyst for Jesus' death. He had sinned—not merely physically, but in betraying his healer.

### Application: Sin No More

As Christians, we possess a great advantage: Christ has been revealed to us. We are born again and saved. Yet Jesus' command to this man applies equally to us:

> See, you are well now. Stop sinning or something worse may happen to you.

We must not jeopardize the salvation purchased at such cost. Consider the warning in Hebrews:

> It is impossible for those who have once been enlightened, who have tasted the heavenly gift, who have shared in the Holy Spirit, who have tasted the goodness of the word of God and the powers of the coming age and who have fallen away, to be brought back to repentance. To their loss they are crucifying the Son of God all over again and subjecting him to public disgrace.

The first time Christ was crucified, we didn't fully understand the price. But now, knowing what it cost him, to re-crucify him through unfaithfulness is inexcusable.

Many young believers postpone their faith to pursue academic or career excellence. Some journey overseas and never return to church. Others take a year off from faith to study for exams and never come back. Others begin dating unbelievers. These are the subtle ways we re-crucify Christ—not through dramatic apostasy, but through the slow erosion of commitment.

We live under grace purchased by Jesus' blood. He made you whole. Therefore, honor him and sin no more. If you sense yourself making decisions that compromise your allegiance to Jesus, talk with friends and leaders. We are all fallen creatures whom God has shown mercy, and he has given us to each other to encourage perseverance.

## 2. Unwilling Jews (31-47)

### The Broader Picture

The problem extends far beyond one man. This invalid represents something larger—the unwilling nation of Israel itself.

John's Gospel emphasizes the Jewish setting and context throughout chapter 5:
- Jesus is in Jerusalem (1)
- A Jewish festival (1)
- Bethesda is an Aramaic/Hebrew term
- The man's age—38 years—echoes Israel's wilderness wandering

In Deuteronomy 2:14:

> Thirty-eight years passed from the time we left Kadesh Barnea until we crossed the Zered Valley. By then, that entire generation of fighting men had perished from the camp, as the Lord had sworn to them.

Israel had been rescued from Egyptian slavery by Moses. At the threshold of the Promised Land, they feared the inhabitants and refused to enter. God punished their lack of faith by making them wander for 38 years. After 38 years, they stood at the doorstep again—given a second chance. But even then, the nation remained unwilling to fully enter.

Now, in Jesus' time, Israel faces its final offer:

> Yet you refuse to come to me to have life.

Literally: they "will not come." This is the unwilling Jew—not unable, but unwilling.

### Evidence Ignored

Jesus provides overwhelming testimony:

> If I testify about myself, my testimony is not true. There is another who testifies in my favor, and I know that his testimony about me is true. You have sent to John and he has testified to the truth. Not that I accept human testimony; but I mention it that you may be saved.

John the Baptist testified to Jesus' identity. Yet:

> John was a lamp that burned and gave light, and you chose for a time to enjoy his light.

They were willing—for a time. Then they turned away, just like the man at the pool.

### The Works Bear Witness

Jesus points to his miracles:

> For the works that the Father has given me to finish—the very works that I am doing—testify that the Father has sent me.

His works fulfill Isaiah's prophecy of the Messiah:

> Strengthen the feeble hands, steady the knees that give way; say to those with fearful hearts, "Be strong, do not fear; your God will come, he will come with vengeance; with divine retribution he will come to save you." Then will the eyes of the blind be opened and the ears of the deaf unstopped. Then will the lame leap like a deer, and the mute tongue shout for joy. Water will gush forth in the wilderness and streams in the desert. The burning sand will become a pool, the thirsty ground bubbling springs. In the haunts where jackals once lay, grass and reeds and papyrus will grow.

Jesus embodies these signs. Yet:

> You have never heard his voice nor seen his form, nor does his word dwell in you, for you do not believe the one he sent.

### Wilful Blindness

The Jews studied Scripture diligently:

> You study the Scriptures diligently because you think that in them you have eternal life. These are the very Scriptures that testify about me.

But they don't know because they do not *want* to know. Their will is set against knowing:

> I know that you do not have the love of God in your hearts. I have come in my Father's name, and you do not accept me; but if someone else comes in his own name, you will accept him. How can you believe since you accept glory from one another but do not seek the glory that comes from the only God?

Even Moses—the founder of Israel and their laws—will accuse them:

> But do not think I will accuse you before the Father. Your accuser is Moses, on whom your hopes are set. If you believed Moses, you would believe me, for he wrote about me. But since you do not believe what he wrote, how are you going to believe what I say?

### Why Believe at All?

Many ask: how can I know Christianity is true? There are several angles:

- **Internal coherency:** Christianity possesses internal logic, though consistency alone doesn't prove truth (fairy tales are internally consistent too)
- **Pragmatic value:** Does it work? Over the long term, yes—though the Christian life involves suffering, not guaranteed earthly glory
- **Historical evidence:** The strongest case rests on history and testimony. Did Jesus exist? What did he do and say? What did his followers do and say?

The facts are well-attested by ancient sources. Christianity has endured the most rigorous testing for two thousand years. Scripture has been carbon-dated and verified. The ideas have been tested and tried—Western liberalism itself is an experiment proving whether Christianity works.

But all evidence requires one thing: **willingness**. A little willingness to believe. A benefit of the doubt. A mustard seed of faith. A willingness to follow where the evidence leads. An acceptance that your question has been answered. Not arguing for argument's sake, but genuine openness.

## 3. The Willing Jew (19-30)

### Jesus Embodies the Father's Will

By contrast with unwilling Jews stands Jesus himself—the willing Jew who perfectly follows the Father's will:

> Very truly I tell you, the Son can do nothing by himself; he can do only what he sees his Father doing, because whatever the Father does the Son also does.

Jesus is not robotic obedience but perfect alignment. His mindset, attitude, decisions, and actions are synchronized with the Father's. They are one:

> For the Father loves the Son and shows him all he does. Yes, and he will show him even greater works than these, so that you will be amazed.

Like a masterful father teaching his son the family trade, the Father shows the Son everything and attests to his qualifications. The Father loves the Son and teaches him completely.

### The Work of Life

> For just as the Father raises the dead and gives them life, even so the Son gives life to whom he is pleased to give it.

The Father is author of life. The Son, in perfect alignment with the Father, gives life to whom he wills.

### The Work of Judgment

> Moreover, the Father judges no one, but has entrusted all judgment to the Son, that all may honor the Son just as they honor the Father. Whoever does not honor the Son does not honor the Father, who sent him.

All judgment is given to the Son. And therefore:

> Very truly I tell you, whoever hears my word and believes him who sent me has eternal life and will not be judged but has crossed over from death to life.

Jesus is the gatekeeper. He determines who enters resurrection life and who faces condemnation. He is the one who grants life and judges all humanity.

### Why We Need Jesus

The Father possesses life inherently and raises the dead. The Father judges the world. Both have been entrusted to the Son. Jesus declares: "I simply follow the Father's will." He acts under the Father's authority. From John 1, he is the Word made flesh—the glory of God manifested in human form.

This matters profoundly because if the fullness of God dwells in Jesus, then through Jesus we know exactly what God is like. We see that God is not distant, merely shouting commands, but that God is **love**. We see that God is **sacrificial**. We see that God is **full of grace and compassion, abounding in love**. We see that God **forgives**. We see that God is **willing**.

---

## Are You Willing?

Like the lame man, we are offered a chance to follow God. So the question remains: **Are you willing?**

Will we be like the Pharisees—unwilling to accept Jesus regardless of evidence?

Or will we be like Jesus, who says: "Not my will, but yours"? He accepts the Father's will and obeys perfectly. **Jesus is the Willing Jew**. Willingly and lovingly, he goes to the cross for us.

The signs point toward one requirement: **willingness**. But will you accept the offer?

## Appendix: The Sabbath

### The Original Commandment

> Remember the Sabbath day by keeping it holy. Six days you shall labor and do all your work, but the seventh day is a sabbath to the Lord your God. On it you shall not do any work, neither you, nor your son or daughter, nor your male or female servant, nor your animals, nor any foreigner residing in your towns.

On the surface: no work.

But consider the context. During Israel's 38 years in the wilderness, God provided sufficient provisions each day—even for the Sabbath, when they didn't collect food. This law taught a fundamental principle: **trust God to supply your needs**.

### The Real Principle

The Sabbath rest didn't mean laziness. The Sabbath involved numerous ceremonies, rituals, and sacrifices. The prohibition centered specifically on work for food—laboring to gather, sell, cook, and prepare provisions (lighting fires, gathering wood). The principle was: **don't work for food; trust God to provide. Stop worrying about tomorrow's sustenance and trust in God's care.**

The deeper meaning: Even in uncertain times, Israel was to cease the endless labor for security and instead spend time looking forward to eternal rest. Rest means ceasing from the anxious pursuit of provision and remembering that God sustains life.

### Legalism's Corruption

But the Jewish leaders transformed this principle into suffocating legalism. Rather than maintaining the underlying principle, they created hundreds of additional bylaws. What counts as work? If you lie down thinking about plowing, is that work? If your donkey falls into a pit on the Sabbath, do you rescue it or let it die? If a log lands on your foot, may you remove it or must you stand there?

The result: 39 additional categories of law codified in the Mishnah. Carrying a mat became a violation. Like the Australian Income Tax Act—now 10,000 pages—the system became so complex that people spent lifetimes learning the rules and forgot the reason entirely. The nation lost sight of the original Sabbath. Isaiah's promise stood before them unfulfilled, and they were too consumed by legalistic minutiae to see it.

### Application for Today

Why does our church encourage Sunday gathering? Not from legalistic Sabbath observance (we meet on Sunday, not Saturday, and our practices differ). Rather, because practically and honestly, it's an effective way to encourage one another. Over history and culture, Sunday has become accessible to most. We gather at a set day, time, and place to:

- Look out for each other
- Learn from God's word
- Serve one another
- Remind ourselves not to be consumed by work

The temptation is real: work not just for today's food but tomorrow's, next week's, next season's. In an insecure world, we seek earthly security through labor and wealth rather than in God. Instead, we work for what we need and spend remaining time remembering eternal rest to come. We practice the Sabbath now in anticipation of heaven's actual Sabbath.
