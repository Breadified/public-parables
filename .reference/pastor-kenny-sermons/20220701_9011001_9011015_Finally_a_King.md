# 1 Samuel 11:1-15 & 12:1-25

**Title:** Finally a King

**Date:** 31 July 2022

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## The Battle We Have Been Waiting For

The narrative opens with Nahash the Ammonite laying siege to Jabesh Gilead. When the city's elders approach him to negotiate a treaty, Nahash responds with a brutal condition: he will only make peace if he is allowed to gouge out the right eye of every man in the city, thereby bringing disgrace upon all Israel. This is not merely a military tactic—it is calculated humiliation.

The elders of Jabesh ask for seven days to send messengers throughout Israel seeking rescue. If no one comes to their aid, they promise to surrender.

When news of this crisis reaches Gibeah, Saul is returning from the fields with his oxen—still engaged in farming, not in any kingly activity. Upon hearing the terrible terms Nahash has demanded, the Spirit of God comes powerfully upon Saul, and he is filled with righteous anger.

In a dramatic display of his newfound authority, Saul takes a pair of oxen, cuts them into pieces, and sends these pieces throughout Israel by messengers, declaring: "This is what will be done to the oxen of anyone who does not follow Saul and Samuel." The terror of the Lord falls upon the people, and they assemble as one force—three hundred thousand men from Israel and thirty thousand from Judah.

Saul sends word back to Jabesh Gilead: "By the time the sun is hot tomorrow, you will be rescued." The city, hearing this promise, tells the Ammonites they will surrender the next day.

That night, Saul divides his forces into three divisions and attacks the Ammonite camp during the last watch before dawn, slaughtering them until the heat of the day. The survivors are scattered, with none left together.

## The Temptation to Vengeance

After this decisive victory, the people want blood. They ask Samuel: "Who were those who said Saul should not be king? Bring them to us so we may put them to death."

But Saul shows mercy and wisdom, saying: "No one will be put to death today, for this day the Lord has rescued Israel."

This is a critical moment. Saul has just proven himself militarily capable, unified the nation, and defeated a foreign enemy. He now stands at a threshold where he could consolidate power through vengeance or through mercy. He chooses mercy, recognizing that today belongs to God's deliverance, not to human retribution.

## The Renewal of the Kingship

Samuel then calls the people to Gilgal to formally renew the kingship. Saul is crowned before the Lord, and the people celebrate with sacrifices and great rejoicing. Yet this "renewal" is not primarily about Saul—it is about renewing the people's relationship with God.

Samuel, now stepping aside from his role as judge, calls the nation to account. He stands before them and asks: "Whose ox have I taken? Whose donkey have I taken? Whom have I cheated or oppressed? From whose hand have I accepted a bribe?" The people unanimously affirm his integrity: "You have not cheated us or taken anything from anyone."

Samuel then reminds Israel of God's faithfulness throughout their history. He recounts how God appointed Moses and Aaron, delivered their ancestors from Egypt, and continuously rescued them from their enemies through leaders like Jerubbaal (Gideon), Barak, Jephthah, and Samuel himself.

## The Conditional Nature of Kingship

But Samuel makes a crucial point: Israel rejected God as their king. They saw Nahash threatening them and said, "We want a human king to rule over us"—even though the Lord their God was already their king.

Samuel declares: "Now here is the king you have chosen, the one you asked for; see, the Lord has set a king over you." But he immediately adds a condition that defines everything that follows: "If you fear the Lord and serve and obey him and do not rebel against his commands, and if both you and the king who reigns over you follow the Lord your God—good! But if you do not obey the Lord, and if you rebel against his commands, his hand will be against you, as it was against your ancestors."

To underscore this point, Samuel performs a sign. Though it is the wheat harvest season—an odd time for rain—Samuel calls upon the Lord, and God sends thunder and rain that very day. The people stand in awe, recognizing both God's power and the gravity of Samuel's warning.

## The People's Repentance

The people are now terrified. They cry out to Samuel: "Pray to the Lord your God for your servants so that we will not die, for we have added to all our other sins the evil of asking for a king."

Samuel's response reveals the heart of God's grace: "You have done all this evil; yet do not turn away from the Lord, but serve the Lord with all your heart. Do not turn away after useless idols. They can do you no good, nor can they rescue you, because they are useless."

He continues with a promise: "For the sake of his great name the Lord will not reject his people, because the Lord was pleased to make you his own. As for me, far be it from me that I should sin against the Lord by failing to pray for you. And I will teach you the way that is good and right."

But the condition remains: "Be sure to fear the Lord and serve him faithfully with all your heart; consider what great things he has done for you. Yet if you persist in doing evil, both you and your king will perish."

## The Heart of the Matter

The renewal at Gilgal is not ultimately about crowning Saul. It is about Israel renewing their covenant with God. The people had wanted a king like the other nations, believing that a human monarch would solve their problems. What they received instead was a stern reminder: no earthly ruler can save them. Only God can.

Saul is chosen, empowered by the Spirit, and crowned before the Lord. But from the very beginning, his kingship is conditional upon obedience to God. The true king of Israel has always been—and always will be—the Lord.
