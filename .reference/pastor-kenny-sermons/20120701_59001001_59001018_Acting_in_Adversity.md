# James 1:1-18

**Title:** Acting in Adversity  
**Date:** 18 July 2012  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Introduction: Understanding Trials

Have you ever felt your trust in Jesus and God's goodness being tested? Perhaps through unemployment, a difficult boss, chronic illness, family problems, relationship struggles, or loss? These trials are part of life—not persecution necessarily, but the ordinary hardships we all face.

I remember a friend from Bible college whose wife was pregnant with twins. The babies were due to be induced on a Saturday at 1pm, but at noon his wife began bleeding profusely. They rushed to the hospital, but it was too late. The babies, suffocating from lack of oxygen, were stillborn—dead just an hour before they were scheduled to arrive. At the funeral, my friend described it simply: "A bandit came into my home, stole my babies, and left a puddle of blood behind."

How do we face such tragedies? How should we respond to the sufferings, mistreatment, and disasters that come our way? James speaks directly to this question.

---

## 1. God's Testing Brings Life

### Rejoice in Your Trials (1:2)

> Consider it pure joy, my brothers, whenever you face trials of many kinds.

This sounds strange. We are complex creatures—capable of holding two states simultaneously. Even in disappointment, frustration, and sadness, we can be happy. Why? Because trials serve a good purpose.

### Trials Produce Perseverance (1:3-4)

> Because you know that the testing of your faith develops perseverance. Perseverance must finish its work so that you may be mature and complete, not lacking anything.

The trial produces perseverance—the ability to trust God and obey Him no matter what circumstances surround us. This perseverance is essential because our ultimate goal is maturity and completeness, to be like Jesus, lacking nothing.

We naturally wish there were a painless way for God to transform us instantly. But God has chosen testing as His method. This isn't strange when we consider how life works: testing separates the genuine from the fake. How else would we know which batteries last longest? God uses the same principle—the testing of our faith reveals and strengthens its quality.

**A Practical Illustration:** At the gym, the most tempting time to stop is when the exercise begins to hurt. Yet this is precisely when old muscle burns away and creates space for new growth. This is exactly why you're at the gym! Stopping now makes no sense. Similarly, when taking antibiotics, failing to complete the course means the infection returns. We must persevere to completion.

The question becomes: Do we persevere when hard times come? We need resilience, and we must teach our children to be resilient.

### Seek Wisdom in Trials (1:5)

> If any of you lacks wisdom, he should ask God, who gives generously to all without finding fault, and it will be given to him.

When facing trials, we can ask God for wisdom—perhaps wisdom to navigate the testing itself, or wisdom that leads to perseverance and maturity. God gives generously without finding fault.

### Trials Are Better Than Comfort (1:9-11)

> The brother in humble circumstances ought to take pride in his high position. But the one who is rich should take pride in his low position, because he will pass away like a wild flower.

The poor person undergoing trials—struggling to put food on the table, low on the social ladder—should take pride in his position. Why? Because if he perseveres and continues to do the right thing, he will become mature and complete. James understood something profound: trials are actually a sign that God loves us. They indicate we are His children.

> Endure hardship as discipline; God is treating you as sons. For what son is not disciplined by his father? (Hebrews 12:7-10)

In contrast, the rich are untested. Comfort and independence can be a disadvantage, not a blessing. An astronaut in space experiences zero gravity—his muscles never have to work hard, never face resistance. But when he returns to earth after a long stay in space, he collapses. Without gravity's resistance, his muscles have atrophied. Those who face resistance yet persevere will endure to the end. Those who live in comfort may fade away.

### Trials Lead to the Crown of Life (1:12)

> Blessed is the man who perseveres under trial, because when he has stood the test, he will receive the crown of life that God has promised to those who love him.

The crown symbolizes victory—like the crowns of the ancient Olympics. For the Christian, victory is life itself: eternal life, salvation, heaven. The man or woman who endures under testing receives this crown.

### Real Testimony: Joni Eareckson Tada

Joni became a quadriplegic nearly forty years ago after a diving accident. She suffers from crippling chronic pain and has recently been diagnosed with breast cancer. Yet she says:

> Only God knows why I was paralyzed. Maybe He knew I'd be ultimately happier serving Him. If I were still on my feet, it's hard to say how things have gone. I probably would have drifted through life—marriage, maybe even divorce—dissatisfied and disillusioned. When I was in high school, I reacted to life selfishly and never built on any long-lasting values, and almost always at the expense of others.

Trials expose our inadequacies and show us where we need to focus. John Calvin wrote: "If we are given gold, would we not test it to determine its value? If we doubted its genuineness, we would test it by fire...and so God does with us. The fire of affliction reveals the quality of our faith."

### A Warning About Our Ease

Many of us here in Australia live in comfort. Our exchange rate has risen 30% without us doing extra work. We subcontract tasks like childcare and cleaning to make life easier. We instantly gratify our wants with one click on the app store, charging it to our credit cards. Life is easier than ever.

We should be thankful for God's blessings. But there is a problem: if we are not maturing because of this ease, we are missing something crucial. Perhaps we should practice delayed gratification. Perhaps we should join the gym, fast during Lent, or give more of our wealth away. God wants a resilient people who remain steadfast when times are tough.

### Encourage One Another to Persevere

Not only do we need to be steadfast ourselves, we need to encourage each other. That is what church exists for—to remind each other to keep being Christian even through hard times. Sometimes people facing trials don't need solutions; they need a friend who cares for them and will listen.

We must also encourage our children to persevere. In our eagerness to care for them, we often protect them from failure so much that we prevent them from learning resilience. When they don't want to study, we get tutoring for them. When exams come, we allow them to skip chores. Every time it gets hard, we let them skip it. But this doesn't teach perseverance; it teaches them that someone else will solve their problems. One day, they will face a problem we cannot fix, and they will give up—not just in studies, but in their Christian faith.

Let us be a tested people. On that final day, there are only two options: the one who persevered and received the crown of life, or the one fading away in the hot sun.

## 2. Do Not Test God

Yet while God testing us leads to life, the reverse is not true. When we test God, it leads to death.

### How We Test God (1:6-8)

> But when he asks, he must believe and not doubt, because he who doubts is like a wave of the sea, blown and tossed by the wind. That man should not think he will receive anything from the Lord; he is a double-minded man, unstable in all he does.

When asking God for wisdom, we must ask in faith—believing that God will do what is good for us. Doubting here doesn't mean asking honest questions about Scripture or God's actions. Rather, it means being suspicious of God's intentions, thinking He is out to get us or is evil. Such a person is like waves of the sea, double-minded and unstable, wavering loyalties between two masters.

God will answer your request for wisdom. But you cannot then turn around and say the test is too hard or doubt God's goodness. How can you expect wisdom when deep inside you're skeptical of God's goodness and intentions? What you're actually doing is *testing God*—and that inevitably brings death.

### Testing God Brings Death (1:13-17)

> When tempted, no one should say, "God is tempting me." For God cannot be tempted by evil, nor does he tempt anyone; but each one is tempted when, by his own evil desire, he is dragged away and enticed. Then, after desire has conceived, it gives birth to sin; and sin, when it is full-grown, gives birth to death.

When tested, people blame God for their temptation. But this thinking leads to death. Here's the progression: temptation → desire → sin → death.

Earlier, the progression was: trials → perseverance → maturity/completeness/wisdom.

Now we see the reverse trajectory toward death.

God is not evil. We are evil. Sin comes not from without but from within us. God does not entrap people in their sins. In fact, God is good.

> Don't be deceived, my dear brothers. Every good and perfect gift is from above, coming down from the Father of the heavenly lights, who does not change like shifting shadows.

God is consistent and not sneaky. He is not double-minded. He doesn't decide one day to be merciful and another to be cruel. He is not playing games with us.

### How We Blame God Today

We often use trials as an excuse for lower standards:
- *Not my fault I'm poor—I'll be stingy and selfish.*
- *Not my fault I have a lousy, underpaid job—I'll just cheat the company.*
- *Not my fault I'm lonely—God didn't supply a Christian partner, so I'll marry a non-Christian instead.*
- *Not my fault movies are expensive—I'll download them illegally.*
- *Not my fault my parents stop me from attending church—I'll stop being Christian.*
- *Not my fault I'm sick—I'll cheat on exams.*

These desires come from within us, not from God. Testing can never justify sin. If you sin, you have already failed in perseverance. You cannot test God this way.

### God Has Already Been Tested (1:18)

> Of his own will he brought us forth by the word of truth, that we should be a kind of firstfruits of his creation.

God's goodness must never be doubted because He has already gone through an awful lot to prove it. By His own initiative, He sent His Son to redeem us from sin. He has made us to be a kind of firstfruits of His creation—beings born not to death, but to the firstfruits (v.18), made alive by the word of truth, the gospel of salvation.

> For God demonstrates his love for us in this: while we were sinners, Christ died for us. (Romans 5:8)

We will always fail in our trials—even as we try not to. But consider Jesus. He not only suffered like us; He actually persevered to the very end. Even crying "My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?" He remained obedient on that cross to the point of death. Jesus never wavered in where His loyalties lay; He never doubted God's goodness or intentions. He underwent all the tests and endured through obedience.

This is the God we must trust, the God we must persevere for, no matter what circumstances life holds. This is the God I remind my friend of: do not be deceived. Blessed is the man who remains steadfast under trial.

---

## Conclusion: Two Paths

There are two responses to trials and temptations. One path leads to joy, perseverance, wisdom, and life. The other leads to sin and death.

C.S. Lewis, in *The Screwtape Letters*, captures this profoundly. Satan recognizes that his cause is never in greater danger than when a human being, no longer desiring but still intending to do God's will, looks around at a universe from which every trace of God seems to vanished, asks why he has been forsaken—and still obeys. In the dull, monotonous years of middle-aged prosperity or adversity, when we have lost relish for our duties, these are the periods when we are most growing into the kind of creatures God wants us to be.

Friends, there are only two options on that last and final day: the one who has persevered and received the crown of life, or the one fading away in the hot sun.

Do not be deceived. Blessed is the man who perseveres under trial.
