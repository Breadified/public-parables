# Hebrews 13:7-25

**Title:** Persevering in Leadership & World  
**Date:** 9 September 2018  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Remember Your Leaders

> Remember your leaders, who spoke the word of God to you. Consider the outcome of their way of life and imitate their faith. (Hebrews 13:7)

The heart of this passage isn't about remembering the leader themselves, but about remembering the **word of God** they proclaimed. When I was young, my parents dropped me off at Sunday school for six years. I don't recall much of the content, but I remember Miss Kwok, our Sunday school teacher—a kind, middle-aged woman who was faithfully there every Sunday, leading us in singing and prayer. What impressed itself on my mind wasn't her personality or charisma, but the simple truth that there is a God who cares for me.

Years later, in youth group, various leaders—university students and parents—taught Bible studies, shared the gospel, and loaned me books. Everything began to make sense. They all shared one thing in common: they **spoke the word of God** to me.

### The Unchanging Message

> Jesus Christ is the same yesterday and today and forever. (Hebrews 13:8)

This doesn't mean nothing changes historically—Jesus isn't physically on earth now as He was then. Rather, it means Jesus Christ's character and the moral will of God remain constant. The word of God endures:

> Your word, LORD, is eternal; it stands firm in the heavens. (Psalm 119:89)

> For, "All people are like grass, and all their glory is like the flowers of the field; the grass withers and the flowers fall, but the word of the Lord endures forever." And this is the word that was preached to you. (1 Peter 1:24-25)

**Leaders come and go.** In the time Hebrews was written, the original leaders had likely been persecuted—some may have died for their faith. Perhaps some even recanted, falling away entirely. It was a time of great doubt. Yet the message remained steady: remember the word. Persecution doesn't change the gospel. Suffering doesn't alter God's truth.

Consider St. Matthias Church, where leadership changed because a former minister's life didn't reflect God's word. We remember our leaders to the extent they stood firm and remained consistent with Scripture. **The word speaks louder than the leader.**

> Do not be carried away by all kinds of strange teachings. (Hebrews 13:9)

The danger the Hebrew Christians faced was false teachers—perhaps Judaizers calling them back to Jewish religion rather than Christ. Charismatic leaders can be compelling, but if they contradict God's word, they are false teachers.

> It is good for our hearts to be strengthened by grace, not by eating ceremonial foods, which is of no benefit to those who do so. (Hebrews 13:9)

We are strengthened by God's grace, not by external rules or rituals. We judge good leaders by what they teach. We never judge the teaching by the leader. Think of 1 Corinthians 1, where Paul rebuked the church for becoming partisan—some following Paul, some Apollos, some Cephas. Christ is not divided. The gospel is what matters.

### The Application

When we lead, we must **speak the word of God.** When we follow, we must **remember the word of God.**

In God's wisdom, His word comes to us through people. God shapes us through others speaking His truth. As we grow from infancy in faith, we need protection from spiritual deception:

> Then we will no longer be infants, tossed back and forth by the waves, and blown here and there by every wind of teaching and by the cunning and craftiness of people in their deceitful scheming. Instead, speaking the truth in love, we will grow to become in every respect the mature body of him who is the head, that is, Christ. (Ephesians 4:14-15)

We are all simultaneously leaders and those being led—formally or informally. Parent to child, employer to employee, older to younger. The principle is simple: **speak the word when you lead; remember the word when you follow.**

## Consider the Outcome

> Consider the outcome of their way of life and imitate their faith. (Hebrews 13:7)

The word "outcome" means the trajectory, where someone's path ultimately leads. It's secondary to the word of God itself, but outcomes matter. A nutritionist who is morbidly obese or a financial advisor who is poor raises reasonable questions about the reliability of their teaching.

> No temptation has overtaken you except what is common to mankind. And God is faithful; he will not let you be tempted beyond what you can bear. But when you are tempted, he will also provide a way out so that you can endure it. (1 Corinthians 10:13)

The way to identify a false prophet is by wrong predictions:

> If what a prophet proclaims in the name of the Lord does not take place or come true, that is a message the Lord has not spoken. (Deuteronomy 18:22)

Similarly, there must be correlation between what someone says and what they do. **Consider the outcome of their way of life.**

> We have an altar from which those who minister at the tabernacle have no right to eat. (Hebrews 13:10)

This likely refers to church leaders who minister at the new "tabernacle"—the church itself. The principle is that **leaders eat last.** They are strengthened by grace, not by temporal provision. They sacrifice their own needs for those they serve.

### Why Leaders Matter

I've been watching Simon Sinek speak about leadership and the power of oxytocin—the chemical of trust and safety. Leaders create safe environments by sacrificing and giving generously. When a leader sacrifices, they trigger oxytocin in themselves and everyone who witnesses that generosity. Witnessing acts of human sacrifice causes our bodies to release oxytocin, making us feel safer and more comfortable. This creates a cycle: the safer we feel, the more generous we become; the more generous we are, the more oxytocin is released.

**Leadership is not a rank—it's a choice.** The choice to look to the person on your left and right and decide to serve them. Leaders identify themselves by action. When there's a problem and no one wants to fix it, the leader steps forward and says, "I will sacrifice to address this."

This isn't a consultant—someone who makes noise, writes a report, and leaves. This is a leader who gets their hands dirty. Imagine seeing a dirty toilet at church and thinking it's embarrassing if a visitor sees it. If you think someone should fix it, you're displaying the qualities of a follower—nothing wrong with that. But if you grab toilet paper, clean it yourself, and flush it, you likely possess the qualities of a leader. **The leader is a servant.**

> You know that those who are regarded as rulers of the Gentiles lord it over them, and their high officials exercise authority over them. Not so with you. Instead, whoever wants to become great among you must be your servant, and whoever wants to be first must be slave of all. For even the Son of Man did not come to be served, but to serve, and to give his life as a ransom for many. (Mark 10:42-45)

### Jesus: The Ultimate Leader

To see the example of the ultimate leader, we look to Jesus.

> The high priest carries the blood of animals into the Most Holy Place as a sin offering, but the bodies are burned outside the camp. (Hebrews 13:11)

In the Old Covenant sacrificial system, most animals killed at the altar could be eaten by priests—it was their livelihood, as they had no land to farm. But once a year, a special sin offering was brought into the temple itself. This animal was different. Instead of being eaten, it had to be taken **outside the camp and burned completely.** The sin was symbolically transferred to this animal, rendering it unclean.

> Then he shall take the bull outside the camp and burn it as he burned the first bull. This is the sin offering for the community. (Leviticus 4:21)

This practice illustrates what Jesus accomplished:

> And so Jesus also suffered outside the city gate to make the people holy through his own blood. (Hebrews 13:12)

Jesus was taken outside Jerusalem—outside the camp—and crucified like a common criminal between two thieves at Golgotha. His sacrifice makes us holy.

> Let us, then, go to him outside the camp, bearing the disgrace he bore. For here we do not have an enduring city, but we are looking for the city that is to come. (Hebrews 13:13-14)

The camp represented safety, sanctity, and comfort. Outside the camp lay danger, uncleanness, and risk. **Leaders leave safety.** They take risks. They sacrifice themselves for others. And the writer calls us to follow Jesus there—outside the comfort of the camp, bearing the abuse He endured.

> If anyone would come after me, let him deny himself and take up his cross daily and follow me. (Luke 9:23)

This is what it means to be a Christian—to follow Jesus into sacrifice and suffering. When leaders sacrifice, their teams work. When leaders eat last, those they serve feel safe and cared for. This is the outcome that matters.

## Imitate Their Faith

> Have confidence in your leaders and submit to their authority, because they keep watch over you as those who must give an account. Do this so that their work will be a joy, not a burden, for that would be of no benefit to you. (Hebrews 13:17)

"Have confidence" can also be translated as "obey" or "be persuaded." There is a call to follow our leaders with conviction—not blind obedience, but thoughtful trust based on their faithfulness to God's word.

The Greek word for leaders here is *hegemon*—those who lead by guiding and directing. These leaders answer to God: they "must give an account." Leadership carries weight and responsibility. When followers make their work a joy rather than a burden, everyone benefits.

### The Closing Encouragement

> Now may the God of peace, who through the blood of the eternal covenant brought back from the dead our Lord Jesus, that great Shepherd of the sheep, equip you with everything good for doing his will, and may he work in us what is pleasing to him, through Jesus Christ, to whom be glory for ever and ever. Amen. (Hebrews 13:20-21)

The letter concludes with a reminder of Jesus as the great Shepherd—the ultimate model of leadership who gave His life for the sheep.

> Brothers and sisters, I urge you to bear with my word of exhortation, for in fact I have written to you quite briefly. I want you to know that our brother Timothy has been released. If he arrives soon, I will come with him to see you. Greet all your leaders and all the Lord's people. Those from Italy send you their greetings. Grace be with you all. (Hebrews 13:22-25)

The writer humbly asks patience with his exhortation, mentions the release of Timothy (perhaps from persecution or imprisonment), extends greetings from brothers in Italy, and ends with grace—the unmerited favor of God that sustains us all.

### The Three Principles

**First: Remember the word.** Not the charisma of leaders, not their personality or the programs they organize, but the **word of God they spoke.** This word is eternal and unchanging.

**Second: Consider the outcome.** Look at the trajectory of their lives. Do their actions align with their teaching? Do they sacrifice for others, or do they serve themselves? Real leaders eat last.

**Third: Imitate their faith.** Follow the example of those whose lives demonstrate faithfulness to Christ. Have confidence in leaders who shepherd you toward Jesus, the great Shepherd.

As you move forward—whether graduating into new seasons or continuing in your current place—remember that you are becoming leaders yourselves. The question is not whether you lead, but how you will lead. Will you speak God's word? Will you sacrifice for others? Will you demonstrate a faith worth imitating?

This is what perseverance looks like. This is how we run the race set before us.
