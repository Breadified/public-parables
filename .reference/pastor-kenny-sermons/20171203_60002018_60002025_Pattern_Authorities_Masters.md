## Understanding Biblical Slavery vs. The Slave Trade

We rightly hate slavery today. The 18th and 19th centuries saw terrible injustice—African people kidnapped from their families, traded across the Atlantic, bought and sold in European and American markets. These horrors are well-documented: the Atlantic slave ships, the Amistad, the stories of Olaudah Equiano, and even the captain who drowned 133 enslaved people to claim insurance money.

Yet this passage seems to contradict Scripture itself. In 1 Timothy 1:9-10, Paul lists slave traders alongside murderers, the sexually immoral, and those who practice homosexuality—all are contrary to sound doctrine. Under Old Testament law, kidnapping a person was punishable by death (Exodus 21:16; Deuteronomy 24:7). The act itself—man-stealing—was evil.

**The crucial distinction:** The Bible prohibits *slave trading* (kidnapping and forced servitude), not *slavery as an institution* (a way to organize society).

### Good Slavery: Legal Forms in Scripture

Biblical slavery took several forms, all regulated by law:

**Debtors and Criminals**
Those who owed money or committed crimes could enter bondage to work off their debt (Exodus 22:3). This was arguably more just than imprisonment—the debtor worked productively rather than sitting idle at the creditor's expense.

**Prisoners of War**
Conquered enemies couldn't be released back to their homeland or allowed to roam freely. Without prisons, lifelong bondage was arguably more merciful than execution (Numbers 31:26; Deuteronomy 21:10-11).

**Voluntary Servitude**
Some entered bondage to escape poverty. The law provided strict protections: Hebrew servants were freed every seven years (Exodus 21:2). If a master gave a servant a wife, the man could choose to stay permanently—a beautiful law showing that submission could be chosen freely (Exodus 21:5-6).

**Female Servants**
Special protections applied. A daughter sold into service was typically destined for marriage. The master had responsibilities: if she didn't please him, she had to be redeemed; he couldn't sell her to foreigners; if he married her to his son, she received daughter's rights; if he took another wife, he had to maintain food, clothing, and marital rights for the first servant (Exodus 21:7-11).

### The Bible's Impact on Slavery

Early Christians actively worked to end slavery:
- Around 90 AD, Clement of Rome noted Christians who gave themselves into bondage to ransom others
- Between 284-305 AD, Chromatius emancipated 1,400 slaves after baptism
- Augustine's congregation regularly used funds to redeem kidnapped victims, recently purchasing and freeing 120 slaves the Galatians were boarding onto ships
- Christian societies eliminated slavery by 1000 AD

In modern times, Christians drove the abolitionist movement. The 1700 pamphlet "The Selling of Joseph" drew attention to Exodus 21:16. Quakers, the author of "Amazing Grace," and William Wilberforce all fought to end the trade. America became one of the first nations in history to abolish all forms of slavery—a massive sacrifice of blood and treasure.

### Slavery Today

While officially ended (Mauritania 1981, Saudi Arabia and Yemen 1962), modern slavery persists through people smuggling. Migrants are promised passage but instead find debt bondage, forced labor, prostitution, and organ removal. This echoes the original African slave trade, where people were first tricked, then kidnapped. The mechanism hasn't changed—only the modern branding.

**The uncomfortable truth:** With the decline of Christendom, slavery has returned. Addressing this requires ending people smuggling, not naive border policies that actually enable the same organized gangs that traffic in modern slavery.

## Submission as a Universal Pattern

> "Slaves, submit yourselves to your masters with all respect" (1 Peter 2:18)

This passage applies far beyond ancient slavery. Most of us are in some form of bondage today:
- HECS debt for degrees that don't lead to promised jobs
- Employment contracts that limit where we can work or live
- Military service, internships, mortgages, family obligations
- Even well-paid positions are still servitude—merely the freedom to move from one master to another

**There's nothing inherently wrong with this.** Work itself isn't designed for our fulfillment; it's designed to sustain us and our families. We submit not because the system is perfect, but because this is how society functions.

### The Reality of Difficult Authorities

Peter speaks directly to real situations: harsh masters, demanding bosses, unjust treatment. Your experience at work is probably familiar:
- Bosses who hurl insults, swear at you for minor mistakes
- Warning about leaving at 6pm, then calling you in at 5:55pm
- Working hard all year expecting recognition, only to watch a friend get promoted
- Being blamed for errors you didn't cause
- Pressure to stay late when you're exhausted

This is tough. But Peter's counsel is clear: **Submit.** You don't have the power; they do.

### Why Submit?

**You benefit from the position itself.** Be thankful you have employment. Your boss was in a position to hire you. Work may not be fulfilling or meaningful, but consider the alternative—idleness. Through mundane work, you:
- Support your family
- Support your church and gospel work
- Stay occupied and productive
- Meet new people and witness about Jesus
- Perhaps model such work that your boss listens one day

**Remember:** Work is not there for you. It's for the owner. This side of heaven promises "weeds and thistles," not passion and purpose.

### But There Are Limits

Submission has boundaries. You submit to the office, not the person. If your boss demands you:
- Kill someone
- Violently threaten a supplier
- Steal money
- Tell a lie
- Recant your faith

You politely decline, escalate to higher authorities, and face the consequences. If nothing works, you resign. Because you have a **higher boss**—God—to whom you must give account.

### The Example of Christ

> "To this you were called, because Christ suffered for you, leaving you an example, that you should follow in his steps. He committed no sin, and no deceit was found in his mouth. When they hurled their insults at him, he did not retaliate; when he suffered, he made no threats. Instead, he entrusted himself to him who judges justly." (1 Peter 2:21-23)

Jesus endured unjust suffering without retaliation. He didn't hit back, scream back, or swear back. He didn't hijack authority or seek revenge. He entrusted himself to the One who judges justly.

This is our pattern. Every injustice will be accounted for one day. You don't need to be calculating—God is calculating. Every wrong against you will be rectified. We can be calm and collected because:

> "He himself bore our sins in his body on the tree, so that we might die to sins and live for righteousness; by his wounds you have been healed." (1 Peter 2:24)

We die to sin. We live for righteousness. We commit no sin, no deceit. We don't steal stationery to make up for unpaid overtime. We don't waste company time on social media (even though studies show 2 million British workers spend over an hour daily on social media at work). We trust God's justice.

## Authority and Submission in All Spheres

Submission isn't unique to employment. It's the fabric of functional society:

**In the workforce:** Hierarchy ensures productivity
**In the military:** The chain of command preserves lives
**In sports:** The captain has authority to lead
**In families:** Parents guide children
**In government:** Citizens follow law
**In the church:** Members listen to leaders

The worst outcome in any system is confusion of authority lines. Someone must lead, or nothing works.

### The Problem with Modern Culture

For 200 years since the Enlightenment, we've emphasized personal rights and freedom so strongly that **submission has become a swear word.** Teachers lost authority (now guidance counselors). Parents lost authority (now friends with money). Police lost authority (now personal bodyguards). Governments lost authority (now facilitators of personal goals).

But this creates a crisis: **If we don't understand submission to human authority, we won't understand submission to God.** If we teach our children to submit to no one but themselves, they won't submit to God.

### A Counter-Example: China

There are now 39-41 million Protestant Christians in China, many in legally recognized organizations. These churches have submitted to the state while maintaining evangelical faith. As a result, they have greater standing with the government—and there are reports the government is consulting these churches to understand how Western democracies developed from Christian foundations.

### The Martyr's Testimony

During Nero's reign, the apostle Peter was persecuted. Rather than rebel, he preached the gospel and lived out his faith. Tradition says he requested crucifixion upside-down, deeming himself unworthy to die as his Master did. **His submission and faithfulness led to his vindication.**

## Submission in Marriage (1 Peter 3:1-6)

> "Wives, in the same way submit yourselves to your own husbands so that, if any of them do not obey the word, they may be won over without words by the behavior of their wives, when they see the purity and reverence of your lives." (1 Peter 3:1-2)

### True Beauty: A Matter of the Heart

Peter redirects attention from outward appearance to inner character:

> "Your beauty should not come from outward adornment, such as braided hair and the wearing of gold jewelry and fine clothes. Instead, it should be that of your inner self, the unfading beauty of a gentle and quiet spirit, which is of great worth in God's sight. For this is the way the holy women of the past who put their hope in God used to make themselves beautiful. They were submissive to their own husbands." (1 Peter 3:3-5)

Submission is beautiful. It requires:
- A **pure life**—one that doesn't store up resentment, disdain, or complaints
- A **reverent life**—one that takes God's Word seriously and lives it out
- A **gentle spirit**—not nagging, manipulating, argumentative, or impatient
- A **quiet spirit**—one that happily accepts the husband's leadership without usurping it

### Why We've Lost This Vision

Modern culture has distorted beauty. We see celebrities and models, all young and similar, reinforced by media showing the skinniest, prettiest people. We compare ourselves to friends our own age, wearing clothes from the same shops. Pressure mounts: Who's skinnier? Who's cuter? **This is dumb.**

In the past, extended families lived together. We saw aunts, grandmothers, great-aunts aging, dying. We naturally learned that physical beauty is fleeting. True beauty—a gentle spirit—endures.

**For young women:**
- God cares about your true beauty, what's inside. Don't be pressured by the world. It's okay to wear ill-fitting clothes, have boring hair, be plain Jane.
- Take advantage of church to see different ages and stages of life.

**For young men:**
- Stop looking for the wrong kind of girls. When you pay attention only to the skinniest and physically attractive, you make it harder for your sisters to be godly. Pay attention to godly and modest women.

### Unqualified Submission

> "Like Sarah, who obeyed Abraham and called him her master. You are her daughters if you do what is right and do not give way to fear." (1 Peter 3:6)

Sarah was no pushover. She laughed at an angel (Genesis 18:10-12). She expelled Hagar when threatened. Yet she called Abraham "master"—the same word for Christ's lordship. This wasn't because Abraham was always right; it was because of his office as husband.

Submission is **unqualified**:
- Not restricted to big decisions or spiritual ones
- Not contingent on the husband's godliness or likability
- Not based on whether you're in love or getting along
- Not a product of the times, but exemplified by Sarah herself

You submit because you're under Jesus, not because your husband deserves it.

### Addressing Objections

**What about domestic abuse?**

Abuse is terrible and inexcusable. Both husbands and wives can abuse—men through violence, women through stinging words and relentless nagging that erodes confidence. Physical abuse gets more press, but emotional abuse is equally damaging.

The solution isn't to eliminate the husband's authority; it's to limit *abuse* of that authority. When police abuse power, we don't replace them with the fire department—we call for better policing. Similarly, husbands need authority *to fulfill their responsibility*: to present their wives as spotless before Christ (Ephesians 5:27), to grow their children in the Lord, to love sacrificially. **These are unqualified responsibilities**, so the authority must be similarly unqualified.

In extreme cases, there's already a limit: civil law. Domestic abuse leads to arrest and jail. If abused, seek police protection, withdraw from fellowship temporarily, get counseling. But don't discard the principle of headship because of abuse.

**What about "mutual submission" in Galatians 3:28 and Ephesians 5:21?**

Galatians 3:28 addresses salvation equality, not the elimination of gender or social structures. Ephesians 5:21 doesn't mean symmetrical, reciprocal submission (A submits to B and B submits to A). It means collective submission within *existing relationships*—asymmetrical structures.

Reality is asymmetrical. "Bear one another's burdens" (Galatians 6:2) doesn't mean everyone exchanges burdens equally; the stronger help the weaker. Revelation 6's language about people slaying "one another" doesn't require simultaneous killing.

Submission is common in all functional systems:
- Workforce hierarchies
- Military chain of command
- Sports teams with captains
- Orchestras with conductors

Someone must lead or chaos ensues. Practical reality: someone must submit in marriage. It's hard to have a two-person democracy with constant debate over every decision. If you take a family vote on dinner, the kids vote for candy. **Someone needs to submit for the family to function as a team.**

**What if my husband is ungodly?**

Peter specifically addresses wives of non-Christian husbands (1 Peter 3:1). Submission is a *means of winning him over to Christ*—not by words, but by behavior. Your gentle, quiet spirit may reach him in ways argument cannot.

This doesn't mean compromising morality. If he demands you steal or sin, you refuse and face consequences. But daily submission—accepting his leadership in non-moral matters—demonstrates Christ's love.

Before marriage is the time to choose wisely. If he doesn't read the Bible, isn't interested in God, is immature spiritually—why risk it? He may grow later, but that's a future option, not a present reality.

### Application for Single Women

These principles determine your choice of a future spouse:
- Can you submit to his authority? Is he selfish or kind?
- Is he a Christian? How can you trust him to lead you spiritually?
- Is he mature? Does he read Scripture? Is he working on godliness?
- Will he care for you sacrificially, as Christ loved the church?

For single men: Can you lead her? Will she listen? How can you present her spotless before Christ if she's not a Christian? Are you mature enough to lead her spiritually?

This isn't about rules; it's about **attitude**—an attitude that encourages him to lead, that's happy for the spotlight on him rather than yourself, that submits even when he's imperfect because you trust God's judgment.

### For Married Women

Keep submitting, knowing your work is not in vain. Support one another and encourage submission and service to family. A gentle, quiet spirit is of great value in God's eyes.

The tension is real: submitting may mean less ministry involvement, more housework, responsibility for hot meals and dirty cups. You may have an unequal division of labor or less exciting holidays. But submission makes marriages work. A Christian woman who submits may do less public ministry, but her marriage stands. Another who refuses may lead Bible studies and play groups but ends in divorce. **Choose wisely.**

## Husbands: The Call to Leadership

> "Husbands, in the same way live considerately with your wives, treating them with respect as the weaker partner and as heirs with you of the gracious gift of life, so that nothing will hinder your prayers." (1 Peter 3:7)

The real issue isn't submission—it's **fatherhood**. It's wanting fathers to take the lead.

### Why Husbands Have Authority

Husbands are given responsibility for their families:
- To present wives as spotless brides to Christ (Ephesians 5:27)
- To grow children in the Lord
- To love sacrificially, as Christ loved the church

**Nothing is worse than responsibility without power.** You're assigned a task worth half your grade but stripped of your tools. You're a police officer without arrest power. You're a father without authority. Authority enables responsibility.

Wives are called the "weaker partner"—not in intelligence (women at North Sydney Girls outperform North Sydney Boys academically), but in size and in the physical demands of pregnancy and motherhood:
- Men are 15-20% larger on average
- Boys grow faster and longer than girls after age 12
- Pregnancy weakens bones, hormones affect joints, motherhood demands sacrifice

### The Call to Husbands

**Never leave.** Whether out of boredom, ambition, or feeling inadequate—your wife and children are never better off without you. To abandon when the battle is hardest is cowardice, not courage.

**Don't abrogate responsibility.** Use your authority wisely and justly. During the London riots, police hesitated to act, worried about accidental abuse. They had power but didn't use it. You have responsibility; fulfill it even if you'll sometimes fail.

**Don't play the hero; do your job.** You're not special for providing, nurturing, loving, and caring for your family. You're doing what you're supposed to do. The buck stops with you.

**Talk, don't just mind-read.** Be interested in your children's lives. How did school go? What happened with friends? Communication builds connection.

**Be an all-rounder.** Don't just provide materially. Invest physically (exercise together), emotionally (listen and care), and spiritually (pray, teach, model faith). Ensure high-quality inputs—TV is a poor substitute for presence.

## The Sphere Sovereignty Principle

Authority isn't monolithic. God has distributed power across different spheres:

**Fathers** have authority over children—not as a grant from the state, but directly from God. The state's only right is to codify this and intervene if a father harms the child's God-given rights.

**Families** have authority to procreate, feed, nurture, and teach—powers the government doesn't possess.

**Churches** have authority to teach doctrine and discipline members.

**Masters** have authority to hire and fire.

**Government** has a defined sphere—not above all others, but alongside them. The state's role is to maintain justice, punish wrongdoing, and protect the innocent—not to steamroll everyday life.

Each sphere has its own tools and appropriate power. A good society respects these boundaries. Christians should work toward a state that rightly relates to citizens, freeing families, churches, and communities to flourish.

## When Submission Has Limits: Civil Disobedience

Scripture records cases of justified disobedience:

**Rahab** directly defied the king of Jericho by hiding Israelite spies (Joshua 2).

**Jonathan** ate when King Saul forbade it, and the people resisted Saul's order to execute him (1 Samuel 14:45).

**Obadiah** hid 100 of God's prophets from Queen Jezebel's execution order (1 Kings 18).

**Joash** was hidden from Athaliah's murderous rampage, and later Jehoiada led a revolt to restore him (2 Kings).

These weren't acts of personal preference or political disagreement. They were defenses of life, innocent people, and God's purposes. The principle: **When an authority demands something that violates a higher authority (God), submission has limits.**

But notice what these figures *didn't* do: They didn't organize protest movements, tax resistance, or public campaigns. They acted quietly, protected the innocent, and trusted God's judgment. Daniel, Peter, and Paul preached when forbidden, but they didn't rebel against taxes or political structures. **They spoke truth and accepted consequences.**

## Conclusion

> "For you were like sheep going astray, but now you have returned to the Shepherd and Overseer of your souls." (1 Peter 2:25)

We submit—to masters, to government, to husbands, to authority—because we ultimately submit to Jesus, the Shepherd and Overseer of our souls. Every injustice will be accounted for. Every wrong will be rectified. We can be calm and collected, knowing God judges justly.

As you go to work tomorrow, remember: Your submission isn't weakness. Your work isn't meaningless. Your gentle spirit isn't a compromise. You're following the pattern of Christ, who suffered unjustly, made no threats, and entrusted Himself to the One who judges justly.

In doing so, you glorify God and create opportunities for the gospel to transform hearts.
