# Mark 9:2-29

**Title:** Reason and Faith

**Date:** 31 October 2021 (ARISE)

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## The Main Idea

We have ample reason to believe in Jesus, yet we cannot muster faith by our own effort. God must work faith in us.

---

## 1. Glory (9:2-12)

### The Transfiguration

After six days of ministry, Jesus took Peter, James, and John up a high mountain. There, before their eyes, Jesus was transfigured—transformed into something greater, more powerful, majestic.

> His clothes became dazzling white, whiter than anyone in the world could bleach them. And there appeared before them Elijah and Moses, who were talking with Jesus.

His garments, likely grey and brown from constant travel, turned a dazzling, blinding white—a light so bright that Mark notes no earthly bleach could achieve such whiteness. In Matthew's account, Jesus' face shone like the sun itself.

### The Disciples' Fear

Peter's response was immediate and understandable: he stammered about putting up three shelters—one for each of them. But the Gospel clarifies something curious: Peter was not simply starstruck. The disciples were *frightened*.

Moses and Elijah—the legendary figures of Israel, the saviors of their people—had appeared. Yet instead of feeling safe and protected, Peter felt terror for his life. Why?

The answer lies not in Moses or Elijah, but in Jesus himself.

### God's Glory and Its Cost

To understand what the disciples witnessed, we must look back to Moses' encounter with God's glory in Exodus 33. There, Moses asked to see God's glory, and God responded: "You cannot see my face, for no one may see me and live."

God placed Moses in a cleft of the rock, covered him with His hand, and allowed him only to see His back as His glory passed by. God explained His own character:

> The Lord, the Lord, the compassionate and gracious God, slow to anger, abounding in love and faithfulness... yet he does not leave the guilty unpunished; he punishes the children and their children for the sin of the parents to the third and fourth generation.

Glory comes with high standards. In the presence of a perfectly pure and holy God—the antithesis of sin—any human being, no matter how righteous, would be consumed. Even Moses, one of the greatest patriarchs of Israel, had to be hidden and protected. His natural sinfulness could not stand before God's holiness.

After this encounter, Moses came down from the mountain with his face radiant—not from his own power, but as a reflection of God's light, like a mirror reflecting an external source.

But Jesus, in His transfiguration, shone of His own accord. He was not reflecting God's glory; He *was* the source of it. The disciples were realizing the profound truth: the holy God Himself stood before them in human flesh.

God the Father confirmed this with a voice from the cloud:

> This is my Son, whom I love. Listen to him!

### The Weight of Seeing Christ's Glory

Jesus is the Son of God. As Christians, we say this casually, but do we understand what we're saying? Jesus shared in God's impossible, sin-destroying glory. In His perfect, sinless life, God's fullness dwelt.

We, by contrast, are full of sin daily. We justify it: *A little white lie won't hurt. Pornography is victimless. Pirating a movie won't dent big corporations. That person will never know I gossiped about them.*

But sin is sin. Like moldy bread, the entire loaf must be discarded. God does not leave the guilty unpunished. On the day when God consolidates all things and judges all wrongdoing, our sin will be held to account before His perfect, shining glory.

## 2. Suffering (9:11-13)

### The Promise and the Problem

The disciples asked Jesus: "Why do the teachers of the law say that Elijah must come first?"

This refers to an Old Testament prophecy: before the Messiah comes, someone will prepare the way. This was fulfilled in John the Baptist, who preached repentance and the forgiveness of sins.

This is hopeful news. Repent of your sins, and the punishment we spoke of earlier can be avoided. But it also raises a difficult question: if God forgives the repentant, does that mean He's a merciful liar who lets sin go unpunished?

Jesus answered:

> To be sure, Elijah does come first, and restores all things. Why then is it written that the Son of Man must suffer much and be rejected? But I tell you, Elijah has come, and they have done to him everything they wished, just as it is written about him.

Elijah—John the Baptist—was rejected and beheaded. As John suffered, so must Jesus suffer. As John was rejected, so must Jesus be rejected.

### Justice and Mercy Reconciled

There is always a cost. Nothing is truly free. The scales must balance.

Sin cannot go unpunished, so God places the full weight of all our sin onto Jesus. At the cross, Jesus bears the punishment for the guilty—all blame taken upon the Son of God. God is both perfectly just and perfectly merciful: He does not leave sin unpunished, yet He forgives those who repent because Christ has already paid the price.

### Living in Light of the Cross

When we force ourselves toward better habits, we should remember that our actions have consequences. We become conscious of consequences when we bear them ourselves—like suddenly noticing how much electricity costs when you pay the bill.

We're fortunate to live in a country with Christian roots, where social morals and laws typically align with God's definitions of right and wrong. Bad consequences often follow sin naturally or by law: liver disease may follow drunkenness; jail may follow theft.

But these consequences are temporary, lasting only in the material world for the duration of this life, not beyond.

The spiritual consequence of our sin is far greater: it's the reason Jesus was crucified. For Christians, understanding this should transform how we live. How can we continue in sin when it cost Jesus His life?

If you haven't yet repented and rested your faith on Jesus' work on the cross, consider this question seriously: Will you still choose to bear the eternal consequences of your sin when God offers forgiveness that has already been purchased?

## 3. Faith (9:14-29)

### Faith Cannot Be Manufactured

When Jesus descended from the mountain and reunited with the other disciples, He found a boy possessed by a demon that robbed him of speech. The spirit would throw him into convulsions, causing him to foam at the mouth and become rigid. The disciples had tried to drive it out but failed.

Jesus rebuked them:

> You unbelieving generation. How long shall I stay with you? How long shall I put up with you?

This was a matter of faith. Despite all the reasons Jesus had shown them for believing—His transfiguration, His glory, His power—the disciples still refused to believe.

### The Father's Honest Struggle

A man brought his son to Jesus:

> Teacher, I brought you my son, who is possessed by a spirit that has robbed him of speech. I asked your disciples to drive out the spirit, but they could not.

The father was desperate, but his words revealed his struggle:

> If you can do anything, take pity on us and help us.

Jesus rebuked him: "Everything is possible for one who believes."

The father's response is one of the most honest and confusing statements in Scripture:

> I do believe; help me overcome my unbelief!

On the surface, this makes no sense. Yet it points to something profound about how faith actually works.

Faith cannot be reached by conscious decision. You cannot simply decide to have faith.

Consider a flimsy shelter made of plastic sheets on a mountainside—rickety trees, loose tent pegs, looking like it could blow over at any moment. Can you decide, in that moment, to have faith that it will protect you? No. Your reason tells you it's shabby and will fail.

But if you sleep in that shelter and it holds firm, and the next night it holds firm again, you gradually begin to believe in it. Without making a conscious decision, you find yourself having faith—because evidence has accumulated and your reason tells you it will likely hold again.

Faith in anything—chairs, friends, God—comes the same way: through accumulated proof discerned by reason.

In Scripture, we have an entire encyclopedia of proof: miracles after miracles, promise after promise kept. So why doesn't everyone believe?

### The Barrier: Our Sin

We are humans born with a bias against the things of God. If God exists, we can no longer define our own right and wrong. If Jesus is Lord, we must obey His commands instead of our interests. If judgment is real, our actions carry eternal consequences.

We are short-sighted. The things of God don't serve our immediate interests, so we naturally ignore them or postpone them: "I'll come back to this another day."

Despite all the proof in Scripture, our natural hearts despise God. Our rational thought becomes clouded to the truth.

### How God Makes Faith Possible

Though faith is necessary for forgiveness, it's impossible to reach by human effort alone. Yet not by God's effort.

Paul writes:

> What we have received is not the spirit of the world, but the Spirit who is from God, so that we may understand what God has freely given us. The person without the Spirit does not accept the things that come from the Spirit of God but considers them foolishness, and cannot understand them because they are discerned only through the Spirit.

What is impossible for humans is possible for God. We can have faith despite our irrationality because God gives us His Spirit of sound rationality. We read the biblical accounts providing proof, and through the Holy Spirit, we discern them properly, leading us to faith.

When the boy's father said, "I do believe," perhaps it was true. Perhaps he did have God-given faith from God-given proof discerned by God-given reason. Yet human doubt remained—the slight fear that perhaps Jesus couldn't heal his son.

Hence his cry: "Help me overcome my unbelief!"

Jesus responded by driving the spirit out before the father's eyes, returning his son healthy and whole.

### Prayer: The Expression of Faith

After healing the boy, Jesus told His disciples:

> Why couldn't we drive it out? This kind can come out only by prayer.

Some translations add: "and fasting."

The disciples had turned to their own power, trusting in themselves, when they should have trusted God and turned to Him in prayer.

Fasting traditionally symbolized trust in God and a willingness to wait on Him. Faith and trust in God must be expressed through prayer—the means by which we speak with God, humble ourselves, and say, "Lord, I cannot do this myself, but I trust in Your ability."

Prayer should be our first response in all matters: when facing something risky, during anxious times, as part of daily routine—when we wake, when we eat. We pray grace for meals three times daily; why not use those moments to think ahead and ask God to carry us through the coming hours?

While we do what we think is best, we also let time pass and see how God has answered, trusting that He has handled our day well.

More importantly, prayer is essential to the repentance we discussed earlier. We say: "I'm sorry for and repent of my sin, God. I trust in Your promise to forgive it and save me from the punishment I'd otherwise face."

And we trust that God will deliver on this promise.

### The Grace That Amazes

There I was in Year 8, at 3 AM on a mountainside, lying in a cold sleeping bag with my plastic tarp torn away, flailing in the wind, with an open view to the sky dropping rain on my face. We did a terrible job constructing our shelter. We paid the price in the middle of the night, and it was our job alone to get up cold and wet to repair it.

How much greater is God's grace: when we sin against a Holy God, instead of leaving us out in the rain, He takes the initiative. He takes the blame. He gives us the ability to have faith and the forgiveness that faith brings.
