# Numbers 1:1–10:13

**Title:** In the Wilderness  
**Date:** 2 June 2013

## One Giant Camping Trip

Think of the Book of Numbers as one massive camping expedition. When you take a large group—especially with children—into the wilderness, three things matter most:

- **Count the people** to ensure no one is lost
- **Set clear rules** about where to camp, where facilities are located, and how to stay organized
- **Don't die** during the journey

The Book of Numbers teaches us exactly these three lessons about living as God's people.

---

## Numbers Is About Numbers: Counting God's People

### The Census (1:1–46)

> The LORD spoke to Moses in the Tent of Meeting in the Desert of Sinai on the first day of the second month of the second year after the Israelites came out of Egypt. He said: "Take a census of the whole Israelite community by their clans and families, listing every man by name, one by one. You and Aaron are to number by their divisions all the men in Israel twenty years old or more who are able to serve in the army." (Numbers 1:1–3)

The book's very name comes from the Greek word *arithmoi*—meaning numbers, calculation, or arithmetic. That name is perfectly fitting, for Numbers records multiple censuses. The people are counted in chapter 1, the tribes are counted again in chapter 2, the Levites are numbered in chapter 3, and another census happens in chapter 26. Even the offerings to the tabernacle are counted in chapter 7.

Just after a year at Mount Sinai, the census reveals 603,550 fighting men—with some scholars estimating the total population, including women, children, and the elderly, at around 2.5 million people. This is extraordinary when you consider that when Jacob's family entered Egypt, they numbered only about 73 people. God has kept Israel alive, preserved all the tribes without loss, and grown them exponentially.

### Why Count?

Practically, a nation preparing to enter foreign territory needs to know its military strength. But the theological reason runs deeper.

**We count what we value.** A father shopping with his six children naturally counts: one, two, three, four, five—where's the sixth? (She's in the stroller, his wife would remind him.) We count our money, our stock portfolios, our treasured collections. 

God counts His people. To be counted among the redeemed, to have your name in the Book of Life—this is a sign that you are blessed and deeply valued. These numbers demonstrate that God loves His people and holds them precious.

### God's Promises Fulfilled

The sheer size of the census proves something remarkable: God is keeping His ancient promises. He promised Abraham countless descendants, a great nation, and a land of their own. Now, four hundred years later, those promises are visibly coming true. The people have multiplied from a small family to a nation. They are alive, organized, and moving toward their inheritance.

## Numbers Is About Rules: Living with God's Presence

### God Is Here (1:1–53)

> The LORD spoke to Moses in the Tent of Meeting... (Numbers 1:1)

The phrase "The LORD spoke" or "The LORD said" appears approximately 44 times throughout Numbers. In Hebrew tradition, books are often named after their opening words. Genesis begins "In the beginning" (*bereshith*); Exodus means "These are the names" (*shemot*). By this standard, Numbers could equally be called "The Lord Said."

This repeated phrase serves two purposes. Structurally, it ties different sections together, giving the book literary coherence. More importantly, it highlights God's constant presence with the Israelites. God is present through His word—He speaks to Moses, who speaks to the people. Even in dry legal passages and census lists, we encounter the living God communicating with His covenant people.

God's continued speech is itself an act of grace. Even when the people rebel, God keeps speaking. Silence would signal judgment; speech demonstrates mercy. And so Numbers, despite its sometimes tedious genealogies, reminds us that we read it because it is God's word to us—His Spirit working through every section to shape and guide us.

### The Tabernacle: God Dwelling Among His People (1:54–2:34)

> The Israelites are to camp around the Tent of Meeting some distance from it, each man under his standard with the banners of his family. (Numbers 2:1–2)

For the first time, God's people will live with their God literally present in their midst. The Tent of Meeting (tabernacle) is where God will meet with Moses and speak to the people. Every detail of its construction is prescribed with extraordinary care—the materials, the dimensions, the ornaments. Like a wedding where every detail matters, the tabernacle's precision reflects its sacred purpose as God's dwelling place.

The tribes are arranged in a specific pattern around the tabernacle, with the tribe of Judah leading on the east (toward the sunrise), Reuben on the south, Ephraim on the west, and Dan on the north. At the very center stands God's tent—the focal point of all community life.

This arrangement teaches a profound spiritual truth. Imagine waking up each morning and seeing the smoke from the tabernacle. As you go about your day—collecting water, visiting friends, working—you encounter the tent again and again. It's a constant, visual reminder: *God is here with us.* Life centers on Him. All activity orients toward Him.

### Purity and Holiness: The Cost of God's Presence (5:1–6:27)

Yet living with a holy God comes with serious demands. God is utterly pure and demands purity from His people.

> Nadab and Abihu, however, fell dead before the LORD when they made an offering with unauthorized fire before him in the Desert of Sinai. (Numbers 3:4)

This terrifying incident illustrates that holiness is not negotiable. Even the priests of God's own family can suffer death for approaching Him carelessly. The people are instructed to maintain ceremonial cleanliness, to remove those with contagious diseases from the camp, and to ensure sexual purity. Chapter 5 addresses these matters—the camp must be kept clean, adultery must be confronted, and the tent itself must remain pure.

Ordinary people cannot merely approach the Tent of Meeting. To do so without authorization means death.

### The Levites: Mediators and Intermediaries (3:5–38)

God's holiness is so intense that He establishes a buffer zone—the Levites serve as intermediaries between the holy God and the rest of the people.

> Bring the tribe of Levi and present them to Aaron the priest to assist him. They are to perform duties for him and for the whole community at the Tent of Meeting by doing the work of the tabernacle. Give the Levites to Aaron and his sons; they are the Israelites who are to be given wholly to him. Anyone else who approaches the sanctuary must be put to death. (Numbers 3:6–10)

The Levites, the only tribe zealous for God during the golden calf rebellion, are set apart for this sacred service. One-twelfth of Israel's male population dedicates itself entirely to the tabernacle's operation. Though this removes fighting men from potential warfare, it acknowledges a deeper truth: there is a war within every human heart—the battle against sin. The Levites' mediation addresses this spiritual warfare.

These priests and Levites:
- **Serve the tent** as the place where God meets His people
- **Live between** the holy God and the unholy people, acting as a buffer zone
- **Substitute for Israel** itself

> I have taken the Levites from among the Israelites in place of the first male offspring of every Israelite woman. The Levites are mine, for all the firstborn are mine. When I struck down all the firstborn in Egypt, I set apart for myself every firstborn in Israel, whether man or animal. They are to be mine. (Numbers 3:12–13)

This recalls the Passover. During the tenth plague in Egypt, God struck down every firstborn—Egyptian and Israelite alike. But the Israelites' firstborns were spared through the blood of the lamb. They were not freed unconditionally; they were set apart for God's service. The Levites now represent all of Israel's firstborns, dedicating themselves fully to God's service.

## Application: Jesus as Our Mediator

### Jesus Dwells with Us

In the Old Testament, the tabernacle was where God met His people. In the New Testament, that reality is fulfilled and surpassed:

> The Word became flesh and made his dwelling among us. (John 1:14)

Jesus "tabernacled"—pitched His tent—among us. Today, through His word, He dwells with us still:

> Let the peace of Christ rule in your hearts, since as members of one body you were called to peace. And be thankful. Let the message of Christ dwell in your hearts richly. (Colossians 3:15–16)

Just as the Israelites organized their lives around the tabernacle, we organize our lives around Christ. When we gather to hear His word preached, that is not a secondary activity. It is the center of our community life. When we read Scripture at home, we invite Christ to dwell richly in us. Our meetings—our church gatherings, our one-to-one conversations—should prioritize encountering God first. When we speak to one another, we should let God speak to us first, which puts our heads into the right perspective before we even begin to discuss our problems with each other.

### Jesus as the Priestly Intermediary

The Levites served as a necessary buffer between a holy God and sinful people. That system acknowledged a fundamental problem: we cannot approach God safely while stained with sin.

Jesus solved this problem once and for all:

> For God gave up his one and only Son, and we have been bought by his blood. Jesus acts as that priestly intermediary. He took the wrath of God upon himself when we sinned. Because of His sacrifice, we can now approach God's most holy place with no consequences. We can safely approach God even though we are unclean. We can come in prayer. We can know Him. We can dwell with Him. (based on Hebrews 4:14–16)

### Living in Purity

Because God's Spirit now dwells in us, we are called to purity:

> Do you not know that your bodies are temples of the Holy Spirit, who is in you, whom you have received from God? You are not your own; you were bought at a price. Therefore honor God with your bodies. (1 Corinthians 6:19–20)

## In the Wilderness: Don't Die (10:1–13)

### The Journey Begins

By the end of chapter 10, the Israelites are ready to move. It has taken 19 days from the start of Numbers. A full year and two months have passed since the exodus. Nearly 400 years have elapsed since God first promised Abraham a great nation and a land.

> On the twentieth day of the second month of the second year, the cloud lifted from above the tabernacle of the Testimony. Then the Israelites set out from the Desert of Sinai and traveled from place to place until the cloud came to rest in the Desert of Paran. They set out, this first time, at the LORD's command through Moses. (Numbers 10:11–13)

It all sounds wonderfully orchestrated. God moves, the cloud lifts, the people follow in perfect order. But they forget the third essential rule: *don't die*.

### The Alternative Name: In the Wilderness

Another name for Numbers is *In the Wilderness*—a place of transition. Not the final destination, but the journey between one place and another. The wilderness is the "in-between." It could have taken only eleven days to journey from Egypt to Canaan. Instead, it would take forty years.

> It takes eleven days to go from Horeb to Kadesh Barnea by the Mount Seir road. (Deuteronomy 1:2)

> In the fortieth year, on the first day of the eleventh month, Moses proclaimed to the Israelites all that the LORD had commanded him concerning them. (Deuteronomy 1:3)

What happened? The people were seduced by sin. They forgot that God dwelled with them. They forgot to trust Him. They forgot the intermediaries He had established for them. They forgot the very numbers that proved God's promises were being kept. Instead, they complained that God had cheated them.

### A Warning for Us

> These things happened to them as examples and were written down as warnings for us, on whom the fulfillment of the ages has come. So, if you think you are standing firm, be careful that you don't fall! No temptation has seized you except what is common to man. And God is faithful; he will not let you be tempted beyond what you can bear. But when you are tempted, he will also provide a way out so that you can stand up under it. (1 Corinthians 10:11–13)

The Israelites' journey mirrors ours. They were saved but not yet at their promised destination. We are saved but not yet in heaven. Salvation is accomplished, but salvation is not completed. This captures the "now and not yet" of the Christian life.

We too are on a wilderness journey. We have been redeemed, we have received God's Spirit, we have been given His word—yet we have not reached our final home. Along the way, we face temptations: the call of basic instincts, the pull of anger and pride, the grip of fear, the seduction of money, the allure of sex.

The Book of Numbers warns us: *Don't die in the wilderness.* Don't be seduced by sin. Don't forget that God dwells with you. Don't forget the blood of Christ that makes you clean. Don't forget His promises, which are proven true in countless ways. Don't forget the intermediary—Jesus—who stands between you and a holy God.

Hold fast to what you have received. Trust God. Remember His faithfulness. So that you, like the faithful remnant of Israel, will enter your inheritance and dwell forever in God's presence.
