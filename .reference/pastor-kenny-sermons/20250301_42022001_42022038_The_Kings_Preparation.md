# Luke 22:1-38

**Title:** The King's Preparation

**Date:** 30 March 2025

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Three Preparations

As we examine Luke 22, we see a chapter about preparation—but preparation that moves in three different directions. While the authorities prepare to destroy Jesus, Jesus prepares himself for his death, and Jesus prepares his disciples for their future ministry. Woven through these preparations is a profound truth: even when evil seems to be winning, God remains sovereignly in control.

---

## Part 1: The Authorities Preparing to Kill Jesus

### The Powers Aligned Against Him

> Now the Festival of Unleavened Bread, called the Passover, was approaching, and the chief priests and the teachers of the law were looking for some way to get rid of Jesus, for they were afraid of the people. (Luke 22:1-2)

The word translated "get rid of" (ἀνέλωσιν) actually means *to kill*. This isn't gentle; Luke is telling us plainly that the religious establishment was plotting Jesus's death. These were not bandits or common criminals—these were the powerful people, the authorities, the guardians of Israel's religious heritage.

The situation darkens further when Satan himself enters the scene:

> Then Satan entered Judas, called Iscariot, one of the Twelve. (Luke 22:3)

This tells us something crucial: the forces arrayed against Jesus were not merely political or personal. Spiritual forces—demonic powers—were actively involved in his opposition. It appeared that darkness had overwhelming advantage.

### The Strange Preparation at Passover

Yet in the midst of this darkness, Jesus gives his disciples unusual instructions:

> He replied, "As you enter the city, a man carrying a jar of water will meet you. Follow him to the house that he enters, and say to the owner of the house, 'The Teacher asks: Where is the guest room, where I may eat the Passover with my disciples?' He will show you a large room upstairs, all furnished. Make preparations there." They left and found things just as Jesus had told them. So they prepared the Passover. (Luke 22:10-13)

A man carrying a pitcher of water was unusual—not because the action was strange in itself, but because water carriers were typically women, and pitchers were cumbersome compared to leather water skins. The peculiarity made him stand out, so the disciples could follow him.

But why all this secrecy and precision? Jesus is signaling something important: *God is in charge of the preparation*. Even as the chief priests scheme, even as Satan enters Judas, Jesus calmly arranges the place where he will celebrate his final Passover. The "cloak and dagger" nature of these instructions demonstrates that God's sovereign hand is directing events, not the chaos of evil.

## Part 2: Jesus Preparing Himself

### Knowledge and Willingness

Despite the gathering darkness, Jesus was not caught off guard. He knew exactly what was coming:

> But the hand of him who is going to betray me is with mine on the table. The Son of Man will go as it has been decreed. But woe to that man who betrays him! (Luke 22:21-22)

Jesus had repeatedly warned his disciples about his coming suffering:

> The Son of Man must suffer many things and be rejected by the elders, the chief priests and the teachers of the law, and he must be killed and on the third day be raised to life. (Luke 9:22)

> He must suffer many things and be rejected by this generation. (Luke 17:25)

> Everything that is written by the prophets about the Son of Man will be fulfilled. He will be delivered over to the Gentiles. They will mock him, insult him and spit on him; they will flog him and kill him. On the third day he will rise again. (Luke 18:31-33)

The disciples didn't understand these words—their meaning was hidden from them—but Jesus knew. His death was not an accident or a failure. It was the fulfillment of God's eternal plan.

### The Symbolic Meal

Now Jesus prepares himself with a meal unlike any Passover before it:

> When the hour came, Jesus and his apostles reclined at the table. And he said to them, "I have eagerly desired to eat this Passover with you before I suffer. For I tell you, I will not eat it again until it finds fulfillment in the kingdom of God."

> After taking the cup, he gave thanks and said, "Take this and divide it among you. For I tell you I will not drink again from the fruit of the vine until the kingdom of God comes."

> And he took bread, gave thanks and broke it, and gave it to them, saying, "This is my body given for you; do this in remembrance of me."

> In the same way, after the supper he took the cup, saying, "This cup is the new covenant in my blood, which is poured out for you." (Luke 22:14-20)

Here's what's remarkable: Jesus is transforming the Passover. The traditional Passover commemorated Israel's escape from Egypt—the lamb's blood on the doorpost, the haste of deliverance. But Jesus redirects the ancient symbol toward himself. He *is* the Passover Lamb. The bread and wine point to his body broken and blood poured out for human sin.

This was no accident or last-minute improvisation. Jesus was consciously preparing himself as the final sacrifice for all humanity. His death would accomplish what centuries of Passover lambs could never accomplish—the complete and permanent redemption of sinners.

Only by dying and then rising—by breaking the bonds of death itself—could Jesus lead the way out of enslavement to Satan and sin.

## Part 3: Jesus Preparing His Disciples

### The Reversal of Greatness

Immediately after the meal, a dispute erupted among the disciples:

> A dispute also arose among them as to which of them was considered to be greatest. Jesus said to them, "The kings of the Gentiles lord it over them; and those who exercise authority over them call themselves Benefactors. But you are not to be like that. Instead, the greatest among you should be like the youngest, and the one who rules like the one who serves. For who is greater, the one who is at the table or the one who serves? Is it not the one who is at the table? But I am among you as one who serves." (Luke 22:24-27)

Jesus flips the world's definition of greatness upside down. In the kingdom, power is measured not by receiving service but by giving it. Authority is measured not by commanding others but by humbling oneself. Jesus himself demonstrated this posture throughout the meal—he found the location, he broke the bread, he poured the wine, he served.

But there's more:

> You are those who have stood by me in my trials. And I confer on you a kingdom, just as my Father conferred one on me, so that you may eat and drink at my table in my kingdom and sit on thrones, judging the twelve tribes of Israel. (Luke 22:28-30)

This is the paradox of Jesus's kingdom: those who serve others become great. Those who humble themselves are exalted. The disciples' willingness to stand with Jesus, even in trials, qualifies them to reign with him.

### Preparation for Failure—and Recovery

But Jesus also prepares them for something harder—their own failure:

> Simon, Simon, Satan has asked to sift all of you as wheat. But I have prayed for you, Simon, that your faith may not fail. And when you have turned back, strengthen your brothers. (Luke 22:31-32)

Peter was about to deny Jesus three times. But notice Jesus's prayer: not that Peter would never stumble, but that his faith would ultimately not fail. And there's a purpose in the recovery—"when you have turned back, strengthen your brothers." Failure, repentance, and restoration become the means by which Peter gains the capacity to strengthen others.

Jesus then says plainly:

> I tell you, Peter, before the rooster crows today, you will deny three times that you know me. (Luke 22:34)

### Preparation for the Long Struggle

Finally, Jesus prepares them for a long, difficult journey ahead:

> Then Jesus asked them, "When I sent you without purse, bag or sandals, did you lack anything?" "Nothing," they answered. (Luke 22:35)

This recalls an earlier mission when Jesus sent seventy-two disciples ahead of him with minimal provisions, and they lacked nothing. It was a quick mission, a last-minute call for workers in the harvest.

But now the situation has changed:

> He said to them, "But now if you have a purse, take it, and also a bag; and if you don't have a sword, sell your cloak and buy one. It is written: 'And he was numbered with the transgressors'; and I tell you that this must be fulfilled in me. Yes, what is written about me is reaching its fulfillment." (Luke 22:36-37)

Jesus is telling them: prepare for hardship. The days ahead will not be quick or easy. They will be numbered among transgressors. This is not a call to violence but a call to readiness—to have what they need for a long, suffering road.

When the disciples reported they had two swords, Jesus replied simply: "That's enough" (Luke 22:38).

## The Application

### When Evil Seems to Be Winning

Three groups were preparing in this passage. The religious authorities were preparing to kill Jesus. Satan was preparing to sift the disciples like wheat. And evil seemed to have all the momentum, all the power, all the advantage.

Yet Jesus moved through it all with quiet sovereignty. He arranged the meal. He prepared himself spiritually. He transformed an ancient symbol into a new covenant. He taught his disciples about servanthood even as he faced death. He prayed for their faith even knowing they would deny him. He prepared them for a long, difficult ministry.

When evil seems to be winning—when life is overwhelming, when doing the right thing doesn't seem rewarding—remember this: God is preparing. Not just for judgment or vindication in some distant future, but for redemption *now*. Jesus's death and resurrection break the power of sin and death in this present age.

### Serving Like Jesus Served

Jesus prepares us, as he prepared his disciples, to serve others as he served us. We are called not to lord it over others but to humble ourselves. The greatest among us are those who serve. This is the pattern Jesus established and invites us to follow.

### Prepared for the Long Road

We are also prepared for difficulty. The Christian life is not a quick mission with guaranteed provision and instant success. It requires purses and bags—resources, endurance, preparation for the long haul. It requires swords—not for violence against flesh and blood, but for spiritual warfare and the willingness to stand firm when opposition comes.

This Passover meal was Jesus's final preparation before the cross. But it was also his preparation of us—teaching us how to serve, how to find greatness in humility, how to endure when faith is tested, and how to know that even in darkness, God is sovereignly at work.
