# Luke 20:20–21:4

**Title:** Government or God

**Date:** 2 April 2017

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Political Trap

Jesus faces a clever trap designed by the religious leaders. They send spies who flatter him, asking whether it is lawful to pay taxes to Caesar. The question is calculated—if Jesus says no, they can accuse him of sedition against Rome. If he says yes, they can claim he doesn't truly honor God's authority. It's an either/or argument where he seemingly cannot win.

But Jesus sees through their duplicity. He asks them to show him a denarius and identify whose image and inscription appear on it. "Caesar's," they reply.

> "Then give back to Caesar what is Caesar's, and to God what is God's." (Luke 20:25)

This response is astonishing because it reframes the entire question. Jesus doesn't choose between Caesar and God—he establishes that both have legitimate claims, but they are not equal.

## Give to Caesar What is Caesar's

### Government's Legitimate Function

Jesus affirms that government has a proper role in society. The apostle Paul later explains this more fully:

> "Let everyone be subject to the governing authorities, for there is no authority except that which God has established. The authorities that exist have been established by God. Consequently, whoever rebels against the authority is rebelling against what God has instituted, and those who do so will bring judgment on themselves." (Romans 13:1-2)

Government is God's instrument for restraining evil and maintaining order. Rulers bear the sword—they have the power to enforce laws and punish wrongdoers. This is a legitimate function. Governments provide police, courts, and the rule of law that protects society from chaos.

> "For rulers hold no terror for those who do right, but for those who do wrong. Do you want to be free from fear of the one in authority? Then do what is right and you will be commended. For the one in authority is God's servant for your good. But if you do wrong, be afraid, for rulers do not bear the sword for no reason. They are God's servants, agents of wrath to bring punishment on the wrongdoer." (Romans 13:3-4)

Therefore, we pay taxes to support this legitimate work:

> "This is also why you pay taxes, for the authorities are God's servants, who give their full time to governing. Give to everyone what you owe them: If you owe taxes, pay taxes; if revenue, then revenue; if respect, then respect; if honor, then honor." (Romans 13:6-7)

### The Proper Limits of Government

Yet Jesus's statement contains a critical qualification: "Give to Caesar what is Caesar's, and to God what is God's." The implication is clear—not everything belongs to Caesar. There are boundaries.

Even imperfect governments serve a purpose. When government is removed or becomes too weak, anarchy and violence increase. Yet this does not mean government should do everything. It has limits.

## Give to God What is God's

### What Bears God's Image and Inscription

Just as the coin bears Caesar's image and inscription, what bears God's image? 

> "Then God said, 'Let us make mankind in our image, in our likeness...'" (Genesis 1:26)

All humans bear God's image. We all have God's inscription written upon us:

> "I will put my laws in their minds and write them on their hearts." (Hebrews 8:10)

Even those who have never heard the gospel have God's law written on their conscience:

> "Indeed, when Gentiles, who do not have the law, do by nature things required by the law, they are a law for themselves, even though they do not have the law. They show that the requirements of the law are written on their hearts, their consciences also bearing witness." (Romans 2:14-15)

Therefore, all people belong to God. We owe God our complete loyalty, allegiance, and worship.

### Personal Tribute to Jesus

The first implication is personal: each of us must pay tribute to Jesus in these last days. We owe him every breath, every bit of energy, all our allegiance and faithfulness.

> "You are worthy, our Lord and God, to receive glory and honor and power, for you created all things, and by your will they were created and have their being." (Revelation 4:11)

> "Then he said to them all: 'Whoever wants to be my disciple must deny themselves and take up their cross daily and follow me.'" (Luke 9:23)

God has given us everything. In return, we give him everything—our loyalty, our praise, our service.

### Government's Subordination to God's Authority

The second implication is equally profound: by contrast to God's eternal rule, human government is limited. Caesar himself is mortal and accountable to God. The government owes God.

> "Let every soul be subject unto the higher powers. For there is no power but of God: the powers that be are ordained of God." (Romans 13:1)

In an age when Caesar was worshipped as divine, when Augustus was called "Son of God," and when the emperor demanded absolute allegiance, Jesus makes a stunning claim: Caesar is subordinate to the true Son of God. Augustus must answer to Jesus.

### The Danger of State Idolatry

Yet there's a tragic reality in our modern age: we have come to worship government in place of God.

When problems arise, where do we look first? To government. When disaster strikes—floods, unemployment, poverty—we cry out to government for salvation. We trust government with our children's education, our retirement funds, our healthcare decisions. We give 30-50% of our earnings to government without complaint, while we grudgingly give perhaps 1% to God's work.

Consider how we allocate our trust:

- We expect government to solve housing affordability, racism, suicide, poverty, obesity, unemployment, healthcare costs, even global warming.
- We believe government can heal the sick, make the lame walk, give sight to the blind.
- We consult government about economics, wage levels, education curriculum, what we should eat, how much we should work.

If an alien observed our behavior, they might reasonably conclude that government is our god.

This is idolatry. And it reflects a fundamental error: we have not truly given ourselves to God. Instead, many of us worship two gods—Jesus for the afterlife, but government for this life.

### Why This Trust is Misplaced

Government cannot do what only God can do:

- **Government cannot create.** It can only redistribute what others have created.
- **Government cannot provide morality.** It cannot transform hearts or forgive sins.
- **Government cannot love or care.** It operates through coercion, not compassion.
- **Government has no money of its own.** Every dollar it spends is taken from its citizens. It cannot give what it has not taken.
- **Government has no special wisdom.** A few hundred officials cannot know more than millions of citizens making daily decisions with their own resources.
- **Government cannot balance a budget.** Yet we expect it to balance the entire economy.

By contrast, through his Son Jesus, God creates. God guides. God teaches. God forgives. God transforms. God cares. God loves.

Even well-intentioned government policies often cause harm. Regulations meant to help frequently hurt those they aim to protect. Historical examples abound: when government removes its presence entirely, as when troops were withdrawn from Iraq or NATO intervened in Libya, chaos and violence increase. Yet when government oversteps its boundaries, tyranny emerges.

## Understanding Church and State

### The Question of Separation

The common modern argument is that church and state should be strictly separated—that religion should stay out of politics and government should not involve itself in religious matters. Yet this phrase doesn't appear in the Australian Constitution. Section 116 protects freedom of religion in government, not freedom from religion.

Our Constitution preamble references God. Parliament opens each session with the Lord's Prayer. Australia was founded as a Christian nation with religious tolerance, not as a secular state with religious prohibition.

More fundamentally, the very notion of separating morals from law is flawed. All laws are moral statements. A law on negative gearing is a statement about whether homelessness is good or bad. A law on Sunday penalty rates is a statement about whether shared family time on Sunday is valuable. A law on environmental protection makes a moral claim about our responsibility to creation.

Because all laws reflect moral frameworks, and because our moral framework derives from either God's revelation or human philosophy, the question is not whether morals and law are connected, but which moral foundation will govern our society.

> "The economic problem will be found to run into the political problem, the political problem into the philosophical problem, and the philosophical problem itself to be almost indissolubly bound up at last with the religious problem. What we believe or do not believe about man and about God determines what kind of society we will have and how our society will govern itself." (Irving Babbitt)

### Multiple Authorities with Distinct Roles

Rather than a strict separation, Scripture presents a better model: multiple God-ordained authorities, each with its own sphere and limits.

**Family authority:** Parents have authority over children. They are called to procreate, feed, nurture, and teach. Government cannot do these things.

**Church authority:** Church leaders guide believers spiritually. The church can exclude members from fellowship, teach biblical truth, and call members to account spiritually. Government has no role here.

**Civil authority:** Government maintains law and order, protects borders, punishes criminals, and ensures basic justice. It bears the sword for these purposes.

**Business authority:** Employers hire, manage, and direct workers. They have legitimate authority over their employees.

These institutions have different tools, different purposes, and different limits. A parent cannot imprison a child. A government cannot save a soul. A church cannot tax citizens. Yet all serve God's purposes when they operate within their proper sphere.

Each institution derives its authority directly from God, not from other institutions:

> "Fathers have power over their children, not as a gift from the state but by the grace of God. The only right the state has at most is to codify the right that fathers have received from God and, should a father want to injure the rights that God has also given to the child, to restore the situation as God has intended it." (Abraham Kuyper)

### The Limits of Government Power

Government should do what it can legitimately do with force—protect people from violence, fraud, and injustice. It should not attempt to do what requires love, creativity, or spiritual transformation. When government overreaches into areas beyond its legitimate sphere, it becomes tyrannical.

Consider how power has been abused in history. Mussolini declared: "Everything within the State, nothing outside the State, nothing against the State." Hitler demanded absolute loyalty to the party and state, claiming supreme authority over every aspect of life. When government becomes god, tyranny inevitably follows.

By contrast, Australia's Christian heritage protects us from such extremes. Our constitutional tradition limits government power. We can replace our government every election. We have freedom of speech, assembly, and religion. These freedoms exist precisely because of our Christian historical foundation—the conviction that government is not absolute.

Yet these freedoms are fragile. They erode when Christians become disengaged from public life or when we adopt bad theology that removes God from cultural and political spheres.

### The Necessity of Christian Engagement

Christians cannot abandon their responsibility to government. When believers withdraw from public life, those without God's wisdom gain control. It would be like sending your children to strangers to raise and having no idea who they are or what they teach.

Australia's institutions have been damaged by Christian neglect. The damage is visible—in eroding freedoms, in government overreach, in moral confusion about fundamental issues. Yet nothing is inevitable. Christians who understand biblical truth and act wisely can help restore sanity.

Some of you should consider entering government—bringing God's wisdom into public life, limiting government power, preserving freedom. It's primarily a young person's game, but not everyone is too old. Armed with God's wisdom and operating under God's sovereignty, you can help preserve freedom for all of us.

## The Foolishness of Trusting Princes

> "Do not put your trust in princes, in human beings, who cannot save. When their spirit departs, they return to the ground; on that very day their plans come to nothing." (Psalm 146:3-4)

Princes—powerful people—cannot ultimately save us. Their plans fail. Their power is temporary. Yet we repeatedly place our ultimate hope in government.

Government is force—organized coercion. As the nineteenth-century statesman Frédéric Bastiat explained:

> "Every individual has the right to use force for lawful self-defense. It is for this reason that the collective force—which is only the organized combination of the individual forces—may lawfully be used for the same purpose; and it cannot be used legitimately for any other purpose."

Government's only legitimate use of force is defensive—to protect people from those who would harm them. Beyond this, government becomes oppressive.

The biblical warnings about kings illustrate this principle. When Israel demanded a king, God warned them through Samuel:

> "This is what the king who will reign over you will claim as his rights: He will take your sons and make them serve with his chariots and horses... He will take your daughters to be perfumers and cooks and bakers. He will take the best of your fields and vineyards and olive groves and give them to his attendants. He will take a tenth of your grain and of your vintage and give it to his officials. He will take a tenth of your flocks, and you yourselves will become his slaves." (1 Samuel 8:11-17)

Government is inherently tempted toward power expansion. Those who rule tend to increase their authority and wealth. As the saying goes, "Governments take resources from the public but use them to maximize their own welfare."

### When Consent is Not Enough

Yet consent of the governed, while important, cannot be the sole foundation of legitimate government. History demonstrates this plainly. Weimar Germany democratically consented to Hitler's rise. The Enabling Act that granted Hitler dictatorial powers passed by large majorities in the elected legislature. If consent alone legitimizes government, then Hitler's dictatorship had a popular mandate.

This is why biblical government requires more than consent—it requires conformity to God's law. A government that violates God's law loses its legitimacy, regardless of popular support.

### Biblical Limits on Rulers

Deuteronomy establishes clear constitutional limits on kings:

> "He must not multiply horses for himself or cause the people to return to Egypt in order to multiply horses. He shall not multiply wives for himself, lest his heart turn away. He shall not greatly multiply for himself silver and gold." (Deuteronomy 17:16-17)

These restrictions target the three great temptations of rulers: military power (horses), foreign alliances (wives), and wealth (gold). A limited government is one where the ruler's appetites for power, prestige, and wealth are restrained by law.

The narrative of Israel's kings demonstrates how easily rulers violate these limits. Yet the principle stands: government is not autonomous. It is subordinate to God's law. When rulers ignore this, they face judgment.

### Civil Disobedience

When government commands what God forbids, or forbids what God commands, believers must obey God rather than government. Scripture provides multiple examples:

- The Hebrew midwives refused Pharaoh's order to drown male babies, instead fearing God (Exodus 1:15-22).
- Peter and John refused the Sanhedrin's command to stop preaching Jesus, declaring, "We must obey God rather than man" (Acts 5:29).
- Shadrach, Meshach, and Abednego refused to bow to Nebuchadnezzar's image, willing to face the fiery furnace (Daniel 3).

Yet biblical civil disobedience has two essential characteristics: it presupposes a high view of law and authority (the norm is obedience), and it assumes willingness to face consequences. The early disciples went to jail rather than escape; they honored the legal system even while disobeying unjust laws.

This principle guided the Reformers. Martin Luther insisted we must respect the office of magistrate, yet he also taught that obedience is not unconditional. Calvin argued that private individuals should not revolt, but proper representatives of the people could and should resist tyranny. Samuel Rutherford's influential work *Lex, Rex* (The Law, the King) established that all authorities, including rulers, are subject to God's law. When a king violates God's law, the people may have the right to remove him.

Tyrannical government is satanic government, and believers must resist it. But this resistance should typically take the form of peaceful refusal, not violent revolution—unless, in extreme cases, duly constituted representatives of the people authorize such measures.

## Two Types of Authority

There are fundamentally two types of people: those who govern themselves under God's law, and those who require external governance. The first live as free people who rule themselves by submitting to God. The second require rulers to control them through force.

When individuals govern themselves through faith, self-discipline, and obedience to God's law written on their hearts, they need minimal external government. When individuals abandon self-government under God, they require increasingly heavy-handed external government.

A Christian nation—not a theocracy, but a nation whose citizens and institutions are shaped by Christian values—requires less government coercion because people voluntarily do what is right. A pagan nation, where God's law is rejected, requires more government control to maintain order.

This is why Australia's Christian heritage has been so valuable. Citizens shaped by Christian values respected authority, kept promises, told truth, and treated others justly without requiring constant enforcement. As Christian influence has waned, government has had to expand its control in compensation.

## The Choice Before Us

> "Which road do you take? Government or God? Who do you trust?" (Luke 20:25, paraphrased)

This is not merely a political question—it is a spiritual one. It determines how we live, what we hope for, and where we place our ultimate loyalty.

We are called to give Caesar what is his—obey legitimate government, pay taxes, respect authorities—and give God what is his—our complete allegiance, our worship, our obedience.

The healthiest society balances these commitments: respecting government's legitimate role while fiercely protecting God's supremacy and the freedom to worship him. When government becomes god, we lose both freedom and justice. When God is truly Lord, we find the wisdom and restraint that make good government possible.

The prayer of the apostle Paul remains vital:

> "I urge, then, first of all, that petitions, prayers, intercession and thanksgiving be made for all people—for kings and all those in authority, that we may live peaceful and quiet lives in all godliness and holiness. This is good, and pleases God our Savior, who wants all people to be saved and to come to a knowledge of the truth." (1 Timothy 2:1-4)

We pray for government because we recognize its God-given role. But our ultimate hope remains in God alone.

> "Blessed are those whose help is the God of Jacob, whose hope is in the Lord their God. He is the Maker of heaven and earth, the sea, and everything in them—he remains faithful forever. He upholds the cause of the oppressed and gives food to the hungry. The Lord sets prisoners free." (Psalm 146:5-7)
