# Revelation 5

**Title:** The Lion, The Switch, and the Throne Room

**Date:** January 12, 2002

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Introduction

How do we understand real power? The world teaches us to seek it through strength, authority, and control. We see this theme everywhere—in stories like *The Lord of the Rings*, where everyone desires the ring's power, some for evil domination and others for what they believe is good. But what does Scripture reveal about true power? That's what we're exploring together today.

## The Throne Room of God (Revelation 5:1-4)

Picture this scene as if it were a grand cinematic vision: John is given a glimpse through an open door into heaven itself. The camera pans across an overwhelming sight—the 24 elders, the four living creatures, and the appearance of God on the throne, shimmering with the colors of jasper and precious gemstones. All honour and power and praise focus on this centre. The praise is deafening, the worship complete.

Then the camera stops on something specific: a scroll.

> I saw in the right hand of him who sat on the throne a scroll with writing on both sides and sealed with seven seals. (Revelation 5:1)

This scroll contains God's complete plans for the world—comprehensive, nothing left out. It's written on both sides, inside and outside. To open these seals is to implement God's purposes. But here's the problem: the scroll is perfectly sealed. Not with one seal, but seven. It is completely protected, and no one can read it.

Then a mighty angel proclaims with a loud voice:

> Who is worthy to break the seals and open the scroll? But no one in heaven or on earth or under the earth could open the scroll or even look inside it. (Revelation 5:2-3)

The answer echoes through heaven: **No one**. Not in heaven, not on earth, not under the earth. Nowhere in this universe is anyone worthy or good enough to fulfill God's plans. Humanity's inability is absolute—we cannot even peek through the cracks of the scroll.

And John weeps:

> I wept and wept because no one was found who was worthy to open the scroll or look inside. (Revelation 5:4)

The agony and hopelessness consume him. The promise of God's good purposes hangs unfulfilled. Is there no one?

## The Lion of Judah Arrives (Revelation 5:5)

Then comfort comes through one of the elders clothed in white—one of the victorious ones in heaven:

> Do not weep! See the Lion of the tribe of Judah, the root of David has triumphed. He is able to open the scroll and its seven seals. (Revelation 5:5)

Hope! **The Lion is worthy.**

The Lion conjures images of great power. Power is strength—the Lion is strong. Power is authority—the Lion rules as King of Beasts. Power is security and trust. Even today, in countries where lions are long extinct, they appear on coats of arms as symbols of kingship and monarchy.

Who is this Lion? It is King Jesus himself. Throughout the Old Testament, ever since the reign of King David, Israel looked for a king as great as he. Finally, here He is. Jesus is the descendant of David—the one who would conquer enemies, bring a new era of peace by crushing those who oppose Him. The great, majestic, powerful Lion who will rule the world.

## The First Switch: The Lion Becomes the Lamb (Revelation 5:6a)

But here comes a shocking twist. John expects to see a mighty Lion, and he turns to look—

> Then I saw a Lamb, looking as if it had been slain, standing in the centre of the throne, encircled by the four living creatures and the elders. (Revelation 5:6a)

A lamb. Not even a grown sheep—a lamb. And slain! **Here is the first switch: the Lion becomes the slain Lamb.**

What an absurd contrast! Imagine America with all its military might—missiles, tanks, battleships, stealth bombers, smart bombs, satellite reconnaissance, nuclear arsenal—but when war is declared, it sends only a baby with a toy into battle. This is the same absurdity.

The Lion is able to open the seals. But He does so as a Lamb. Jesus, the Messiah and chosen King, conquers not through overwhelming force but through becoming the slain Lamb of God.

Jesus could have done it differently. He could have amassed His own army. He could have called on a legion of angels to fight for Him, and at a stroke, all the kingdoms of the world would be His. He could have come in triumph, but instead He chose the way of the cross.

Why? Because **the way of the cross is the way of trust in God.** Jesus, in His obedience and trust, follows God all the way to the cross. He disarms Himself of anything remotely powerful and enters the battlefield like a defenseless baby.

### Understanding Power Through Trust

Here's the fundamental principle: **Power is trust in the powerful God.**

We naturally seek power through the world's standards—through wealth, wisdom, and strength. We look at Jesus, this pathetic slain Lamb, and we don't see power at all. So we trust the world's perception instead.

Consider this illustration: I drive my Corolla hatchback down a busy road. When someone ahead of me is on their L-plates or driving an old car, I have no problem revving up and zooming past. But when it's a luxury car—a BMW convertible—I hesitate to overtake. Here is a powerful person, and I'm intimidated.

But one day I drove a big, champagne-coloured Verada. It was luxurious—power windows, leather seats, a CD player, those antennas that go up and down automatically. Suddenly, I wasn't worried about the opinions of other drivers. I cruised along feeling powerful and secure.

That's the problem: I put my confidence in a car. I felt significant because of a material possession.

We all use the world's props to feel powerful and successful. It's the school we attended, the university course we're studying, the job we'll have, the upper-middle-class address where we live, the church's social buzz, the boyfriend or girlfriend on our arm. We search for significance in exam results, in being successful, in what others think of us.

But here's the test: **We haven't truly discovered where our trust lies until everything falls away.** When we're terribly sick, when we fail exams, when someone dies, when we lose our job, when we're rejected by those whose opinions we value—that's when we find out whether we really trust God.

When we lose all that makes us feel powerful in this world, when our earthly props give way, then we see where our trust really is.

**Don't be deceived by worldly views of power.** The way of the cross is the demotion of self-reliance and the promotion of God-reliance. Jesus is the Lion without the props—shave the mane, mute the roar, file the nails and teeth, shrink the size—and what you have is the Lamb. Jesus relying not on Himself, but on God. And that is real power.

## The Second Switch: The Lamb is Exalted (Revelation 5:6b-14)

Here's the good news—the second switch. In a split second, **the Lamb is exalted.**

> Then I saw a Lamb, looking as if it had been slain, standing in the centre of the throne, encircled by the four living creatures and the elders. He had seven horns and seven eyes, which are the seven spirits of God sent out into all the earth. (Revelation 5:6)

This mortally wounded Lamb is standing. **He is alive.** He occupies a place of great prominence—standing in the centre of the throne, encircled by the four living creatures and the elders. The Lamb has seven horns, signifying complete power, and seven eyes, pointing to complete knowledge. He possesses the complete Spirit of God. The Lamb is exalted and elevated to the highest status.

> It is this Lamb who came and took the scroll from the right hand of him who sat on the throne. (Revelation 5:7)

The Lion who triumphed was said to be worthy to take the scroll. Yet the Lion is never seen or heard of again in Revelation. Only the Lamb that looked as if it had been slain takes the scroll. This Lamb is worthy to take the scroll and reveal the purposes of God. The Messiah, the Son of David, is the divinely appointed conqueror—but only through His death. It is not the Lion as Lion who takes the scroll; it is the Lion as the Lamb, looking as if it had been slain.

When He took the scroll, all of heaven responded:

> The four living creatures and the twenty-four elders fell down before the lamb. Each one had a harp and they were holding golden bowls full of incense, which are the prayers of the saints. (Revelation 5:8)

All creation, all authority, worships the Lamb. And it gets louder:

> Then I looked and heard the voice of many angels, numbering thousands upon thousands, and ten thousand times ten thousand. They encircled the throne and the living creatures and the elders. In a loud voice they sang: Worthy is the Lamb, who was slain, to receive power and wealth and wisdom and strength and honor and glory and praise! (Revelation 5:11-12)

Thousands upon thousands, ten thousand times ten thousand—more angels than we can count, singing in surround sound. "Worthy is the Lamb who was slain!" Worthy to receive sevenfold adoration, complete adoration: power, wealth, wisdom, strength, honor, glory, and praise.

Earlier, the question hung over heaven, earth, and the place under the earth: who is worthy? The answer was silence. Now:

> Every creature in heaven and on earth and under the earth and on the sea, and all that is in them, singing: To him who sits on the throne AND to the Lamb be praise and honour and glory and power, for ever and ever! The four living creatures said, Amen and the elders fell down and worshipped. (Revelation 5:13-14)

Before, in Revelation 4:9, all glory and honor was given to the One who sits on the throne. Now, all glory and honor is given **also to the Lamb**. Jesus is raised to the right hand of God and is exalted. The highest honour and respect, reserved for God alone, is given to the Lamb—because He is God, and He is worthy.

Imagine one baby being the most powerful being in the world, defeating great military nations and receiving divine honour. Absurd! And yet, the very song of heaven changed.

> You are worthy to take the scroll and to open its seals; because you were slain, and with your blood you purchased men for God from every tribe and language and people and nation. You have made them to be a kingdom and priests to serve our God and they will reign on the earth. (Revelation 5:9-10)

In Revelation 4:11, God is worthy because He created all things. Here, the song changes: the Lamb is worthy because He was slain. **God's song of all eternity is changed.** What a profound truth—the Lamb's worthiness is based not on power or creation, but on sacrifice.

## The Application: Power Worth Following

Is the Lamb's way worth it? Is trusting God all the way to the cross worth it? **Yes.** Judge for yourself by looking at Revelation 11-14. He is exalted. All of heaven worships Him.

For us, the question becomes: **Is following the Lamb worth it?** Is giving up my money, my time, my relationships for the sake of the kingdom worth it? Is being a loser in this world's eyes worth it? Is trusting God all the way worth it for me?

Yes. Jesus lived on the premise that it is better by far to trust God, to be a loser in this world, to die—and go to heaven. This is how real power works. By trusting in God. And that fact has to affect how we live in this world.

Why give up two weeks of summer for a beach mission? Why give up Tuesday nights for Bible study? Why give up half your paycheck for gospel work? Don't you want to have fun? Don't you want to enjoy yourself while you have the chance?

Of course we do. Just later.

A friend once commented to me that greed is not wanting more and more—it's wanting more and more **now**. As Christians and citizens of heaven, we have everything. We are heirs to everything in earth and heaven. We just have to wait for it. Let's persevere in our suffering meanwhile.

The temptation is real: excel in subjects to make a mark for ourselves rather than live as a Lamb, pathetic in the world's eyes. But where does real power actually lie? In the pursuit of significance through worldly achievement, or in generous sacrifice? The Lamb approach asks us to give—our money, our time, our comfort—when the world watches and calls us fools.

Christians are not suffering for nothing. This isn't a party-pooper theology. There is a real promise of good things in the future, which sustains us in the present. If we see heaven like this, if we can see the Lamb glorified and exalted, it gives us the reason to give up the things of this world. **Power comes in giving up the props of this world and trusting God.**

That's hard. But we are given a vision from heaven today about what it will be like. The exalted Lamb means that trusting and relying in God makes sense.

## The Promise: We Can Follow the Lamb

Here's the beautiful part: **we can follow the Lamb into heaven.**

> You were slain, and with your blood you purchased men for God from every tribe and language and people and nation. You have made them to be a kingdom and priests to serve our God and they will reign on the earth. (Revelation 5:9-10)

The Lamb was slain to purchase people for God—men and women in slavery to sin and to Satan. God gives a ransom to buy back His people, and nothing would satisfy except the blood of His Son.

There is a switch in ownership. We no longer belong to Satan. Now we are the people of God, His Kingdom. We've been purchased not to do whatever we like, but to be a kingdom and priests to serve our God and to reign on the earth.

The Lamb on the throne shows us two things: not only is the way of the Lamb worth it, but **the way of the Lamb is followable.** We have been purchased for that very reason. We are His.

## Conclusion: Living as Subjects

How do we have power? How do we gain wealth, wisdom, and strength? The world offers one way: self-promotion and self-preservation. God offers another way: demotion and servitude.

From the vision of the slain Lamb on the throne, we know that the way to power works. The Lamb is exalted. All of it is done for us so that we can follow—by trusting and by faith, believing that God has our best interest at heart.

**What is real power?** It is the Lion trusting God even if it means becoming a Lamb all the way to the cross.
