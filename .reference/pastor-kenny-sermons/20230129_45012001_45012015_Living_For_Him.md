## True Worship: Offering Our Bodies

> Therefore, I urge you, brothers, in view of God's mercy, to offer your bodies as living sacrifices, holy and pleasing to God—this is your spiritual act of worship. (Romans 12:1)

When Paul calls us to worship, he's not referring to the modern religious practices we often think of—the songs, candles, and ceremonies. He's using Old Testament temple language as a metaphor for how we're to live our entire lives.

The word translated "worship" here (Greek: *latreia*) literally means "service in the temple." In the Old Testament, priests would offer dead animal sacrifices to God. Paul is saying something radical: now, in light of God's mercy revealed through chapters 1-11 of Romans, we are to offer ourselves—*living* sacrifices.

This is profoundly different from the old system. A living sacrifice means we don't offer ourselves once and die; we continuously, daily, lay down our desires and serve God. Just as Jesus called us to carry our cross daily, we engage in recurring, ongoing sacrifice of our flesh and selfish ambitions.

The opposite of worshiping idols is not independence or autonomy—it's service. When the Thessalonians turned from idols to serve God, they weren't freed to do whatever they wanted. They were freed to serve the living God.

## Renewing Your Mind

> Do not conform any longer to the pattern of this world, but be transformed by the renewing of your mind. Then you will be able to test and approve what God's will is—his good, pleasing and perfect will. (Romans 12:2)

The foundation of all Christian living is the mind. Notice that Paul calls this a "spiritual act of worship"—and the word translated "spiritual" means *logical* and *reasoned*. This is deliberate, thoughtful service based on understanding.

Our culture is fundamentally self-serving. The messaging is all about you, your desires, your comfort. The challenge for us as Christians is clear: do not be conformed to this age, but be transformed by renewing your thinking.

### Christianity Is Fundamentally About the Mind

You cannot be a mature Christian while leaving your mind at home. Some Christians want to feel God without thinking deeply, but Christianity is the only world religion centered on a text that demands study, memorization, and critical thinking. The Bible is not a mystical utterance to be chanted—it's a document with history, context, stories, and meaning.

Look at history: wherever Christianity spread, superstition declined and learning flourished. The monasteries preserved education through the Dark Ages. The Reformation spurred the printing press. The early universities—Cambridge, Oxford, Harvard, Princeton—were originally seminaries. Even the University of California traces its roots to clergy leadership. Christian missionaries were often the first to translate the languages of remote peoples. In Australia, Rev. Richard Johnson established the first schoolhouse in 1793, serving as both church and school. The Puritans, Wesleyans, Presbyterians, and Lutherans all built schools with the Bible as the central text.

The lending libraries that made knowledge public (not government-run, but open to the public) were started by churches. Christian towns have consistently sent the most students to schools and universities.

If you call yourself a Christian and want to leave your mind at home, I'm not sure what kind of Christian you are. Perhaps one who is lazy or still superstitious. To accuse Christians of being anti-intellectual is entirely false. No one should care more about education and developing the mind than Christians—because this is how we come to know and approve God's will.

### Understanding God's Will

When Paul says we will be able to "test and approve" God's will, he doesn't mean we sit in judgment of God. He means we'll be able to understand, discern, and recognize God's will. This requires a trained, renewed mind.

This is why we read the Bible at church. This is why we have Sunday School and Bible Studies. This is why we expect our young people to develop their minds even when a long talk feels boring—this is discipline, training for how to learn. Good for you to stick with it and grow.

## Serving with Humility

> For by the grace given me I say to every one of you: Do not think of yourself more highly than you ought, but rather think of yourself with sober judgment, in accordance with the measure of faith God has given you. (Romans 12:3)

As our church grows and seasons of life change, this becomes deeply relevant. Many of you are transitioning to full-time work for the first time. You've been leaders, served in many capacities, been the mature one. But now it's hard to step down—not hard to release responsibilities, but hard to release the identity and status that came with leadership.

In any career, we climb the ladder and don't look back. So when life circumstances change and you're now under a leader younger than yourself, or serving in a less prominent role, it can feel diminishing. But what does verse 3 call us to? Think of yourself with *sober judgment*.

If you can continue or expand your responsibilities, wonderful. But if you're in a season where you're busy—a new job, a young child—then someone else must lead. Be humble about it. You may have more experience, but this is someone else's opportunity. Support them. Serve in the new capacities God has given you. It is not beneath you to be accountable to a younger person.

The truth is this: only the humble serve. When we think ourselves more highly than we ought, we won't genuinely lay ourselves on the altar. We'll find reasons to climb back down.

## The Body of Christ

> Just as each of us has one body with many members, and these members do not all have the same function, so in Christ we who are many form one body, and each member belongs to all the others. We have different gifts, according to the grace given us. If a man's gift is prophesying, let him use it in proportion to his faith. If it is serving, let him serve; if it is teaching, let him teach; if it is encouraging, let him encourage; if it is contributing to the needs of others, let him give generously; if it is leadership, let him govern diligently; if it is showing mercy, let him do it cheerfully. (Romans 12:4-8)

Our church is beautifully diverse. We represent ten nationalities, multiple proficiency levels in language, different schools, varied talents, multiple ages. Yet we are one body where each member belongs to all the others.

This isn't just theory. When we serve each other, we serve King Jesus Himself. Matthew 25 makes this explicit. When we feed the hungry, clothe the naked, visit the prisoner, we're doing these things unto Christ. And conversely, when we fail to serve the vulnerable, we fail to serve Him.

God's love language is service to others. We serve the church because it's not a restaurant where things are done for you—it's a healthy family kitchen where everyone is invested and involved in serving one another.

### S-Teams: Committed Service

Here at our church, our Bible study groups are called "S-Teams"—Serving Teams. This isn't just Bible study and encouragement, though those are important. We specifically look for those committed enough to the church that they're willing to serve it. A true knowledge of God leads to service. Bible study groups can easily become insular, caring only for themselves. S-Teams ensure that our study leads to prayer for the lost, to others-centeredness, to service, to genuine love for the broader body.

## Living in Love

> Love must be sincere. Hate what is evil; cling to what is good. Be devoted to one another in brotherly love. Honor one another above yourselves. Never be lacking in zeal, but keep your spiritual fervor, serving the Lord. Be joyful in hope, patient in affliction, faithful in prayer. Share with God's people who are in need. Practice hospitality. Bless those who persecute you; bless and do not curse. Rejoice with those who rejoice; mourn with those who mourn. Live in harmony with one another. Do not be proud, but be willing to associate with people of low position. Do not be conceited. (Romans 12:9-16)

The theme throughout these verses is sincere love lived out in the context of community. We are to love one another genuinely, honor each other, maintain zeal for serving the Lord, share with those in need, show hospitality, and rejoice and mourn together.

This is how we do life together. This is how we live at church. It requires that we don't take our time with one another for granted—especially knowing there are young people who would long to be here but are prevented by their families. We are blessed to have each other. Let us love one another and encourage each other with God's Word.

## Relating to Those Outside the Church

> Do not repay anyone evil for evil. Be careful to do what is right in the eyes of everybody. If it is possible, as far as it depends on you, live at peace with everyone. Do not take revenge, my friends, but leave room for God's wrath, for it is written: "It is mine to avenge; I will repay," says the Lord. On the contrary: "If your enemy is hungry, feed him; if he is thirsty, give him something to drink. In doing this, you will heap burning coals on his head." Do not be overcome by evil, but overcome evil with good. (Romans 12:17-21)

Our service and love extend beyond the church to all people, especially those who oppose us. We are not to repay evil with evil. Instead, we pursue peace as far as it depends on us, leaving judgment to God. We overcome evil by doing good.

This is the scandal of grace: we feed our enemies, we serve those who hate us, we respond to hostility with kindness. This is how the gospel transforms the world—not through force or retaliation, but through the quiet, persistent power of sacrificial love.
