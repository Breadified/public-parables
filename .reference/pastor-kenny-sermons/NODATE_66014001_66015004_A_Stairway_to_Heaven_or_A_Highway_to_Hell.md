# Revelation 14:1-15:4

**Title:** A Stairway to Heaven or A Highway to Hell

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Introduction

The gospel message is both wonderfully positive and soberly negative. We often emphasize the positive aspects—the hope, grace, and redemption found in Christ. Yet we cannot ignore the negative implications for those who reject Jesus as Lord and Savior. Understanding both dimensions shapes how we witness and why we must share our faith with urgency and compassion.

---

## Vision 3: The 144,000 (14:1-5)

John's vision opens with the Lamb standing on Mount Zion with 144,000 with Him. This echoes Psalm 2:6, where the Psalmist declares that the Lord has installed His King on Zion, His holy mountain. Just as the Messiah claims His rightful throne, these 144,000 emerge as His faithful witnesses.

These 144,000 possess distinctive qualities:
- They are sealed and protected by God
- Their foreheads bear the Lamb's name and Father's name
- They are virgins who have not defiled themselves—maintaining spiritual purity
- They follow the Lamb wherever He goes—absolute loyalty and obedience
- They are without fault before God's throne—redeemed and sanctified

They represent the redeemed people of God, those who have remained faithful despite pressure and temptation.

---

## Vision 4: The Three Angels (14:6-13)

An angel, in Scripture, is God's messenger. This vision presents three distinct messages, each escalating in urgency and consequence.

**First Message (v6-7):** An angel proclaims the eternal gospel to every nation, tribe, language, and people. The message is simple yet comprehensive: fear God, give Him glory, and worship Him as Creator. This is the gospel's fundamental call—recognition of God's sovereignty and our proper response.

**Second Message (v8):** Another angel announces the fall of Babylon the Great, representing the world system opposed to God. All nations have drunk the maddening wine of her idolatries. This symbolizes the judgment of human rebellion and false systems built apart from God.

**Third Message (v9-13):** A final angel warns of severe consequences for those who worship the beast and accept his mark. No rest, day or night, for those who refuse God's truth. Yet paradoxically, this leads to patient endurance among the saints—those who keep God's commands and remain faithful to Jesus.

The result, captured in verse 12, is that God's people persevere. They maintain patient endurance while keeping God's commandments and remaining faithful to Jesus, even in the face of persecution and judgment.

Verse 13 declares those blessed who die in the Lord: "From now on, blessed are the dead who die in the Lord." Why? Because their labor is not in vain. Their works follow them—the fruit of their faith continues beyond death. They rest from their labor, yet their witness and obedience echo into eternity.

---

## Vision 5: The Harvest (14:14-20)

Jesus sits on a white cloud with a sharp sickle, ready to reap. But He waits. What must He wait for? The Scripture indicates He waits for the completion of God's purposes—for all who will believe to come to faith, for the full number of the elect to be gathered. Only then does the harvest begin.

Two groups are being harvested:

1. **The Grain Harvest (v14-16):** This represents the righteous—those who have borne fruit through faith and obedience. Jesus Himself reaps this harvest with joy.

2. **The Grape Harvest (v17-20):** This represents judgment on the wicked—those who rejected God's message. The grapes are thrown into the great winepress of God's wrath. This imagery, echoing Matthew 13:30 and 39, shows both blessing and judgment occurring simultaneously at the end of the age.

---

## Vision 6: Victory Song (15:1-4)

Vision 6 (15:1) is not a new scene but a sign in heaven—the culmination and conclusion of what precedes it. It announces the completion of God's plan and the final judgment.

Vision 7 (15:2-4) shows a sea of glass mixed with fire, and on it stand those who have overcome. They sing the Song of Moses and the Song of the Lamb:

> "Great and marvelous are your deeds, Lord God Almighty. Just and true are your ways, King of the nations. Who will not fear you, Lord, and bring glory to your name?"

When Moses and the Israelites crossed the Red Sea, they sang with joy and thanksgiving, celebrating their deliverance from slavery and Pharaoh's army (Exodus 15). This was not merely personal relief—it was recognition of God's mighty power and faithfulness. We, too, should be singing, for we have been delivered from a far greater bondage: slavery to sin and death through Christ's resurrection.

The Passover and Jesus's death and resurrection share profound parallels:
- The Passover lamb's blood marked and protected God's people from judgment
- Jesus, our Passover Lamb, shed His blood for the forgiveness of sins
- Just as the Israelites were freed from Egypt through blood and redemption, we are freed from sin through Christ's sacrifice
- Just as they crossed into a new life, we are resurrected to new life in Christ

## Implications for Our Witness

How should what we have looked at tonight shape our lives and the way we witness to people?

The reality of both blessing and judgment must compel us to action. When we truly grasp that those without Christ face separation from God's kingdom, our witness becomes urgent and loving. We are not proclaiming mere moral advice but a message of eternal consequence.

Yet genuine barriers prevent us from sharing effectively:

- **Fear of rejection or conflict** with family and friends
- **Uncertainty about what to say** or how to explain the gospel clearly
- **Shame or embarrassment** about appearing preachy
- **Spiritual apathy** when we lose sight of eternity's weight
- **Relational hesitation** not wanting to damage friendships

We overcome these barriers through:
- **Prayer and dependence on the Holy Spirit** to give us boldness and wisdom
- **Clarity about our message**—the gospel is simple: Christ died, rose, and offers forgiveness
- **Living authentically** so our life demonstrates the reality of our faith
- **Genuine love for people** that transcends our discomfort
- **Remembering the stakes**—eternal separation from God is the alternative

---

## Closing Reflection

As Charles Wesley wrote:

> The world, with sin and Satan, in vain our march opposes,
> By thee we shall break through them all, and sing the song of Moses.

We are not called to fear the world's opposition but to press forward with confidence in Christ's victory. Our witness is not our own effort but participation in God's eternal redemptive plan. Those we know and love without Christ desperately need to hear that a stairway to heaven is available through Jesus—but they must choose it before the highway to hell becomes their final destination.
