# 1 Samuel 18:1-30

**Title:** The Next King
**Date:** 21 August 2022
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## The Problem of Succession

This passage tells the story of David's rise as the new crown prince of Israel—a transition as significant and controversial as any succession crisis in human history. Just as modern kingdoms wrestle with the question of who will rule next, Israel faced a pivotal moment when God's Spirit departed from Saul and anointed David as the future king.

God had already made His choice clear through the prophet Samuel. David would be king—but not yet. In the meantime, everyone loved this new crown prince.

---

## The Women Love David

When David returned victorious after slaying Goliath, the women of Israel came out from all the towns to celebrate, playing instruments and singing:

> "Saul has slain his thousands, and David his tens of thousands."

This was meant to honor both king and warrior, but there was a problem. The women had come out to meet King Saul, yet their song gave David vastly greater credit. Upon learning that David—not Saul—had killed Goliath, they changed their tune accordingly.

Saul's reaction reveals the danger of wounded pride:

> "Saul was very angry; this refrain displeased him greatly. They have credited David with tens of thousands, he thought, but me with only thousands. What more can he get but the kingdom?" And from that time on Saul kept a close eye on David.

He watched David with an "evil eye"—the eye of envy.

### Understanding Envy

Envy is fundamentally different from ambition. Ambition says, "I want to be as strong as that person" or "I want to be as smart as her." That's healthy striving. But envy operates in a zero-sum world where your neighbor's gain feels like your loss. The envious person doesn't just want a car like yours; they want *your* car. They don't just want to be successful; they want your success taken away.

Envy naturally progresses to theft: "I want that too, but rather than working for it, I'll just take theirs." It poisons relationships and destroys peace.

Saul was driven by fear and insecurity. Yet this made no sense—God Himself had appointed Saul as king. And God had appointed David. Either way, it was always God's choice, not a limited prize to be fought over.

---

## All Israel and Judah Love David

Saul's envy drove him to a desperate strategy: send David to war and let the Philistines do the job for him. 

> "So he sent David away from him and gave him command over a thousand men, and David led the troops in their campaigns. In everything he did he had great success, because the Lord was with him."

The plan backfired spectacularly. David's military success only increased his popularity:

> "When Saul saw how successful he was, he was afraid of him. But all Israel and Judah loved David, because he led them in their campaigns."

Saul found himself in an impossible catch-22: envious of David, he was given an evil spirit that tormented him. Yet only David's music could calm him. He couldn't live with David and couldn't live without him. His envy and fear had trapped him.

---

## Michal Loves David

Saul's next scheme was marriage. He would give David his daughter Merab, ostensibly as a reward for bravery, but secretly hoping the Philistines would kill David in the ensuing battles. When Merab was given to another man instead, Saul discovered his younger daughter Michal was already in love with David.

Saul saw an opportunity:

> "I will give her to him, so that she may be a snare to him and so that the hand of the Philistines may be against him."

David asked what bride-price would be required. Saul's answer was calculated to be impossible: one hundred Philistine foreskins, taken in battle.

> "David took his men with him and went out and killed two hundred Philistines and brought back their foreskins. They counted out the full number to the king so that David might become the king's son-in-law. Then Saul gave him his daughter Michal in marriage."

David's success once again thwarted Saul's plans. Now David was not merely a general—he was the king's son-in-law, a legitimate heir to the throne. 

> "When Saul realized that the Lord was with David and that his daughter Michal loved David, Saul became still more afraid of him, and he remained his enemy the rest of his days."

Every plan Saul devised, God used to establish David's position further.

---

## Jonathan Loves David

> "After David had finished talking with Saul, Jonathan became one in spirit with David, and he loved him as himself. From that day Saul kept David with him and did not let him return home to his family. And Jonathan made a covenant with David because he loved him as himself. Jonathan took off the robe he was wearing and gave it to David, along with his tunic, and even his sword, his bow and his belt."

In our modern culture, such deep devotion between two men is often misinterpreted. But Jonathan's love was not erotic—it was the unconditional devotion and sacrifice that characterizes the greatest friendships. Love can manifest with sex (as in marriage) or without it (as in familial bonds, deep friendships, or our love for God). In heaven, there will be no more marriage, yet love will remain eternal.

When Jonathan took off his royal robe and gave it to David, he was surrendering his royal uniform—his identity as crown prince. When he laid down his weapons before David, he was symbolizing submission. Jonathan recognized what his father refused to accept: David was God's anointed king.

This was the crucial difference between father and son.

**Saul's response:** Defensive, plotting, attempting to eliminate the threat.
**Jonathan's response:** Submission, joy in serving the anointed, voluntary abdication of his own claims.

Jonathan did not rival David; he embraced him. Where Saul saw a competitor for the throne, Jonathan saw God's chosen king. The more Saul resisted, the more obvious David's divine appointment became.

---

## The Consequences of Refusing to Submit

By the end of this chapter, Saul's kingdom was collapsing—not because David was a threat, but because Saul refused to submit to God's will.

**Saul could not govern:**
- His own emotions (rage and jealousy consumed him)
- David (every plan to eliminate him failed)
- His people (they loved David, not him)
- Even his own children (Jonathan sided with David; Michal deceived him to save David's life)

Everything spiraled out of control because of envy and fear rooted in a refusal to accept what God had ordained.

---

## The Principle: Follow the Anointed

> "Whats more important than being loved by people is being loved by God."

David's life demonstrates this truth. Despite Saul's relentless pursuit, despite years as a fugitive, David knew he was loved by God. That was enough. That made him invincible—not because harm couldn't touch him, but because his identity was secure in God's choice, not in human approval.

Jesus teaches us the same principle. At His baptism, the Father declared:

> "This is my beloved Son, with whom I am well pleased."

Though Jesus suffered on the cross, God exalted Him to the highest place. And Paul writes:

> "We know that in all things God works for the good of those who love him, who have been called according to his purpose. For those God foreknew he also predestined to be conformed to the image of his Son, that he might be the firstborn among many brothers and sisters. And those he predestined, he also called; those he called, he also justified; those he justified, he also glorified." (Romans 8:28-30)

This promise transforms how we face trials and temptations. They are not full-stops to our lives but means to a greater end. We can be hopeful because God's love and purposes cannot be thwarted.

---

## Application: What Will You Do With Jesus?

The central question this passage raises is deeply personal: What is your response to Jesus, the true King?

Are you still at war with Him? Do you have your armor and sword drawn, defending your own kingdom? Or will you lay those weapons down and submit?

Do you still believe you are the crown prince of your own life, that the world revolves around you? Or will you relinquish that title and acknowledge that the world revolves around Jesus?

If you are still in rebellion against God, the invitation is open today. Lay down your sword. Submit to the Anointed One. Let Him be King.

Like Jonathan, we find true freedom not in grasping for the throne but in joyfully serving the one God has chosen. That is the path to blessing, to purpose, and to the abundant life that only submission to Christ can bring.
