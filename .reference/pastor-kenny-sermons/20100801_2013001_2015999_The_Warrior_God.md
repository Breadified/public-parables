# Exodus 13-15
**Title:** The Warrior God
**Date:** 22 August 2010
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## The Heart That Changes

We live in a world of changing minds. Politicians shift their positions based on new facts. Constituents reconsider their votes. There's nothing inherently wrong with reconsidering when presented with fresh evidence. But in today's passage, we encounter a profound question about how God relates to human choice and divine sovereignty.

### Pharaoh's Final Change of Heart

After enduring nine plagues and watching his firstborn son die, Pharaoh finally released Israel. Yet the moment the people departed, something shifted in his mind:

> When the king of Egypt was told that the people had fled, Pharaoh and his officials changed their minds about them and said, "What have we done? We have let the Israelites go and have lost their services!" So he had his chariot made ready and took his army with him. He took six hundred of the best chariots, along with all the other chariots of Egypt, with officers over all of them. (Exodus 14:5-7)

The irony cuts deep. After witnessing God's power through ten cosmic judgments, after seeing the death of Egypt's firstborn, Pharaoh's concern reduces to one thing: economic loss. The prospect of losing a massive labor force drives him to send his army to certain death.

Yet we must reckon with his spiritual blindness. After attempting infanticide, after enslaving an entire people, after ignoring God's repeated warnings—he still refuses to yield. His hardened heart leads him to march six hundred chariots into the sea.

### The Problem of God's Hardening

Here we encounter one of Scripture's most troubling teachings: God actively hardened Pharaoh's heart.

> Then the LORD said to Moses, "Tell the Israelites to turn back and encamp near Pi Hahiroth, between Migdol and the sea. They are to encamp by the sea, directly opposite Baal Zephon. Pharaoh will think, 'The Israelites are wandering around the land in confusion, hemmed in by the desert.' And I will harden Pharaoh's heart, and he will pursue them. But I will gain glory for myself through Pharaoh and all his army, and the Egyptians will know that I am the LORD." (Exodus 14:1-4)

This troubles us deeply. If God hardened Pharaoh's heart, how can Pharaoh be held responsible? How can punishment be just if the condemned had no choice? Doesn't fairness require freedom?

This is precisely what Paul anticipates in Romans 9:

> What then shall we say? Is God unjust? Not at all! For he says to Moses, "I will have mercy on whom I have mercy, and I will have compassion on whom I have compassion." It does not, therefore, depend on man's desire or effort, but on God's mercy. For the Scripture says to Pharaoh: "I raised you up for this very purpose, that I might display my power in you and that my name might be proclaimed in all the earth." Therefore God has mercy on whom he wants to have mercy, and he hardens whom he wants to harden. (Romans 9:14-18)

Our immediate response is to protest:

> "Then why does God still blame us? For who resists his will?" But who are you, O man, to talk back to God? "Shall what is formed say to him who formed it, 'Why did you make me like this?'" Does not the potter have the right to make out of the same lump of clay some pottery for noble purposes and some for common use? What if God, choosing to show his wrath and make his power known, bore with great patience the objects of his wrath—prepared for destruction? What if he did this to make the riches of his glory known to the objects of his mercy, whom he prepared in advance for glory—even us, whom he also called, not only from the Jews but also from the Gentiles? (Romans 9:19-24)

## God's Sovereignty vs. Human Responsibility

Scripture holds two truths in tension:

### God is Sovereign

God is absolutely free. He has the sole right to choose because he is God—the Creator, the All-Powerful One, the final Arbiter. His sovereignty is non-negotiable:

> In his heart a man plans his course, but the LORD determines his steps. (Proverbs 16:9)

Throughout Christian history, there has been resistance to this view. Some argue that God is powerful but has carved out space for human free will. Others suggest God doesn't determine events but merely knows them in advance—like watching a DVD of the future. Yet Romans 9 and the broader testimony of Scripture teach something different: God's sovereignty is absolute.

**Why does this matter?** A God who is 99% powerful is not a God who can be trusted. Our confidence in Him doesn't merely drop; it collapses entirely. We cannot place our hope in a weak deity. If God cannot guarantee His purposes or truly know the future, He becomes no different from us—merely clever, not almighty. There is no hope in such a God, for He cannot act decisively in our behalf.

### We Are Responsible

Yet in the realm of human action, we bear responsibility. This is the great paradox Scripture never resolves but simply affirms:

> We freely choose to sin.

We are not passive puppets. We are captive beings—bound by our nature to pursue our own desires—but we choose that captivity. The tragedy is our bondage. Martin Luther captured this in his work *The Bondage of the Will*:

> "Let all the 'free-will' in the world do all it can with all its strength; it will never give rise to a single instance of ability to avoid being hardened if God does not give the Spirit, or of meriting mercy if it is left to its own strength."

This doesn't excuse us. It indicts us. We are not victims; we are rebels who have chosen our Master.

## Israel's Faithlessness

If Pharaoh's hardness reveals human obstinacy, Israel's response reveals human ingratitude.

> As Pharaoh approached, the Israelites looked up, and there were the Egyptians, marching after them. They were terrified and cried out to the LORD. They said to Moses, "Was it because there were no graves in Egypt that you brought us to the desert to die? What have you done to us by bringing us out of Egypt? Didn't we say to you in Egypt, 'Leave us alone; let us serve the Egyptians'? It would have been better for us to serve the Egyptians than to die in the desert!" (Exodus 14:10-12)

Consider what God had done for them. Ten cosmic plagues. The death of Egypt's firstborn. Signs and wonders beyond numbering. The entire nation freed, enriched with Egyptian gold and silver. The greatest miracle in human history—deliverance from slavery.

And the moment they face a problem, their response is: *Let us serve Pharaoh.*

God had even shown wisdom in their route:

> When Pharaoh let the people go, God did not lead them on the road through the Philistine country, though that was shorter. For God said, "If they face war, they might change their minds and return to Egypt." So God led the people around by the desert road toward the Red Sea. (Exodus 13:17-18)

God provided constant companionship:

> By day the LORD went ahead of them in a pillar of cloud to guide them on their way and by night in a pillar of fire to give them light, so that they could travel by day or night. (Exodus 13:21-22)

Yet despite all this mercy, despite the pillar of cloud by day and fire by night, they became captive to fear and doubt. Their refrain would echo through the wilderness: *It would have been better to serve the Egyptians.*

The contrast is devastating. They carried Joseph's bones, trusting his ancient promise:

> God will surely come to your aid, and then you must carry my bones up with you from this place. (Exodus 13:19)

A dead man showed more faith than the living people whom God was actively saving.

## The Human Condition

This is the condition of unredeemed humanity. Given wealth, we hoard it. Given talents, we waste them on temporal pursuits. Given the opportunity to serve the living God, we choose to serve dead Pharaohs. When we could fish for souls, we lay bricks building monuments to kings long dust.

Without the Spirit and the Word of God, we are captive beings. We freely choose our bondage.

And our nature doesn't change easily. The moment trouble comes—sickness, opposition, unmet expectations—we think: *I wish I weren't a Christian. God, you should have left me alone.* When an atheist offers a semi-plausible critique, we're tempted to abandon everything we believe.

But there's something even more subtle. We become seekers of experience rather than students of Scripture. We long for the next spiritual high—the emotional moment, the felt presence, the extraordinary encounter. We look for warm and fuzzy feelings in a charismatic experience or a mystical encounter.

Yet we forget the greatest experience God offers: forgiveness. We have been saved from eternal death. We have been transformed from sinners to saints. The Passover Lamb has been sacrificed for us. This is the supreme miracle, the ultimate experience.

If you seek experiences, pursue these: the experience of conquering sin; the experience of forgiving those who wrong you; the experience of suffering for Christ; the experience of sacrifice—giving your time, money, and energy for the gospel; the experience of love—acting for others' good rather than your own.

But we forget all this and chase empty feelings. We can do better. We must not be fickle like the Israelites of old. We have been given something infinitely greater than they had: Jesus. We have the Word of promise and the Spirit of God.

## God Does Not Change

Amidst all this human vacillation, we have profound comfort: God does not change His mind.

When Israel's fear threatened to undo them, God's word to them was stable:

> Moses answered the people, "Do not be afraid. Stand firm and you will see the deliverance the LORD will bring you today. The Egyptians you see today you will never see again. The LORD will fight for you; you need only to be still." (Exodus 14:13-14)

"Be still" doesn't mean passivity or meditation. It means trust. It means steadying yourself in God's previously given direction. It means remembering what God has already commanded and following through.

Then came a rebuke with an assignment:

> Then the LORD said to Moses, "Why are you crying out to me? Tell the Israelites to move on. Raise your staff and stretch out your hand over the sea to divide the water so that the Israelites can go through the sea on dry ground. I will harden the hearts of the Egyptians so that they will go in after them. And I will gain glory through Pharaoh and all his army, through his chariots and his horsemen. The Egyptians will know that I am the LORD when I gain glory through Pharaoh, his chariots and his horsemen." (Exodus 14:15-18)

And so God acted with the same power and the same purpose:

> Then the angel of God, who had been traveling in front of Israel's army, withdrew and went behind them. The pillar of cloud also moved from in front and stood behind them, coming between the armies of Egypt and Israel. Throughout the night the cloud brought darkness to the one side and light to the other side; so neither went near the other all night long. Then Moses stretched out his hand over the sea, and all that night the LORD drove the sea back with a strong east wind and turned it into dry land. The waters were divided, and the Israelites went through the sea on dry ground, with a wall of water on their right and on their left. (Exodus 14:19-22)

## God Is a Warrior God

Moses captures the reality in his song of celebration:

> The LORD is my strength and my song; he has become my salvation. He is my God, and I will praise him, my father's God, and I will exalt him. The LORD is a warrior; the LORD is his name. (Exodus 15:2-3)

> Who among the gods is like you, O LORD? Who is like you—majestic in holiness, awesome in glory, working wonders? (Exodus 15:11)

> In your unfailing love you will lead the people you have redeemed. In your strength you will guide them to your holy dwelling. (Exodus 15:13)

God is faithful to His promise to save. He does not change, and therefore His people are secure.

This is the profound comfort of God's sovereignty. Thank God He does not change His mind about salvation. Thank God He holds to His purpose. If His commitment were as fickle as ours, we would be lost. But He has promised to save, and save He will.

For those who walk with Him, nothing can separate us from His love:

> And we know that in all things God works for the good of those who love him, who have been called according to his purpose. For those God foreknew he also predestined to be conformed to the likeness of his Son, that he might be the firstborn among many brothers. And those he predestined, he also called; those he called, he also justified; those he justified, he also glorified. What, then, shall we say in response to this? If God is for us, who can be against us? (Romans 8:28-31)

> I am convinced that neither death nor life, neither angels nor demons, neither the present nor the future, nor any powers, neither height nor depth, nor anything else in all creation, will be able to separate us from the love of God that is in Christ Jesus our Lord. (Romans 8:38-39)

Even our own faithlessness cannot disqualify us from His grace:

> If we are faithless, he will remain faithful, for he cannot disown himself. (2 Timothy 2:13)

We do stupid things as Christians. We act in stubborn selfishness. We stumble and fall. But our stupidity does not disqualify us. Our stubbornness does not separate us. Our selfishness does not undo His commitment.

Thank God that He is bigger than us. Thank God that He holds on to us even when we foolishly try to let go. In our weakness, He remains strong. In our faithlessness, He remains faithful. In our rebellion, He remains our Redeemer.

This is the God of Exodus. This is the Warrior God who saves.
