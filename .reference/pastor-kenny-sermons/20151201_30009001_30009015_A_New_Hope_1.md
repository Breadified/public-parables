# Amos 9:1-15

**Title:** A New Hope  
**Date:** 27 December 2015  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## A Terrible Year

This has been a terrible year. Lockdown. We thought the vaccine would solve our problems, but it hasn't. And for many of you, it's been worse still—sickness, death in the family, unemployment, failed endeavors, and perhaps most painfully, relationship breakdown.

Amos speaks directly into times of despair. He was a prophet from the southern kingdom of Judah, called to proclaim a difficult message to the northern kingdom of Israel. His words predict not just one terrible year, but years of turmoil for God's people.

## The Thresholds Shake (Verses 1-10)

> I saw the Lord standing by the altar, and he said: Strike the tops of the pillars so that the thresholds shake. Bring them down on the heads of all the people; those who are left I will kill with the sword. Not one will get away, none will escape. (1:1)

The shaking language here likely refers to an earthquake mentioned in Amos 1:1—predicted two years before it would occur. When you shake something, everything goes up and down, like a snow globe. The Israelites' lives would be turned upside down.

### Nowhere to Hide

The passage develops this theme with vivid imagery:

> Though they dig down to the depths below, from there my hand will take them. Though they climb up to the heavens above, from there I will bring them down. Though they hide themselves on the top of Carmel, there I will hunt them down and seize them. Though they hide from my eyes at the bottom of the sea, there I will command the serpent to bite them. (2-3)

This is a merism—two extremes that encompass everything in between. There is nowhere to escape God's judgment. The passage emphasizes this with imagery of water rising and falling like the Nile (verses 5-6), illustrating that judgment penetrates everywhere.

The theological point is profound: You cannot take God's assurance and security in one moment and then avoid His scrutiny and judgment in the next.

### God's All-Seeing Eyes

> Surely the eyes of the Sovereign Lord are on the sinful kingdom. I will destroy it from the face of the earth. Yet I will not totally destroy the descendants of Jacob, declares the Lord. (8)

This reverses the original promise God made to Israel in Deuteronomy 11:11-12. When Moses first addressed the Israelites before entering the promised land, he assured them that God's eyes would be upon them constantly—watching over them with care, protecting them from giants, enemies, and unknown dangers. But now, Amos reveals the twist: God's eyes are still upon them, but for judgment, not protection.

No one escapes God's justice. Just as it took years to locate Osama Bin Laden despite the resources of the world's most powerful nation, ultimately justice caught up with him. In the same way, no sinner evades God's eye.

### A Hope for Justice

This is actually good news. In a year marked by injustice—terrorism, corporate corruption, exploitation, governmental malfeasance—we can rest assured that God sees it all. One day those operating illegally will answer. Those crafting laws to benefit themselves will face consequences. Those committing genocide will face God's reckoning.

We don't have to carry the anxiety of injustice alone. If we can address wrongdoing, we should. But if we've done all we can, we can trust God with the outcome. We don't have to grow bitter or be weighed down by hatred and vengeance. That burden belongs to God now.

For those personally wronged this year—bullied, discriminated against, betrayed, stolen from—the promise is the same. We can let go of personal hurts and wait for God's judgment. This frees us from the bitterness that would destroy our souls. We can park these wounds with God and start the new year afresh, perhaps not fully forgiving those who wronged us, but at least letting go of the burden to carry.

## The Threshold Rebuilt (Verses 11-15)

Yet Amos doesn't end with judgment alone. As the prophecy concludes, there is a glimmer of hope—so hopeful that some scholars suspect it was added later. But there's no textual evidence for this. We must take it as written.

> In that day I will restore David's fallen shelter; I will repair its broken walls and restore its ruins and will rebuild it as it used to be. (11)

The imagery shifts from destruction to restoration: restore, repair, rebuild. David's shelter—his tabernacle, his dynasty—will be rebuilt. And when God completes His final judgment, He will restore the world again.

### Abundance Without End

> The days are coming, declares the Lord, when the reaper will be overtaken by the plowman and the planter by the one treading grapes. (13)

This echoes Leviticus 26:5—Moses' promise of unbroken agricultural prosperity where harvest flows seamlessly into planting, and planting into harvest. But Amos has deliberately altered this. Now the abundance is so great that these cycles overlap and intensify each other. Different fields compete with each other in productivity. Imagine a warehouse so efficient in sending stock that shelves fill faster than employees can stock them. Boxes and pallets pile up. The supply is so immense that it overflows:

> New wine will drip from the mountains and flow from all the hills. (12)

Like a dam bursting, the abundance cascades down the slopes.

### Return and Permanence

> I will bring my people Israel back from exile. They will rebuild the ruined cities and live in them. They will plant vineyards and drink their wine; they will make gardens and eat their fruit. (14)

Just as when they first entered the promised land, finding cities they didn't build and vineyards they didn't plant, so again they will enjoy what they didn't labor for. But the difference is crucial:

> I will plant Israel in their own land, never again to be uprooted from the land I have given them, says the Lord your God. (15)

Never again will they be torn away. This will be permanent.

### How Is This Possible?

How can those who forfeited their place through sin be restored permanently? The answer points to Jesus. Acts 15:16-18 quotes this prophecy directly:

> After this I will return and rebuild David's fallen tent. Its ruins I will rebuild, and I will restore it, that the rest of mankind may seek the Lord, even all the Gentiles who bear my name, says the Lord, who does these things.

It is through the grace of our Lord Jesus Christ that we are saved. Jesus, the Son of David, is the one who rebuilds David's shelter. Though we deserve judgment—all of us have acted unjustly toward others and toward God—through Jesus we can be saved from judgment. We receive permanent forgiveness, not just a temporary reprieve.

The promise to us is heaven itself—a place where supply outstrips demand, where we will never be uprooted again.

## A New Year and a New Hope

Perhaps this year has brought unwanted changes. But here is good news: you have the chance to start over. We don't know what the coming year will bring, but we will be ready because we are living in God's forgiveness.

This is another year that God has given us to do His work of telling others the good news. Enter it with hope, knowing that though we live in a world still shaken by injustice and suffering, our God sees all, judges all, and has promised through His Son a restoration that will never be undone.
