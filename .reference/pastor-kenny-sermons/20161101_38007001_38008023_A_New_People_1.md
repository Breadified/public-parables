# Zechariah 7:1-8:23

**Title:** A New People
**Date:** 27 November 2016
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Setting the Scene

We find ourselves in the festive season—that time of year when the heat builds, Christmas trees appear in shops, and Mariah Carey plays on the radio. It's easy to get caught up in the busyness of celebrations.

But our passage invites us to consider something deeper: the balance between fasting and feasting. Let me set the historical context for you.

Nearly 2,500 years ago, the Jewish people had just returned to their ruined homeland after seventy years of exile in Babylon. They were rebuilding—their city, their temple, their lives. Yet uncertainty lingered: Where was God in all this? His answer came clearly: Return to me, and I will return to you.

## Part 1: The Question About Fasting

The people came to Zechariah with a practical question. After seventy years of exile, they had established fasts to commemorate key moments of devastation:

> In the fourth year of King Darius, the word of the Lord came to Zechariah on the fourth day of the ninth month, the month of Kislev. The people of Bethel had sent Sharezer and Regem-Melek, together with their men, to entreat the Lord by asking the priests of the house of the Lord Almighty and the prophets, "Should I mourn and fast in the fifth month, as I have done for so many years?"

Their question was direct: Can we stop fasting now that we're back in the land?

### Understanding Their Fasts

These weren't arbitrary practices. The people had observed four major fasts during the exile:

- **Fifth month:** Commemorating the destruction of the temple itself (2 Kings 25:8)
- **Tenth month:** Remembering the beginning of Nebuchadnezzar's siege (2 Kings 25:1-2)
- **Fourth month:** Marking the fall of Jerusalem's walls (2 Kings 25:3-4)
- **Seventh month:** Observing the assassination of Gedaliah, the governor (2 Kings 25:25)

For seventy years, this second and third generation had kept these memorial fasts, wearing sackcloth and mourning as an expression of repentance. Fasting was meant to be symbolic—a physical expression of humility before God and sorrow over sin.

Only one fast was actually commanded in the Old Testament: the Day of Atonement (Leviticus 23:27). But out of their history, the people had created these additional fasts to remember what they had lost.

### God's Piercing Question

But God's answer to their question wasn't what they expected. Instead, He turned the tables:

> Ask all the people of the land and the priests, "When you fasted and mourned in the fifth and seventh months for the past seventy years, was it really for me that you fasted?

And when you were eating and drinking, were you not just feasting for yourselves?

God wasn't questioning whether they had fasted diligently. He was questioning their hearts. Were they fasting out of genuine repentance and devotion to Him, or merely out of self-pity? Were they feasting for themselves, indulging their own desires—just as they had been fasting for themselves, using the ritual to make themselves feel righteous?

Think of it this way: A criminal weeps, but not for the victim or the damage caused—only for his own plight. The Israelites followed their fasting rituals diligently, yet their attitudes were far from God. They had the form without the substance.

### The Flashback: A Reminder of Stubbornness

To drive home His point, God reminded them of what the earlier prophets had proclaimed. When the land was at rest and prosperous, the prophets called them to justice and faithfulness. But the people's response was stubborn refusal:

> But they refused to pay attention; stubbornly they turned their backs and covered their ears. They made their hearts as hard as flint and would not listen to the law or to the words that the Lord Almighty had sent by his Spirit through the earlier prophets.

Notice the layers of stubbornness: turning their backs, covering their ears, hardening their hearts like flint. They actively resisted God's word.

And so came the consequence:

> When I called, they did not listen; so when they called, I would not listen, says the Lord Almighty.

> I scattered them with a whirlwind among all the nations, where they were strangers. The land they left behind them was so desolate that no one traveled through it. This is how they made the pleasant land desolate.

The pleasant land—the land of milk and honey—became a wasteland. Their disobedience had consequences.

### The Real Issue with Fasting

This brings us to the heart of the matter. Fasting was meant to address something crucial: **The Israelites were supposed to be mourning over their sin and resolving to stop sinning.**

When Moses fasted in the wilderness, God used that time to teach: "Man does not live on bread alone but on every word that comes from the mouth of the Lord" (Deuteronomy 8:3). Fasting was meant to humble us, to remind us of our dependence on God, to help us remember that our true nourishment comes from His word, not from earthly food.

The Day of Atonement fast wasn't simply about going hungry. It was about remembering sin, about standing before God in acknowledgment of our brokenness, about recognizing that someone—ultimately Christ—would die to atone for that sin. It was meant to turn us from sin and toward righteousness.

### The Pharisee Problem

Fast forward to Jesus' day, and we see the same problem in a new form. The Pharisees had created extensive fasting practices:

> The Pharisee stood by himself and prayed: "God, I thank you that I am not like other people—robbers, evildoers, adulterers—or even like this tax collector. I fast twice a week and give a tenth of all I get."

But notice: No one asked the Pharisees to fast twice a week. This wasn't commanded. They invented it to signal their own righteousness—what we might call "virtue signaling" today.

We see this pattern everywhere now. Schools and offices are filled with special harmony days, NAIDOC weeks, World Health Day, Earth Day, purple days, pride days. One calendar I looked at had one or two events almost every week. And the message is often: if you wear a certain color or display a certain symbol, you're a better person than those who don't.

But do these token gestures actually solve the problems they claim to address? Often they seem to make things worse—they polarize, demonize people, radicalize children, and offer poor solutions. Yet we continue these "man-made fastings" as if they're accomplishing something.

This is why God spoke through the prophet Amos:

> I hate, I despise your religious festivals; your assemblies are a stench to me.

Why? Because these ceremonies don't address the real problem: a heart turned away from God. Token rituals and political correctness—without genuine justice, mercy, and transformation—are worthless.

## Part 2: The Path Forward (Zechariah 8)

### God's Promises of Restoration

Chapter 8 shifts from rebuke to hope. God makes ten declarations beginning with "This is what the Lord Almighty says." These are divine promises, spoken with absolute authority:

> I am very jealous for Zion; I am burning with jealousy for her.

This jealousy isn't negative. It's not envy or bitterness—that would be the opposite of contentment. This is the jealousy of love, like a husband protecting his wife because she belongs to him. It's passion. It's care. God's jealousy reflects His deep commitment to His people.

And His promise:

> I will return to Zion and dwell in Jerusalem. Then Jerusalem will be called the Faithful City, and the mountain of the Lord Almighty will be called the Holy Mountain.

God will return and live with His people. The city will be transformed into a place of faithfulness. But here's the critical point: **The people were not ready.** God was ready to return, but the temple—His dwelling place—remained incomplete. Build the temple! was the urgent call.

### The Real Call: Justice and Mercy

But beyond the physical building, God issued a call to the people's hearts. This is what truly mattered:

> Administer true justice; show mercy and compassion to one another. Do not oppress the widow or the fatherless, the foreigner or the poor. Do not plot evil against each other.

These aren't abstract principles. They're concrete directives. Widows, orphans, foreigners, the poor—these are the vulnerable, those with no legal standing or protection. God's people were called to be their defenders.

True justice means enforcing the law fairly—eye for an eye, neither more nor less. But justice must be tempered with mercy and compassion for genuine cases where circumstances demand exception. English law understood this with its two court systems: courts of law and courts of equity.

The critical phrase appears again in chapter 8:

> Speak the truth to each other, and render true and sound judgment in your courts; do not plot evil against each other, and do not love to swear falsely.

God demands both righteousness in our dealings and honesty in our words. We must not scheme against one another or take false oaths.

### The Reversal: From Fasting to Feasting

Then comes the joyful declaration:

> The fasts of the fourth, fifth, seventh and tenth months will become joyful and glad occasions and happy festivals for Judah. Therefore love truth and peace.

The very days they had mourned would become days of celebration! But notice the condition: this transformation depends on loving truth and peace. True joy comes not from mere festivity but from righteousness.

And look at the picture God paints of restored Jerusalem:

> Once again men and women of ripe old age will sit in the streets of Jerusalem, each of them with cane in hand because of their age. The city streets will be filled with boys and girls playing there.

This is complete community—the old and young together. In war, you lose the elderly and the children. But here, in peace, all generations flourish. Children play freely; the elderly sit in the squares. This is shalom—wholeness and security.

### The Universal Welcome

But God's vision extends even further:

> I will save my people from the countries of the east and the west. I will bring them back to live in Jerusalem; they will be my people, and I will be faithful and righteous to them as their God.

And most remarkably:

> Many peoples and the inhabitants of many cities will yet come, and the inhabitants of one city will go to another and say, "Let us go at once to entreat the Lord and seek the Lord Almighty."

> Many peoples and powerful nations will come to Jerusalem to seek the Lord Almighty and to entreat him.

> In those days ten people from all languages and nations will take firm hold of one Jew by the hem of his robe and say, "Let us go with you, because we have heard that God is with you."

This is extraordinary. Foreigners would seek out God's people, drawn by the presence of God among them. They would grab hold of a Jewish person's robe, begging to be included, to follow where God is.

The Jews were meant to be that model to the nations. But they never became it. The Pharisees were legalistic, forfeiting their role as bringers of the Gentiles to God.

**Until one Jew came.** Jesus was the true Jew—the one who acted with right motivations and right deeds. He cared for widows, orphans, foreigners, and the poor. He confronted unjust rulers. He embodied what Israel was called to be.

When Jesus came, the Spirit of God returned. And when His disciples followed Him, they grabbed hold of His robe—just like the bleeding woman in Mark 5—seeking to follow Him and receive healing.

Listen to what Jesus said about fasting and feasting:

> How can the guests of the bridegroom mourn while he is with them? The time will come when the bridegroom will be taken from them; then they will fast.

While Jesus was present, His disciples didn't fast—they feasted with Him. After His ascension, they would fast. But the ultimate feast is still to come.

## Practical Application: Our Fasting and Feasting Today

We don't emphasize fasting much in our Christian circles today, but fasting can still serve important purposes:

**Symbolic mourning and humility:** We remember our dependence on God, not on bread alone.

**Thankfulness:** We learn not to take our provision for granted.

**Discipline:** We exercise control over our bodies and our appetites.

**Generosity:** By denying ourselves, we have more to give to others.

Fasting reminds us to refrain from sin, to remember God, and to deny ourselves for the sake of others.

Consider what happens at our church lunches. Yes, it's a simple meal—perhaps simpler than what you might buy for yourself elsewhere. In one sense, we're fasting from "better food" for the sake of fellowship. Or when some of you buy lunch for others, you're making an opportunity cost—spending money that could go to your own future meal. That's fasting, too.

And I've seen it: when we've undercatered, some of you have given up your meals so others could eat. You stayed to encourage one another, quietly fasting so others could be fed. These are the fruits of genuine fasting.

This is what Jesus meant when He said:

> Whoever wants to be my disciple must deny themselves and take up their cross and follow me.

We're in a season of fasting. But it won't always be like this.

## The Promise of the Feast

As we move into the festive season, think about what we're really celebrating. Summer offers great opportunities for our friends and family to hear about Jesus. Our church calendar is full of gatherings—Christmas activities, carols, and a Festivale season beginning with a church picnic on New Year's Day.

These are opportunities for feasting—in the biblical sense. Remember:

> The people of Judah and Israel were as numerous as the sand on the seashore; they ate, they drank and they were happy.

Joy and celebration are meant to be expressions of God's blessing and our gratitude.

The fasts turned to feasts. And one day, we'll gather for the ultimate feast:

> Blessed is the one who will eat at the feast in the kingdom of God.

## Final Word: Are You Ready?

There's an important question beneath all this. Yes, we know from God's promises that He is returning. But are *you* ready for His return?

> The day of the Lord will come like a thief in the night. While people are saying, "Peace and safety," destruction will come on them suddenly, as labor pains on a pregnant woman, and they will not escape.

> But you, brothers and sisters, are not in darkness so that this day should surprise you like a thief. You are all children of the light and children of the day. So then, let us not be like others, who are asleep, but let us be awake and sober.

> Since everything will be destroyed in this way, what kind of people ought you to be? You ought to live holy and godly lives as you look forward to the day of God.

The question applies to us as it did to the Jews centuries ago. God is ready. His promises are sure. His temple—now His church, His people—is being built. But are you ready to welcome Him?

If you're not yet a Christian and want to be ready, there will be an opportunity after this talk to make that decision.

The party is coming. While we wait, we fast. We deny ourselves. We practice justice and mercy. We love truth and peace. And we invite others into the feast.

Because that's what a new people looks like—a people prepared for the return of their King.
