# James 1:19-27

**Title:** The Word Transplanted
**Date:** 22 April 2012
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Wasted Knowledge

University taught me theory without practice. I studied law for years, yet I didn't step into a courtroom until jury duty decades later. This is true for most education—we learn to pass exams, cram information into our heads for a semester, then forget it the week after. The knowledge changes nothing about how we actually live.

But Bible knowledge is fundamentally different. James doesn't challenge us merely to know Scripture; he demands that we *use* it. This is about transformation, not information.

## 1. Get Rid of the Filth (v.21)

> Therefore, get rid of all moral filth and the evil that is so prevalent and humbly accept the word planted in you, which can save you.

The word of God has already been planted in us as Christians. This is the gospel of Jesus' death and resurrection—something anyone who has repented possesses. And this word is not an accessory, not optional like a handbag or air freshener. It's integral to our existence, like being born.

James echoes Jeremiah's covenant promise:

> "I will put my law in their minds and write it on their hearts. I will be their God, and they will be my people." (Jeremiah 31:33-34)

This is the new covenant—God gives His people a heart oriented toward Him. When forgiven, they will know God intimately, not through external laws but through internal transformation.

It's like a heart transplant. It's not an accessory to our existence; it's part of who we are now. But sometimes the old body rejects the new heart. We want our old habits back. We want the moral filth.

Yet a new heart requires new practices—just as a transplant patient must exercise and change their diet.

**The Evil is Real**

James calls us to recognize and reject "all moral filth" and "the evil that is so prevalent." We live in a world where wickedness runs deep. We don't like to admit it—we prefer to think people are inherently good. But the Bible tells us plainly: we are capable of genuine evil, not because we're mad, but because we're sinful.

**Receive with Humility**

Rather than anger or proud arrogance, God calls us to listen with humility:

> "This is the one I esteem: he who is humble and contrite in spirit, and trembles at my word." (Isaiah 66:2)

When God's word cuts close to our hearts, our natural response is defensiveness and anger. We attack rather than listen. But James calls us to something different: to receive the word with humility and teachability, not with wrath.

## 2. Do the Word (v.22-25)

> Do not merely listen to the word, and so deceive yourselves. Do what it says.

This is the heart of James' challenge: action. We are called not just to hear but to obey.

**The Mirror Analogy**

> Anyone who listens to the word but does not do what it says is like a man who looks at his face in a mirror and, after looking at himself, goes away and immediately forgets what he looks like. (v.23-24)

Imagine constantly going back to the mirror—"Do I look good? Do I have zits? Did I comb my hair?" A forgetful person. A double-minded person.

This describes us sometimes. We hear God's word at church, agree it's true, commit to following Him. But then we see something attractive in the world, or face a trial, and suddenly what God said matters less. We forget.

Or we encounter trials and think Bible knowledge is just theory—something we learned but don't need to use. But trials are precisely when the word becomes practice. They test whether we truly believe what we claim to believe.

**Remember and Continue**

> But the man who looks intently into the perfect law that gives freedom, and continues to do this, not forgetting what he has heard, but doing it—he will be blessed in what he does. (v.25)

The "perfect law that gives freedom" is the gospel. Don't forget it. Continue in it. Do it. And you will find joy—real blessing—as you put faith into action.

## 3. Pure Religion (v.26-27)

> If anyone considers himself religious and yet does not keep a tight rein on his tongue, he deceives himself and his religion is worthless. Religion that God our Father accepts as pure and faultless is this: to look after orphans and widows in their distress and to keep oneself from being polluted by the world.

**What James Means by Religion**

The word "religion" is strange here. You might expect James to say pure religion is prayer or fasting or ritual. But he defines it as caring for the vulnerable and staying unstained by worldly corruption.

In James' time, many Jewish Christians equated religion with observing laws and maintaining ritual purity. But James redefines what it means to be different from the world. It's not about rules—it's about love.

The new covenant mark of God's people is not external conformity but internal transformation expressed through love:

> "By this all men will know that you are my disciples, if you love one another." (John 13:35)

**Not an Intellectual Exercise**

Pure religion isn't about having the right doctrine (though doctrine matters). It's a package deal, like Freedom Furniture—when you buy the product, you get the whole thing. Your faith must have feet. Your beliefs must have hands. Your convictions must shape your wallet.

**Practically Living It Out**

Visiting orphans in their affliction—this means concrete help. Food. Guidance. Transportation. Friendship.

Caring for widows—especially in cultures where they're isolated and lonely. They need companionship and practical support.

Staying unstained from the world—this doesn't mean withdrawing into isolation. It means refusing to join the world in rampant selfishness. It means your life looks different because you follow Jesus.

## James' Three Challenges

**First: Consistency**

Are your deeds matching your words? You say Christ is important—when did you last share the gospel? You say church matters—are you serving with full effort? Examine yourself. Are you deceiving yourself about your spiritual condition?

**Second: Radical Love**

Be practically loving toward the weakest and neediest. The world is defined by self-interest. Christians are defined by sacrificial love for one another.

**Third: Wholeness**

Christianity isn't a private faith. What you believe shapes everything—your purchases, your choices, your conversations, how you treat the vulnerable. There is no conversion only on the inside. The gospel converts your hands, your mouth, and your wallet. Everything about you should speak Christ.

## The Call to Persevere

We will struggle. Our deeds won't always match our beliefs. This is hard. But keep persevering—the crown of life awaits you.

Faith untested isn't worth much. A life without going out of your way, without sacrifice, without fruit—that's worth nothing. So when you fall short, look once again to the law of liberty won by Christ. Jesus is the one who not only heard God's word but obeyed it. He listened to His Father without retaliating in anger. On the cross, He practically demonstrated His love for us and His obedience.

Keep trusting Jesus, the author and perfector of your faith. Practice that law of liberty. Then look for people you can serve.

Hearing the word and doing the word—this is pure religion.
