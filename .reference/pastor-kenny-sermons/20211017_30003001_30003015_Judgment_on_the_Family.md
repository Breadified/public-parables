# Amos 3:1-15

**Title:** Judgment on the Family

**Date:** October 17, 2021 (ARISE) / November 22, 2015 (SMAC draft)

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## God Knows His Family

> "You only have I chosen of all the families of the earth" (Amos 3:2)

The Hebrew word here is *yada*—"to know"—but it means something far more intimate than casual acquaintance. When God says He "knows" Israel, He means He intimately understands them in their entirety.

This knowledge echoes throughout Scripture. In Psalm 139:4, David writes: "Before a word is on my tongue you, Lord, know it completely." Similarly, Jeremiah recalls God's words: "Before I formed you in the womb I knew you, before you were born I set you apart; I appointed you as a prophet to the nations" (Jeremiah 1:4-5).

This is foreknowledge—God's intimate awareness of all things. Hence, His choice of Israel flows from this complete knowledge.

### The Covenant Promise

God didn't simply select Israel arbitrarily. He made a covenant with them based on ancient promises to Abraham. Out of all the families on earth, Israel became God's chosen tribe—His family. The promise included people, land, and blessings that would extend through generations.

When God brought Israel out of Egypt after 400 years of slavery, He fulfilled these ancient words to Abraham: "I have chosen him, so that he will direct his children and his household after him to keep the way of the Lord by doing what is right and just" (Genesis 18:18-19).

From Egypt's bondage through the Red Sea, from the wilderness to Canaan—God remembered them. He was faithful to His covenant.

### What It Means to Be Known

Family has many definitions—DNA, blood, shared history. But perhaps the deepest definition is this: *a place where you are known*. In a family, everyone knows you. You belong. You've grown up together. They remember your birthday, your struggles, your victories. You've walked through ups and downs side by side.

A church family operates similarly, though without blood relation. It's a community of people who know and belong to one another—not defined by genetics, but by shared faith and commitment to follow Christ together.

But more importantly, God's family is defined by being *known by God*. This isn't a social club or a historical fellowship. It's a gathering of people transformed by God, committed to following His will. What distinguishes the church is that we are known by God Himself.

## God Will Destroy the House of Israel

> "Hear this word, people of Israel, the word the Lord has spoken against you—against the whole family I brought up out of Egypt" (Amos 3:1)

This comes as a shock. Israel is God's chosen family, bound by covenant. They should be secure. Instead, God turns against them. The very relationship they took for granted is now in jeopardy.

### The Dark Side of Being Known

Here lies the bitter truth: if God *knows* you intimately, He also knows all your faults, all your failures, all your sins. Just as family members know each other's weaknesses, God sees everything about Israel. And what He has seen condemns them.

### God's Rhetorical Warning

Rather than immediately listing specific charges, God uses powerful imagery to justify His judgment. His rhetoric is relentless:

> "Do two walk together unless they have agreed to do so?" (Amos 3:3)

Israel agreed to walk with God. They entered the covenant at Joshua 14. Judgment naturally follows covenant-breaking.

> "Does a lion roar in the thicket when it has no prey? Does it growl in its den when it has caught nothing? Does a bird swoop down to a trap on the ground when no bait is there? Does a trap spring up from the ground if it has not caught anything? When a trumpet sounds in a city, do not the people tremble?" (Amos 3:4-6)

This imagery is devastating. A lion roars when it has prey. A bird falls into a trap when baited. A trumpet sounds when invasion comes. In every case, cause precedes effect. Israel's destruction isn't arbitrary—it's the inevitable consequence of sin.

> "Surely the Sovereign Lord does nothing without revealing his plan to his servants the prophets." (Amos 3:7)

God has warned them. Through Amos and other prophets, He has made His intentions clear.

> "The lion has roared—who will not fear? The Sovereign Lord has spoken—who can but prophesy?" (Amos 3:8)

The warning is unmistakable. God Himself has spoken.

### Suffering as God's Megaphone

When hardship comes—whether COVID, natural disaster, or social upheaval—it serves as God's invitation to reflection. We should ask ourselves: What role do I play in this? What must I change? What warning am I meant to heed?

The pandemic exposed systemic weaknesses: economic fragility, inadequate healthcare systems, a fragmented society unable to communicate, let alone solve collective problems. Will we reflect? Will we hear God's warning?

### Israel's Refusal

But Israel refuses to listen. Instead of repenting, they double down on injustice:

> "They do not know how to do right, declares the Lord, who store up in their fortresses what they have plundered and looted." (Amos 3:10)

Their response to God's warnings is hardened hearts and continued oppression.

### Complete Destruction

The consequence is total:

> "An enemy will overrun your land, pull down your strongholds and plunder your fortresses." (Amos 3:11)

> "On the day I punish Israel for her sins, I will destroy the altars of Bethel; the horns of the altar will be cut off and fall to the ground. I will tear down the winter house along with the summer house; the houses adorned with ivory will be destroyed and the mansions will be demolished." (Amos 3:14-15)

All the markers of material blessing—luxurious houses with ivory inlays, summer and winter estates—will be demolished. They will lose the very land God promised to Abraham.

Within 40 years of Amos's prophecy, the Assyrians will destroy the northern kingdom of Israel. The symbol of Assyria was a lion—fulfilling Amos's imagery perfectly. The great Assyrian lion, a 15-ton stone guardian once stationed at temples, becomes the instrument of God's judgment.

By 722 BC, the northern kingdom is annihilated. The people are scattered and interbred until no distinct national identity remains. This is why modern Israelites are called "Jews"—descendants of Judah, the southern kingdom. The other tribes—Reubenites, Gadites, Ephraimites—vanish from history, their genetic legacy erased through Assyrian policy. They lost their land, their people, their lineage. They forgot to know God and did not know how to do right.

## Hope in the Remnant

Yet even in this catastrophic judgment, God preserves hope:

> "As a shepherd rescues from the lion's mouth only two leg bones or a piece of an ear, so will the Israelites living in Samaria be rescued, with only the head of a bed and a piece of fabric from a couch." (Amos 3:12)

Amos was a shepherd. He knew exactly what a lion's prey looks like—when a predator finishes its meal, almost nothing remains. Perhaps a bone or two. Perhaps just an ear. That's all. But even that small remnant is rescued.

Israel will be nearly obliterated, yet a sliver survives. Not many. Not a great nation. Just a few pieces left—but alive. There is hope even in devastation.

### The Samaritan Connection

Centuries later, when Jesus walked the earth, He encountered ten lepers in Samaria. One of them, described as a Samaritan, was healed. This man was a descendant of those who remained after the Assyrian conquest—carrying perhaps an "ear's worth" of Israelite DNA, the genetic remnant Amos prophesied.

When this Samaritan leper was healed, he alone returned to Jesus with gratitude and praise (Luke 17:11-19). How different from the old Samaria, which took God's knowledge for granted and refused to listen.

### The Lesson of Gratitude

Christians are a grateful people. We are the remnant in a loveless world. Yet gratitude doesn't come naturally to us. Many of us tend toward pessimism—seeing life as half-empty rather than half-full. We struggle to recognize God's provision even in small things.

But those of us with grateful hearts see God's hand everywhere. To those who find gratitude difficult, we need to hear from the thankful ones more. After a week of rain, when the sun finally emerges, we shouldn't remain indoors alone. Come out. Encourage one another. Let gratitude become contagious.

Despite calamities, be thankful. For in thanksgiving, we remember that God preserves His people—not through might or perfection, but through His faithful love.
