# John 16:16-33

**Title:** In A Little While
**Date:** 3 April 2016
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Opening Prayer

> Father, tough passage. Help us to read, mark, learn and inwardly digest this word. In Jesus' name we pray Amen.

## Introduction: The Apparent Contradictions

As we read this passage, several statements seem contradictory or confusing. When stress comes, our careful plans collapse—just as a student with exam strategy abandons it in the final panicked minutes, scrambling to write down half-formed thoughts. This passage presents four similar contradictions that deserve careful attention:

1. You won't see me, you will see me (16-19)
2. You will weep, you will rejoice (20-22)
3. You won't ask, you will ask (23-26)
4. I speak figuratively, I won't speak figuratively (25-33)

The commentaries I've consulted largely avoid these tensions. What follows is my attempt to understand what Jesus is actually saying. I welcome your thoughts.

## 1. "You Won't See Me, You Will See Me" (16-19)

> In a little while you will see me no more, and then after a little while you will see me. (16:16)

The disciples are understandably confused. Jesus repeats this statement three times (vv. 16, 17, 19), and they ask plainly: "What does he mean by 'a little while'? We don't understand what he is saying" (v. 18).

### The Answer: Multiple "Little Whiles"

Jesus is referring to events about to unfold. Within hours, he will be arrested and face a false trial. By the end of the next day, he will be killed. In that first "little while," they won't see him.

But then, in another "little while," they will see him again—resurrected from the dead:

> A week later his disciples were in the house again, and Thomas was with them. Though the doors were locked, Jesus came and stood among them and said, "Peace be with you!" Then he said to Thomas, "Put your finger here; see my hands. Reach out your hand and put it into my side. Stop doubting and believe." (20:26-27)

However, verse 17 hints at something deeper: "because I am going to the Father." Jesus is not simply warning them about the weekend's events. He is preparing them for his ascension. Throughout chapters 14-17, he has been speaking about his substitute—the Holy Spirit—who will indwell them after his departure, not just between Good Friday and Easter.

> I came from the Father and entered the world; now I am leaving the world and going back to the Father. (16:28)

After his ascension, there is another "little while"—the entire period between his return to heaven and his second coming:

> He who is coming will come and will not delay. (Hebrews 10:37)

We live in that time now, waiting for his return. It doesn't feel like "a little while" to us, but in God's perspective:

> With the Lord a day is like a thousand years, and a thousand years are like a day. The Lord is not slow in keeping his promise, as some understand slowness. Instead he is patient with you, not wanting anyone to perish, but everyone to come to repentance. (2 Peter 3:8-9)

When Jesus appears, we will see him:

> Blessed are those who have not seen and yet have believed. (20:29)

We are called to faith now. When we see him at his return, we will rejoice.

## 2. "You Will Weep, You Will Rejoice" (20-22)

> Very truly I tell you, you will weep and mourn while the world rejoices. You will grieve, but your grief will turn to joy. (16:20)

The disciples will grieve at his death—as Mary Magdalene did:

> Now Mary stood outside the tomb crying. As she wept, she bent over to look into the tomb and saw two angels in white, seated where Jesus' body had been, one at the head and the other at the foot. They asked her, "Woman, why are you crying?" "They have taken my Lord away," she said. (20:11-13)

But they will rejoice later:

> On the evening of that first day of the week, when the disciples were together, with the doors locked for fear of the Jewish leaders, Jesus came and stood among them and said, "Peace be with you!" After he said this, he showed them his hands and side. The disciples were overjoyed when they saw the Lord. (20:19-20)

Jesus illustrates this pattern with childbirth:

> A woman giving birth to a child has pain because her time has come; but when her baby is born she forgets the anguish because of her joy that a child is born into the world. So with you: Now is your time of grief, but I will see you again and you will rejoice, and no one will take away your joy. (16:21-22)

The pain of labor is intense, but the joy that follows erases the memory of anguish. The joy is worth the pain. That pain produces something of lasting value.

### Application Today

We experience something similar in our own time. We grieve trials and sorrows, yet we are called to rejoice even now:

> In his great mercy he has given us new birth into a living hope through the resurrection of Jesus Christ from the dead. In all this you greatly rejoice, though now for a little while you may have had to suffer grief in all kinds of trials. (1 Peter 1:3, 6)

Notice verse 22: "no one will take away your joy." This joy is not dependent on external circumstances. It is rooted in our resurrection hope through Christ.

## 3. "You Won't Ask, You Will Ask" (23-26)

> In that day you will no longer ask me anything. (16:23)
> In that day you will ask in my name. (16:26)

The apparent contradiction resolves when we notice the different Greek words for "ask":
- **erotao** (v. 23): To ask a question, to inquire
- **aiteo** (v. 26): To request, to petition

The disciples have been asking Jesus questions directly:

> The Samaritan woman asks him for living water. (John 4)
> Martha asks him to resurrect Lazarus. (John 11:22)

After Jesus' ascension, they cannot ask him in the same way. So he redirects them:

> Very truly I tell you, my Father will give you whatever you ask in my name. Until now you have not asked for anything (from the Father) in my name. Ask and you will receive, and your joy will be complete. (16:23-24)

### Direct Access to the Father

Jesus clarifies that he is not becoming a mere intermediary or relay:

> I am not saying that I will ask the Father on your behalf. (16:26)

This does not diminish his high priestly role. Rather, he is saying: I have opened a relationship between you and the Father through my death. You now have direct access to God the Father in your own right.

> The Father himself loves you because you have loved me and have believed that I came from God. (16:27)

### The Meaning of "In Jesus' Name"

To pray "in Jesus' name" is to claim his authority and access:

> Whatever you ask in my name, so that the Father may be glorified in the Son. You may ask me for anything in my name, and I will do it. (14:13-14)
> You did not choose me, but I chose you and appointed you to go and bear fruit—fruit that will last. Then the Father will give you whatever you ask in my name. (15:16)

It is like having someone's access code to a restricted building or their Netflix password. You don't need them physically present—their name and authority grant you entry.

Having Jesus' name means full access to God. Therefore, we pray directly to God the Father in Jesus' name. We need not pray to Jesus as an intermediary.

### A Pastoral Note on Prayer

I addressed earlier that there is no biblical evidence we should pray to the Holy Spirit. Similarly, this passage suggests we should not pray to Jesus either.

Some may object on the basis of Stephen's prayer:

> While they were stoning him, Stephen prayed, "Lord Jesus, receive my spirit." Then he fell on his knees and cried out, "Lord, do not hold this sin against them." (Acts 7:59-60)

But Stephen's prayer occurs in a unique context. He is full of the Holy Spirit and sees Jesus standing at the right hand of God (v. 55). His prayer is a death prayer to the one he sees in heaven—an exceptional circumstance.

There are formulas in Scripture that approach Jesus—like the Aramaic *Maranatha* ("Come, Lord"):

> Come, Lord Jesus! (Revelation 22:20)

But these are more properly understood as direct apostolic calls rather than prayers in the instructional sense.

As we mature in faith, we should pray as Jesus taught us. Equality is not sameness. God defines equality, not our culture. The Father has instructed us to pray to him in Jesus' name, by the Spirit. That is the biblical pattern:

> This, then, is how you should pray: Our Father in heaven, hallowed be your name. (Matthew 6:9)

## 4. "I Speak Figuratively, I Won't Speak Figuratively" (25-33)

> Though I have been speaking figuratively, a time is coming when I will no longer use this kind of language but will tell you plainly about my Father. (16:25)

Yet just four verses later:

> Now you are speaking clearly and without figures of speech. (16:29)

### The Comfort of Running Out of Time

The comfort comes from how quickly Jesus moves from speaking figuratively to speaking freely. Events are accelerating. In just one more chapter, soldiers will enter Gethsemane and arrest him. Jesus senses his time is up. He is now free to speak plainly. His hour has come.

> A time is coming and in fact has come when you will be scattered, each to your own home. You will leave me all alone. Yet I am not alone, for my Father is with me. I have told you these things, so that in me you may have peace. In this world you will have trouble. But take heart! I have overcome the world. (16:32-33)

It is like an exam nearing its end. Throughout the test, you pace yourself carefully, unsure how long different sections will take. But as the final minutes approach, everything becomes clear. You know exactly how much time remains. The uncertainty lifts.

## The Hour Has Come

Throughout this passage, Jesus emphasizes *time*—"a little while," "in that day," "*the hour* is coming." When stress comes, our careful plans fall apart. But Jesus doesn't panic. Events are unfolding exactly as predicted:

- You won't see me, but the hour is catching up—you will see me
- You will weep, but the hour is catching up—you will rejoice
- You won't ask me, but through my death, the hour arrives—you will ask the Father directly
- I speak figuratively, but the hour is here—I won't speak figuratively anymore

The time is no longer distant. It's here. 

Jesus has told us these things so that we may have peace, even in a world of trouble. He has already overcome the world.
