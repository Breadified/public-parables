# Genesis 25:19-34

**Title:** The Promise Unfolds
**Date:** 9 March 2008
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## The Account of Isaac's Family

> This is the account of Abraham's son Isaac. Abraham became the father of Isaac, and Isaac was forty years old when he married Rebekah daughter of Bethuel the Aramean from Paddan Aram and sister of Laban the Aramean.

Isaac enters the narrative as the promised son, now stepping into his own story of faith. At forty, he marries Rebekah, connecting the family line through the Aramean region—the same place where Abraham had sent his servant to find a bride.

## Prayer and Barrenness

> Isaac prayed to the LORD on behalf of his wife, because she was barren. The LORD answered his prayer, and his wife Rebekah became pregnant.

Like Abraham before him, Isaac faces the tension between God's promise and present circumstances. Rebekah is barren, yet Isaac prays. His prayer demonstrates faith—not that he demands God fulfill His promise, but that he believes God will act according to His character. The Lord answers, and Rebekah conceives.

## The Struggle Within

> The babies jostled each other within her, and she said, "Why is this happening to me?" So she went to inquire of the LORD.

The pregnancy is not smooth or peaceful. The twins struggle violently within Rebekah's womb, and she is troubled—perplexed by what she experiences. In her distress, she seeks God's counsel. This is significant: when confused, she goes to the Lord.

## God's Word Over the Womb

> The LORD said to her, "Two nations are in your womb, and two peoples from within you will be separated; one people will be stronger than the other, and the older will serve the younger."

God speaks directly to Rebekah, revealing the cosmic significance of what is happening within her. This is not merely the birth of two sons—it foreshadows two nations, two peoples, two histories. The reversal of the natural order ("the older will serve the younger") announces God's sovereign choice, which will operate according to His purposes, not human expectation or law.

## The Birth of the Twins

> When the time came for her to give birth, there were twin boys in her womb. The first to come out was red, and his whole body was like a hairy garment; so they named him Esau. After this, his brother came out, with his hand grasping Esau's heel; so he was named Jacob. Isaac was sixty years old when Rebekah gave birth to them.

The births are marked by physical details that carry meaning. Esau emerges red and hairy—wild, earthy, robust. Jacob follows, grasping his brother's heel in what seems almost a prophetic gesture—suggesting struggle, pursuit, even the wrestling that will characterize their relationship. The names themselves tell their stories: Esau (meaning "hairy" or "rough"), Jacob (meaning "he grasps the heel" or "supplanter").

## Different Sons, Different Paths

> The boys grew up, and Esau became a skillful hunter, a man of the open country, while Jacob was a quiet man, staying among the tents.

As they mature, their personalities diverge sharply. Esau is the man of action, outdoors, mastering the hunt. Jacob is contemplative, dwelling in the tents where the family's domestic and spiritual life unfolds. Neither temperament is inherently wrong—God uses all kinds of people. But their different natures set them on different trajectories.

## Parental Preference

> Isaac, who had a taste for wild game, loved Esau, but Rebekah loved Jacob.

The division deepens through parental favoritism. Isaac loves Esau because Esau brings him the wild game he enjoys—a bond formed through shared appetites and activity. Rebekah loves Jacob. Perhaps she remembers God's word that the younger will rule over the older. Perhaps she sees in Jacob something of the spiritual sensitivity the family needs. Either way, the family is divided by preference, and this division will have profound consequences.

## The Birthright Bargain

> Once when Jacob was cooking some stew, Esau came in from the open country, famished. He said to Jacob, "Quick, let me have some of that red stew! I'm famished!"

Esau returns from hunting exhausted and ravenous. The physical hunger is real and overwhelming. Jacob is cooking stew—nourishment, comfort, sustenance. In that moment, Esau's craving for immediate relief is all-consuming.

> Jacob replied, "First sell me your birthright."

Jacob seizes the moment. He does not offer charity; he proposes a transaction. The birthright—the spiritual and material inheritance rights of the firstborn—for a meal. It is a calculated move, and Jacob makes his terms clear before offering anything.

> "Look, I am about to die," Esau said. "What good is the birthright to me?"

Esau's response is telling. He is so consumed by present hunger that the future holds no value. The birthright represents covenant blessing, spiritual authority, the promise God made to Abraham—yet Esau counts it as worthless compared to filling his belly. His words reveal a profound spiritual shallowness, an inability to see beyond the moment.

> But Jacob said, "Swear to me first." So he swore an oath to him, selling his birthright to Jacob. Then Jacob gave Esau some bread and some lentil stew. He ate and drank, and then got up and left. So Esau despised his birthright.

Jacob insists on a binding oath. Once Esau swears, Jacob provides the meal. Esau eats, drinks, and leaves—satisfied for now, but forever changed. The text's final judgment is stark: "Esau despised his birthright." He treated as contemptible what God had marked as precious.

---

## The Deeper Truth

This passage reveals the nature of spiritual inheritance. It is not obtained by the strong or the clever alone, but by those who value it. Esau had the advantage of being first—of strength, of his father's approval, of the hunter's honor. Yet he threw it away for momentary satisfaction.

Jacob, for all his scheming, at least recognized that the birthright mattered. His methods were wrong, his character still flawed, but his sense of its value was right. God's purposes will work through Jacob, not because Jacob is morally superior, but because Jacob desires the spiritual blessing above all else.

The question for us: What do we despise through our choices? What spiritual inheritance do we trade away for the immediate gratification of the moment?
