# James 1:1-5:20

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## James 1:1-18: Acting in Adversity

### Greeting and the Purpose of Trials (1:1-4)

James opens his letter to scattered believers with an unexpected instruction: consider your trials a source of pure joy. This isn't naïve optimism. James grounds this perspective in spiritual reality—trials test our faith, and this testing develops perseverance, the quality that allows us to endure and mature.

The word "testing" (Greek: *dokimion*) refers to the process of being proven genuine, like metal refined in fire. When our faith is tested, perseverance is not a burden imposed on us but rather the fruit that emerges from standing firm. This perseverance must be allowed to do its complete work, bringing us to maturity and wholeness—lacking nothing that matters spiritually.

### Wisdom in Trials (1:5-8)

When trials expose our lack of wisdom about how to respond, we have access to God's generous provision. God gives without fault-finding or reluctance. Yet receiving requires faith. Doubt—the unstable wavering between belief and skepticism—makes us like a wave tossed by wind, unable to receive anything from the Lord because we are double-minded, divided within ourselves.

The contrast is clear: the person who believes God gives receives; the person who doubts receives nothing, not because God withholds, but because their divided heart cannot receive what is offered.

### Perspective on Status (1:9-11)

True status in God's eyes inverts the world's measure. The brother in humble circumstances should take pride in his high position—his position in Christ, where the poor are often richest in faith. Conversely, the rich should recognize their "low position"—the temporality of wealth and earthly advantage.

James uses vivid imagery: like a wildflower that withers in the scorching heat, the rich fade away even while conducting their business, their beauty destroyed. Wealth is fleeting; spiritual standing endures.

### The Crown Promised to the Persevering (1:12)

Blessed is the person who endures trial. This blessing isn't mere congratulation—it's the promise of the crown of life, which God has promised to those who love Him. This ties trials to love: perseverance under pressure is the measure and proof of our love for God.

### The Source of Evil—Not God (1:13-15)

A critical clarification: God is never the source of temptation to evil. God cannot be tempted by evil, nor does He tempt anyone toward sin. Instead, we are tempted when our own desires entice and drag us away. The progression is stark: desire conceives and gives birth to sin; sin, when full-grown, gives birth to death.

God's character is consistent and pure. Do not be deceived into blaming Him for evil.

### God's Gifts Are Good (1:16-18)

Every good and perfect gift comes from above, from the Father of heavenly lights. Unlike shifting shadows that change their position, God does not change. He chose to give us birth through the word of truth, making us firstfruits of His creation—the first and best of what He is accomplishing in the world.

## James 1:19-27: Just Do It

### Listening, Speaking, and Anger (1:19-21)

James calls believers to be quick to listen and slow to speak. Our natural inclination runs opposite: we rush to speak and slow to listen. Anger, moreover, does not produce the righteous life God desires. When we speak in anger without listening, we work against God's purposes.

We must rid ourselves of all moral filth and evil, then humbly accept the word planted in us—the gospel that can save us. The word is already there, planted in our hearts; our task is to receive it with humility rather than defend ourselves with hasty words.

### Hearing Versus Doing (1:22-25)

Listening to the word without doing it deceives us. James uses a striking image: a person who looks at himself in a mirror, sees his face clearly, then immediately forgets what he looks like. Similarly, someone who hears God's word but doesn't act on it has seen the truth but forgotten it.

Blessing comes to the person who looks intently into the perfect law that gives freedom—not out of obligation, but to understand and live it. This person doesn't forget what he's heard but does it. The focus is on action rooted in remembrance.

### True Religion (1:26-27)

If someone considers himself religious yet cannot keep a tight rein on his tongue, his religion is worthless and he deceives himself. All the external trappings of faith mean nothing if the tongue—that small member—remains untamed.

True religion that God accepts as pure and faultless consists of two things: caring for orphans and widows in their distress, and keeping oneself from being polluted by the world. Religion is not doctrine alone; it is the lived expression of God's compassion and purity.

## James 2:1-26: Put Some Life in Your Faith

### Favoritism Violates the Law of Love (2:1-9)

Believers in the glorious Lord Jesus Christ must not show favoritism. James illustrates the problem: if a rich man in fine clothes is given the best seat while a poor man is told to stand or sit on the floor, the church has become judges with evil thoughts, discriminating among themselves.

God has chosen the poor in the world's eyes to be rich in faith and to inherit the kingdom. When the church rejects the poor, it insults them while often fawning over the rich—the very ones exploiting them, dragging them into court, and slandering the name of Christ to which they belong.

The royal law of Scripture is clear: "Love your neighbor as yourself." Showing favoritism is sin; it violates the law and convicts us as lawbreakers.

### The Law's Unity (2:10-13)

Breaking one point of the law makes you guilty of breaking it all. This doesn't mean all sins are equal in consequence, but it means the law is unified under the principle of love. He who said, "Do not commit adultery," also said, "Do not murder." You cannot obey selectively.

Judgment without mercy awaits the unmerciful. Mercy triumphs over judgment—a principle that should guide how we treat others and how we expect to be treated by God.

### Faith Without Deeds Is Dead (2:14-20)

What good is faith if it produces no action? If a brother or sister lacks food and clothing, and someone says, "Go, be warm and fed," but does nothing, the words are empty. In the same way, faith by itself, without action, is dead.

Someone might say, "You have faith; I have deeds." James responds: show me your faith without deeds, and he will show his faith by his deeds. Intellectual belief alone—even demons believe there is one God and shudder—is not enough.

### Abraham and Rahab: Faith Made Complete by Action (2:21-26)

Abraham was considered righteous for what he did when he offered his son Isaac. His faith and his actions worked together; his faith was made complete by what he did. The Scripture was fulfilled: "Abraham believed God, and it was credited to him as righteousness," and he was called God's friend.

Even Rahab the prostitute was considered righteous for what she did when she gave lodging to the spies and sent them off safely. Her faith moved her to action, and that action justified her.

The conclusion is absolute: as the body without the spirit is dead, so faith without deeds is dead.

## James 3:1-12: The Uncontrollable Tongue

### The Severity of Teaching (3:1-2)

Not many should become teachers, because teachers will be judged more strictly. Why? Because the tongue is the instrument through which teaching flows. We all stumble in many ways, but the person who never stumbles in speech is perfect, able to keep his whole body in check.

### The Tongue's Power (3:3-6)

Though small, the tongue wields enormous power. A bit placed in a horse's mouth turns the entire animal. A small rudder steers a massive ship wherever the pilot wishes. The tongue, similarly small, makes great boasts.

Consider how a small spark sets a great forest on fire. The tongue is a fire—a world of evil within the body. It corrupts the whole person, sets the whole course of his life ablaze, and is itself set on fire by hell. The tongue's destructive potential is matched only by its intimacy with our hearts.

### The Paradox of the Tamed and the Untamed (3:7-8)

Animals of every kind—birds, reptiles, sea creatures—have been tamed by humans. Yet no one can tame the tongue. It is restless evil, full of deadly poison. This is not mere exaggeration; it reflects the spiritual reality that the tongue, connected to our deepest desires and pride, resists our control.

### The Contradiction of the Double Tongue (3:9-12)

With the tongue we praise our Lord and Father, then curse men made in His image. This should not be. Fresh water and salt water do not flow from the same spring. A fig tree cannot bear olives; a grapevine cannot bear figs. Neither can a salt spring produce fresh water.

The point is clear: our words should reflect a unified heart turned toward God and others.

## James 3:13-4:12: The Fight

### Heavenly Wisdom Versus Earthly Wisdom (3:13-18)

Who is wise and understanding? Let him show it through a good life and humble deeds. But if someone harbors bitter envy and selfish ambition in his heart, he should not boast or deny the truth. Such "wisdom" does not come from heaven; it is earthly, unspiritual, even demonic. Where envy and selfish ambition exist, there disorder and every evil practice follow.

The wisdom from heaven, by contrast, is first pure, then peace-loving, considerate, submissive, full of mercy and good fruit, impartial, and sincere. Those who sow in peace reap a harvest of righteousness.

### The Source of Conflict (4:1-3)

What causes fights and quarrels? They arise from desires battling within us. We want something but don't get it. We covet, we fight, yet remain empty-handed. The problem is that we don't ask God. When we do ask, we don't receive because we ask with wrong motives—to spend what we get on our pleasures rather than God's purposes.

### Friendship with the World (4:4-6)

You adulterous people—unfaithful to your covenant with God—do you not know that friendship with the world is hatred toward God? Choosing to be a friend of the world makes you an enemy of God. The Spirit He placed in us longs intensely for our loyalty.

Yet God gives more grace. Therefore Scripture says: "God opposes the proud but gives grace to the humble."

### The Path to Restoration (4:7-10)

Submit yourselves to God. Resist the devil, and he will flee. Come near to God, and He will come near to you. Wash your hands—cleanse your actions. Purify your hearts—cleanse your desires and loyalties. Do not be double-minded.

Grieve and mourn. Change your laughter to mourning, your joy to gloom. This is not despair but repentance—genuine sorrow over having sought the world's friendship instead of God's. Humble yourselves before the Lord, and He will lift you up.

### Do Not Judge One Another (4:11-12)

Do not slander or judge your brother. When you speak against him or judge him, you speak against the law and judge it. But you are not the lawgiver; you are not the one who can save or destroy. God alone is Judge. Who are you to judge your neighbor?

## James 4:13-5:11: Wealth and Poverty

### The Brevity of Life and the Problem of Pride (4:13-17)

Some say, "Today or tomorrow we'll go to this city, spend a year there, do business, and make money." But you don't know what tomorrow will bring. What is your life? You are a mist that appears briefly then vanishes.

Instead, you ought to say, "If it is the Lord's will, we will live and do this or that." As it is, you boast and brag. All such boasting is evil. Anyone who knows the good he ought to do and doesn't do it—that is sin.

### A Warning to the Rich (5:1-6)

Listen, you rich people: weep and wail because of the misery coming upon you. Your wealth has rotted; moths have eaten your clothes. Your gold and silver are corroded, and their corrosion will testify against you, eating your flesh like fire.

You have hoarded wealth in the last days. The wages you failed to pay your workers cry out against you. The cries of the harvesters have reached the Lord Almighty. You have lived in luxury and self-indulgence, fattening yourselves for the day of slaughter. You have condemned and murdered innocent men who were not opposing you.

### Patience Until the Lord's Coming (5:7-11)

Be patient, brothers, until the Lord's coming. See how the farmer waits for his land to yield its crop, how patient he is for autumn and spring rains. You too, be patient and stand firm; the Lord's coming is near. Don't grumble against each other, or you will be judged. The Judge is standing at the door.

As examples of patience in suffering, consider the prophets who spoke in the Lord's name. We call blessed those who have persevered. You've heard of Job's perseverance and seen what the Lord finally brought about. The Lord is full of compassion and mercy.

## James 5:12-20: How Powerful Is Prayer

### Integrity in Speech (5:12)

Above all, do not swear—not by heaven, by earth, or by anything else. Let your "yes" be yes and your "no" be no, or you will be condemned. Integrity demands that our words be trustworthy without need for oaths to reinforce them.

### Prayer in Every Circumstance (5:13-15)

Is anyone in trouble? Let him pray. Is anyone happy? Let him sing songs of praise. Is anyone sick? He should call the church elders to pray over him and anoint him with oil in the Lord's name.

The prayer offered in faith will make the sick person well; the Lord will raise him up. If he has sinned, he will be forgiven. The connection is intimate: physical healing and spiritual forgiveness flow from the prayer of faith.

### Confession and Prayer for Healing (5:16)

Therefore, confess your sins to each other and pray for each other so that you may be healed. The prayer of a righteous person is powerful and effective. Confession breaks isolation and pride; prayer in community channels God's power toward healing.

### Elijah as Example of Prayer's Power (5:17-18)

Elijah was a man just like us. He prayed earnestly that it would not rain, and it did not rain for three and a half years. He prayed again, and the heavens gave rain, and the earth produced its crops.

An ordinary man's earnest prayer altered the course of nature. This is the power available to those who pray in faith.

### Bringing Back the Wandering (5:19-20)

If one of you wanders from the truth and someone brings him back, remember: whoever turns a sinner from his error saves him from death and covers a multitude of sins. This is the culmination of James—the community of faith, bound together by faith that works through deeds, prayers that are answered, and the grace to restore one another.
