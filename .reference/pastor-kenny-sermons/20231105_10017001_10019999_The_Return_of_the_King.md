## Part 1: Absalom Not Decisive (Chapter 17)

### The Story So Far

David has discovered the coup d'état and escaped. Civil war has broken out as the consequences of David's sin reach national proportions. He made decisions based on appearances—his handsome, beautiful son now rises up against him, judged by the same standard his father used. Absalom has taken over the throne.

### Ahithophel's Counsel: A Quick Surgical Strike

When Absalom seeks advice, his counselor Ahithophel—Bathsheba's grandfather—offers a decisive plan:

> "I would choose twelve thousand men and set out tonight in pursuit of David. I would attack him while he is weary and weak. I would strike him with terror, and then all the people with him will flee. I would strike down only the king and bring all the people back to you. The death of the man you seek will mean the return of all; all the people will be unharmed."

This plan is immediate ("tonight"), overwhelming (concentrated force), precise (target the king only), and politically clean (the people remain unharmed). Everyone—Absalom and the elders of Israel—thinks it's brilliant.

There is no love lost between Ahithophel and David. After what David did to his grandson Uriah, he has every reason for revenge.

The same tactic would later be used against Jesus. As Matthew records: "This very night you will all fall away on account of me... I will strike the shepherd, and the sheep of the flock will be scattered."

### Hushai's Counter-Counsel: A Better Appearance

But Absalom asks for a second opinion and summons Hushai the Arkite. Hushai, loyal to David and sent back by the king with a mission to undermine Ahithophel's counsel, presents a competing argument:

> "The advice Ahithophel has given is not good this time... You know your father and his men; they are fighters, and as fierce as a wild bear robbed of her cubs."

Hushai plays to Absalom's weakness—his obsession with appearance and image. He appeals to his ego, his desire for glory:

- David and his men are terrifyingly fierce—like a mother bear. There's nothing more dangerous than a mother bear. You don't run from it; you play dead and hope it leaves you alone.
- David is already in hiding and isolated. He's no immediate threat. You have time.
- If you rush and he strikes first, even a small victory for David would be catastrophic for morale. The rumors alone would terrify your troops.
- Instead, consolidate. Mobilize the entire nation. Rally everyone from Dan to Beersheba—as numerous as the sand on the seashore. You yourself lead them. Everyone will support you. You'll be the hero of Israel.

Hushai's argument depends entirely on appearance. He makes David seem like a lion and a bear when, in reality, David in verse 29 is weary, hungry, and barefooted. But Hushai appeals to Absalom's vanity and his desire to be seen as a great leader—"You lead." "Dan to Beersheba." "As numerous as the sand." "Drag the whole city."

Absalom forgets he's not fighting Goliath. He's trying to kill his own father.

### God's Hand in the Matter

> "Absalom and all the men of Israel said, 'The advice of Hushai the Arkite is better than that of Ahithophel.' For the Lord had determined to frustrate the good advice of Ahithophel in order to bring disaster on Absalom."

The delay Hushai creates allows David to escape across the Jordan into Gilead, to the city of Mahanaim, where he regroups and prepares his counter-offensive.

### The Fate of Ahithophel

When Ahithophel sees that his wise advice has been rejected:

> "He put his house in order and then hanged himself. So he died and was buried in his father's tomb."

We can only speculate why. Perhaps he was too wise for his own good—he realized he'd joined the wrong side and that David would pursue him anyway. Perhaps it was an ego thing; everyone came to him for counsel, and rejection was unbearable. Either way, one threat to David is eliminated.

### Application: Beyond Appearance

**The danger of judging by appearances.** Absalom built his coup on surface charm. He stood by the gate, kissed people, promised justice. He stole hearts through carefully crafted image—the chariot and horses, the fifty men running ahead, the flattery and touch. Everything based on how it looked.

**We do the same.** We worry too much about how things appear and miss crucial moments. We hesitate to tell people we're Christians when we meet them—worried about how it will look—and then the moment passes. It becomes awkward to say it later.

**We compare and envy.** Ecclesiastes speaks to this:

> "I saw that all toil and all achievement spring from one person's envy of another. This too is meaningless, a chasing after the wind."

When we achieve only to compete with others, when we toil only to appear successful, we end up with Ecclesiastes' conclusion: meaningless. Just vanity. We get depressed and paralyzed. Ahithophel ended his own life.

**But there's a better reason to work hard.** Not for yourself. Not to outdo others. But for the sake of others who depend on you. Your family needs you. Your church needs you. Your neighbors need you. There is a world of need out there.

Rather than envy and competition, realize we are in a team. We all have our parts. We have our own race—there's no point comparing. You don't get better by envying someone else. You get better when someone else gets better.

When you feel depressed, ask yourself: Am I thinking "What's in it for me?" Or am I thinking "If I do this, I can help these many people?"

## Part 2: Joab Decisive (Chapter 18)

### The Counter-Offensive Begins

David musters his forces and appoints commanders over them. He sends out his troops in three divisions—under Joab, Joab's brother Abishai, and Ittai the Gittite. The king says, "I myself will surely march out with you."

But his men refuse him:

> "You must not go out; if we are forced to flee, they won't care about us. Even if half of us die, they won't care; but you are worth ten thousand of us. It would be better now for you to give us support from the city."

David agrees: "I will do whatever seems best to you."

### David's Weakness: A Conflict of Interest

This is a strange command from a king, and it reveals David's fundamental weakness. He should be leading from the front—that's what a warrior-king does. But David cannot. He has a fatal conflict of interest.

Look at verse 5: The king commands his generals, "Be gentle with the young man Absalom for my sake."

David is a father first, a king second. He wants to win the war but also protect his son. An army cannot function with two contradictory orders:
1. Defeat the enemy
2. Be gentle with the enemy

An army is a blunt instrument, not surgical. It rolls in with overwhelming force. Civilian casualties are inevitable. You cannot fight a war and protect one person at the same time.

The soldiers remember this command. When Absalom is found hanging in the oak tree, caught by his famous hair—the very pride and joy that made him beautiful—the soldier refuses to strike him:

> "Even if a thousand shekels were weighed out into my hands, I would not lay a hand on the king's son. In our hearing the king commanded you and Abishai and Ittai, 'Protect the young man Absalom for my sake.'"

### Joab's Decisive Action

But Joab is different. He's a man of action, not sentiment. He sees the reality: Absalom is still a threat. Absalom is charming, yes—but he's already decided to kill David. You cannot keep an enemy in your house and think that embarrassment will lessen his hatred.

Joab doesn't wait. He takes three javelins and plunges them into Absalom's heart while he hangs helpless. His armor-bearers finish the task.

Ahithophel's advice, we now see, was wise. The swift, decisive strike works. David wins the battle—twenty thousand casualties on Absalom's side. The forest itself swallows up more men than the sword.

### Application: When Righteousness Demands Action

**We waste too much time thinking about appearances.** We think about how something will look instead of whether it's right.

**A perfect opportunity comes before us.** We know what's right. But we second-guess ourselves. Doubts creep in. "How will it look?"

*Telling the gospel.* You see a perfect opening. Someone's hurting, asking real questions about meaning. You know you should speak. But you hesitate—worried about how it will look. How weird it might be. Then the moment is gone. It never comes back.

*Having children.* You're married. You're fertile. You know it's important. It's your desire. But you wait a little longer. Just a bit more money. One more holiday. Maybe next year. And then the opportunity passes by—biologically or otherwise.

*Dealing with sin.* You know you need to stop. You're convinced it's wrong. But you like to play around with it first. You domesticate sin, thinking you have it under control. "Sin doesn't mean it when it says it wants to kill me. I've got it managed. I'm more powerful."

But sin cannot be reformed. Sin cannot be a prisoner that becomes good. Sin has one mission in life—to kill you. 

> "Be killing sin or it be killing you."

## The Question of Evil

This sermon series has been a hard slog through the consequences of David's sin. The initial question stands: Do we believe evil is evil?

This week, Israel responded to the October 7 massacres by eliminating Hamas leadership. In World War 2, there was an attempted assassination of Hitler. In 1939, British Military Attache Noel Mason-Macfarlane had a perfect shot—a room overlooking Hitler's podium during his 50th birthday parade. Hitler had already opened concentration camps. He had already begun his war path and war crimes.

But Mason-Macfarlane's superiors rejected the plan. Lord Halifax argued, "We have not reached that stage when we have to use assassination as a substitute for diplomacy." The British thought war was like a game, unsuitable to assassinate.

Hitler was freed. He went on to start a war that killed 75 million people—20 million combatants, 6 million Jews.

One Hamas leader was arrested by Israel in 2005 for kidnapping and murder, but released in 2011 as part of a prisoner exchange. Evil came back.

The lesson is clear: **Don't we make the same mistake.** When you see evil, you deal with it decisively. Not out of vengeance, not out of hatred, but out of the clarity that evil is evil and must be stopped.
