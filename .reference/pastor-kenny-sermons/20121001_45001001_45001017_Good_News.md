## The Problem With Bad News

We live in a world saturated with negative news. The media operates by a simple principle: "If it bleeds, it leads." Research shows that each negative word in a news headline increases click-through rates by more than 2 percent, while positive words actually decrease engagement. Day after day, we're bombarded by stories of war, terrorism, crime, pollution, and oppression across every platform—online, in print, on television, and radio.

This tendency isn't new. News has always been designed to alert us to dangers: approaching wars, diseases, severe weather. But in our modern media landscape, where newspapers must compete for clicks and viewership to survive, the sensationalism only intensifies. We'd do well to consume news with discernment, seeking multiple sources and questioning not just the facts presented but the conclusions and opinions drawn from them.

Yet there is an antidote to this relentless tide of bad news. It is to hear—and share—the **good news**.

## 1. The Gospel: Jesus Christ Is Lord

> Paul, a servant of Christ Jesus, called to be an apostle and set apart for the gospel of God—

The word "gospel" simply means "good news"—from the old English "good spell," meaning good words or good message. But what makes this news so good?

Paul gives us the answer in four transformative words:

**"Jesus Christ is Lord."**

### Jesus Christ: The Promised King

> regarding his Son, who as to his earthly life was a descendant of David,

Jesus is presented to us in two dimensions:

**Physically**, he is a descendant of David—the rightful heir to Israel's throne. David reigned about 1,000 years before Jesus, and God made an eternal covenant with him:

> "The Lord himself will establish a house for you... I will raise up your offspring to succeed you... He is the one who will build a house for my Name, and I will establish the throne of his kingdom forever."

This promise meant the Messiah would come from David's line. Both Joseph and Mary traced their descent back to David, fulfilling this ancient prophecy.

**Spiritually**, Jesus is far more than a political heir:

> through the Spirit of holiness was appointed the Son of God in power by his resurrection from the dead: Jesus Christ our Lord.

Unlike the earthly kings who came before him—David and all the others—Jesus would not merely rule and then die. He was raised from the dead, demonstrating His authority over death itself. As Peter proclaimed at Pentecost:

> "Fellow Israelites, I can tell you confidently that the patriarch David died and was buried... But he was a prophet and knew that God had promised him on oath that he would place one of his descendants on his throne... God has raised this Jesus to life, and we are all witnesses of it."

### Why This Is Good News

Real power for lasting change doesn't come from being merely good or great—it comes from being good and great **forever**. Dead prophets and dead gods cannot help us. Only a living Savior can.

Jesus is the Christ—the anointed one from David's line. Jesus is Lord—the powerful king who reigns over the universe. He cannot be tired. He will not retire. He is not mortal. He is reliable, dependable, and eternal.

> For I am not ashamed of the gospel, because it is the power of God that brings salvation to everyone who believes.

"Salvation" means rescue. We are rescued from death, rescued from hell and judgment. Many treat hell as a joke—a Halloween costume, a punchline shared over drinks. But Scripture presents it as real: a place of complete darkness and isolation where there is no family, no friends, no God—only suffering and loneliness for eternity.

The more we understand the reality of what we are rescued *from*, the more we appreciate what Christ has done *for* us.

## 2. Set Apart to Proclaim: The Gospel's Purpose

> Paul, a servant of Christ Jesus, called to be an apostle and set apart for the gospel of God

An apostle is literally one who is "sent out." Paul was commissioned with a specific calling:

> Through him we received grace and apostleship to call all the Gentiles to the obedience that comes from faith for his name's sake.

At his conversion, the Lord told Paul:

> "Go! This man is my chosen instrument to proclaim my name to the Gentiles and their kings and to the people of Israel."

Paul's singular passion was to preach the gospel to Rome itself—the capital of the empire, the heart of the Gentile world. He longed to see them made strong through the gospel:

> First, I thank my God through Jesus Christ for all of you, because your faith is being reported all over the world. God, whom I serve in my spirit in preaching the gospel of his Son, is my witness how constantly I remember you... I long to see you so that I may impart to you some spiritual gift to make you strong.

### Not Just Paul—But All of Us

But Paul's calling extends to us as well. We are also set apart for the work of the gospel. Not with the same apostolic authority Paul possessed, but with the same essential task: to proclaim and live out the gospel message.

> For we are God's handiwork, created in Christ Jesus to do good works, which God prepared in advance for us to do.

> Instead, speaking the truth in love, we will grow to become in every respect the mature body of him who is the head, that is, Christ.

> And let us consider how we may spur one another on toward love and good deeds... encouraging one another—and all the more as you see the Day approaching.

This is why Paul says:

> For I am not ashamed of the gospel... that is why I am so eager to preach the gospel also to you who are in Rome.

### Overcoming Our Shame

We are sometimes afraid to speak about Jesus and the things of God—even with each other. Many excuses hold us back:

- We doubt the gospel's power
- We fear it won't change anything
- We think only special people like Paul are called to evangelize
- We feel embarrassed to go "spiritual"
- We don't know how to turn conversations naturally toward Jesus
- We fear judgment—if we ask about someone's spiritual life, they'll ask about ours, and our lives aren't perfect
- We worry we sound judgmental or old-fashioned
- We're afraid of causing offense

But consider: What a tragedy if you leave a workplace where you've spent years and no one even knew you were a Christian. What a loss if those closest to you never hear about your faith.

God has set you apart. Whether you realize it or not, you are His instrument in your sphere—at work, in your family, in your neighborhood.

Do not be ashamed.

When we speak about Jesus to one another, when we share our struggles and growth, when we offer correction and teaching grounded in Scripture—these acts build us up. They make us strong. They develop in us the resilience and patience we need as we wait for Christ's return. This is the gift that keeps giving.

The best way to do this is to keep meeting together regularly, encouraging one another week after week. Speak openly about how God is working in your life—through your struggles, through your growth, in what you're grateful for.

## Reformation Note: Why Romans Changed Everything

Over 500 years ago, on Reformation Day (31 October 1517), a monk named Martin Luther nailed 95 theses to a church door in Wittenberg, Germany. His action sparked the Protestant Reformation—a movement that would reshape Christianity.

What awakened Luther? He had read the Book of Romans.

In medieval times, few people read their own Bibles. Only educated monks like Luther had access to Scripture. When Luther encountered Romans, he was transformed. In his commentary preface, he wrote:

> "This letter is truly the most important piece in the New Testament. It is purest Gospel."
>
> "We find in this letter the richest possible teaching about what a Christian should know: the meaning of law, Gospel, sin, punishment, grace, faith, justice, Christ, God, good works, love, hope and the cross."
>
> "It seems that St. Paul, in writing this letter, wanted to compose a summary of the whole of Christian and evangelical teaching which would also be an introduction to the whole Old Testament."
>
> "Therefore each and every Christian should make this letter the habitual and constant object of his study."

One book. One letter. One encounter with Paul's gospel of grace. It changed everything.
