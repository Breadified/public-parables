# John 17:1-26

**Title:** Glorify the Son  
**Date:** 10 April 2016  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Understanding Glory

When we hear the word "glory," we know it when we experience it—yet it's difficult to define. We recognize it in moments of celebration: standing on a podium with a medal, graduation, your wedding day, or your twenty-first birthday. But what exactly is glory?

The Greek word *doxa* carries the sense of magnificence, weightiness, honor, and reputation. It's the quality of being recognized and celebrated for your worth. Yet there's a paradox at the heart of Christ's glory: it's nothing like the worldly recognition we typically imagine.

Jesus opens John 17 with a prayer that strikes us as oddly presumptuous: "Father, glorify your Son." Our instinct is to recoil—isn't this arrogant? But there are two crucial ways to understand this request.

First, it's not arrogant to ask for deserved glory. Just as Paul legitimately glorified his apostolic ministry because he was worthy of it, Jesus asks for glory because He *is* the glory of God. From before time itself, His glory has been secure. To ask for recognition of what is true is not vanity.

But more importantly, Jesus isn't asking for the kind of glory we imagine. Look at the rest of verse 1: "that your Son may glorify you." Jesus' glory will involve glorifying the Father. And verse 4 reveals how: "I have brought you glory on earth by finishing the work you gave me to do."

This work is not about conquest or celebration. In John 12:27-28, when Jesus was troubled about His coming hour, He said, "Father, glorify your name"—and the Father replied, "I have glorified it and I will glorify it again." The glory Jesus seeks is **the glory of the cross**.

## The Paradox: God's Glory vs. Worldly Glory

The first half of John's gospel shows us signs and miracles—what we might call glorious demonstrations of power. The second half shows us something different: Jesus going away, suffering, dying. We naturally prefer the first half. But God's version of glory is radically different from ours.

God's glory is expressed in **service and faithful love**. It's seen in self-sacrifice for the good of others. Consider which is more glorious: a soldier walking away from battle unscathed, or a soldier dying to protect his wounded friend? The latter displays a strange and terrible glory—not the glory of violence for its own sake, but the glory of love that gives everything.

This is what Jesus came to do. Through His death, the fruits of His sacrifice would flow to all who believe. Verses 2-3 tell us: "For you granted him authority over all people that he might give eternal life to all those you have given him. Now this is eternal life: that they know you, the only true God, and Jesus Christ, whom you have sent."

Jesus is not a sadist or a suicide bomber. He is prepared to die for the sins of the world out of pure love. This is the glory of God—displayed supremely in the Lamb of God offered on a stake at Calvary. As one commentator wrote: "In the cross of Christ, as in a splendid theatre, the incomparable goodness of God is set before the whole world."

### Living in God's Glory

This understanding should reshape how we view service and sacrifice. It calls us to recognize the glory in humble, sacrificial work—in volunteering, in motherhood, in caring for others when the world offers no applause. True glory lies not in what we gain, but in what we give.

## The Apostles Glorify the Son (John 17:6-19)

Jesus will be glorified not only by the Father but also through His apostles—those twelve men He spent three years training.

> I pray for them. I am not praying for the world, but for those you have given me, for they are yours... And glory has come to me through them. (verses 9-10)

The apostles have proven themselves genuine disciples. They have obeyed God's word, accepted Jesus' teaching, and believed with certainty that He came from the Father. Through their future words and miracles, through the New Testament writings that bear their names, they will proclaim Jesus' death and resurrection to the world.

### Jesus Prays for Their Protection

But there's a problem: the gospel appears inglorious to a watching world. The apostles are "not of this world any more than I am of the world" (verse 14). Consequently, **the world hates them**.

The persecution of Christians is not a recent phenomenon. In the early centuries, apostles were tortured and killed. According to church tradition:

- **Peter** was crucified with his head downward
- **Andrew** was crucified at Patrae in Achaia
- **Matthew** died a martyr in Ethiopia
- **James son of Alpheus** was thrown down from the temple, stoned, and killed with a fuller's club
- **Jude** was martyred and buried in what is now Iran
- **James son of Zebedee** was beheaded by Herod

These founding apostles all died for their faith. This historical reality proves something profound: it's one thing for followers to die for a religion they've been taught. It's another entirely for the apostles—who *wrote* the New Testament, who *invented* the story—to die for something they knew was false. They would never have given their lives for a fabrication. Their martyrdom witnesses to the truth of Jesus' resurrection.

Yet even today, Christians face persecution. Global statistics are staggering: nearly 200 million Christians (10 percent of the world's Christian population) are socially disadvantaged, harassed, or actively oppressed. According to the Pew Research Center, Christianity is the world's most extensively persecuted faith. In Islamic countries, 95 percent of the more than 100,000 Christians martyred annually are killed.

- In **Pakistan**, 700 Christian girls are raped, abducted, and forced to convert to Islam every year
- In **Iraq**, the Christian population has fallen from 1.5 million to just a few thousand since 2003—genocide
- In the **Middle East**, Christians have been driven from lands they've called home for two millennia
- Even in the **West**, Christians face a gentler but persistent persecution: mockery in media and entertainment, legal threats to pastors and conscientious objectors, boycotts and social shaming for holding biblical convictions

### Sanctification Through God's Word

Jesus prays not for physical protection but for something deeper:

> Sanctify them by the truth; your word is truth. (verse 17)

This means keeping them holy, protecting them from falling away. The apostles are sustained not by force but by God's word—the very message Jesus gave them. By holding fast to Scripture, they remain united with Jesus and empowered to glorify Him despite persecution.

### Application for Today

If we cannot endure the "soft" persecution of social ridicule and online mockery, what will we do when facing actual pain—loss of livelihood, imprisonment, death? Will we bring glory to God?

This is not theoretical. We are called to prepare ourselves and our children for potential dangers ahead, assuring them that they are—and will be—sanctified by God's truth. Our commitment to Christ must run deeper than comfort.

## The World Will Glorify the Son (John 17:20-26)

Finally, Jesus prays for future believers—not just the apostles, but all who will believe through their message. That includes us.

> My prayer is not for them alone. I pray also for those who will believe in me through their message. (verse 20)

Jesus' prayer for us is unity:

> That all of them may be one, Father, just as you are in me and I am in you... I have given them the glory that you gave me, that they may be one as we are one. (verses 21-22)

### The Nature of Christian Unity

This is crucial: Christian unity is **not** about being in the same building on Sunday or belonging to the same denomination. It's not about finding the lowest common denominator of belief. True unity is rooted in **shared faith in God's word**.

Note what verse 21 emphasizes: "Father, just as you are in me and I am in you. May they also be in us so that the world may believe that you have sent me."

We are united with each other *because* we are united with the Father and the Son. This unity transcends any cultural, racial, or social diversity. I have more in common with a Christian from Africa than with an unbelieving relative. When Christians from different backgrounds gather united by God's word, it's like talking with old friends—there's an immediate recognition of common purpose and vision.

### The Power of Unity

> Then the world will know that you sent me and have loved them even as you have loved me. (verse 23)

The world will witness this unity and be drawn to Christ. This is not unity for unity's sake. It's not diversity celebrated in isolation. The power lies in different people finding **oneness around a shared purpose**.

Consider the Peanuts cartoon where Lucy tells Linus: "These five fingers, individually they're nothing. But when I curl them together into a single unit, they form a weapon that is terrible to behold." Strength comes from unity of purpose, not diversity alone.

Similarly, a soccer team drawn from different races and backgrounds wins not because of diversity but because of their **unity in pursuit of victory**. Multiculturalism is only strength when people of different origins unite around shared values and convictions. A diverse society that has no unifying principles is fractured and weak.

**Christians are unified by God's word.** We believe in Jesus through the apostles' testimony, receive the Spirit, and become one with the Father and Son. This unity is then visible to the world.

### Our Glory and Our Path

Just as Jesus' true glory was found in lowly service culminating in the cross, so our glory lies in the same path:

> The glory that you have given me I have given to them. (verse 22)

If we share Jesus' glory, we share His path. We are called to follow Him through suffering to glory. As one writer expressed it: "We must never think of our cross as our penalty; we must think of it as our glory... When it is hard to be a Christian, we must regard it as our honor given to us by God."

> If anyone serves me, he must follow me; and where I am, there will my servant be. If anyone serves me, the Father will honor him... Where I am going you cannot follow me now, but you will follow me afterwards. (John 12:26; 13:36)

### The Final Glory

On that last day, when all believers gather before Jesus, what will it mean to bear on our bodies "the marks of Jesus" (Galatians 6:17)? His pierced hands will be reached out to us. The Captain of our salvation will say: "Well done, good and faithful servant, true disciple and follower." That will be glory indeed.

## Conclusion

In His last hours of freedom before the cross, Jesus' final act was to pray for His disciples. He served them on His knees. And at Golgotha, on a little mound of earth, He stretched out His arms—not to gain applause, but to be crucified.

**This is the glory of God.**
