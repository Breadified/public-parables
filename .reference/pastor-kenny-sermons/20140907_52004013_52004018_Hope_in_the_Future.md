# 1 Thessalonians 4:13-18

**Title:** Hope in the Future

**Date:** September 7, 2014

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Introduction: What Happens to the Dead?

The Thessalonians faced a pressing question that the first-century church grappled with: what becomes of Christians who die before Christ returns? Paul addresses this concern directly, building on his previous letter where he spoke of being "blameless and holy in the presence of our God and Father when our Lord Jesus comes with all his holy ones" (3:13). The natural follow-up question was immediate and anxious: *Will the dead miss out?* What is the future for our departed loved ones?

Paul's answer centers on three profound truths that should reshape how we grieve and live.

---

## 1. Christ Will Come Down First

> For the Lord himself will come down from heaven, with a loud command, with the voice of the archangel and with the trumpet call of God. (1 Thessalonians 4:16)

The coming of Christ will be unmistakable and universal. This is not a secret event. The Bible throughout the Old Testament speaks of God's ultimate intervention in history—from Isaiah's prophecy of gathering the scattered Israelites with "a great trumpet" (Isaiah 27:12-13), to the warrior King described in Isaiah 42, to Zechariah's vision of the Lord appearing as a shepherd to save His flock.

### His Coming Will Be Certain

When Christ returns, the whole world will know. There will be no ambiguity, no debate about whether it happened. Just as an invasion is announced by helicopters, ground troops, and artillery—or the way everyone knows when a major figure arrives—so too will Christ's return be unmistakable. Every person on earth will be aware. Whether you're working, in Antarctica, or at the bottom of a mine, you will know.

This stands in stark contrast to some Christian traditions (such as Jehovah's Witnesses) that claim Christ returned secretly in 1914, invisible to all but a faithful few. No—His coming will be loud, public, and undeniable. The dead themselves will be awakened by it.

### His Coming Will Definitely Happen

How can we be confident in a future event we cannot yet see? Because Christ already came once. Two thousand years ago, though His first coming was quiet—announced only to shepherds, with angels singing and a star—His life and deeds became known to all. His resurrection is the foundation of our hope.

> But Christ has indeed been raised from the dead, the firstfruits of those who have fallen asleep. For since death came through a man, the resurrection of the dead comes also through a man. (1 Corinthians 15:20-21)

Because Christ rose, we can be absolutely certain that He will come again, and that death is not the final word for believers.

---

## 2. The Dead in Christ Will Rise

> And the dead in Christ will rise first. (1 Thessalonians 4:16b)

When Christ returns, all who have died will be raised. The Old Testament prophets gradually expanded their vision from speaking only about this life—deliverance from war, a better quality of life—to speaking apocalyptically about the heavens, the afterlife, and ultimate judgment.

### All the Dead Will Rise, But Not Equally

Daniel's vision captures this reality:

> Multitudes who sleep in the dust of the earth will awake: some to everlasting life, others to shame and everlasting contempt. Those who are wise will shine like the brightness of the heavens, and those who lead many to righteousness, like the stars for ever and ever. (Daniel 12:2-3)

All will rise, but the resurrection leads to two very different destinies: eternal life for those in the book of life, eternal shame and contempt for those who rejected God.

### Our Resurrection Is Guaranteed by Christ's Resurrection

Paul grounds the certainty of our resurrection not in speculation about the future, but in historical fact:

> For we believe that Jesus died and rose again, and so we believe that God will bring with Jesus those who have fallen asleep in him. According to the Lord's word, we tell you that we who are still alive, who are left until the coming of the Lord, will certainly not precede those who have fallen asleep. (1 Thessalonians 4:14-15)

For the Christian who dies, the fate of Christ becomes their fate. Just as Jesus rose to glory, so too will those united to Him. This is not wish-fulfillment or vague hope—it is hope grounded in the historical reality of the empty tomb.

### A Vivid Illustration

Think of tandem skydiving. Your safety depends entirely on being intertwined with an instructor—someone trustworthy and capable. Your faith in Christ works similarly. If you are united to Him, you are joined to someone good, someone who has already conquered death. But this only works if you are truly intertwined with Him, truly committed to Him.

The application is clear: you must choose which side you want to be intertwined with. The side with hope, or the side without hope—a nihilism that sees nothing beyond the grave. At funerals, people often speak of loved ones being in a "better place," but how do they know? What basis do they have beyond wishful thinking? Only those in Christ have genuine hope grounded in historical reality.

> Brothers and sisters, we do not want you to be uninformed about those who sleep in death, so that you do not grieve like the rest of mankind, who have no hope. (1 Thessalonians 4:13)

If you are not yet a Christian, if you are not confident about your ultimate destiny, please speak with someone about the hope that Christ offers.

## 3. The Living Will Be Caught Up With Christ

> After that, we who are still alive and are left will be caught up together with them in the clouds to meet the Lord in the air. And so we will be with the Lord forever. (1 Thessalonians 4:17)

### Understanding "Caught Up"

The word "caught up" has been misunderstood by some Christian traditions, particularly in popular culture, where the concept of a "Rapture" (a secret, pre-tribulation removal of believers) has captured the imagination. But Paul's own language earlier in this passage makes this view impossible: Christ's coming is announced with loud commands, angelic voices, and trumpets. There is nothing secret about it. Jesus doesn't arrive invisibly while the world works out its repentance.

### Two Better Understandings

**First**, this may be a picture of a triumphal reception, like when Jesus entered Jerusalem and people came out to welcome Him, laying cloaks and branches on the road. The living believers join the returning Christ in a welcoming procession.

**Second**, and not mutually exclusive, Paul may be describing believers' safe position during judgment. When Christ's judgment falls upon the earth, believers will not be in danger beneath it; instead, they will be with the Judge Himself, lifted up in the clouds. The "air" represents a safe distance from the cataclysmic judgment that will befall the world (Zechariah 14:5).

### Forever With the Lord

The ultimate promise is not escape, but union: "And so we will be with the Lord forever." This is the true resting place—genuine relationship with God and with each other. All of us together. The living and the dead, reunited in God's presence.

---

## Our Task: Encourage One Another

> Therefore encourage one another with these words. (1 Thessalonians 4:18)

We have been given this truth not merely for comfort in the abstract, but for a purpose. We are called to encourage one another constantly.

> Let us not give up meeting together, as some are in the habit of doing, but let us encourage one another—and all the more as you see the Day approaching. (Hebrews 10:25)

Many of us need to be reminded constantly that there is hope in the future. Let us spend our days doing this work of encouragement for one another.

---

## Grieving With Hope

When we visit cemeteries, we see the reality of death: the crematorium, the grass that will be cut by tractors, the ponds that grow old and corroded with algae. If this were all that awaits us, the picture would be unbearably bleak. But for those in Christ, this is not the end.

> Jesus died, Jesus was buried, Jesus was raised. Those who die in the Lord, we grieve with hope. If and when we die, we die in hope. And those we may one day leave behind will grieve with hope.

This is what distinguishes Christian hope from mere wishful thinking. We do not pretend death is not real or painful. We grieve. But we grieve as those who know the story doesn't end at the grave—it ends with resurrection, reunion, and eternal life in the presence of the Lord.

This hope should transform how we live today. When anxieties about this life overwhelm us, when we worry we're missing out or that things aren't working out as we'd hoped, we can remember: there is a light at the end of the tunnel, and it is not death, but resurrection. Christians, at heart, are optimists—not because we ignore suffering or difficulty, but because we have seen the end of the story, and Christ wins.
