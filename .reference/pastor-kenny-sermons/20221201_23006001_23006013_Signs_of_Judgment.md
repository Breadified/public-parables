# Isaiah 6:1-13

**Title:** Signs of Judgment  
**Date:** 11 December 2022  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## The Holiness of God Results in Judgment (verses 1-7)

> In the year that King Uzziah died, I saw the Lord, high and exalted, seated on a throne; and the train of his robe filled the temple.

Isaiah's vision is dated to 740 BC by the death of King Uzziah. Uzziah was one of Judah's most successful kings, becoming king at sixteen and reigning for fifty-two years. For his first four decades, he was a good and godly king, but pride eventually led to his downfall.

> 2 Chronicles 26:14-16: "[Uzziah's] fame spread far and wide, for he was greatly helped until he became powerful. But after Uzziah became powerful, his pride led to his downfall. He was unfaithful to the Lord his God, and entered the temple of the Lord to burn incense on the altar of incense."

When the priests confronted him, Uzziah grew angry and was struck with leprosy. His story is a parable of national pride and disobedience—a people whom God had prospered, yet who rebelled against Him. In contrast to this fallen earthly king, Isaiah sees a different King entirely.

### God's Transcendent Holiness

God is described as "high and exalted"—not in prideful arrogance like Uzziah, but in true, righteous power. He is seated upon a throne, the place of kingship, and the train of His robe fills the entire temple. The whole earth is full of His glory.

> 2 Above him were seraphim, each with six wings: With two wings they covered their faces, with two they covered their feet, and with two they were flying. 3 And they were calling to one another: "Holy, holy, holy is the Lord Almighty; the whole earth is full of his glory." 4 At the sound of their voices the doorposts and thresholds shook and the temple was filled with smoke.

The seraphim are six-winged creatures. The word "seraphim" comes from the Hebrew root *saraph*, meaning "to burn"—likely referring to their fiery nature. As they call out to one another like heralds announcing a royal presence, they proclaim the holiness of God three times. In Hebrew, there is no superlative form ("holy, holier, holiest"); the triple repetition conveys infinite holiness—very, very, very holy.

The seraphim cover their faces with two wings, suggesting the overwhelming danger of God's holy presence. We see this theme in Exodus:

> Exodus 33:21-23: "The Lord continued, 'There is a place near Me where you are to stand upon a rock, and when My glory passes by, I will put you in a cleft of the rock and cover you with My hand until I have passed by. Then I will take My hand away, and you will see My back; but My face must not be seen.'"

The smoke filling the temple adds another layer of covering, protecting against the consuming holiness of God's presence.

### Isaiah's Confession

Isaiah recognizes the danger and confesses his unworthiness:

> 5 "Woe to me!" I cried. "I am ruined! For I am a man of unclean lips, and I live among a people of unclean lips, and my eyes have seen the King, the Lord Almighty."

The consequence of encountering the holy God is death. Isaiah understands this. Yet he specifies his sin: unclean lips. Why lips? Because lips reflect the heart.

> Luke 6:45: "For the mouth speaks what the heart is full of."

If our hearts are unclean, our lips will be unclean. We speak evil because we think evil thoughts. Isaiah recognizes not only his own uncleanness but also that he dwells among a people of unclean lips—a double burden of corruption.

**The deeper principle emerges: the holier God is, the greater the judgment.** We sometimes ask why God cannot simply overlook sin, turn a blind eye to wrongdoing. The answer is that holiness and judgment are inseparable. God cannot compromise His holiness by ignoring sin—it is the very nature of His being. If we desire a holy God, we must accept a God of judgment. A holy God is a consistent God. He will not be holy for everyone else while being corrupt toward us.

### Restoration Through Grace

Yet Isaiah does not perish. God's grace intervenes:

> 6 Then one of the seraphim flew to me with a live coal in his hand, which he had taken with tongs from the altar. 7 With it he touched my mouth and said, "See, this has touched your lips; your guilt is taken away and your sin atoned for."

This is a striking image—almost like a parent's worst nightmare. A live coal is intensely hot, capable of cooking meat from pink to dark brown. The seraph even needs tongs to carry it, such is its heat. Yet it is applied to the most sensitive, vulnerable place—Isaiah's lips, among the thinnest and most heat-sensitive parts of the human body.

What we expect to be judgment becomes cleansing. The coal, which symbolizes pain and judgment, becomes the instrument of forgiveness. Coals are porous and have the remarkable property of drawing impurities to their surface—they are nature's filter. Our guilt and sin are taken away; our sin is atoned for.

Significantly, the coal comes from the altar—the place of sacrifice in the temple system, where propitiation occurs. The place of violence becomes a place of forgiveness. The seraph, burning with holiness, becomes God's agent of grace for Isaiah, who cannot cleanse himself. The coal touches his lips, and from there, the cleansing extends to his heart. And so Isaiah is now fit to serve God.

## Isaiah Sent to Judge, Not to Save (verses 8-10)

> 8 Then I heard the voice of the Lord saying, "Whom shall I send? And who will go for us?" And I said, "Here am I. Send me!"

There is an almost theatrical quality to this moment. The Lord asks, "Whom shall I send? Who will go for us?"—yet Isaiah is alone in the presence of God. On cue, Isaiah responds with immediate volunteering: "Here am I. Send me!" His willingness is admirable, yet Isaiah may not fully understand the commission he is accepting.

> 9 He said, "Go and tell this people: 'Be ever hearing, but never understanding; be ever seeing, but never perceiving.'"

Isaiah's task is peculiar. He is to declare a message that the people will not understand. He is to prophesy that they will see without perceiving. They will remain in perpetual ignorance.

> 10 Make the heart of this people calloused; make their ears dull and close their eyes. Otherwise they might see with their eyes, hear with their ears, understand with their hearts, and turn and be healed."

This is judgment—a hardening of hearts and closing of understanding. To understand the context, we must look back at Isaiah 1-5. Throughout these chapters, Israel and Judah have borne no fruit. God planted a vineyard, invested deeply in it, yet it produced only bitter berries instead of grapes. He sent calamities and disasters to wake them, but they did not hear, did not see, and did not understand.

Now God sends a servant—Isaiah—with a message of judgment. This parallels the parable in Mark:

> Mark 12:1-5: "Jesus then began to speak to them in parables: 'A man planted a vineyard. He put a wall around it, dug a pit for the winepress and built a watchtower. Then he rented the vineyard to some farmers and moved to another place. At harvest time he sent a servant to the tenants to collect from them some of the fruit of the vineyard. But they seized him, beat him and sent him away empty-handed. Then he sent another servant to them; they struck this man on the head and treated him shamefully.'"

The message begins with judgment and separation. This is echoed in Paul's words:

> 2 Corinthians 2:15-16: "For we are to God the sweet aroma of Christ among those who are being saved and those who are perishing. To the one, we are an odor of death and demise; to the other, a fragrance that brings life."

And in Jesus's own teaching:

> Mark 4:9-12: "Then Jesus said, 'Whoever has ears to hear, let them hear.' When he was alone, the Twelve and the others around him asked him about the parables. He told them, 'The secret of the kingdom of God has been given to you. But to those on the outside everything is said in parables so that, "they may be ever seeing but never perceiving, and ever hearing but never understanding; otherwise they might turn and be forgiven!"'"

This raises a profound question: Does Jesus want people to understand or not? The answer lies in nuance. Elsewhere, Paul appeals to us as ambassadors of Christ (2 Corinthians 5:20), and Peter instructs us to give a reason for the hope within us (1 Peter 3:15). Jesus Himself explains the parable of the soils to His disciples. We are not called to be rude, unwelcoming, prejudicial, or to ignore timing.

But we must accept this reality: many will reject this message. We need not dilute the gospel, nor make it artificially easier by removing the miraculous or the challenging commandments. The world has a real problem. We are unclean. We face judgment. We are called to be salt and light, the spice of the world. But who will go and tell this message?

## Judgment Is Not Forever (verses 11-13)

Now that Isaiah has received his commission—not an enviable one—he asks a natural question:

> 11 Then I said, "For how long, Lord?" And he answered: "Until the cities lie ruined and without inhabitant, until the houses are left deserted and the fields ruined and ravaged, 12 until the Lord has sent everyone far away and the land is utterly forsaken."

Isaiah's question is direct: How long must he proclaim this message of judgment? The answer is bleak—the judgment will continue until cities are destroyed, houses abandoned, fields ravaged, and the people are sent far away. The land itself will be utterly forsaken.

> 13 And though a tenth remains in the land, it will again be laid waste.

Even a remnant of ten percent will not escape. Everything points to destruction. The judgment seems total and unrelenting.

But the passage does not end in despair:

> 13... But as the terebinth and oak leave stumps when they are cut down, so the holy seed will be the stump in the land."

The metaphor shifts to great trees—symbols of the greatness Israel once possessed. These trees are chopped down, brought low to nothing but stumps. Yet a stump is not dead. It lives. From deep roots, new growth springs forth. The image evokes the resilience of life itself—a small plant refusing to die, cut down year after year, yet always returning with new leaves and shoots.

This connects to the prophecy in Isaiah 11:

> Isaiah 11:1: "A shoot will come up from the stump of Jesse; from his roots a Branch will bear fruit."

The stump is not merely Israel itself, but the *holy seed*—the messianic promise. This seed is Jesus. From judgment comes hope. From destruction comes the possibility of new growth. From the darkest moment emerges redemption.

Returning to the parable of the vineyard:

> Mark 12:6-8: "'He had one left to send, a son, whom he loved. He sent him last of all, saying, "They will respect my son." But the tenants said to one another, "This is the heir. Come, let's kill him, and the inheritance will be ours." So they took him and killed him, and threw him out of the vineyard.'"

On that piece of carbon in the shape of a cross, Jesus took judgment upon Himself so that others might be cleansed and go free.

### The Good News Beyond Judgment

On this side of Isaiah 6, we have reason for gratitude. Our message is not all judgment, not all doom and gloom. There is an element of good news. There is a second chance for all of us. Not all is "Woe." Our guilt and sin can be taken away. As we move toward the Christmas season, we ask: Who will go and tell this message?
