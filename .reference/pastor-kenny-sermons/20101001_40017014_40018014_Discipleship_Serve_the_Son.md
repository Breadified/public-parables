# Matthew 17:14-18:14

**Title:** Discipleship: Serve the Son
**Date:** 3 October 2010
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Overview

This passage teaches us what it truly means to follow Jesus through four key movements:

1. **Follow the Son** (denying self)
2. **Listen to the Son** (Matthew 17:1-13)
3. **Serve the Son** (Matthew 17:14-18:14)
4. **Proclaim the Son** (Matthew 18:15-35 - forgiveness, binding and loosing)

The central theme that binds these passages together: **Welcome the little ones into the kingdom through forgiveness, proclamation, and genuine hospitality.**

## 1. Be Little: The Foundation of Greatness

> At that time the disciples came to Jesus and asked, "Who is the greatest in the kingdom of heaven?" He called a little child and had him stand among them. And he said: "I tell you the truth, unless you change and become like little children, you will never enter the kingdom of heaven. Therefore, whoever humbles himself like this child is the greatest in the kingdom of heaven." (Matthew 18:2-4)

Jesus is not calling us to regress to childhood or recover some lost innocence. Rather, he is calling us to **humility**.

In first-century Jewish culture, childhood was not romanticized as it is today. There was no concept of adolescence or "the best years of your life." A child was either an infant or essentially a small adult, and once they could walk and talk, they were put to work. Children received the menial tasks—cleaning, gathering fuel, drawing water from wells, tending flocks, caring for cattle. All the work no one else wanted to do.

Children were like apprentices assigned the hardest, most humbling labor. Significantly, the Greek word for boy or child (pais in Matthew 17:18) carries the same meaning as "slave" or "servant." **Being a child meant being a servant—humble, lowly, without status or power.**

This is Jesus's criterion for entering the kingdom and for becoming great within it. The pathway to greatness in God's kingdom runs through humility and service.

## 2. Welcome the Little: What Service Looks Like

> "And whoever welcomes a little child like this in my name welcomes me." (Matthew 18:5)

To welcome means to receive and accept (Greek: *dechomai*). But what does this look like in practice? Jesus clarifies through contrast: to welcome means **not to block** others from coming into the kingdom.

### The Warning: Causing the Little Ones to Stumble

> But if anyone causes one of these little ones who believe in me to sin, it would be better for him to have a large millstone hung around his neck and to be drowned in the depths of the sea. (Matthew 18:6)

This is severe language. Jesus is saying that if we prevent others—especially the vulnerable and new believers—from entering or remaining in God's kingdom, we face terrible judgment. The same word Jesus uses here for "stumbling block" appears in Matthew 16:23 when Peter rebukes Jesus, and Jesus calls Peter a "stumbling block."

Our foolish actions, pride, arrogance, and selfish behavior can close off the gospel for others. We block people through:
- Stupid behavior and poor witness
- Arrogant arguments about "who is the greatest"
- Unrestricted exercise of our gifts and rights without regard for others
- Simple insensitivity and neglect

### The Story of the Epileptic Boy: Disciples Failing to Welcome

> When they came to the crowd, a man approached Jesus and knelt before him. "Lord, have mercy on my son," he said. "He has seizures and is suffering greatly. He often falls into the fire or into the water. I brought him to your disciples, but they could not heal him." (Matthew 17:14-16)

A desperate father brings his suffering son to Jesus's disciples, but they cannot heal him. Jesus rebukes them:

> "O unbelieving and perverse generation," Jesus replied, "how long shall I stay with you? How long shall I put up with you? Bring the boy here to me." (Matthew 17:17)

Jesus heals the boy, and the disciples later ask why they couldn't drive out the demon. Jesus responds:

> "Because you have so little faith. I tell you the truth, if you have faith as small as a mustard seed, you can say to this mountain, 'Move from here to there' and it will move. Nothing will be impossible for you." (Matthew 17:19-20)

The disciples had failed this little one. Why? They lacked faith, but more than that—they were motivated by ambition rather than service. They wanted to demonstrate power; Jesus wanted to demonstrate mercy. The disciples were obsessed with status and greatness (notice that immediately after this, in Matthew 18:1, they ask "Who is the greatest?"), while Jesus was focused on welcoming and serving the vulnerable.

In their failure to welcome this boy with faith and compassion, they blocked him from healing and from experiencing God's kingdom.

### Welcome Each Other

The other children Jesus speaks of include:
- **Each other**—our brothers and sisters in the body of Christ
- **The elect**—those God is calling into his kingdom

Practically, to welcome means: do not block them from the kingdom. Serve by receiving them. Jesus himself is gathering disciples, and he invites us to serve alongside him by welcoming others in.

## 3. Even Sons Are Not Exempt from Service

> After Jesus and his disciples arrived in Capernaum, the collectors of the two-drachma tax came to Peter and asked, "Doesn't your teacher pay the temple tax?" (Matthew 17:24)

Peter initially answers (in the most literal reading), "He doesn't need to." And Jesus confirms this principle:

> "What do you think, Simon?" he asked. "From whom do the kings of the earth collect duty and taxes—from their own sons or from others?" "From others," Peter answered. "Then the sons are exempt," Jesus said to him. (Matthew 17:25-26)

Kings do not pay taxes—they collect them. The sons of kings are likewise exempt from taxation. But then Jesus does something remarkable:

> "But so that we may not cause them to stumble, go to the lake and throw out your line. Take the first fish you catch; open its mouth and you will find a four-drachma coin. Take it and give it to them for my tax and yours." (Matthew 17:27)

Notice the language: so that we do not "stumble" or "block" them. Even though Jesus has every right as the Son of God to be exempt from the temple tax, he chooses to pay it—to humble himself, to serve, to avoid causing offense.

This teaches us that **our status as sons and daughters of God does not exempt us from service.** True greatness means setting aside our rights for the sake of welcoming others into God's kingdom.

## 4. The Father's Will: Not One Lost

> "What do you think? If a man owns a hundred sheep, and one of them wanders away, will he not leave the ninety-nine on the hills and go to look for the one that wandered off? And if he finds it, I tell you the truth, he is happier about that one sheep than about the ninety-nine that did not wander off. In the same way your Father in heaven is not willing that any of these little ones should be lost." (Matthew 18:12-14)

Once, you too were a lost sheep. You did not know the Father. Yet he found you through his Son, who:

- Denied himself
- Carried his cross
- Followed the Father's will to its completion
- Gave himself for your sake

The glorious Son—the one radiating God's glory, the one of whom God said "This is my Son, whom I love; with him I am well pleased"—was willing to be humbled, rejected, and killed so that you might be welcomed into the kingdom.

Now Jesus asks: **Will you deny your own self-interest, carry your cross, and follow the Father's will so that others will, like you, be welcomed into the kingdom?**

The Father is not willing that any of these little ones should be lost. The question is: **Are you willing?** How willing are we to orient our lives around welcoming others into God's kingdom, even when it costs us comfort, convenience, or status?

## Application: Welcome in Practical Ways

This calling requires a **giant mindshift** about why we do the things we do—whether it's the sermon length, comment cards, giving financially, maintaining traditions, or anything else in our church life.

### For Those Older or Stronger

Are you older? Do you welcome and serve?

- **Consider the young in our church.** Are we looking after our year 6s and 7s who are with us? I'm grateful you're here and enjoy reading your thoughts. When my sermons run long or my illustrations feel dated (sorry about the Power Rangers reference), know that I'm trying to look out for you, and I hope all of us are.
- **Consider our younger children.** Let's not block them from Sunday school or other gatherings through our negligence.
- **Transportation for high schoolers.** Providing rides to events is a form of service and welcome. If you're able, learn to drive, get a car, or loan a spare. Get trained in safe ministry practices.

### For Everyone: Welcome the Newcomer

The newcomer is weaker—unfamiliar with our church, uncertain where things are, unsure of what to do. They may feel lost and alone. To welcome them:

**Principle 1: Be Here Consistently**
You cannot welcome people you don't know, and you can't know people if you're not here. When someone is absent, they're missed by name. When you're absent most Sundays, you're the one who doesn't know anybody.

**Principle 2: Early and Late (BELL)**
A good host arrives early and leaves late. Not everyone can do this—especially parents with young children—but we all should come earlier than we do to help welcome. It's not just the welcoming team's job; it's everyone's responsibility.

**Principle 3: Feet, Seat, Meet**
- **Feet:** Move yourself across the room
- **Seat:** Sit next to a new person
- **Meet:** Talk to them

People are often more nervous about approaching you than you are about approaching them. Include them in a group conversation. Think of yourself as a party host—the host arrives well before guests, stays after everyone leaves to clean up, and is genuinely present. The person who arrives late, mingles briefly, and leaves early is just using the party.

### The Sacred Responsibility

People are walking through our church doors to find out about God. We must not block them. Not through our insensitivity, not through our selfishness, not through our pride.

Some of us have worked hard and summoned great courage to invite friends to church. We cannot betray that courage by ignoring them or making them feel unwelcome. Feet, seat, meet—is it really so hard? Being here early and staying late—is it truly impossible?

This is the will of your Father in heaven.

## The Gospel Through Welcome

Some of us think: "I don't want to just welcome people; that's a job anybody can do. I want to use my gifts to really glorify God."

But listen: **If you don't welcome, there is no way you can glorify God.** In fact, you might lose an arm or leg for causing others to stumble.

When you welcome others, you welcome Jesus.

> "And whoever welcomes a little child like this in my name welcomes me." (Matthew 18:5)

> "He who receives you receives me, and he who receives me receives the one who sent me. Anyone who receives a prophet because he is a prophet will receive a prophet's reward, and anyone who receives a righteous man because he is a righteous man will receive a righteous man's reward. And if anyone gives even a cup of cold water to one of these little ones because he is my disciple, I tell you the truth, he will certainly not lose his reward." (Matthew 10:40-42)

When you serve others, you serve God. This is not a secondary calling or a lesser gift. It is the foundation of the Christian life.

## The Experience We Create

What kind of experience do people encounter when they enter our church? Some churches feel like entertainment venues—everything big, loud, emotionally stirring. Others feel imposing and distant—grand, religious, untouchable. What about us?

We meet in a primary school hall. What experience should it be? I hope it's the experience of love for one another. I hope it's warm and welcoming. I hope it feels like we're loving each other as we love Christ. I hope that when we pray together, it genuinely connects us with people we don't normally interact with.

I hope we treat each other as though it is Christ we are serving, because in welcoming each other, we welcome him.
