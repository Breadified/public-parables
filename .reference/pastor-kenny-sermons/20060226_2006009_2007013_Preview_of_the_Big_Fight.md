# Exodus 6:9–7:13

**Title:** Preview of the Big Fight  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew  
**Date:** February 26, 2006

## The Setup: A Movie Trailer for God's Victory

Have you ever watched a movie trailer? The best ones give you a snapshot of what's coming—they introduce the main characters and build anticipation for the story. They're a setup, designed to draw you in.

That's exactly what we find in today's passage. As we read Exodus 6:9 through 7:13, God is doing something similar. He's setting up the greatest conflict in Israel's history—not a movie, but the *big fight* between His power and Pharaoh's stubborn resistance. Throughout the last five chapters, Yahweh has repeatedly promised to take His people out of Egypt. Now it's time. But before the battle begins, God introduces the fighters and gives us a preview of what to expect.

## The Fighters: Moses and Aaron in the Red Corner

### The Problem: Doubt and Discouragement

Moses and Aaron face a crisis of confidence—from both the people and from within themselves.

The Israelites refuse to listen:

> Moses reported this to the Israelites, but they did not listen to him because of their discouragement and cruel bondage. (Exodus 6:9)

Moses himself doubts his own ability:

> But Moses said to the LORD, "If the Israelites will not listen to me, why would Pharaoh listen to me, since I speak with faltering lips?" (Exodus 6:12)

How will God answer this crisis of confidence? Through genealogy.

### Why the Genealogy Matters

Genealogies can seem tedious—we're tempted to skip over them. But this genealogy is deliberately placed here, sandwiched between Moses' objections (6:12) and repeated throughout the passage (through 6:30). It's not accidental. It's God's answer to the doubt.

The genealogy serves two purposes:

**First, it looks backward.** It traces Moses and Aaron's lineage back through Levi, Kohath, and Amram. The point is this: Moses is of the line of Israel. He is circumcised—a mark of the covenant, of belonging to God's people. At a time when his loyalty and credentials are questioned, the genealogy proves his heritage. He is not an Egyptian outsider; he is one of God's own.

**Second, it looks forward.** Notice that the genealogy culminates in Phinehas, grandson of Aaron. Phinehas appears later in Numbers as a man of exceptional faith and prophetic character—someone who acts decisively in defense of God's honor. The genealogy connects Aaron's line to a legacy of faithfulness and prophetic power.

### God's Guarantee

The genealogy is God's way of saying: *I have prepared these men. I have worked this plan from the beginning. They are fit for the task.*

This is confirmed in what comes next:

> Then the LORD said to Moses, "See, I have made you like God to Pharaoh, and your brother Aaron will be your prophet." (Exodus 7:1)

Moses and Aaron are not stepping into this battle unprepared. God has positioned them. They carry His authority and His word.

## The Preview: What to Expect

Now that God has introduced His fighters, He gives Moses a foretaste of the coming conflict.

### First: The Hardening

> But I will harden Pharaoh's heart, and though I multiply my miraculous signs and wonders in Egypt, he will not listen to you. Then I will lay my hand on Egypt and with mighty acts of judgment I will bring out my divisions, my people the Israelites. (Exodus 7:3–4)

What does it mean that God will "harden" Pharaoh's heart? The word carries the sense of making something stubborn, immovable—the same word used earlier when describing the hardness of the Israelites' labor.

Some wonder if God is simply squeezing out what was already in Pharaoh's heart—revealing his true nature without changing it. But I believe it's more than that. The repeated declaration—"I will harden"—suggests that God is actively controlling Pharaoh's response. God is managing both sides of this conflict. He will harden Pharaoh's heart, yet multiply His signs and wonders. Pharaoh will resist, but it's all part of God's sovereign plan.

### A Hard Question: What About Free Will?

This raises an uncomfortable question: If God is controlling Pharaoh's heart, what about human freedom? Is God a cosmic tyrant, playing with people's lives like a video game with two controllers?

This is a deep theological question, and we can't resolve it completely in one message. But Paul addresses it directly in Romans 9, using Pharaoh as his very example. Let me read it:

> Not only that, but Rebekah's children had one and the same father, our father Isaac. Yet, before the twins were born or had done anything good or bad—in order that God's purpose in election might stand: not by works but by him who calls—she was told, "The older will serve the younger." Just as it is written: "Jacob I loved, but Esau I hated." (Romans 9:10–13)

God chose Jacob over Esau *before they had done anything*—before they had any opportunity to earn His favor or incur His displeasure. Is this unjust? Paul says no:

> What then shall we say? Is God unjust? Not at all! For he says to Moses, "I will have mercy on whom I have mercy, and I will have compassion on whom I have compassion." It does not, therefore, depend on man's desire or effort, but on God's mercy. For the Scripture says to Pharaoh: "I raised you up for this very purpose, that I might display my power in you and that my name might be proclaimed in all the earth." Therefore God has mercy on whom he wants to have mercy, and he hardens whom he wants to harden. (Romans 9:14–18)

Someone will say, "Then why does God still blame us? For who resists his will?" But Paul responds:

> But who are you, O man, to talk back to God? "Shall what is formed say to him who formed it, 'Why did you make me like this?'" Does not the potter have the right to make out of the same lump of clay some pottery for noble purposes and some for common use? (Romans 9:20–21)

God is God. His control is not tyrannical; it is exercised in love and wisdom that far exceeds our understanding.

**Three applications:**
1. God is truly sovereign—this is not about human free will, but about His ultimate authority.
2. Be thankful. We exist because of His mercy, not because we deserve it.
3. Pray, recognizing that we don't know His will until He reveals it.

### Second: A Foretaste of Victory

After this preview of hardening, God gives Moses and Aaron an immediate demonstration:

> When Pharaoh says to you, "Perform a miracle," then say to Aaron, "Take your staff and throw it down before Pharaoh," and it will become a snake. (Exodus 7:9)

They do exactly as commanded. Aaron throws down his staff, and it becomes a serpent. Pharaoh's magicians do the same—their staffs also become serpents. But then:

> But Aaron's staff swallowed up their staffs. Yet Pharaoh's heart became hard and he would not listen to them, just as the LORD had said. (Exodus 7:12–13)

This is more than a foretaste. It's a microcosm of the entire conflict to come. God and His servants match Pharaoh and his magicians, move for move. And God triumphs. Pharaoh's power, represented by his magicians, is consumed by God's power.

Yet Pharaoh's heart remains hard—exactly as God predicted. The hardening has already begun, and the pattern is set: God will act with power; Pharaoh will resist; God will prevail.

## Looking Ahead: The Ultimate Victory

This battle between Moses and Pharaoh is a preview of something even greater. The entire story of Exodus points forward to Jesus Christ, who would wage the ultimate conflict against sin, death, and the powers of darkness—and emerge victorious. The fight has already been finished at the cross and resurrection.

But that's a teaching for another day. For now, remember this: When you face seemingly impossible obstacles, God has already prepared His fighters. He is sovereign. He will harden the hearts of those who resist Him, multiply His wonders, and ultimately display His power to all the earth. The outcome is already determined. Trust in Him.
