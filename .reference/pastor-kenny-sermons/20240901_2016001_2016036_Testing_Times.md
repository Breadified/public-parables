# Exodus 16:1-36

**Title:** Testing Times  
**Date:** 22 September 2024  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Israel Testing God

The Israelites arrived in the Desert of Sin on the fifteenth day of the second month after leaving Egypt—approximately six weeks into their wilderness journey. Tired and hungry from the long journey, they began to grumble against Moses and Aaron, forgetting the deliverance they had just experienced.

> 2 In the desert the whole community grumbled against Moses and Aaron. 3 The Israelites said to them, "If only we had died by the Lord's hand in Egypt! There we sat around pots of meat and ate all the food we wanted, but you have brought us out into this desert to starve this entire assembly to death."

### The Distorted Memory of Slavery

The Israelites remembered Egypt not as it was, but as they wished it to be. While they recalled abundance, the reality was far different. Ancient slaves rarely had meat daily—bread was the staple when meat was unavailable. The fuller picture of their diet in Egypt is revealed elsewhere:

> Numbers 11:5 We remember the fish we ate in Egypt at no cost—also the cucumbers, melons, leeks, onions and garlic.

Their reminiscence reveals a psychological phenomenon: they romanticized their bondage. Even if plenty of food had been available to them as slaves, it came at an unconscionable cost—their freedom. Slavery is fundamentally a false bargain. A slave might receive food, shelter, medical care, and housing, but remains enslaved. The material provision masks the deeper reality of lost liberty.

This applies spiritually as well. Sin, like the devil, can appear as an attractive master offering temporary pleasure and excitement. But it is a cruel master that uses us and eventually discards us, leaving only pain and regret. When we find ourselves nostalgic for our old life of sin, we are remembering falsely—clinging to a false bargain.

### The Nature of Grumbling

When Israel fondly recalled Egypt, they were not merely complaining to Moses—they were grumbling against God himself.

> 7 "In the morning you will see the glory of the Lord, because he has heard your grumbling against him. Who are we, that you should grumble against us?"
>
> 8 Moses also said, "You will know that it was the Lord when he gives you meat to eat in the evening and all the bread you want in the morning, because he has heard your grumbling against him. You are not grumbling against us, but against the Lord."
>
> 9 Then Moses told Aaron, "Say to the entire Israelite community, 'Come before the Lord, for he has heard your grumbling.'"
>
> 10 While Aaron was speaking to the whole Israelite community, they looked toward the desert, and there was the glory of the Lord appearing in the cloud.

Their grumbling was, in essence, testing God—demanding proof of His trustworthiness, insisting He prove Himself worthy of their faith.

> Exodus 17:2-3 They quarreled with Moses and said, "Give us water to drink." Moses replied, "Why do you quarrel with me? Why do you put the Lord to the test?"

To test God is to place ourselves above Him—to assume we have the knowledge and authority to judge Him. It is arrogance. A student cannot test his teacher; an ant cannot test a human. How much less can we test God?

> Matthew 4:7 Jesus answered him, "It is also written: 'Do not put the Lord your God to the test.'"

### How We Test God Today

We test God when we complain without gratitude about our circumstances, our family, or our church. We test God when we voice discontent without acknowledging what we have been rescued from. We test God when we look only at our present difficulties without remembering the journey or trusting the future.

> 1 Thessalonians 5:18 Give thanks in all circumstances; for this is the will of God in Christ Jesus for you.
>
> Philippians 2:14 Do everything without grumbling or arguing, so that you may become blameless and pure.

## God Testing Us

In hardship, the proper posture is not to test God, but to recognize that God is testing us. The Lord Himself explained this to Moses:

> 4 Then the Lord said to Moses, "I will rain down bread from heaven for you. The people are to go out each day and gather enough for that day. In this way I will test them and see whether they will follow my instructions. 5 On the sixth day they are to prepare what they bring in, and that is to be twice as much as they gather on the other days."

God's test had two primary purposes: to teach dependence and to build trust in Him.

### A. To Teach Daily Dependence

> 11-12 The Lord said to Moses, "I have heard the grumbling of the Israelites. Tell them, 'At twilight you will eat meat, and in the morning you will be filled with bread. Then you will know that I am the Lord your God.'"
>
> 13 That evening quail came and covered the camp, and in the morning there was a layer of dew around the camp. 14 When the dew was gone, thin flakes like frost on the ground appeared on the desert floor. 15 When the Israelites saw it, they said to each other, "What is it?" For they did not know what it was. Moses said to them, "It is the bread the Lord has given you to eat."

The manna appeared fresh each morning but could not be stored. Those who tried to keep it overnight found it full of maggots and reeking.

> 16 This is what the Lord has commanded: "Everyone is to gather as much as they need. Take an omer for each person you have in your tent."
>
> 17-18 The Israelites did as they were told; some gathered much, some little. And when they measured it by the omer, the one who gathered much did not have too much, and the one who gathered little did not have too little. Everyone had gathered just as much as they needed.

The manna teaches a critical lesson: we cannot hoard provision. Just as the manna melted away in the heat, all our wealth, possessions, and experiences are temporary. Our photos fade, our memories blur, and our things decay. Each day's hunger becomes a reminder that we depend not on our stockpiles, but on God's daily faithfulness.

> 19-21 Then Moses said to them, "No one is to keep any of it until morning." However, some of them paid no attention to Moses; they kept part of it until morning, but it was full of maggots and began to smell. So Moses was angry with them. Each morning everyone gathered as much as they needed, and when the sun grew hot, it melted away.

This echoes Jesus' teaching in the wilderness:

> Matthew 4:4 Jesus answered, "It is written: 'Man shall not live on bread alone, but on every word that comes from the mouth of God.'"

We are to live daily on God's word, just as Israel lived daily on God's provision.

### B. To Teach Trust in God for the Future

> 22-23 On the sixth day, they gathered twice as much—two omers for each person—and the leaders of the community came and reported this to Moses. He said to them, "This is what the Lord commanded: 'Tomorrow is to be a day of sabbath rest, a holy sabbath to the Lord. So bake what you want to bake and boil what you want to boil. Save whatever is left and keep it until morning.'"
>
> 24 So they saved it until morning, as Moses commanded, and it did not stink or get maggots in it.

The Sabbath teaches that God is not a slave driver. In Egypt, they labored seven days a week. Under God's covenant, they work six days and rest on the seventh. This rhythm reveals a profound truth: God provides abundantly enough that we need not work without cessation. More than this, the Sabbath points forward—from one day of rest each week to the eternal rest awaiting God's people in heaven.

Some disobeyed this instruction, going out to gather manna on the seventh day, finding nothing.

> 28 Then the Lord said to Moses, "How long will you refuse to keep my commands and my instructions? 29 Bear in mind that the Lord has given you the Sabbath; that is why on the sixth day he gives you bread for two days. Everyone is to stay where they are on the seventh day; no one is to go out." 30 So the people rested on the seventh day.

### The Purpose of God's Testing

God tests us not to harm us, but to mature us.

> Exodus 20:20 And Moses said to the people, "Do not fear; for God has come to test you, and that His fear may be before you, so that you may not sin."
>
> James 1:2-4 Consider it pure joy, my brothers and sisters, whenever you face trials of many kinds, because you know that the testing of your faith produces perseverance. Let perseverance finish its work so that you may be mature and complete, not lacking anything.

Testing reveals our weaknesses and mistakes so we can improve. God squeezes our hearts to show us our true desires—and how they diverge from His. This refining is essential for growth. Without testing, we never reach maturity.

Remarkably, God's testing of the Israelites was gracious. They complained about hunger, and He gave them meat and bread. They longed for provision, and He provided abundantly. His test called them to be less like the world—less concerned with ego, status, and luxury—and more like Jesus, who focused on serving and proclaiming God's kingdom to the next generation.

### A Word to Students in Testing Times

For students facing exams like the HSC: this season of testing is a gift. It reveals what you are capable of, what you need to improve, and who you are becoming. The exam is not the end—it is the beginning. Your worth does not come from your score, but from Christ, who passed the ultimate test on your behalf.

## The Commemoration of Manna

> 31 The people of Israel called the bread manna. It was white like coriander seed and tasted like wafers made with honey. 32 Moses said, "This is what the Lord has commanded: 'Take an omer of manna and keep it for the generations to come, so they can see the bread I gave you to eat in the wilderness when I brought you out of Egypt.'"
>
> 33 So Moses said to Aaron, "Take a jar and put an omer of manna in it. Then place it before the Lord to be kept for the generations to come."
>
> 34 As the Lord commanded Moses, Aaron put the manna with the tablets of the covenant law, so that it might be preserved. 35 The Israelites ate manna forty years, until they came to a land that was settled; they ate manna until they reached the border of Canaan.

A sample of manna was preserved alongside the tablets of the covenant as a perpetual testimony to God's faithfulness. For forty years in the wilderness, God sustained His people. Every day was a fresh miracle, every meal a reminder that He is trustworthy.
