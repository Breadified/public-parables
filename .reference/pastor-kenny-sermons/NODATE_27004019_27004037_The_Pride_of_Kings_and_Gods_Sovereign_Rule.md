# Daniel 4:19-37

**Title:** The Pride of Kings and God's Sovereign Rule

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Introduction: The Letter of Reference

Nebuchadnezzar begins this chapter with an official letter addressed to all nations, peoples, and languages throughout his empire. This is a remarkable shift—for the first time in Daniel, we hear directly from the king himself, speaking in the first person about his own experience with God.

The format of this letter mirrors something many of us know: a letter of recommendation. Just as we seek references for jobs or medical referrals, Nebuchadnezzar is writing to testify about an encounter with the Most High God that will transform his understanding of power and sovereignty.

---

## The Tree: A Picture of the Promise of Rulers

### The Vision

Nebuchadnezzar dreams of a magnificent tree—large, strong, with its top reaching the clouds and its branches visible to the whole earth. Only Daniel can interpret this dream, just as in the previous chapters.

### The Interpretation: A Kingdom's Potential

**Daniel's words to the king:**

> Your Majesty, you are that tree! You have become great and strong; your greatness has grown until it reaches the sky, and your dominion extends to distant parts of the earth. (Daniel 4:22)

The tree represents far more than physical might. It embodies the God-given potential of a kingdom:

- **Beautiful leaves and abundant fruit, providing food for all**—The tree produces prosperity for its citizens. This echoes Joseph's wise governance in Egypt, where proper administration of resources ensures the welfare of the people.

- **Giving shelter to the wild animals, nesting places for the birds**—The kingdom becomes a place of refuge and blessing even for foreigners.

The prophet Ezekiel describes a similar vision of imperial power and its responsibility:

> All the birds of the sky nested in its boughs; all the animals of the wild gave birth under its branches; all the great nations lived in its shade. (Ezekiel 31:5-6)

### The Role of Government: Justice and Protection

The tree symbolizes the fundamental purpose of government—not self-aggrandizement, but the protection and provision for those under its rule. God's design for earthly rulers includes:

**Justice, not oppression:** A government's most basic role is to protect people from the sinful conduct of others—to safeguard life, property, and the keeping of covenants. This is why God gave rulers the sword.

> For the one in authority is God's servant for your good. But if you do wrong, be afraid, for rulers do not bear the sword for no reason. They are God's servants, agents of wrath to bring punishment on the wrongdoer. (Romans 13:4)

Peter echoes this:

> Submit yourselves for the Lord's sake to every human authority: whether to the emperor as the supreme authority, or to governors, who are sent by him to punish those who do wrong and to commend those who do right. (1 Peter 2:13-14)

**True justice, not exploitation:** Justice means individual trials, evidence, and fair application of law—not class warfare or arbitrary confiscation. When a ruler serves justice faithfully, the result is grain that abounds and a nation that flourishes under the rule of law.

The Psalms celebrate this ideal:

> Endow the king with your justice, O God, the royal son with your righteousness. May he judge your people in righteousness, your afflicted ones with justice. (Psalm 72:1-2)

## The Stump: The Arrogance That Brings Judgment

### The Fall of the Mighty

But the vision shifts dramatically. The tree is cut down—reduced to a stump. What was glorious becomes humbled:

> You will be driven away from people and will live with the wild animals; you will eat grass like the ox and be drenched with the dew of heaven. Seven times will pass by for you until you acknowledge that the Most High is sovereign over all kingdoms on earth and gives them to anyone he wishes. (Daniel 4:25)

### The Root of His Fall: Forgotten Sovereignty

Twelve months pass, and Nebuchadnezzar walks on the roof of his palace. Pride floods his heart:

> Is not this the great Babylon I have built as the royal residence, by my mighty power and for the glory of my majesty? (Daniel 4:30)

The king speaks truth—but it is a truth that denies the deeper reality. He has built the city. He has amassed the wealth. He has created the power. And he has forgotten the One who made him king.

> The decision is announced by messengers, the holy ones declare the verdict, so that the living may know that the Most High is sovereign over the kingdoms of men and gives them to anyone he wishes. (Daniel 4:17)

This is repeated in God's judgment:

> You will be driven away from people and will live with the wild animals; you will eat grass like cattle and be drenched with the dew of heaven. Seven times will pass by for you until you acknowledge that the Most High is sovereign over the kingdoms of men and gives them to anyone he wishes. (Daniel 4:25)

### The Judgment: Therianthropy and Humiliation

What follows is shocking and humiliating. The king develops *therianthropy*—a transformation where he lives like a beast:

> Immediately what had been said about Nebuchadnezzar was fulfilled. He was driven away from people and ate grass like the ox. His body was drenched with the dew of heaven until his hair grew like the feathers of an eagle and his nails like the claws of a bird. (Daniel 4:33)

The great king, brought low to the level of cattle. A form of insanity that strips him of everything—authority, dignity, humanity itself. Yet notice: this is not random punishment. It is a carefully measured consequence designed to teach a specific lesson.

## The Stump with Roots: The Mercy of a Second Chance

### Hope Remains

Yet the vision contains a crucial detail of mercy:

> Cut down the tree and destroy it, but leave the stump, bound with iron and bronze, in the grass of the field, while its roots remain in the ground. (Daniel 4:23)

The stump is not destroyed. It is bound—perhaps to preserve it from rot, to keep it from being uprooted, to give it the chance to grow again. The roots remain in the ground. This is not a dead stump; it is a stump with potential.

The binding with iron and bronze may even suggest God's protection—keeping the king from harming himself during his affliction.

### Restoration Through Repentance

After seven times pass—seven years of humiliation—Nebuchadnezzar raises his eyes to heaven. This single gesture encapsulates repentance:

> At the end of that time, I, Nebuchadnezzar, raised my eyes toward heaven, and my sanity was restored. Then I praised the Most High; I honored and glorified him who lives forever. (Daniel 4:34)

His sanity returns. His honor and splendor are restored. He is returned to his throne—and becomes even greater than before. But now he understands:

> Now I, Nebuchadnezzar, praise and exalt and glorify the King of heaven, because everything he does is right and all his ways are just. And those who walk in pride he is able to humble. (Daniel 4:37)

---

## The Universal Pattern: Sin, Judgment, and Restoration

Nebuchadnezzar's experience reveals a pattern that applies to all of us:

**We sin through pride and forgetfulness.** We forget that God is sovereign. We forget that He gives authority to anyone He wishes. We elevate ourselves and our accomplishments.

**We are judged for it.** Some suffering in our lives comes from accidents and unfairness—mysteries we will understand in eternity. But much of the difficulty we face is self-inflicted: our pride, our careless words, our lack of recognition of God's rule, our oppression of others (or the election of officials to oppress on our behalf).

**But repentance brings restoration.** There is always a stump with roots. God offers mercy to anyone who repents and acknowledges that Heaven rules. Even in judgment, He preserves the possibility of redemption.

## The King Today: Jesus and the Upside-Down Kingdom

The prophecies of Scripture point us to a King who inverts Nebuchadnezzar's trajectory entirely:

> I myself will take a shoot from the very top of a cedar and plant it; I will break off a tender sprig from its topmost shoots and plant it on a high and lofty mountain. On the mountain heights of Israel I will plant it; it will produce branches and bear fruit and become a splendid cedar. (Ezekiel 17:22-23)

This points to Jesus, who came not in splendor but in humility:

> He grew up before Him like a tender shoot, and like a root out of dry ground. He had no stately form or majesty to attract us, no beauty that we should desire Him. He was despised and rejected by men, a man of sorrows, acquainted with grief. (Isaiah 53:2-3)

> Foxes have dens and birds of the air have nests, but the Son of Man has no place to lay His head. (Luke 9:58)

Yet this humiliation led to exaltation:

> Being found in appearance as a man, he humbled himself by becoming obedient to death—even death on a cross! (Philippians 2:8)

The kingdom of heaven operates by this same principle of the smallest becoming greatest:

> The kingdom of heaven is like a mustard seed that a man planted in his field. Although it is the smallest of all seeds, yet it grows into the largest of garden plants and becomes a tree, so that the birds of the air come and nest in its branches. (Matthew 13:31-32)

### Our Application Today

We must consider what God has given us—our talents, our influence, our position—and recognize them as stewardship, not possession. The call is to humble ourselves and use what we have for His kingdom, not our own glory.

This applies to nations as well. When a people forget their foundation—when they abandon the God who established them, the biblical principles that built them—they invite judgment. Yet there is always the possibility of repentance, of calling a nation back to its roots, back to God, back to the ideals upon which it was founded.

> There is no authority except that which God has established. The authorities that exist have been established by God. (Romans 13:1)

Nebuchadnezzar's story teaches us: God humbles those who walk in pride, and He exalts those who acknowledge His sovereignty.
