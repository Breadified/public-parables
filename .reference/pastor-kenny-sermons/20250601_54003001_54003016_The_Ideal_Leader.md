**Title:** The Ideal Leader

## What Makes an Ideal Leader?

The world celebrates leaders like Elon Musk for innovation, Barack Obama for eloquence, Steve Jobs for vision, Winston Churchill for resolve, and Nelson Mandela for moral courage. We admire Oprah Winfrey's influence, Jeff Bezos's business empire, and Taylor Swift's cultural sway. These leaders captivate the world through their achievements, presence, and power to move people.

But does the world's definition of leadership reflect what pleases God?

We've been studying 1 Timothy to understand church order—the roles of males and females, and their implications. Today we examine what Scripture says about leadership in the church.

## The Character of a Leader (1 Timothy 3:1-7)

### Leadership is a Noble Task

> "Here is a trustworthy saying: Whoever aspires to be an overseer desires a noble task." (1 Timothy 3:1)

Leadership in God's household is described as a **good work** (literally, "noble task")—not power, but service. Jesus modeled this: "The Son of Man did not come to be served, but to serve, and to give his life as a ransom for many" (Mark 10:45). Leaders wield power to serve and to do good work, not to elevate themselves.

### Understanding "Overseer" and "Elder"

The Greek term "episkopos" (overseer) comes from roots meaning "scope," "telescope," "microscope"—literally, "to see over" or supervise. From this word we derive "bishop" and "episcopal." This term is often interchangeable with "elder" (presbuteros).

Scripture shows this dual terminology:
- Acts 20:17, 28: Paul called for the elders of Ephesus, then referred to them as overseers of the flock
- Titus 1:5-7: An elder must be blameless; an overseer manages God's household

The two descriptions highlight complementary aspects:
- **Overseers** emphasize the supervisory function
- **Elders** typically suggests maturity and experience
- In church structures, these offices overlap significantly

### Essential Qualities for Leadership

The qualifications Paul lists apply to bishops, elders, and deacons alike, though with slight variations. These aren't exceptional standards—they're the baseline for Christian character.

#### **Above Reproach** (v. 2)

The Greek word literally means "not arrested" or "not taken hold of" (lambano). A leader must be blameless in reputation.

For deacons, Paul states it slightly differently: "They must first be tested; and then if there is nothing against them, let them serve as deacons" (1 Timothy 3:10). The sense is "not accused"—someone against whom no legitimate charge can be brought.

#### **Faithful to His Wife** (v. 2)

> "The husband of one wife" (mia gunaikos)

This phrasing is curious. If it simply meant "married," why not just say "a husband"? The specific wording suggests Paul is addressing two issues: polygamy and adultery. A leader must demonstrate covenant faithfulness in his most intimate relationship.

This qualification appears consistently:
- For overseers/elders (1 Timothy 3:2)
- For deacons (1 Timothy 3:12)

#### **Not Given to Drunkenness** (v. 3)

> "Not given to much wine"

Scripture calls all believers to this standard (Ephesians 5:18: "Do not get drunk on wine, which leads to debauchery. Instead, be filled with the Spirit"). Deacons similarly must not be "indulging in much wine" (1 Timothy 3:8).

#### **Not a Lover of Money** (v. 3)

The Greek word "aphilargorus" literally means "lover of silver." This reflects a broader biblical principle (Hebrews 13:5). Paul emphasizes this throughout 1 Timothy, warning against those who "want to be teachers of the law" but are motivated by dishonest gain.

Deacons face the same requirement: "not pursuing dishonest gain" (1 Timothy 3:8).

#### **Able to Manage His Own Household** (v. 4)

> "He must manage his own family well and see that his children obey him with proper respect."

The Greek word for "manage" (proistemi) literally means "to stand before" or "to preside." Paul uses this same term elsewhere to describe leadership roles (Romans 12:8; 1 Thessalonians 5:12; 1 Timothy 5:17).

The children must be "in submission"—the same word used in 1 Timothy 2:11. This isn't about tyranny; it's about a household ordered by wise, loving authority.

**Why is this so important?** Verse 5 provides the answer:

> "If anyone does not know how to manage his own family, how can he take care of God's church?"

This is the litmus test. A leader's character shows most clearly at home, not in public. The church is God's household, and a man who cannot lead his own household well has no business leading God's.

This principle cuts through idealism. We're tempted to impose theories and visions on others without testing them at home first. Kenny's observation: "We are very happy to impose our worldview on others. A hundred people in Canberra decide xyz is the way to do things. Do they have any empirical evidence? Maybe try it in Canberra first before imposing it on everyone else?" The test of character is real life, not rhetoric.

#### **Not a Recent Convert** (v. 6)

> "He must not be a recent convert, or he may become conceited and fall under the same judgment as the devil."

The Greek words paint a vivid picture:
- "Recent convert" (neophutos) literally means "new plant"
- "Conceited" means "puffed up" or filled with smoke—hollow pride
- "Fall" (empipto) means to perish or be ruined

The danger is spiritual pride. A new believer who is suddenly elevated to leadership may become arrogant, following the same path of pride that led Satan to judgment (diablos—the Greek term for devil, meaning "slanderer" or "accuser").

Paul references Hymenaeus and Alexander (1 Timothy 1:20) as cautionary tales of those who fell to Satan's influence.

Deacons face similar requirements: "They must first be tested; and then if there is nothing against them, let them serve as deacons" (1 Timothy 3:10).

#### **A Good Reputation with Outsiders** (v. 7)

> "He must also have a good reputation with outsiders, so that he will not fall into disgrace and into the devil's trap."

This passage is often misunderstood. It doesn't mean leaders need the world's approval. Jesus promised that "the world hates you" (John 15:18-19), and believers should expect persecution and slander (Matthew 5:11). Yet Paul also teaches that our conduct should be such that even our critics "may be ashamed of their slander" (1 Peter 3:16).

The phrase "good reputation from outsiders" (martyria—testimony) likely means the testimony outsiders give based on observing the leader's behavior. As Christians interact with the world, their integrity should be so clear that even skeptics cannot legitimately accuse them.

Kenny notes the interpretive options:
- A testimony from interactions with outsiders, as judged by Christian standards
- A reputation for being separated from worldly values
- Conduct so exemplary that even pagans recognize genuine goodness

Whatever the precise interpretation, the point is clear: a leader's character must withstand scrutiny from all quarters.

## The Distinguishing Mark of Elders/Overseers (v. 2)

### Able to Teach

The Greek word "didaktikos" is sometimes translated "skilled in teaching," which makes it unique in the list—a skill rather than a moral quality. But Kenny argues this misses the intent.

A better translation may be "of teachable character" or "one who holds and transmits sound doctrine." Titus 1:9 clarifies this:

> "He must hold firmly to the trustworthy message as it has been taught, so that he can encourage others by sound doctrine and refute those who oppose it."

The emphasis isn't on pedagogical skill or engaging delivery (though these help). The focus is on **holding firm to the truth** and passing it faithfully to others. An effective communicator who strays from truth is dangerous; a humble, faithful witness to Scripture is invaluable.

This matters because the church's core identity is tied to truth:

> "If I am delayed, you will know how people ought to conduct themselves in God's household, which is the church of the living God, the pillar and foundation of the truth." (1 Timothy 3:15)

The church stands as the pillar and foundation of truth. This is why teaching matters—the household of God exists to uphold and proclaim God's truth.

Throughout 1 Timothy, Paul addresses who may teach and who may not:
- 1 Timothy 1:3: prohibition against certain teachers
- 1 Timothy 1:7: false teachers who "want to be teachers of the law"
- 1 Timothy 2:7: Paul appointed as "teacher of the Gentiles"
- 1 Timothy 2:11: women not permitted to teach (in certain contexts)

All these references underscore that **truth-telling is the church's primary task**, and those who teach must guard it carefully.

The difference between elders and deacons, then, reflects maturity and experience. Deacons serve in practical, administrative roles; elders provide doctrinal leadership and spiritual oversight.

But consider Stephen's example (Acts 6). He was chosen as a deacon to "serve tables," yet he went on to preach and teach with power: "Stephen, a man full of God's grace and power, performed great wonders and signs among the people... They could not stand up against the wisdom the Spirit gave him as he spoke" (Acts 6:8, 10). The boundary between roles is not rigid; faithful service in a smaller capacity can lead to greater responsibility.

## These Qualities Apply to All of Us (1 Timothy 3:14-15)

> "Although I hope to come to you soon, I am writing you these instructions so that, if I am delayed, you will know how people ought to conduct themselves in God's household, which is the church of the living God, the pillar and foundation of the truth."

In fact, we are all leaders in some form or another. When we examine Paul's full list, every single qualification appears elsewhere in Scripture as a mandate for **all Christians**:

**Blameless:** "Give the people these instructions, so that no one may be open to blame" (1 Timothy 5:7)

**Faithful to one wife:** The principle of covenant fidelity stretches back to Adam and Eve (1 Timothy 2). Divorce is addressed across the Gospels and Epistles.

**Temperate, self-controlled, respectable:** "Teach the older men to be temperate, worthy of respect, self-controlled, and sound in faith, in love and in endurance" (Titus 2:2)

**Hospitable:** "Offer hospitality to one another without grumbling" (1 Peter 4:9)

**Not given to drunkenness:** "Do not get drunk on wine, which leads to debauchery. Instead, be filled with the Spirit" (Ephesians 5:18)

**Not violent or quarrelsome:** "I want the men everywhere to pray, lifting up holy hands without anger or disputing" (1 Timothy 2:8). "Be peaceable and considerate, and always be gentle toward everyone" (Titus 3:2)

**Not a lover of money:** "Command those who are rich in this present world not to be arrogant nor to put their hope in wealth... but to put their hope in God, who richly provides us with everything for our enjoyment" (1 Timothy 6:17)

**Able to manage household and children:** "Fathers, do not exasperate your children; instead, bring them up in the training and instruction of the Lord" (Ephesians 6:4). "Anyone who does not provide for their relatives, and especially for their own household, has denied the faith and is worse than an unbeliever" (1 Timothy 5:8)

**Do good deeds:** "Command them to do good, to be rich in good deeds, and to be generous and willing to share" (1 Timothy 6:18)

### The Connection Between Church and Home

There is a profound link between the household of faith and our own households. The church is God's household, and our homes are meant to reflect it. Just as God's household order should guide the church, so too should it guide family life.

Many men are not eligible to be elders in the church—but most Christian men (those who are married with children) are eligible to be elders in their own families. The family unit is, in essence, a microcosm of what the church should be. The head of the home should be a microcosm of Christ himself—servant-leader, truth-bearer, covenant-keeper.

This isn't a burden reserved for the clergy. All of us exercise leadership:
- Within the congregation today
- Within our own households later

We don't need to be charismatic, witty, or commanding. We simply need to **serve** and **teach**—to lead by example, grounded in character and truth.

*This is the ideal leader: not the world's hero, but the church's faithful servant.*
