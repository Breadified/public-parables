# Deuteronomy 5:12-33

**Title:** The Sabbath - Freedom and Rest in God

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Command

> Observe the Sabbath day by keeping it holy, as the Lord your God has commanded you. Six days you shall labor and do all your work, but the seventh day is a sabbath to the Lord your God. On it you shall not do any work, neither you, nor your son or daughter, nor your male or female servant, nor your ox, your donkey or any of your animals, nor any foreigner residing in your towns, so that your male and female servants may rest, as you do. (Deuteronomy 5:12-14)

The Sabbath command is straightforward: complete cessation from work one day each week. No labor, no cooking, no harvesting, no productivity. In Jewish practice, this extends to no driving, no pressing buttons on pedestrian crossings—a comprehensive rest from all forms of human effort.

## Why the Sabbath? The Context of Freedom

> Remember that you were slaves in Egypt and that the Lord your God brought you out of there with a mighty hand and an outstretched arm. Therefore the Lord your God has commanded you to observe the Sabbath day. (Deuteronomy 5:15)

The Sabbath law arrives after liberation. Under Pharaoh's rule, the Israelites endured unimaginable labor—24/7/365 without reprieve. The pressure, the stress, the feeling of making no progress while always working harder. For them, it was like an endless HSC examination with no graduation day in sight. Work consumed their entire existence.

Then God heard their cry. He sent Moses, delivered them through mighty plagues, and freed them. The Sabbath becomes God's way of saying: *You are no longer slaves. This is not Egypt. You have a different ruler now.*

## The Deeper Meaning: Trust in God

The Sabbath's true purpose emerges in the wilderness wandering. For forty years, God instructed the Israelites to gather food daily—but only six days per week. On the seventh day, they rested. God would provide enough. They couldn't stockpile or work harder to ensure security. They had to trust.

This was training in radical dependence. When a storm threatened an incomplete harvest, they trusted God. When a financial transaction loomed, they trusted God. When important work remained undone at sunset on Friday, they trusted God. The Sabbath taught them: *Everything does not depend on you. You are not responsible for making it all work.*

## What the Sabbath Teaches Us

**Rest from the illusion of self-sufficiency.** We cannot be perfectionists. We cannot accomplish everything. There are more important things in life than productivity and economic gain.

**Freedom from the slavery of work.** Labor, suffering, danger, and drudgery are not what we were created for. Life is not merely about food, economics, and accumulation. The Sabbath declares that greed and endless striving jeopardize our souls.

**Dependence on God alone.** The Sabbath principle is this: *Fully Rely on God.* We say we trust God intellectually, but true reliance only appears when we actually stop working. When we cease striving, we practice faith.

## Living the Sabbath Today

The literal Sabbath—as observed in Israel—has shifted. Jesus is our rest (Hebrews 4). The ultimate Sabbath awaits in heaven. Yet the principle remains eternally relevant.

We observe the Sabbath principle by:

- Setting aside one day each week for genuine rest, trusting God to sustain our lives and work
- Choosing to attend church or youth group instead of maximizing productivity—deliberately relying on God while others press forward
- Studying all week, then stepping back from cramming the night before an exam—trusting God with the outcome
- Passing up overtime or extra pay, choosing rest—relying on God for provision rather than our own labor
- Pausing in the midst of a busy day to remember we are freed from sin and its burden

Every time you intentionally stop, you observe the Sabbath. Every time you resist the urge to fill Sunday with work, you remember: *I am free. I do not carry this alone. My life does not depend on my productivity.*

This is the gift God offers those He has redeemed—not slavery to work, but the rhythmic reminder that we belong to Him.
