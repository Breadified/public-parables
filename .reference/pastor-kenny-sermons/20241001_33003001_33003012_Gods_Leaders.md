# Micah 3:1-12

**Title:** God's Leaders  
**Date:** 17 October 2024  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## The Question of Leadership

This week we've seen significant leadership transitions in the news—from the Archbishop of Canterbury's resignation over child abuse reporting to new cabinet appointments. These events raise a fundamental question: Who should be our leaders?

The prophet Micah addressed this very question in the 8th century BC, speaking primarily to Jerusalem. His prophecy warned of destruction coming to Jerusalem and Samaria, yet also promised future restoration. But the core of his message condemned the people of Judah—and particularly their leaders—for dishonesty and idolatry.

---

## 1. Bad Leaders: Justice as the Foundation

> Listen, you **leaders** of Jacob, you **rulers** of Israel. Should you not embrace **justice**, you who **hate good and love evil**?

This is the fundamental indictment. God's requirement for rulers is simple and singular: **justice**. Leaders have one job.

Throughout Scripture, this principle recurs:

> He has made you king to maintain justice and righteousness. (1 Kings 10:9)
>
> Maintain justice and do what is right for my salvation is close at hand. (Isaiah 56:1)
>
> Submit yourselves for the Lord's sake to every human authority: whether to the emperor, as the supreme authority, or to governors, who are sent by him to punish those who do wrong and to commend those who do right. (1 Peter 2:13-14)
>
> For the one in authority is God's servant for your good. But if you do wrong, be afraid, for rulers do not bear the sword for no reason. They are God's servants, agents of wrath to bring punishment on the wrongdoer. (Romans 13:4)

Justice here means restraining evil, upholding contracts, protecting the vulnerable, and maintaining the moral order reflected in God's law. It is **not** the government's role to provide education, healthcare, redistribute wealth, or engineer society. Justice means criminal accountability and civil order.

### The Horror of Exploitative Leadership

But Israel's leaders had twisted this responsibility:

> Who tear the skin from my people and the flesh from their bones; who eat my people's flesh, strip off their skin and break their bones in pieces; who chop them up like meat for the pan, like flesh for the pot?

This vivid imagery paints a picture of shepherds exploiting their flock. Ezekiel captures the same rebuke:

> Woe to you shepherds of Israel who only take care of yourselves! Should not shepherds take care of the flock? You eat the curds, clothe yourselves with the wool and slaughter the choice animals, but you do not take care of the flock. You have not strengthened the weak or healed the sick or bound up the injured. (Ezekiel 34:1-4)

The leaders were using their positions to extract wealth and power from those they governed. Taxation without justice becomes mere plunder—taking from people what is rightfully theirs through honest labor.

### The Danger of Mercenary Leadership

Beyond exploitation, Micah condemns corruption—leadership driven by money rather than principle:

> As for the **prophets** who lead my people astray, they proclaim 'peace' if they have something to eat, but prepare to wage war against anyone who refuses to feed them.

These aren't prophets of God; they're mercenaries. They say whatever produces income.

> Her leaders judge for a bribe, her priests teach for a price, and her prophets tell fortunes for money.

This is why Paul's qualification for Christian leaders is emphatic:

> The overseer is to be above reproach, faithful to his wife, temperate, self-controlled, respectable, hospitable, able to teach, not given to drunkenness, not violent but gentle, not quarrelsome, **not a lover of money**. (1 Timothy 3:1-3)

Money cannot be the motivation. The conviction of truth must drive ambition, not financial gain.

The church has historically recognized this danger. In the past, some churches funded ministers through glebe lands—property whose income paid clergy salaries independent of congregational giving. This freed preachers to teach difficult truths without fear of losing financial support. When that system failed and churches became dependent on giving, ministers could no longer speak against divisive issues like divorce, same-sex marriage, or cultural trends—they might offend donors.

The solution has taken several forms: glebe lands, stipends (living allowances distinct from wages), and most importantly, volunteer leadership. The pastors, Bible study leaders, and Sunday school teachers who serve without pay will receive a greater reward in heaven than those of us paid for our work.

As a paid minister myself, I'm acutely aware of this danger. It's why I intentionally take positions that may not please everyone. If my sermons only affirmed what people already believe, I'd know I was tickling their ears rather than preaching God's Word. My hope is to offend half the congregation each time I preach—hopefully not always the same half. If I don't, judgment is coming.

## 2. Judgment: God's Withdrawal

The consequence for bad leadership is severe:

> Then they will cry out to the Lord, but he will not answer them. At that time he will hide his face from them because of the evil they have done.

When leaders reject justice and exploit the people, God withdraws His protection. He hides His face—not in anger that responds, but in complete rejection that withdraws entirely.

The darkness is total:

> Therefore night will come over you, without visions, and darkness, without divination. The sun will set for the prophets, and the day will go dark for them.

Night, darkness, sunset, and gloom—layer upon layer of darkness metaphors. It's zero dark thirty for these prophets. But notice: it goes dark for them because God covers His face. The withdrawal is complete—no light, no guidance, no communication from God.

> The seers will be ashamed and the diviners disgraced. They will all cover their faces because there is no answer from God.

There's bitter irony here. The "seers"—those who should see—cannot see. The "diviners" who claimed to know the future are blind. They cover their mouths because they have nothing to say; God no longer speaks to them.

### The Terror of Silence

What's worse than arguing with your spouse? Her refusing to speak to you. Suddenly you're shut out, unable to understand what's wrong, powerless to fix the situation. This is what hell truly is—not merely pain and fire, but abandonment and silence:

> Let me not be put to shame, LORD, for I have cried out to you; but let the wicked be put to shame and be silent in the realm of the dead. (Psalm 31:17)

Dark. Silent. Alone. You leave God, and He gives you what you want: a world without Him and without His blessings. Even your own shadow leaves you in darkness. This is the severest punishment—not torture, but isolation.

### The Final Indictment

Micah then speaks directly to Jerusalem's leaders:

> Hear this, you leaders of Jacob, you rulers of Israel, who despise justice and distort all that is right; who build Zion with bloodshed, and Jerusalem with wickedness. Yet they look for the Lord's support and say, "Is not the Lord among us? No disaster will come upon us."

They want God's blessing while rejecting God's justice. But judgment is coming:

> Therefore because of you, Zion will be plowed like a field, Jerusalem will become a heap of rubble, the temple hill a mound overgrown with thickets.

This is precisely what happened. The Babylonians leveled Jerusalem. The temple was destroyed, and today the Al-Aqsa mosque stands on the ruins of the temple foundations.

## Prophetic Voice in Every Generation

Modern Christianity often resists prophetic critique of political leaders, insisting that church and politics don't mix. But the prophets were clear: we must speak to what leaders do. We must address their policies. We must call them to justice. That conversation will continue in the coming weeks, and yes, political figures will likely be mentioned again. The gospel demands nothing less.
