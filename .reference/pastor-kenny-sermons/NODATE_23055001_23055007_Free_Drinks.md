# Isaiah 55:1-7

**Title:** Free Drinks  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Bargains: When Benefits Exceed Cost

What makes a bargain truly great? When the benefits far outweigh what you pay. We love bargains—checking sites like Ozbargain, hunting at garage sales, finding rare items for a fraction of their original price. During university, a $2 gravy and rice in the cafeteria was a bargain when everything else cost $10. Even small wins count: $1 Coke slushies became available everywhere when McDonald's started offering them. These deals delight us because we get genuine value.

> Come, all you who are thirsty, come to the waters; and you who have no money, come, buy and eat! Come, buy wine and milk without money and without cost.

Isaiah speaks into a context of genuine need. Ancient Israel faced war, famine, and scarcity. Food and water were precious resources. The prophet's invitation is extraordinary: water, wine, milk—the finest things—all free. Not cheap. Not subsidized. Completely free.

## The Trap of Bad Bargains

But here's what Isaiah wants us to see:

> Why spend money on what is not bread, and your labor on what does not satisfy?

Bad bargains are everywhere. You pay good money for something that looks appealing—a crispy chip packet that's mostly air, an expensive gadget that breaks immediately, a fancy restaurant with tiny portions and a massive bill. You feel ripped off because the reality doesn't match the promise.

This pattern extends beyond physical goods. We chase things that never truly satisfy:

- **Wealth**: Money alone doesn't guarantee peace. James Packer has riches yet struggles with fulfillment.
- **Education**: Half of university students change their major—courses they fought hard to get into turn out to be wrong paths.
- **Travel**: Another temple in Japan, another church in Europe, another beach in Bali. More and more, but it never ends the searching.
- **Possessions**: Important today, worthless tomorrow—destined for the kerbside recycle bin.

Life is full of these bad bargains. Advertising agencies, corporations, and politicians want to enslave you as a consumer, always hungry for the next purchase. What a tragedy if you spend your entire life chasing counterfeit bread at twice the price, only to discover at the end that it wasn't even bread. That's the ultimate rip-off: paying and still not being satisfied.

When Isaiah compares these empty pursuits to God's genuine offer, shouldn't it make us angry at how we've been deceived?

## Your Soul Needs Real Food

But Isaiah isn't ultimately talking about physical hunger:

> Listen, listen to me, and eat what is good, and you will delight in the richest of fare. Give ear and come to me; listen, that you may live.

He's addressing your soul—that eternal part of you that thinks, reflects, yearns for meaning. Just as your body needs food, your soul needs spiritual nourishment. And your soul cannot be satisfied by food, drink, or physical comfort alone.

This is Maslow's hierarchy of needs applied to faith. Yes, we need safety, love, and esteem. But at the top is self-actualization—the need to find meaning, to understand purpose, to answer the question "Why?" We all intuitively sense that mere survival isn't enough. We want something transcendent.

Consider the evidence: Australians have never been wealthier, healthier, or more educated. Yet happiness eludes us. Since the 1950s, those reporting deep unhappiness have increased 20%. Depression is now so common we need a word for it—"emo." Family breakdown is rising. Suicide rates are climbing. We're among the top three wealthiest nations on earth, yet we're unhappy. The soul is thirsty for something money cannot buy.

Science textbooks, philosophy, man-made religions—these are all bad bargains when it comes to your soul. They're on sale, but they don't satisfy.

## The Better Bargain: Jesus as King

Into this spiritual hunger, God brings an extraordinary promise:

> I will make an everlasting covenant with you, my faithful love promised to David. See, I have made him a witness to the peoples, a ruler and commander of the peoples. Surely you will summon nations you know not, and nations you do not know will come running to you, because of the Lord your God, the Holy One of Israel, for he has endowed you with splendor.

This references the covenant God made with King David long ago—a promise of a land flowing with milk and honey, of prosperity and peace. But by Isaiah's time, David was dead. All Israel's kings were gone, exiled by Babylon. So Isaiah speaks of a new king coming—one whose influence and power would exceed even David's. And this king would offer the free food, the spiritual sustenance, that the soul desperately needs.

This king is Jesus.

When Jesus fed five thousand people with five loaves and two fish, it wasn't an accident that Mark describes the crowd sitting "in groups of hundreds and fifties"—language that evokes a king presiding over his people. The disciples protested: "That would cost eight months' wages!" But Jesus provided abundantly. Everyone ate and was satisfied. There were even leftovers. And it was free.

> Never again will they hunger; never again will they thirst. The sun will not beat upon them, nor any scorching heat. For the Lamb at the center of the throne will be their shepherd; he will lead them to springs of living water. And God will wipe away every tear from their eyes.

Jesus quenches the thirst of your eternal soul. Those who follow Him receive eternal life—a life no longer enslaved to the world's empty promises. He fills:

- Your hunger for truth
- Your longing for certainty
- Your need for meaning and purpose
- Your deep ache for love and forgiveness
- Your gut-level awareness that there's a God, that you've done wrong, and that you need reconciliation

## The Cost of Following Jesus

Many wonder: "What's the cost of being Christian?" What must I give up?

- Living a certain way
- Abandoning certain habits
- Committing to a belief system
- Surrendering autonomy to God

But here's the truth: the cost is simply forsaking things that don't satisfy. It's walking away from the bad bargains. That's not a cost—that's freedom.

When I was in year 11, from a non-churched background, what held me back was fear: "What if it's all wrong?" But life itself requires this kind of faith. We don't investigate our spouses before marriage. We don't check shopping centers for terrorists. We take small leaps of trust with people we barely know. Friendship grows through repeated leaps of faith, confirmed over time.

Christianity has something far better than blind faith: the best historical evidence from the ancient world. Multiple attestation, coherent accounts, superior to all other religions and philosophies. And I've been a Christian for twenty years—the evidence doesn't weaken; it strengthens. The rebuttals improve. If you're wavering, ask.

## The Bargain Is Time-Limited

> Seek the Lord while he may be found; call on him while he is near. Let the wicked forsake their ways and the unrighteous their thoughts. Let them turn to the Lord, and he will have mercy on them, and to our God, for he will freely pardon.

Notice the word "while"—it implies limited time. This offer has an expiration date. Like the McDonald's slushie deal, like any genuine bargain, it won't last forever.

Limited by when Jesus returns. Limited by how long you live on this earth. Phillip Hughes, the cricketer who died on the pitch when a ball struck his temple—his death reminds us that life is shockingly short. You may think you have plenty of time to decide. But you've already been given years. You've been exposed to Scripture classes. You hold a Gideon Bible. You're here now. Whether you came for friendship, for games, or because you're genuinely thirsty for God—make that decision today.

## Why This Bargain Is Real

Here's what makes salvation certain: **it's already been paid for**.

Yes, salvation is free for you and me. But it wasn't free. It wasn't free for Jesus.

Someone always pays for bargains. A company absorbs losses. Profits are sacrificed. But in this case, the price was paid in blood. The man who fed five thousand—who loved lavishly, who taught with authority, who embodied God's kingdom—was executed. Within a few years, He went willingly to the cross and paid the debt that should have been ours. He paid for the bad bargains we've chosen. He paid for our rebellion against God, against each other, against ourselves.

Because Jesus paid this price, you can accept salvation with absolute confidence. You will gain eternal life. The bargain is real. The offer stands.

But bargains don't last forever. Seek the Lord while He may be found. Call on Him while He is near. Today is the day to accept the ultimate bargain—spiritual life, eternal meaning, complete forgiveness—absolutely free.
