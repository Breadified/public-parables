# John 1:1-51

**Title:** The Word Made Flesh and the First Disciples

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Incarnation and Nature of the Word (1:1-18)

In the beginning was the Word (*logos*), and the Word was with God, and the Word was God. This distinctive phrasing expresses that Jesus is God without implying that Jesus and God the Father are the same person. He existed with God from the very beginning.

Through him all things came into being. Nothing that exists was made apart from him. This foundational claim establishes the Word's role in creation itself.

In him was life, and that life was the light of all mankind. The light shines in the darkness, and the darkness has not overcome it.

John the Baptist came as a witness to testify about this light, so that through his testimony all might believe. He himself was not the light—he came only as a witness to point people toward the true light.

The true light that enlightens everyone was coming into the world. This echoes Genesis 1:3, where God spoke light into existence. Though the world was made through him, the world did not recognize him. He came to that which was his own, but his own did not receive him.

Yet to all who did receive him, to those who believed in his name, he gave the right and authority to become children of God. These are born not of natural descent, nor of human decision or will, but born of God.

The Word became flesh and made his dwelling among us. The Greek word for "dwelling" (skene) evokes the tabernacle, suggesting the temporary nature of his earthly residence. We have seen his glory, the glory of the one and only begotten Son, who came from the Father, full of grace and truth. This glory is the manifestation of God's presence and character—the very image of the invisible God.

Out of his fullness we have all received grace in place of grace already given—grace upon grace. The law was given through Moses; grace and truth came through Jesus Christ. This marks the transition from the law (which was given through a mediator) to grace (which came through the personal presence of Christ himself—far more intimate and transformative).

No one has ever seen God, but the one and only begotten Son, who is himself God and is in the closest relationship with the Father, has made him known. Jesus is the best revealer of God because he is God's only begotten Son, uniquely positioned and authorized to reveal the Father to humanity.

## John's Testimony about Jesus (1:19-34)

The Jewish leaders in Jerusalem sent priests and Levites to ask John the Baptist directly: "Who are you?" John did not hesitate to confess clearly, "I am not the Messiah." When they asked if he was Elijah or the Prophet, he answered each question with equal clarity: "I am not."

Frustrated, they pressed him: "Who are you then? Give us an answer to take back to those who sent us. What do you say about yourself?" John replied in the words of the prophet Isaiah, "I am the voice of one calling in the wilderness, 'Make straight the way for the Lord.'"

The Pharisees continued questioning him: "Why then do you baptize if you are not the Messiah, nor Elijah, nor the Prophet?" John answered simply, "I baptize with water, but among you stands one you do not know. He is the one who comes after me, the straps of whose sandals I am not worthy to untie." This happened at Bethany on the other side of the Jordan, where John was baptizing.

The next day, John saw Jesus coming toward him and declared, "Look, the Lamb of God, who takes away the sin of the world! This is the one I meant when I said, 'A man who comes after me has surpassed me because he was before me.' I myself did not know him beforehand, but the reason I came baptizing with water was that he might be revealed to Israel."

John then gave his powerful testimony: "I saw the Spirit come down from heaven as a dove and remain on him. I myself did not know him, but the one who sent me to baptize with water told me, 'The man on whom you see the Spirit come down and remain is the one who will baptize with the Holy Spirit.' I have seen and I testify that this is God's Chosen One."

## The First Disciples (1:35-42)

The next day, John was standing with two of his disciples. When he saw Jesus passing by, he said again, "Look, the Lamb of God!" When the two disciples heard him, they followed Jesus. Turning around, Jesus saw them following and asked, "What do you want?" They replied, "Rabbi (which means Teacher), where are you staying?" Jesus said, "Come, and you will see." They went and saw where he was staying, and spent that day with him. It was about four in the afternoon.

Andrew, Simon Peter's brother, was one of the two who heard John and followed Jesus. The first thing Andrew did was find his brother Simon and tell him, "We have found the Messiah (that is, the Christ)." He brought Simon to Jesus, who looked at him and said, "You are Simon son of John. You will be called Cephas" (which is translated as Peter).

## The Call of Philip and Nathanael (1:43-51)

Jesus decided to go to Galilee and found Philip, saying to him, "Follow me." Philip, Andrew, and Peter were all from the same town, Bethsaida.

Philip found Nathanael and said to him, "We have found the one about whom Moses in the law and also the prophets wrote—Jesus, son of Joseph, from Nazareth." Nathanael responded skeptically, "Can anything good come from Nazareth?" Philip simply answered, "Come and see."

When Jesus saw Nathanael approaching, he said, "Here is truly an Israelite in whom there is no deceit." Nathanael asked, "How do you know me?" Jesus replied, "I saw you under the fig tree before Philip called you." Nathanael then declared, "Rabbi, you are the Son of God; you are the King of Israel."

Jesus responded, "Do you believe because I told you I saw you under the fig tree? You will see greater things than these." He continued, "Very truly I tell you, you will see heaven opened and the angels of God ascending and descending upon the Son of Man."

## Dynamics of Discipleship

Notice the different ways these disciples came to Jesus:

- **First Two Disciples**: John the Baptist sends his own disciples to follow Jesus
- **Andrew**: Named as one of the first two; brings his brother Simon (Peter) to Jesus
- **Simon Peter**: Brought by his brother Andrew
- **Philip**: Jesus finds and invites him directly: "Follow me"
- **Nathanael**: Philip tells him about Jesus, invites him to "Come and see"

This pattern differs from the accounts in Mark and Matthew, where Jesus calls two pairs of brothers (Peter and Andrew, James and John) as his first disciples, while Luke focuses primarily on Peter. John's Gospel emphasizes a more organic process of witness and personal invitation.

## Temporal Structure

Scholars note the deliberate progression of days in this passage:
- **Day 1**: The initial questioning of John (1:19, implicit)
- **Day 2**: "The next day"—John sees Jesus and testifies (1:29)
- **Day 3**: "The next day"—John points his disciples to Jesus (1:35)
- **Day 4**: Implied by the "four in the afternoon" hour (1:39)
- **Day 5**: "The next day"—Jesus calls Philip to go to Galilee (1:43)

Some scholars suggest this seven-day structure intentionally echoes the creation account, with John's testimony and Jesus's gathering of disciples representing a new creation. The phrase "On the third day" (2:1, referring to the wedding at Cana) serves a deeper purpose—connecting this discipleship narrative with the death and resurrection of Jesus (2:19-21; 20:1).
