# 1 Samuel 21:1-15

**Title:** The King on the Run
**Date:** 28 August 2022
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Deception in Desperation (21:1-15)

David has fled from Saul, knowing the king intends to kill him. He arrives at Nob, a Levitical city where the Ark of the Covenant and the tent of meeting—Israel's portable temple—have apparently been relocated. 

When Ahimelek the priest meets David alone and trembling, he immediately notices something wrong: Where is David's entourage? Where are his men? The commander arrives without his usual retinue, raising immediate suspicion.

### The First Deception

> And David said, "The king sent me on a mission and said to me, 'No one is to know anything about the mission I am sending you on.' As for my men, I have told them to meet me at a certain place. Now then, what do you have on hand? Give me five loaves of bread, or whatever you can find." (1 Samuel 21:2-3)

David's story is calculated to reassure the priest—a secret royal mission requires secrecy, his men are gathering separately. The request for bread seems modest. But David is deceiving Ahimelek to get what he needs without arousing suspicion.

### Breaking the Law for Survival

The priest hesitates. He has no ordinary bread available, only the consecrated bread—the showbread that sits before the Lord. According to the law given in Leviticus, this bread belongs exclusively to Aaron and his sons, to be eaten in the sanctuary.

> He answered, "I don't have any ordinary bread on hand; however, there is some consecrated bread here—provided the men have kept themselves from women." (1 Samuel 21:4)

Despite the law's restriction, Ahimelek gives David the consecrated bread. This violation appears in Jesus's teaching centuries later:

> Haven't you read what David did when he and his companions were hungry? He entered the house of God, and he and his companions ate the consecrated bread—which was not lawful for them to do, but only for the priests. (Matthew 12:3-4)

Jesus uses David's transgression to teach the Pharisees something crucial: the law itself has exceptions built in. The priests serve on the Sabbath and work when the law forbids work to others. The underlying principle is mercy and the preservation of life—values that supersede ceremonial law when they come into conflict.

### The Sword of Goliath

David's deception deepens. He asks Ahimelek for a weapon, claiming the king's urgent mission left no time to bring one.

> David asked Ahimelek, "Don't you have a spear or a sword here? I haven't brought my sword or any other weapon, because the king's mission was urgent." The priest replied, "The sword of Goliath the Philistine, whom you killed in the Valley of Elah, is here; it is wrapped in a cloth behind the ephod. If you want it, take it; there is no sword here but that one." (1 Samuel 21:8-9)

Ahimelek produces the sword of Goliath—an artifact of David's greatest triumph, now repurposed for his escape. David's mounting desperation is evident in his escalating deceptions.

### Pretending Madness

Armed and supplied, David flees to Gath—ironically, the hometown of Goliath himself. But there, the servants recognize him:

> "Isn't this David, the king of the land? Isn't he the one they sing about in their dances: 'Saul has slain his thousands, and David his tens of thousands'?" (1 Samuel 21:11)

Recognizing immediate danger, David responds with a third deception—this time physical rather than verbal:

> So he pretended to be insane in their presence; and while he was in their hands he acted like a madman, making marks on the doors of the gate and letting saliva run down his beard. (1 Samuel 21:13)

The king's military commander becomes a raving madman, scratching walls and drooling. The performance works. Achish, the king of Gath, dismisses him:

> "Look at the man! He is insane! Why bring him to me? Am I so short of madmen that you have to bring this fellow here to carry on like this in front of me? Must this man come into my house?" (1 Samuel 21:14-15)

David escapes alive.

### The Ethical Tension

Here we encounter a profound biblical question: What happens when God's laws collide? David breaks the law by eating consecrated bread. He deceives a priest. He lies to rulers. Yet Jesus validates his actions, and Scripture honors his survival.

This raises the critical question of moral priorities. The ninth commandment forbids bearing false witness, yet the law itself allows exceptions. Consider the examples Scripture and Christian theology have long recognized:

- **Corrie ten Boom** hiding Jews from the Nazis: She repeatedly lied to the Gestapo. Rather than condemning her, the Christian tradition celebrates her as a saint. She weighed two values—the prohibition against lying and the obligation to preserve innocent life—and correctly prioritized life.

- **Priests working on the Sabbath**: The law forbids work on the Sabbath, yet priests were assigned altar duties and God commanded Sabbath offerings. Some laws create their own exceptions.

- **Protecting the innocent**: A soldier wears camouflage. A woman threatened with rape may deceive her attacker. A whistleblower may conceal their identity to expose corruption.

Jesus teaches the principle directly:

> "If any of you has a sheep and it falls into a pit on the Sabbath, will you not take a hand to it and lift it out? How much more valuable is a person than a sheep! Therefore it is lawful to do good on the Sabbath." (Matthew 12:11-12)

### The Principle: Purpose Over Letter

The law is *telic*—it exists for a purpose. The Sabbath law exists to preserve rest and honor God. But when honoring the Sabbath would mean watching a sheep die, the purpose of the law overrides its letter. David's hunger and Ahimelek's conscience recognized that the showbread's deeper purpose—sustaining God's anointed—mattered more than its ceremonial restriction.

Similarly, Jesus quotes Hosea to the Pharisees: "I desire mercy, not sacrifice" (Matthew 12:7). The entire sacrificial system exists to express repentance and restore relationship with God. But if someone uses strict adherence to ritual law to condemn the innocent and reject the needy, they've inverted the law's purpose entirely.

### Important Boundaries

Yet this principle has clear limits. We do not live in an idealistic world where we can simply invoke "emergency exceptions" for personal convenience. Several guardrails matter:

**Genuine crisis, not preference**: There is a difference between Corrie ten Boom hiding Jews from death and lying to avoid minor consequences. There is a difference between a young woman resisting assault and an employee lying to avoid responsibility.

**Real sacrifice, not escape**: If we rebel against law or authority, we must bear genuine cost for doing so. Corrie ten Boom lost her father and sister in concentration camps. Her resistance was not a cheap escape clause but a costly commitment to justice. If someone breaks laws or deceives without consequence, they should ask themselves honestly whether self-interest rather than genuine good motivated the choice.

**Community consideration**: David deceives Ahimelek, and Ahimelek pays the price. Doeg the Edomite reports David to Saul, and in response, Saul orders the massacre of the priestly town of Nob—eighty-five priests and their families. David's survival comes at staggering cost to the innocent.

When we consider breaking laws or deceiving authorities—even for what we believe is right—we must think carefully about whom our actions might harm and whether we're truly willing to bear the consequences of our choices.

## Rebellion and Its Costs (22:1-23:29)

### Gathering the Desperate

> David left Gath and escaped to the cave of Adullam. When his brothers and his father's household heard about it, they went down to him there. All those who were in distress or in debt or discontented gathered around him, and he became their commander. About four hundred men were with him. (1 Samuel 22:1-2)

David establishes a base in the cave of Adullam and becomes a magnet for the desperate. Those with nothing to lose—debtors, the distressed, the discontented—rally to him. He becomes a commander of the dispossessed.

From there, he moves to Mizpah in Moab, seeking safety for his family. This is strategic: Moab was an enemy nation, but David apparently had family connections there through his ancestor Ruth, a Moabite. Saul has fought Moab, but David bets that the Moabites won't turn him over.

### Saul's Paranoia and Tyranny

Meanwhile, Saul becomes consumed with obsession. Seated with his spear in hand, surrounded by officials, he addresses his court:

> He said to them, "Listen, men of Benjamin! Will the son of Jesse give all of you fields and vineyards? Will he make all of you commanders of thousands and commanders of hundreds? Is that why you have all conspired against me? No one tells me when my son makes a covenant with the son of Jesse. None of you is concerned about me or tells me that my son has incited my servant to lie in wait for me, as he does today." (1 Samuel 22:7-8)

Saul's fear has turned to tyranny. He accuses his loyal followers of conspiracy. He focuses on what they won't gain—no fields, no vineyards, no military promotion—if David becomes king. Paranoia now rules the kingdom.

### The Massacre of Nob

Doeg the Edomite, Saul's chief shepherd, had been at Nob when David visited Ahimelek. He reports everything to Saul: David received bread, the sword of Goliath, and Ahimelek inquired of the Lord for him.

Saul summons Ahimelek and accuses him of conspiracy:

> "Why have you conspired against me, you and the son of Jesse, giving him bread and a sword and inquiring of God for him, so that he has rebelled against me and lies in wait for me, as he does today?" (1 Samuel 22:13)

Ahimelek protests his innocence:

> "Who of all your servants is as loyal as David, the king's son-in-law, captain of your bodyguard and highly respected in your household? Was that day the first time I inquired of God for him? Of course not! Let not the king accuse your servant or any of his father's family, for your servant knows nothing at all about this whole affair." (1 Samuel 22:14-15)

But Saul is beyond reason. He commands his guards to execute Ahimelek and all the priests of Nob. The guards refuse—they will not raise a hand against the priests of the Lord.

Then Saul turns to Doeg:

> The king then ordered Doeg, "You turn and strike down the priests." So Doeg the Edomite turned and struck them down. That day he killed eighty-five men who wore the linen ephod. He also put to the sword Nob, the town of the priests, with its men and women, its children and infants, and its cattle, donkeys and sheep. (1 Samuel 22:18-19)

An entire priestly town is massacred. Eighty-five priests and all their households—men, women, children, infants—are slaughtered. Only one escapes: Abiathar, Ahimelek's son, who flees to David.

When Abiathar arrives bearing news of the slaughter, David says:

> "That day, when Doeg the Edomite was there, I knew he would be sure to tell Saul. I am responsible for the death of your whole family. Stay with me; don't be afraid. The man who wants to kill you is trying to kill me too. You will be safe with me." (1 Samuel 23:11-12)

David acknowledges his culpability. His deception of Ahimelek, meant to give the priest plausible deniability, failed to protect him. The consequences of David's choices fell on the innocent.

### The Question of Righteous Rebellion

This narrative raises a theological and political question that has troubled Christian thinkers for centuries: Is rebellion against a tyrannical government justified?

Romans 13:1 states clearly:

> Let everyone be subject to the governing authorities, for there is no authority except that which God has established. The authorities that exist have been established by God.

Some have interpreted this to mean Christians can never resist government authority. But the verse itself must be read with attention to its purpose—to punish wrongdoing and reward righteousness. If a government perverts its purpose and becomes an instrument of evil rather than justice, the logic of the verse itself breaks down.

Political philosophy has long grappled with this. John Locke drew on the Old Testament story of Hezekiah's rebellion against the King of Assyria, arguing that "it is plain that shaking off a power which force, and not right, hath set over any one, though it hath the name of rebellion, yet it is no offence before God, but that which He allows and countenances."

Thomas Aquinas argued that a tyrant who uses power for personal gain rather than the common good actually commits "sedition" against the people. Overthrowing such a tyrant is not sedition but the restoration of order.

David's rebellion against Saul fits this pattern. Saul has become a tyrant—murdering innocent priests, consuming his people with paranoia, abusing his power. David is not rebelling for personal power but because Saul's tyranny has become unbearable.

### The Cost of Standing Against Evil

Yet rebellion against evil authority, however justified, comes at a price. David's resistance to Saul saves his own life but costs the lives of the priests of Nob. His conscience acknowledges this terrible calculus.

Later, Jonathan visits David in the desert and encourages him:

> "Don't be afraid," he said. "My father Saul will not lay a hand on you. You will be king over Israel, and I will be second to you. Even my father Saul knows this." (1 Samuel 23:17)

Jonathan recognizes that David's kingship is God's will. Yet Jonathan's own position as heir to Saul is sacrificed. Jonathan will die fighting alongside his father against the Philistines, never seeing David's kingdom established.

The cost of righteous resistance is not always paid by the one who acts. It is often borne by the innocent.

### Continuing Pursuit

The narrative that follows shows David constantly hunted. The Ziphites betray his location to Saul repeatedly. At one point, Saul closes in so tightly that David seems certain to be captured—but a messenger arrives with news of a Philistine raid, and Saul is forced to abandon his pursuit.

God protects David, but the protection comes through narrow escapes and constant flight, not through comfortable refuge. David lives in the wilderness strongholds—in caves and deserts, always moving, never at peace.

### The Moral Complexity

Scripture presents David's rebellion against Saul not as simple heroism but as a genuine moral complexity. David is right to flee from a tyrant. His cause is just. Yet his actions—his deceptions, his evasions—harm the innocent. Ahimelek dies. The priests of Nob are slaughtered. Abiathar loses his entire family.

The lesson is that standing against evil is sometimes necessary and right. But it is never cheap. It is never consequence-free. Anyone who considers resisting tyranny, exposing corruption, or defying unjust authority should do so with clear eyes: understanding the costs, accepting the risks, and being willing to bear the consequences of their choices rather than escaping them through further deception.

If one can resist evil and bear no cost, one should ask whether genuine principle or hidden self-interest motivated the choice.
