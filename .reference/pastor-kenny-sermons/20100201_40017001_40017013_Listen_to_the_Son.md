## The Glorious Son (17:1-9)

Jesus takes Peter, James, and John up a high mountain after six days. There, something extraordinary happens—Jesus is transfigured before them. His face shines like the sun, and his clothes become as white as light. Suddenly, Moses and Elijah appear, talking with Jesus.

> After six days Jesus took with him Peter, James and John the brother of James, and led them up a high mountain by themselves. There he was transfigured before them. His face shone like the sun, and his clothes became as white as the light. Just then there appeared before them Moses and Elijah, talking with Jesus. (Matthew 17:1-3)

Peter's response is immediate but confused. He wants to build three shelters—one for Jesus, one for Moses, and one for Elijah. But before he can finish speaking, a bright cloud covers them, and a voice speaks from the cloud:

> This is my Son, whom I love; with him I am well pleased. Listen to him! (Matthew 17:5)

The disciples fall facedown in terror, but Jesus touches them and says, "Get up. Don't be afraid." When they look up, they see only Jesus. As they come down the mountain, Jesus tells them not to tell anyone what they've seen until the Son of Man has been raised from the dead.

### Why This Scene Matters: Jesus as the Better Moses

To understand this passage, we need to look back to Exodus 24, where Moses encounters God on Mount Sinai with three companions. There are striking parallels:

- **Six days**: Both accounts mention six days before the seventh day when God speaks
- **Three companions**: Moses brings three others (Aaron, Nadab, Abihu) as Jesus brings three disciples
- **The mountain and the cloud**: Both ascents involve a mountain covered with a cloud
- **God's glory**: In both cases, God's glory is revealed

But Jesus is not simply repeating Moses' experience—he's surpassing it. This is the crucial point.

**Jesus shines more gloriously.** When Moses came down from Mount Sinai, his face was radiant from speaking with God (Exodus 34:29-35). But Jesus doesn't just have a shining face—his entire body shines with the brightness of the sun itself. His glory overflows.

**Jesus has a better relationship with God.** The voice from heaven doesn't declare Jesus to be a prophet. It declares him to be God's Son: "This is my Son, whom I love; with him I am well pleased." Jesus isn't merely God's messenger; he is God himself in human form.

**Jesus needs no tabernacle.** Peter suggests building three shelters, using the same word for dwelling or tabernacle (as in Exodus 25:8, where God commands a sanctuary to be built for him to dwell). But Jesus is God dwelling among us. As John later writes:

> The Word became flesh and made his dwelling among us. We have seen his glory, the glory of the One and Only, who came from the Father, full of grace and truth. (John 1:14)

Jesus is the better Moses. He brings a better message. He has a better relationship with the Father. He *is* God himself.

## The More Glorious Messenger: Jesus as Our Authority (17:1-5)

In Exodus 23, God speaks about sending an angel (messenger/prophet) ahead of the people to guard them and bring them to the promised land:

> Pay attention to him and listen to what he says. Do not rebel against him; he will not forgive your rebellion, since my Name is in him. If you listen carefully to what he says and do all that I say, I will be an enemy to your enemies and will oppose those who oppose you. (Exodus 23:20-22)

Jesus *is* that angel. He is the messenger we are called to listen to. And the promise remains: if we listen carefully to what he says and do all that he commands, we will find blessing and be brought to the promised land.

But notice what has changed. When the Israelites heard God's voice at Mount Sinai, they trembled in fear:

> Everyone in the camp trembled. (Exodus 19:16)

In Matthew 17, the disciples also fall in fear when they hear God's voice. But look at what happens next:

> But Jesus came and touched them. Get up, he said. Don't be afraid. (Matthew 17:7)

Jesus is not a distant, terrifying authority. He is a loving authority who comes near, touches us, and reassures us. He is an authority who serves.

### Jesus and the Authority of Culture

Today, we face competing authorities claiming our allegiance. Culture tells us what to believe about identity, morality, and truth. Science claims authority over questions of meaning. Media influences us constantly. Politicians shape our thinking. But the voice from heaven is clear: "Listen to him!"—not to celebrities, not to political leaders, not to the shifting opinions of culture, but to Jesus Christ as revealed in Scripture.

When culture dismisses Jesus or twists his teachings to suit its agenda, remember: the culture has an agenda. It may sound convincing, but it is not the ultimate authority.

When science oversteps its boundaries and claims authority over spiritual truth, remember: science is asking questions it cannot answer. Where do you learn your deepest convictions about morality, identity, and purpose? Not from any human institution, but from Jesus.

## The More Glorious Message: Grace and Truth (17:6-7)

The message Jesus brings is fundamentally different from the law that came through Moses. As John writes:

> For the law was given through Moses; grace and truth came through Jesus Christ. (John 1:17)

Jesus does not come to condemn us under the law. The law has already exposed our failure. Instead, Jesus comes with words of rescue. He comes to fulfill the law's requirements in our place. He is the truthful, righteous man who can represent us before God. And then, as our perfect representative, he pays for our failure—taking on himself the world's penalty and judgment. On the cross, he was condemned by the law so that we would not be.

This is why the disciples need not be afraid when they hear the Father's voice. This is why Jesus touches them and says, "Don't be afraid." He has already made a way for us.

The question then becomes: what will we do with such a master who has served us? With the Christ who denied himself and carried his cross?

> Listen to him. Follow me. (implied from Matthew 17:5 and the broader teaching of the Gospels)

## When We Hear Jesus' Voice: Three Wrong Responses (Application)

Yet we struggle to listen to Jesus. When we encounter his teachings—especially the difficult ones—we often respond in one of three ways:

### 1. Appeal to Ignorance

We skim quickly past hard passages or ignore them entirely. When we encounter something we don't like in Scripture, we mumble something about it being "too complicated" and carry on as before. We acknowledge that the Bible is true, but we're too lazy to really understand it.

### 2. Appeal to Culture

We insist that the Bible is true "for that age" but not for ours. We dismiss Jesus' teachings as outdated—on sexuality, on discipleship, on forgiveness—as though Christians have nothing to say to modern objections. We let culture define what makes sense rather than letting Scripture reshape our thinking.

### 3. Appeal to Exceptionalism

We say the Bible is true for everyone else but not for me. "I'm too young for this." "I'm too old for this." "This applies to that kind of person, not to someone like me." We qualify the Bible's authority until it means nothing to us or anyone else.

## A Better Way: Learning to Listen

When we encounter something in the Bible that seems strange or difficult, the solution is not to dismiss it but to learn from it.

We often think: *The Bible is weird. I am normal. The Bible must be wrong.*

But what if the reverse is true? What if the Bible is normal—the true standard of what life should be—and *I* am the weird one who doesn't conform to it?

When we realize that we are the problem, not Scripture, we are much more likely to make an effort to understand. After all, none of us wants to be weird.

When Jesus says, "Deny yourself and take up your cross and follow me," we must sit with this seriously. We must let his words affect us, even if they challenge us deeply. We don't have to immediately understand every implication, but we must be willing to change.

### On Hard Passages: An Example

Consider a passage like Matthew 18:8-9, where Jesus says:

> If your hand or your foot causes you to sin, cut it off and throw it away. It is better for you to enter life maimed or crippled than to have two hands or two feet and be thrown into eternal fire.

Many of us quickly dismiss this as hyperbole, cultural context, or something that "doesn't really apply." We may have good reasons for not taking it literally. But we often jump to this conclusion too fast, before we've let the passage affect us.

Better to pause and ask: What is Jesus really saying here? He's saying that sin is serious. God hates sin. Sin deserves judgment. What would it mean to cut off my hand? It would hurt. I would miss it. I would be disabled. And yet, for Jesus, losing a limb is better than facing judgment.

So the real application is not necessarily to cut off our hand, but to develop that same radical commitment to avoiding sin. Maybe it means canceling that internet subscription. Maybe it means stopping a harmful behavior. Maybe it means putting away the things that tempt us. All these responses flow from truly hearing what Jesus is saying—that sin matters, that our soul matters, and that nothing is worth the loss of our relationship with God.

## Listen to Jesus: He Alone Has the Words of Eternal Life

When the disciples come down the mountain, they see no one except Jesus. Moses and Elijah have disappeared. Symbolically, the message is clear: listen to Jesus alone. No one else has the words of eternal life.

What does it mean that Jesus is our authority?

It means that when we read Scripture, we are not seeking wisdom from culture, tradition, or popular opinion. We are listening to the Son of God who loved us and gave himself for us.

It means that when Jesus calls us to deny ourselves and follow him, we answer that call. We are not teenagers with attitude who need to be given permission by society to follow. We are disciples who recognize his authority and submit to it gladly.

It means that transformation becomes possible. Just as Jesus was transfigured on that mountain, so we—through faith in him, through listening to him, through following him—are transformed from one degree of glory to another.

**It's morphin time. Christ has died for the penalty of our sin. Christ has risen to give us new life. So transform. Morph into the image of Christ, whose authority is absolute and whose grace is inexhaustible.**
