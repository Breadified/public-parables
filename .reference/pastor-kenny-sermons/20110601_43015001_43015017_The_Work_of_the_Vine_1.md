# John 15:1-17

**Title:** The Work of the Vine
**Date:** 21 June 2011
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Growing Fruit

Our family started a vegetable patch this year, discovering that gardening produces both fruit and lessons. When Jesus teaches about vines and branches, He's inviting us into a gardening lesson about what ministry and leadership truly mean.

## The True Vine

> "I am the true vine, and my Father is the gardener." (John 15:1)

Jesus positions Himself as the central character in this passage. But what does it mean to be "the true vine"?

### The Connection to Israel (Psalm 80)

The vine is a common metaphor for ancient Israel. In Psalm 80, the psalmist recalls how God brought a vine out of Egypt, cleared the ground for it, and watched it take root and fill the land. The mountains were covered with its shade, and its branches stretched to the sea.

But then judgment came. The walls were broken down, enemies plundered it, and barbarians ravaged it. The psalmist cries out for God to restore the vine—specifically calling for God to raise up "the son of man" whom He has planted.

For centuries after the exile, God's people waited for this promised son. False messiahs came and went. Then Jesus appeared and said, "I am the true vine." This is the restoration they had been waiting for. Jesus is God Himself coming down from heaven—not just looking down, but actually descending to restore and bring salvation.

## The Branches: Jesus' Ministry Through His Word

> "You are already clean because of the word I have spoken to you." (John 15:3)

Jesus cleanses His disciples through His word. Like a gardener who prunes branches to make them more productive, Jesus' words cleanse and prune us to bear further fruit. He continues this ministry to every generation through the word spoken to us.

His words carry authority—the authority to forgive, to reconcile, to cleanse. This authority is backed by His atoning sacrifice. When we receive His word, we receive a word of pardon that transforms us.

### The Centrality of Jesus' Word in John's Gospel

Throughout the Gospel of John, everything centers on listening to and receiving Jesus' word:

- **John 1:1, 14**: "In the beginning was the Word, and the Word was with God, and the Word was God... The Word became flesh and made his dwelling among us."

- **I Am Declarations**: Each reveals Jesus' identity and our dependence on His word:
  - "I am the true bread"—we live by every word from God
  - "I am the true light"—His words guide us to do the Father's work
  - "I am the Gate"—His words divide people and call for decision
  - "I am the Good Shepherd"—we listen to His voice
  - "I am the Resurrection and Life"—we believe His word and live
  - "I am the Way"—He speaks the Father's words, and the Father's work is accomplished through them

### The Content of Jesus' Word

Jesus taught about the kingdom of God, about God's true character, about judgment and eternity, about His own death and resurrection. He revealed how God actually is—the truth about reality itself. When Jesus challenged His disciples' loyalty, they could only respond: "Lord, to whom shall we go? You have the words of eternal life" (John 6:68).

### The Authority of God's Word

Scripture is our ultimate authority—not popes or bishops, not cultural trends, not personal experience alone, but God's word. We must be careful not to use the Bible merely to validate what we already want to believe. The word of God should challenge us, correct us, and transform us.

## Remain in Me

> "Remain in me, and I will remain in you. No branch can bear fruit by itself; it must remain in the vine. Neither can you bear fruit unless you remain in me." (John 15:4-5)

Branches depend completely on the vine for life and fruitfulness. We must remain connected to Jesus through:

- **Reading God's word** regularly and personally
- **Staying engaged** in church and Bible teaching—not as a newcomer's activity, but as essential for leaders and mature believers
- **Obeying His commands**—not just theoretically understanding we are "word people," but practicing obedience

> "If anyone does not remain in me, he is like a branch that is thrown away and withers; such branches are picked up, thrown into the fire and burned." (John 15:6)

Without remaining in Jesus, we cannot bear fruit. We become useless.

## Bearing Fruit: Love One Another

> "My command is this: Love each other as I have loved you. Greater love has no one than this, that he lay down his life for his friends." (John 15:12-13)

Remaining in Jesus and bearing fruit are two sides of the same coin. To remain is to bear fruit. To bear fruit is to follow His commands. To follow His commands is to love one another. This is not a sequence but a unified whole.

The practical summary of all Jesus teaches is this: **love others as He has loved us**—with the cleansing, transformative word of God.

## Ministry as Vine Work, Not Trellis Work

True ministry focuses on the vine—the word of God and genuine relationships—not on the trellis, which represents organization and logistics.

When we engage in ministry, we:
- Welcome people genuinely
- Follow up with care
- Ask real questions
- Build authentic friendships, not superficial ones
- With Christians: hold each other accountable and encourage Bible reading and prayer
- With non-Christians: have wise conversations that lead them toward repentance

This is hard work. It's awkward. It requires genuine love and commitment.

**Three crucial cautions:**

1. **Don't become so preoccupied with organizing that you forget to remain in Jesus yourself.** Leaders must feed on God's word. Don't assume the teaching is only for newcomers.

2. **Don't become so preoccupied with organizing that you forget the word.** Ministry without Scripture is empty activity.

3. **Don't become so preoccupied with organizing that you forget the people.** Know their names. Have genuine conversations. Invite them warmly.

## Commitment and Faithfulness

Remain in Jesus' love through commitment and faithfulness. While there are unlimited options for where to spend time, there are not unlimited options for love. Love requires rootedness—faithfulness to the same church, the same Christian community, week in and week out.

Start now, in your own church and local Christian group. Don't wait until summer to practice these habits. Grow disciples in your own backyard. Be faithful, be present, be available to the people God has placed around you.

This is vine work—bringing God's word into individuals' lives and bearing fruit that will last.
