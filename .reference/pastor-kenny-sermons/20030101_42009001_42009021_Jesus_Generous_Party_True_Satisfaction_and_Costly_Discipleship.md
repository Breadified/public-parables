# Luke 9:1-21

**Title:** Jesus' Generous Party: True Satisfaction and Costly Discipleship
**Date:** January 2003
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction: What Makes a Party Successful?

Happy New Year! As we begin 2003, let me ask: How do you evaluate whether a party is successful? Perhaps by the people who show up? Or by the quality and abundance of food?

I once organized a New Year's Eve party with a friend. We prepared food, board games, and videos—but unfortunately, only three of us showed up. My friend, myself, and one other person who then left for the fireworks. It was quite the gathering.

Today we're going to look at another party story in Scripture, and we'll ask the same question: Was it a success or a failure?

## A Successful Party: The Feeding of the Five Thousand

> When Jesus had called the Twelve together, he gave them power and authority to drive out all demons and to cure diseases, and he sent them out to proclaim the kingdom of God and to heal the sick. (Luke 9:1-2)
>
> So they set out and went from village to village, proclaiming the good news and healing people everywhere. (Luke 9:6)
>
> When the apostles returned, they reported to Jesus what they had done. Then he took them with him and they withdrew by themselves to a town called Bethsaida, but the crowds learned about it and followed him. He welcomed them and spoke to them about the kingdom of God, and healed those who needed healing. (Luke 9:10-11)

Picture this scene: crowds upon crowds of people following Jesus—imagine cars after cars streaming into a remote location. And what does Jesus do? Like a gracious host, he welcomes them all, speaking about the kingdom and healing those who needed it.

### Reason One: People Showed Up

The disciples had been sent out to proclaim the kingdom of God, and their message brought results. Over five thousand men arrived—and if we include families, the number was far greater. These were real people, responding to the invitation to encounter Jesus.

### Reason Two: They Were Satisfied

> Late in the afternoon the Twelve came to him and said, "Send the crowd away so they can go to the surrounding villages and countryside and find food and lodging, because we are in a remote place here." (Luke 9:12)

As evening approached and hunger set in, the disciples wanted to send everyone away. But Jesus had a different solution:

> He said to his disciples, "Have them sit down in groups of about fifty each." The disciples did so, and everyone sat down. Taking the five loaves and the two fish and looking up to heaven, he gave thanks and broke them. Then he gave them to the disciples to distribute to the people. They all ate and were satisfied, and the disciples picked up twelve basketfuls of broken pieces that were left over. (Luke 9:14-17)

It's a miracle. The disciples had only five loaves and two fish—an impossible situation. Yet Jesus provided abundantly.

### The Exodus Connection

Luke connects this miracle to Israel's wilderness experience. Notice several parallels:

- **Groups of fifty:** In Exodus 18, Moses organized people under leaders "over thousands, hundreds, fifties and tens."
- **The word "satisfied":** In Egypt, the people complained about the food until God provided manna in the desert—and here, Jesus' crowd is truly satisfied.
- **Looking to heaven:** Jesus' posture mirrors the thanksgiving of God's people in the wilderness.
- **Bread and fish, not just bread:** This is even more generous than the manna of old—now there's an abundance of seafood as well.

I remember once being at Sydney Harbour on Boxing Day, watching the start of the Sydney to Hobart yacht race. They had set out a wonderful buffet. It was truly satisfying. But nothing—absolutely nothing—compares to what Jesus can provide.

### Application: True Satisfaction

Jesus offers more than food for a day. He offers a picture of food that satisfies eternally. You can grow tired of even the finest seafood. There's only so much the human body can consume. But Jesus offers something far deeper.

True satisfaction is not physical alone, but spiritual and emotional. It comes when our brokenness is healed and we are made complete. This can only come through Jesus.

Consider Mark Zuckerberg: when asked about his faith, he acknowledged that his billions, his seemingly perfect family, and his thriving company aren't enough to satisfy him. There's something more out there. He's halfway there—recognizing the hunger in the human soul. Perhaps one day he'll realize that Jesus truly satisfies.

## A Party Failure: The Problem of Rejection

Yet there's a hint that this party is not entirely successful:

> They all ate and were satisfied, and the disciples picked up twelve basketfuls of broken pieces that were left over. (Luke 9:17)

Twelve basketfuls of leftovers. Why?

### The Question of Why Leftovers?

One might think Jesus simply over-catered. But God's miracles aren't mistakes. When God provided manna in the desert, it satisfied completely—enough and no more. This leftover food suggests something else: perhaps not everyone who should have come actually came.

The explanation follows immediately:

> Once when Jesus was praying in private and his disciples were with him, he asked them, "Who do the crowds say I am?" They replied, "Some say John the Baptist; others say Elijah; and still others, that one of the prophets of long ago has come back to life." "But what about you?" he asked. "Who do you say I am?" Peter answered, "God's Messiah." Jesus strictly warned them not to tell this to anyone. And he said, "The Son of Man must suffer many things and be rejected by the elders, the chief priests and the teachers of the law, and he must be killed and on the third day be raised to life." (Luke 9:18-22)

The crowds were divided in their opinions about Jesus. They saw signs and wonders, but many didn't truly understand who he was. And those who rejected his message? Jesus had given the disciples clear instruction:

> If people do not welcome you, leave their town and shake the dust off your feet as a testimony against them. (Luke 9:5)

### The Party Killer: The Cost of Discipleship

But the real reason people wouldn't come fully into the party is revealed in verse 23:

> "Whoever wants to be my disciple must deny themselves and take up their cross daily and follow me." (Luke 9:23)

To be a Christian, to be Jesus' disciple, requires self-denial and a willingness to suffer. "Take up your cross" means to die. "Daily" means to die slowly. "Follow me" means to die the way Jesus died—with sacrificial love for the welfare of others.

Who wants to do that? It sounds like an invitation to join a loser's party—something no one wants to be associated with. The way is narrow. Being Christian is hard. Not everyone came, and not everyone will come.

## An Optimistic Party: The Promise of Eternal Satisfaction

Yet this is only bad if we think short term:

> "For whoever wants to save their life will lose it, but whoever loses their life for me will save it. What good is it for someone to gain the whole world, and yet lose or forfeit their very self? Whoever is ashamed of me and my words, the Son of Man will be ashamed of them when he comes in his glory and in the glory of the Father and of the holy angels." (Luke 9:24-26)

The promise is stunning: when Jesus comes in his glory and the glory of the Father and the holy angels, there will be a far greater party than anything we experience today. Long-term perspective changes everything. The sacrifice of following Jesus now is nothing compared to the eternal satisfaction we'll experience then.

### The Twelve Baskets: A Symbol of Welcome

Perhaps the twelve basketfuls also point to something encouraging. If twelve represents the twelve tribes of Israel, then the leftover food symbolizes God's generosity: there's room for more. Lots more. A new people. Our God is generous. All are welcome. There is more than enough to satisfy everyone.

### Our Mission: Sharing the Gospel

As we begin this year, our mission is clear: we will learn more about Jesus—not merely for our own satisfaction, but to use that knowledge to tell others about him. Our purpose in life is not self-centered. It's for the welfare of others. We're called to share the gospel with our children, our parents, our families, our friends, our colleagues, and strangers.

## Conclusion

It's wonderful to be here with you as we begin the new year, gathering together outdoors in this lovely part of God's creation.

We are not five thousand, I am not Jesus, and this food didn't just appear from nowhere—it came through the hard work of a dedicated team. But the hope is the same. Jesus is the one who truly satisfies his people, and he desires more to benefit from his sacrifice.

As we gather today and long for his kingdom to come, may this moment invigorate us. Let us renew our commitment and carry out this mission together throughout this year.
