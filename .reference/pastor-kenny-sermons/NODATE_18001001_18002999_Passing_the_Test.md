# Job 1-2

**Title:** Passing the Test

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction

The book of Job presents one of Scripture's most profound explorations of suffering, faith, and God's character. As we open this narrative, we encounter a man of extraordinary spiritual maturity facing trials that would test everything he believes about God's goodness and justice.

## Job's Character and Circumstances

Job was a man of remarkable integrity—blameless and upright, feared God and turned away from evil. His wealth reflected God's blessing: thousands of livestock, numerous servants, and a large family. Yet none of these external markers defined his true wealth. Job's genuine riches lay in his relationship with God and his faithful obedience.

His children gathered regularly for celebrations, and Job's pastoral concern for their spiritual lives extended even to his worry about their hearts. He made burnt offerings on their behalf, concerned that they might have sinned in their revelry. This reveals a man whose faith penetrated beyond surface obedience into genuine spiritual care for those entrusted to him.

## The Heavenly Council and Satan's Challenge

Scripture unveils a reality often hidden from our eyes: the throne room of God, where supernatural forces gather. Satan approaches with a specific accusation—that Job serves God only because of protection and blessing. "Does Job fear God for nothing?" Satan challenges. Remove the hedges of protection, he argues, and Job's faith will crumble.

This is not a trial Job invited or deserved. It emerges from a cosmic conflict where Satan tests the reality of human faith. Yet even in this test, God maintains parameters: Satan may touch Job's possessions and family, but not his life.

## The First Trial: Loss of All Things

In a single day, Job loses everything—his livestock, his servants, and most devastatingly, all ten of his children. Four messengers arrive in succession, each bearing news of catastrophic loss. A fifth blow comes as he learns his children have been killed in a great wind that collapsed the house where they gathered.

In that moment of unimaginable grief, Job's response becomes the foundation of the book's teaching: he tears his robe, shaves his head—the traditional expressions of mourning—and falls to the ground in worship. His declaration echoes across the centuries: "The Lord gave and the Lord has taken away; may the name of the Lord be praised."

This is not the response of a man whose faith was merely transactional. This is genuine reverence—the acknowledgment that Job owns nothing ultimately, that all belongs to God, and that God's character remains trustworthy even in tragedy.

## The Second Trial: Physical Affliction

The test deepens. Satan returns, claiming that Job will renounce God if his own body is attacked. God grants the second trial, and Job is struck with painful boils from head to foot. He sits among ashes, scraping his skin with broken pottery, immobilized by suffering.

Even his wife urges him to curse God and die—to end his suffering through rebellion against God. Yet Job maintains his integrity: "Shall we accept good from God, and not trouble?" His faith transcends the comfort of health. He does not claim to understand why suffering has come, but he refuses to surrender his conviction that God is good.

## What This Teaches Us

The book of Job fundamentally rejects the assumption that righteousness always produces prosperity or that suffering indicates divine displeasure. Job's trials come not because of hidden sin but precisely because his faith is real and worth testing.

True faith is not faith in blessings. True faith is loyalty to God himself, maintained even when every external support is stripped away. This is what makes Job's testimony so powerful and his subsequent questions so valid—he comes to God not as an outsider but as one whose faith has been forged in the fires of genuine trial.
