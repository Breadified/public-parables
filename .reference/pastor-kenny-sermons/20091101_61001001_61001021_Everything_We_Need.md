# 2 Peter 1:1-21

**Title:** Everything We Need
**Date:** 23 November 2009
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Everything We Need for Life (1:1-4)

The Christian life can feel like we're lacking something essential. We often sense a gap—powerlessness, insufficiency, a missing piece. But Peter opens his letter with a radical claim: we have *everything* we need.

> His divine power has given us everything we need for life and godliness through our knowledge of him who called us by his own glory and goodness. (1:3)

Think of MacGyver—the resourceful secret agent who refuses to carry a gun yet escapes every impossible situation armed with nothing but a Swiss pocket knife and duct tape. He finds what he needs wherever he is: ammonia in a laundry, bullets in a jail cell, an egg in a radiator. The name itself became a verb: "to MacGyver" means to improvise extraordinary solutions from ordinary resources.

In the same way, we come to our Christian life with everything we need. But what exactly have we been given?

### Understanding the Parallel Structure

Verses 2, 3, and 4 operate in parallel, which helps us understand what Peter means:

**Verse 2:** Grace and peace be yours in abundance through the knowledge of God and of Jesus our Lord.

**Verse 3:** His divine power has given us everything we need for life and godliness through our knowledge of him who called us by his own glory and goodness.

**Verse 4:** Through these he has given us his very great and precious promises, so that through them you may participate in the divine nature and escape the corruption in the world caused by evil desires.

When we align these verses, we see what we've been given: the gospel itself—the good news that God will save us from the coming judgment. Jesus swapped places with us on the cross. We deserved that death, but he took our place. His righteousness became ours. This is grace. This is peace with God. We receive both in abundance.

These are the very great and precious promises of the Old Testament, now fulfilled in Christ. This is everything we need.

Jim Elliott said it beautifully: "He is no fool to give up what he does not have to gain what he cannot lose."

### Applying the Gospel as Sufficiency

It's a strange concept to grasp in our materialist, hedonistic world. Every magazine, billboard, and advertisement tells us we don't have enough. We need the latest iPhone, the newest game, more money. Yet Peter insists the gospel is all we need for life.

Consider this: Christians in many countries survive without food, without clothes, without peace. The essentials we think we need aren't always necessary. And even if our earthly life ends, we have "a rich welcome" into our great inheritance in heaven. The gospel is truly sufficient.

## Everything We Need to Know (1:3)

How do we first receive this gospel? Through knowledge.

> His divine power has given us everything we need for life and godliness through our knowledge of him who called us by his own glory and goodness. (1:3)

Peter emphasizes knowledge repeatedly throughout this chapter:

- "Grace and peace be yours in abundance through the knowledge of God and of Jesus our Lord" (1:2)
- "They have escaped the corruption of the world by knowing our Lord and Saviour Jesus Christ" (2:20)
- "So I will always remind you of these things, even though you know them and are firmly established in the truth you now have" (1:12)

### Knowledge Is Good

Here's what matters: the gospel comes to us through knowledge. God has not left us in the dark. He takes the initiative. God sends messengers—teachers, Bible studies, pastors, Sunday schools. Through Scripture and faithful instruction, we learn the gospel.

This means Christianity is not anti-intellectual. We are learners. We are seekers of knowledge. It is entirely proper for our churches to have Bible studies, talks, and Sunday schools. It is essential that church leaders be teachers.

Yet these are the very things our churches are pressured to dilute. In our zeal to be "relevant" and "exciting," have we forgotten how we truly know God? If you're heading into a long holiday, don't let your mind go cold. Keep your brain warm, your mind sharp. Keep studying. Keep learning.

### Addressing Objections

Some argue that God is bigger than knowledge and books, so constant study isn't proper. They say God reveals himself through dreams and other ways, placing knowledge as secondary. But this attacks the sufficiency of knowing the gospel.

Head knowledge alone is insufficient—*but* this knowledge has a purpose. It's not meant to remain abstract. It leads to action, to godliness, to a transformed life.

## Everything We Need to Do (1:5-11)

If we have everything we need and everything we need to know, then what? We have everything we need to *do*.

> For this very reason, make every effort to add to your faith goodness; and to goodness, knowledge; and to knowledge, self-control; and to self-control, perseverance; and to perseverance, godliness; and to godliness, brotherly kindness; and to brotherly kindness, love. For if you possess these qualities in increasing measure, they will keep you from being ineffective and unproductive in your knowledge of our Lord Jesus Christ. (1:5-8)

### The Purpose of Knowledge

Knowledge leads to godliness. And godliness—being like God in holiness and character—is something every Christian can pursue, regardless of education or circumstances. The poor, the uneducated, the forgotten can all be godly because all have the gospel.

When Peter speaks of "participating in the divine nature," he's not describing some mystical power infused into us. It's not theosis or a "God gene." It means being godly—reflecting God's love and care, his character and holiness. Not his omnipotence, but his compassion.

### Make Every Effort

The emphasis throughout these verses is urgent: "Make every effort" (1:5) and "be all the more eager" (1:10).

The list reads like a spiritual multiplier effect:
- Add goodness to faith
- Add knowledge to goodness
- Add self-control to knowledge
- Add perseverance to self-control
- Add godliness to perseverance
- Add brotherly kindness to godliness
- Add love to brotherly kindness

Note that in verse 5, "add knowledge" (not "full knowledge") follows goodness. We already have full knowledge of the gospel—now we add practical understanding, wisdom, application of what we know.

### An Illustration: Unproductive Land

I have a small square backyard that, for two years, was completely unproductive. It was overgrown with noxious weeds and stubborn stumps, tangled with dead vines and broken lighting wires. There was a small pond with nothing but mosquitoes and spiders. My children couldn't play there safely. I couldn't grow vegetables. It was wasted space.

During a two-week break, I spent countless hours cutting down trees, removing 30 stumps with a 200-kilogram stump grinder, and breaking the compacted earth. I'm still working on it, but now it has purpose. My children can play safely. The backyard is becoming productive.

Similarly, don't be unproductive in the knowledge you have of the gospel. You know the good news. Now *use it*. Make every effort to be godly.

### Practical Application: Holiday Godliness

Many of you are heading into the longest holiday of your lives. Holidays are perfect opportunities for godliness—especially when time is no longer an excuse.

Before, you might have said: "I can't look out for people; I'm busy." "I can't commit to youth group; I have my HSC." "I can't serve; I have an assignment." Now you have time. So ask yourself: are you using it to be godly?

**Attend church regularly.** Don't let holidays become an excuse to skip. Yes, there are birthday parties, farewells, celebrations, and schoolies—one by itself is nothing. But they add up silently until you're there only half the summer. Make every week count. Show up regularly, not just for yourself, but to welcome strangers, to follow up with others, to care for your community. Being godly means being present.

If you're inconsistent—two weeks in, one week out—how will you add knowledge? Imagine treating tutoring, school classes, or sports training that way. If you truly believe the gospel, then commit to it. Holidays don't mean saying yes to unimportant things. They mean saying yes to important things: to love, to brotherly kindness, to knowledge.

**See your life as an outlet for godliness, not self-gratification.** Your summer agenda might include Xbox, movies, a new job, getting your license, visiting places, schoolies. But God's list is different:

- Add goodness
- Add knowledge
- Add self-control
- Add perseverance
- Add godliness
- Add brotherly kindness
- Add love

### The Promise

> Therefore, my brothers, be all the more eager to make your calling and election sure. For if you do these things, you will never fall, and you will receive a rich welcome into the eternal kingdom of our Lord and Savior Jesus Christ. (1:10-11)

## Living the Truth You Know

Here's the challenge: we don't believe we have everything we need. We say:

- "I'll be godly if only I had more money"
- "If only I felt more secure"
- "If only I had more time"
- "If only my parents were different"
- "If only my church were better"
- "If only my friends were more supportive"

We're always looking for formulas, shortcuts, reasons to delay. But we have everything we need *right now*.

Be like MacGyver. Stop whinging and get on with the job. You're stuck in a situation? Look at the wonderful provision you've been given. You can be godly because you have the knowledge of the gospel.

This is the entire point of Second Peter. This is why we gather to study Scripture. This is the invitation before you this holiday season: see what you have, stop making excuses, and live accordingly.
