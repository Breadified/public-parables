# Ecclesiastes 11:1–12:14

**Title:** Eternity and the End  
**Date:** June 2, 2014  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Be Bold (11:1–6)

Solomon's message to the young is clear: be bold in your actions. This doesn't mean recklessness, but rather taking calculated risks despite uncertainty.

> Cast your bread upon the waters, for after many days you will find it again. (11:1)

This phrase speaks to bold action—whether it means sending grain across the seas for trade, practicing generosity, or embracing the principle of sowing and reaping. The context of verse 6 ("Sow your seed in the morning, and at evening let not your hands be idle") suggests Solomon is urging us to act decisively even when conditions aren't perfect.

### Living with Uncertainty

The passage repeats the phrase "you do not know" four times, emphasizing the reality we all face:

> Give portions to seven, yes to eight, for you do not know what disaster may come upon the land. (11:2)

> If clouds are full of water, they pour rain upon the earth. Whether a tree falls to the south or to the north, in the place where it falls, there will it lie. (11:3)

> As you do not know the path of the wind, or how the body is formed in a mother's womb, so you cannot understand the work of God, the Maker of all things. (11:5)

> Sow your seed in the morning, and at evening let not your hands be idle, for you do not know which will succeed, whether this or that, or whether both will do equally well. (11:6)

We cannot predict outcomes. We don't know if the wind will bring rain or destruction, if our efforts will succeed or fail, or when life will change unexpectedly. Yet Solomon reminds us:

> Whoever watches the wind will not plant; whoever looks at the clouds will not reap. (11:4)

**Three principles flow from this reality:**

**First, act even though conditions are not ideal.** Perfect circumstances rarely arrive. We often delay our work—our homework, our piano practice, our responsibilities—waiting for the perfect temperature, mood, or moment. But there is never a truly good time. Adulthood means acting despite imperfect conditions.

**Second, act even though you are uncertain of results.** This is especially hard for those with perfectionistic tendencies. We want to research everything, check all possibilities, guarantee success before we begin. But life doesn't work that way. The rain falls where it falls. The tree falls where it falls.

**Third, plan and mitigate risks.** This doesn't mean making foolish decisions. Just because our plans sometimes fail doesn't mean we shouldn't plan. We make good decisions—bring an umbrella, not stay home all winter. We diversify—"Give portions to seven, yes to eight." But after we've done our best with limited time and knowledge, we step forward in boldness.

> For God will bring every deed into judgment, including every hidden thing, whether it is good or evil. (12:14)

God holds us accountable for our choices. Gambling leads to loss. Extreme risk-taking leads to injury. Neglecting studies leads to failure. We are called to make good, godly decisions in conformity with God's commandments. This is our duty. We do this duty even with no guarantee of success, but by making good decisions, we already increase our odds.

## Be Joyful (11:7–10)

Solomon repeats a vital word four times: be happy, be joyful. Youth perceives light as sweet; the sun pleases the eyes.

> Light is sweet, and it pleases the eyes to see the sun. (11:7)

> However many years a man may live, let him enjoy them all. (11:8)

> Be happy, young man, while you are young, and let your heart give you joy in the days of your youth. (11:9)

> So then, banish anxiety from your heart and cast off the troubles of your body, for youth and vigor are meaningless. (11:10)

### The Moral Obligation of Happiness

This isn't permission for hedonism or unrestricted pursuit of pleasure. The passage qualifies: "Follow the ways of your heart and whatever your eyes see, but know that for all these things God will bring you to judgment" (11:9). Joy here means contentment—learning to be happy with existing circumstances, looking to the bright side of life.

Happiness is more than a feeling; it's a moral obligation. Those who are chronically unhappy—the moody, the chronic complainers, the drama queens and kings—cast a shadow over everyone around them. They burden their children's childhoods, damage their marriages, and demoralize their parents. There is rarely a correlation between suffering and disposition. The cheerful simply recognize that life has ups and downs, that reality contains meaninglessness, yet they persevere with the duty of doing good.

This doesn't mean hiding our troubles from our closest friends and spouses. But it does mean that whatever we feel internally, we still try to act happy. Young people often recoil at this as inauthentic. Yet we embrace many "inauthentic" practices daily—mouthwash for authentic morning breath, deodorant for authentic body odor, seatbelts despite authentic impulses to be reckless.

There is a profound difference between feelings and action. I may not feel like obeying traffic laws, but I obey them. I may not feel like doing my duty, but I do it. I may not feel happy, but I can act happy.

Research shows this actually works. When students were asked to act like optimists for fifteen minutes, even without feeling optimistic, the more energetic and assertive they acted, the happier they became. Our behavior changes us. As the twelve-step programs wisely put it: fake it till you make it.

This is especially important for the young. There is so much ahead of you. A cheerful person at a café, a cheerful colleague at work, someone in the lift with a joke—these small moments of joy make a real difference in people's quality of life. We owe it to everyone we encounter to act as upbeat as possible.

## Be Godly (12:1–7)

The final section turns to spiritual priorities. Youth is the time to remember your Creator, before old age diminishes capacity.

> Remember your Creator in the days of your youth, before the days of trouble come and the years approach when you will say, "I find no pleasure in them." (12:1)

Solomon then paints a haunting picture of aging—a cascading descent into dimness and decay:

> Before the sun and the light and the moon and the stars grow dark, and the clouds return after the rain; when the keepers of the house tremble, and the strong men stoop, when the grinders cease because they are few, and those looking through the windows grow dim; when the doors to the street are closed and the sound of grinding fades; when men rise up at the sound of birds, but all their songs grow faint; when men are afraid of heights and of dangers in the streets; when the almond tree blossoms and the grasshopper drags himself along and desire no longer is stirred. Then man goes to his eternal home and mourners go about the streets. (12:2–5)

> Remember him—before the silver cord is severed, or the golden bowl is broken; before the pitcher is shattered at the spring, or the wheel broken at the well, and the dust returns to the ground it came from, and the spirit returns to God who gave it. (12:6–7)

The message is clear: time is short. The time to act is now, in your youth, before you lose boldness, before you fear heights and dangers, before you retreat from the world.

### Three Calls to Action

**For the unbeliever:** If you've been investigating Christianity, coming to church, checking the facts but hesitating to commit, now is the time. You may be waiting for ideal conditions—every question answered perfectly, all unknowns resolved. But life doesn't work that way. We put higher burdens of proof on Christianity than on anything else in life. Even if we had perfect evidence, skepticism finds room to doubt.

There is no perfect answer. What matters is whether you've found a genuine objection or whether the real obstacle is something else. If you've investigated for a while and can't identify a real objection—and you know this when someone asks and you can't think of one—then perhaps God is saying: cast your bread into the waters. Be bold. Make that commitment now, while you're young and adaptable, before life hardens you.

**For marriage:** Now is the time. The purpose of marriage is to start a family, and for women biologically, youth is the only real window. Yet many wait for ideal conditions—the perfect person, the perfect time, the perfect circumstances.

There is no right time. The Asian approach says: wait until your career is settled, you've bought a house, taken holidays. But if you're waiting for that, you're waiting too long. For women especially, fertility declines sharply after 30. By the late thirties, eggs that have been in the body for forty years aren't as viable. This creates a triple hurdle: it's harder to conceive, more likely to miscarry, and higher risk of chromosomal abnormalities.

There is no perfect person. You don't need to find someone beautiful, wealthy, with perfect chemistry and shared interests. The right person is simply a Christian who loves the Lord as much as you do and with whom you can work well together. You can discover this by being in the same church, playing sports together, serving alongside them. Everything else is minor or potentially detrimental. Focusing on beauty is disaster because beauty fades. Focusing on common interests is risky because interests change. Unrealistic expectations lead to bitter disappointment.

There is no perfect way to start. A fancy proposal isn't necessary. What matters is the commitment to daily work—the tough slog of committing to each other for your children and for society.

In this church, in Sydney, there are many choices. Cast your bread into the waters and see what you find. Be bold. Do this even though you don't know the future. No one promises happily ever after. But if both of you are Christian and can work well together, that's as good as it gets.

**For women specifically:** You can only do so much in your twenties. If you choose marriage and children, understand that time is working against you. The first wave of feminism fought for the vote. The second wave said be like men, have it all. The third wave is realizing: no, we can't. We have to choose.

Research on highly successful American women, those raised to believe they could have everything, found that 49% were childless—most not by choice, and many bitterly regretted it. Fertility starts dropping in the late twenties and significantly drops after 30. This isn't just about getting pregnant; it's also about health. Older mothers are less fit, experience more back pain, take longer to recover. Grandparents are older and help less. You may end up caring for elderly parents while raising children.

For men: fertility also declines with age. While it starts later than for women (declining in the forties), the impact is real. It takes longer, and children of older fathers face higher risks of developmental disorders like autism.

The deeper issue: children are gifts from God who carry the gospel to the next generation. Fewer children affects all of society. When people stop working, others pick up the tab. This is why we currently debate raising the retirement age.

**For ministry:** Now is the time to serve, to be trained, to serve each other and the church. You have the time and energy to learn and adapt. You have time to make mistakes and bounce back. Your ego isn't fragile yet. Others' expectations of you aren't as high.

Look at history. Martin Luther was 22 when he entered the monastery in 1505. Within ten years, he started the Reformation. Howard Guinness started the London Inter Faculty Christian Union in 1923 before his final exams. In 1928, he went to Canada and started the Inter-Varsity Fellowship there, which led to evangelical movements in Australia.

You can do this. You have energy. You can rough it at camp. You can spend Sundays together, stay late, go home and cram for midnight assignments, wake up for university. You can serve God with your adaptability and flexibility.

The world's corporations know this. They hire the young because you're adaptable, flexible, able to withstand pressure, teachable. Instead of prioritizing work for the world, prioritize work for God.

But I hear excuses: "I'm too tired. I don't like change. The food isn't nice. It's too hot, too cold, too far. I need my afternoon nap. It might not work." These aren't young excuses. They're old excuses. It's the old who are tired, who don't like change, who complain about food and weather. You are the youth. You can do it.

## Wisdom Culture (12:8–14)

> "Meaningless! Meaningless!" says the Teacher. "Everything is meaningless!" (12:8)

The problem for the young is a lack of wisdom. You have sharp minds but limited depth. Quick reactions but poor judgment. This is why Solomon's closing words matter:

> Not only was the Teacher wise, but also he imparted knowledge to the people. He pondered and searched out and set in order many proverbs. The Teacher searched to find just the right words, and what he wrote was upright and true. The words of the wise are like goads, their collected sayings like firmly embedded nails—given by one Shepherd. Be warned, my son, of anything in addition to them. Of making many books there is no end, and much study wearies the body. (12:9–12)

We often critique old people unfairly, assuming that old equals bad, outdated, unreliable. But wisdom comes from experience. History teaches us so we don't repeat past mistakes. We need to combine the strength of youth with the wisdom of the old.

The Bible is a shortcut to wisdom. Ecclesiastes is such wisdom. Solomon has been through it all. He stands at the height of not just his own experience but Israel's—their richest, most successful, wisest moment. And he says: it's meaningless. I've been to the top of this world and there's nothing here. Like climbers on Mount Everest, you get up there and it's just death.

He searched for meaning and found: if you live just for this life, it is meaningless. Whether money, success, ambition, envy, or health—all limited. Real meaning is found only in the afterlife. But this doesn't mean we're unproductive. We've been given a task and purpose: to do our duty and obey God. It may not feel meaningful to us, but it's meaningful to God and meaningful to the people we serve.

Recall Jesus's sacrifice on the cross. By worldly standards, it wasn't meaningful. Jesus didn't live a successful life. He never married. He died an insignificant death, arrested and executed like a common criminal—not a heroic death, just a criminal's death. But he did his duty. It was meaningful to God, and it's meaningful to all of us who shelter under the protection of his death.

> Fear God and keep his commandments, for this is the whole duty of man. (12:13)

> For God will bring every deed into judgment, including every hidden thing, whether it is good or evil. (12:14)

So let's get on with obeying God. Do your duty. Be bold while you're young. Be joyful in all circumstances. Remember your Creator. Serve his kingdom with the time, energy, and adaptability you have now. Time is short, and the time to act is today.
