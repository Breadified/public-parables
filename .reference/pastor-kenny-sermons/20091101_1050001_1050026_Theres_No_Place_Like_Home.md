# Genesis 50:1-26

**Title:** There's No Place Like Home  
**Date:** 29 November 2009  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Home: Where Do You Want to Be Buried?

Jacob, the great patriarch, was dying. His final instruction was remarkably specific—not merely to be mourned or honored, but to be buried in a particular place. He made his sons swear an oath: "Bury me with my fathers in the cave in the field of Ephron the Hittite" (Genesis 49:29-33).

This wasn't a casual request. When Joseph's turn came to make the same plea, he had to seek permission from Pharaoh himself to return to Canaan for his father's burial. The Egyptian king's approval was necessary, which tells us something profound: dying in Egypt and being buried there would have been far easier and more politically safe. Yet Joseph insisted on this same burial ground (Genesis 50:4-6).

Years later, when Joseph himself lay dying at 110 years old, he made his own sons swear to carry his bones out of Egypt when God fulfilled His promise to bring the people back to Canaan (Genesis 50:24-26). Why was this so important?

### The Promised Land: A Slice of Heaven

The answer lies in Abraham's covenant with God. In Genesis 12, the Lord promised Abram:

> "Leave your country, your people and your father's household and go to the land I will show you. I will make you into a great nation and I will bless you; I will make your name great, and you will be a blessing."

Jacob and Joseph clung to this promise. They understood something crucial: God had sworn an oath to give their descendants a land—Canaan, flowing with milk and honey.

Abraham never fully possessed this land. Yet he did something symbolic that became a deposit on the promise. When his wife Sarah died, the Hittites offered him land freely, but Abraham insisted on buying it—paying in full, so the debt would be clear. He purchased the cave of Machpelah, a tiny foothold in Canaan (Genesis 23). This small plot became a memorial: "We believe God's promise. This land belongs to us."

That burial cave became a kind of living statement. Abraham rested there. Isaac and Rebekah rested there. Jacob buried Leah there. It was a physical anchor to the invisible promise—a slice of heaven in the middle of a foreign land.

### The Resurrection: Their Hope and Ours

But what's the point of being buried in a land you won't see again? The answer reveals the depth of their faith: *they believed in the resurrection*.

These patriarchs understood that God's promises couldn't be stopped by death. He would raise them from the grave. When the promise came to full fruition—when God brought their descendants back to Canaan—they would rise again in the promised land itself. Their faith reached beyond the grave.

The prophets later articulated this same hope. Daniel wrote:

> "Multitudes who sleep in the dust of the earth will awake: some to everlasting life, others to shame and everlasting contempt. Those who are wise will shine like the brightness of the heavens, and those who lead many to righteousness, like the stars for ever and ever." (Daniel 12:1-2)

For Jacob and Joseph, the promise was so certain, so guaranteed by God Himself, that not even death could take it away. They would be buried there, waiting for resurrection in the place of God's blessing.

### Where Are You Being Buried?

This raises a searching question for us: Where are you being buried? What is the resting place of your heart and hopes?

We live in one of the most prosperous suburbs in Sydney. We have access to the latest phones, fashion, gadgets. Many of us work hard—some holding multiple jobs—to afford these things. We dream of dream houses, dream cars, dream cities: Paris, Hong Kong, New York, Venice.

But here's the hard truth: *it's all just stuff*. These things will be destroyed at the resurrection. That wonderful gadget will be gone. That beautiful dress will be gone. The finest pyramid couldn't preserve Joseph's ambitions any more than our possessions will preserve ours.

Some of us aren't captivated by things, but by experiences. We live as tourists through life, snapping photos at every moment, trying to capture every feeling. We pursue the "Good Weekend" lifestyle: gym, coffee, parties, good food. We've convinced ourselves that every public holiday and weekend must be filled with something fun—networking, eating, drinking. But *these experiences also pass away*. When you spend the whole event taking photos, you miss the real experience—the relational connections that come from commitment and love.

Consider how many of us order our lives:

- We work all week for money and things.
- We spend weekends recovering and enjoying.
- If there's time left over, we might fit in God.

Or in school:

- We study hard now, telling ourselves we'll build relationships later, when the holidays come.
- When the break arrives, we don't study or serve or train—we need to "recharge."
- The attitude becomes: *I'll fit Christianity in after everything else.*

If that's your life, if that's your priority structure, then it shouldn't be. You're being buried in the wrong place.

### Instead: Be Buried with Christ

We should want to be buried with Christ. We should want to be buried in relationships characterized by love and service. We should want to be buried in faith in Him.

Not buried in career ambitions. Not buried in your dream position. Not buried in the pursuit of money. Not buried in a school or building. Not buried in self-promotion or personal achievement.

Jesus is the true King of Israel—the ultimate fulfillment of all God's promises to Abraham. Every knee will bow to Him, and every tongue will confess that He is Lord. Do you bow to Him? Or do you treat Him like He's optional, available whenever you need Him, but not the defining center of your life?

When you serve a king, you don't put his agenda last. You rearrange everything else around his priorities.

### Real Home: Heaven

At the end of Genesis, the promises to Abraham are coming toward fulfillment, but not yet complete. Jacob and Joseph enjoyed incredible prosperity in Egypt—children, security, abundance in Goshen. Yet by their actions, they made it clear: *they were not home*.

They longed for home. They made their families swear to carry their bones there.

Think about it: What's your homepage? Where does your mind go when you wake up? What defines you?

We live as strangers and aliens here. Hebrews 11 captures this beautifully:

> "All these people were still living by faith when they died. They did not receive the things promised; they only saw them and welcomed them from a distance. And they admitted that they were aliens and strangers on earth. People who say such things show that they are looking for a country of their own... Instead, they were longing for a better country—a heavenly one. Therefore God is not ashamed to be called their God, for he has prepared a city for them." (Hebrews 11:13-16)

This is our calling too. Our real home is in heaven. It's not in things. It's not in experiences. It's not even in this church building or a particular location. It's in Christ and in the eternal city God is preparing.

So let us cry out with genuine longing: "Come, Lord Jesus. Come." Not "Come, but not just yet—I need to finish my career first" or "Come, but let me enjoy this game first" or "Come after this assignment." But truly meaning it: *Come, Lord. My home is with You.*

I've watched you this year. I've seen some of your struggles as a church—challenges with commitment to one another that we must face together in the year ahead. But I've also seen beautiful evidence of your faith. I've seen some of you:

- Regular at church and Bible study all year, committed to one another
- Volunteering for community work
- Starting to read Scripture with others
- Holding three jobs and still coming to church, serving in ministry, attending Bible study, leading youth groups
- Some of you declining to spend money on shopping trips because you've rightly sensed: "I don't need to go. That's not where my home is."

This is maturity. This is faith. This is what it looks like to be buried in the right place—in Christ, in His kingdom, in His promises.

Real home isn't found in toys. It's found in Jesus. Let us be buried with Him, that we may rise with Him on the day of resurrection. Let us put our hope in the things of heaven, not the things of earth.

Where is your home?
