## The Big Picture

In the midst of a destroyed Jerusalem, Yahweh promises not merely a rebuild but a future of glory—a city that will overflow with prosperity, where the Lord will again comfort Zion and choose Jerusalem (1:17). This restoration involves three key elements:

1. **The What:** Yahweh will return via His temple (Zechariah 1 and visions 1–3, 8)
2. **The Who:** The temple will be built by His anointed priest and ruler (visions 4–7, Zechariah 7–11)
3. **The When:** All of this will happen on the Day of the Lord (Chapters 12–14)

## Part 1: What That Day Will Look Like

### The D-Day Illustration

Consider the 60th anniversary of June 6, 1944—D-Day, the Battle of Normandy. It was the largest seaborne invasion in history: 156,000 troops on the beaches, 23,400 airborne troops, 11,590 aircraft in support, and 13,000 naval vessels. By the end of July, one million Allied troops had established a beachhead. Paris was liberated in late August, and by May 8, 1945, Germany had officially surrendered.

D-Day was a death blow to Nazi Germany. For those waiting—the Germans, the French, the Russians, the English—the question echoed: *When will this day come?* When will we be liberated?

In much the same way, post-exilic Israel waited for a special day. They waited for God to return and bless them, for their enemies to stop mocking them, for sin to be no more. This is their version of D-Day—what we might call their P-Day: the day of Peace.

### Jerusalem Will Be Secure

> I am going to make Jerusalem a cup that sends all the surrounding peoples reeling. Judah will be besieged as well as Jerusalem. (12:2)

On that day, Jerusalem itself becomes an immovable rock—whoever tries to move it will only injure themselves. The nations will be blinded; God will strike every horse with panic and its rider with madness (12:4). While God keeps a watchful eye over the house of Judah, He blinds the horses of the nations, effectively removing their capacity for war.

### Jerusalem Will Have Great Leaders and Strong People

The leaders of Judah will be like a firepot in a woodpile, like flaming torches among sheaves—they will consume the surrounding peoples, yet Jerusalem will remain intact (12:6).

What's striking is that God will ensure equality among His people. The dwellings of Judah will be saved first so that the honor of David's house and Jerusalem's inhabitants may not be greater than that of Judah as a whole (12:7–8). Yet the house of David retains a special place—they will be like God Himself, like the Angel of the Lord going before them (12:8). And upon the house of David and the inhabitants of Jerusalem, God will pour out a spirit of grace and supplication (12:10).

### Sin and False Prophets Will Be Removed

> On that day a fountain will be opened to the house of David and the inhabitants of Jerusalem, to cleanse them from sin and impurity. (13:1)

On that day, God will banish the names of idols from the land, and they will be remembered no more. He will remove both the prophets and the spirit of impurity (13:2). The passage is stark: if anyone still prophesies, their own parents will say to them, "You must die, because you have told lies in the Lord's name" (13:3). Even a false prophet will be ashamed of his vision and will claim to be merely a farmer, hiding the wounds given him by those who discovered his lies (13:4–6).

### The Result: Complete Peace

When a city is secure and free from threats, when all are equal so no leadership can become corrupt, when sin and lies are removed, and when even the sinner repents—what do you get? Peace. Wholeness. Complete peace.

This is why Zechariah writes:

> Rejoice greatly, O Daughter of Zion! Shout, Daughter of Jerusalem! See, your king comes to you, righteous and having salvation, gentle and riding on a donkey, on a colt, the foal of a donkey. (9:9)
>
> I will take away the chariots from Ephraim and the war-horses from Jerusalem, and the battle bow will be broken. He will proclaim peace to the nations. His rule will extend from sea to sea and from the River to the ends of the earth. (9:10)

The king comes not on a chariot of war but on a donkey—a symbol of peace. No more war horses, no more chariots, no more battle bows. Peace extends from sea to sea, from river to the ends of the earth. The whole world rests.

### Our Longing

What day do we long for? What do we truly wait for? Perhaps an iPod, an iPhone, a new computer, a certain lifestyle, or the right person? These are mere wishes. But do we long for genuine peace? For God to dwell among us? For truth to rule? For justice? For an end to sin?

Kenny challenges us: align ourselves with God's type of day—not a false peace maintained by military might or political alliance, but the genuine peace that comes when God Himself returns and sin is vanquished.

For those of you waiting patiently, enduring: know that your wait is not in vain. The day is coming.

## Part 2: Suffering Must Come First

But Zechariah's vision comes with a price. The Day will only come after much suffering.

### The Pierced One and Deep Mourning

> And I will pour out on the house of David and the inhabitants of Jerusalem a spirit of grace and supplication. They will look on me, the one they have pierced, and they will mourn for him as one mourns for an only child, and grieve bitterly for him as one grieves for a firstborn son. (12:10–11)

Who is this pierced one? God Himself. And the mourning will be as great as the weeping over King Josiah, the righteous king who rediscovered God's law after centuries of neglect, only to die in battle against Egypt (see 2 Chronicles 35:22–25). The entire land will mourn—each clan by itself, with their wives by themselves—the house of David, the house of Nathan, the house of Levi, the house of Shimei, and all the rest (12:12–14).

### The Shepherd Is Struck

> Awake, O sword, against my shepherd, against the man who is close to me! declares the LORD Almighty. Strike the shepherd, and the sheep will be scattered, and I will turn my hand against the little ones. (13:7–9)

When the shepherd is struck, the sheep scatter. Two-thirds will be struck down and perish; yet one-third will be left. This third, God will bring into the fire to refine them like silver and test them like gold. They will call on God's name, and He will answer them. He will say, "They are my people," and they will say, "The LORD is our God."

## Part 3: The Day Is Actually Now—It Has Already Come

So when is this Day? Zechariah points Israel forward to it. But here's the stunning realization: the day has already come. It finds its fulfillment in Jesus Christ and the cross.

The New Testament interprets Zechariah this way. Consider the evidence:

### The Donkey

Matthew sees the prophecy of the king on the donkey fulfilled when Jesus enters Jerusalem:

> As they approached Jerusalem and came to Bethphage on the Mount of Olives, Jesus sent two disciples, saying to them, "Go to the village ahead of you, and at once you will find a donkey tied there, with her colt by her. Untie them and bring them to me." This took place to fulfill what was spoken through the prophet: "Say to the Daughter of Zion, 'See, your king comes to you, gentle and riding on a donkey, on a colt, the foal of a donkey.'" (Matthew 21:1–5)

Jesus is the king riding on the donkey—riding into death.

### The Shepherd Struck

Again, Matthew applies Zechariah's prophecy to Jesus:

> Then Jesus told them, "This very night you will all fall away on account of me, for it is written: 'I will strike the shepherd, and the sheep of the flock will be scattered.' But after I have risen, I will go ahead of you into Galilee." (Matthew 26:31–32)

Jesus is the shepherd who is struck, and all the sheep scatter. But after His resurrection, He goes ahead of them into Galilee—the gathering begins again.

### Understanding Jesus's Promise About Mountains

This understanding of Zechariah clarifies something that has long puzzled readers of Matthew:

> He replied, "Because you have so little faith. I tell you the truth, if you have faith as small as a mustard seed, you can say to this mountain, 'Move from here to there' and it will move. Nothing will be impossible for you." (Matthew 17:20)

And again:

> If you have faith and do not doubt, not only can you do what was done to the fig tree, but also you can say to this mountain, 'Go, throw yourself into the sea,' and it will be done. If you believe, you will receive whatever you ask for in prayer. (Matthew 21:21–22)

But what does Zechariah 14 say about mountains?

> On that day his feet will stand on the Mount of Olives, east of Jerusalem, and the Mount of Olives will be split in two from east to west, forming a great valley, with half of the mountain moving north and half moving south. (14:4)

Jesus is not promising that believers can command literal mountains to move based on personal faith. Rather, He is pointing to the Day—the day when the Messiah stands on the Mount of Olives and splits the mountain itself. He is saying to the disciples: *Have faith in the coming day that I am about to fulfill. Have faith in the Messiah moving mountains. Have faith in the Day. If you believe in my day and ask for it, it will be answered.*

Jesus is calling them to faith in D-Day—which is the cross. The cross is the day when Jesus stands on the Mount of Olives (figuratively speaking, in the broader theological sense) and accomplishes the great reversal.

### The Full Vision of That Day

Zechariah paints a cataclysmic picture—a vision as great as the Exodus and Exile combined:

There will be a great fight. Nations will gather against Jerusalem, but God will go out and fight for His people (14:2–3). The Mount of Olives will split, forming a great valley, and God's people will flee to safety (14:4–5). Living water will flow out from Jerusalem to both the eastern and western seas in summer and in winter (14:8).

The day will be unique—without daytime or nighttime—a day known to the Lord. When evening comes, there will be light (14:6–7). God will be king over the whole earth, and His name will be the only name (14:9).

Jerusalem will be raised up and remain in its place, inhabited, never again destroyed. It will be secure (14:10–11). Those nations that fight against Jerusalem will be struck with plague—their flesh will rot while standing, their eyes will rot in their sockets (14:12). Men will be stricken with panic and attack one another (14:13–14). The wealth of surrounding nations will be collected—gold, silver, and clothing (14:15).

Survivors from all nations will go up year after year to worship the King and celebrate the Feast of Tabernacles (14:16). If any people do not go up, the Lord will bring upon them the plague He inflicts on those who do not come (14:17–19).

Even the ordinary will become holy. Bells on horses will be inscribed "HOLY TO THE LORD," and cooking pots in the Lord's house will be like sacred bowls before the altar (14:20). Every pot in Jerusalem and Judah will be holy, and all who come to sacrifice will cook in them. There will no longer be any Canaanite—any outsider—in the house of the Lord (14:21).

Zechariah envisions one who will stand on the Mount of Olives, and the mountain itself will split to allow the exodus—the saved ones—to pass through to the new Jerusalem.

## Part 4: Faith in the Accomplished Victory

For us, our faith is the same as what the Jews believed—faith in the priest and king who comes on the Day. But whereas Zechariah and the disciples believed in the future, we put our faith in the past. The Day has been accomplished.

Jesus has stood on the mountain (spiritually speaking), shaken it, and set in motion a mass exodus. An exodus from sin and condemnation to righteousness and salvation. A return from exile back to the new Jerusalem. We trust that past day—that all is done, that it has been fulfilled, that victory has been accomplished.

Do not be distracted by new prophecies of future wars or nuclear threats. We believe in what happened historically in the past—the cross.

### D-Day and the Victory Already Won

Think again of D-Day. Once the Allies landed and formed a beachhead on the beaches of Normandy, that was really the end of the war—from a military standpoint, Germany had lost. But Hitler still fought on. It took just under a year more—until May 7, 1945—for the official surrender. During that time, any German victory was only temporary. The outcome was already determined.

So it is with us. We have the victory already. Satan doesn't realize it yet, and he still fights on. But we have won. If you are a Christian, you are on the winning side. And we are simply waiting for the final formalities.

Look forward to the cross. The cross is atonement. The cross is victory over Satan. The cross is the ridding of the penalty of sin. The cross is where God was just.

It would be foolish to keep living in sin after being liberated—like a Frenchman who speaks German and cries "Heil Hitler" after the occupation has ended. Why would you? Keep trusting in Jesus's victory on the cross, the victory that defeats Satan and deprives him of the power to accuse us (though he may still hurt us).

The day has come. The victory is won. We are waiting for the final return—the consummation of all things when Christ returns and makes all things new. Until then, we live as people who have already tasted the victory, already experienced the grace of the cross, already been set free.
