# Matthew 16:13-28

**Title:** Jesus the Christ, Son of God  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew  
**Date:** 5 April 2009

---

## The Question Jesus Posed

Jesus asked his disciples a penetrating question: "Who do you say I am?" This was not a moment of confusion on his part, but rather a turning point in Matthew's gospel—a climactic moment after all his healings, teachings, and miracles pointed toward a single truth. The disciples had witnessed Jesus' power, yet they still struggled with faith and doubt. Peter had even sunk into the water moments before, paralyzed by fear. Now Jesus pressed them: it was time to decide.

The crowds offered various answers—John the Baptist, Elijah, Jeremiah, or one of the prophets. But Jesus was asking the disciples themselves: "Who do you say I am?"

---

## 1. YOU ARE THE CHRIST (v.16)

> **Matthew 16:16** – Simon Peter answered, "You are the Christ, the Son of the living God."

Peter's confession marks the first time anyone had recognized Jesus as the Christ during his adult ministry. After all the teaching and signs, finally—a glimmer of understanding.

### What Does "Christ" Mean?

"Christ" is not a surname; it's a title. The word means "Messiah"—literally, "the Anointed One." In the Old Testament, people were anointed with oil when God set them apart for a specific, crucial task. Kings received this anointing above all others.

This is why Matthew so carefully traces Jesus' lineage to David, the greatest king of Israel (Matthew 1:17). But David himself was not the final Christ. The prophets looked forward to another David—a greater Messiah who would accomplish what Israel longed for:

- **The end of exile:** Israel would rise again in power and status, freed from bondage to foreign governments
- **Healing and restoration:** The people would be cured of their diseases and suffering; their living conditions would improve
- **Justice:** The corrupt would be judged; evil rulers would be banished; the weak would be protected
- **Forgiveness:** This Messiah would bring God's forgiveness and comfort—signs that the nation had been reconciled to the Lord

Peter saw all this in Jesus. Here was a teacher preaching that the kingdom was at hand. Here was a healer restoring the sick. Here was a judge calling corruption to account. This was the New David—the one Israel had been waiting for.

### Two Unexpected Twists

Yet immediately after this beautiful recognition, Jesus introduces two shocking twists that challenge everything Peter and the disciples believed about the Messiah.

---

## 2. THE FIRST TWIST: BE QUIET (v.20)

> **Matthew 16:20** – Then he warned his disciples not to tell anyone that he was the Christ.

This makes no sense. When the true Messiah arrives, the whole world will know. There will be a grand parade, a triumphant procession. Enemies will be defeated openly. When Russia entered Berlin, there were tanks and bombs—unmistakable power.

But Jesus says, "Be quiet." A king doesn't sneak into his own palace. A true king enters with fanfare, with all the servants rushing out to greet him. When does a king sneak into his own palace? When he is not king.

This is the first hint of something strange—something that doesn't fit the Messianic expectation.

---

## 3. THE SECOND TWIST: I'M GOING TO DIE (v.21)

> **Matthew 16:21** – From that time on Jesus began to explain to his disciples that he must go to Jerusalem and suffer many things at the hands of the elders, chief priests and teachers of the law, and that he must be killed and on the third day be raised to life.

This is utterly un-Messianic. The New David is not supposed to die. His body should never decay. We couldn't place our hopes in the dead King David—he was powerless. And if Jesus is God himself, the ultimate source of power, how can he suffer and be bullied? How can a local Jewish group—the Sanhedrin—kill him? If the Messiah can be executed by the elders, chief priests, and teachers of the law, what hope is there against Rome's might? Israel will never be liberated.

It's like those movie titles that don't match the story: *Never Ending Story* (it ends), *Star Wars II: Attack of the Clones* (they mostly defend), *Die Hard* (he doesn't die). The title "Christ" simply does not fit with suffering and death.

---

## 4. PETER'S REBUKE

> **Matthew 16:22** – Peter took him aside and began to rebuke him. "Never, Lord!" he said. "This shall never happen to you!"

Peter's reaction is completely understandable. Just as Jesus began to teach about his coming suffering, Peter began to rebuke him—the same kind of rebuke Jesus used when he calmed the wind and waves. Peter is rebuking the Messiah.

This is the same Peter whom Jesus had just affirmed moments before, blessed and chosen as the rock upon which the church would be built. Yet now, with the best of intentions and out of deep love for Jesus, Peter tries to steer him away from death. It echoes the temptations in the desert (Matthew 4:10)—a voice saying, "Don't do this. There's another way." Peter is tempting Jesus to abandon his mission, to save himself instead of saving others.

---

## 5. YOU ARE PETER (v.17-19)

> **Matthew 16:17-19** – Jesus replied, "Blessed are you, Simon son of Jonah, for this was not revealed to you by man, but by my Father in heaven. And I tell you that you are Peter, and on this rock I will build my church, and the gates of Hades will not overcome it. I will give you the keys of the kingdom of heaven; whatever you bind on earth will be bound in heaven, and whatever you loose on earth will be loosed in heaven."

This passage contains layers of meaning that Christians have debated for centuries. Let me unpack some of the key elements:

**Gates of Hades:** This means death itself. Jesus is saying that his church will not be overcome by death—it will endure forever, unlike the temporary gatherings of the Old Testament.

**Binding and loosing:** This likely refers to making decisions that align with what has already been decided in heaven, rather than making arbitrary judgments.

**The Church:** This is the eschatological gathering—the heavenly assembly that began at Mount Sinai and is reconstituted in heaven. It's not that we gather on earth to copy heaven or get closer to God in a building. Rather, we meet together to help one another reach heaven at the end.

### The Word-Play: Peter and Rock

The most fascinating part is the play on words in verse 18:

> You are Peter (Petros—masculine, a stone), and on this rock (Petra—feminine, a foundation rock or cliff) I will build my church.

There's genuine debate among scholars about the significance of this word-play. Here are the main interpretations:

**Roman Catholic interpretation:** Petros and Petra are essentially the same word. Jesus builds his church on Peter himself. Peter becomes the first pope, and authority passes to his successors. The keys grant him authority over heaven.

We as Reformed Christians reject this view. We have only one Pope—Christ himself. There's no New Testament evidence of Peter as head of the church in Rome or of any intended apostolic succession.

**Protestant distinction:** Petros (movable stone) differs from Petra (immovable rock). This leads to two further possibilities:
- The rock is Peter's confession—that Jesus is the Christ. Upon this confession, the church is built. Everyone who confesses Jesus as Christ belongs to the church. The keys represent knowledge of the gospel.
- The rock is Christ himself, as suggested by Old Testament references to God as the Rock (1 Corinthians 10:4).

**Modern scholarly view:** Many recent scholars believe Petros and Petra are similar enough that Matthew may have simply chosen the masculine form (Petros) because Petra would have been an unusual name for a man, even though he clearly intended to call Simon by a rock-related name.

### Peter's Unique Role

What we can say with confidence is that Peter is given a special responsibility by Jesus in light of his confession. Consider the evidence:

- He's consistently listed first among the disciples
- He's the leading character in many gospel stories
- He's often the spokesperson for the group
- He receives special commissions from Jesus
- He appears first in resurrection accounts
- In Acts, he guides the choosing of Matthias, preaches to the Jews, and then to the Gentiles with Cornelius

Peter clearly has a unique place in salvation history. His teachings, and the teachings of all the apostles, are foundational. We see this in Ephesians, where our faith is described as built on the apostles' foundation. In Acts, the early church devoted themselves to the apostles' teachings.

**Important caveat:** When we read narrative and descriptive passages like this, we cannot automatically jump from Peter's experience to our own experience. This account is about Peter's unrepeatable role in salvation history, not about us. The danger lies in reading Matthew straight to ourselves, missing the historical particularity of what God was doing in Peter's life at that specific time.

---

## 6. GET BEHIND ME: THE TRUE CHRIST (v.23)

> **Matthew 16:23** – Jesus turned and said to Peter, "Get behind me, Satan! You are a stumbling block to me; you do not have in mind the things of God, but the things of men."

Despite Peter's insight and Jesus' recent affirmation of him, Peter has more to learn. Just as Peter rebuked Jesus, Jesus now rebukes Peter.

Peter becomes a "stumbling block"—like toys left on the floor that cause someone to trip and fall. The same word is used in the parable of the sower, where the rocky places represent those who fall away when trouble comes.

Peter is well-intentioned. He loves Jesus deeply. But he's tempting Jesus to abandon his calling—much like the temptations Jesus faced in the wilderness. Peter is urging him to turn back, to choose a different path.

The problem is this: all the powerful descriptions of the Messiah have overshadowed the suffering descriptions. The disciples have forgotten what Isaiah teaches—that God anoints his suffering servant to bear the sins of the people. They've overlooked Zechariah's prophecy of a pierced leader. They've done what we normally do with God's word: we keep the parts we like and discard the uncomfortable parts.

To be the true Christ means accepting death. There is no Messiah without the cross.

---

## 7. THE TRUE CHRISTIAN (v.24-28)

> **Matthew 16:24-28** – Then Jesus said to his disciples, "If anyone would come after me, he must deny himself and take up his cross and follow me. For whoever wants to save his life will lose it, but whoever loses his life for me will find it. What good will it be for a man if he gains the whole world, yet forfeits his soul? Or what can a man give in exchange for his soul? For the Son of Man is going to come in his Father's glory with his angels, and then he will reward each person according to what he has done. I tell you the truth, some who are standing here will not taste death before they see the Son of Man coming in his kingdom."

Here Jesus calls his disciples—and by extension, all believers—to follow his example. He tells Peter (and all of us), "Get behind me" and follow. Three instructions encapsulate true discipleship:

**Deny yourself:** Stop living for your comfort, your agenda, your plans. Surrender your will.

**Take up your cross:** This is a metaphor for the journey toward death. Losing your life for Jesus' sake is the path to finding true life.

**Follow me:** Align yourself with Jesus' agenda, his program, his purpose.

Just as Jesus the true Christ denied himself, accepted his cross, and followed God's will, so we must be true Christians. We must follow him on this same difficult path.

### A Critical Error in Our Culture

There's a terrible misconception prevalent in Sydney's Christian circles—and perhaps beyond. It's the idea that we must live up to our academic or artistic potential. We each have some great gift we can contribute to the world. Achieving this potential will be satisfying to both us and the world. It's a compelling narrative.

If you're bright, you must become a doctor or lawyer so you can save lives and help people. If you're artistic, you need a fulfilling career that uses your talents. This sounds virtuous. But it's more a product of Oprah and migrant aspirationalism than a fruit of Jesus' teaching.

Did Jesus live a fulfilled life by this standard? He was an unemployed carpenter who was executed. His life doesn't fit our template of success.

We tell ourselves: "I'm too talented for a mere 9-to-5 job. I need job satisfaction. God has called me to better things." And notice how "better things" always seem to be literally better—God calls people to be doctors but never nurses, to be businessmen but never garbage collectors, to live in Epping and Eastwood but never Blacktown. God is somehow exactly like our aspirational, upper-middle-class selves.

### What Has God Actually Called You To?

**Deny yourself.** Stop defining yourself by your economic potential, your salary, your academic credentials, your social media presence.

**Take up your cross.** Accept the difficult, unglamorous, often invisible work of following Jesus.

**Follow him.** Get on with his program.

I was recently at my old school's 50th anniversary celebration at James Ruse—a place of considerable academic success. In that environment, it's so tempting to pray that my own children will attend and have a "fulfilled" life there. But I'm learning to pray instead that they will deny themselves, take up their cross, and follow Jesus. That's a harder, more costly prayer. It's like praying they'll have cancer.

### Identity Redefined

In times of economic hardship—unemployment, retrenchment—we feel the sting deeply. Why? Because our identity is wrapped up with our work, our earning potential, our status. If you're struggling with this, please come speak with me. I want to help.

But Jesus teaches us a different way. Your identity is not based on:
- Your economic or financial potential
- What you earn
- What you buy or wear
- Your academic or social achievements (number of Facebook friends)

Your identity is based on one thing: **You are a child of God, saved by the blood of Jesus. You are loved by God.**

Yes, we should all be ready to meet our Judge and Maker—yesterday would not be too soon. But God has graciously given us today and tomorrow. That gift means he has work left for us to do.

Deny yourself. Take up your cross. Follow him.

This isn't pretty, and it's not what our culture teaches. But don't discard this truth. This is what discipleship is.

And while we're not shirking our responsibilities in this world, let's get on with God's program. How are you using your money? Another unnecessary phone? More importantly, how are you using your time? Is too much of it going to academics and work at the expense of God's kingdom? During times of crisis—GFC, retrenchment, economic uncertainty—it's easy to get caught up in the treadmill. But let's remember: we all participated in going along with the system.

It's time to get back on God's program.
