# Romans 9:1-26

**Title:** The Choice of God  
**Date:** 3 February 2013  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Introduction: The Problem of Choice

In *The Matrix*, the Architect reveals a profound insight: humans accept virtual reality when given a choice—even if only at a subconscious level. The system achieves 99.9% acceptance through the illusion of choice. The 0.1% who reject it become the resistance, yet even this is part of the predetermined design.

Today we examine a passage about choices—specifically, God's choices.

---

## 1. God Has Not Failed; He Has Preserved His Choice

Paul opens with a passionate defense of God's faithfulness:

> I speak the truth in Christ—I am not lying, my conscience confirms it through the Holy Spirit—I have great sorrow and unceasing anguish in my heart. For I could wish that I myself were cursed and cut off from Christ for the sake of my people, those of my own race. (Romans 9:1-3)

**The Crisis**

The Jews were rejecting Jesus as Messiah. Despite God's covenant promises stretching back thousands of years, the chosen people were increasingly hostile toward the gospel. While the early church began with Jewish believers—the twelve apostles, the 500 at Pentecost—it was driven by persecution into the Gentile world. Today, Christians are 99% non-Jewish, creating an apparent contradiction with God's grand promises to Israel.

God promised Israel:

> Theirs is the adoption to sonship; theirs the divine glory, the covenants, the receiving of the law, the temple worship and the promises. Theirs are the patriarchs, and from them is traced the human ancestry of the Messiah, who is God over all, forever praised! (Romans 9:4-5)

Yet chapter 8 concludes with Paul's assurance:

> I am convinced that neither death nor life, neither angels nor demons, neither the present nor the future, nor any powers, neither height nor depth, nor anything else in all creation, will be able to separate us from the love of God that is in Christ Jesus our Lord. (Romans 8:38-39)

**The Objection**

Empirically, the Jews—God's chosen people—ARE separated from God. Has God's word failed?

**Paul's Answer: The True Definition of "Israel"**

> It is not as though God's word had failed... For not all who are descended from Israel are Israel. Nor because they are his descendants are they all Abraham's children. (Romans 9:6b-7)

The problem lies in how we interpret "all." God's promises to Israel never meant literally *every* descendant—this pattern extends back to Abraham himself.

### The Example of Isaac

Abraham had two sons: Isaac (through Sarah) and Ishmael (through Hagar, the servant). Though Ishmael was technically Abraham's child, God designated only Isaac as the heir:

> On the contrary, "It is through Isaac that your offspring will be reckoned." (Romans 9:7b)

> Get rid of that slave woman and her son... because it is through Isaac that your offspring will be reckoned. (Genesis 21:10, 12)

The distinction is crucial: Ishmael was a descendant, but not a son of the *promise*. God's covenant worked through Sarah—the infertile one. The miracle was not Abraham producing a child, but Sarah bearing Isaac. From the beginning, God's promises were *selective*, not universal.

### The Example of Jacob

The pattern continued with Isaac's twins, Jacob and Esau:

> Not only that, but Rebekah's children were conceived at the same time by our father Isaac. Yet, before the twins were born or had done anything good or bad—in order that God's purpose in election might stand: not by works but by him who calls—she was told, "The older will serve the younger." Just as it is written: "Jacob I loved, but Esau I hated." (Romans 9:10-13)

**Why was Jacob chosen and Esau rejected?**

The text emphasizes their *equality* to rule out human merit:
- **Same father** (v. 10)
- **Twins**—born simultaneously from the same mother (v. 10)
- **Before they were born**—before nurture could influence them (v. 11)
- **Before they did anything good or bad**—prior to any moral choices (v. 11)

The only reason: **God's choice.**

Jacob became Israel, the father of the twelve tribes. Esau's descendants, the Edomites, became enemies. This pattern isn't surprising—God has *always* chosen:

> Are not two sparrows sold for a penny? Yet not one of them will fall to the ground apart from the will of your Father. And even the very hairs of your head are all numbered. (Matthew 10:29-30)

> In his heart a man plans his course, but the LORD determines his steps. (Proverbs 16:9)

In salvation itself:

> No one can come to me unless the Father who sent me draws him. (John 6:44)

> When the Gentiles heard this, they were glad and honored the word of the Lord; and all who were appointed for eternal life believed. (Acts 13:48)

God chooses Abraham. God chooses Isaac. God chooses Jacob. So why would anyone think "Israel" means *all* Israel?

## 2. God Has His Purpose, and It Includes Justice

**The Objection Intensifies**

> What then shall we say? Is God unjust? Not at all! For he says to Moses, "I will have mercy on whom I have mercy, and I will have compassion on whom I have compassion." It does not, therefore, depend on human desire or effort, but on God's mercy. (Romans 9:14-16)

Our modern world bristles at such inequality. We abhor discrimination. Yet Paul is clear: God's choices rest on *His criteria*, not ours.

### The Example of Pharaoh

Scripture records God's purpose for Pharaoh:

> For Scripture says to Pharaoh: "I raised you up for this very purpose, that I might display my power in you and that my name might be proclaimed in all the earth." (Romans 9:17)

> For by now I could have stretched out my hand and struck you and your people with a plague that would have wiped you off the earth. But I have raised you up for this very purpose, that I might show you my power and that my name might be proclaimed in all the earth. (Exodus 9:15-16)

**Double Predestination**

This presents a difficult truth: God chooses some for mercy and others for destruction. Some respond to God's grace; others are "prepared for destruction" (Romans 9:22).

Many Christians soften this by suggesting God merely "lets people go their own way," but this begs the question: *Who allowed them to go their own way? Who controls their actions?* 

The uncomfortable answer is: **God.**

Pharaoh made his choices and deserves judgment, but God was sovereign over those choices. Consider the testimony of Scripture:

> A stone that causes men to stumble and a rock that makes them fall. They stumble because they disobey the message—which is also what they were destined for. (1 Peter 2:8)

> For certain men whose condemnation was written about long ago have secretly slipped in among you. (Jude 4)

> While I was with them, I protected them and kept them safe by that name you gave me. None has been lost except the one doomed to destruction so that Scripture would be fulfilled. (John 17:12)

**The Comfort in God's Sovereignty**

If God is not a sovereign, predestining God, what replaces Him? *Sheer terror.* A world out of His control. Disasters happen randomly. Your future is uncertain. The devil could still win. You could be eternally lost.

But if God *is* sovereign:

> All things work together for the good of those who love him. (Romans 8:28)

When you pray, God doesn't respond, "I'll try, but I'm not sure if I can." Your future has assurance. Christ guarantees salvation.

**God's sovereignty isn't tyranny—it's the foundation of your hope.**

## 3. His Purpose Is YOU

**The Fatalistic Objection**

> One of you will say to me: "Then why does God still blame us? For who is able to resist his will?" (Romans 9:19)

This is the fatalistic trap: *If God ordains everything, I'm not responsible. I'm just a robot. I can sin without guilt.*

But Paul's response cuts deeper:

> But who are you, a human being, to talk back to God? Shall what is formed say to the one who formed it, "Why did you make me like this?" Does not the potter have the right to make out of the same lump of clay some pottery for special purposes and some for common use? (Romans 9:20-21)

We are clay. God is the potter. He has the right to shape us as He sees fit.

> The secret things belong to the LORD our God, but the things revealed belong to us and to our children forever, that we may follow all the words of this law. (Deuteronomy 29:29)

**The Purpose Revealed**

Yet here's what matters: God's choice isn't *against* you—it's *for* you.

> What if God, although choosing to show his wrath and make his power known, bore with great patience the objects of his wrath—prepared for destruction? What if he did this to make the riches of his glory known to the objects of his mercy, whom he prepared in advance for glory? (Romans 9:22-23)

**Why the arrangement?**
- Perhaps the only way God could demonstrate His love was through the radical sacrifice of His Son.
- Perhaps we cannot appreciate mercy without witnessing judgment.
- Perhaps humans cannot comprehend profound goodness except by comparison with profound evil.
- Perhaps God's "perception of choice" is essential to being human—and one day we will meet the Architect and ask for more explanation.

**The Concrete Example: Israel's Hardening Serves the Gentiles**

> Even us, whom he also called, not only from the Jews but also from the Gentiles? As he says in Hosea: "I will call them 'my people' who are not my people; and I will call her 'my loved one' who is not my loved one." And: "In the very place where it was said to them, 'You are not my people,' there they will be called 'children of the living God.'" (Romans 9:24-26)

The majority of Jews rejected Jesus. But what if their hardening was *the very mechanism* by which the gospel spread to the Gentiles?

**Think of it like a video game:** There are players and NPCs (non-player characters). NPCs add story, background, and narrative depth. They make the game interesting. But the game is *for the players*.

- Isaac is the player. Ishmael is the NPC.
- Jacob is the player. Esau is the NPC.
- The Gentiles who believe are the players. The Jews who reject Jesus are part of the narrative backdrop.

Don't become fatalistic just because you might be an NPC. That's a strange reaction. If God is the architect of this story, then the better question is: *Whose side do you want to be on?*

---

## The Decision Before Us

God's sovereignty doesn't eliminate human responsibility—it frames it. We make real choices *within* God's will. Our decisions genuinely matter because they align us either with God or against Him.

**The matchups are clear:**
- Will you be like Jacob or Esau?
- Will you be like Isaac or Ishmael?
- Will you be among the Gentiles who received the gospel, or like the Jews who rejected it?
- Will you be an object of mercy or an object of wrath?

God already knows the answer. But *you* don't. You can choose to align yourself with God's purpose *right now*.

> Unless the Lord Almighty had left us descendants, we would have become like Sodom, we would have been like Gomorrah. (Isaiah 10:9)

What then shall we say? The Gentiles—who didn't pursue righteousness—obtained it through faith. But Israel, pursuing righteousness through works, stumbled:

> Why not? Because they pursued it not by faith but as if it were by works. They stumbled over the stumbling stone. (Romans 9:32)

---

## Conclusion: The Solution Is Choice

The problem isn't choice. **The solution is choice—God's choice, which invites your choice.**

Open that Schrödinger's box. Make a choice to follow Jesus and embrace God's will. Jesus offers us the assurance of salvation. Not "I'll try"; not "maybe"; but *assurance*.

The question is: Will you submit your will to His? Will you trust that His sovereignty is for your good?
