# James 5:13-20

**Title:** Prayer of Faith
**Date:** 23 September 2012
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## A Church Built on God's Word

We're launching as a new church in unusual circumstances—without a building, without institutional resources, yet beginning with this passage from James. Some might think this strange timing, but it reflects our deepest conviction: we listen to God first. The Word of God matters to us, not merely in theory but in how we *read* it—word by word, sentence by sentence, in order as written.

We could have started a fresh series. Instead, we're continuing our journey through James because we trust that God's Word speaks to our present situation, whatever it brings.

## Part 1: Understanding Spiritual Sickness (13-15)

> Is anyone among you in trouble? Let them pray. Is anyone happy? Let them sing songs of praise. Is anyone among you sick? Let them call the elders of the church to pray over them and anoint them with oil in the name of the Lord. And the prayer offered in faith will make the sick person well; the Lord will raise them up. If they have sinned, they will be forgiven. (James 5:13-15)

We typically read this passage as addressing physical sickness. But examining the original Greek reveals something deeper—James is speaking primarily about *spiritual* sickness.

### The Greek Words Matter

**Verse 13 — "trouble"** (kakopatheo): This word carries meaning beyond physical illness. It encompasses suffering and hardship, as James uses it earlier when discussing Job's trials (5:10). Job suffered not only physical sickness but also loss of family, wealth, and devastating loneliness.

**Verse 14 — "sick"** (astheneo): Remarkably, in the New Testament, this word translates equally as physical sickness or spiritual/moral sickness. Yet in the Old Testament Greek (Septuagint), it never refers to physical sickness. When Hebrew words for disease are rendered into Greek, a variety of words appears—but *never* this one. The word appears for emotional despair (Job 4:4), economic collapse (Psalm 105:37), loss of strength (Lamentations 1:14), and even death (Daniel 11:19).

**Verse 15 — "sick"** (kamno): This word means weary, tired, or discouraged—the same word Paul uses in Hebrews 12:3 when warning against growing weary and losing heart.

### The Root Problem: Sin

This spiritual sickness is sin. Notice how sin surfaces repeatedly in verses 15-16:

> If they have sinned, they will be forgiven. Therefore confess your sins to each other and pray for each other so that you may be healed. (James 5:15-16)

Reading through James, we encounter sin at every turn. It manifests in many forms:
- *Not doing good* is sin (James 4:17)
- *Favoritism* is sin (James 2:9)
- *Desire and envy* conceive and give birth to sin, which when full-grown produces death (James 1:15)

James intends to make us uncomfortable. We should feel the weight of our sin. We should fear it, because sin at full growth brings death—not merely physical death, but separation from God forever.

### On Physical Healing

I'm not denying that God heals physically. He is sovereign and can heal anyone, anytime. We hear genuine testimonies of healing, and we believe them because God is God.

Yet the church often promotes an exaggerated version of faith healing—a formula where the right words produce guaranteed results, as if we control God's power. This dangerously inverts the order: God heals in His time and way, not through human manipulation or payment. We've witnessed the harm: unrealistic expectations, people claiming promises God never made, then doubting His goodness when healing doesn't come, feeling unworthy.

But rejecting this excess shouldn't push us toward a purely scientific worldview where only the measurable exists. We've seen what science alone cannot fix. Prayer works in ways we cannot always quantify—and that's okay.

The deeper point: physical illness, while terrifying, is not our greatest problem. *Death* is. *God's judgment* is. Cancer might kill us in this life, but sin kills us eternally. James addresses the ultimate problem—our standing before a holy God.

## Part 2: Spiritual Healing (15-16)

If the sickness is spiritual, the healing must be spiritual too. Look closely at these verses:

> And the prayer offered in faith will make the sick person well; the Lord will raise them up. If they have sinned, they will be forgiven. Therefore confess your sins to each other and pray for each other so that you may be healed. The prayer of a righteous person is powerful and effective. (James 5:15-16)

The phrase "will make the sick person well" literally means "shall save the sick." Forgiveness isn't a transaction we earn—it's salvation itself.

The healing described here is different from anything a hospital can provide. It comes through a restored relationship with God. As Peter writes:

> He himself bore our sins in his body on the tree, so that we might die to sins and live for righteousness; by his wounds you have been healed. For you were like sheep going astray, but now you have returned to the Shepherd and Overseer of your souls. (1 Peter 2:24-25)

Our church takes its name, Calvary, from Golgotha—the place of suffering. Here Christ suffered for our sins, bore them in His body, and purchased our forgiveness. On that hill, *real hope* is finally given. We need not remain dead in our sins, forever facing God's wrath. We can be well.

Many are physically healthy yet internally desperate. We confuse physical wellness with true wholeness, forgetting what Jesus asked:

> What good is it for someone to gain the whole world, yet forfeit their soul? (Mark 8:36)

Our soul's healing is what matters most.

## Part 3: How to Access This Healing

### Prayer of Faith

> And the prayer offered in faith will make the sick person well; the Lord will raise them up. If they have sinned, they will be forgiven. Therefore confess your sins to each other and pray for each other so that you may be healed. (James 5:15-16)

The "prayer of faith" (literally, "prayer of the faith") is not some magical quality dependent on our intensity, repetition, or emotion. Jesus warned against this thinking:

> And when you pray, do not keep on babbling like pagans, for they think they will be heard because of their many words. (Matthew 6:7)

Instead, the prayer of faith means *trusting that God will forgive us*. It means approaching God with the conviction that He keeps His promises. The Lord's Prayer models this:

> Our Father in heaven, hallowed be your name, your kingdom come, your will be done on earth as it is in heaven. Give us today our daily bread. Forgive us our debts, as we also have forgiven our debtors. And lead us not into temptation, but deliver us from the evil one. For if you forgive men when they sin against you, your heavenly Father will also forgive you. But if you do not forgive men their sins, your Father will not forgive your sins. (Matthew 6:9-15)

This salvation is for those who trust in Jesus. When we repent in prayer, confessing our sins, God promises to save us, forgive us, and spiritually heal us. This is genuinely good news for a people facing death.

Whether we pray for ourselves in confession or intercede for others, the foundation is the same: *we trust that God will do what He says*.

### Confession With One Another

James 5:16 shows the church's corporate responsibility:

> Therefore confess your sins to each other and pray for each other so that you may be healed.

This isn't about finding a priest with special powers. The elders and mature believers mentioned in verse 14 have no authority to grant forgiveness—that belongs to God alone. We confess to one another so that we might be *lifted up*.

When someone confesses genuine sin—perhaps on their knees, tears flowing—we respond by reminding them of their forgiveness. Whether they sinned against us (and we offer our forgiveness) or against God (and we assure them of His), we anoint them with oil as a sign that mourning is over. This was mentioned in James 4:7-10:

> Submit yourselves, then, to God. Resist the devil, and he will flee from you. Come near to God and he will come near to you. Wash your hands, you sinners, and purify your hearts, you double-minded. Grieve, mourn and wail. Change your laughter to mourning and your joy to gloom. Humble yourselves before the Lord, and he will lift you up.

Genuine confession sounds like: "I was wrong. I made a commitment I didn't keep. I was lazy. I'm sorry and I want to make it right." Not "I'm sorry you feel that way" or "I'm sorry I got caught."

When confession is real, we respond with grace. We welcome them back into fellowship and community. We say: "Arise, brother. Your sins are forgiven."

### The Church as a Community of Sinners

Why do we gather as a church? To learn Scripture, certainly. To build community, yes. But fundamentally, we come because we are sinners who need God's grace.

We sin against God, and we're here to confess those sins to one another, sharing our spiritual weakness in a safe community.

We sin against each other—stepping on toes, speaking harshly, acting selfishly. The church is where we can admit these failures. We can talk to those we've wronged. We can share our struggles openly.

When we confess and pray for one another, something beautiful happens:

> My brothers and sisters, if one of you should wander from the truth and someone should bring that person back, remember this: Whoever turns a sinner from the error of their way will save them from death and cover over a multitude of sins. (James 5:19-20)

There is power in correction rooted in love. When we help a wandering brother or sister return to the truth, we're participating in their salvation from death itself.

## Part 4: The Example of Elijah

> The prayer of a righteous person is powerful and effective. Elijah was a human being, even as we are. He prayed earnestly that it would not rain, and it did not rain on the land for three and a half years. Again he prayed, and the heavens gave rain, and the earth produced its crops. (James 5:16-18)

We read this and think, "That's not us. We're not like Elijah!" But James makes his point clear: *Elijah was a human being, even as we are*. He was a sinner who struggled to obey God, yet his prayers moved the hand of God. If he could pray with such power, so can we.

Scripture tells us:

> For the eyes of the Lord are on the righteous and his ears are attentive to their prayer, but the face of the Lord is against those who do evil. (1 Peter 3:12)

As Christians, we are called "the righteous ones." God's ears are attentive to our prayers—especially our prayers for righteousness, for confession of sin, for repentance.

Elijah's example shows us that prayer isn't reserved for spiritual giants. It's the privilege of every believer who trusts God's promises.

## Conclusion: The Gospel at the End of James

James has asked much of us—to produce fruit: humility, patience, gentleness, care for the orphan and the poor, active goodness. It's a demanding book.

I confess: I finish each week regretting harsh words, missed opportunities to do good, failures in love. Yet what a gracious way James concludes. Despite our failures, we can confess our sins to God and to one another and find forgiveness in Jesus Christ.

James doesn't tell us these things to condemn us but to bring us wholeness. God wants to see the good fruit of His Word in our lives. He wants to see Jesus reflected in all we do and say. Yes, we fail. But we persevere, trying again, secure in Christ's salvation.

Some say James contains no gospel. But here it is: Through Jesus's blood, we have a promise of friendship and forgiveness. What a wonderful gospel this is.

**Prayer of Confession**

We are the people of God. Scripture reminds us that we still sin. We need to confess our failures, knowing that Jesus intercedes for us with the Father, who freely forgives us.

*Heavenly Father,*
*You have loved us with an everlasting love,*
*But we have gone our own way and broken your laws.*
*We are sorry for our sins and turn away from them.*
*For the sake of your Son who died for us,*
*Forgive us, cleanse us and change us.*
*By your Holy Spirit, enable us to live for you,*
*And to please you more and more;*
*Through Jesus Christ our Lord. Amen.*
