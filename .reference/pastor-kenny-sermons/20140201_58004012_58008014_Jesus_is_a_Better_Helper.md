# Hebrews 4:12-8:14

**Title:** Jesus is a Better Helper
**Date:** 15 February 2014
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Where Do We Go for Help?

When life brings trouble, we instinctively know where to turn. Our car breaks down—we call the NRMA. We have a legal problem—we consult a lawyer. We're sick—we see a doctor. We face financial hardship—we ask our parents.

But when our deepest struggle is with God—with sin, guilt, shame, or spiritual failure—where do we go? The answer Hebrews proclaims is clear: **we go to Jesus.**

Over these past weeks, we've learned that Jesus is better: a better messenger than the angels, a better man than Moses, a better leader than Joshua. Today we discover that Jesus is also a **better helper**—specifically, a better high priest.

---

## 1. We Have a Better Representative (4:14-5:10)

### The Role of a High Priest

In Israel's history, the high priest held a sacred role: he was God's appointed representative, standing between God and the people.

> Therefore, since we have a great high priest who has gone through the heavens, Jesus the Son of God, let us hold firmly to the faith we profess. (Hebrews 4:14)

> Every high priest is selected from among men and is appointed to represent them in matters related to God, to offer gifts and sacrifices for sins. (Hebrews 5:1)

The high priest's work centered on matters related to God, accomplished primarily through gifts and sacrifices for sin—the means by which the people sought appeasement for their wrongs against God.

### The Essential Requirement: Being Like the People

There's something crucial here: because the priest represents the people, he must *be* like the people.

> He is able to deal gently with those who are ignorant and are going astray, since he himself is subject to weakness. (Hebrews 5:2)

The priest's humanity is not a weakness—it's a necessity. It takes one to know one. A good politician understands his constituents because he comes from among them, lives in their community, knows their struggles firsthand. Imagine a politician who flies in from overseas, unfamiliar with local issues after years abroad—how could he truly represent those people? Or a member of parliament representing one suburb while living in another—he's out of touch, unable to understand the real needs of those he claims to represent.

Similarly, a priest must share the weaknesses of those he represents so he can deal with them gently and understand their struggles.

### The Appointment Must Come from God

Yet there's another requirement: the priest cannot appoint himself.

> No one takes this honor upon himself; he must be called by God, just as Aaron was. (Hebrews 5:4)

The priesthood isn't a position someone seizes or earns through popularity. It's a calling from God Himself.

### Jesus Fulfills Both Requirements—and Surpasses Them

Jesus meets both of these demands perfectly, and then exceeds them entirely.

**First, Jesus was truly appointed by God:**

> So Christ also did not take upon himself the glory of becoming a high priest. But God said to him, "You are my Son; today I have become your Father." And he says in another place, "You are a priest forever, in the order of Melchizedek." (Hebrews 5:5-6)

His priesthood is not self-assumed. God Himself declared it.

**Second, Jesus was truly representative—he genuinely identified with our human experience:**

> During the days of Jesus' life on earth, he offered up prayers and petitions with loud cries and tears to the one who could save him from death, and he was heard because of his reverent submission. Although he was a son, he learned obedience from what he suffered and, once made perfect, he became the source of eternal salvation for all who obey him and was designated by God to be high priest in the order of Melchizedek. (Hebrews 5:7-10)

Notice something profound here: Jesus' prayers and petitions were not simply for His own deliverance. The text says "he was heard"—His prayers were answered positively. And the passage culminates with the promise of eternal salvation. Jesus was crying out with loud cries and tears *for us*, interceding with God to deal kindly with us.

This is what makes Him an effective representative: not only does He understand our weakness and can deal gently with us, but His prayers on our behalf are actually heard and answered. His intercession matters. It accomplishes something.

### The Fatal Flaw of Human Representatives

But here's the problem with merely human priests. While representation is good—they understand weakness and can deal gently—the representative is only as good as the people he comes from.

We complain about our politicians, but they are us. We're frustrated by their corruption, yet they're simply doing what everyone else does. As the saying goes, we're led by the people we deserve.

The same problem plagued the Jewish priesthood. Their strength as human representatives was also their fatal flaw:

> This is why he has to offer sacrifices for his own sins, as well as for the sins of the people. (Hebrews 5:3)

Before the priest could help the people, he had to fix himself first. He was trapped in the very sin he was trying to cover for others. But what if his sins were too great? What if everyone's sins were too much? Then you have the blind leading the blind—like asking an aging, blind eye surgeon to remove your cataract. You appreciate his qualifications and care, but he simply cannot help you.

The old high priests were ultimately unable to deal with sin effectively. They were sinful themselves. They were like obese nutritionists giving diet advice, high school dropouts tutoring students, smokers telling others to quit. If they had truly solved the problem for themselves, they would have already applied the solution long ago. But they didn't—which proved they couldn't.

### The Effective Representative

But in Jesus, we have an effective representative:

> For we do not have a high priest who is unable to sympathize with our weaknesses, but we have one who has been tempted in every way, just as we are—yet was without sin. (Hebrews 4:15)

Here is the divine mystery and miracle: Jesus is truly one of us, our brother who understands weakness. He was tempted in every way just as we are. Yet He remained without sin. Somehow—and I confess I don't fully understand how—through self-control, sacrifice, and a heart of pure gold, He conquered sin in a way we haven't.

So as our representative, we can approach Him in our times of need:

> Let us then approach the throne of grace with confidence, so that we may receive mercy and find grace to help us in our time of need. (Hebrews 4:16)

This invitation covers *everything*. Nothing is too small to bring before Jesus. Nothing is too big for Him to handle.

### We Can Bring Everything to Jesus

The apostle Paul captures this beautifully:

> Do not be anxious about anything, but in everything, by prayer and petition, with thanksgiving, present your requests to God. And the peace of God, which transcends all understanding, will guard your hearts and your minds in Christ Jesus. (Philippians 4:6-7)

In everything. Nothing is excluded. The promise isn't always a "yes" to our requests—we can't treat God like a genie granting wishes. But God promises something even better: He will guard our hearts and minds. Whatever His answer—yes, no, maybe, or ask again—His answer is always for our good.

## 2. Our Only Priest

There's another critical implication of Jesus being our high priest: **He is our only priest.** We can only approach God through Jesus in matters concerning God.

### The Order of Melchizedek

Why is this? Because Jesus belongs to a different priestly order entirely, one mentioned repeatedly throughout this passage:

> "You are a priest forever, in the order of Melchizedek." (Hebrews 5:6, 5:10)

To Jewish readers, this was revolutionary. What does it mean to be a priest in the order of Melchizedek? Who was Melchizedek, and why does this matter?

Chapter 7 reveals more about this mysterious figure:

> This Melchizedek was king of Salem and priest of God Most High. He met Abraham returning from the defeat of the kings and blessed him, and Abraham gave him a tenth of everything. (Hebrews 7:1-2)

This brief story appears in Genesis 14, and it's the only time Melchizedek appears in Scripture—until Hebrews. He emerges once, then vanishes. Yet his significance is enormous.

### What Made Melchizedek Greater

Melchizedek had claims to authority that no ordinary priest possessed:

**He was a king.** Melchizedek carried a royal title. No Israelite priest was ever called a king. His very name means "king of righteousness," and "king of Salem" means "king of peace"—righteousness and peace combined in one figure.

**He was eternal.** The text says:

> Without father or mother, without genealogy, without beginning of days or end of life, like the Son of God he remains a priest forever. (Hebrews 7:3)

Melchizedek appears in Scripture with no genealogy, no ancestors, no descendants—he's a mysterious, eternal figure who simply exists.

**He was greater than Abraham himself.** Consider this:

> Just think how great he was: Even the patriarch Abraham gave him a tenth of the plunder! Now the law requires the descendants of Levi who become priests to collect a tenth from the people—that is, their brothers—even though their brothers are descended from Abraham. (Hebrews 7:4-5)

Here's the family hierarchy: Abraham had Isaac. Isaac had Jacob. Jacob had twelve sons. From Judah came Jesus. From Levi came the high priests. The Levites were in charge of collecting tithes, so every other tribe—every descendant of Abraham's other sons—submitted to Levi and gave them a tenth.

But Melchizedek did something extraordinary:

> This man, however, did not trace his descent from Levi, yet he collected a tenth from Abraham and blessed him who had the promises. And without doubt the lesser person is blessed by the greater. (Hebrews 7:6-7)

Who is greater—Abraham or Melchizedek? Obviously Melchizedek, because he's the one who receives the tithe and pronounces the blessing. If Melchizedek is greater than Abraham, then logically, Melchizedek is greater than all of Abraham's descendants, including Levi and all the high priests who came after him.

The argument continues:

> In the one case, the tenth is collected by men who die; but in the other case, by him who is declared to be living... Levi, who collects the tenth, paid the tenth through Abraham, because when Melchizedek met Abraham, Levi was still in the body of his ancestor. (Hebrews 7:8-10)

It's almost as if Abraham, Isaac, Jacob, and all their descendants—all Israel itself—submitted to Melchizedek's greater authority while still in Abraham's loins. Melchizedek's order supersedes even the Levitical priesthood.

### Jesus as a Priest Not After the Order of Levi

Now, here's the Jewish problem: Jesus came from Judah, not Levi.

> For it is clear that our Lord descended from Judah, and in regard to that tribe Moses said nothing about priests. (Hebrews 7:14)

The Jews could legitimately ask: How can Jesus be a priest if He doesn't come from the priestly tribe? How can He be a legitimate representative? His genealogy disqualifies Him from the Levitical priesthood.

The answer is revolutionary:

> If perfection could have been attained through the Levitical priesthood (for on the basis of it the law was given to the people), why was there still need for another priest to come—one in the order of Melchizedek, not in the order of Aaron? For when there is a change of the priesthood, there must also be a change of the law. (Hebrews 7:11-12)

The old system was insufficient. A new priest—one in the order of Melchizedek—was necessary precisely because the Levitical system couldn't achieve perfection. When the priesthood changes, the law changes too. The entire system is replaced.

> And what we have said is even more clear if another priest like Melchizedek appears, one who has become a priest not on the basis of a regulation as to his ancestry but on the basis of the power of an indestructible life. For it is declared: "You are a priest forever, in the order of Melchizedek." (Hebrews 7:15-17)

Jesus traces His priestly authority not through genealogy but through immortality. Like Melchizedek, His priesthood is based not on lineage but on eternal, indestructible life.

It's like when the FBI shows up at a local police scene and takes over. The junior officer who's been bullied by the other cops suddenly reveals he's an undercover FBI agent—and now he calls the shots. The local police are superseded by a greater authority.

Jesus doesn't need to follow the genealogy line to be high priest. He trumps the genealogy entirely by drawing on another link—the order of the immortal Melchizedek. By His eternal life, He joins that superior priesthood.

### The Old Priesthood Is Ended

As a result, there is no more Levitical priesthood.

> The former regulation is set aside because it was weak and useless (for the law made nothing perfect), and a better hope is introduced, by which we draw near to God. (Hebrews 7:18-19)

The old system was weak and useless because it made nothing perfect. It could not accomplish what God desired—the complete, permanent forgiveness of sin and the restoration of the relationship between God and His people.

**There are no earthly priests today.** Not one.

I know this may surprise some of you, especially if you come from traditions that use priestly language. But it's important to understand: Christian ministers and pastors are servants and shepherds, but they are not priests in the biblical sense.

Years ago, a Year 12 boy came into one of the churches I was serving and asked for a priest. I saw an opportunity and said, "Here I am—let's talk." But I had to clarify: I'm not a priest. (That young man actually became a Christian that very day.)

Part of why we mistakenly think church pastors are priests comes from something that happened centuries after Jesus. A few hundred years into the Christian era, the church began reusing Old Testament Jewish language and imagery. The church building became a "temple." The Lord's Supper became a "sacrifice." The person leading worship became a "priest." Even though the actual temple was gone, the actual sacrifice was complete, and the priesthood had been fulfilled and abolished.

We fall into the same trap, sometimes without realizing it. Depending on our background, it's easier to explain Christian ideas using existing religious language. We say, "The church is like a temple. The minister is like a monk." We transpose old categories onto new realities. It becomes a special person in a special place offering special things to a power.

Additionally, there's an etymological issue. The Greek word for elder or leader is *presbyter*. When this was translated into Latin, the word became "priest." Eventually, the Latin word *priest* was used to translate the Greek *hiereus* (priest). In the confusion, the meaning shifted. What once meant "leader" became "hiereus"—someone standing between God and people. We lost the meaning in translation.

**There are no heavenly priests either.** No mediators in heaven to whom we can pray and expect an answer.

This is a sensitive area, I know. Many feel deeply connected to loved ones who have passed away. But we must be honest with Scripture: the dead cannot answer prayers. We cannot pray to our ancestors, the saints, or Mary, no matter how dear they are to us. They are dead, and the dead do not intercede.

Whatever view we hold about the intermediate state—the condition of the dead between now and the final judgment—the conclusion remains: they cannot answer prayers. They cannot intercede for us. They are as useless as the Levitical priesthood for purposes of approaching God.

Instead, **pray to Jesus.** He is the one who sits in the heavenly realms with direct access to the Father. He lives eternally to intercede for us.

## 3. The Better Guarantee (6:16-8:2)

Because Jesus is our better representative and our only priest, we have something the old system could never provide: **absolute assurance and guarantee for our hope.**

> Men swear by someone greater than themselves, and the oath confirms what is said and puts an end to all argument. Because God wanted to make the unchanging nature of his purpose very clear to the heirs of what was promised, he confirmed it with an oath. God did this so that, by two unchangeable things—the promise and the oath—in which it is impossible for God to lie, we who have fled to take hold of the hope offered to us may be greatly encouraged. (Hebrews 6:16-18)

God didn't just make a promise. He backed it with an oath. Two unchangeable things. It is impossible for God to lie. Our hope rests on a foundation that cannot fail.

> We have this hope as an anchor for the soul, firm and secure. It enters the inner sanctuary behind the curtain, where Jesus, who went before us, has entered on our behalf. He has become a high priest forever, in the order of Melchizedek. (Hebrews 6:19-20)

Our hope is an anchor—a secure, immovable anchor for the soul. And it is fastened to Jesus Himself, who has already entered the heavenly sanctuary on our behalf.

### Jesus' Oath Sets Him Apart

The old high priests were appointed without any oath:

> And it was not without an oath! Others became priests without any oath, but he became a priest with an oath when God said to him: "The Lord has sworn and will not change his mind: 'You are a priest forever.'" Because of this oath, Jesus has become the guarantee of a better covenant. (Hebrews 7:20-22)

Jesus' priesthood is guaranteed by God's own oath. This is His unique distinction. While the Levites became priests through mere appointment, Jesus was established through a divine promise that God has sworn never to change. **Jesus Himself is the guarantee of everything God has promised to us.**

### Jesus Offers a Permanent, Effective Priesthood

Because Jesus lives forever, His priesthood is permanent and effective in a way the old system never was:

> Now there have been many of those priests, since death prevented them from continuing in office; but because Jesus lives forever, he has a permanent priesthood. Therefore he is able to save completely those who come to God through him, because he always lives to intercede for them. (Hebrews 7:23-25)

The old priests were many because they died. They could serve only for a time. But Jesus lives forever. He never dies. His priesthood never ends. And most importantly: **He always lives to intercede for us.**

He's not passive, sitting in heaven while we fend for ourselves. He's actively, continuously interceding—praying for us, advocating for us before the Father. His work on our behalf never stops.

> Such a high priest meets our need—one who is holy, blameless, pure, set apart from sinners, exalted above the heavens. Unlike the other high priests, he does not need to offer sacrifices day after day, first for his own sins, and then for the sins of the people. He sacrificed for their sins once for all when he offered himself. For the law appoints as high priests men who are weak; but the oath, which came after the law, appointed the Son, who has been made perfect forever. (Hebrews 7:26-28)

Jesus is qualitatively different from the old priests. He doesn't need to offer repeated sacrifices because He offered Himself once—a complete, final, perfect sacrifice. He doesn't need to atone for His own sins because He is holy, blameless, and pure. He is exalted above the heavens, seated at the right hand of the Father.

> The point of what we are saying is this: We do have such a high priest, who sat down at the right hand of the throne of the Majesty in heaven, and who serves in the sanctuary, the true tabernacle set up by the Lord, not by man. (Hebrews 8:1-2)

## The Better Helper

So let's return to our opening question: Where do we go for help?

It's wonderful to have someone helping you. But Jesus offers us something incomparably greater:

**Someone there 24/7.** Jesus' priesthood is permanent. He will never retire, never abandon His post, never become unable to help because of age or death.

**Someone on the inside who knows exactly what to do.** Jesus is seated in the heavenly realms with direct access to the Father's throne. He understands the full picture and knows precisely how to intercede on our behalf.

**Someone who is not a stranger, but a friend.** You know He's not in it for the money or personal gain. He simply wants to help. As Hebrews 7:25 says, He "always lives to intercede" for you. His entire existence is devoted to your welfare.

The writer of Hebrews urges us: **Trust Jesus.**

Some of you may be feeling the acute need for help more keenly than ever—sickness, death in the family, serious financial problems, a breaking family, deep worries. Whatever the burden you carry, Hebrews announces good news: you can approach God through your priest Jesus. He will help you.

Go ask Him.
