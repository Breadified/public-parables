# Colossians 2:1-23

**Title:** Christ Sufficiency  
**Date:** 15 May 2022 (ARISE), 30 October 2022 (PHCAC)  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Freedom from Judgment in Christ

Every culture has its social rules and norms—expectations about how to dress, what to eat, how to greet others, which traditions to honor. Japan has its intricate etiquette about shoes and bowing. Russia has its customs about not whistling indoors or shaking hands with gloves on. These rules reflect deeper patterns of social order and judgment: fail to follow them, and you invite criticism and exclusion.

But as Christians, we face a profound question: what rules must we follow to have freedom from judgment?

The answer is paradoxical but liberating: **no rules**. We have freedom from judgment in Christ. This passage opens with Paul's passionate concern for the Colossian church, threatened by false philosophies that would enslave them through human traditions and worldly principles. But Christ's sufficiency means we are no longer captive to these systems.

---

## Don't Be Captive to Hollow Philosophy (2:8)

Paul opens with an urgent command:

> See to it that no one takes you captive through hollow and deceptive philosophy, which depends on human tradition and the elemental spiritual forces of this world rather than on Christ.

The danger is real. Philosophy ("hollow and deceptive") here refers to systems that depend on:
- **Human tradition** (Greek: *tradition of men*)—particularly the Jewish traditions of the Pharisees and teachers of the law
- **Elemental spiritual forces** (Greek: *stoicheia*, the basic principles or patterns of this world)

These are worldly systems that stand opposed to Christ. They promise wisdom but deliver only captivity. Paul's warning is direct: don't let anyone take you prisoner through these ideas.

---

## The Shadow and the Reality: Human Traditions (2:16-17)

The specific threat becomes clear in verse 16:

> Therefore do not let anyone judge you by what you eat or drink, or with regard to a religious festival, a New Moon celebration or a Sabbath day.

Paul is speaking directly about **Jewish traditions**—the extra-biblical laws that had grown up around the Torah through centuries of rabbinical interpretation:
- The written Torah itself
- The Oral Law (Mishnah)
- The commentary (Gemarah)
- Countless derived regulations about purity, Sabbath observance, dietary laws, and festival practices

The traditional reading of this passage is clear: these Old Testament observances were **shadows pointing to Christ**. Christ has come and fulfilled the law. As Paul writes elsewhere: "For sin will have no dominion over you, since you are not under law but under grace" (Romans 6:14). Therefore, no one should judge Jewish Christians for not keeping these old regulations, nor should they judge Gentile Christians for never having kept them at all.

However, there's another valid interpretation worth considering. The passage may also protect Jewish Christians *in* their practice. Look at Paul's words to the Corinthians:

> Each person should live as a believer in whatever situation the Lord has assigned to them. Was a man already circumcised when he was called? He should not become uncircumcised. Was a man uncircumcised when he was called? He should not be circumcised. (1 Corinthians 7:17-18)

Jews remain Jews; Gentiles remain Gentiles. So why should Jews be judged for keeping their ancestral practices? The deeper principle Paul emphasizes is that:

> Circumcision is nothing and uncircumcision is nothing. Keeping God's commands is what counts. (1 Corinthians 7:19)

Whether we interpret this as "you're free from these rules" or "you're free to keep your traditions without judgment," the emphasis is the same: **the rules themselves don't matter**. What matters is Christ.

---

## The Reality Is Christ's Body (2:17-19)

This is where the passage becomes deeply theological. Verse 17 is difficult in the Greek and ambiguous in English, but it points to something crucial:

> These are a shadow of the things that were to come; however, the reality, *[judge]* the body of Christ.

The grammar suggests a contrast: on one hand, don't judge by external regulations; on the other hand, *consider* the body of Christ. Look at Christ's physical reality.

Why? Because in Christ, something utterly complete has happened:

> For in Christ all the fullness of the Deity lives in bodily form, and in Christ you have been brought to fullness. He is the head over every power and authority. (2:9-10)

**All of God is in Jesus.** When you see Christ, you see God. All that Jesus does is what God does. Therefore, Christ's body is sufficient. His life, death, and resurrection have accomplished everything necessary for salvation and freedom.

---

## Circumcision and Baptism: The Rescue Through Christ's Body

Paul illustrates this through two powerful images:

**First, circumcision** (2:11):

> In him you were also circumcised with a circumcision not performed by human hands. In the removal of the body of the flesh in the circumcision of Christ, your whole self ruled by the flesh was put off when you were circumcised by Christ.

When Christ was cut—when He shed His blood on the cross—we were cut with Him. Our sins, the fleshly nature that enslaves us, were vicariously cut away. The physical rite of circumcision becomes a shadow of what Christ accomplished spiritually.

**Second, baptism** (2:12):

> Having been buried with him in baptism, in which you were also raised with him through your faith in the working of God, who raised him from the dead.

Baptism signifies death and resurrection. When you go under the water, you die to your old self; when you rise, you're raised into new life. John the Baptist called people to repent and be immersed as a symbol of turning from the old way of life.

The implication is profound: **the law can't condemn you if the charges have been pinned on someone else.** The law can't catch you if you're dead. How does this liberation work?

> When you were dead in your sins and in the uncircumcision of your flesh, God made you alive with Christ. He forgave us all our sins, having canceled the charge of our legal indebtedness, which stood against us and condemned us; he has taken it away, nailing it to the cross. And having disarmed the powers and authorities, he made a public spectacle of them, triumphing over them by the cross. (2:13-15)

The charges against us have been nailed to the cross. Christ died in our place. The authorities—whether demonic powers, human judgment, or the condemnation of the law—have been defeated. Christ is the victor.

---

## The Danger of Losing Connection with Christ (2:18-19)

Yet there's a subtle danger Paul warns against. Some are attracted to mystical experiences and the "worship of angels":

> Do not let anyone who delights in false humility and the worship of angels disqualify you. Such a person also goes into great detail about what they have seen; they are puffed up with idle notions by their unspiritual mind. They have lost connection with the head, from whom the whole body, supported and held together by its ligaments and sinews, grows as God causes it to grow. (2:18-19)

The problem isn't experiences themselves—it's that people become fascinated with their visions and spiritual encounters, then develop an inflated, fleshly mind that's disconnected from Christ. You can have great spiritual experiences at church and then live like the world the rest of the week. **The test is the mind—whether it's truly being renewed by Christ or still operating in earthly, fleshly patterns.**

When you lose connection with Christ (the head), you lose what holds the body together. Growth becomes impossible.

---

## Worldly Rules Are Powerless (2:20-23)

Paul's conclusion is stark. If you've died with Christ and been raised with Him, why would you submit to worldly rules?

> Since you died with Christ to the elemental spiritual forces of this world, why, as though you still belonged to the world, do you submit to its rules: "Do not handle! Do not taste! Do not touch!"? These rules, which have to do with things that are all destined to perish with use, are based on merely human commands and teachings. (2:20-22)

These rules might look wise—there's an appearance of piety in harsh self-denial and strict regulations. But here's the proof they don't work:

> Such regulations indeed have an appearance of wisdom, with their self-imposed worship, their false humility and their harsh treatment of the body, but they lack any value in restraining sensual indulgence. (2:23)

**Rules that aren't rooted in Christ don't actually transform us.** Whether it's the old Jewish regulations, modern philosophies about what to eat and wear, or contemporary political correctness, external rules without inner transformation are powerless.

Think about today's equivalents. We live in a culture increasingly filled with judgment about:
- What car you drive (electric or fossil fuel?)
- How you dispose of trash (recycling and composting?)
- What you eat (organic? cruelty-free?)
- What causes you support, what flags you display, what pronouns you use

To not follow the crowd is to invite judgment. We're living on eggshells. Yet here's the truth: **those same people pushing rigid moral rules often live in hidden contradiction, without real transformation of the heart.** The rules don't work because rules separated from Christ have no power.

---

## The Gospel Answer

Christ is sufficient. You are free.

Not free to do whatever you want, but free from the burden of performing for judgment. Free from the endless multiplication of rules. Free from the tyranny of human opinion and worldly systems.

Because:

- **In Christ, all fullness dwells.** You have access to everything you need.
- **Through His body (His death and resurrection), your body has been rescued.** You are no longer enslaved to sin or to the flesh.
- **The charges against you have been nailed to the cross.** You are legally and spiritually freed from condemnation.
- **Christ is the head; you belong to His body.** True growth, true community, true transformation happens only through connection with Him.

Therefore, don't be captive to hollow philosophies. Don't be judged by rules that promise wisdom but deliver only bondage. **Look to Christ.** His sufficiency is your freedom.
