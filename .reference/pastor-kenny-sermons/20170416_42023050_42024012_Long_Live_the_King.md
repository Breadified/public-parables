# Luke 23:50-24:12

**Title:** Long Live the King
**Date:** April 16, 2017
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Jesus Really Died (23:50-24:1)

The first truth we encounter in Scripture is that Jesus experienced genuine, physical death. Good Friday had come—Jesus was betrayed, tried, and executed. But the Gospel doesn't stop at his death on the cross. He was buried.

> There was a good and righteous man named Joseph, a member of the council, who had not agreed with their plan and action. He was from Arimathea, a Judean town, and was looking forward to the kingdom of God. He approached Pilate and asked for Jesus' body. Taking it down from the cross, he wrapped it in fine linen and placed it in a tomb cut into the rock, where no one had ever been placed.

Joseph of Arimathea was a man of influence—significant enough to approach the governor himself. He took Jesus's body with care and dignity, wrapping it in fine linen from his own supplies and placing it in his own tomb. This detail matters profoundly.

> The women who had come with Him from Galilee followed along and observed the tomb and how His body was placed. Then they returned and prepared spices and perfumes. And they rested on the Sabbath according to the commandment. On the first day of the week, very early in the morning, they came to the tomb, bringing the spices they had prepared.

The women witnessed the burial. They saw exactly where Jesus was laid. This is crucial testimony—multiple witnesses confirmed that Jesus was truly dead and truly buried.

### Why the Burial Matters

Burial is the universal sign of death. People may become unconscious and revive. They may suffer apparent death only to recover. But burial marks the final acknowledgment that someone is dead. When we bury someone, we say goodbye. We don't expect them to return. We lay them to rest in peace.

As one observer noted: "The statistics on death are quite impressive: one out of one people die." Death is the one absolute certainty that unites all humanity across all cultures and all centuries. Whether rich or poor, whether born in the first century or the twenty-first—all humans share our mortality. We are all truly equal in death.

### Why Jesus Had to Die

Death came as the penalty for sin. The Scripture is clear: "The wages of sin is death." All have sinned and fallen short of God's glory, and thus all face death as judgment.

But Jesus was different. He was perfect and righteous—he had no sin of his own. Yet he died anyway. He died not for his own sins, but for ours. His death was a ransom, a substitutionary payment. Like a hero in a story who offers himself in exchange for his loved ones, Jesus placed himself in death's grip so that we might go free. He died to redeem us from the clutches of sin and to give us a second chance at life.

## Jesus Really Resurrected (24:2-10)

The disciples knew Jesus was really dead. When they discovered the empty tomb, their shock was genuine.

> They found the stone rolled away from the tomb. They went in but did not find the body of the Lord Jesus. While they were perplexed about this, suddenly two men stood by them in dazzling clothes. So the women were terrified and bowed down to the ground.

Everything was wrong. The stone was gone. The body was gone. Only the linen cloths remained. When the women reported these astonishing events to the disciples:

> These words seemed like nonsense to them, and they did not believe the women. Peter, however, got up and ran to the tomb. When he stooped to look in, he saw only the linen cloths. So he went home, amazed at what had happened.

Notice their responses: nonsense, disbelief, perplexity, terror, amazement. These were not gullible people eager to believe anything. They knew with absolute certainty that Jesus was dead. Someone coming back from the dead was extraordinary and dismissible—just as it would be today.

### The Reality of the Resurrection

The angels spoke with clarity:

> "Why are you looking for the living among the dead? He is not here, but He has been resurrected!"

Not resuscitated. Not revived. Not reincarnated. Resurrected. Jesus was among the living, not in a tomb.

**The empty tomb itself is significant evidence.** If Jesus had merely fainted or somehow survived crucifixion, the Roman authorities and Jewish leaders could have simply produced his body and ended the movement immediately. They had guards. They had authority. Yet no body ever appeared.

**The witnesses testify to what they saw.** Multiple women, the disciples, eventually hundreds of believers all witnessed the risen Jesus. Their testimony was consistent and cost them dearly—many would face persecution and death for claiming Jesus was alive.

**The resurrection fulfills prophecy.** The angels reminded the women:

> "Remember how He spoke to you when He was still in Galilee, saying, 'The Son of Man must be betrayed into the hands of sinful men, be crucified, and rise on the third day'?" And they remembered His words.

Jesus had predicted this all along. This was not a fabrication or surprise invention—it was the fulfillment of what he had repeatedly taught. The apostles themselves recognized this as fulfillment of Old Testament prophecy, including the sign of Jonah who spent three days in the great fish.

### Addressing Objections

Some may demand "extraordinary evidence" for an extraordinary claim. This sounds reasonable but misses the point. Extraordinary claims don't require extraordinary evidence—they require ordinary evidence, properly evaluated. We don't need special evidence to know when a person is alive. We see them. We don't need special evidence to know when a person is dead. We attend their funeral. Why would we need more evidence to know a dead person is alive again? The evidence is the same: eyewitnesses, consistency, fulfilled prophecy, the absence of contrary explanations.

What is truly extraordinary is not the evidence, but the implications. If this is true—if Jesus conquered death itself—then everything changes.

## The Hope the Resurrection Brings

For those who reject God, the resurrection has no meaning. If Jesus did not rise, then logically nothing has meaning at all. Any accomplishment will decay. Any good work will become obsolete. When our brief lives end, nothing we've done will matter. As the atheist view suggests: eat, drink, for tomorrow we die. No point in building for the future or investing in anything eternal.

But for Christians, the resurrection transforms everything.

The resurrection is our promise that the life we live is not in vain. We have significance both in time and for eternity. Our lives have purpose beyond these eighty or so years on earth because God has promised that our investments in eternity will not return empty.

Because Jesus conquered death, we now anticipate victory over the grave. His triumph gives us courage to endure all temporal tragedies and wisdom to cherish every earthly joy. His victory over death assures us that nothing is too dead for him to revive. Whatever our circumstances, better days are coming. We have no fear of our own death.

### The Power of Hope

Viktor Frankl, a Holocaust survivor who endured unimaginable horrors—physical abuse, daily threats of death, loss of all possessions and loved ones—observed that people in these circumstances reacted in radically different ways. Some lost hope and gave up. Others, maintaining hope, found the strength to endure and even to praise God in the face of certain death.

He concluded that a person can survive without food for weeks, but cannot survive without hope for more than a minute. Hope is that vital to human resilience.

Our children need to believe in the resurrection. Not so they collapse into despair or depression at every failure, but so they can rise again. Suffering and loss are part of every human life. Minor setbacks and catastrophic tragedies come to us all. But why do some persevere while others quit? Hope. The resurrection gives us that hope.

### Living in Light of the Resurrection

When we truly grasp that Jesus is no longer dead, it transforms our expectations about death itself. A man has come through death to the other side and emerged alive. Death is not the end. There is more to the story.

As Paul wrote:

> We do not want you to be uninformed, brothers, concerning those who are asleep, so that you will not grieve like the rest, who have no hope.

Christian funerals are fundamentally different from those without hope. We can say "see you later" and mean it, knowing we will meet again in resurrected bodies. We don't say goodbye as to the dead. We say goodbye as to the living, awaiting resurrection.

### The Assurance of Salvation

The resurrection matters directly for our salvation. Paul writes:

> For if, while we were enemies, we were reconciled to God through the death of His Son, then how much more, having been reconciled, will we be saved by His life!

Christ didn't merely die for our sins—he rose to intercede for us. He lives now to plead our case before the Father. His death covered our past sins, but his resurrection ensures that our present and future sins are also covered. Jesus constantly intercedes for those who trust in him.

This is why the resurrection is the foundation of Christian hope. Death has no final claim on us. Suffering will not be our end. And we will not face judgment alone, for Jesus himself stands before God on our behalf.

---

## Living for the Resurrection

How should this change the way we live? Titus tells us:

> The grace of God has appeared with salvation for all people, instructing us to deny godlessness and worldly lusts and to live in a sensible, righteous, and godly way in the present age, while we wait for the blessed hope and appearing of the glory of our great God and Savior, Jesus Christ.

We are called to deny godlessness—living as though God doesn't matter—and to reject worldly lusts that bury us alive in temporary pursuits. Instead, we live righteous and godly lives now, in anticipation of his appearing.

Consider the Sri Lankan man who tried to set a world record for being buried alive, only to discover the record didn't exist. He lived for a lie and died for foolishness. How many of us are doing the same? Chasing records that don't exist? Burying ourselves alive in pursuit of wealth, status, possessions, or pleasure—things that won't matter when we stand before God?

In God's eyes, there is no record for the richest person on earth. There is no medal for the one with the most toys. The person who serves is the greatest. The greatest investment is not in accumulating stuff but in doing the works prepared for us to do—works of justice, mercy, and love.

We have been forgiven. We have been given new life through the resurrection. So use your time well. Don't waste your holidays on empty pursuits. Live for the resurrection. Be alive to serve God and advance his kingdom.

This is the heart of the Gospel: Jesus really died, and Jesus really rose. And because he did, death has no final word over us. This is hope. This is why we celebrate. This is why, as we face our own mortality and the suffering of this world, we can say with confidence: the best is yet to come.
