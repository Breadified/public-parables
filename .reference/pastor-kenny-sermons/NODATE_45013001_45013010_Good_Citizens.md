# Romans 13:1-10

**Title:** Good Citizens
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction: What Makes a Good Citizen?

Many international students at Western Sydney University dream of staying in Australia long term, but they often tell me how rigorous the citizenship process is. For those of us born here, we rarely think about it—but becoming an Australian citizen requires persistent residency, passing a citizenship test covering everything from prime ministers to the opal as our national gemstone, and paying fees along the way.

From a government's perspective, this rigor makes sense. When people join a nation, we want to ensure they'll be good citizens—productive members of society who serve their community. This raises an important question for us as Christians: **What does God say about being a good citizen? How should we relate to our government and society?**

## Point 1: Submit to the Law (Romans 13:1-7)

### God Establishes Authority for Our Good

Paul begins this passage by appealing to our readers to live a life that worships God through our conduct. But he goes further: living under Jesus doesn't just change us personally—it transforms how we relate to our government and governing authorities.

> Let everyone be subject to the governing authorities, for there is no authority except that which God has established. The authorities that exist have been established by God. (v1)

Paul is clear: all Christians must be subject to their governing authorities and obey their laws. Whether it's kings, ministers, or other forms of government, God is the one who establishes them in their positions—and he does so for a vital purpose.

> For rulers hold no terror for those who do right, but for those who do wrong. Do you want to be free from fear of the one in authority? Then do what is right and you will be commended. (v3)

Government exists as a deterrent against evil. Its job is to maintain order by introducing fear and consequences for those who do wrong. In this way, rulers act as God's servants for our benefit:

> For the one in authority is God's servant for your good. But if you do wrong, be afraid, for rulers do not bear the sword for no reason. They are God's servants, agents of wrath to bring punishment on the wrongdoer. (v4)

### The Power of Consequences

Think about what actually prevents evil in society—consequences. When someone robs another person, police hunt them down, arrest them, and the government's laws penalize that action with imprisonment. Government bears the sword; they are God's agents of wrath, making would-be criminals think twice before committing evil.

Without law and order, chaos reigns. We see this illustrated in Judges 17, during a time when Israel had no ruler. A man named Micah exemplifies the lawlessness of that era—he steals from his mother, lies about it, then uses the stolen money to build an idol. The text tells us why this was possible:

> In those days Israel had no king; everyone did as they saw fit. (Judges 17:6)

Without authority to enforce consequences, people like Micah were free to pursue whatever evil they desired. As 1 John 3:4 reminds us, sin itself is lawlessness—it's in our sinful nature to resist God's rules. We naturally do more evil when restrictions are removed.

### The CHAZ Example

This principle played out vividly in 2020 during the Capitol Hill Autonomous Zone (CHAZ) in Seattle. Protesters declared a section of the city free from police and government, hoping to build community without "governmental oppression." The theory sounded nice, but the reality was predictable: almost instantly, CHAZ descended into crime, drugs, robbery, and nightly gunshots. Within days, the same people demanding the police leave were begging them to return for help.

**The lesson is clear:** without rulers enforcing law and order, everything falls into anarchy and lawlessness. Even imperfect government is necessary and ordained by God for our good.

### Give What Is Owed

Because our rulers are ordained by God for this vital responsibility, we must submit to them:

> Therefore, it is necessary to submit to the authorities, not only because of possible punishment but also as a matter of conscience. (v5)

We submit for two reasons: first, to avoid immediate consequences like arrest or imprisonment. But more importantly, we submit as a matter of conscience. If rulers are instituted by God, then rebelling against them is the same as rebelling against God—it becomes a matter of righteousness.

What does this submission look like? It starts with paying taxes:

> This is also why you pay taxes, for the authorities are God's servants, who give their full time to governing. (v6)

No one enjoys paying taxes, and we all want to complain about rising tax rates and government spending. But government needs that revenue to do its job. Without funding, how can they protect us from criminals and maintain order?

Submission goes beyond taxes:

> Give to everyone what you owe them: If you owe taxes, pay taxes; if revenue, then revenue; if respect, then respect; if honor, then honor. (v7)

We owe them not just money, but honor and respect. We must listen to what they say and obey the laws they establish.

### Practical Application: Respecting Authority in a Rebellious Culture

As Christians, we must submit to the law. We stop at stop signs, we tap on and off the bus, and when the tax office calls, we pay what's owed. We should be the best at complying with our government's rules—more law-abiding than anyone else in society.

But where we really struggle to give what's owed is **honor and respect for our rulers themselves.**

Notice the culture we live in today—it increasingly encourages rebellious attitudes toward authority. In TV shows and movies, the bad guy is always the person in charge: the police, government, or king is corrupt, while the "I hate authority, I don't follow rules" character is the hero. Even Brooklyn 99, a show about police officers, often frames the moral lesson as "the police are the real problem, not the criminals breaking the law." We consume this messaging daily.

Look at the social climate around us. Protests consistently carry anti-authority undertones. They call for police to be defunded, for governments to cut military spending—essentially seeking to prevent government from fulfilling its God-given role according to Romans 13. Young people at universities and high schools should be careful of these narratives. They romanticize fighting against government, which contradicts Paul's instruction to honor our rulers.

Even in Christian circles, this anti-government mindset has taken hold. Many believers think politics is inherently dirty or corrupt. While corruption certainly exists, our default attitude shouldn't be to hate politicians or assume good can only happen through evangelism, not politics. **Politics is instituted by God.** Be careful not to let our culture's anti-authority stance infect the church.

**Respect your politicians.** They do important work. Pray for them. When media attacks conservative or Christian politicians, don't just join the chorus—do your research. Honor the government. Be subject to the laws of the land. This is part of being a good citizen in God's eyes.

## Point 2: Submit to God's Law (Romans 13:8-10)

### God's Hierarchy: Middle Management Under the CEO

But this raises a crucial question: Does being a good citizen mean Christians should always do whatever the government says?

Look again at verse 4:

> For the one in authority is God's servant for your good. But if you do wrong, be afraid, for rulers do not bear the sword for no reason. They are God's servants, agents of wrath to bring punishment on the wrongdoer.

Notice the language: "God's servants" and "God's agents." Paul makes clear that **God is at the top of the hierarchy.** Without God, there is no authority. Governments don't stand equal to God—they are middle management, operating under His authority.

While government is responsible for upholding order and restraining evil, it doesn't get to determine what good and evil are. Only God does. Laws should reflect God's law, not the other way around.

**Think of it this way:** Imagine you're at work on a project to build a bridge when your manager suddenly says, "I've decided we're building a skyscraper instead." You'd say, "No way! You're just a manager—the CEO makes those calls. Your job is to execute the CEO's orders." Likewise, government is not the CEO; it's middle management. It cannot simply do whatever it wants or decide what's good and evil. Government serves God.

**Obedience to God's law is even more important than obeying our earthly laws.**

### God's Law Is Love

So what is God's law?

> Let no debt remain outstanding, except the continuing debt to love one another, for whoever loves others has fulfilled the law. The commandments, "You shall not commit adultery," "You shall not murder," "You shall not steal," "You shall not covet," and whatever other command there may be, are summed up in this one command: "Love your neighbor as yourself." (vv8-9)

**God's law is love your neighbor.** This is the law above all laws—the law that all earthly rulers are ultimately meant to point toward.

This law of love is at the very heart of the gospel. As Romans 5 tells us, "This is love, that while we were sinners, Christ died for us." Jesus perfectly models the law of love by dying on the cross for our sins, giving His own life in exchange for ours. Through His love, we are forgiven and given eternal life.

If you're here today and haven't yet put your trust in Jesus, I urge you: tick the box that says you want to know more about Him. Because getting God's love for you in Jesus right—that's the most important thing.

As followers of Christ, if we want to honor God's laws, **our ultimate priority must be loving our neighbors**, even if it means going against what our government says. These are the CEO's direct orders—they're the final word on what makes us good citizens.

### When Laws Harm Rather Than Love

More and more, some of our laws seem to encourage harm to our neighbors rather than loving them. In just the past five years:

- The **Abortion Act** and **Voluntary Euthanasia Act** have legalized killing babies and the elderly, removing consequences for ending lives.
- The **Conversion Practices Ban Act** restricts what churches and Christian organizations can say to those struggling with gender identity issues, effectively preventing loving counsel and normalizing harmful lifestyles.

These laws are not loving—they cause deep harm to our neighbors.

Currently, we can still practice our faith without being explicitly forced to disobey God. But as things progress, who knows what will happen in the coming years? In Canada—a nation often compared to Australia—a law was passed banning any communication that could cause an LGBT person to "feel harassed or offended." This means respectfully declining to use someone's pronouns of choice could carry legal consequences. It's closer than we think.

### Civil Disobedience When Necessary, and Prevention

What if, in a few years, NSW makes it illegal to refer to someone with pronouns they don't prefer? Would you compromise and lie, giving approval to sinful lifestyles? Or would you obey God?

Let me be clear: this doesn't mean starting an insurrection or attacking police. But if a law compels us to disobey God, we may need to disobey that law and submit to the legal consequences that follow—just as Daniel did when a Persian law forbade prayer to anyone but the king. Daniel kept praying to God and submitted to being thrown into the lion's den rather than abandon his faith.

Yes, it would be hard. You might lose your job, reputation, everything. There would likely be much disagreement about how to apply Romans 13 in such circumstances. But rather than waiting for crisis, **maybe we need to think prevention, not cure.**

Doesn't this emphasize how important it is to get godly people into power—people who care about God and instituting laws that love others?

**We need to be politically active.** Politics isn't everything, but it's definitely not inconsequential. We can't afford to be Christians who don't care about politics. If government's role is restraining evil, we must ensure our government has God's definition of evil, not a distorted one.

Consider these questions: Are you aware of the laws your government is trying to pass? Do you know their implications for your workplace or your children's schools? Do you know which politicians and parties support Christian values?

**For some of you younger believers:** Could you consider a career in politics? Could you work for the government to have a voice in what changes happen? Being a good Christian citizen includes honoring God's law of love in daily acts of kindness, but also **platforming people who love God's law** and ensuring we have godly rulers who will follow in Jesus' footsteps.

## Conclusion: Good Citizens Under God

When I researched Australian citizenship requirements, one thing stood out: new citizens must take a pledge. While the phrase is now optional, the pledge has always begun with these words:

> From this time forward, under God, I pledge my loyalty to Australia and its people.

**Honor the laws of Australia. Honor the government, for they bear God's sword.** But never forget that God is the one who makes the ultimate rules—He alone decides how we ought to live.

Let our attitude toward authority, politics, obedience, and disobedience all give glory to Him.
