# Exodus 9:13–12:43

**Title:** The Passover Lamb Saves and Rescues  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew  
**Date:** April 2006

---

## Introduction: Religious Festivals and God's Plan

As we approach Easter, it's worth asking: What is the role of religious festivals in our faith today? Should we celebrate Easter as a sacred holiday? The question becomes complicated when we consider that Easter's date has more to do with ancient pagan festivals dedicated to fertility deities than with historical accuracy about Jesus' death.

Yet throughout Scripture, God has given His people festivals—not arbitrary customs, but divinely ordained celebrations pregnant with spiritual meaning. Today we encounter the Passover and the Feast of Unleavened Bread, festivals that still shape Jewish practice today. These weren't minor observances: the Passover began with a sacred family meal (reminiscent of Chinese New Year Eve dinner), followed by a week-long festival of unleavened bread. The question before us is this: Do these festivals apply to us? Are they merely Jewish traditions, or do they speak to something deeper about God's character and His plan of salvation?

To answer this, let me walk you through two profound truths about the Passover.

---

## 1. The Passover Lamb Saves from Judgment

### The Final Plague: Death Comes

After nine plagues—blood, frogs, gnats, flies, livestock disease, boils, hail, locusts, and darkness—Pharaoh still refused to release God's people. God's patience was not endless. The time had come for the final judgment.

> When the LORD goes through the land to strike down the Egyptians, he will see the blood on the top and sides of the doorframe and will pass over that doorway, and he will not permit the destroyer to enter your houses and strike you down. (Exodus 12:23)

What God announced was unthinkable: the death of every firstborn in Egypt. From the palace of Pharaoh to the dungeon of the prisoner, from human to animal—the destroyer would come through every household. It was a judgment without mercy. The only discriminating factor would be blood on the doorframe.

### The Blood as Sign and Salvation

Where did this blood come from? A lamb. But this wasn't a symbolic gesture—it required slaughter. The lamb had to die so that the firstborn would live. The blood on the doorframe was a sign that a death had already occurred, a substitution of judgment. As Isaiah would later prophesy of the coming Servant:

> He was led like a lamb to the slaughter, and as a sheep before her shearers is silent, so he did not open his mouth. (Isaiah 53:7)

This wasn't random symbolism in Scripture. Throughout the Old Testament, we see the consistent pattern: a lamb dies for many. And when we reach the New Testament, everything becomes clear. John the Baptist, seeing Jesus, declares:

> Look, the Lamb of God, who takes away the sin of the world! (John 1:29)

And Paul writes:

> Christ, our Passover lamb, has been sacrificed. (1 Corinthians 5:7)

### Trusting in the Lamb's Protection

The Israelites didn't need to repaint their doors or perform complex rituals. What they needed was trust. They trusted that God's promise through Moses was true, and they acted on that trust by marking their doorframes with blood. It was an act of faith in God's faithfulness.

Some, I'm sure, doubted. Some thought, "This is unnecessary and messy. It will ruin the house." Others thought, "I'll just step out for milk before the shops close—surely the destroyer isn't coming yet." Those who failed to trust found themselves unprotected when judgment came.

Here's the invitation to us: Don't be like those doubters. Trust that Jesus' blood and death will save you from judgment. If you haven't yet, today is the day to begin trusting Him—to hide under His blood, to believe that God keeps His promises.

This is magnificent news for us. If our life is fundamentally about being saved by the Lamb, then so much else becomes secondary. It's not about grades or wealth or fashion. It's not about popularity or romantic relationships (as if they were mere accessories for parties). It's not about titles or positions of responsibility. Pharaoh had a title. The slaves had faith. The slaves were saved.

## 2. The Passover Rescues to a Better Life

### Freed from Slavery, Freed for Service

After the plague of the firstborn, Pharaoh finally relented:

> During the night Pharaoh summoned Moses and Aaron and said, "Up! Leave my people, you and the Israelites! Go, worship the LORD as you have requested. Take your flocks and herds, as you have said, and go." (Exodus 12:31–32)

What God had promised from the beginning was now coming to pass. He had said in Exodus 3:

> I have heard them crying out because of their slave drivers, so I have come down to rescue them from the hand of the Egyptians and to bring them up out of that land into a good and spacious land, a land flowing with milk and honey.

Now it was happening. The Israelites left Egypt with such speed that they carried their unleavened dough on their shoulders. The Egyptians, terrified and wanting them gone, gave them gold, silver, and clothing—effectively repaying them for 430 years of service.

But here's the crucial insight: **Freedom is not an end in itself.** The Israelites weren't freed to wander aimlessly through the desert. They were freed *for something*. They were freed to enter the land God had promised. And more importantly, they were freed to serve God Himself.

Notice the language in Exodus 12:31: "Go, worship the LORD." The Hebrew word here doesn't mean singing hymns or attending a service. It means to *serve*. For four hundred years, the Israelites had served the Egyptians in hard labor and oppression. Now they were called to serve someone else—the living God. But this was a different kind of service. His yoke is light; His burden is easy.

### An Illustration: From Ice Skating to Ten Pin Bowling

During school, I was stuck in ice skating—a sport I despised. Every week, I had to travel from Baulkham Hills to a dingy rink in Blacktown for two hours of monotony, accompanied by the same Jon English song on repeat. All my friends were in ten pin bowling, but that group was full.

Then imagine someone offered to free me from ice skating *and* pay for my spot in ten pin bowling. I'd be freed from ice skating—but I couldn't now use that freedom to be idle on Wednesdays or play tennis instead. I'd be freed *to ten pin bowl*. That's my new activity. That's where my energy goes.

It's exactly this way with our salvation. Jesus didn't free us from sin so that we could be idle or pursue other masters. We're freed from the slavery of sin to serve God. How, then, can we say we don't want to serve Him? We got the spot; we bowl.

### Living as Yeast-Free People

One of the most powerful metaphors for this transformation comes through the Feast of Unleavened Bread, which immediately followed Passover. For an entire week, no yeast was permitted. Unleavened bread—bread that doesn't rise, like Lebanese bread—became the symbol of this new life.

Paul uses this imagery profoundly when addressing Christians who had become complacent about sin:

> Don't you know that a little yeast works through the whole batch of dough? Get rid of the old yeast that you may be a new batch without yeast—as you really are. For Christ, our Passover lamb, has been sacrificed. Therefore let us keep the Festival, not with the old yeast, the yeast of malice and wickedness, but with bread without yeast, the bread of sincerity and truth. (1 Corinthians 5:6–8)

Paul uses yeast as a metaphor for malice and wickedness. Unleavened bread represents sincerity and truth. We are, in fact, already a new batch—yeast-free—because Christ has been sacrificed. We live freed from malice and wickedness to live in sincerity and truth.

If you're carrying malice toward a friend, sibling, or parent right now, hear Paul's words: "Get rid of the old yeast that you may be a new batch without yeast—as you really are. For Christ, our Passover lamb, has been sacrificed." You have already been freed. This yeast-free life isn't something you're working toward; it's who you already are in Christ.

## Conclusion: Easter, the Passover, and Our Daily Reality

So what about Easter? The Passover was a shadow, a metaphor pointing to Jesus' sacrifice. The Feast of Unleavened Bread was a metaphor for living a life free from malice. In a similar way, Easter—though it has no biblical mandate and historically has pagan roots—can serve as a reminder, as long as we don't confuse the reminder with the reality.

But here's the critical point: **We don't need special days to remember Jesus.** The Passover is fulfilled in Christ. The Feast is fulfilled in Christ. Even Easter is fulfilled in Christ. We could celebrate Easter every year, or skip it for years—it matters little. What matters is Jesus and what He did on the cross.

Consider how Chinese New Year is celebrated differently in Hong Kong versus Australia. In Hong Kong, CNY is mandatory: new clothes, no cleaning (lest you sweep away good luck), red packets, dinners, visits to every relative imaginable. In Australia? Some years we celebrate with excitement. Other years we forget. We might have dinner if it falls near the weekend. Red packets go to special relatives. It's simply no big deal.

Religious holidays should be treated this way. They're nice reminders, but everyday should be a celebration of Jesus. Whenever we gather—at house parties, at youth group, here at church, over lunch together—let's remind each other of the Passover Lamb who shed His blood, who was crucified on our behalf to save us from judgment.

**So keep the Passover metaphorically:** Remember daily that Jesus is the Lamb who paid for our sins.

**Keep the Festival metaphorically:** Live a yeast-free life—a life of sincerity and truth, free from malice and wickedness.

**Keep Easter metaphorically:** Every day, remember His resurrection and that He is coming again.

All special days have been fulfilled in Christ. That's where our focus belongs—not on the calendar, but on the Lamb.
