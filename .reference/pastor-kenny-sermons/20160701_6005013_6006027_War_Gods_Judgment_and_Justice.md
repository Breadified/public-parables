# Joshua 5:13-6:27

**Title:** War: God's Judgment and Justice
**Date:** 2 July 2016
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction: The Nature of War

We live in a time when different worldviews clash profoundly. When differences between nations or peoples become irreconcilable, conflict often follows. Yet we're privileged to live in democracies where we can resolve our disagreements through elections rather than warfare—a system that, while imperfect, spares us the bloodshed of actual combat.

As we examine the conquest of Jericho, we must address three serious accusations leveled against God: that He is genocidal, that He is a bully, and that He plays favorites. Each accusation deserves careful, honest consideration in light of Scripture.

---

## 1. God's Judgment Is Not Genocide (Joshua 6:15-21)

The destruction of Jericho appears brutal and absolute:

> When the trumpets sounded, the army shouted, and at the sound of the trumpet, when the men gave a loud shout, the wall collapsed; so everyone charged straight in, and they took the city. They devoted the city to the Lord and destroyed with the sword every living thing in it—men and women, young and old, cattle, sheep and donkeys. (6:20-21)

This command to "devote" the city requires understanding. The term means something was set apart as sacred to God—in this case, devoted to destruction. Yet several factors suggest this was not indiscriminate genocide:

**The language of ancient warfare:** Ancient Near Eastern military narratives often used hyperbolic rhetoric. When we say in sports we will "demolish" and "annihilate" the opposition, we don't mean literal total destruction. Similarly, "destroying all who breathed" likely meant achieving complete military victory rather than literal extermination of every person.

**Who actually died:** The people killed were likely primarily combatants. In the ancient world, there were no prisoners of war—military necessity demanded the destruction of enemy forces. The phrase "men and women and children" describes the spectrum of inhabitants who resisted, not necessarily innocents. Jericho may have been a military fortress rather than a domestic city, potentially housing soldiers and support staff like Rahab and her family, who had opportunity to escape when the walls fell.

**The Canaanites remained:** If genocide had truly occurred, no Canaanites would have survived. Yet Scripture clearly indicates survivors remained in the land:

> If you turn away and ally yourselves with the survivors of these nations that remain among you... (Joshua 23:12)

Joshua 10:40 and 11:15 describe "utter destruction," yet Joshua 23:12 speaks of "survivors of these nations that remain." This contradiction resolves if we understand the command as military and spiritual—not literal annihilation.

**The true aim was expulsion and spiritual cleansing:** The conquest's purpose was to drive inhabitants out and destroy the religious systems of Canaanite idolatry:

> You shall tear down their altars, and smash their sacred pillars, and hew down their Asherim, and burn their graven images with fire. (Deuteronomy 7:1-5)

This process was gradual:

> The Lord your God will clear away these nations before you little by little. You will not be able to put an end to them quickly, for the wild beasts would grow too numerous for you. (Deuteronomy 7:22)

**War's terrible but real nature:** War remains destructive. Combatants lose limbs, suffer trauma, families are torn apart. Yet sometimes war becomes necessary—when two sides cannot reconcile and differences run too deep. The borders between nations today mark where ancient combatants finally stopped fighting, tired or defeated. Those lines represent peace settlements, not permanent conquest.

Not all wars are evil. Sometimes war prevents greater evil. The bombing of Pyongyang during the Korean War destroyed 75% of the city, yet ultimately saved South Korea from communist subjugation. This is a hard truth but an important one: sometimes destruction is necessary for justice and freedom.

## 2. God's Judgment Is Not Bullying (Joshua 6:1-14)

The second accusation claims Israel was the aggressor—that God was a bully, helping one tribe steal another's land without provocation. Yet this misses the entire spiritual and historical context.

The conquest was not conventional warfare. The account bristles with unusual details:

> Now the gates of Jericho were securely barred because of the Israelites. No one went out and no one came in. Then the Lord said to Joshua, "See, I have delivered Jericho into your hands, along with its king and its fighting men. March around the city once with all the armed men. Do this for six days. Have seven priests carry trumpets of rams' horns in front of the ark. On the seventh day, march around the city seven times, with the priests blowing the trumpets." (6:1-4)

Notice the recurring sevens—the number of completeness. Notice the ark of the covenant, God's sacred throne, leading the way. Notice the priests with trumpets, not warriors with weapons. This feels like a temple ceremony, not a military campaign.

**The symbolism reveals the truth:** Trumpets had sacred purposes:

- They signaled God's appointed times (Numbers 10:5-10)
- Only priests could sound them (Numbers 10:8)
- They called Israel to sacred assembly and remembrance (Numbers 10:10)
- They summoned God's help in battle (Numbers 10:9)

This ritual, centered on God's presence and holiness, wasn't arbitrary—it proclaimed that Israel's conquest was God's judgment, not human aggression.

**Historical context provides justification:** God made a covenant with Abraham generations earlier:

> Then the Lord said to him, "Know for certain that for four hundred years your descendants will be strangers in a country not their own and that they will be enslaved and mistreated there... In the fourth generation your descendants will come back here, for the sin of the Amorites has not yet reached its full measure." (Genesis 15:13-16)

God was executing righteous judgment on a nation whose sin had reached its fullness. This was not conquest for conquest's sake.

**The Canaanites were deeply wicked:** The Canaanite religion involved practices that shock our conscience even today. They sacrificed their own children to their gods:

> They have built the high places of Baal to burn their children in the fire as offerings to Baal—something I did not command or mention, nor did it enter my mind. (Jeremiah 19:5)

Centuries later, when Israel itself adopted these abominations, God showed the same impartial justice—judging His own people with devastating conquest by Assyria and Babylon.

**Application:** Israel's conquest of Canaan was not imperialism but spiritual judgment. They didn't continue to conquer other lands—they occupied only the promised land. Unlike empires that expand endlessly, Israel maintained their borders. This distinguishes their war from colonial aggression.

## 3. God's Judgment Is Not Favoritism (Joshua 5:13-15)

The final accusation suggests God simply picks teams: whichever side claims Him gets victory. Joshua encounters the commander of the Lord's army and asks directly:

> "Are you for us or for our enemies?" "Neither," he replied, "but as commander of the army of the Lord I have now come." (5:13-14)

This "neither" doesn't mean God is neutral or distant. It means God doesn't align Himself with human sides. God has only one side: His own—the side of justice and righteousness. The question isn't "Which team is God on?" but "Are you on God's side?"

**God's refusal to play favorites is absolute:** He doesn't choose nations or teams or political parties. He asks: Is your nation godly? Is your party just? Does your country worship Him?

This principle applies universally. The Nazis wore belt buckles inscribed "Gott mit uns"—"God with us." They claimed divine favor. God was not with them. Their wickedness—the Holocaust, the systematic murder of millions, the euthanasia of the disabled, the totalitarian terror—disqualified them utterly.

Similarly, centuries after conquering Canaan, when Israel itself began sacrificing children to Molech and practicing the very abominations God had judged the Canaanites for, God showed no favoritism. Israel faced the same devastating judgment: conquest and exile.

**Rahab proves God's impartiality:** Among those destroyed in Jericho was Rahab, a Canaanite prostitute. Yet she turned to the God of Israel and sought His protection:

> Joshua spared Rahab the prostitute, with her family and all who belonged to her, because she hid the men Joshua had sent as spies to Jericho—and she lives among the Israelites to this day. (6:25)

God saved a Canaanite and condemned Israelites. Ethnicity, nationality, and social status mean nothing to Him. What matters is whether you align yourself with His justice and mercy.

**The scope of God's judgment:** Nations that reject God face consequences. God judges with perfect justice:

> I saw heaven standing open and there before me was a white horse, whose rider is called Faithful and True. With justice he judges and wages war. His eyes are like blazing fire... He is dressed in a robe dipped in blood, and his name is the Word of God... "He will rule them with an iron scepter." He treads the winepress of the fury of the wrath of God Almighty. (Revelation 19:11-15)

This judgment comes in two stages: Christ conquered Satan at His first coming. At His return, He will complete the work, separating those who belong to Him from those who rejected Him.

**A nation's reckoning:** Every nation faces God's judgment. Consider our own society: we sacrifice approximately 100,000 children annually through abortion—cutting them from the womb and incinerating them in the name of careerism, materialism, and personal freedom. This echoes the very Canaanite practices God judged as abominable.

If God is just, judgment will come. Yet there is hope: like Rahab, anyone can turn to God and find mercy before that judgment arrives.

**Application:** There is no neutrality before God. You must choose whose side you're on. Trust Jesus. Repent of your sins. Join God's army before the judgment comes, as Rahab did when she saw the Israelites entering Jericho.

---

## Conclusion: Choosing Sides

God is not genocidal, bullying, or playing favorites. He is perfectly just, executing judgment against wickedness while offering salvation to all who turn to Him. His character is revealed not in arbitrary destruction but in righteous judgment paired with boundless mercy.

The question remains: Which side are you on?
