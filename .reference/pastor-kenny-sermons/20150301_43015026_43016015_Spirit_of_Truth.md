# John 15:26-16:15

**Title:** Spirit of Truth
**Date:** 20 March 2015
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction: A Difficult Topic

The theology of the Holy Spirit is challenging to study well. Many treatments either incorporate pagan superstition—ghosts, spooky mysterious things—or they're technically correct but unnecessarily convoluted. I've developed my own simpler understanding through careful biblical study, and I continue to refine it. If your church background differs, I welcome biblical discussion over time.

This topic is difficult enough that I'd normally avoid it, but one advantage of teaching through Scripture expositionally is that eventually you must address even uncomfortable subjects. Today we have many passages to work through.

Our study focuses on three main points:
1. The Advocate (15:26-16:7)
2. How the Holy Spirit points to Jesus (16:8-15)
3. The Holy Spirit dwelling in us (20:19-23)

## The Person of the Holy Spirit as Advocate

### Understanding "Paraclete"

Let me illustrate with a personal story. Years ago, my friends and I were crossing a street near UTS when a motorcycle came suddenly at us. Some of us rushed across; others waited in the median. The motorcycle wobbled, turned on its side, and slid down the road. We rushed to help the cyclist, and my friend exchanged details as a witness. But then the motorcycle's insurance company sued my friend for $5,000 in damages, blaming us for the accident. They didn't send a junior solicitor—they sent a QC barrister against a poor second-year student. We were terrified. We needed help. We needed a lawyer.

Jesus uses similar imagery when he describes the Holy Spirit:

> When the Advocate comes, whom I will send to you from the Father—the Spirit of truth who goes out from the Father—he will testify about me. (John 15:26)

The Greek word *paraclete* carries a rich meaning: comforter, encourager, helper, witness, advocate, or lawyer. Essentially, it describes someone who speaks or acts on your behalf in your favor—whether professionally or personally. Notice how comfort and encouragement naturally flow from such advocacy.

This is the same role Jesus himself fulfills:

> If anyone sins, we have an Advocate with the Father—Jesus Christ, the Righteous One. (1 John 2:1)

Jesus speaks and acts on our behalf in our favor. His advocacy occurred on the cross, where he died to bear the penalty for our sins on behalf of the world. He dies so that others need not die. Throughout John's Gospel, Jesus predicts this sacrifice, which comes to pass on Good Friday.

### Jesus Advocates in Heaven

As Jesus departs, the Holy Spirit comes to continue his work of advocacy on earth. But Jesus himself doesn't retire. He continues to advocate for us in heaven:

> Who will bring any charge against those whom God has chosen? It is God who justifies. Who is the one who condemns? No one. Christ Jesus, who died—more than that, who was raised to life—is at the right hand of God and is also interceding for us. (Romans 8:33-34)

On Easter, Jesus rose from the dead. After a brief time, he ascended to the right hand of God, where he now serves as our high priest. Using his body as the once-for-all sacrifice, he continually intercedes for us because we continually sin.

Think of it this way: our chief lawyer, Jesus, had to leave Sydney to argue our case in the High Court. While he's there, he sends someone to look after us here—the Holy Spirit. The Spirit cannot come until Jesus goes away:

> Very truly I tell you, it is for your good that I am going away. Unless I go away, the Advocate will not come to you; but if I go, I will send him to you. (John 16:7)

Jesus describes the Holy Spirit as "another advocate" to replace his earthly advocacy with heavenly advocacy:

> I will ask the Father, and he will give you another Advocate to help you and be with you forever. (John 14:15-16)

### The Spirit Intercedes in Our Weakness

The disciples will need this help. Jesus warns them:

> All this I have told you so that you will not fall away. They will put you out of the synagogue; in fact, the time is coming when anyone who kills you will think they are offering a service to God. They will do such things because they have not known the Father or me. (John 16:1-3)

The disciples will face intense opposition just as Jesus did. They'll encounter the daunting task of witnessing in a hostile world. That's why the Spirit intercedes:

> The Spirit himself intercedes for us through wordless groans. And he who searches our hearts knows the mind of the Spirit, because the Spirit intercedes for God's people in accordance with the will of God. (Romans 8:26-27)

### Application: Two Advocates for One Great Case

Together, we have not just one but two advocates:
- An Advocate in heaven (Jesus)
- An Advocate on earth (the Holy Spirit)

It's a major case. In important legal matters, you need both a solicitor and a barrister—the best lawyers you can afford, who believe in you and can be trusted with your defense.

We face a major case regarding guilt and sin. We are in serious trouble and cannot afford legal fees. In our desperation, we have Jesus and the Holy Spirit as our advocates.

**Petition Jesus for help! Rely on him and the Holy Spirit he sends.**

This week, Mother Teresa will be canonized as a saint. In some churches, this means people can pray to her for aid. But her body lies buried in Calcutta—she cannot hear prayers or help. In contrast, Jesus is risen from the dead. Ask him for help.

## The Work of the Holy Spirit

Here's where it gets difficult. We're happy with understanding the Holy Spirit as our advocate, but the challenging question remains: **How does he do his work? What kind of help does he offer?**

### The Spirit Exposes Truth to the World

According to John, the Holy Spirit's role is to expose truth to the world:

> When he comes, he will prove the world to be in the wrong about sin and righteousness and judgment. (John 16:8)

The Spirit exposes regarding three things:

**Sin:** "about sin, because people do not believe in me" (16:9)
Sin is turning away from God, refusing to believe him.

**Righteousness:** "about righteousness, because I am going to the Father, where you can see me no longer" (16:10)
Jesus is righteous—he does not remain dead. He returns to heaven because of his righteous sacrifice and intercession for those who believe.

**Judgment:** "about judgment, because the prince of this world now stands condemned" (16:11)
There is condemnation for those who continue to disbelieve. The prince of this world and those belonging to it stand judged.

This is the gospel: sin, justification, and judgment.

If this sounds familiar, it's because it's the exact same message Jesus proclaimed while on earth.

### Jesus' Own Message: Exposing Sin, Righteousness, and Judgment

**Jesus Exposes Sin**

Jesus' enemies tried unsuccessfully to expose him as sinful:

> Can any of you prove me guilty of sin? If I am telling the truth, why don't you believe me? (John 8:46)

Instead, Jesus exposes the wickedness and sin of others:

> If I had not come and spoken to them, they would not be guilty of sin. Now, however, they have no excuse for their sin. (John 15:22)

**Jesus Exposes His Righteousness**

Jesus judges with perfect righteousness:

> By myself I can do nothing; I judge only as I hear, and my judgment is just, for I seek not to please myself but him who sent me. (John 5:30)

> Stop judging by mere appearances, but instead judge correctly. (John 7:24)

> If I do judge, my decisions are true, because I am not alone. I stand with the Father, who sent me. (John 8:16)

Three times, Governor Pilate declares Jesus innocent:

> I find no basis for a charge against him. (John 18:38, 19:4, 19:6)

**Jesus Exposes the Judgment of Satan**

> Now is the time for judgment on this world; now the prince of this world will be driven out. (John 12:31)

Jesus pronounces judgment on those who belong to this world:

> You are from below; I am from above. You are of this world; I am not of this world. I told you that you would die in your sins; if you do not believe that I am he, you will indeed die in your sins. I have much to say in judgment of you. But he who sent me is trustworthy, and what I have heard from him I tell the world. (John 8:23-26)

Jesus brings a sobering message: there is a problem of sin and God's wrath. Jesus is the solution. Death awaits those who don't address this truth.

### Why the Spirit's Work Mirrors Jesus' Work

You shouldn't be shocked that the Holy Spirit's message is identical to Jesus'. Two reasons explain this:

**First,** the Holy Spirit performs the same function as Christ because the Holy Spirit is also called the "Spirit of Truth," while Jesus is "the Way, the Truth, and the Life." Just as Jesus guides us into truth, so the Spirit guides us into truth:

> When he, the Spirit of truth, comes, he will guide you into all the truth. (John 16:13)

**Second,** they have the same function because everything the Spirit does depends entirely on Jesus:

> He will not speak on his own; he will speak only what he hears, and he will tell you what is yet to come. He will glorify me because it is from me that he will receive what he will make known to you. All that belongs to the Father is mine. That is why I said the Spirit will receive from me what he will make known to you. (John 16:13-15)

The relationship flows downward: the Spirit depends on the Son, who depends on the Father. The Holy Spirit teaches what Christ taught. The Spirit of Truth communicates the truth about God—the essence of God's work in Christ.

Importantly, the Spirit glorifies Christ:

> He will glorify me. (John 16:14)

Though the Son and Spirit are equally divine, there is an order. The Spirit glorifies the Son just as the Son glorifies the Father. The Holy Spirit's job is to continue Christ's teaching work with the disciples—not teaching whatever the Spirit thinks suitable, but teaching Christ's teachings. The Spirit reminds them of all Christ's words and deeds.

### Illustration: The Executor of a Will

Think of the Holy Spirit as the executor of a will. I have a will at home for when I leave this world, and it's overdue for updating since my family has changed. In that will, there are specific instructions, and an executor is appointed with power of attorney. The executor carries out the will's teachings and instructions with the same authority I would have, but only within the bounds of my instructions. The executor can close accounts, sell assets, distribute property, and finalize affairs according to careful instructions—but cannot overstep them.

The Holy Spirit functions similarly. He is Jesus' representative—the Spirit of the Son—carrying out Jesus' advocacy and making it effective in the disciples' lives. He won't do anything different from what Jesus did.

### Application: Common Misconceptions About the Spirit

There are many misunderstandings about the Holy Spirit that need correction.

**The Spirit is not a Spirit of the Gaps**

The Holy Spirit is not the answer to spooky and weird things. He is not a spirit-of-the-gaps—filling in the supernatural phenomena we cannot explain. The Holy Spirit is not magic, not a genie, not a talisman. He is not here to make us wealthy or healthy.

While strange things happen in the world—coincidences and miracles—the explanation is God. Not just the Holy Spirit alone. While miracles do occur, God the Father and God the Son are involved, and rarely is it attributed exclusively to the Spirit (1 Corinthians 12:11).

We treat the Holy Spirit like a pagan force that, if we have it on our side, will give us all our worldly needs and wants. That's pagan belief, not Christianity. The Spirit will not heal you. The Spirit will not help you strike the lottery. The Spirit will not help you pass exams.

That's not what the Spirit does.

**God is not our pet.** The Holy Spirit tells us about the truth of Jesus. The focus of the Spirit is Jesus.

**The Spirit Points to Jesus, Not Himself**

Therefore, to be Spirit-centered is to be Jesus-centered. A sermon on the Spirit is about Jesus. A spiritual song points to Jesus. A church filled with the Spirit is filled with Christ.

But there's an order:
- A sermon about Jesus doesn't necessarily have to mention the Spirit
- A Jesus song doesn't necessarily have to mention the Spirit
- Spirit-filled people are not those chanting "Holy Spirit" repeatedly

Spirit-filled people are those talking about Jesus.

A sermon about the Spirit that doesn't lead to Jesus is useless. A song about the Spirit that doesn't lead to Jesus is useless.

It's like a ring that holds a diamond. The ring is essential, but no one talks about the gold ring—we talk about the diamond. The ring's whole purpose is to hold the diamond. Without the diamond, the ring is just a circle of metal. Without the ring, we ask where the diamond went. No one says, "Let's focus on the ring."

Jesus is the diamond. The Holy Spirit is the ring. Both form the Godhead with God the Father. Both are equally divine. But the order is clear: it's all about Jesus. Whether we talk about Jesus or the Spirit, the focus remains Jesus.

**The Spirit Teaches Jesus' Words, Not New Information**

Second, the Holy Spirit is not a supplier of extra information to help us make better life decisions. We use phrases like "led by the Spirit" or "the Spirit told me" very carelessly. While these can be true (as we'll see), we misuse them when we claim the Spirit tells us which job to take, where to live, which course to study, or whom to marry.

The Spirit cannot tell you these things because Jesus has not addressed them directly.

- To be led by the Spirit = following the commands of God
- To be spiritual = following Jesus
- To be led by the Spirit = following Jesus

## The Spirit in Us

### How Does the Spirit Work?

How does all this work practically? How does the Spirit guide disciples? What does it mean for us today?

This requires jumping over some intellectual hurdles, so stay with me.

After his resurrection, Jesus gave the Spirit to his disciples:

> On the evening of that first day of the week, when the disciples were together, with the doors locked for fear of the Jewish leaders, Jesus came and stood among them and said, "Peace be with you!" After he said this, he showed them his hands and side. The disciples were overjoyed when they saw the Lord. Again Jesus said, "Peace be with you! As the Father has sent me, I am sending you." And with that he breathed on them and said, "Receive the Holy Spirit. If you forgive anyone's sins, their sins are forgiven; if you do not forgive them, they are not forgiven." (John 20:19-23)

Jesus literally breathed on them—the Greek word *pneuma* means "breath" or "spirit." He passed his spirit to them. This echoes the pattern of farewell discourses in Scripture. When a departing person passes his spirit to followers or a successor, great wisdom and authority transfer. The best examples are Moses passing his spirit to Joshua and Elijah to Elisha (Deuteronomy 31-34; 2 Kings 2:9-14).

### The Spirit's Work: Reminder and Remembrance

As a result, the disciples remembered—and this is crucial. John writes his Gospel in hindsight, inserting commentary that the disciples gained after Jesus died, rose again, and breathed out the Holy Spirit.

> His disciples remembered that it is written: "Zeal for your house will consume me." (John 2:17)

> After he was raised from the dead, his disciples recalled what he had said. Then they believed the Scripture and the words that Jesus had spoken. (John 2:22)

> At first his disciples did not understand all this. Only after Jesus was glorified did they realize that these things had been written about him and that these things had been done to him. (John 12:16)

> You do not realize now what I am doing, but later you will understand. (John 13:7)

> All this I have spoken while still with you. But the Advocate, the Holy Spirit, whom the Father will send in my name, will teach you all things and will remind you of everything I have said to you. (John 14:25-26)

> I have told you this, so that when the time comes you will remember that I warned you. (John 16:4)

The phrase "bring to your remembrance all that I have said to you" means the Holy Spirit makes the disciples understand inwardly the words of Jesus. The Spirit helps them grasp these words in light of faith, to perceive all their significance and importance for the life of the church.

The disciples taught others to do the same:

> Keep reminding them of these things. (2 Timothy 2:14)

> Remind the people to be subject to rulers and authorities. (Titus 3:1)

> I will always remind you of these things. (2 Peter 1:12)

### The Relationship Between Spirit and Word

How did the disciples practice this reminding? Initially, they passed down Jesus' message orally—person to person, leader to church. When distance made personal communication difficult or when they realized Jesus might not return in their lifetimes, they committed their memories to paper. Over time, these documents were carefully copied, recopied across generations, translated, and passed down roughly 2,000 years to us—to the Bibles in front of you.

### The Bible as God-Breathed Word

> This is the Spirit of God!

Not the ink or paper or leather—we're not bibliolators. I don't worship the book itself; I have plenty of books I can write in, mark up, and eventually discard when they've served their purpose.

But the message—the gospel—this is the Spirit of God telling you the truth about Jesus. This word exposes the world about sin, righteousness, and judgment. This word tells and reminds us what Christ accomplished on our behalf.

Scripture itself is described as God-breathed:

> Continue in what you have learned and have become convinced of, because you know those from whom you learned it, and how from infancy you have known the Holy Scriptures, which are able to make you wise for salvation through faith in Christ Jesus. All Scripture is God-breathed and is useful for teaching, rebuking, correcting and training in righteousness, so that the servant of God may be thoroughly equipped for every good work. (2 Timothy 3:14-17)

It is "God-breathed"—God-spoken, expired by God, recorded for us. All that the disciples remembered. This is how God works in us. The gospel—breathed out from God in history, in the Son, recorded for us so that we can believe. When we do believe, the Spirit dwells in us.

### The Words of Jesus Are Spirit and Life

Jesus wants his word inside us:

> The Spirit gives life; the flesh counts for nothing. The words I have spoken to you are spirit and they are life. (John 6:63)

When Jesus told the disciples to "eat his flesh and drink his blood," he wasn't speaking literally. Rather, he was saying: digest the Spirit. These are the words of eternal life. The message of Jesus becomes internalized in the believer's life through the Holy Spirit.

> Take the helmet of salvation and the sword of the Spirit, which is the word of God. (Ephesians 6:17)

The word of God cuts us. The Spirit works through God's Word.

### Illustration: The Will as Message

Consider a will: it might be just a piece of paper. But its value lies not in the paper itself but in the message—the last will and testament. That message carries authority, power, and the will of the testator. A single document can instantly transform lives and rewrite world history. The paper is merely the vehicle.

### Application: How the Spirit Works Today

How does the Spirit work in us today? While mystery remains about exactly how and why some people receive it and others don't, the conclusion is clear: **the Holy Spirit's activity is located here—in God's Word as recorded in the Bible.**

Abandon the idea of a "zap theory" of spiritual growth or treating the Spirit as mere motivation.

The Spirit works through his truth and word. As Paul writes:

> Be transformed by the renewing of your mind. (Romans 12:2)

The word convicts us. Logic and reason win out—not through mere cognitive assent, but through metaphor, poetry, and words that cut to the heart. History hits home. Shared experiences convince us that God knows what he is doing. Passages about judgment confirm what we observe in reality. The tragedy and rejoicing move us. Poetry elevates us. History builds our confidence in Scripture's truth. Laws prove their worth in society. Philosophy gives life meaning. The grand narrative of Scripture uplifts us.

There are infinite ways the word works in us. But it is this word—this same Spirit of Christ—that guides and teaches us.

**Read it! Absorb it! Digest it! Believe it! The Spirit of God is in you and working in you.**

### The Closing Story

My friends and I eventually found ourselves in a half-day arbitration hearing with a judge. The insurance company spared no expense—they brought in a QC barrister probably charging $1,000 an hour to recover $5,000. It wasn't about the money; they wanted to make an example of us.

We had no one. We couldn't afford a lawyer. We were terrified.

But we had one thing that helped us: the law. We were law students. The law guided us. The law taught us. We studied the law and our case carefully. We had the truth in us. We presented it to the judge.

We won.

Jesus isn't with us physically. It's painful to acknowledge—when someone dies, they're truly gone. There's no point pretending otherwise with flowery words. But Jesus has left us with someone else: **the Spirit of Truth, the Holy Spirit. We truly have Jesus with us in spirit.**
