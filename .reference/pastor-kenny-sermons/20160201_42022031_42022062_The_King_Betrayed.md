# Luke 22:31-62

**Title:** The King Betrayed
**Date:** 8 February 2016
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## What is Betrayal?

Betrayal strikes at the heart of human relationship in ways that ordinary conflict cannot. Several elements make betrayal uniquely destructive:

- **Unexpectedness** — it catches us off guard
- **Violation of trust** — the breaking of confidence that binds people together
- **Intimacy of the wound** — betrayal can only come from friends, never from enemies

Betrayal is the breaking of trust or confidence that creates moral and psychological conflict within relationships. History shows us its devastating power. Osama bin Laden, hidden for years despite the full resources of US Intelligence, was ultimately betrayed by one of his own wives—jealousy turned into exposure. Even the most fortified positions crumble when trust is broken from within.

Today's passage shows us Jesus experiencing betrayal at multiple levels, from his closest companions.

## Betrayal by Judas (Luke 22:47-48)

Jesus is in the Garden of Gethsemane on the Mount of Olives when the arrest unfolds through betrayal:

> While he was still speaking a crowd came up, and the man who was called Judas, one of the Twelve, was leading them. He approached Jesus to kiss him, but Jesus asked him, "Judas, are you betraying the Son of Man with a kiss?"

The emphatic nature of this betrayal cuts deep:

**Judas was of the inner circle.** He is described as "one of the Twelve"—not merely a follower, but part of an elite group of twelve closest friends. He spent three years with Jesus, living daily alongside him as his best friend. This was a relationship forged through constant companionship.

**Judas actively led the arrest.** He did not passively point Jesus out from a distance. He led the mob that came to arrest him. Earlier in the chapter, Luke tells us Judas had already conspired with the authorities (22:3-6), plotting actively rather than reluctantly.

**The kiss was an act of identification.** Because it was Passover season with crowds filling Jerusalem, and people were sleeping in public places like this garden, mistaken identity was entirely possible in the darkness. A kiss was the definitive way to identify the right person. Yet Judas chose this gesture of intimacy—one of life's most tender actions—to betray Jesus. In a single gesture of friendship, he stabbed his best friend in the back.

The name Judas has become forever synonymous with traitor, much like names that carry shame in our own time. What was once a common, respectable name is now ruined forever.

## The Resistance and Jesus' Response (Luke 22:49-53)

When the disciples grasp what is happening, they react:

> When Jesus' followers saw what was going to happen, they said, "Lord, should we strike with our swords?" And one of them struck the servant of the high priest, cutting off his right ear.

One disciple immediately strikes, severing the servant's ear. But Jesus responds with a clarity that reveals his nature:

> But Jesus answered, "No more of this!" And he touched the man's ear and healed him. Then Jesus said to the chief priests, the officers of the temple guard, and the elders, who had come for him, "Am I leading a rebellion, that you have come with swords and clubs? Every day I was with you in the temple courts, and you did not lay a hand on me. But this is your hour—when darkness reigns."

Jesus had the power and right to defend himself, yet he stops the violence. He heals the very man his disciple wounded—an act of mercy even in the moment of arrest. He questions the authorities: why treat an innocent man as a criminal? He pointed out the absurdity of their approach. There is no rebellion. He has not broken any laws. The true aggressors are those carrying swords and clubs. This is their hour—the dominion of darkness.

**A Puzzling Shift in Jesus' Teaching**

Yet there seems to be a contradiction. Earlier in the same evening, Jesus told his disciples:

> "But now if you have a purse, take it, and also a bag; and if you don't have a sword, sell your cloak and buy one... The disciples said, 'See, Lord, here are two swords.' [Jesus replied,] 'That is enough!'"

This instruction to arm themselves appears to conflict with Jesus' refusal to fight at his arrest. How do we reconcile this?

The answer lies in what Jesus said immediately before: "It is written: 'And he was numbered with the transgressors'" (22:37). This is a reference to Isaiah 53:12:

> "He was numbered with the transgressors. For he bore the sin of many, and made intercession for the transgressors."

Luke is making a crucial theological point: Jesus is being identified with criminals, but he himself is not a criminal. The earlier instruction to carry a sword was preparation for persecution. Before, Jesus had sent disciples on quick missionary journeys without provisions (Luke 9:3). Now he tells them to prepare as if for a long journey in dangerous conditions—because they will be hunted and persecuted. They will be identified with Jesus and treated as outlaws by the authorities.

Yet being identified as a transgressor does not make one guilty. Jesus committed no crime. He broke no law. He is innocent. He chooses not to resist because his purpose is redemptive, not revolutionary.

## Betrayal by All the Disciples (Luke 22:45-46, 54)

In the garden, when Jesus needs them most, his disciples fail him:

> When he rose from prayer and went back to the disciples, he found them asleep, exhausted from sorrow. "Why are you sleeping?" he asked them. "Get up and pray so that you will not fall into temptation."

Jesus had warned them: "Pray that you will not fall into temptation" (22:40). The temptation he meant was the temptation to betray him, to abandon their commitment when facing fear and suffering. Yet even as he prayed in agony, they slept.

When the arrest comes, their failure becomes complete:

> Then seizing him, they led him away and took him into the house of the high priest. Peter followed at a distance.

Where are the other disciples? Only Peter remains, and even his loyalty wavers.

## Betrayal by Peter (Luke 22:54-62)

The deepest wound comes from Jesus' closest friend. Peter was part of the inner circle of three—the key disciple, the Rock on which the church would be built. Yet:

> A servant girl saw him seated there in the firelight. She looked closely at him and said, "This man was with him." But he denied it. "Woman, I don't know him," he said.

Three times Peter denies any connection to Jesus:

1. **To a servant girl:** He denies even knowing Jesus. Fear of a person of no social standing frightens him into lies.

2. **To another bystander:** He denies being one of the disciples. "Man, I am not!"

3. **To yet another:** He denies being from Galilee—the very region where he first met Jesus. "Man, I don't know what you're talking about!"

Then the rooster crowed:

> The Lord turned and looked straight at Peter. Then Peter remembered the word the Lord had spoken to him: "Before the rooster crows today, you will disown me three times." And he went outside and wept bitterly.

Peter's fall is devastating precisely because of what he had promised:

> "Lord, I am ready to go with you to prison and to death." Jesus answered, "I tell you, Peter, before the rooster crows today, you will deny three times that you know me."

How does someone go from "I will be with you to prison and death" to "I am not with him"? Was it embarrassment? The desire for acceptance? Fear? A failure of faith?

**The Slow Drift Away**

As you head into university, many forces will tempt you away from faith in Jesus:

- The desire to earn money and the pressure to overwork
- Romantic relationships with those who don't share your faith
- The desire to look cool, to fit in
- The drive to excel academically at any cost

Few people decide suddenly and dramatically that they no longer want to be Christian. Instead, the drift happens gradually, almost imperceptibly. The first assignment becomes the first excuse. Without Christian fellowship, your motivation to pray and read the Bible fades. Then you're busy here, busy there—another activity, another commitment. You miss the youth group gathering for a work shift. You skip campus ministry for a society meeting. Each decision seems reasonable in isolation, each legitimate in its own way. But slowly, there is no room left for Jesus. And one day you wake up living in a completely different pattern, having betrayed the faith that once defined you.

The danger is that by then, few people will even ask what happened. You simply won't be around anymore. And one day, Jesus becomes nothing more than a distant memory of childhood.

I've watched this happen. A young man who grew up in the church decided to take a year away to study for his final exams. He stopped coming to church, stopped connecting with Christian friends. He got so comfortable in that new lifestyle that he never came back. He told himself he would read the Bible alone, but without his Christian community, it was too easy to drift. He betrayed a friendship that had sustained him since childhood.

Or consider the Christian young man who fell in love with a non-Christian woman. He reasoned that he needed to accompany her away from church so that he could eventually share the gospel with her. But there was always something else to do when they were together, always a reason why bringing up Jesus would be awkward. After a year, he realized she had no real interest in faith. By then, he was in love and didn't want to end the relationship. So Jesus went to the background—a promise for "someday" that never comes. He had betrayed his King.

## The Faithful King (Luke 22:42-43, 49-53)

In stark contrast to his friends' betrayals, Jesus remained completely faithful.

### Not My Will, But Yours Be Done

In the garden, Jesus prayed with anguish:

> "Father, if you are willing, take this cup from me; yet not my will, but yours be done."

The "cup" is a biblical symbol for God's wrath and judgment. Jesus is asking to be spared from drinking it. His temptation—and it was real—was to avoid the cross, to escape pain and suffering. This is not the prayer of a coward but the prayer of one who understood the full weight of what lay ahead. The cross meant not only physical flogging and crucifixion, not only the mental anguish of being despised and rejected even by his own people, but something infinitely worse: the spiritual agony of bearing the sins of the world, of experiencing the divine judgment those sins deserved.

This is the cup Jesus faced, and any right-thinking person would recoil from it. Yet Jesus did not refuse the cup on his own terms. He submitted: "Not my will, but your will be done."

An angel strengthened him, and he prayed more earnestly, his sweat falling like drops of blood. His prayer was answered not by escape but by Judas's arrival—the moment he would fulfill his purpose.

> An angel from heaven appeared to him and strengthened him. And being in anguish, he prayed more earnestly, and his sweat was like drops of blood falling to the ground.

Jesus did not betray God's will. Even as his friends abandoned him, even as Peter denied him, even as he faced the cup of divine wrath—Jesus remained faithful.

## The Promise of Restoration

Before his denial, Jesus gave Peter a promise that extends to all his followers:

> "Simon, Simon, Satan has asked to sift all of you as wheat. But I have prayed for you, Simon, that your faith may not fail. And when you have turned back, strengthen your brothers."

Satan asked for the right to test them—as he did with Job. But Jesus prayed for Peter specifically, and his prayer was not that Peter would never stumble, but that his faith would not ultimately fail. Even more: when Peter turned back—when he repented—he would have the privilege of strengthening others.

Peter's fall was not final. Unlike Judas, whose betrayal seemed to mark him permanently, Peter represents reversal and restoration. When you fall away, you can turn back. When you betray, repentance is possible. Jesus himself ensures it.

## A Challenge for Today

Friends, you are not like those who permanently turned away. You are those for whom Jesus prays. He has prayed for your faith not to fail.

The critical question is this: When times get tough, when following Jesus costs you popularity or comfort, will you betray him?

Perhaps you haven't fallen away completely—otherwise you wouldn't be here. But if you sense yourself heading that direction, why not do something about it now? Talk to a Christian friend. Strengthen your resolve. Make a deliberate choice to stay loyal.

And more than that: **start declaring your faith openly.** Don't wait until you're older and more secure. If you're in school, take the opportunity to be different. When the opportunity comes, say, "I'm going to the Christian group." If you don't do it now—in these formative years when you think popularity comes from a teacher saying hello to you—when will you do it?

Don't betray him when times get tough. Turn back if you stumble. Strengthen your feeble arms. Stay loyal to the King who remained faithful to you.
