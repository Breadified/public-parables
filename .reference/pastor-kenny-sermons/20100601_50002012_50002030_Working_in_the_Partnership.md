# Philippians 2:12-30

**Title:** Working in the Partnership

**Date:** 20 June 2010

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Cost of Partnership

During World War Two, British citizens faced severe rationing of meat, sugar, eggs, and butter. Faced with scarcity, housewives became creative, developing recipes for "mock" foods—mock goose from lamb, mock duck from sausage meat, mock chicken from wheat. These weren't preferences; they were necessary sacrifices. The resources went to the war effort. The homefront worked for victory, even if it meant eating fake food.

Paul calls the Philippians to the same kind of austerity in their partnership of the gospel. This isn't about half-measures. It's about pouring everything out for the cause.

## 1. Work for the Victory (2:12-13)

> Therefore, my dear friends, as you have always obeyed—not only in my presence, but now much more in my absence—continue to work out your salvation with fear and trembling, for it is God who works in you to will and to act according to his good purpose.

### Understanding "Work Out Your Salvation"

This verse is often misunderstood as promoting salvation by works. It doesn't. Paul addresses the Philippians as "saints" and "beloved"—terms he reserves for those eternally saved. The issue isn't whether they are saved, but what their salvation means for how they live.

The word "salvation" here carries a specific meaning in Philippians. When Paul uses it elsewhere in this letter:

- In 1:19, referring to his deliverance from prison or death
- In 1:28, meaning vindication or triumph over adversaries

So in 2:12, "work out your salvation" means **pursue your vindication, your triumph, your ultimate victory**. Paul encourages exhausted believers: Keep going. Keep obeying God. The end is near. You will triumph and be vindicated. Keep going till the end. It will be worth it.

This resonates with what we've just learned about Jesus in verses 8-11:

> He humbled himself and became obedient to death—even death on a cross! Therefore God exalted him to the highest place and gave him the name that is above every name, that at the name of Jesus every knee should bow... and every tongue confess that Jesus Christ is Lord, to the glory of God the Father.

Triumph through obedience. Vindication through sacrifice.

### God Works in Us (2:13)

> For it is God who works in you to will and to act according to his good purpose.

God's work in us isn't mystical or spooky. As Paul says in 1 Thessalonians 2:13, when we receive and understand God's word, it "is at work in you who believe." His word changes us. We are transformed by truth.

Three crucial truths:

1. **It is God's working in us.** We're not left to our own strength.

2. **It is according to his good purposes.** God delights to work in us. It pleases him. As Paul notes elsewhere (1:29), we've been granted not only to believe in Christ but also to suffer for him.

3. **We obey for an audience of One.** We don't work out our salvation to impress people or earn God's approval. We do it because we fear God—we recognize his authority as judge. We do it "with fear and trembling," knowing that God himself works in us according to his good purpose.

## 2. Do Not Grumble (2:14-16)

> Do everything without complaining or arguing, so that you may become blameless and pure, children of God without fault in a crooked and depraved generation, in which you shine like stars in the universe as you hold out the word of life.

### What Does "Everything" Mean?

This doesn't mean "do anything without complaining." Rather, it connects back to verse 3: "Do nothing out of selfish ambition... but in humility consider others better than yourselves. Each of you should look not only to your own interests, but also to the interests of others."

"Do everything without complaining" means: **When you have opportunity to serve each other, don't grumble about it.** Don't let pride make you think you're too good for humble service. Don't approach your responsibilities with a spirit of complaint.

This echoes a warning from Israel's wilderness wandering. Deuteronomy 32:5 describes Israel as "a warped and crooked generation." Numbers 14:27-35 records God's response to their grumbling:

> "How long will this wicked community grumble against me? I have heard the complaints of these grumbling Israelites. So tell them, 'As surely as I live, I will do to you the very things I heard you say: In this desert your bodies will fall.'"

God provided everything for Israel, and they grumbled. Many of us do the same:

- We grumble about our circumstances, then do nothing to improve them
- We complain about work situations we've chosen
- We pray hard for admission to a course, get in, then grumble that the exams are hard—as if God's fault
- We lament the lack of suitable partners but never make time to meet people or commit to places where Christians gather
- We complain about our church but do nothing to build it up

The pattern is destructive. Don't be a grumbler.

### Becoming Blameless and Pure (2:15)

When you serve without complaining, you "become blameless and pure, children of God without fault in a crooked and depraved generation."

You become what Israel failed to become—a people marked by faithfulness, by genuine obedience, by the absence of complaint and grumbling.

### Shining Like Stars (2:15b-16a)

> In which you shine like stars in the universe as you hold out the word of life.

Think about what provides light in a dark universe: stars. Galaxies. Stars are the universe's primary source of light.

As you emulate Christ, as you serve humbly, as you hold onto the word that saves you—you become beacons of hope in a dark world. You shine with the light of Christ. The very people Israel failed to become, you become. Others see Christ in you, just as Paul's jailers came to see something different about him.

## 3. Paul Poured Out (2:16b-18)

> In order that I may boast on the day of Christ that I did not run or labor for nothing. But even if I am being poured out like a drink offering on the sacrifice and service coming from your faith, I am glad and rejoice with all of you. So you too should be glad and rejoice with me.

### The Drink Offering

Paul uses the image of an ancient sacrifice. In the Old Testament, a drink offering meant pouring liquid—blood or wine—onto the sacrifice. Paul sees his ministry to the Philippians as a pouring out of himself.

"I am poured out upon the sacrifice—namely, the service of your faith."

Even if his very blood is poured out in service to them, he rejoices. He doesn't demand their gratitude. He invites them into his joy. "So you too should be glad and rejoice with me."

This is profoundly different from how the world operates. When leaders sacrifice much, they often expect recognition and reward. Paul offers sacrifice and asks only that his people share in his gladness.

### The Weight of Sacrifice

Consider Prime Minister Kevin Rudd, who poured everything into his role, implementing progressive policies—recognition of China, legal aid, Medicare, the end of the White Australia policy. Yet once his polls declined, he was removed. The party had used him for the hard work of winning, then discarded him when convenient. One wonders if he felt it was worth it.

Paul says: Yes. I'll do that. I'll be the sacrifice in service to the Philippians. Just as Jesus did. Just as my companions do.

## 4. Timothy: The Likeminded Servant (2:19-24)

> I hope in the Lord Jesus to send Timothy to you soon, that I also may be cheered when I receive news about you. I have no one else like him, who takes a genuine interest in your welfare. For everyone looks out for his own interests, not those of Jesus Christ. But you know that Timothy has proved himself, because as a son with his father he has served with me in the work of the gospel. I hope, therefore, to send him as soon as I see how things go with me. And I am confident in the Lord that I myself will come soon.

### Why Timothy?

The Philippians were expecting Timothy to deliver this letter, but he's not coming—at least not yet. Paul explains why Timothy is so valued:

- **He is unique.** "I have no one else like him." His singularity lies not in skill, but in character.

- **He takes genuine interest in your welfare.** While others look out for their own interests, Timothy cares about the Philippians.

- **He has proved himself through service.** "As a son with his father he has served with me in the work of the gospel." The Greek word here means he "slaved" in regard to the gospel—the same word Paul used in 2:7 when describing Jesus "taking the form of a slave."

Timothy embodies the very humility and other-centeredness Paul is calling the Philippians to embrace.

## 5. Epaphroditus: Risking Everything (2:25-30)

> But I think it is necessary to send back to you Epaphroditus, my brother, fellow worker and fellow soldier, who is also your messenger, whom you sent to take care of my needs. For he longs for all of you and is distressed because you heard he was ill. Indeed he was ill, and almost died. But God had mercy on him, and not on him only but also on me, to spare me sorrow upon sorrow. Therefore I am all the more eager to send him, so that when you see him again you may be glad and I may have less anxiety. Welcome him in the Lord with great joy, and honor men like him, because he almost died for the work of Christ, risking his life to make up for the help you could not give me.

### The Sacrifice of Partnership

Epaphroditus came from Philippi to serve Paul in prison. He became seriously ill—nearly died. Rather than simply recovering and returning home, Paul frames his homecoming as a commission: Go back to the church that sent you. Let them see that you risked everything.

"Welcome him in the Lord with great joy, and honor men like him, because he almost died for the work of Christ, risking his life."

This is the essence of partnership in the gospel. It costs something. It requires:

- Risking your life
- Risking your career
- Risking your comfort
- Risking your happiness
- Being willing to pour out in service

And yet, like Paul, like Jesus, like Timothy and Epaphroditus—it is worth it.

## Conclusion: The Challenge to Partnership

As you consider your partnership together, what are your priorities?

- **If you're a visitor:** Welcome. Just come and observe.

- **If you're new:** Commit to regular attendance and to being trained in the word of life.

- **If you've been here:** Consider how you might serve. Put your name down. Use holidays as opportunities to serve someone struggling in faith, or organize something extra, or stay back and help with cleanup. Look around. Where is your service needed?

### The Ministry of the Housewife's Pail

During the war, rationing was effective. Every housewife saved scraps—bones, waste—in a pail. These went into communal bins that the refuse collectors emptied every few days. The bins became dented, smelly, unpleasant. But housewives did it anyway.

A Ministry of Food advertisement captured it perfectly:

> Because of the pail, the scraps were saved,
> Because of the scraps, the pigs were saved,
> Because of the pigs, the rations were saved,
> Because of the rations, the ships were saved,
> Because of the ships, the island was saved,
> Because of the island, the Empire was saved,
> And all because of the housewife's pail.

**Your work is needed.**

As we work together through this partnership, we need both large sacrifices and small ones. All are necessary if we are to reach more people in the Hills with the gospel of Christ.

But it will be worth it.
