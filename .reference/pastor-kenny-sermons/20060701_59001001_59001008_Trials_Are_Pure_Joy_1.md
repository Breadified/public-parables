# James 1:1-8

**Title:** Trials Are Pure Joy  
**Date:** 2 July 2006  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## How Should We Think About Trials?

When we speak of trials, what comes to mind? For some, it's exam season. For others, it might be bullying or ridicule at school. Perhaps you're dealing with a chronic illness you've battled for years. Maybe you struggle with studies or friendship issues at school—the pain of feeling unwanted. Or perhaps home feels unstable, and you don't know what to do with the weight of it all. We simply know we dislike trials and want them over.

But James calls us to something radically different.

> Consider it pure joy, my brothers, whenever you face trials of many kinds. (James 1:2)

That's a shocking statement. It sounds cruel—as if God sits on high, playing Xbox with our lives and emotions. But that's not who God is.

## God Is Good and Trustworthy

Later in this same letter, James describes God clearly:

> Every good and perfect gift is from above, coming down from the Father of the heavenly lights, who does not change like shifting shadows. (James 1:17)

God is our Father who gives good gifts. He is good. He is faithful. He does not change his mind or shift like shadows. He is reliable.

This raises the age-old problem: if God is good, how can suffering and trials exist? Non-believers ask this question and conclude either that God doesn't exist, or that He's weak or cruel. How can a good God allow such hardship?

But the logic doesn't hold. A good God and the existence of suffering don't contradict each other—God may have something better in mind. God believes the way of trials is best for us, for our ultimate good.

Consider our own parents: they're good people who care for our wellbeing, yet they discipline us. If they never allowed us to face any difficulty or correction, that wouldn't be good parenting at all.

## Trials Are Not Cruel Tests

God's testing is not designed to fail you—not like a high jump competition where you keep going until you lose, where everyone eventually fails and the last person to fail "wins." That's just losing on a sliding scale.

Instead, God tests to see how you've progressed. He's asking: "What have you learned? How much do you know?" This kind of testing consolidates what you already know—like exams that measure what you've mastered, not what you've missed.

And what do you know? You know that God loves you and that you can depend on Him because of Jesus. You know that when your present life and this difficult world end, a better life awaits. Your trials now are opportunities to confirm these truths. You go through an experience and ask yourself: "How am I depending on God right now?"

Therefore, James says rejoice—not by pretending suffering doesn't hurt or that you're okay when you're not. Rather, rejoice because trials remind you of what you know: that God is good to you. Trials prompt you to look forward to the future and tell yourself again that God is faithful.

## Two Ways Trials Are Good

### 1. Trials Develop Perseverance

> Because you know that the testing of your faith develops perseverance. (James 1:3)

There are two kinds of perseverance. The first is like athletic endurance: you start running a couple of laps, then gradually increase the distance, add gym work, stretch, and slowly build strength and capacity. You grow more powerful.

But the perseverance James speaks of is different. It's more like patience—doing the same basic thing over and over, day after day. It's like walking up an escalator that's going down. You're not necessarily getting stronger, and improvement isn't the point. You're simply keeping going, refusing to follow the escalator down.

You don't need to be a super-Christian athlete. Just a regular, unfit shopper, walking one foot in front of the other. Keep going.

James points us to Job as the example. He waited. The farmer waits for autumn and spring rains:

> Be patient, then, brothers, until the Lord's coming. See how the farmer waits for the land to yield its valuable crop and how patient he is for the autumn and spring rains. (James 5:7)

And James speaks of those who persevere:

> As you know, we consider blessed those who have persevered. You have heard of Job's perseverance and have seen what the Lord finally brought about. The Lord is full of compassion and mercy. (James 5:10-11)

Perseverance is long-term. It's not always visible. It's boring. God is changing you, keeping you on that escalator—one foot over the next, over and over. *Jesus loves me. Trust in Jesus. The day is coming.*

You can't see much progress now. Luther noted that the world drives us backward constantly, so that we may not advance—but at least we're not going backwards. This protects us from unrealistic expectations and from the false teaching that if you name it and claim it, everything will be successful. That's not the Christian life.

**The Application of Waiting:**

The danger in waiting is that we sin. But all we must do is wait—and not sin. We wait without panic or fear, trusting that good is coming.

*Short-term waiting:* A mosquito bite. Food poisoning. A teenager's problems vanish after high school. Sickness. These pass with time. Sometimes you don't need to change; circumstances do.

*Long-term waiting:* Of course, take steps to alleviate suffering where you can. But sometimes you can't fix things. That's okay. We have one great goal: heaven. Some things we simply can't fix, and we trust God with them.

### 2. Trials Produce Completeness

> Perseverance must finish its work so that you may be mature and complete, not lacking anything. (James 1:4)

The goal is *completeness*—maturity, godliness, wholeness, wisdom. It means lacking nothing. Ultimately, it means becoming like Jesus.

What God starts, He finishes.

Now you may be incomplete, immature, struggling with waiting. But then you'll be complete, with a mind thoroughly obedient to God. Trials bring us to this completeness.

If you lack wisdom, ask:

> If any of you lacks wisdom, he should ask God, who gives generously to all without finding fault, and it will be given to him. (James 1:5)

But here's the key: if you want maturity, trials must run their course. You must stick with it to the end. You can't ask God for humility and then decide to be proud again. You can't ask for maturity while living immaturely.

> But when he asks, he must believe and not doubt, because he who doubts is like a wave of the sea, blown and tossed by the wind. That man should not think he will receive anything from the Lord; he is a double-minded man, unstable in all he does. (James 1:6-8)

Don't blame God when He uses trials to mature you.

## The Motivation: Life Beyond Trials

The question now isn't "What about trials?" but "What's on the other side of trials?" Everything changes when you fix your eyes on what's ahead.

We set many aims in life—becoming the best musician or artist in the world. But consider: you might play different instruments in heaven. Your paintings might be burned up. One goal truly matters and requires us to reassess everything: *eternity with God.*

So let us wait patiently. Let us pray, asking for wisdom. Let us expect suffering, knowing we have a Father who loves us. Rejoice, because through trials, the Father is making us complete.

How we respond to trials reveals how much we live for today versus tomorrow. It's an opportunity to repent and turn again to trust the living God.

## Pastoral Encouragement

Continue to encourage one another to wait. If something feels unbearably hard and you have no one to talk to, my wife and I are available. This is why we gather together—to share our burdens, to wait together, to walk this boring escalator together.
