# Job 42:1-17

**Title:** The End of Suffering  
**Date:** August 2, 2013  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## God Speaks

After over forty long chapters of questions, suffering, and despair, God finally breaks His silence. This is the turning point of Job's entire struggle.

> Then Job replied to the Lord: "I know that you can do all things; no plan of yours can be thwarted. You asked, 'Who is this that obscures my counsel without knowledge?' Surely I spoke of things I did not understand, things too wonderful for me to know. You said, 'Listen now, and I will speak; I will question you, and you shall answer me.' My ears had heard of you but now my eyes have seen you." (Job 42:1-5)

Throughout the book, Job's deepest longing was not for restoration of his possessions—he never asked for his money or animals back. His cry was simpler and more profound: he wanted God to break His silence. He yearned to speak with God, to see Him, to ask why his suffering had come, and to hear God's response. Job needed assurance that God was not against him.

God answers not with explanations, but with presence. He speaks to Job out of the storm, and His speech is remarkable. God doesn't explain the cause of Job's suffering. Instead, He points to His creative power and sustained control over all things.

### God as Master Builder

God speaks like an expert builder surveying his own work. He asks Job: "Where were you when I laid the earth's foundation? Who marked off its dimensions? On what were its footings set, or who laid its cornerstone?" (Job 38:4-6). 

The logic is clear and humbling. God created the earth before Job existed. He has been sustaining it for millennia—long before Job was born, long before his parents were born. The earth still stands. A building that has endured for eighty years testifies to the builder's skill. How much more does creation testify to God's mastery?

### God Controls the Wild

God then turns to the animal kingdom—those creatures wild and untamed, beyond human control. The mountain goats, the wild donkeys, the wild oxen—none consent to human service. None can be harnessed or commanded. Yet God knows when they give birth, where they find pasture, how they thrive.

> "Will the wild ox consent to serve you? Will it stay by your manger at night? Can you hold it to the furrow with a harness? Will it till the valleys behind you?" (Job 39:9-12)

These creatures exist outside Job's sphere of influence. God controls what Job cannot control.

### The Logic of Faith

Here is the theological principle God establishes: He controlled the past. He controls the present. Therefore, Job must trust Him with the future.

If God is sovereign over creation and history, then faith in ultimate justice becomes reasonable. Not because we understand all the answers—we don't. But because we trust the One who knows.

## The Nature of Suffering's Answer

Job doesn't receive a complete theodicy—a full explanation of why suffering exists. This sermon echoes throughout Scripture: suffering often has no simple answer that satisfies our demand for cosmic justice.

We ask why with scientific precision. Why cancer? Because cells mutate and grow out of control. Why did I get cancer? Perhaps poor diet, sun exposure, genetics. But even these answers beg the final question: why? The world, in this regard, offers only silence.

Yet God has spoken into this vacuum. Two thousand years ago, He came as the man Jesus and did something far more powerful than offering explanations. He entered into suffering Himself. He demonstrated that God knows what He is doing, and that His knowledge is not arbitrary—it is loving, purposeful, and redemptive.

> "God working good through those who love him." (Romans 8:28)

The answer is not found in explanation but in trust. And when Job hears God's voice—not an answer to his "why," but a revelation of God's character and control—his faith deepens. He is ready to move forward.

## Moving On From Mourning

Job's response is striking:

> "Therefore I despise myself and repent in dust and ashes." (Job 42:6)

This verse has confused many readers. How can Job repent when God Himself declares him righteous? Look at God's rebuke to Job's friends:

> "After the Lord had said these things to Job, he said to Eliphaz the Temanite, 'I am angry with you and your two friends, because you have not spoken the truth about me, as my servant Job has.'" (Job 42:7-8)

God repeats this twice: Job has spoken what is right; his friends have not. So repentance from sin makes no sense here. You cannot repent from righteousness.

The Hebrew is more subtle. Job is saying: "I despise and repent from dust and ashes"—not from sin, but from mourning. The "dust and ashes" is his posture of lament and grief. Recall where Job has been sitting:

> "He throws me into the mud, and I am reduced to dust and ashes." (Job 30:19)

Job had been in continuous mourning, crying out to God in anguish. But now that God has spoken and revealed His sovereignty, Job is ready to turn from mourning. He repents—turns aside, changes direction—from the ashes and dust of grief.

### The Danger of Extended Mourning

There is a proper time for mourning. In many cultures, there is an appropriate period to grieve, to cease normal activities, to refrain from celebration. But some of us mourn too long.

Consider Queen Victoria. When Prince Albert died in 1862, she declared the longest period of public mourning in modern times. But she went further—she wore black mourning clothes for the rest of her life until her death in 1901. For forty years, she remained in mourning.

Like Queen Victoria, we can allow suffering and loss to become permanent identities. We grieve over broken relationships, failed exams, rejected opportunities, our family history. And we cannot move on.

**But suffering cannot become an excuse for everything.** It cannot excuse us from obeying God, from hoping in Him, from being productive in His service. Yes, there is a time for mourning. But there is also a time to move on.

### Perseverance Through Suffering

The purpose of a test is to pass it. Job has passed his test by remaining faithful despite his suffering. Now it is time to move forward.

> "Endure hardship as discipline; God disciplines those he loves. No discipline seems pleasant at the time, but painful. Later on, however, it produces a harvest of righteousness and peace for those who have been trained by it." (Hebrews 12:7-11)

Some of us are stuck. We cannot move past the broken relationship, the uncertainty about the future, the sense of failure. We are paralyzed by what happened and what might have been.

But resilience is the ability to get up after being knocked down and try again. It is choosing to change what you can control—yourself—and trusting God with what you cannot—others, the world, the future.

## The Reward of Perseverance

When Job moves out of mourning and returns to his normal life, he discovers something remarkable: God blesses him with twice as much as he had before.

> "After Job had prayed for his friends, the Lord made him prosperous again and gave him twice as much as he had before." (Job 42:10)

### Double the Possessions

> "He had fourteen thousand sheep, six thousand camels, a thousand yoke of oxen and a thousand donkeys." (Job 42:12)

Job returns to being a man of great wealth—doubled from his original state.

### Double the Family

> "He also had seven sons and three daughters. The first daughter he named Jemimah, the second Keziah and the third Keren-Happuch. Nowhere in all the land were there found women as beautiful as Job's daughters, and their father granted them an inheritance along with their brothers." (Job 42:13-15)

The fact that even his daughters receive inheritance is significant. In patriarchal societies, daughters typically do not inherit land—sons do. But Job is so abundantly blessed that even his daughters inherit. Their exceptional beauty would have secured their futures through marriage alone, yet their father grants them wealth as well.

### Double the Years

> "After this, Job lived a hundred and forty years; he saw his children and their children to the fourth generation. And so he died, old and full of years." (Job 42:16-17)

The normal lifespan in Scripture is about seventy years. Job lives to one hundred forty—twice the normal span. He sees his descendants to the fourth generation, becoming a great-grandfather before his death.

### A Grace Beyond Compensation

These blessings exceed what Job lost. They are not merely compensation but gestures of grace. Job received undeserved suffering; now he receives undeserved blessing. From the same hand comes both trial and triumph. This reveals God's true character: His last word is not judgment but blessing.

As Job himself said:

> "Naked I came from my mother's womb, and naked I will depart. The Lord gave and the Lord has taken away; may the name of the Lord be praised." (Job 1:21)

## Looking Beyond This Life

The comfort of Job's story is that God cannot be thwarted. If He can send great suffering, He can also send great blessing. His ultimate promise to His people is not merely restoration in this life, but redemption beyond it.

> "And we know that in all things God works for the good of those who love him, who have been called according to his purpose. For those God foreknew he also predestined to be conformed to the likeness of his Son, that he might be the firstborn among many brothers." (Romans 8:28-29)

Suffering in this world is not meant to make life miserable. It is meant to conform us to Christ's image and to prepare us for an inheritance that awaits. The end of suffering comes not in this life alone, but in the life to come.

> "I consider that our present sufferings are not worth comparing with the glory that will be revealed in us. The creation waits in eager expectation for the sons of God to be revealed. For the creation was subjected to frustration, in hope that the creation itself will be liberated from its bondage to decay and brought into the glorious freedom of the children of God." (Romans 8:18-21)

### The Patient Endurance of Faith

Scripture calls us to patient endurance, just as the farmers wait for the autumn and spring rains to bring in their harvest:

> "Be patient, then, brothers, until the Lord's coming. See how the farmer waits for the land to yield its valuable crop and how patient he is for the autumn and spring rains. Be patient and stand firm, because the Lord's coming is near. As an example of patience in the face of suffering, take the prophets who spoke in the name of the Lord. As you know, we consider blessed those who have persevered. You have heard of Job's perseverance and have seen what the Lord finally brought about. The Lord is full of compassion and mercy." (James 5:7-11)

## Jesus: God's Ultimate Answer to Suffering

Jesus speaks to this same tension. When asked why certain people suffer, He refuses the easy answer of karma:

> "Do you think that these Galileans were worse sinners than all the other Galileans because they suffered this way? I tell you, no! But unless you repent, you too will all perish." (Luke 13:1-3)

No—suffering is not proportional to sin. But it is a call to repentance and a call to get right with God.

Jesus Himself did not provide a complete theological explanation of suffering. Instead, He entered into it. He got on the cross and paid the penalty for sin. He suffered so that our suffering might one day end—not in explanation, but in redemption.

As C.S. Lewis wrote: "God whispers to us in our pleasures, speaks to us in our conscience, but shouts in our pains: It is His megaphone to rouse a deaf world."

## Application: Trust and Move Forward

God has spoken to you through His Word. He has provided comfort to those who suffer. And He promises that for those who persevere in faith, the end is not suffering but blessing—both in this life and the life to come.

The question before us is simple: Will we trust Him? Will we move on from our mourning? Will we persevere through our trials, knowing that God is working all things together for our good and conforming us to the image of Christ?

The answer is not found in complete understanding. It is found in trust. And in that trust, we find peace.
