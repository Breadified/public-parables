# James 3:13 -- 4:12

**Title:** The Root of Fighting: Desires, Envy, and Humility

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## 1. Fighting Against Each Other

Our world is filled with conflict—from international wars to the silliest personal quarrels. We fight over parking spots, clearance sale bargains, and even who gets the better-looking food at dinner. But why do we fight so much?

> What causes fights and quarrels among you? Don't they come from your desires that battle within you? You want something but don't get it. (James 4:1-2)

The root cause is simple: **our desires**. We live in a world of infinite desires but finite resources. When we see what others have—their clothes, phones, looks, success—we become envious. We want what they have, and when we can't get it through normal means, frustration drives us to take it by other methods.

This desire-driven conflict escalates dangerously. As James points out:

> You kill and covet, but you cannot have what you want. You quarrel and fight. (James 4:2)

The connection between envy and violence runs deep through Scripture. Consider these tragic examples:

- **Cain and Abel**: Cain's envy led him to murder his own brother
- **Saul and David**: Saul's jealousy of David's popularity drove him to pursue David's life
- **Joseph and his brothers**: Joseph's brothers were so jealous they plotted to kill him
- **Jesus' persecution**: The chief priests handed Jesus over *out of envy* (Mark 15:10)

Envy doesn't just make us want things—it makes us willing to destroy others to get them. It's what transforms ordinary people into those capable of murder.

Even if we're not literally killing others, this same hostile energy shows up in our relationships. We become envious when we're not someone's closest friend. We hate those who won't return our romantic interest. We sabotage their other relationships to satisfy our frustrated desires.

The pattern is clear: unmet desires lead to envy, envy leads to conflict, and conflict escalates to violence—whether physical or relational.

---

## 2. Fighting Against God

But there's a second level to this conflict. When we can't get what we want from each other, we often turn our frustration on God.

> You do not have, because you do not ask God. When you ask, you do not receive, because you ask with wrong motives, that you may spend what you get on your pleasures. (James 4:2-3)

We fight first and pray later—only when our own efforts fail. And when God doesn't answer our prayers for selfish things, we turn on Him. We shake our fists at God, questioning His goodness, His love, or His power. But James tells us the real problem: **we're asking with wrong motives**. We want things for our own comfort, pleasure, and ego.

This is what God thinks of such desires:

> You adulterous people, don't you know that friendship with the world is hatred toward God? Anyone who chooses to be a friend of the world becomes an enemy of God. (James 4:4)

When we prioritize our selfish desires over our relationship with God, we're committing adultery—cheating on Him. We're saying that what we want matters more than our covenant with Him. And in doing so, we become God's enemies.

But this is not who we are called to be. God has given us something far better:

> Or do you think Scripture says without reason that the spirit he caused to live in us envies intensely? But he gives us more grace. That is why Scripture says: "God opposes the proud but gives grace to the humble." (James 4:5-6)

God didn't give us the Holy Spirit so we could be envious. He gave us **grace**—undeserved favor. The problem is pride. When we're proud, we feel entitled to what others have. We think we deserve to keep up with our peers, own the same things, wear the same clothes. We even think God *owes* us answers to our prayers. But God is not a cosmic vending machine. He opposes the proud.

Instead, we must be humble:

> Submit yourselves, then, to God. Resist the devil, and he will flee from you. Come near to God and he will come near to you. Wash your hands, you sinners, and purify your hearts, you double-minded. Grieve, mourn and wail. Change your laughter to mourning and your joy to gloom. Humble yourselves before the Lord, and he will lift you up. (James 4:7-10)

Humility means submitting our desires to God's will. It means being willing to give up what we want for what He wants. It means purifying our hearts and turning away from self-centeredness.

This humility shows up in small, practical ways. It means giving up that parking spot to someone else—it's just a few more minutes to find another. It means letting others have the bargains at clearance sales, saving yourself hassle and letting someone less fortunate benefit. It means being willing to let others have their way, even when you're right, because some things in this world simply aren't worth fighting over.

## 3. God Fights For Us

We have the perfect model of humility in Jesus Christ:

> Your attitude should be the same as that of Christ Jesus: Who, being in very nature God, did not consider equality with God something to be grasped, but made himself nothing, taking the very nature of a servant, being made in human likeness. And being found in appearance as a man, he humbled himself and became obedient to death—even death on a cross! (Philippians 2:5-8)

Jesus showed us true humility. He didn't retaliate when people insulted Him. He didn't fight back when they tried to kill Him. He lived a humble life toward God, and even in His final moments, He submitted to God's will:

> Father, if you are willing, take this cup from me; yet not my will, but yours be done. (Luke 22:42)

Though Jesus could have called down legions of angels, He gave up His pride as the Son of God and died on a cross for our sins. He died for people who fought each other, for people who fought against Him, even for those who crucified Him.

Because Jesus fought on the cross for us, we don't have to fight for ourselves:

> There is only one Lawgiver and Judge, the one who is able to save and destroy. But you—who are you to judge your neighbor? (James 4:12)

**Jesus will save us.** He will save us from those who fight with us. Though we might lose our possessions or even our lives, we will never lose God. Through Jesus' sacrifice, we've been saved from God's judgment. He's paid the price and satisfied what we truly need: forgiveness of our sins and rebellion against God. He saved us then, and He will save us when He returns.

**Jesus will also judge His enemies.** He is the only Lawgiver and Judge. This means we don't need to fight our battles or judge those who wrong us. God will bring justice on our behalf. That's why it's safe to back down from fights, to forgive, to let go.

> Resist the devil, and he will flee from you. Come near to God and he will come near to you. (James 4:7-8)

God will protect us. He will shield us from evil. There is nothing we need to fear.

## 4. From Fighting Against to Fighting For

The wisdom to live humbly comes from God:

> Who is wise and understanding among you? Let him show it by his good life, by deeds done in the humility that comes from wisdom. But the wisdom that comes from heaven is first of all pure; then peace-loving, considerate, submissive, full of mercy and good fruit, impartial and sincere. Peacemakers who sow in peace raise a harvest of righteousness. (James 3:13, 17-18)

When we have God's wisdom, it produces a good life marked by humility. We stop pursuing impure desires. We become peacemakers instead of fighters.

So how do we live this out? We must **shift our energy from fighting against each other to fighting for each other**.

**First, be content with what you have.** The newest iPhone, the trendiest clothes, the perfect relationship—these are all temporary luxuries. When we see others get them, envy whispers that we need them too. But we don't. Apart from necessities, everything else is a luxury, and we can learn to live without it. All we truly need is Jesus.

**Second, don't tempt others with what you have.** If you own something others don't, don't flaunt it. Dress modestly. Don't show off your latest gadgets or expensive purchases. If you're in a relationship, keep public displays of affection minimal—they can tempt single people and fuel their own desires.

**Third, be self-sacrificial toward each other**, the way Jesus was toward us. Put others' needs before your own. Give your seat on the bus to someone who needs it more. Buy lunch for a friend who's short on cash. Offer rides to church even if it's inconvenient. It's better for them to come to church than to stay away.

**Fourth, be careful with your words.** 

> Brothers, do not slander one another. Anyone who speaks against his brother or judges him speaks against the law and judges it. When you judge the law, you are not keeping it, but sitting in judgment on it. (James 4:11)

There's a difference between slander and biblical rebuke. Slander speaks *against* your brother; rebuke speaks *for the good* of your brother. Correct one another in love, through Scripture, so you can grow together in godliness.

Peter Jensen, the Anglican Archbishop of Sydney, modeled this beautifully in a recent public debate. Though people lashed out at him and mocked Christianity, he didn't retaliate. Instead, he spoke with humility and love, sharing the truth of Christ. Let us follow his example and love the people around us the same way.

The best fights are the ones we avoid. And we avoid them by fighting—against our own pride, our own desires, our own envy—so that we can fight for each other instead.
