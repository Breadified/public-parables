# Zephaniah 1:14–2:3

**Title:** Judgment Day: Salvation  
**Date:** 18 April 2010  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## The Day of the Lord: A Siege (1:14–17)

Zephaniah describes the Day of the Lord using the imagery of a siege—a military blockade designed to conquer through attrition. The prophet paints a vivid picture of God laying siege to Jerusalem, with the Babylonians as His instrument of judgment in 586 BC.

> The great day of the LORD is near—near and coming quickly. Listen! The cry on the day of the LORD will be bitter, the shouting of the warrior there. That day will be a day of wrath, a day of distress and anguish, a day of trouble and ruin, a day of darkness and gloom, a day of clouds and blackness, a day of trumpet and battle cry against the fortified cities and against the corner towers. (Zephaniah 1:14–16)

Notice the emotional weight here. The "bitter cry" speaks not just to what will be heard, but what will be tasted—sulphur, blood, ash. Each element compounds the horror: wrath, distress, anguish, trouble, ruin, darkness, clouds. This is not merely physical destruction but complete devastation.

> I will bring distress on the people and they will walk like blind men, because they have sinned against the LORD. Their blood will be poured out like dust and their entrails like filth. (Zephaniah 1:17)

In a siege, the city starves. Disease spreads unchecked for months, even years. When the walls finally breach, carnage follows. Bodies and dust become indistinguishable from filth. Everyone suffers. As the saying goes, the army is a broad sword, not a scalpel.

### Wealth Cannot Save You

Yet what strikes Zephaniah most profoundly is this: at the time of King Josiah, Judah enjoyed unprecedented prosperity. Assyria was waning, so tribute payments ceased. Trade flourished. The nation grew rich. And yet:

> Neither their silver nor their gold will be able to save them on the day of the LORD's wrath. In the fire of his jealousy the whole world will be consumed, for he will make a sudden end of all who live on the earth. (Zephaniah 1:18)

God is jealous over idolatry. No amount of money can buy Him off. What triggers the Babylonian invasion is Judah's refusal to pay protection money to the new power—spiritual neglect masked as financial independence.

## The Day Still Coming (Present Age)

For us in the New Testament era, that Day did not end in 586 BC when the Babylonians breached Jerusalem's walls. The Day of the Lord is still coming—God's final wrath poured out on all the earth.

He will put ladders over our walls. He will build ramps straight to our doors. He will level the mountains and raise the valleys. He will destroy the gates. This is one invasion no military force on earth can resist—not our physical isolation as an island continent, not our alliances with America or China.

But judgment is not merely physical destruction. Listen to the emotional and spiritual devastation described in verses 14–17:

> Distress, anguish, bitterness, regret. We don't know exactly what hell will be like, but it will be lonely and bitter.

Some imagine hell as a place to hang around with friends, bringing beer and continuing the same bad behavior forever. They are mistaken. There will be no mates, no mateship, no enjoyment of any sort. There will be loneliness and regret. There will be distress and anguish. There will be a cry of desperation.

**It's all bad.**

And nothing will be able to save us—not our great wealth.

> What good is it for a man to gain the whole world, yet forfeit his soul? Or what can a man give in exchange for his soul? (Mark 8:36–37)

Australia emerged from the global financial crisis as the second wealthiest large economy in the world. We think money can buy us out of trouble, buy us time, buy us youth, buy us health. We think our superannuation will provide for us. But no matter what we spend, in the end, we cannot reverse death. We cannot buy back time. We cannot escape the judgment of God. We cannot avoid death.

Money breeds self-reliance. In Sydney, residents can pay for food without knowing or caring where it comes from. Money buys distractions on the way to hell. But good works cannot pay the way either. Going to church cannot pay the way. Being nice cannot pay the way. Calling yourself merely a Christian cannot buy life. Religion won't save you.

## Seeking the Lord Before It's Too Late (2:1–3)

But chapter 2 offers hope. Zephaniah's advice is found in verse 3, and it's so important that it's repeated three times:

> Seek the LORD, all you humble of the land, you who do what he commands. Seek righteousness, seek humility; perhaps you will be sheltered on the day of the LORD's anger. (Zephaniah 2:3)

Before that day comes, Zephaniah urges:

> Gather together, gather together, O shameful nation, before the appointed time arrives and that day sweeps on like chaff, before the fierce anger of the LORD comes upon you, before the day of the LORD's wrath comes upon you. (Zephaniah 2:1–2)

**Three times before.** We don't know when this day is, but it sweeps in quickly. Do something today before it's too late.

### The Three Seeks

**Seek the LORD** — This seems strange. Like a mouse looking for the cat? But seeking the Lord is about making peace with Him. Jesus illustrated this with a parable:

> A king about to go to war considers whether he can oppose an enemy with ten thousand men against twenty thousand. If he cannot, he will send a delegation while the other is still a long way off and will ask for terms of peace. (Luke 14:31–32)

The wise king surrenders and seeks peace. This is acknowledgment of desperate need—not running from judgment, but making terms with the Judge.

**Seek humility** — Matthew 5 captures this:

> Blessed are the meek, for they will inherit the earth. Blessed are those who hunger and thirst for righteousness, for they will be filled. (Matthew 5:5–6)

Bring yourself down, acknowledging the Judge over us.

**Seek righteousness** — Matthew 6 teaches us to seek first God's kingdom:

> Do not store up for yourselves treasures on earth, where moth and rust destroy, and where thieves break in and steal. But store up for yourselves treasures in heaven, where moth and rust do not destroy, and where thieves do not break in and steal. For where your treasure is, there your heart will be also. (Matthew 6:19–21)

> No one can serve two masters. Either he will hate the one and love the other, or he will be devoted to the one and despise the other. You cannot serve both God and Money. (Matthew 6:24)

> But seek first his kingdom and his righteousness, and all these things will be given to you as well. Therefore do not worry about tomorrow, for tomorrow will worry about itself. Each day has enough trouble of its own. (Matthew 6:33–34)

### Seeking Forgiveness

Altogether, seeking God means seeking forgiveness. We must acknowledge our shame and our sin. This is hard for us. We struggle to say sorry to each other, let alone to God.

We've invented ways to apologize that avoid responsibility: "I'm sorry you feel that way" instead of "I'm sorry I hurt you." Admitting we were wrong is humiliating, too shameful. If it's hard with friends, how much harder is it with God? How often do we truly humble ourselves and seek His forgiveness instead of standing arrogantly, claiming we don't need Him?

### The Promise: Shelter

And yet look at the possible good result:

> Perhaps you will be sheltered on the day of the LORD's anger. (Zephaniah 2:3)

Sheltered means hidden, protected. When the siege comes and invaders pour in, where is shelter to be found? Where can we hide?

For the Christian, this promise is no longer a "perhaps." It is a guaranteed yes.

> Ask and it will be given to you; seek and you will find; knock and the door will be opened to you. For everyone who asks receives; he who seeks finds; and to him who knocks, the door will be opened. Which of you, if his son asks for bread, will give him a stone? Or if he asks for a fish, will give him a snake? If you, then, though you are evil, know how to give good gifts to your children, how much more will your Father in heaven give good gifts to those who ask him! (Matthew 7:7–11)

## The Cross: The Day That Changed Everything (33 AD)

This hope is possible because of another day in 33 AD—the day Jesus was crucified for you and me.

It was a day when darkness came over the whole land and the sun stopped shining. The earth shook and the rocks split. Mothers wailed and wept. A Roman warrior cried out: "Surely this was a righteous man!"

On that day, God's wrath was poured out—not on us, but on His Son in our place. The siege was breached. The wall was broken through. And now, through faith in Jesus, we can be sheltered.

## A Question for Today

If you haven't done so, seek God soon.

If you are not a Christian today—if you're not sure that you are saved—honestly, what's stopping you? Maybe you've been coming to church for a while, or you know a few things, or you feel you don't know enough. That's okay. Keep finding out. But remember: God is not asking you to know the Bible perfectly, or to be religious, or to do all these things before you can be one.

God is asking:

- Do you acknowledge your sin?
- Do you seek Him?
- Will you ask for Jesus?
- Will you do this before it's too late?
