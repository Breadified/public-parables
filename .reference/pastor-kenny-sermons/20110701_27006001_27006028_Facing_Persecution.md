# Daniel 6:1-28

**Title:** Facing Persecution
**Date:** 17 July 2011
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Reality of Persecution

In the early centuries of the church, persecution of Christians was brutal and systematic. The early Christian leader Ignatius of Antioch was bound and transported to Rome, where he was devoured by wild beasts in the Colosseum around 108 AD. On his journey to Rome, he wrote letters to the churches, documenting his suffering. The methods of torture devised against Christians were horrific—burning plates of iron, deep dungeons, racks, wild beasts, and worse. Many were denied proper burial, left as warnings to others.

Daniel faces a similar situation in chapter 6, which parallels the persecution narrative of chapter 3. As we see throughout Scripture, persecution of the righteous is a reality for those who follow God.

## Part One: The Persecution of the Righteous (v. 1-5)

### Daniel's Excellence Provokes Envy

After Belshazzar's death (as we saw last week), King Darius takes the throne. He appoints 120 satraps to rule throughout the kingdom, with three administrators overseeing them. Daniel is one of these administrators.

> Now Daniel so distinguished himself among the administrators and the satraps by his exceptional qualities that the king planned to set him over the whole kingdom. (Daniel 6:3)

Daniel's advancement is not through favoritism or corruption, but through genuine excellence. He is trustworthy, diligent, and completely honest in his work.

> At this, the administrators and the satraps tried to find grounds for charges against Daniel in his conduct of government affairs, but they were unable to do so. They could find no corruption in him, because he was trustworthy and neither corrupt nor negligent. (Daniel 6:4)

### The Trap: Attack What Makes Him Different

Unable to find any legitimate grounds for charges, Daniel's enemies resort to a different strategy altogether:

> Finally these men said, "We will never find any basis for charges against this man Daniel unless it has something to do with the law of his God." (Daniel 6:5)

This is the nature of persecution against the righteous. The persecutors cannot attack Daniel's character or work, so they attack the one thing that sets him apart—his faith in God. They conspire to create a law that directly targets his religious practice.

Using flattery toward the king, they manipulate him into issuing a decree:

> "The royal administrators, prefects, satraps, advisers and governors have all agreed that the king should issue an edict and enforce the decree that anyone who prays to any god or man during the next thirty days, except to you, O king, shall be thrown into the lions' den." (Daniel 6:7-8)

The decree is temporary—just thirty days—and designed specifically for Daniel. It is not a consistent law meant to benefit the kingdom, but a trap.

## Part Two: Daniel Remains Righteous (v. 10-13)

### Faithful to His God, Faithful to His Habits

When Daniel learns of the decree, he does something remarkable. He does not hide or compromise:

> Now when Daniel learned that the decree had been published, he went home to his upstairs room where the windows opened toward Jerusalem. Three times a day he got down on his knees and prayed, giving thanks to his God, just as he had done before. (Daniel 6:10)

Notice the phrase "just as he had done before." Daniel is not being rebellious or defiant. He is simply continuing his normal practice of prayer. These are private prayers in his own home—he is not displaying his faith for show.

The open windows toward Jerusalem are symbolic. Though the temple no longer exists in Daniel's time (destroyed by Nebuchadnezzar), and though Daniel is in exile far from home, his prayer toward Jerusalem expresses trust. He prays with hope that one day the city will be restored, that the exiles will return. It echoes the longing of Psalm 137: "By the rivers of Babylon, we sat and wept."

In the same way, when we pray today, we are putting our hope in God. We do not pray toward a physical temple, but through Jesus Christ, who is the temple of God. In His body dwells all the fullness of God, and by His blood, God listens to our prayers for help.

### Discovered and Reported

Of course, Daniel's enemies find him exactly where they knew he would be:

> Then these men went as a group and found Daniel praying and asking God for help. (Daniel 6:11)

They report him to the king:

> "Daniel, who is one of the exiles from Judah, pays no attention to you, O king, or to the decree you put in writing. He still prays three times a day." (Daniel 6:13)

## A Word About Persecution Today

It is indeed annoying and unfair that Daniel is persecuted simply for believing in God. He did his job well, obeyed the laws of the kingdom, and gave no legitimate grounds for charges. Yet a law specifically targeting his faith is enacted against him. This is the nature of persecution.

### Persecution is a Promise, Not a Punishment

Scripture is clear about this reality:

> In fact, everyone who wants to live a godly life in Christ Jesus will be persecuted, while evil men and impostors will go from bad to worse, deceiving and being deceived. (2 Timothy 3:12)

Christians around the world face severe persecution today. While those of us in Australia may not face persecution unto death, we are not exempt. In 1999, missionary Graham Staines and his two young sons, Philip and Timothy, were killed when an anti-Christian mob burned their car while they slept inside.

In our own context, persecution takes smaller forms—but it is persecution nonetheless. At work, you may be gossiped about if you leave on time instead of staying late. At school, you may be excluded from social groups if you attend a Christian gathering. The world expects you to play by its rules, and if you do not conform, you are marked.

The common thread? The world picks on what is distinctive about you—your faith in God, not your nationality or background.

### Persecution Comes in Different Forms

We live in an age of shifting cultural norms. Debates over marriage, education, and religious freedom reveal how quickly cultural consensus can turn against Christian convictions. Yet we must be prepared. If you stand for something, there will be a cost.

Some of us are fortunate to live in schools and communities where Christian culture is accepted, even valued. But Scripture warns: *Everyone who wants to live a godly life will be persecuted.* If you commit to it, be prepared for hardship.

The question is not whether persecution will come, but how you will respond when it does.

## Part Three: Hope in God Who Saves (v. 14-23)

### The King Cannot Save

When Darius hears what has happened, he is deeply distressed:

> When the king heard this, he was greatly distressed; he was determined to rescue Daniel and made every effort until sundown to save him. (Daniel 6:14)

But the king's efforts are futile. He cannot even overrule his own law. The officials remind him:

> "Remember, O king, that according to the law of the Medes and Persians no decree or edict that the king issues can be changed." (Daniel 6:15)

So Daniel is thrown into the lions' den. But the king's parting words carry profound hope:

> The king said to Daniel, "May your God, whom you serve continually, rescue you!" (Daniel 6:16)

### Daniel's Confidence in God

The stone is sealed over the den, locked with the king's signet ring. The king returns to his palace unable to eat or sleep, consumed with anxiety for Daniel.

At dawn, Darius hurries to the lions' den and calls out in anguish:

> "Daniel, servant of the living God, has your God, whom you serve continually, been able to rescue you from the lions?" (Daniel 6:20)

And Daniel answers:

> "O king, live forever! My God sent his angel, and he shut the mouths of the lions. They have not hurt me, because I was found innocent in his sight. Nor have I ever done any wrong before you, O king." (Daniel 6:21-22)

Daniel is alive. Not a single wound marks him. Why? Because:

> He had trusted in his God. (Daniel 6:23)

### The Lesson: Trust God, Not Government

This passage teaches us something crucial. We cannot trust earthly rulers to protect us. Australia has benefited from good government that reflects Christian values in its origins, but we cannot expect that to continue forever. Laws change. Cultures shift. What was once protected may become persecuted.

Scripture is clear: *You cannot trust the government. You must trust God.*

Paul writes:

> For I know that through your prayers and the help given by the Spirit of Jesus Christ, what has happened to me will turn out for my deliverance. I eagerly expect and hope that I will in no way be ashamed, but will have sufficient courage so that now as always Christ will be exalted in my body, whether by life or by death. For to me, to live is Christ and to die is gain. (Philippians 1:19-21)

This is not naive optimism. This is the perspective of someone who knows that if God does not save, then the next life awaits. Either way, there is hope.

### The Cost of Compromise

Daniel could have prayed in secret for thirty days. He could have bowed to the king's authority temporarily, reasoning that it was just a month. But Daniel understood something vital: you cannot take a break from God.

Small compromises become patterns. Temporary sacrifices become permanent losses. Peter and John understood this when they were commanded not to speak in Jesus' name:

> But Peter and John replied, "Judge for yourselves whether it is right in God's sight to listen to you rather than God. For we cannot stop speaking about what we have seen and heard." (Acts 4:19-20)

What are you willing to compromise for? Daniel was willing to face lions rather than hide his faith. He came to work, he prayed, he was transparent about his God. He did not shrink from the smallest things—and so he was ready when the greatest test came.

## Part Four: A Greater Fear Than Lions (v. 24-28)

### Justice and the Fear of God

After Daniel's rescue, those who falsely accused him are brought before the same lions:

> At the king's command, the men who had falsely accused Daniel were brought in and thrown into the lions' den, along with their wives and children. And before they reached the floor of the den, the lions overpowered them and crushed all their bones. (Daniel 6:24)

Then King Darius issues a new decree:

> I issue a decree that in every part of my kingdom people must fear and reverence the God of Daniel. For he is the living God and he endures forever; his kingdom will not be destroyed, his dominion will never end. He rescues and he saves; he performs signs and wonders in the heavens and on the earth. He has rescued Daniel from the power of the lions. (Daniel 6:26-27)

The king has learned an essential truth: there is a God greater than any earthly ruler. There is a fear that supersedes all other fears—the fear of the living God.

### The Exaltation of God

This chapter, like chapter 3 before it and chapter 4 after it, shows how the pagan king comes to acknowledge the supremacy of the true God. Through Daniel's faithfulness, through his refusal to compromise, through his trust in God even unto the lions' den, the kingdom itself is transformed.

> So Daniel prospered during the reign of Darius and the reign of Cyrus the Persian. (Daniel 6:28)

## Conclusion: What Will You Fear?

The ultimate lesson is this: **Fear God, not lions.**

There is a greater fear than persecution, greater than the roar of lions, greater than the displeasure of rulers. It is the fear of God—the reverent awe that recognizes His power, His justice, His faithfulness.

When you fear God, you are free from every other fear. When you trust God, you can face anything.

Will you come out as a Christian in your workplace? At your school? Will you maintain your habits of prayer and Scripture reading, even if it becomes unpopular? Will you speak truth, even when it costs you friendship or advancement?

Daniel's answer echoes through the centuries: Yes. Because there is no pit darker than a life lived in hiding from God. And there is no freedom greater than the freedom found in faithful, open trust in the God who saves.
