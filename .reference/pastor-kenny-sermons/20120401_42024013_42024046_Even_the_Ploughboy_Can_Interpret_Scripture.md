# Luke 24:13-24:46

**Title:** Even the Ploughboy Can Interpret Scripture  
**Date:** April 1, 2012 (SMAC) | Revised April 27, 2025 (ARISE)  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Opening Context

This week Pope Francis died. While we extend our condolences to our Roman Catholic friends—they are allies with us against an increasingly hostile world toward Christianity—we must acknowledge a fundamental difference that emerged from the Reformation: the question of who can interpret Scripture.

The Roman Catholic Church claims that the Pope, and by extension the clergy, possess special knowledge and authority to interpret Scripture with infallibility. The Protestant Reformation reacted precisely to this claim, recognizing that the Church had been demonstrably wrong on many counts. If the Pope could err, his infallibility must be questioned.

From this conviction arose a profound principle: **"Even the ploughboy can interpret Scripture."** This doesn't mean the ploughboy needed no help or education—it means there is no supernatural impediment to ordinary people understanding God's Word when they make the effort and listen to others. With faculties and commitment, any person can know Jesus and learn to live rightly.

---

## 1. A Question on the Road Trip (Luke 24:13-24)

On the day of Resurrection, two disciples walked toward Emmaus, about seven miles from Jerusalem—a journey of two to three hours on foot. As they walked, they discussed everything that had happened: Jesus' crucifixion, the empty tomb, the women's testimony about angels, the reports from those who found the tomb empty.

Their discussion was intense—repeated four times the text mentions them discussing, reasoning, arguing, and disputing. Yet for all their conversation, they remained confused and downcast.

> "As they talked and discussed these things with each other, Jesus himself came up and walked along with them; but they were kept from recognizing him. He asked them, 'What are you discussing together as you walk along?' They stood still, their faces downcast." (Luke 24:15-17)

### The Problem: Not Knowing Jesus

Here lies the irony: they did not *know* (ἐπιγνῶναι) Jesus. So complete was their failure to recognize him that one of them, Cleopas, actually admonished the stranger for not knowing about the events in Jerusalem.

Yet the disciples possessed the facts:
- Jesus was a prophet, powerful in word and deed (24:19)
- The chief priests and rulers had handed him over to be crucified (24:20)
- The women reported they had seen a vision of angels saying he was alive (24:22)
- Some of their companions found the tomb empty just as the women had said (24:24)

But they had profound questions:
- Where is the Redeemer of Israel? (24:21)
- It is the third day—shouldn't the promises be fulfilled? (24:21)
- His body is missing, yet he is not seen (24:24)

The clues were all present. The evidence mounted. Yet understanding eluded them.

---

## 2. The Key to Understanding (Luke 24:25-27)

Jesus' rebuke cut to the heart of the matter:

> "How foolish you are, and how slow to believe all that the prophets have spoken! Did not the Messiah have to suffer these things and then enter his glory?" (Luke 24:25-26)

The disciples' foolishness was not a lack of intelligence but a failure to understand Scripture rightly. They knew facts but missed the pattern. They expected a triumphant king but did not comprehend that **the Messiah must suffer first.**

### Jesus as the Interpretive Key

The solution was profound: Jesus began with Moses and all the Prophets and **explained** (διερμήνευσεν—the root of "hermeneutics," Scripture interpretation) to them what was said in all the Scriptures concerning himself.

> "And beginning with Moses and all the Prophets, he explained to them what was said in all the Scriptures concerning himself." (Luke 24:27)

This does not mean every verse in the Old Testament explicitly mentions Jesus. Rather, Jesus showed them the passages that did address him—the suffering Messiah, the redemptive work, the divine purpose woven throughout Israel's story.

Later, he confirmed this pattern:

> "This is what I told you while I was still with you: Everything must be fulfilled that is written about me in the Law of Moses, the Prophets and the Psalms." (Luke 24:44)

### Understanding: Jesus as the Center, Not the Periphery

Before this encounter, the disciples viewed Jesus as one great prophet among others, perhaps a redeemer—good in degree but not fundamentally different from other prophets. This remains the modern error: Islam sees Jesus as a great prophet; Mormonism as a great prophet; atheists as a great moral example; Hindu thinkers as a great pacifist.

But Jesus is not great in *degree*—he is great in *kind*. He is **the** suffering servant, **the** Messiah, **the** end toward which all Scripture points. The prophets are reinterpreted in light of him. He is the climax and fulfillment of all that has been written. He is the key that unlocks understanding.

## 3. A New Understanding Opens Eyes (Luke 24:28-35)

As they approached Emmaus, Jesus made as if to continue on. But the disciples urged him to stay. When he sat at table with them, something shifted:

> "When he was at the table with them, he took bread, gave thanks, broke it and began to give it to them. Then their eyes were opened and they recognized him, and he disappeared from their sight." (Luke 24:30-31)

What opened their eyes? Not magic or mere miracle, but understanding:

> "They asked each other, 'Were not our hearts burning within us while he talked with us on the road and opened the Scriptures to us?'" (Luke 24:32)

The burning heart—that deep spiritual fire of recognition—came not from the bread but from **the opening of the Scriptures**. As they sat together, the pieces fell into place. The Old Testament narratives they had known their whole lives suddenly made sense: the suffering servant passages, the Psalms of lamentation and vindication, the prophetic declarations of resurrection.

This moment embodied what genuine Scripture study awakens: not mere information but transformation.

---

## 4. The Pattern Repeated (Luke 24:33-45)

The two disciples immediately returned to Jerusalem and found the Eleven gathered, who had their own testimony: "It is true! The Lord has risen and has appeared to Simon" (24:34).

Then the cycle repeated. Jesus appeared among them, and again they struggled to believe, thinking they saw a ghost. He offered physical proof—his hands, his feet, flesh and bones. Yet still doubt lingered.

> "While they still did not believe it because of joy and amazement, he asked them, 'Do you have anything here to eat?' They gave him a piece of broiled fish, and he took it and ate it in their presence." (Luke 24:41-43)

Again, Jesus offered explanation:

> "This is what I told you while I was still with you: Everything must be fulfilled that is written about me in the Law of Moses, the Prophets and the Psalms." (Luke 24:44)

And finally: **"Then he opened their minds so they could understand the Scriptures."** (Luke 24:45)

### The Significance of the Resurrection

We often marvel at the Resurrection as a miracle—a man returning to life. But people have been resuscitated throughout history. The true significance is not the miracle itself but **the fulfillment of prophecy**. The Resurrection proves that God has been faithful to his Word. It confirms that we can trust him completely. When he promises that his Son will reign as king of the universe, he means it. When he declares that death itself has been defeated, it is true.

This is why the apostles pointed not primarily to the marvel of the resurrection but to **the truthfulness of Scripture**. Abraham's words to the rich man capture the sufficiency of this testimony:

> "They have Moses and the Prophets; let them listen to them... If they do not listen to Moses and the Prophets, they will not be convinced even if someone rises from the dead." (Luke 16:29, 31)

We need not be personal eyewitnesses. The Old Testament itself is full of prophets who never saw Jesus in the flesh, yet believed in the coming Messiah. They looked forward to an unseen deliverer. We look back to remember an unseen risen Lord. Trust in Scripture is sufficient.

---

## 5. The Calling Unfolds (Luke 24:46-53)

Understanding, however, was not the endpoint but the beginning:

> "He told them, 'This is what is written: The Messiah will suffer and rise from the dead on the third day, and repentance for the forgiveness of sins will be preached in his name to all nations, beginning at Jerusalem. You are witnesses of these things.'" (Luke 24:46-48)

Jesus suffered for our sins. Forgiveness flows from the cross. But the story does not end there. Now comes the task: **repentance and forgiveness of sins proclaimed to all nations.**

We are called to warn of sin, to call people to repentance—for we constantly invent new ways to break God's law. Yet simultaneously, we proclaim hope: forgiveness is available through Jesus. The aim of life is not personal comfort or career fulfillment or the script the world has written for us. The aim is **God's purposes**.

> "I am going to send you what my Father has promised; but stay in the city until you have been clothed with power from on high." (Luke 24:49)

This power is not for holidays or health-wealth prosperity. It is power to serve—the strength to carry out the task he assigns. Jesus is not here to do the grunt work. That responsibility falls to us.

Finally, Jesus led them out to Bethany, blessed them, and was taken up into heaven. They worshiped him and returned to Jerusalem with great joy, staying continually in the temple praising God (24:50-53). The Acts account tells what happened next.

---

## Application: Study and Explain Scripture Together

It is remarkable that the risen Jesus' first action was to conduct a Bible study. He opened Scripture. He explained its meaning. He transformed confusion into comprehension.

If we truly understand that ordinary people—the ploughboy and the scholar alike—can know Jesus through diligent engagement with Scripture, then we must commit ourselves to this work.

**Open the Bible.** Take the initiative. If you have attended church but never seriously studied Scripture, begin now. Seek out mentors and study groups. The principle of the ploughboy does not mean solitary interpretation divorced from the body—it means that with effort, with community, with the Holy Spirit's illumination, understanding is accessible to all who seek it earnestly.

**Study together.** We need each other. The Bible teaches us not only about Jesus but about morality, decision-making, the future, history, science, and politics. As a community of faith, we learn together what it means to know Jesus and live rightly.

This is how the kingdom advances: through people who have met the risen Jesus in Scripture, whose hearts burn as they understand, and who go forth proclaiming repentance and forgiveness to all nations.
