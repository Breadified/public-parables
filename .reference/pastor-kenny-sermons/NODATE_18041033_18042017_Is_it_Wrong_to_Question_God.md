# Job 41:33-42:17

**Title:** Is it Wrong to Question God?  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## 1. The Recipient of Our Questions (41:33-34)

Job has been wrestling with profound questions about suffering. How can an innocent person suffer? In response, God doesn't answer Job's questions directly. Instead, God overwhelms him with a series of counter-questions about the Leviathan—a magnificent creature beyond human control and understanding.

> "Nothing on earth is his equal—a creature without fear. He looks down on all that are haughty; he is king over all that are proud." (Job 41:33-34)

Through these questions about the Leviathan, God is illustrating a fundamental truth: Who are you to question me? Can you control the forces of creation? Can you pull in the Leviathan with a fishhook? Can you make it your pet? This isn't God being evasive—it's God redirecting Job's attention to the vast gap between human understanding and divine wisdom.

God asks Job in Job 40:8: "Would you discredit my justice? Would you condemn me to justify yourself?" This is the crucial question. It's not wrong to ask God questions, but we must remember who we're questioning. We are asking questions of the God who sustains all creation.

**The posture matters.** We can question God, but only with humility, not arrogance. The problem arises when we approach God as though He owes us answers—when our questions come from a place of pride rather than genuine seeking.

James 1:5-8 captures this principle beautifully:

> "If any of you lacks wisdom, he should ask God, who gives generously to all without finding fault, and it will be given to him. But when he asks, he must believe and not doubt, because he who doubts is like a wave of the sea, blown and tossed by the wind. That man should not think he will receive anything from the Lord; he is a double-minded man, unstable in all he does."

The difference is attitude. Ask God with faith and humility. Don't ask with doubt masquerading as wisdom, with arrogance masquerading as justice.

## 2. The End of Questions (42:1-6)

After God's relentless questions, Job finally understands. His response is remarkable:

> "I know that you can do all things; no plan of yours can be thwarted. You asked, 'Who is this that obscures my counsel without knowledge?' Surely I spoke of things I did not understand, things too wonderful for me to know." (Job 42:2-3)

Notice what happens next:

> "My ears had heard of you but now my eyes have seen you. Therefore I despise myself and repent in dust and ashes." (Job 42:5-6)

This is crucial: Job wasn't repenting of sin. Job is righteous throughout this book. Rather, Job was repenting of his *questioning*, his *mourning*, his wrestling with God without understanding. He moves from knowledge *about* God to knowledge *of* God—from intellectual certainty to experiential encounter.

Jesus exemplified this principle in His darkest hour. On the cross, He cried out:

> "Eloi, Eloi, lama sabachthani?"—which means, "My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?" (Mark 15:34)

But notice what comes next: "With a loud cry, Jesus breathed his last" (Mark 15:37). The time for asking ended. The time for persevering—for faithfully completing what He came to do—had arrived.

**This is where many of us get stuck.** We spend years questioning God about our circumstances. Why am I in love with an unbeliever? Why are my parents like this? Why am I suffering? These are natural questions, but there comes a time when questioning must transform into action.

When you realize God is God and you are not, the questions end and obedience begins. The time to persevere arrives. Don't spend your whole life bitter and unproductive, nursing questions that paralyze you. Instead, do the right thing. Break free from that unhealthy relationship. Honor your parents. Endure your suffering with faith. Move forward.

## 3. The Acceptance of Prayer (42:7-9)

What happens after Job stops questioning? God turns to Job's friends, who had offered hollow answers and theological platitudes. God rebukes them:

> "I am angry with you and your two friends, because you have not spoken of me what is right, as my servant Job has. So now take seven bulls and seven rams and go to my servant Job and sacrifice a burnt offering for yourselves. My servant Job will pray for you, and I will accept his prayer and not deal with you according to your folly." (Job 42:7-8)

Here's the beautiful irony: Job, who has suffered so much, becomes the intercessor for those who judged him. Job's prayers are accepted by God and become powerful—not because Job understands everything, but because he has stopped questioning and started trusting.

This is the invitation to us. Rather than being paralyzed by anxiety and unanswered questions, we are called to pray:

> "Do not be anxious about anything, but in everything, by prayer and petition, with thanksgiving, present your requests to God. And the peace of God, which transcends all understanding, will guard your hearts and your minds in Christ Jesus." (Philippians 4:6-7)

We are genuinely terrible at faithfulness. We cancel commitments at the last minute. We make lame excuses. Our promises are worth very little. Yet God invites us to bring everything before Him in prayer, to present our requests with thanksgiving, and to find that the peace of God—which makes no logical sense given our circumstances—will guard our hearts and minds.

## 4. The Reward of Perseverance (42:10-17)

When Job stopped questioning and started praying, something remarkable happened:

> "After Job had prayed for his friends, the LORD made him prosperous again and gave him twice as much as he had before." (Job 42:10)

Job lived another 140 years, saw his children to the fourth generation, and experienced restoration beyond what he lost. The crown waiting at the end of perseverance is real.

**The final lesson:** Stop questioning. Start persevering. Bring your requests to God in prayer, not in complaint. Trust that He is God and you are not. And discover that His peace, His provision, and His faithfulness sustain you through every season.
