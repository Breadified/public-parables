## Introduction

This message applies the *Riches Found* series to our community today. While the series concluded with "the moment of salvation," this teaching focuses on the practical implications for how we live with our material resources—specifically overseas travel spending.

This word may sting initially, but remember the truths from the previous five weeks. That context will help this teaching make sense and prevent us from becoming defensive.

## Important Caveats

Before we proceed, I need to clarify several things:

- **Not personal, but corporate:** This isn't about guilt-tripping those who've already taken trips. I've traveled, you've traveled, and those plans already made are in the past. This is about future choices.

- **You are godly:** You serve faithfully each week—not as consumers but as producers. You prepare meals, sacrifice parking spaces, give financially to support the church and my family. You are the elite serving team of our church, and I'm grateful.

- **You prioritize church:** You return from holidays for Sunday worship—a pattern unknown in other churches. Your pastors envy us. This is genuinely excellent.

- **You are great warriors:** Like Greek heroes, you excel in many areas. But I'm saying we have an Achilles' heel that will prevent us from reaching the next level of spiritual maturity.

- **Not the only struggle:** There are other temptations to address, but we've just completed a series on money. Our wallet needs conversion.

- **Exceptions exist:** Work trips, obligatory family obligations when you have children, expiring airline credits, and true bucket-list exceptions are real. But these are the minority, not the pattern.

- **God is sovereign, but works matter:** We're not being fatalistic. Faith without works is dead—we're exploring the works dimension today.

- **General application:** If this hits close to home, that's likely God's work of refinement, not personal attack.

- **Primarily for workers:** This applies especially to those with disposable income, though it concerns us all.

- **Not anti-vacation:** I'm not saying you shouldn't have breaks, only what kind of breaks.

- **Subjective, not absolute:** This is a complex area without clear black-and-white answers. I'm reasoning from principle and instinct to help us think more clearly together.

**Please don't be defensive.** If my tone feels harsh, translate it to another voice and hear the message. I'm trying to help, not attack—to inoculate us before this pattern becomes entrenched.

## The Problem: Pagan Creep

### A Generational Shift

Our grandparents' generation traveled overseas only in retirement. Our parents' generation visited Hong Kong for family and perhaps added a quick Disneyland detour. But our generation has transformed travel into an annual—or more frequent—habit.

We go on a whim, stay longer, seek exotic destinations. We justify trips: after HSC as a reward, after graduation before starting work, during our careers when we have disposable income, for honeymoons, and again before children arrive. Church attendance ebbs and flows with school holidays. When people should be busiest during term, they're actually away traveling.

Our church sometimes feels like an airport departure lounge. I struggle to track who's traveling where and when.

**Where did this become normal?** How did we adopt the habit of jetsetting like the rich and famous?

### Why We've Become Habitual Travelers

We've adopted this lifestyle because **we are wealthy**—relatively speaking. Jetstar flights are cheap. The Australian dollar exchanges favorably. We look at bank accounts with thousands or tens of thousands available, and we think, "I can replace this in six months. No problem."

### The Real Questions

But this raises deeper questions: **Is this a good way to spend money? Why are we spending this money?**

#### We claim it's for "rest"

Where did we get the idea that one month yearly is "me time"? This comes from employment law and the labor movement, not from Scripture. Yet an overseas trip isn't restful. International travel multiplies complexity: booking, logistics, exchange rates, time zones, jet lag. You feel obligated to do everything because you're only there "once." You return home exhausted and sick, then spend two weeks catching up.

Why not find better rest? God has given us sleep, a one-in-seven pattern, the Sabbath. We have holy-days. I'm not sure He's given us holidays.

#### We claim it's for "experience"

Where did we adopt the belief that we must experience every culture and country in the world? We live in one of the world's most diverse cities. Why travel to Delhi to meet Indians when they're in our schools? Why fly to Vietnam when Cabramatta is forty minutes away?

### What Are We Really After?

Let's be honest: **This is hedonism**—the worship of pleasure. We taste worldly delights, become "cultured," accumulate stories to tell friends, enjoy airline service, watch movies at altitude, relax in airport lounges, feel special.

> Instead of loving God, they will love pleasure... They will be traitors. They will be reckless and conceited. They will love pleasure rather than God. (2 Timothy 3:4)

**What are we actually seeking?** Escapism. We're tired of our ordinary lives. For a week, fortnight, or month, we become different people in different countries—eating freely, sleeping when we want, answering to no one. No church. No responsibilities. No people to follow up with. No boss. Freedom.

The word "vacation" comes from the Latin *vacare*—to vacate, to escape.

But this isn't a biblical pattern. The Christian life is one of service and mutual obligation. **This is the pagan's pattern—escaping when life gets difficult.** And unknowingly, this pagan lifestyle creeps into us. Everyone does it. It's normal.

## The Consequences

### An Insatiable Appetite

Once people live for the world's pleasures, it never ends. There are roughly 250 countries to visit, infinite experiences to chase. What starts as a lifetime trip becomes annual, then four times yearly. Every conversation centers on the last vacation or the next one planned. Money is the only limiting factor.

Some families at our church appear once monthly. At term's start, they tell me about previous holidays. By term's end, they're already planning the next trip. School holidays interrupt their school term recovery.

### Dropout and Disconnection

These patterns lead to progressive disconnection:

- They drop out of serving
- They drop out of church attendance
- They drop out of family presence

I knew a father who took annual ski trips, leaving his family to describe that week as "the best time of my year." What message does that send? That his deepest desire is to escape wife and children. That selfishness feels amazing.

**It's always amazing to be away from obligation.** It's always exhilarating to be selfish. The majority of the world live this way not because it's boring and painful, but because it's exhilarating and fun. Short-term thinking. Self-centeredness.

> For Demas, having loved the pleasures of this present world, has deserted me and gone to Thessalonica. (2 Timothy 4:10)

This pattern cannot be us. **This trajectory sets up our future selves to fail in faithfulness.**

## Financial Reality: You're Not Rich Enough

### The Opportunity Cost

There are numerous opportunity costs: Sunday school ministry, youth groups, your own children, church commitments, SRE programs. We have obligations not easily untangled.

### The Direct Financial Cost

Overseas travel consumes enormous sums: flights, hotels, passports, visas, taxis, food, entertainment, attraction tickets. Meanwhile, rent continues at home, car registration sits unused, your house waits unguarded.

We should be modest in all spending. We don't buy fifty-dollar steaks daily. But the problem with overseas travel is different: **you're not wasting tens of dollars but thousands in one hit—tens of thousands.** What's the logic of saving a dollar on eBay then losing a thousand instantly forever? We worry about a ten-thousand-dollar share investment that might lose money—"I worked hard for this!"—yet happily waste ten thousand on a trip with no return.

At minimum, an investment could provide returns. **It's probably not small daily expenses destroying our budgets—it's our overseas holidays.**

### The Wealth Paradox

You look at your account: ten thousand, twenty thousand, fifty thousand, a hundred thousand dollars. You think, "If I blow five thousand, it's no big deal. Ten thousand for a trip? I'll earn it back."

**But you don't understand:** You are not rich enough to go on overseas holidays.

- Some of you work part-time jobs, skip church and commitments, just to afford one trip. You're not rich enough.
  
- Even those earning six figures professionally are too poor to go on these trips.

**The Christian life is more expensive than you think.**

Yes, basic Christian life costs money: church giving, buying lunch for friends, hospitality. Pagans don't have these expenses. With a full-time job, you can manage this and eventually have disposable income.

But there's a **next level**—institutional, multi-generational responsibility:

- We can afford to give to church. Thank you. But we can't buy a ten-million-dollar building.
- We can afford to feed homeless people. But we can't build a shelter.
- We're rich enough to debate online but not to publish newspapers.
- We're rich enough to be comfortable and help a little, but not to make lasting systemic change.

**This should not be us.** In this room sits the elite of our state and nation. How many attended top schools? Top universities? Obtained degrees, masters, PhDs? Were in top Christian groups? Chose universities for networking?

> From everyone who has been given much, much will be demanded. (Luke 12:48)

**With much is expected.** Is church attendance and tithing our maximum? Is that elite-level commitment? That's the minimum, not the maximum!

It's like an elite athlete: trains at the Australian Institute of Sport, consumes premium nutrition, hires trainers, receives acupuncture and hyperbaric treatment—then only jogs around the block. No medals. No nationals. No Olympics. Disappointing.

**Where is the return on God's investment in us?** We complain there are no good people in politics—one of you should be there. You're that person. It takes a million dollars for campaign ads and political capital. When same-sex marriage arrived, the entire Anglican church gave one million dollars collectively (300 churches, four thousand each). Alan Joyce, one person, gave a million dollars—wealth he accumulated because many loved overseas trips. **Where is our Alan Joyce?**

Building institutions requires enormous capital. A school, a church, a business that provides Christian employment. Where is that wealth coming from?

> He who loves pleasure will become poor. (Proverbs 21:17)

### Not Rich Enough to Help Yourself

The problem isn't just insufficient wealth for systemic change. **You're not even rich enough to protect yourselves:**

- **Religious freedom costs money.** As Australia turns against Christians, do you have reserves to weather persecution? Can you afford to lose a job for righteous conviction? Can you stand for truth to a woke employer when a mortgage hangs over your head?

- **Career independence costs money.** One person this year discovered: a simple franchise costs $130,000. Can you afford unemployment?

- **Job creation costs money.** When brothers from overseas needed work, we couldn't help them. Building a business costs capital, and businesses often fail—you need enough for two attempts. That's $260,000.

- **Housing in our community costs money.** A deposit and monthly repayments. (There's nothing wrong with living in a country town affordably—sometimes that's the wiser choice. Better one income in Bathurst than two incomes in Sydney.)

- **Educating children costs money.** Christian school runs ten to fifteen thousand yearly. High school costs sixty to one hundred thousand. Three children: one hundred eighty to three hundred thousand—just for high school. Double that if primary school becomes unsafe.

- **Single-income families cost money.** If you want your wife at home raising children, you're replacing fifty to one hundred thousand yearly in household income.

**You're already half a million behind before you start.** Even with both working at current expense trajectories, your salaries won't suffice. You need money to make money—investments, passive income. We need to pool resources like the Jewish community did.

**When do you want to start saving and investing?** The best time was yesterday. The second best is today.

You know you're too poor because when I ask about children or the next baby, the answer is always: "I need to be financially stable first. I need two incomes. I don't have enough money." **You admit: I'm too poor.** Yet you're rich enough to travel overseas like the wealthy? Which is it?

### The Irony

**We are rich enough to be selfish and indulgent.** We are too poor to be righteous and help others.

This is the common rich Christian: wealthy enough to pay bills, live in large homes, drive SUVs, own pets, buy large televisions, travel yearly, and give one percent to church. The societal elite that looks out for themselves and their own.

Wouldn't it be better if we were different? Rich enough to help our wider communities? Rich enough to protect our families? To do this, **we must cut our single biggest discretionary expense: overseas holidays.**

## The Deeper Principle: Assets vs. Liabilities

Don't settle for middle-class mediocrity. The financial truth is simple:

- The poor have only expenses
- The rich buy assets  
- The middle class buys liabilities

The poor can't help it. The rich help themselves. But the middle class can help themselves yet chooses not to. That's laziness. Society taxes them most heavily—they might as well lose that money to redistribution since they squander it anyway.

What a privilege to have disposable income—money left after obligations. You can use that for pleasure **or for God.**

If you use it wisely, together, with creativity and compound interest, you can take the next step: institutional work. Your ambitions should encompass more than personal comfort or tutoring your children. Christian ambition should equip many children with businesses and wealth to fight for the future.

## The Challenge

### A Ten-Year Commitment

For the next ten years—realistically—**don't go overseas.** (Exceptions exist: a grandmother's funeral, genuinely unavoidable family obligations.)

**Let your passport expire.** Mine did. Let that passport die like your desire for worldly pleasures.

Work hard for the present. We're rich enough to give to church and sustain our community—be proud of that. We're growing. What an amazing four months.

But also work for the future: side hustles, marriage, children (as many as possible), generational wealth. We'll form discussion groups on investing to share what works and doesn't. We need to do this together.

### The Ten-Year Horizon

Why ten years?

- Properties typically double in value over ten years
- It takes ten years to make partner in a law firm
- Malcolm Gladwell notes it takes ten years to master something
- Most families will have three to four children in that span
- It takes ten years of resilience and perseverance

After that period, if overseas travel still matters, take those holidays—**paid by your investments, not your labor.** That's generally a good rule.

Renew your passport when you become a company director. You'll need it for company ID verification.

### This Is Not Retirement Time

We're like servants in a parable. The master hasn't finished eating. Now is not the time to rest. **You're not retired.** Finish this first, then we can rest a bit.

Ironically, Christians invented instant gratification, yet the world seems better at delayed gratification. **They understand patience better than we do.**

### Our Unique Historical Moment

We're at a unique juncture in history. The world is shrinking. Baby boomers will be gone in twenty years. The enemies of the gospel are aging out.

Just as there's a teacher shortage, there will be shortages everywhere: media, politics, school principals, board members, P&C committees, CEOs, policymakers, business owners.

**Who will take over?** We need people—**babies.** We need capital—**money.**

You're positioned uniquely to establish the foundation for the next generation to take dominion.

**With God's help, we can save many.** This is the moment of salvation.
