# Colossians 3:18-4:1
**Title:** Christ and the Family
**Date:** 22 May 2022 (ARISE)
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Opening Reflection

Before diving into this passage, we need to acknowledge the reality of our times. This week brought news of another tragedy in America—nineteen children and two teachers killed in Texas. The emotional response is visceral and immediate. We feel the injustice deeply, the frustration at our inability to prevent such horrors.

When something appears clearly wrong, we're tempted by simplistic solutions: ban all guns, identify and isolate troubled individuals, remove money from politics. But as we look deeper, we discover the issues are far more complex than they first appear. This passage will give us that same visceral reaction, and we'll be tempted toward similarly simplistic answers—denying Scripture's authority or reinterpreting it to fit our preferences. Yet patience and context reveal deeper truths at stake.

> Colossians 3:17 And whatever you do, whether in word or deed, do it all in the name of the Lord Jesus, giving thanks to God the Father through him.

## 1. Husbands and Wives (3:17-19)

### The Challenge of Submission

> 18 Wives, submit yourselves to your husbands, as is fitting in the Lord.
>
> 19 Husbands, love your wives and do not be harsh with them.

For many, this is offensive. In marriage counseling sessions, I've heard it countless times: "This is so outdated." The word **submit** literally means "to place oneself under authority"—to be a subject. This language appears not only in Colossians but throughout Scripture: Titus, 1 Peter, and Ephesians all affirm this pattern.

Some argue: "This is only for Colossian women," or "It's cultural," or "Women are inferior." But submission doesn't imply inferiority. We see this principle taught consistently across multiple epistles by different authors—this is not a culturally isolated instruction.

### Understanding Submission

There's simply an order to things. This doesn't mean women are less valuable. At work, we happily submit to our bosses without feeling diminished—in fact, we compete to be the best employee, to show we can submit effectively. Yet we resist submitting to the person we love, with whom we share our life as one flesh. That's curious.

Consider **Jesus's submission** in Philippians:

> 2:5-8 Have the same mindset as Christ Jesus: Who, being in very nature God, did not consider equality with God something to be used to his own advantage; rather, he made himself nothing by taking the very nature of a servant, being made in human likeness. And being found in appearance as a man, he humbled himself by becoming obedient to death—even death on a cross!

Jesus submitted. Is he inferior? Is he less because he gives himself to another? Some object, "Yes, but Jesus submitted to the holy and perfect Father." But consider the reciprocal: God the Father didn't have to love an unsubmissive Jesus. 

### The Balance of Roles

These commands are not conditional—they're not "You submit if he loves, and I'll love if you submit." They're orders from God the Father to his children, establishing the structure of things.

**For wives:** You are to submit and help the team function.

**For husbands:** You are in charge. You bear the responsibility. The weight falls on you. Notice: Eve sinned first, but Adam receives the blame for humanity's fall. This is equality—not identical roles, but equal dignity with differentiated responsibilities.

**One role carries power and responsibility.** The other provides essential support and helps the other lead. A husband's calling is to die for his wife. A wife's role is to let him do that—to honor him, praise him, and submit to him in ways that enable his leadership.

### The Crisis of Modern Marriage

Today's culture pits men and women against each other as competitors in a zero-sum game. If the husband is winning, the wife is losing. If the wife is winning, the husband is losing. This destroys love. It destroys children. It destroys families.

### Practical Application: Recovering Masculine Leadership

In our Christianized Western culture, we have the saying "women and children first"—think of the Titanic, where men sacrificed their lives. Yet consider who actually dies protecting our societies:

- Ukrainian soldiers giving their lives for family and nation—these are men
- Deep-sea fishermen risking death in the ocean's most dangerous waters to provide our food—these are men
- Miners ensuring the electricity that powers our world—these are men
- Most dangerous occupations in the world—predominantly men

Where is the equality? Yet men are called to embrace this inequality, to die for others.

This training for masculine sacrifice should start young. While the command to submit applies specifically between husbands and wives, perhaps we should see something deeper in our churches: young men carrying bags, opening car doors for young women, paying for dates, walking girls home at night. And young women learning to honor and praise these men, to submit to them, to help them lead.

This is true equality—different expressions of equal worth.

## 2. Children and Fathers (3:20-21)

### Children's Obedience

> 20 Children, obey your parents in everything, for this pleases the Lord.

**"Children"** (Greek: *tekna*) refers to those still under parental responsibility—those living at home and dependent on their parents' care. Once you've formed your own household and moved out, you're no longer technically a child in this sense. But while you're home, obey your parents.

**"Obey"** means to submit to their voice and direction. **"Parents"** are your begetting ones. **"In everything"** is comprehensive. **"Pleases the Lord"** reminds children that this obedience honors God himself.

### A Practical Challenge

Here's a challenge for young people: keep communicating with your parents about your commitments. Often, the problem isn't the activity itself—it's the lack of notice. Your parents, who are responsible for you, would naturally like to know what's happening in your life.

- If you have a friend's party next Saturday, tell them today—not next Friday. Surprise announcements usually get "no."
- If you have a church commitment, inform them immediately so they aren't shocked. In most cases, they'll let you go.

This simple act of communication shows respect and honors your parents' role.

### Fathers' Responsibility

> 21 Fathers, do not embitter your children, or they will become discouraged.

Fathers are in charge of the household. They're the final arbiters and disciplinarians. Mothers tend toward nurturing—teaching unconditional love. Fathers, traditionally, maintain the rougher edge—teaching strength and boundaries. But the danger is going too far: discipline that becomes harshness, that embitters rather than shapes.

The Old Testament speaks repeatedly about discipline:

> Proverbs 13:24 Whoever spares the rod hates their children, but the one who loves their children is careful to discipline them.

> Proverbs 23:13-14 Do not withhold discipline from a child; if you punish them with the rod, they will not die. Punish them with the rod and save them from death.

> Hebrews 12:7-9 For what children are not disciplined by their father? If you are not disciplined—and everyone undergoes discipline—then you are not legitimate, not true sons and daughters at all. Moreover, we have all had human fathers who disciplined us and we respected them for it.

**To be trained is to be disciplined.** Without this, children die spiritually—they lack the self-control and character necessary for a good life.

Fathers are not meant to delegate all discipline to mothers. As John Piper noted, you can't leave it all to mom or she becomes the bad guy. But when mom and dad work together, with dad providing the final say and consistent discipline, children develop the character they need.

Research confirms this. Psychologist Dr. Peter Langman, an expert on school shooters, found that among the twenty-five most-cited school shooters since Columbine, 75 percent came from broken homes. Their backgrounds included not just divorce, but infidelity, substance abuse, criminal behavior, domestic violence, and child abuse.

The rapper Tupac Shakur, who was murdered in 1996, said: "I know for a fact that had I had a father, I'd have some discipline. I'd have more confidence. Your mother can't calm you down the way a man can. You need a man to teach you how to be a man." Tupac joined gangs seeking the family structure he lacked at home.

Today, the pendulum has swung the opposite direction—fathers are no longer allowed to discipline. The moment a father corrects his child, there are threats of reporting to authorities. Many fathers have simply withdrawn.

But someone will discipline the child. If fathers don't, the police will—in the future.

## 3. Servants and Masters (3:22-4:1)

### Understanding Biblical Slavery

> 22 Slaves, obey your earthly masters in everything;

This word—**slaves**—triggers modern emotional responses. But we must understand: biblical slavery is not what we mean by slavery today.

**Modern slavery** equals **slave trading**—the kidnapping of people from Africa in the 17th century, or the capture of Slavs by Muslims in the 9th century (from which the word "slave" may derive), or Vikings capturing and selling the Irish. This slave trading is directly condemned by Scripture:

> 1 Timothy 1:9-10 The law is made not for the righteous but for lawbreakers and rebels, the ungodly and sinful, the unholy and irreligious, for those who kill their fathers or mothers, for murderers, for the sexually immoral, for those practicing homosexuality, for **slave traders** and liars and perjurers—and for whatever else is contrary to the sound doctrine.

Slave traders are listed alongside murderers and those who kill their parents. The Bible is unequivocally against kidnapping and trading human beings.

### Indentured Servitude

What the Bible discusses is **indentured servitude**. If you fell into bad debt or stole something you couldn't repay, servitude was one way forward. Prison wouldn't help anyone. Instead, you could work off your debt:

> Exodus 21:2 If you buy a Hebrew servant, he is to serve you for six years. But in the seventh year, he shall go free, without paying anything.

There were extensive protections. What happened if a servant married while in service? What if children were born? These matters had detailed regulations—not because they were ignored, but because servants were part of the household and their welfare mattered.

The Bible's effort was to **humanize economic systems** that were harsh by modern standards. The servant had legal rights to physical protection (Exodus 21:20-21, 26-27), freedom of movement, and access to liberty (Deuteronomy 23:15-16). If purchased from non-Hebrews, a servant could flee their master and be protected by law.

### Modern Parallels

Today, in prosperous countries, indentured servitude still exists in different forms. A scholarship to university might come with an obligation to work for a particular company for a set number of years. A teaching fellowship might require service in a regional school. A cousin of mine received a government scholarship for medical studies in Australia—but with it came a ten-year obligation for the government to determine where she worked. She paid her time and earned her freedom.

**This is an economic system**, not a moral evil. Our modern labor laws—with restrictions on hours, penalty rates, and restraint of trade regulations—reflect similar principles.

The point is: not everyone is born into the same circumstances. Servitude can be a pathway to progress. **When we eliminate these arrangements, we remove the bottom rungs of the ladder.**

Yet the Bible shows concern for protecting these servants:

### Servants' Responsibilities

> 22... and do it, not only when their eye is on you and to curry their favor, but with sincerity of heart and reverence for the Lord.
>
> 23 Whatever you do, work at it with all your heart, as working for the Lord, not for human masters, 24 since you know that you will receive an inheritance from the Lord as a reward. It is the Lord Christ you are serving. 25 Anyone who does wrong will be repaid for their wrongs, and there is no favoritism.

Work whether supervised or unsupervised. In today's work-from-home culture, this applies powerfully. The real motivation isn't your employer's approval but **reverence for the Lord**.

Whatever work you do, do it as if working for Christ. You will receive an inheritance from him as your reward. You're ultimately serving Christ, not just your earthly employer. No favoritism exists before God—he sees all, judges all fairly.

### Masters' Responsibilities

> 4:1 Masters, provide your slaves with what is right and fair, because you know that you also have a Master in heaven.

Masters—you have responsibilities. Treat your servants as you would want to be treated by your Master in heaven. These people are part of your household.

The problem of globalization is that it depersonalizes labor. Workers become mere numbers in a factory in China. But biblical economics assumes personal relationship—masters know their servants, and servants are part of the family.

Some silver linings in current economic pressure: **Supply shortages force us to use local manufacturers**—creating more familiar relationships. **Rising wages** result from **immigration restrictions**, which forces masters to value their servants. When workers are scarce, they must be treated well.

## The Bigger Picture: Family as Society's Foundation

Here is the household of God. The fundamental unit of society is not the individual. It's not the state. **It's the family.**

In Colossians, we see the supremacy of Christ. He is before all things and above all things. He is Lord of the universe. If that's true—and it is—then Christ must be Lord over the church, over society, and over the fundamental unit of society: **the family**.

Yet look at what we've done. We say we honor Christ, but we neglect the family:

- Fathers abuse their families
- Mothers abandon their children  
- Consequently, children rebel

### The Root of Violence

When tragedy strikes—school shootings, mass violence—we look for simple explanations. Guns, we say. But Switzerland and Nordic countries have similar rates of gun ownership, yet they don't have mass shooting epidemics. A hundred years ago, America had widespread gun ownership and no mass murders. Perhaps guns aren't the main issue.

There's a better correlation: **broken families**. Salvador Ramos, the Texas shooter, came from a broken home. His mother was a drug addict. His father had a criminal record. Looking back through his family line—broken families.

Research by Dr. Peter Langman confirms this pattern across school shooters. Among the most-cited cases, 75 percent came from severely fractured homes marked by infidelity, substance abuse, criminal behavior, domestic violence, and child abuse. The absence of fathers is **one of the most powerful predictors of crime**.

America has the highest rates of fatherlessness.

### The Crisis in Our Response

And then there are the police—predominantly men in one of the most dangerous jobs. They're confused. One moment, society condemns them for shooting a criminal. The next, society condemns them for not shooting a criminal. Are they supposed to take down a mentally ill teenager or not? They don't know. Their instincts, honed from young manhood, tell them to be the ones who die for others without thinking. But now they second-guess everything. And that hesitation costs lives.

Our leaders offer band-aid solutions while the wound is infected.

### A Call to Action

It's time to hunker down. The church is not the fundamental unit of society on earth—that awaits heaven. On earth, the church is a collection of Christian families, and it serves as a family for orphans, especially those whose biological families aren't Christian. But **long-term change depends on Christian families.**

**To our men:** You are leaders of your families. Lead in prayer. Lead in Bible study—small and big ways. Lead by working hard even with a bad boss. Cut off social media habits that don't serve you or your family.

**To our women:** Be free to help your husband and cover his many inadequacies. Make him look good. You need to be home. No divorce.

**To our children:** Obey your parents and don't make their job harder. Don't surprise them with commitments. Communicate. Honor them.

**To our fathers:** You're modeling strength and mercy. Find that balance. Discipline with love, not harshness.

**To those who can be masters:** Be good ones. Voting once every few years isn't enough. In the meantime, be good servants.

These are not the days when both parents can work and children turn out fine because schools are good. These are not the days when fathers can work overseas or interstate and visit once a month. There's too much pressure outside—too much sexual immorality, too much greed, too many enemies seeking to divide you from your children.

**The fundamental unit of society is breaking down.** It's time to stabilize it.
