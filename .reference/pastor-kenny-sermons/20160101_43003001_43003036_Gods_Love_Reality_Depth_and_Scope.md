# John 3:1-36

**Title:** God's Love: Reality, Depth, and Scope

**Date:** 17 January 2016

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Introduction

> For God so loved the world that he gave his one and only Son, that whoever believes in him shall not perish but have eternal life. (John 3:16)

According to Bible Gateway, John 3:16 is the most popular searched verse in Scripture. While it's wonderful that this verse resonates with so many, it's best understood not as an ending point but as a starting place for deeper exploration of God's character and His plan of salvation.

This verse captures the essence of Jesus' message in a single, pithy statement—teaching us about sacrificial love and revealing the heart of God toward His creation. Let's examine three dimensions of this truth: the reality of God's love, the depth of God's love revealed through Jesus, and the universal scope of that love.

---

## 1. The Reality of God's Love (God's Nature)

> For God so loved the world that he gave his one and only Son...

### God as the Giver

The most striking phrase is simply this: "He loved" and "He gave." These verbs capture something fundamental about God's character—He is not passive but actively loves and generously gives.

This pattern runs throughout Scripture. In Genesis 2, God gave Adam a garden to tend and gave him Eve as a helper, establishing the foundation for human flourishing. When Adam and Eve rebelled in Genesis 3, they faced judgment, yet God responded with mercy—giving them clothing and protection from death, even in their disobedience.

Later, when the Israelites wandered in the desert and complained bitterly against God, forgetting His deliverance from Egypt, they faced consequences for their ingratitude:

> They traveled from Mount Hor along the route to the Red Sea, to go around Edom. But the people grew impatient on the way; they spoke against God and against Moses, and said, "Why have you brought us up out of Egypt to die in the desert? There is no bread! There is no water! And we detest this miserable food!" (Numbers 21:4-5)

We would expect retributive justice—crime deserves punishment. Indeed, God sent judgment through venomous snakes. But here's what makes God fundamentally different from other gods: He doesn't stop at judgment. He takes initiative to save.

> The LORD said to Moses, "Make a snake and put it up on a pole; anyone who is bitten can look at it and live." (Numbers 21:8-9)

This bronze serpent became a sign of healing. Even today, the medical profession uses a snake-on-pole symbol, though few realize its biblical origins predate Greek mythology by centuries. The serpent that bit became the symbol of healing—a profound picture of God's mercy overcoming judgment.

### God's Love is Unique Among Religions

Consider how other religious systems view the divine:

- **Impersonal forces** (like Star Wars' "The Force") lack any capacity for love.
- **Islamic theology** presents Allah as powerful and personal, yet conditional in love—bestowing favor only on the pure and obedient, never initiating self-giving sacrifice.
- **Pagan gods** (Mesopotamian, Roman, Greek) are fundamentally takers. They demand blood sacrifices from humans in exchange for blessings.

By contrast, our God breaks the pattern entirely. He doesn't demand; He gives. He doesn't wait for us to earn His favor; He loves first. He initiates reconciliation.

### The Reversal of How the World Works

This is completely upside-down from human experience. In normal relationships, when someone wrongs us, we expect the wrongdoer to initiate reconciliation. If I hurt someone, I must buy flowers, take them to dinner, make amends. The transgressor bears the responsibility to restore the relationship.

But when we sin against God, the roles reverse in an astonishing way. We are powerless and ignorant of how to approach Him. Yet God—the one wronged—takes the initiative. Rather than waiting for us to earn His favor, God extends love and mercy first. It's as if the one who was hurt buys flowers for the one who hurt them. How radical. How contrary to human justice. How divine.

### Love Defines God's Being

> Whoever does not love does not know God, because God is love. (1 John 4:8)

Love is not something God does; it's who He is. He cannot help but love. Love defines His nature from eternity.

> This is love: not that we loved God, but that he loved us and sent his Son as an atoning sacrifice for our sins. (1 John 4:10)

Because God loved us first, we are enabled to understand love itself. And this creates a community of believers marked by a love that astounds the world:

> Dear friends, since God so loved us, we also ought to love one another. (1 John 4:11)

### Christian Love: A Distinctive Witness

While many communities naturally love their own—families, tribes, ethnic groups, sports teams—Christian love reaches beyond these natural boundaries. We are called to love strangers, those from whom we gain nothing, even our enemies.

> Love is patient, love is kind. It does not envy, it does not boast, it is not proud. It does not dishonor others, it is not self-seeking, it is not easily angered, it keeps no record of wrongs. (1 Corinthians 13:4-5)

We do not always achieve this perfectly. Individual Christians fail regularly, and some non-believers exhibit extraordinary love. Yet over centuries, the Christian community has consistently demonstrated sacrificial, unconditional love in ways that shaped civilization.

### Historical Witness to Christian Love

In ancient Rome, when Christians were persecuted, they nursed the sick abandoned on roadsides and raised baby girls left to die in the sun—caring for those who would become their persecutors.

When plague struck Carthage in the second century, pagan households threw sufferers onto the streets. The entire Christian community, personally led by their bishop, responded by caring for the sick in their own homes at great personal risk.

Even Emperor Julian, attempting to restore paganism in AD 355, acknowledged in his writings that the old religion would need to care for people far better than Christians cared to have any hope of success. He recognized Christian compassion as unmatched.

The charities that have most profoundly shaped our world—Vinnies, the Salvation Army, the Red Cross, St John Ambulance, World Vision, Alcoholics Anonymous, and even animal welfare organizations—were founded by Christians on the vision of loving one's neighbor sacrificially. The Catholic Church remains the world's largest charitable organization and the largest non-government provider of healthcare globally.

Early migrants and students found refuge in churches not because governments had established welfare systems, but because Christians understood they were called to love those with nothing to offer in return.

Christians should be proud of this heritage. There is no reason for shame on Australia Day or any day—this is the legacy of God's love lived out through His people.

## 2. The Depth of God's Love (Jesus' Sacrifice)

> For God so loved the world that he gave his one and only Son, that whoever believes in him shall not perish but have eternal life. (John 3:16)

### The Cost of Love

How much did God love us? He gave His one and only Son. This raises a difficult question that skeptics rightfully press: How is that fair? How is that loving?

The objection runs like this: If I sin and my wife (in a marriage context) should be the one I reconcile with, how is it just for a third party—say, my daughter—to bear the cost? She did nothing wrong. How is it fair to make an innocent third party pay for my transgression?

### Understanding the Incarnation and the Trinity

But Jesus is no third party. This is where the doctrine of the Trinity becomes essential for understanding the gospel. The "one and only Son" is literally unique—He is divinity itself incarnate. The transcendent God, beyond all material creation, became material—flesh and bones. God the Father, God the Son, and God the Holy Spirit are one God in three persons.

When God gave His Son, He gave Himself. Jesus is not a scapegoat standing outside the situation but God personally entering into human suffering and bearing the cost of our redemption. God paid the price in Himself, through Himself, at tremendous personal cost.

Any parent would sooner sacrifice themselves than their child. The fact that God gave up the Son He loved before the beginning of time—the One who shared His glory and nature—reveals the depth of His commitment to our salvation. This is not child abuse; it is the ultimate expression of sacrificial love.

### The Limitation and Completion of the Sacrifice

The bronze serpent in the wilderness was limited in its effectiveness. While it saved many Israelites, it ultimately failed as a permanent solution. Centuries later, King Hezekiah found the Israelites had begun to worship the bronze serpent itself as an idol, burning incense to it.

> He broke into pieces the bronze serpent that Moses had made, for up to that time the Israelites had been burning incense to it. (2 Kings 18:4)

Ironically, what was meant to point people to God—to look and live—had become an end in itself. People were trusting in the object rather than in the God to whom it pointed.

### From "Do" to "Done"

This reveals a fundamental misunderstanding that plagued Israel and plagues us still: the belief that salvation requires our works. Other gods demand perpetual sacrifices—"Do this," "Do that"—endless striving to appease divine anger or earn favor.

The Christian God declares something radically different: "Done."

Jesus said on the cross, "It is finished" (John 19:30). The sacrifice is complete. The price is paid. We cannot and need not add to it.

### Our Response: Belief, Not Works

Many of us carry an unconscious expectation that we must earn grace through discipline, obedience, or delayed gratification. We assume that salvation, like everything else, must be earned.

But the gospel calls us to something simpler and harder: belief. To look. To respond positively. To accept the gift.

Just as the Israelites looked at the bronze serpent and lived, so we must look to Christ and believe. We are not called to purchase salvation through our efforts but to receive it through faith.

This is why Jesus is presented in John 3:14 as the fulfillment of the brazen serpent symbol:

> Just as Moses lifted up the snake in the wilderness, so the Son of Man must be lifted up.

The one who looks in faith—who accepts the gift—shall have eternal life.

## 3. The Scope of God's Love (Universal Redemption)

> For God so loved the world that he gave his one and only Son, that whoever believes in him shall not perish but have eternal life. (John 3:16)

### The Word "World"

Notice that God loved "the world," not merely one tribe, nation, or ethnic group. And the promise extends to "whoever believes"—no exceptions, no exclusions based on background, nationality, or past failure.

This universality stands in sharp contrast to the bronze serpent, which could only be effective within the Israelite camp. But belief in Christ knows no geographical or cultural boundaries. The gospel moves from the particular (God's covenant with Israel) to the universal (God's redemption for all who believe).

### Who is "Whoever"?

The word "whoever" encompasses:

- Those who are guilty, burdened by shame and regret
- Those who have failed to love as they should
- Those who desire this grace
- Those who fear God's righteous judgment
- Those who desire to know God's love
- Those who want to learn how to love

No one is excluded. The invitation is radically open.

### The Promise

To believe means:
- **Not to perish**: We escape condemnation and eternal separation from God
- **To have eternal life**: To enter into relationship with God, beginning now and extending forever

### God's Love Enables Our Love

God is love. From before time began, the Trinity existed in perfect relationship—Father, Son, and Holy Spirit in eternal communion. God is never lonely because He exists in relationship. He understands what it means to love and be loved.

This love enables us to:
- Love one another sacrificially, the way Christ loved us
- Love God with grateful hearts
- Understand what true love is

### The Example of Sacrificial Love

Tim Tebow became famous not just for his athletic talent but for his public witness to Christian faith. He famously refused to sleep with his girlfriend, supermodel Olivia Culpo, before marriage. The media was baffled—some questioned whether he was even attracted to her.

But Tebow understood something the world has largely forgotten: true love sometimes means saying no to present pleasure out of respect for the other person and obedience to God. He sent her love letters and professed his love while maintaining boundaries because he genuinely loved her—and loved God more.

He knew how to love because God loved him first. He understood the gospel of John 3:16.

---

## Conclusion

God's love is not a sentimentality or an abstract principle. It is the reality that defines His nature, demonstrated supremely through the sacrificial gift of His Son, and extended universally to all who will believe.

This love creates a community radically different from the world—a people who love sacrificially, who serve the weak and marginalized, who care for those with nothing to offer in return. This is the heritage of the Christian faith, written in history through hospitals, charities, and countless acts of humble service.

The question before us is not whether God loves us—He has already answered that decisively. The question is whether we will accept His gift, look to Christ in faith, and allow His love to transform how we love one another.
