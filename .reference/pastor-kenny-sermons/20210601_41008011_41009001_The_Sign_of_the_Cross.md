# Mark 8:11-9:1

**Title:** The Sign of the Cross
**Date:** 20 June 2021
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction: Why We Seek Signs

In an uncertain world, we naturally look for signs to guide us. We scan infection rates hoping for an end to lockdowns. We study investment charts, watch timber futures, search exam hints, or look for romantic reassurance. We're all searching for clues to navigate complexity and gain some sense of control.

This human need for signs is the backdrop for what Jesus encounters in this passage. But the Pharisees aren't simply asking for guidance—they're testing Jesus with a hostile intent.

## 1. Looking for a Sign (8:11-13)

> The Pharisees came and began to question Jesus. To test him, they asked him for a sign from heaven.

Jesus' response is striking—not a sign, but a sigh:

> He sighed deeply and said, "Why does this generation ask for a sign? Truly I tell you, no sign will be given to it." Then he left them, got back into the boat and crossed to the other side.

Jesus isn't against signs altogether. The Gospel of John is full of them. Jesus himself spoke of the sign of Jonah and pointed to the budding fig tree as a sign of coming judgment. The problem isn't with signs themselves—it's with the Pharisees' motives.

They weren't seeking signs that might lead to belief. They wanted to test Jesus in order to *disbelieve*. They had constructed an impossible logic: if Jesus gave no sign, that "proved" he wasn't the Messiah. If he performed an amazing sign, they could dismiss it as demonic. No matter what happened, they would reject him. There's no winning with such thinking.

### The Yeast of the Pharisees (8:15)

> "Be careful," Jesus warned them. "Watch out for the yeast of the Pharisees and that of Herod."

Yeast carries significant symbolism in Jewish culture. At Passover, unleavened bread commemorates the hasty exodus from Egypt—the Israelites had no time to let yeast work in their dough. In this context, yeast symbolizes either bondage or corruption.

Like yeast, a little bit of the Pharisees' thinking spreads quickly and infects everything. The Pharisees and Herod, puffed up with their own importance, release a kind of poisonous gas that permeates an entire kingdom.

The underlying principle Jesus warns against: **Don't put God to the test.** This requires humility and openness to reason. Are we willing to examine our objections honestly? When we dislike an idea, is it because the argument is flawed, or because it challenges our assumptions and threatens our interests?

There's abundant evidence for the historicity of Jesus, the consistency of his claims, the practicality of his ethics, and the genuineness of his character. Yet people refuse to believe because it contradicts their traditions, their selfishness, their lusts, their lifestyles. They claim to want evidence, but when shown it, they ignore it, find no time to investigate, or simply stop replying.

## 2. Strange Signs (8:14-26)

The disciples, forgetting to bring bread on the boat, become anxious about their provisions. Jesus uses this moment to test their faith and understanding:

> Do you have eyes but fail to see, and ears but fail to hear? And don't you remember?

He reminds them of the feeding of the five thousand (twelve basketfuls of leftovers) and the feeding of the four thousand (seven basketfuls). The disciples witnessed divine provision firsthand, yet their anxiety about missing bread reveals spiritual blindness.

### The Healing of the Blind Man at Bethsaida (8:22-26)

This healing is unusual—it's the only healing in Mark that isn't immediately successful:

> They came to Bethsaida, and some people brought a blind man and begged Jesus to touch him. He took the blind man by the hand and led him outside the village. When he had spit on the man's eyes and put his hands on him, Jesus asked, "Do you see anything?" He looked up and said, "I see people; they look like trees walking around." Once more Jesus put his hands on the man's eyes. Then his eyes were opened, his sight was restored, and he saw everything clearly.

This two-stage healing serves as an illustration. The blind man represents the disciples themselves—they're beginning to see, but not yet fully. Peter is about to confess Jesus as Messiah, yet he still doesn't fully perceive what that means.

## 3. Seeing! (8:27-30)

Jesus moves to Caesarea Philippi and asks a crucial question:

> Who do people say I am? ... But what about you? Who do you say I am?

The crowd offers options—John the Baptist, Elijah, one of the prophets. These are the heroes of Israel, the greatest spiritual figures in their tradition. They're correct that Jesus is a prophet; he even spoke of himself that way. But he is more than a prophet.

Peter's answer marks a turning point:

> You are the Messiah.

**Messiah** (from Hebrew; *Christos* in Greek) means "anointed one." It's not a surname but a title—the title given to Israel's kings at their coronation. When Peter calls Jesus Messiah, he's saying: "You are the King prophesied to rule the whole world, to bring justice, to save the oppressed, and to humble the proud."

This confession likely follows the pattern of signs Jesus had just performed—the healing of the deaf man and now the gradual opening of the blind man's eyes. These fulfill Isaiah's prophecy about what the Messiah would accomplish:

> Then will the eyes of the blind be opened and the ears of the deaf unstopped. Then will the lame leap like a deer, and the mute tongue shout for joy.

Peter has finally seen. But his vision is still incomplete.

## 4. But Not Perceiving (8:31-9:1)

Into this moment of joy and revelation comes a jarring reality:

> He then began to teach them that the Son of Man must suffer many things and be rejected by the elders, the chief priests and the teachers of the law, and that he must be killed and after three days rise again.

This makes no sense to Peter. Kings don't willingly walk toward death. The powerful cling to power—think of CEOs grilled in Congress, or authoritarian leaders who never relinquish control. It's like someone achieving the highest test score and then saying they'll enlist for war. It's incomprehensible.

Peter can't contain himself:

> He spoke plainly about this, and Peter took him aside and began to rebuke him.

Peter actually scolds Jesus, as if teaching him how the world works. Jesus must not understand political reality. Let me explain, Peter seems to say.

But Jesus' response is fierce:

> Get behind me, Satan! You do not have in mind the concerns of God, but merely human concerns.

Jesus calls Peter an "adversary"—not because Peter is demon-possessed, but because he's opposing God's purpose. The problem is clear: Peter doesn't want to die. He has in mind the things of men, not the things of God.

Perhaps Peter speaks from love, wanting to protect his master. But there's also self-interest woven in. If the one who protects you, leads you, and provides for you dies, then who's next? Self-interest is what Jesus really wants his disciples to abandon.

### Radical Discipleship (8:34-38)

Jesus then calls the crowd and disciples:

> Whoever wants to be my disciple must deny themselves and take up their cross and follow me.

In the first century, "taking up one's cross" isn't metaphorical—it means carrying the instrument of your own execution. The condemned prisoner brought the crossbeam to the place of crucifixion. It's a vivid image of death.

For Jesus, this meant literally taking on the death penalty that belongs to us. We are sinners condemned by the judge of the world to eternal death. Jesus takes that judgment upon himself so we don't have to.

For his disciples, it means the same principle: we must deny our self-interest and live for the gospel welfare of others. We're not here to accumulate wealth, status, or experiences. We're here to serve—to tell others that Jesus died for them and offer them security beyond this life.

Yet we often try to negotiate with God's terms:

- "I'll serve when I'm rich and successful, so people will look up to me."
- "Let me be popular first, then I'll do the unpopular thing."
- "I'll sacrifice when it doesn't hurt to sacrifice."
- "I want to follow, but only under these conditions..."

But a king gives orders; we don't give orders to him. Jesus is sovereign. His command is clear: deny yourself, take up your cross, and follow me.

### The Paradox of Life (8:35-38)

Jesus offers a profound paradox:

> For whoever wants to save their life will lose it, but whoever loses their life for me and for the gospel will save it. What good is it for someone to gain the whole world, yet forfeit their soul? Or what can anyone give in exchange for their soul?

And a warning:

> If anyone is ashamed of me and my words in this adulterous and sinful generation, the Son of Man will be ashamed of them when he comes in his Father's glory with the holy angels.

Death should never be our fear. Poverty and suffering should never be our fear. Losing our standing or reputation should never be our fear. The only thing worthy of fear is losing our eternal soul—and that, Jesus has secured for us.

## Conclusion

Peter sees the Messiah but hasn't yet seen what the Messiah does. He recognizes the king but doesn't yet grasp that following a king means radical obedience to the king's terms, not ours.

The greatest sign isn't a wonder in the sky. It's the cross itself—God's judgment rendered, God's mercy offered, God's invitation to join him in service to a broken world.

Will you follow him?
