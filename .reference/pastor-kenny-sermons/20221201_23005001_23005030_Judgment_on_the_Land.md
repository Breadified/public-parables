# Isaiah 5:1-30

**Title:** Judgment on the Land  
**Date:** 3 December 2022  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## No Fruit from the Land (1-7)

The passage opens as a love song—a duet about God's investment in Israel. Isaiah invites us into an intimate portrait of God as a vineyard keeper, lavishing care on the nation he chose.

> I will sing for the one I love a song about his vineyard: My loved one had a vineyard on a fertile hillside.

God prepared the land with meticulous care. He dug it up and cleared it of stones, planted it with the choicest vines, built a watchtower to protect it, and cut out a winepress from stone. This describes joyful yet hard work—a labour of love. Picture a gardener preparing an untouched patch of land: digging out rocks, adding topsoil and fertiliser, building a scarecrow. Months pass as the gardener cultivates with anticipation. Then comes harvest.

> Then he looked for a crop of good grapes, but it yielded only bad fruit.

Something has gone terribly wrong. After all that time, money, and effort—nothing. The fault is not with the gardener, who did everything possible. So God invites judgment:

> "Now you dwellers in Jerusalem and people of Judah, judge between me and my vineyard. What more could have been done for my vineyard than I have done for it? When I looked for good grapes, why did it yield only bad?"

The answer is clear: nothing else could have been done. If not the gardener, then the crop itself is defective. The DNA of Israel is the problem.

God moves to judgment:

> I will take away its hedge, and it will be destroyed; I will break down its wall, and it will be trampled. I will make it a wasteland, neither pruned nor cultivated, and briers and thorns will grow there. I will command the clouds not to rain on it.

This is a scorched-earth reversal—the land returns to desert, like the beginning of creation itself. The dwellers clap their hands in agreement, for the judgment is just.

But then comes the explanation that changes everything:

> The vineyard of the Lord Almighty is the nation of Israel, and the people of Judah are the vines he delighted in.

The vineyard is not plants—it is a people. And the bad fruit Isaiah exposes is their behaviour:

> He looked for justice (mishpat), but saw bloodshed (mishpak); for righteousness (tsedeqah), but heard cries of distress (tse'eqah).

Notice the wordplay in the Hebrew. God expected justice but found violence. He looked for righteousness but heard only screams. This pattern continues to this day—corruption and murder instead of the justice God demands.

Why is God justified in this judgment? Because Israel was the smallest of all nations, yet God chose them above all peoples. As Deuteronomy reminds us:

> The LORD did not set his affection on you and choose you because you were more numerous than other peoples, for you were the fewest of all peoples.

God invested in Israel despite their weakness. They were called to bear fruit—justice, mercy, obedience. Instead, they produced bloodshed and injustice. God is completely justified in his judgment against them. More than that, given how they treated him in return, his judgment is merciful restraint.

## Mixing Good and Evil (8-25)

Yet Israel did produce some fruit—just not the fruit God sought. Before the judgment fell, they enjoyed prosperity.

> Woe to those who rise early in the morning to run after their drinks, who stay up late at night till they are inflamed with wine.

This is merism—a literary device that uses opposite extremes to mean "all the time." Morning drinkers and evening drinkers means drinking 24/7. The fact that people drink in the morning (abnormal and wasteful) emphasizes their constant intoxication.

While alcohol itself is not wrong—Jesus made wine at a wedding—habitual drunkenness leads to debauchery and violence. The World Health Organization estimates that roughly 55% of domestic abuse perpetrators were drinking alcohol before assault. In Australia's drinking culture, this is increasingly relevant as modern distillation creates abnormally strong beverages.

Christians have wisely erred on the side of caution. We should be careful not to bring workplace or university drinking culture into our homes. Churches in Australia have rightly banned alcohol on premises except for communion wine, and keep all church events alcohol-free—for the sake of children and those struggling with addiction, and to remain beyond reproach.

But ancient Judah had no such restraints:

> They have harps and lyres at their banquets, pipes and timbrels and wine, but they have no regard for the deeds of the Lord, no respect for the work of his hands.

They had parties. Though the vineyard itself was nearly barren—producing only a tenth of what it should—they used even that meagre fruit to mock God. Instead of offering thanks, they used the wine from God's blessing to get drunk and debauchery. They took what God gave and spat it back in his face.

God's judgment on their greed comes next:

> Woe to you who add house to house and join field to field till no space is left and you live alone in the land.

Property development itself is not wrong. Two adjacent properties are worth more than two separated ones, and thoughtful development helps a community flourish. But their intent reveals the problem: "you live alone in the land." They accumulated for themselves alone, hoarding rather than building community. The paradox is cruel—they gathered all the land but ended up isolated:

> Surely the great houses will become desolate, the fine mansions left without occupants.

Property only has value if people inhabit it. A land with no inhabitants is worthless. This predicts the exile:

> Therefore my people will go into exile for lack of understanding; those of high rank will die of hunger and the common people will be parched with thirst.

The merism here expands—high and low, hunger and thirst—emphasizing that judgment is total and universal:

> Therefore Death expands its jaws, opening wide its mouth; into it will descend their nobles and masses with all their brawlers and revelers.

God's judgment is severe. Yet even this is not the end:

> Yet for all this, his anger is not turned away, his hand is still upraised.

God adds insult to injury. The judgment compounds. Over and over, God blessed Israel. Over and over, they threw that blessing back at his face and called it evil.

We see this pattern in Australia—the "lucky country" God has blessed. We take his good and call it evil:

- God gave us schools. Yet public schools now denigrate him.
- God gave us sex within marriage as a blessing. Yet we mock his purposes and call them evil.
- God gave us church as a gathering place. Yet we treat it as an inconvenience.

God gave us the five-day working week—an unprecedented gift of time. Sunday once meant fellowship. Yet many of us cannot commit even two hours on a Sunday morning for Christian community. We say we are too busy because we spent the weekend partying, shopping for more possessions, or checking property values. We have never had more flexible time with work-from-home, yet we always seem to run out of time for church. When holidays come, we fly overseas. We take what God gave—time—and throw it back at his face by using it to avoid him and his people.

Are we God's gift of fellowship to each other? Or are we rejecting it?

## The Land Given Away (26-30)

If Israel does not want the land God gave them, he will give it to someone else:

> He lifts up a banner for the distant nations, he whistles for those at the ends of the earth.

This is not a flag of nationhood but a flag of defeat—a white flag signalling surrender. Like a "for sale" sign at a real estate auction, it announces: "Free land. Come and take it."

The invaders respond with terrible efficiency:

> Here they come, swiftly and speedily! Not one of them grows tired or stumbles, not one slumbers or sleeps; not a belt is loosened at the waist, not a sandal strap is broken. Their arrows are sharp, all their bows are strung; their horses' hooves seem like flint, their chariot wheels like a whirlwind.

Unlike Israel, drunk and disorganised, these warriors are disciplined and prepared. The horses and chariots tell us they are foreigners—not native to Israel's terrain. They have sharpened their arrows and strung their bows. They are ready.

> Their roar is like that of the lion, they roar like young lions; they growl as they seize their prey and carry it off with no one to rescue.

Lions are the most fearsome of beasts—symbols of mighty empires like Assyria and Babylon. No one rescues Israel's people. Destruction is absolute.

> In that day they will roar over it like the roaring of the sea. And if one looks at the land, there is only darkness and distress; even the sun will be darkened by clouds.

Israel rejected God. So God moves on. As Paul writes:

> Because of their transgression, salvation has come to the Gentiles. Israel has experienced a hardening in part until the full number of the Gentiles has come in.

If you do not want God, he will give that blessing to others. This should humble us. How do we feel about that?

## Today

This principle works slowly, sometimes haphazardly, with exceptions—on individual, family, and national levels. The gospel makes a nation flourish. When they reject it, it declines.

We object to Europe's demise, yet Europe has rejected God for over a century. It caused two world wars. Other nations will take its place. It is sad because of the history, but is it not justice?

These questions trouble us. When we see God's judgment on nations that reject him, we must ask ourselves: What about our nation?

We cannot afford to let it die. We live here. Our families live here. If we do not live godly, we are next in line. God will replace us.

Consider what must change:

- We must be individually functioning—not drunk on the world's intoxicants
- Our families must be growing and thriving
- Our churches must be serving with purpose
- Our politicians must govern with wisdom

We must invest in another land—not real estate, but the kingdom of God. This is something worth investing in. This is the investment that yields eternal fruit.
