# Zephaniah 3:6-20

**Title:** Judgment Day: Future  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew  
**Date:** May 2, 2010

## Main Point

Judgment for some means salvation for others.

---

## 1. Gathering for Judgment

### The Gentiles Destroyed

> Therefore wait for me," declares the LORD, "for the day I will stand up to testify. I have decided to assemble the nations, to gather the kingdoms and to pour out my wrath on them—all my fierce anger. The whole world will be consumed by the fire of my jealous anger. (Zephaniah 3:8)

God uses the metaphor of gathering to express judgment—like a police sting gathering evidence and witnesses. The nations will be assembled, the kingdoms gathered, so that God may pour out His wrath and consume the world with the fire of His jealous anger.

This echoes Matthew 3:11, where John the Baptist speaks of one coming after him who will baptize with the Holy Spirit and fire, whose winnowing fork will separate wheat from chaff.

### Wait for the Lord

The promise embedded in this passage is crucial: **"Wait for me."** Though Jerusalem had been invaded and the people sent into exile, the promise was that this captivity would not last forever. The day was coming when their captors would be destroyed and the exiles would return.

This message speaks powerfully to those of us who feel hurt, unjustly treated, surrounded by enemies, or living in unfair circumstances. God's word to us is simple: **Wait for me. I am coming soon. I will right the wrongs of this world.**

Waiting is difficult, especially when suffering makes us bitter. There are no shortcuts in the Christian experience—we are called to perseverance, to be a trusting people of the God who loves us, and to leave the timing of judgment in His hands.

#### Learning from David

David's experience in 1 Samuel 24 illustrates this perfectly. Hunted by a jealous King Saul, David found himself in a cave where Saul entered unaware. David could have killed him—his companions urged him to—but he refused:

> "I will not lift my hand against my master, because he is the LORD's anointed... May the LORD judge between you and me. And may the LORD avenge the wrongs you have done to me, but my hand will not touch you." (1 Samuel 24:10, 12)

David's example teaches us to keep persevering and trusting God, to refuse bitterness, and to actively look out for others. When we focus on our own suffering, we tend toward envy of those with more; instead, we should count our blessings and remain faithful.

## 2. Gathering for Salvation

Out of judgment comes salvation. In fact, judgment for some means salvation for others.

### The Gentiles Saved

> "Then I will purify the lips of the peoples, that all of them may call on the name of the LORD and serve him shoulder to shoulder. From beyond the rivers of Cush my worshipers, my scattered people, will bring me offerings." (Zephaniah 3:9-10)

The purification of lips speaks to a reversal of Babel. At Babel, God confused the languages of humanity because their united pride threatened to accomplish whatever they imagined. But here, God will purify the lips of the peoples so that all may call upon His name and serve Him together—a new unity, not based on human pride but on worship of God.

The mention of worshipers coming from beyond the rivers of Cush (Ethiopia) recalls the glory days of Solomon, when the Queen of Sheba traveled to Jerusalem to witness his wisdom and wealth, then praised the God of Israel (1 Kings 10). This will be repeated: the nations will come bringing offerings to Jerusalem, not for human glory, but to honor the God who dwells there.

### The Remnant Jews

> "On that day you will not be put to shame for all the wrongs you have done to me, because I will remove from this city those who rejoice in their pride. Never again will you be haughty on my holy hill. But I will leave within you the meek and humble, who trust in the name of the LORD." (Zephaniah 3:11-12)

Jerusalem will be restored, but not as it was before. God will remove the proud and leave a remnant—the meek and humble who trust in His name.

> "The remnant of Israel will do no wrong; they will speak no lies, nor will deceit be found in their mouths. They will eat and lie down and no one will make them afraid." (Zephaniah 3:13)

This remnant will be characterized by righteousness, truth, and fearlessness. They will live in security and peace, like sheep and lion lying together, finally free from the trauma of exile.

#### The True Remnant is Jesus

Ultimately, the true remnant is not the nation of Israel, nor is it the Church—the true remnant is Jesus. He alone perfectly embodies this righteous remnant, enduring forty days in the wilderness without yielding to temptation, praying in Gethsemane in obedience. Jesus gathers His people, beginning with Peter, gathering His Church in the heavenly Jerusalem. As Hebrews 12:22-23 tells us:

> "You have come to Mount Zion, to the heavenly Jerusalem, the city of the living God... to the church of the firstborn, whose names are written in heaven."

We have a new citizenship. When we gather on earth, we do so functionally to encourage one another and ensure we end up in that final gathering. As Hebrews 10:24-25 exhorts:

> "Let us consider how we may spur one another on toward love and good deeds. Let us not give up meeting together... but let us encourage one another—and all the more as you see the Day approaching."

### The Remnant's Comfort

The remnant will experience restoration:

> "The sorrows for the appointed feasts I will remove from you; they are a burden and a reproach to you... At that time I will deal with all who oppressed you; I will rescue the lame and gather those who have been scattered. I will give them praise and honor in every land where they were put to shame." (Zephaniah 3:18-19)

## 3. What the Gathering Looks Like

### A Song of Joy and Security

> "Sing, O Daughter of Zion; shout aloud, O Israel! Be glad and rejoice with all your heart, O Daughter of Jerusalem! The LORD has taken away your punishment, he has turned back your enemy. The LORD, the King of Israel, is with you; never again will you fear any harm. On that day they will say to Jerusalem, 'Do not fear, O Zion; do not let your hands hang limp. The LORD your God is with you, he is mighty to save. He will take great delight in you, he will quiet you with his love, he will rejoice over you with singing.'" (Zephaniah 3:14-17)

Notice the beautiful parallel structure:
- **Rejoice** (verses 14, 17)
- **For he has saved us** (verses 15, 17)
- **He is in our midst** (verses 15, 17)
- **Never fear again** (verses 15-16)

### A Safe Community

While the heavenly gathering may be future, shouldn't we begin to exhibit its characteristics now? A good gathering is marked by the absence of fear—fear of getting lost, being ignored, being judged, or being harmed.

What would it look like if our gatherings became truly safe places where we could:
- Share our struggles and long suffering without fear of judgment or ridicule
- Offer patient rebuke rather than condemnation
- Protect one another from gossip and slander
- Extend Christ's forgiveness to one another instead of condemnation
- Seek reconciliation without anger
- Speak truthfully without harsh criticism or mockery

When such righteousness and holiness mark our gatherings, we would truly have reason to rejoice and sing together, just as the heavenly gathering will.

## 4. The Ultimate Party

The future holds not just judgment and salvation, but celebration:

> "Blessed are those who are invited to the wedding supper of the Lamb!" (Revelation 19:9)

> "Then I looked and heard the voice of many angels, numbering thousands upon thousands, and ten thousand times ten thousand. They encircled the throne and the living creatures and the elders. In a loud voice they sang: 'Worthy is the Lamb, who was slain, to receive power and wealth and wisdom and strength and honor and glory and praise!'" (Revelation 5:11-12)

The gathered people of God will feast and sing in the presence of the Lamb, forever freed from fear, forever secure in His love.
