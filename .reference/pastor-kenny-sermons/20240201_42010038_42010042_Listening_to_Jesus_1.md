# Luke 10:38-42

**Title:** Listening to Jesus  
**Date:** 18 February 2024

## The Problem of Busyness

We live in a culture where "busy" has become the default answer when someone asks how we're doing. It's replaced "fine" as our standard response. We're swamped with careers to develop, businesses to keep afloat, children to raise, classes to attend, appointments to keep, emails to answer, meetings to go to, and endless errands. There are so many things demanding our attention that we rush frantically from one activity to the next.

The statistics bear this out starkly. In Australia, we work 3.2 billion hours a year in unpaid overtime, have accumulated 134 million days of unused annual leave, and 3.8 million people don't even take lunch breaks. Perhaps most troubling: 7.4 million Australians don't get enough sleep. We are too busy to stop working, too busy to eat, and too busy to even sleep.

This season of Lent—derived from an old English word meaning "to lengthen"—marks the lengthening days of spring in the Northern Hemisphere. It's a time when some Christians traditionally fast in the 40 days leading to Easter. But this is not a time to remain busy. It's a time for reflection and to listen to Jesus as we journey toward Jerusalem.

## Martha: Distracted by Many Things

### She Opened Her Home

> As Jesus and his disciples were on their way, he came to a village where a woman named Martha opened her home to him. (Luke 10:38)

Jesus and his followers were traveling toward Jerusalem, stopping at villages along the way. The disciples had been given instructions to seek out those who "promote peace" and to stay there, eating and drinking what they offered. Martha was such a person—she welcomed Jesus into her home with genuine hospitality.

She embodied what seemed like ideal discipleship: she opened her door to the Lord and even prepared food for him.

### Yet She Was Distracted

> But Martha was distracted by all the preparations that had to be made. (Luke 10:40)

The word "distracted" carries the sense of being "pulled in every direction"—like a burden laid upon her. Picture the scene: on one side of the kitchen the water is boiling over, on the other the meat is burning. The chicken in the oven is overcooking while she still needs to set the table and the dessert is melting. She is pulled in every direction by the multiplicity of preparations before her.

It's a great burden. It's exhausting work.

Finally, overwhelmed and exasperated, she goes to Jesus with a complaint:

> She came to him and asked, "Lord, don't you care that my sister has left me to do the work by myself? Tell her to help me!" (Luke 10:40)

### The Accusation

Martha makes a significant charge: "Don't you care?" She concludes that because Jesus isn't helping and Mary isn't helping, Jesus must not care about her. In her anxiety, she even commands Jesus: "Tell her to help me!"

Jesus responds by describing her state:

> Martha, Martha, the Lord answered, "you are worried and upset about many things." (Luke 10:41)

"Worried" suggests the anxious preoccupation that chokes out peace. "Upset" conveys noise and internal turbulence. She is consumed by anxiety about her many tasks.

### The Pattern We Know

This resonates with so many of us. We are anxious about work—over assignments, exams, promotions, salary, buying a house, our children's education. We assign far too high a value to these things. We lack satisfaction with the present and feel an unnecessary desperation for more.

We don't just need clothes; we need fashion, celebrity status, and popularity. We don't need shelter; we need a house with more bathrooms than people, filled with possessions. We don't need food; we need restaurant-quality meals where the cows have been massaged and the vegetables sung to. We don't just want to experience friends and family; we need to travel halfway around the world to eat at the same McDonald's franchise so we don't "miss out."

We must have the right weight, the right look, the right image, the right education, the right job. This is anxious work.

Yet despite living in one of the world's wealthiest, most prosperous countries with the freshest food, most beautiful beaches, and best healthcare—a paradise by any reasonable standard—we are depressed. We are anxious. We are upset. All that work is for nothing.

It's like the example of Japanese workers who stay late at the office, not for productivity but to look good to their boss, while their boss stays late to look good to them. We are busy going nowhere.

We work and save for a house, then blow it on an overseas holiday and fancy cars. We end up in the same place but with added anxiety and troubles. We keep our children busy with piano lessons and tutoring, but if we don't disciple them ourselves, we end up with educated but rotten children. We work to provide for our families, but what's the point if it results in family breakdown? And after 80 years of busyness, we die anyway. We end up in the same place but with added anxiety and troubles.

### Jesus' Word to Martha

> "Martha, Martha, the Lord answered, "you are worried and upset about many things, but few things are needed—or indeed only one." (Luke 10:41-42)

When Jesus calls someone's name twice—"Martha, Martha"—it carries both emphasis and reassurance. Throughout Scripture, this pattern appears: "Abraham, Abraham" at the binding of Isaac; "Jacob, Jacob" when he discovers Joseph is alive; "Saul, Saul" on the Damascus road. The repetition signals both weight and comfort.

Jesus says: don't be worried and upset. Some things matter. But few things are needed—or indeed only one.

## Mary: Choosing the Better Thing

### She Sat at Jesus' Feet

> She had a sister called Mary, who sat at the Lord's feet listening to what he said. (Luke 10:39)

"Sitting at the Lord's feet" is the posture of discipleship. While the master sits elevated on a chair, students sit on the floor at their feet—the location of humility and learning. We see this same image when the sinful woman pours perfume on Jesus' feet, or when Jairus falls at Jesus' feet pleading for his daughter's life. The feet represent the place of reverence and submission.

Mary is "listening"—the activity of learning. She listens "to what he said" about the kingdom of God, about God's reign breaking into the world. What a privilege—to sit at the feet of the King of the universe and hear his teaching!

In Luke 11, Jesus compares this moment to something even more extraordinary:

> The Queen of the South will rise at the judgment with the men of this generation and condemn them; for she came from the ends of the earth to listen to Solomon's wisdom, and now one greater than Solomon is here. (Luke 11:31)

Mary's opportunity surpasses even the Queen of Sheba coming to hear King Solomon's wisdom. She is listening to Jesus himself—greater than any philosopher, greater than any teacher, greater than any concert or celebrity performance.

### She Is Not Distracted

Mary is not pulled by every wind and temptation, not distracted by fleeting bargains or temporary things. Her life is not determined by what will fade away. Her timetable is not controlled by the temporary. She prioritizes Jesus.

This is the one thing God desires from us: to listen and follow him. To put Jesus as the priority in our lives.

> "Man shall not live on bread alone but on every word that comes from the mouth of the Lord." (Luke 4:4; Deuteronomy 8:3)

We live by God's word.

### What Mary Chose

> "Mary has chosen what is better, and it will not be taken away from her." (Luke 10:42)

What Mary has chosen is "better."

Part of our busyness stems from having too many choices. The paradox of choice—the freedom to select among many options—often leaves us feeling powerless and frustrated. We become paralyzed trying to make the "better choice" over trivial things. We spend hours researching which kettle is fastest and best-looking rather than simply buying a $15 one. We agonize over phone colors, clothes, pizza flavors, and countless other commodities.

But we never spend time on the truly important choice: Do you want to follow Jesus or not?

It doesn't matter if your kettle is white, blue, or steel. What matters is your answer to Jesus. If you're unsure, church is a good place to find out.

### The Application

Jesus has come. It is time to listen to him. The word of God is near us—in our hands. We cannot pretend it doesn't exist or find distractions away from it. Make space to read your Bible this year. Prioritize it during the week, not just on Sundays.

This word—which built Western civilization, brought it from medieval darkness into light, and through the British Empire brought enlightenment to India, Singapore, Hong Kong, and Malaysia—will also bring us from death into eternal life.

---

## The Choice Before Us

Lent is not a time to remain in the kitchen, not a time to stay busy. It is a time for reflection and listening to Jesus. It is a time to pause and consider which direction we're headed as we journey toward Jerusalem.

We must choose what Mary chose: to sit at the feet of Jesus and listen. To prioritize his word above the clamor of our anxious busyness. To remember that there is only one thing necessary—and it will never be taken away from us.
