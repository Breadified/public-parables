# 1 Corinthians 12:7-27

## Many Gifts

God distributes spiritual gifts throughout the church in remarkable variety. Paul acknowledges this diversity in answering the Corinthian church's question about which gifts matter most.

> To one there is given through the Spirit a message of wisdom, to another a message of knowledge by means of the same Spirit, to another faith by the same Spirit, to another gifts of healing by that one Spirit, to another miraculous powers, to another prophecy, to another distinguishing between spirits, to another speaking in different kinds of tongues, and to still another the interpretation of tongues. (1 Corinthians 12:8-10)

### Understanding These Gifts

The precise nature of all these gifts remains somewhat mysterious. Even ancient Christian commentators admitted uncertainty about their exact definitions. For instance, does "healing" refer to physical restoration or spiritual wholeness? The ambiguity suggests these gifts may not function exactly as we imagine today.

### A Word on Cessationism

Some argue that certain miraculous gifts—healings, tongues, interpretations—were temporary, given only during the apostolic era to establish the church's foundation. Once Scripture was completed, these signs and wonders ceased since God had revealed all He intended through His Word. While this view has merit, it seems primarily concerned with the apostles' unique role in Acts, not necessarily the broader church in Corinth.

However, the passage appears to address the entire Corinthian congregation, not just apostles. So these gifts likely exist today—though we should understand them primarily as **speaking and word gifts** rather than miraculous signs:

- **Message of wisdom** and **message of knowledge**—declarations of godly insight
- **Prophecy**—speaking God's truth
- **Distinguishing between spirits**—discerning what is of God
- **Speaking in different kinds of tongues** and **interpretation**—languages and their understanding

### Healing: A Broader Understanding

Healing in Scripture encompasses more than physical recovery:

> He himself bore our sins in his body on the cross, so that we might die to sins and live for righteousness; by his wounds you have been healed. (1 Peter 2:24)

> You were like sheep going astray, but now you have returned to the Shepherd and Overseer of your souls. (1 Peter 2:25)

Christian theology actually validates suffering rather than eliminating it. A crushed spirit causes far more damage than physical illness:

> The human spirit can endure in sickness, but a crushed spirit who can bear? (Proverbs 18:14)

The gift of healing often means offering spiritual restoration through words, prayer, and compassionate presence—healing the inner person through God's truth.

### The Working of Power

Consider Paul's understanding of power (dynamis):

> We proclaim Him, admonishing and teaching everyone with all wisdom, so that we may present everyone perfect in Christ. To this end I strenuously contend with all the energy Christ so powerfully works in me. (Colossians 1:28-29)

> I pray that the eyes of your heart may be enlightened in order that you may know the hope to which he has called you... and his incomparably great power for us who believe. That power is the same as the mighty strength he exerted when he raised Christ from the dead. (Ephesians 1:18-20)

### Many Speakings, One Message

These gifts manifest throughout church life in countless ways:

- Official teaching and preaching
- Bible readings and study
- Prayer and intercession
- Sharing testimonies and questions
- Comment card discussions
- Breakout room conversations
- Translating God's Word
- Sunday school presentations
- Conversations over tea and meals
- Singing together
- Bible studies
- Casual fellowship during sports
- Car conversations
- Digital communication—Facebook, WhatsApp, emails, cards
- Worship songs (Quarantunes!)

**All of them are speakings of God's Word!**

So many gifts building up God's church—all for the common good. Yet we cannot rank them by importance. To suggest one gift exceeds another would be like saying a sermon is more valuable than the prayer spoken before it, more important than the hospitality extended afterward, or more crucial than the quiet conversation between two believers seeking to understand Scripture together.

## One Spirit and One Body

The unity of the Spirit frames everything. Paul emphasizes repeatedly that all gifts come **through** the same Spirit:

> All these are the work of one and the same Spirit, and he distributes them to each one, just as he determines. (1 Corinthians 12:11)

This phrase "of the Spirit" describes not the *origin* of the gifts (God the Father gives them) but the *nature* of the message. It's the same breath, the same speaking, the same words—**Jesus is Lord!** Different methods and expressions, but one unified message.

### We Need ALL These Speakings

No single gift suffices. The church requires diverse voices speaking God's truth in different ways. Everyone must contribute their particular gift.

### The Body Operates as One Unit

> Just as a body, though one, has many parts, but all its many parts form one body, so it is with Christ. (1 Corinthians 12:12)

The church that gathers is one body despite its many parts. Our church, with all its diverse speakings, remains one unified body.

### United by Spiritual Baptism

> For we were all baptized by one Spirit so as to form one body—whether Jews or Gentiles, slave or free—and we were all given the one Spirit to drink. (1 Corinthians 12:13)

Baptism represents identification with Christ's lordship. Not water baptism itself, but our spiritual identification:

> For Christ did not send me to baptize, but to preach the gospel—not with wisdom and eloquence, lest the cross of Christ be emptied of its power. (1 Corinthians 1:17)

The Spirit's baptism unites us under one Master. We share the same spiritual truth:

> Let anyone who is thirsty come to me and drink. Whoever believes in me, as Scripture has said, rivers of living water will flow from within them. By this he meant the Spirit, whom those who believed in him were later to receive. (John 7:37-39)

This inner transformation—not external washing—marks our unity. Our problem with sin lies within us, not outside. We've been given the antidote: Christ himself.

### Application: A Faith That Transcends Boundaries

We Christians share a bond deeper than nationality, ethnicity, or background. Whether from Gansu province or born in Sydney—if we have Christ, we have everything essential in common. This unity must be reflected in our gatherings. We are vital to each other.

## Two Attitudes About Our Place in the Body

### Attitude One: "I Don't Belong"

> Now if the foot should say, "Because I am not a hand, I do not belong to the body," it would not for that reason stop being part of the body. And if the ear should say, "Because I am not an eye, I do not belong to the body," it would not for that reason stop being part of the body. (1 Corinthians 12:15-16)

Some compare gifts as if certain ones rank higher. A hand seems more useful and beautiful than a foot. An eye appears more important than an ear. We display our hands; we hide our feet in shoes. We adorn our eyes; no one celebrates ears.

The temptation arises to think: *I don't belong.*

**This feeling is common and widespread:**

- **Newcomers** feeling out of place in an unfamiliar gathering
- **Non-Christians** wondering why they came, sensing they're different
- **The sick or suffering** fearing judgment and rejection
- **Parents** longing for adult companionship
- **Those who look different** in appearance or background
- **Young people** feeling small and invisible in a large community
- **Sinners** convinced others would judge their struggles
- **Even the pastor** sometimes feeling old, out of touch, radical—wondering if he truly belongs

We all feel like minority groups. Everyone sees age differences differently depending on their own age. If you're the oldest, you see young people everywhere. If you're young, everyone seems old. In the middle? Nobody shares your age. **We all feel out of it.**

Yet this judgment is fundamentally flawed:

> If the whole body were an eye, where would the sense of hearing be? If the whole body were an ear, where would the sense of smell be? But in fact God has placed the parts in the body, every one of them, just as he wanted them to be. If they were all one part, where would the body be? As it is, there are many parts, but one body. (1 Corinthians 12:17-20)

No healthy person removes a foot because it seems less important than an eye, or dismisses an ear because it's less visible. God has positioned every part with perfect design and proportion. Even the tiny pinky toe matters—you feel its importance acutely when you stub it.

A body made entirely of eyes would be grotesque and non-functional.

**If our church became self-centered and isolating:**

Imagine a congregation where nobody cares about others, everyone speaks for themselves, people grab food without thinking of those around them, newcomers are ignored, nobody prays together because what's the point when they won't follow up, deep conversations never form because people remain strangers, musicians do their own thing, sermons feel random, people talk over each other and come and go as they please. Why would anyone make themselves vulnerable here? Why share anything?

This is monstrous. This is what a body made of only one part becomes.

**Don't let our church become like that. Make a difference.**

### Application: You Belong

We are all important to each other. If you feel you don't belong—whether as a newcomer, a non-Christian, someone struggling with sickness or sin, or anyone else feeling out of place—understand this: **You do belong.**

But sometimes when hard times hit, we withdraw from Christian fellowship because we fear judgment. If that's happened to you, I'm genuinely sorry. I'm committed to making church a safe and caring place. Don't disappear on us. Don't pull out. We need each other.

Consider what your presence provides:

- **Newcomers** teach us how to love strangers, embodying the Good Samaritan principle
- **The sick** give us opportunity to demonstrate genuine concern and love
- **Those who challenge us** help us grow through interaction and accountability
- **The elderly** bring wisdom we desperately need
- **Even non-Christians** keep us honest by asking tough questions, ensuring we're not a cult
- **The young** bring energy and fresh perspective

Without these varied parts, we become incomplete and dysfunctional.

### Attitude Two: "I Don't Need You"

> The eye cannot say to the hand, "I don't need you!" And the head cannot say to the feet, "I don't need you!" (1 Corinthians 12:21)

The opposite problem strikes those who are strong, capable, and doing well. We think: *I don't need this community. I can study Scripture alone. I can attend conferences elsewhere. I'm growing fine without regular church attendance.*

You may be right. You don't need us.

But consider the divine design:

> On the contrary, those parts of the body that seem to be weaker are indispensable, and the parts that we think are less honorable we treat with special honor. And the parts that are unpresentable are treated with special modesty, while our presentable parts need no special treatment. But God has put the body together, giving greater honor to the parts that lacked it, so that there should be no division in the body, but that its parts should have equal concern for each other. (1 Corinthians 12:22-25)

**Your strength exists for one purpose: to help the weaker parts.**

> We who are strong ought to bear with the failings of the weak and not to please ourselves. (Romans 15:1)

Treating parts with "special modesty" doesn't mean hiding them away—it means caring for them with intentionality.

### Application: We Need Consistent Commitment

We pack our Sundays too full: birthday parties, holidays, social gatherings, work obligations, camps, university meetings, recitals, friends' baptisms, confirmations, weddings. We justify our absence with: *I don't need to be here. I'm doing fine. I'll catch up next week—or the week after.*

But relationships don't form in constant flux. **We still need you.**

Churches that lack stability suffer:

- You never know who will attend
- Different prayer partners each week create new dynamics
- Teaching quality varies week to week
- Nobody knows when to start
- Members can't genuinely challenge each other
- You won't see someone long enough to understand how they think
- Disagreements can be brushed aside because you won't see them next week anyway

Churches thrive on **stability, consistency, and commitment.**

Listen to what Scripture asks:

> If one part suffers, every part suffers with it; if one part is honored, every part rejoices with it. (1 Corinthians 12:26)

We share interconnected experience. When one struggles, all feel the impact. When one thrives, all celebrate.

This requires:
- Regular attendance
- Consistent presence
- Willingness to be vulnerable
- Commitment to confess sins and pray for each other
- A place where people know they can return week after week

**The question becomes: Do you want parts with equal concern for each other, or concern only when you feel like fitting it in?**

If you are strong, show up to help. If you are thriving, use that strength to serve those who struggle.

## Who Is Most Important?

> Now you are the body of Christ, and each one of you is a part of it. (1 Corinthians 12:27)

Every role matters. Movie credits—often the most egalitarian moment in cinema—show this principle. Key grips, gaffers, best boys, set decorators, sound engineers, and countless others without whose specific contributions the film wouldn't exist.

**Everyone has a role.**

So to answer our opening question: The most important person in this room is the person next to you. The other person. We are all important to each other. We need each other for this body to function properly.

### Imagine a Different Church

Imagine instead a gathering where God's Word is proclaimed faithfully each week—not just from the pulpit but from one another as we share what we're learning from our Lord. We confess our troubles, temptations, and difficulties. We listen and offer help and prayer. When outsiders come in, they hear God's Word and witness how love actually operates among us.

**This is what it means to be truly spiritual.**
