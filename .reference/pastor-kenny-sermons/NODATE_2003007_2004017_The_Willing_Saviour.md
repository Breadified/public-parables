# Exodus 3:7–4:17

**Title:** The Willing Saviour
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Understanding Excuses

An excuse is a time-delay tactic—a way of avoiding a direct "no" in hopes that the person asking will eventually give up. We see this played out in Moses' encounter with God, where he presents excuse after excuse rather than simply accepting God's call.

## 1. Who Am I? (3:11–22)

After 80 years of slavery in Egypt, God finally acts. Moses has spent the first 40 years as a prince of Egypt, then fled after killing an Egyptian. The last 40 years he lived as a shepherd in the wilderness, married to Zipporah, Jethro's daughter. Now God appears to Moses in a burning bush.

> 7 The Lord said, "I have indeed seen the misery of my people in Egypt. I have heard them crying out because of their slave drivers, and I am concerned about their suffering. 8 So I have come down to rescue them from the hand of the Egyptians and to bring them up out of that land into a good and spacious land, a land flowing with milk and honey. 9 And now the cry of the Israelites has reached me, and I have seen the way the Egyptians are oppressing them."

God calls Moses directly: "So now, go. I am sending you to Pharaoh to bring my people the Israelites out of Egypt."

But Moses responds with his first excuse:

> 3:11 But Moses said to God, "Who am I that I should go to Pharaoh and bring the Israelites out of Egypt?"

On the surface, this sounds like humility. But something is strange about it. Moses was a prince of Egypt—he knows the royal protocols, has connections with Pharaoh's court, and possesses the freedom the Israelite slaves do not. If not him, who else is qualified?

God's response cuts through the false humility:

> 3:12 And God said, "I will be with you."

This is the core answer: The task is not about Moses' abilities—it's about God's presence. God assures him that he will bring the people to worship on that very mountain as confirmation.

But Moses continues, pressing his second concern:

> 3:13 Moses said to God, "Suppose I go to the Israelites and say to them, 'The God of your fathers has sent me to you,' and they ask me, 'What is his name?' Then what shall I tell them?"

Moses is saying: Your presence isn't enough. I need credentials. I need a Name they'll recognize and respect.

God answers by revealing his covenant name:

> 3:14 God said to Moses, "I am who I am. This is what you are to say to the Israelites: 'I am has sent me to you.'"

The name **YHWH** ("I am who I am" or "I will be what I will be") is a non-committal, circular definition. God cannot be defined by or confined to anything else. He is his own definition and source of existence. This name is then translated as **Adonai** ("Lord") in Hebrew tradition.

God also connects this to the patriarchs:

> 3:15 God also said to Moses, "Say to the Israelites, 'The **Lord**, the God of your fathers—the God of Abraham, the God of Isaac and the God of Jacob—**has sent me to you.'**
>
> This is my name forever, the name you shall call me from generation to generation."

This reveals God as both God of the past (the God Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob knew and trusted) and God of the future (the name that will endure forever).

God then outlines his complete plan in remarkable detail:

> 3:16–22 "Go, assemble the elders of Israel and say to them, 'The **Lord**, the God of your fathers appeared to me and said: I have watched over you and have seen what has been done to you in Egypt. 17 And **I have promised** to bring you up out of your misery in Egypt into the land of the Canaanites, Hittites, Amorites, Perizzites, Hivites and Jebusites—a land flowing with milk and honey.'
>
> 18 The elders of Israel will listen to you. Then you and the elders are to go to the king of Egypt and say to him, **'The Lord**, the God of the Hebrews, has met with us. Let us take a three-day journey into the wilderness to offer sacrifices to the Lord our God.' 19 But I know that the king of Egypt will not let you go unless a mighty hand compels him. 20 So I will stretch out my hand and strike the Egyptians with all the wonders that I will perform among them. After that, he will let you go.
>
> 21 And I will make the Egyptians favorably disposed toward this people, so that when you leave you will not go empty-handed. 22 Every woman is to ask her neighbor and any woman living in her house for articles of silver and gold and for clothing, which you will put on your sons and daughters. And so you will plunder the Egyptians."

The incredible detail in God's plan reveals his foreknowledge and sovereignty. He already knows Pharaoh's resistance. He has already planned the plagues. He has foreseen that the Israelites will leave with Egyptian wealth. The repeated emphasis on "the Lord" (YHWH) throughout this passage makes clear: God will do this. It is not about Moses' abilities, intelligence, or charisma. The entire operation depends on God.

### The Application: God-Confident, Not Self-Confident

It is right and proper for Christians to be humble. We live in a narcissistic age where people are raised like little emperors, expecting accolades and attention for their every action. But we are not all gold and silver vessels:

> Colossians 3:12 Therefore, as God's chosen people, holy and dearly loved, clothe yourselves with compassion, kindness, humility, gentleness and patience.
>
> 2 Timothy 2:20 In a large house there are articles not only of gold and silver, but also of wood and clay; some are for special purposes and some for common use.

Most of us are wood and clay—for common use. We are not that talented, not that good. We are nobodies. And that is precisely why we cannot afford the false humility that paralyzes us when God calls.

There is a dangerous third option between arrogance and paralysis: we must be **God-confident**. When faced with a challenge that seems impossible, we acknowledge our limitations but place our trust in God's power, not our own.

When we are called to serve, it is not about us or our confidence—it is about the need God has identified and his power to meet it through us:

> Matthew 11:28–30 "Come to me, all you who are weary and burdened, and I will give you rest. 29 Take my yoke upon you and learn from me, for I am gentle and humble in heart, and you will find rest for your souls. 30 For my yoke is easy and my burden is light."
>
> Philippians 4:13 I can do all this through him who gives me strength.
>
> James 1:5 If any of you lacks wisdom, you should ask God, who gives generously to all without finding fault, and it will be given to you.
>
> 2 Timothy 1:7 For the Spirit God gave us does not make us timid, but gives us power, love and self-discipline.
>
> 1 Peter 4:11 If anyone speaks, they should do so as one who speaks the very words of God. If anyone serves, they should do so with the strength God provides, so that in all things God may be praised through Jesus Christ. To him be the glory and the power for ever and ever. Amen.

The credit remains with God, not with us. When we serve, it is about the greater purpose—meeting the need—and trusting God to supply what we lack. If God has commanded us to do something, he will provide the resources. Why then let fear, worry, or our lack of ability stop us from doing his will?

**The critical question: Who are we really depending on?**

## 2. Who Are You? (4:1–9)

Moses' second objection takes a different form:

> 4:1 Moses answered, "**What if they do not believe me** or listen to me and say, 'The Lord did not appear to you'?"

He has moved past his own inadequacy to a new concern: What if the people won't accept his message? They might demand proof. The name alone may not convince them.

God provides the answer through three miraculous signs:

### The Staff

> 4:2–5 Then the Lord said to him, "What is that in your hand?"
>
> "A staff," he replied.
>
> The Lord said, "Throw it on the ground." Moses threw it on the ground and it became a snake, and he ran from it. 4 Then the Lord said to him, "Reach out your hand and take it by the tail." So Moses reached out and took hold of the snake and it turned back into a staff in his hand. 5 "This," said the Lord, "is so that they may believe that the Lord, the God of their fathers—the God of Abraham, the God of Isaac and the God of Jacob—has appeared to you."

The staff was an ordinary shepherd's tool—something Moses had despised from his days in the wilderness. Yet God transforms it into an instrument of power.

### The Leprous Hand

> 4:6–7 Then the Lord said, "Put your hand inside your cloak." So Moses put his hand into his cloak, and when he took it out, the skin was leprous—it had become as white as snow. 7 "Now put it back into your cloak," he said. So Moses put his hand back into his cloak, and when he took it out, it was restored, like the rest of his flesh.

### The Blood of the Nile

> 4:8–9 Then the Lord said, "If they do not believe you or pay attention to the first sign, they may believe the second. 9 But if they do not believe these two signs or listen to you, take some water from the **Nile** and pour it on the dry ground. The water you take from the river will become blood on the ground."

These signs demonstrate God's authority over creation itself. Whether they represent attacks on Egyptian gods (serpent worship, the Nile's divinity) or prefigure the gospel (serpent destroyed, disease healed by blood and water), they show that ordinary things in Moses' hands become extraordinary when God uses them.

### The Application: Being God's Instrument

Moses himself is like these ordinary objects. A shepherd with a stutter, a fugitive from Egypt—he is nothing special. Yet God will transform him into his instrument:

> 2 Timothy 2:20–21 In a large house there are articles not only of gold and silver, but also of wood and clay; some are for special purposes and some for common use. 21 Those who cleanse themselves from the latter will be instruments for special purposes, made holy, useful to the Master and prepared to do any good work.
>
> Romans 6:13 Do not offer any part of yourself to sin as an instrument of wickedness, but rather offer yourselves to God as those who have been brought from death to life; and offer every part of yourself to him as an **instrument of righteousness.**
>
> 1 Peter 2:9 But you are a chosen people, a royal priesthood, a holy nation, **God's special possession,** that you may declare the praises of him who called you out of darkness into his wonderful light.

God does not accomplish his work apart from us, but through us. He takes what we have—our gifts, our weaknesses, our very selves—and transforms us into his instruments.

Many of us struggle with a fixed mindset. We say: "I can only do this ministry" or "I cannot do that, it's too hard." We become inflexible and paralyzed. But God calls us to a growth mindset—to be willing to be stretched and shaped:

> 2 Peter 3:18 Grow in the grace and knowledge of our Lord and Savior Jesus Christ.
>
> Romans 12:2 Be transformed by the renewing of your minds.

Christianity is fundamentally a growth mindset. When faced with legitimate struggles—fear about children falling away, financial anxiety, difficulty finding a spouse—we cannot remain defeated. We must acknowledge the real challenge and then take practical steps forward: teach and trust God with the results, explore job opportunities, leverage networks and modern tools for connection. We must be willing to be challenged and stretched by God.

## 3. Who Am I? (4:10–17)

After God provides the signs, Moses circles back to his original concern—now more desperate:

> 4:10 Moses said to the Lord, "Pardon your servant, Lord. I have never been eloquent, neither in the past nor since you have spoken to your servant. I am slow of speech and tongue."

Some scholars suggest Moses had a stutter. But the real issue is deeper than his speaking ability. God has already answered every objection:

- He promised his presence
- He revealed his name
- He provided miraculous signs
- The words to say are already given (3:14–17)
- Moses doesn't even need words to perform the signs

God responds with exasperation:

> 4:11–12 The Lord said to him, "Who gave human beings their mouths? Who makes them deaf or mute? Who gives them sight or makes them blind? Is it not I, the Lord? 12 Now go; I will help you speak and will teach you what to say."

God is the creator of speech itself. He can make a donkey speak if he chooses. The objection is absurd.

### The Heart of the Matter

But Moses gives one final answer that reveals everything:

> 4:13 But Moses said, "Pardon your servant, Lord. **Please send someone else.**"

After all of God's assurances, solutions, and provisions, Moses' true heart emerges: **He simply does not want to go.**

The excuses were never really about ability or resources. They were a time-delay tactic—a drawn-out refusal. God has called his bluff. And what comes out when God squeezes Moses' heart is not humility but stubborn rebellion and lack of trust.

> 4:14 Then the Lord's anger burned against Moses and he said, "What about your brother, Aaron the Levite? I know he can speak well. He is already on his way to meet you, and he will be glad to see you. 15 You shall speak to him and put words in his mouth; I will help both of you speak and will teach you what to do. 16 He will speak to the people for you, and it will be as if he were your mouth and as if you were God to him. 17 But take this staff in your hand so you can perform the signs with it."

God is genuinely angry. He grants Moses his wish to avoid the primary role, but Moses must still go. He will have Aaron's help, but there are consequences to his disobedience and lack of faith.

This is strikingly different from Jesus' response to his own calling:

> Mark 14:32–36 They went to a place called Gethsemane, and Jesus said to his disciples, "Sit here while I pray." 33 He took Peter, James and John along with him, and he began to be deeply distressed and troubled. 34 "My soul is overwhelmed with sorrow to the point of death," he said to them. 35 Going a little farther, he fell to the ground and prayed that if possible the hour might pass from him. 36 "Abba, Father," he said, "everything is possible for you. Take this cup from me. Yet not what I will, but what you will."

Jesus does not give excuses. He prays honestly about his anguish—he asks for another way—but ultimately submits: "Not what I will, but what you will." He is willing, even unto death.

### The Application: Willingness and Growth Through Challenge

Moses' story offers us both warning and encouragement. He had real reasons for hesitation—past trauma from his time in Egypt, rejection from the Israelites, years of exile. Our own mental hurdles and past pain are real too. Growth takes time.

But the goal is to grow past these obstacles. We address them one step at a time, encouraged by progress, taking courage for future challenges. In fact, **facing difficulty is how we grow**. Moses still goes forward despite his resistance. He receives Aaron's help but doesn't escape the call. His life becomes the proof of God's faithfulness.

The key is willingness. We may not feel like obeying. We may have legitimate fears. But we obey anyway, trusting that God will enable us:

> Acts 7:20–22 At that time Moses was born, and he was no ordinary child. For three months he was cared for by his family. 21 When he was placed outside, Pharaoh's daughter took him and brought him up as her own son. 22 Moses was educated in all the wisdom of the Egyptians and was powerful in speech and action.

Despite his initial reluctance, Moses became the great deliverer God intended. **You just have to be willing.**

## The Contrast: The Unwilling Saviour and the Willing Saviour

Moses is the great figure of Scripture, but his character is sobering. Four times he refuses God's call. Four times he makes excuses. God offers solutions, but excuses remain excuses. The greatest mediator in Israel's history is fundamentally reluctant.

This leaves us without hope in ourselves. Today we are enslaved to sin and its consequences—a far worse slavery than Egypt. We cannot rely on an unwilling saviour.

But we have another Saviour: **Jesus, the willing one.**

Where Moses resisted, Jesus submitted. Where Moses gave excuses, Jesus embraced his mission. Where Moses was angry with God, Jesus loved us enough to die for us. Jesus did not come to Pharaoh but to the cross. He faced not plagues but hell itself. And he did it willingly, out of love:

> Mark 14:36 "Abba, Father," he said, "everything is possible for you. Take this cup from me. Yet not what I will, but what you will."

The difference is everything. We do not earn salvation through our willingness or our obedience. All we must do is trust him. If we do not yet trust him, now is the time.

But understand: becoming a Christian does not make us like Jesus in our willingness. We will continue to make excuses. We will break appointments with God. We will be unfaithful. When God says "don't be selfish," we often are. When he says "love one another," we hold back.

But that is why we need Jesus. The willing Saviour paid the price for our unwillingness. He did what Moses could not: he said yes with his whole heart, and in so doing, he saved us from our sins.

The question for us is simple: Will we trust him?
