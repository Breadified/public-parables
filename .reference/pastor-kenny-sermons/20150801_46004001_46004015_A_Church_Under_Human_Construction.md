# 1 Corinthians 4:1-15

**Title:** A Church Under Human Construction
**Date:** 9 August 2015
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction

Today marks a significant milestone—exactly twelve years of marriage for my wife and me. We've journeyed through life together, and I want to address some feedback I've received about these teachings. But first, let me set the context for what we're exploring in 1 Corinthians 4.

The Corinthian church faced a fundamental problem: they were divided over leaders. Some followed Paul, others Apollos, still others Peter. Paul's response cuts to the heart of what the church actually is. The priority isn't building personality cults around leaders—it's building Christ's church. There may be many leaders, but there is only one building. Leaders are replaceable. They are merely servants.

## 1. Servants of Christ (4:1a)

> This, then, is how you ought to regard us: as **servants of Christ**

Paul begins with humility. Yes, he planted the church in Corinth. Yes, he holds apostolic authority. But his first self-designation is "servant." The Corinthians must understand: leadership in God's kingdom means servanthood, not dominion.

This creates a real tension. Throughout history, we've seen both extremes fail:

- **On one hand:** Leaders with unchecked power become domineering, leading people astray, inflating their own importance
- **On the other hand:** Communities without any leadership structure descend into directionless chaos and mob rule

John Huss, a 15th-century priest, refused to submit to papal authority despite intense pressure. He was burned at the stake in 1415, singing hymns as the flames consumed him. The religious leaders feared that honoring individual conscience would create anarchy. Yet his martyrdom sparked the Reformation a century later.

The Anabaptists tried radical democracy in the church—rejecting hierarchical leadership entirely. In Münster in 1534-35, this led to the very chaos the authorities feared: temporary abandonment of their pacifism and descent into violent mob rule.

The question Paul addresses is unavoidable: How do we have leaders without tyranny? How do we avoid anarchy without authoritarianism?

**Application:** There is no room for arrogance or ego in church leaders. There is no room for followers to develop personality cults around them. Every Christian should benefit from multiple leaders, never just one. There are no spiritual gurus in God's economy.

> 4:6 Now, brothers and sisters, I have applied these things to myself and Apollos for your benefit, so that you may learn from us the meaning of the saying, "Do not go beyond what is written." Then you will not be **puffed up in being a follower of one of us over against the other.** 7 For who makes you different from anyone else? What do you have that you did not receive? And if you did receive it, why do you boast as though you did not?

## 2. Those Entrusted (4:1b)

> This, then, is how you ought to regard us: as servants of Christ **and as those entrusted with the mysteries God has revealed.**

Paul is not just a servant—he is an *entrusted* servant. This word "mysteries" doesn't mean something mysterious or unknowable. It refers to the gospel itself—truths that were hidden for ages but are now revealed through Christ.

> Romans 16:25 Now to him who is able to establish you in accordance with my gospel, the message I proclaim about Jesus Christ, in keeping with the revelation of the **mystery hidden** for long ages past

> 1 Corinthians 2:7 No, we declare God's wisdom, a **mystery** that has been **hidden** and that God destined for our glory before time began.

> 1 Timothy 3:16 Beyond all question, the **mystery** from which true godliness springs is great: He appeared in the flesh, was vindicated by the Spirit, was seen by angels, was preached among the nations, was believed on in the world, was taken up in glory.

We didn't know that the adopted son of a carpenter would be God's Son. We didn't know that the cross—a symbol of terror and torture—would become the means of salvation. We didn't know that God would simultaneously fulfill both justice and mercy at the cross. We didn't know the gospel would reach not just Jews but all nations. These were genuine mysteries, now revealed through Scripture.

Paul was entrusted with this gospel after meeting the risen Christ on the Damascus Road. He became the apostle to the Gentiles. He is important not because Paul himself is special, but because the message he carries is infinitely valuable.

> 2 Now it is required that those who have been **given a trust must prove faithful**.

The messenger is important only because of the message. Think of it like drinking bottled water. You appreciate the bottle because of what's inside, then you throw the empty container away. The vessel matters only for its contents.

> 4:3 I **care very little** if I am judged by you or by any human court; indeed, I do not even judge myself.

**Paul is accountable not to the Corinthians—he is accountable to God.**

> 4:4 My conscience is clear, but that does not make me innocent. **It is the Lord who judges me.** 5 Therefore judge nothing before the appointed time; **wait until the Lord comes.** He will bring to light what is hidden in darkness and will expose the motives of the heart. At that time each will receive their praise from God.

Paul's conscience is clear because he is faithful to his trust. He is trying to fulfill his duties and speak truth. But he makes no claims to perfection. Only God has the authority to make the final judgment.

This principle extends beyond Paul. He hints at this in chapter 3:

> 3:10 By the grace God has given me, **I laid a foundation as a wise builder,** and **someone else** is building on it. But **each one** should build with care. 11 For no one can lay any foundation other than the one already laid, which is Jesus Christ. 12 **If anyone** builds on this foundation using gold, silver, costly stones, wood, hay or straw, 13 their work will be shown for what it is, because the Day will bring it to light. It will be revealed with fire, and the fire will test the quality of **each person's work.** 14 If what has been built survives, the builder will receive a reward. 15 If it is burned up, the builder will suffer loss but yet will be saved—even though only as one escaping through the flames.

Paul laid the foundation in Scripture. But others will build on it. Each builder will be judged by the quality of their work. The "gold, silver, costly stones" survive the fire of God's judgment. The "wood, hay, or straw" burn away. Some churches last only a generation because they're built poorly. Others endure because they're built on solid theological foundations.

The Anglican tradition represents over 1,400 years of Christian witness. It has come to us. If we build well, it will pass to the next generation. If we fail, it ends—as has happened in America and even in England, Anglicanism's birthplace.

This is the weight of stewardship. We are accountable ultimately to God for what we do with the gospel entrusted to us. We may have accountability structures and advisors, but our deepest allegiance is to the one who judges all things.

## 3. Imitate Me (4:15-21)

> 4:15 Even if you had **ten thousand guardians** in Christ, you do not have many fathers, for in Christ Jesus **I became your father** through the gospel. 16 Therefore I urge you **to imitate me.** 17 For this reason I have sent to you Timothy, my son whom I love, who is faithful in the Lord.

Paul shifts from mere servanthood to fatherhood. This is a paternal relationship born through the gospel. He's asking the Corinthians to follow his example, to imitate his way of life in Christ.

> 1 Corinthians 11:1 Follow my example, as I follow the example of Christ.

Timothy will reinforce Paul's teaching and, if necessary, rebuke them. But Timothy will also remind them of Paul's way of life—his character, his consistency, his sacrifice. Paul's life validates his message.

> v.17 He will remind you of my way of life in Christ Jesus, which agrees with what I teach everywhere in every church. 18 Some of you have become **arrogant**, as if I were not coming to you. 19 But I will come to you very soon, if the Lord is willing, and then I will find out not only how these arrogant people are talking, but what power they have. 20 For the kingdom of God is not a matter of talk but of power. 21 What do you prefer? Shall I come to you with **a rod of discipline**, or shall I come in love and with a gentle spirit?

> 4:8 Already you **have all you want!** Already **you have become rich!** You have begun to **reign**—and that without us! How I wish that you really had begun to reign so that we also might reign with you!

Paul's tone is sharp here. The Corinthians act as though they've outgrown him, that they've already reached spiritual maturity and don't need his correction. The kingdom isn't advanced through mere talk—it requires power, and that power comes through suffering, faithfulness, and sacrifice.

> 9 For it seems to me that God has put us apostles on display at the end of the procession, like those condemned to die in the arena. We have been made a **spectacle** to the whole universe, to angels as well as to human beings. 10 We are fools for Christ, but you are so wise in Christ! We are **weak**, but you are strong! You are honored, we are **dishonored!** 11 To this very hour we go **hungry and thirsty**, we are in **rags**, we are brutally **treated**, we are **homeless**. 12 We **work hard with our own hands.** When we **are cursed, we bless; when we are persecuted, we endure it; 13 when we are slandered, we answer kindly.** We have become the **scum** of the earth, the **garbage of the world—right up to this moment.** 14 I am writing this not to shame you but to warn you as my dear children.

This is Paul's appeal to character. What reason would he have to lie to them? He's not profiting from them. He works with his own hands. He endures persecution, slander, hunger, homelessness—all for the gospel. His life is a living testimony to his authenticity.

In the Roman arena, condemned prisoners were displayed at the end of processions before being fed to the lions as entertainment. Paul uses this image to describe apostolic suffering—a spectacle to the entire universe, angels and humans witnessing their sacrifice. Yet this is where true power lives. The kingdom advances when faithful leaders endure hardship for truth.

**Application:** I must be honest about my own limitations here. I am not innocent of failures. I have made mistakes in judgment, been unnecessarily divisive, and spoken with the wrong tone at times. But I come before you as one who has genuinely sought to be faithful to the gospel entrusted to us.

I've been married for twelve years, and ten of those have been spent with you. You've been to my home. You know how we live, how we spend our money, how we order our priorities. You're in a position to evaluate whether my life matches my teaching.

### On Being Offended

I want to address something directly. Many of you have given feedback that my tone is harsh or off-putting. I appreciate this feedback, and I want to be thoughtful about how I communicate. My Malaysian background means words sometimes come out fast and loudly in a way that sounds like scolding—it's simply how Malaysian aunties speak to their families. I've surrounded myself with gentle, articulate leaders who can interpret for me.

But I want to be clear about something: **If you're offended, ask yourself whether it's the truth that offends you or merely my tone.**

If it's my tone alone, let's talk about it. I'm not trying to hurt anyone. But if it's the truth of Scripture that offends you, then we have a deeper conversation to have. The word of God is meant to cut, to convict, to challenge. A preacher who never offends anyone isn't faithfully proclaiming God's Word.

I've noticed a troubling trend, especially among younger people: using "I'm offended" as shorthand for "please censor yourself." Disagreement isn't offensive. Walking away from conversation without engagement, swearing in response, refusing dialogue—that's offensive. In healthy communities, we stay and reason together. We develop thick skin. We learn that not liking an idea doesn't give us permission to shut down dialogue.

The world wants to silence Christian voices through shame and accusations of insensitivity. But Christian leaders must be entrusted with a message that sometimes requires us to speak unpopular truth. Are we to be politicians, sniffing the wind and pandering to polls? Or are we servants of Christ, accountable to him first?

The secular world speaks with confidence about materialism, consumerism, atheism, individualism—promoting these ideas aggressively. But when Christians speak with equal conviction about biblical truth, we're told to be measured, balanced, and inoffensive. This is a double standard designed to silence us.

### A Call to Leadership

You are not ordinary people. You come to church each week to think deeply about theology, history, and philosophy. You gather to critique worldviews, consider solutions, examine ancient texts with critical faculties. Which of your friends do this things regularly? Which of your atheist friends attend weekly gatherings to develop wisdom? You develop arguments through disagreement. You learn humility before God. **You are leaders of wisdom to the world.**

Don't sell yourself short. Don't let your confidence be undermined by the confident opposition, whose position rests on flimsy evidence and inconsistencies. Their confidence is borrowed, not earned.

In a world that's trying to silence speech—that calls clapping offensive and policing words as violence—it's not Christians who are the problem. It's a world that refuses to hear truth, that won't engage in real debate.

We desperately need leaders with conviction. Leaders willing to endure hardship for truth. The kingdom here and in eternity advances only when good people die for the truth.

And in doing so, we build well upon the foundation of Christ.

### The Example of John Huss

Six hundred years ago, on July 6, 1415, a man named John Huss was burned at the stake. He was a learned priest from Prague who loved Scripture and sought to reform the church from within. But when the religious authorities felt threatened, they killed him rather than debate him.

As the flames were being lit, the executioner said mockingly, "Now we will cook the goose" (Huss means goose in Bohemian). Huss replied, "Yes, but there will come an eagle in a hundred years that you will not reach."

That prophecy was fulfilled in Martin Luther, who a century later sparked the Reformation, heavily influenced by Huss's theology and martyrdom. As the official executioner lit the pyre, Huss sang hymns. His ashes were shoveled into a wheelbarrow and dumped into the Rhine. But his death rippled through history.

> "In the truth of the gospel which I have written, taught, and preached, drawing on the sayings and positions of the holy doctors, I am ready to die today."

As the flames consumed him: "Christ, son of the living God, have mercy on us."

His death sparked a movement that changed the world. That's what entrusted leaders are—people willing to suffer, to die, to endure for the truth of the gospel.

> 4:1 This, then, is how you ought to regard us: as servants of Christ **and as those entrusted with the mysteries God has revealed.**

And willing to die for that mystery revealed.

## Reflection and Response

Paul is confident in his conscience. He knows he's faithful to his calling. He will be judged by God, not by the Corinthians or by history's verdict on him. Yet his legacy matters because others will build on his foundation.

Where is Apollos today? We don't know much about him. After 2,000 years, Paul's name endures because he built with gold and silver—solid theological foundations that have lasted. Paul is confident the gospel he preaches is true, clear, and right.

The free market of ideas works. Truth stands. Error burns away like wood and hay.

We need your feedback. Critique helps us sharpen our thinking. But as you critique, ask: Is this true? Is this biblical? Does this serve the gospel? If you can find a way to say the same truth with a gentler tone, I apologize in advance—I should have done so. If you cannot, then what was said needed to be said.

Don't worry about judging me. Judgment awaits us all before the one who sees the motives of the heart. Let that fear of God—not the fear of man—guide how we live, how we lead, and how we build his church.
