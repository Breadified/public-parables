# Acts 3:1–4:32

**Title:** The Greatest Miracle of All
**Date:** November 20, 2013
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Introduction: What Makes a True Miracle?

When I was in primary school, I was following someone carrying a sharp iron rebar at my height, looking at it with curiosity. He suddenly stopped, and I walked straight into it, poking my eye. Miraculously, I was wearing glasses—the rod slid off the lens and merely scratched it. Had I not been wearing them, I likely would have lost my eye. I've never complained about wearing glasses since that day.

Last week we discussed how the apostles miraculously spoke in languages they'd never learned. Today, we encounter another miraculous event—the healing of a crippled beggar at the temple gate. Our question is simple yet profound: What is the purpose of miracles?

---

## A Sign at the Temple (Acts 3:1–10)

### The Setting

Peter and John were going up to the temple at the time of prayer—three in the afternoon. According to Jewish tradition, faithful Jews prayed three times daily: morning, midday, and evening. They were entering the Second Temple, a magnificent structure built by King Herod—one of the great construction projects of the first century. The temple was so enormous that an entire mountainside had to be leveled to create a foundation. It took 46 years to complete and was breathtaking in its beauty. This was the same temple where Jesus had taught, and it would not be destroyed until AD 70 by the Romans.

### The Beggar: A Picture of Contrast

At the temple gate called "Beautiful" sat a man who had been crippled from birth. Every day, his family carried him there to beg. He had never run as a child, never played, never danced—and economically, he was utterly destitute and unemployable. His only survival lay in the mercy of passersby entering the temple courts.

This presents a stark contrast: the most beautiful, most expensive building in Israel stood alongside a neglected, helpless man at its very gates. This wasn't accidental—it was a critique of Israel's leadership.

### God's Vision for Israel

According to Deuteronomy 15:4, there should be no poor among God's people. The land God promised was to be so prosperous—a land of milk and honey—that poverty would be virtually nonexistent. When hardship did strike, fellow Israelites were commanded to be openhanded and generous in helping one another.

But in the first century, this vision had collapsed. Heavy taxes imposed by religious leaders drained the economy to build magnificent temples while the poor were left outside the gates. The blame lay with leaders who prioritized buildings over godliness, and with a people who had neglected God.

This pattern repeats in every generation: when a nation turns from God, social ailments multiply—homelessness, poverty, mental illness, corruption. The sustainable solution is not social programs managed from on high, but repentance and a return to God.

True generosity is not about supporting distant corporations with unaccountable budgets. It's local, personal, and sacrificial. When someone in your community faces hardship, do you offer help? When loneliness afflicts someone you know, do you spend time with them? These are real generosity, real sacrifice—not token gestures.

For this crippled man, such genuine generosity was about to be shown.

### The Healing

When Peter and John approached, the beggar asked for money, his eyes downcast—he'd been looking at legs all morning, not faces. Peter looked straight at him and said, "Look at us!" The man's attention shifted, expecting coins.

But Peter said, "Silver or gold I do not have, but what I have I give you. In the name of Jesus Christ of Nazareth, walk."

At first glance, this seems insufficient. What about his meal? His rent? But Peter and John looked beyond immediate need to permanent transformation. Instead of giving him fish, they gave him a fishing rod. Healing is better than money, because healing enables a man to provide for himself.

Taking him by the right hand, Peter helped him up. Instantly, the man's feet and ankles became strong. He jumped to his feet and began to walk, then jumped again, then walked—his joy overflowing. He went with them into the temple courts, walking, jumping, and praising God.

### The Significance of This Healing

The people recognized him immediately. This was the same man who had sat at the temple gate for 40 years—long enough that the entire community knew his condition intimately. His healing was no fabrication or recent hoax. They were filled with wonder and amazement.

Notice what happened: the man entered the temple courts. According to Leviticus 21, those with physical defects were excluded from the temple—deemed unclean, unworthy to enter God's presence. His healing was not merely physical; it was spiritual. It symbolized forgiveness and restoration. He was now right with God.

## Understanding Miracles: Two Worldviews

### The Diminishing God

One perspective views God as a distant watchmaker. He wound up creation and left it running automatically. Occasionally, to prove He still exists, He intervenes—miracles are His way of stepping in to show He's still boss. In this view, miracles prove God's existence because they violate natural law.

But as our scientific knowledge grows, fewer events seem truly miraculous. Halley's Comet appears every 70 years—now we understand it, so we no longer see God in it. As science explains more, God becomes smaller and smaller. This is the "god of the gaps"—shrinking as our knowledge expands. This is the god portrayed in popular media: primitive, tribal, replaced by modern science.

But this is not the God of Scripture.

### The Present God

The Bible reveals a different God entirely. God has not left us alone. He is intimately, constantly involved in His creation.

Jesus taught:
> Are not two sparrows sold for a penny? Yet not one of them will fall to the ground outside your Father's care. And even the very hairs of your head are all numbered. So don't be afraid; you are worth more than many sparrows. (Matthew 10:29–31)

God providentially cares for creation. Nothing—not even a sparrow, not even a single hair—falls outside His care. If that's true of something as small as a hair, then every part of our body, every aspect of our life, is under His providential watch.

### Miracles in the Present Day

This is where the discussion becomes delicate. Personal experiences are inherently subjective. If someone claims to have seen something miraculous, it's difficult for me to disagree without seeming cynical. When someone shares that they've been healed of cancer, it would be cruel for me to demand proof.

The reality is this: all healing comes from God. Sometimes it's dramatic—remission from cancer. Sometimes it's ordinary—recovering from a cold. If God didn't help us recover from a cold, we'd develop pneumonia and die. All healing, from the smallest bruise to the greatest recovery, comes from God. So does all suffering. He is in charge of both good and bad things.

I'm less concerned with whether we can explain every healing and more concerned with understanding what God is communicating through certain miracles at particular times in history.

In Acts 4:27–28, we read:
> Indeed Herod and Pontius Pilate met together with the Gentiles and the people of Israel in this city to conspire against your holy servant Jesus, whom you anointed. They did what your power and will had decided beforehand should happen.

This is profound: Herod, Pilate, Gentiles, and Israel all conspired against Jesus. Yet God's sovereignty was at work behind the scenes. God is so powerful that He controls every decision without making it obvious. We feel free and are fully responsible—yet we are under God's control. The world seems chaotic, yet God governs it completely. He's not an absent God on autopilot. He's actively breathing life into every moment, determining events, directing every blood cell and every star. The sun doesn't rise automatically each day—He chooses to raise it. And one day, He will stop.

### Signs and Wonders

In this view, miracles are not primarily evidence of God's existence—they're signs and messages from Him. The distinction is crucial. They're less about whether they're explainable and more about what they're communicating.

The Bible calls them "wonders and signs." The term "miraculous signs" simply means signs that happen to be miraculous. Signs point to something. They point to revelation. They point to the fulfillment of prophecy. They're extraordinary events announcing that God has something new to say.

Isaiah 35 tells us what signs to expect in the messianic age:
> Strengthen the feeble hands, steady the knees that give way... Then will the eyes of the blind be opened and the ears of the deaf unstopped. Then will the lame leap like a deer, and the mute tongue shout for joy. (Isaiah 35:3–6)

This healing of the crippled man fulfilled Isaiah's prophecy. It announced that the time of fulfillment had come.

## Revelation from God (Acts 3:11–4:12)

### Peter's Message

The healing created an opportunity for Peter to proclaim God's word. When the crowd gathered in amazement at Solomon's Colonnade, Peter didn't accept their wonder. He redirected it:

> Men of Israel, why does this surprise you? Why do you stare at us as if by our own power or godliness we had made this man walk? (Acts 3:12)

Peter's message centered entirely on Jesus. He reminded them that though they had handed Jesus over to be killed—though they had disowned Him before Pilate, though they demanded a murderer's release instead—God had raised Him from the dead. And it is by faith in Jesus' name that this man stood before them, completely healed.

### The Gospel in Crisis

But there was more. Peter reminded them of what the prophets had foretold: that Christ would suffer. Yet in mercy, God offered them a second chance:

> Repent, then, and turn to God, so that your sins may be wiped out, that times of refreshing may come from the Lord, and that he may send the Christ, who has been appointed for you—even Jesus. (Acts 3:19–20)

This crippled man's healing was a sign pointing to Jesus and the times of refreshing He brings. Peter also spoke to the Gentiles:

> You are heirs of the prophets and of the covenant God made with your fathers. He said to Abraham, "Through your offspring all peoples on earth will be blessed." When God raised up his servant, he sent him first to you to bless you by turning each of you from your wicked ways. (Acts 3:25–26)

The gate was called "Beautiful," but now something more beautiful was revealed. The man's feet were beautiful, for he jumped to his feet and proclaimed God. The gospel was unshackled and would now spread to Judea, Samaria, and to the ends of the earth.

## The True Experience of Christianity (Acts 4)

We often imagine that the Christian life centers on extraordinary experiences—miracles, visions, signs. But Scripture suggests something different. The normal Christian experience involves two things: persecution and fellowship.

### Persecution: The Expected Response

Immediately after Peter and John's proclamation, the religious authorities arrested them. The priests, the captain of the temple guard, and the Sadducees were greatly disturbed because the apostles were teaching about the resurrection of the dead in Jesus' name. That evening, they were thrown in jail.

Yet something remarkable happened: many who heard the message believed. The number of believers grew to about five thousand.

The next day, Peter and John stood before the Sanhedrin—the high priest Annas, Caiaphas, and other members of the ruling council. They were questioned: "By what power or what name did you do this?"

Peter, filled with the Holy Spirit, answered boldly:
> Rulers and elders of the people! If we are being called to account today for an act of kindness shown to a cripple and are asked how he was healed, then know this: It is by the name of Jesus Christ of Nazareth, whom you crucified but whom God raised from the dead, that this man stands before you healed... Salvation is found in no one else, for there is no other name under heaven given to men by which we must be saved. (Acts 4:8–12)

The Sanhedrin was astonished at the courage of these unschooled, ordinary men. They recognized that these men "had been with Jesus." Yet because the healed man stood right there before them, they could say nothing to refute what had happened. The entire city knew it was an outstanding miracle.

But rather than admit the truth, they ordered Peter and John to stop speaking in Jesus' name. Peter and John's response was simple and profound:

> Judge for yourselves whether it is right in God's sight to obey you rather than God. For we cannot help speaking about what we have seen and heard. (Acts 4:19–20)

This is the Christian experience: when you follow Jesus, persecution comes. As Paul wrote, "Everyone who wants to live a godly life in Christ Jesus will be persecuted" (2 Timothy 3:12). The rage of those who oppose God cannot be satisfied by compromise. They rage not against us personally but against our Master. Because they cannot reach Jesus, they attack His people.

Many assume appeasement or compromise will buy peace. But the objective of those who rage against God is relentless: to eliminate Christianity itself. Giving ground on small issues provides the foundation for demanding surrender on larger ones.

### Fellowship: The Sustaining Grace

Yet notice what happened next. After their release, Peter and John returned to their own people and reported everything. When the believers heard, they raised their voices in prayer:

> Sovereign Lord, you made the heaven and the earth and the sea, and everything in them... Why do the nations rage and the peoples plot in vain? The kings of the earth take their stand and the rulers gather together against the Lord and against his Anointed One. (Acts 4:24–26)

They recognized the pattern: the world rages against God, and because it cannot touch Him, it targets His people. Yet they didn't despair. They prayed not for comfort or safety but for boldness to speak God's word:

> Now, Lord, consider their threats and enable your servants to speak your word with great boldness. (Acts 4:29)

The believers responded with courage, unity, and generosity. In Acts 4:32–35, we see the early church's remarkable practice:

> All the believers were together and had everything in common. Selling their possessions and goods, they gave to anyone as he had need. Every day they continued to meet together in the temple courts. They broke bread in their homes and ate together with glad and sincere hearts, praising God and enjoying the favor of all the people.

This was not forced communism but voluntary, joyful generosity born from faith. They understood that their true treasure wasn't material but relational—with God and with one another.

A powerful picture appears at the end of Acts 4. Joseph, a Levite from Cyprus (whom the apostles called Barnabas, meaning "Son of Encouragement"), sold a field he owned and brought the money and laid it at the apostles' feet. Here was someone from the temple establishment—a Levite who traditionally had no property—now repenting and giving generously. It was a marvelous reversal of the waste and neglect represented by Herod's temple.

## Application: The Gospel as the Answer

### The True Miracle: Salvation

We don't need spectacular healings to prove God exists. The sign has been given. Jesus rose from the dead, and that's the sign upon which all faith rests. We don't need another revelation after that.

The true miracle of Christianity is not physical healing but spiritual transformation—the forgiveness of sins, reconciliation with God, the assurance of salvation. This is the greatest miracle of all: that God, through Christ, has made a way for sinful humans to be restored to right relationship with Him.

### The Gospel Changes Everything

The gospel generates the motivation necessary to live as God intends. Many fall into legalism, thinking that obedience to God's law doesn't work. But the gospel transforms us. When we understand that we're forgiven, that we're loved despite our failures, that we're part of God's eternal family—this understanding produces the kind of motivation that mere rules can never generate.

In Peter's second speech (Acts 2:23–36), he proclaimed:
> This Jesus, whom you crucified, God has raised from the dead, and we are all witnesses of this. Exalted to the right hand of God, he has received from the Father the promised Holy Spirit and has poured out what you now see and hear. Therefore let all Israel be assured of this: God has made this Jesus, whom you crucified, both Lord and Christ.

When people heard this, they were "cut to the heart." Peter called them to repent and be baptized in the name of Jesus for the forgiveness of their sins, and they would receive the gift of the Holy Spirit. Three thousand believed that day.

### The Spirit's Indwelling Presence

It's important to distinguish between the baptism of the Spirit and the indwelling of the Spirit. The baptism was the dramatic outpouring at Pentecost—a one-time, unrepeatable event that announced the arrival of the eschatological age, the fulfillment of John the Baptist's prophecy and Joel's promise.

But the indwelling of the Spirit is different. When people are converted, they receive the indwelling Spirit. At times throughout Acts, believers are "filled with the Spirit"—not a loss and refilling of something they lack, but a temporary empowering for bold speech. Peter was "filled with the Spirit" when standing before the Sanhedrin. The church was "filled with the Spirit" when they prayed together. Paul was "filled with the Spirit" at crucial moments of proclamation.

The filling of the Spirit is always connected to fearless speech—the ability to proclaim God's word boldly in the face of opposition.

### Why Pentecost Mattered

Pentecost was not arbitrary. In the Old Testament, it was the feast celebrating the giving of the Law on Mount Sinai. God's law was given to the people of God at that festival. Now, on the same festival, God pours out His Spirit to write His law on human hearts.

Jeremiah and Ezekiel prophesied that in the new covenant, God would write His law on people's hearts rather than on stone tablets. Hebrews 10:16 captures this:
> This is the covenant I will make with them after that time, says the Lord. I will put my laws in their hearts, and I will write them on their minds.

The difference between the Old and New Covenants is this: the old was external law on stone. The new is the Spirit's internal work on the heart. Only the gospel can generate the motivation to obey. Law alone produces guilt and failure. But the gospel—the message that we're forgiven, loved, and restored through Christ—produces the kind of grateful response that makes obedience not a burden but a joy.

---

## Conclusion: Have We Been Forgiven?

The greatest miracle is not the healing of a crippled beggar, remarkable though that was. The greatest miracle is the forgiveness of sins. It is the assurance that despite our failures, our guilt, our brokenness, God has made a way through Christ for us to be completely restored.

This is the message that Peter proclaimed. This is what the early church understood. This is what gave them courage to face persecution, generosity to share all they had, and joy that no opposition could diminish.

The crippled man's feet became beautiful because he stood and proclaimed God. Our lives become beautiful when we, too, stand and proclaim the One who has made us whole.
