# Luke 16:1-18

**Title:** A Wasted Opportunity  
**Date:** April 6, 2008  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction

I hate waste. It grieves me—wasted electricity, unnecessary plastic wrapping, discarded containers. Our parents have trained us well to honor resources, to seize every opportunity and ability to reach our goals. Yet the Bible shows us another story of waste, one with spiritual weight.

## 1. A Story of Waste (Luke 16:1-8a)

Jesus begins with a parable that immediately troubles us.

> "There was a rich man whose manager was accused of wasting his possessions. So he called him in and asked him, 'What is this I hear about you? Give an account of your management, because you cannot be manager any longer.'" (Luke 16:1-2)

The manager, facing dismissal, realizes his desperate situation. He's not strong enough for physical labor, and his pride forbids begging. So he devises a plan:

> "I know what I'll do so that, when I lose my job here, people will welcome me into their houses." (Luke 16:3-4)

What follows is audacious. The manager calls in his master's debtors and systematically reduces their obligations. To one who owes eight hundred gallons of olive oil, he says, "Make it four hundred." To another owing a thousand bushels of wheat, he instructs, "Make it eight hundred."

Then comes the shocking conclusion:

> "The master commended the dishonest manager because he had acted shrewdly." (Luke 16:8a)

### The Ethical Problem

This troubles us immediately. The master commends dishonesty? And Jesus will echo this approval in verse 9? This seems to violate everything the New Testament teaches about integrity.

Yet look closely at verse 8: the master commended him *because he had acted shrewdly*—not because he had acted dishonestly. The focus is on his cleverness, his resourcefulness in a desperate situation.

### Understanding Shrewdness

Shrewdness is something many of us understand deeply, especially those from migrant backgrounds. When your parents arrived in a new country unable to speak the language, their savings halved by exchange rates, they faced choices: give up, or become ingenious. They had to work harder, think smarter, maximize every resource and opportunity. They learned to negotiate, to compare unit prices, to find every advantage in the system—not through dishonesty, but through intelligence and determination.

This is what Jesus commends: using your mind, your position, your resources wisely. The manager didn't waste away in self-pity. He used his wits to secure his future. The point is clear: **Don't waste your opportunities. Use your resources wisely.**

## 2. Wasted Opportunities Reveal Your Ultimate Priorities (Luke 16:8b-13)

But Jesus moves beyond mere worldly shrewdness. He's pointing to something far deeper—the kingdom of God dimension. Our approach to earthly wealth reveals what we truly value.

> "For the people of this world are more shrewd in dealing with their own kind than are the people of the light. I tell you, use worldly wealth to gain friends for yourselves, so that when it is gone, you will be welcomed into eternal dwellings." (Luke 16:8b-9)

Notice the progression of Jesus's teaching:

> "Whoever can be trusted with very little can also be trusted with much, and whoever is dishonest with very little will also be dishonest with much. So if you have not been trustworthy in handling worldly wealth, who will trust you with true riches? And if you have not been trustworthy with someone else's property, who will give you property of your own?" (Luke 16:10-12)

Stewardship is the key. How we handle earthly resources—money, possessions, opportunities—is a test. It reveals whether we're trustworthy with eternal realities.

And then the ultimate statement:

> "No servant can serve two masters. Either he will hate the one and love the other, or he will be devoted to the one and despise the other. You cannot serve both God and Money." (Luke 16:13)

Money (Mammon in Aramaic—personified wealth) becomes a rival god. When you trace where your money flows, you see your true priorities. Do you spend for eternal purposes or temporal ones? That choice reveals your heart.

## 3. The Pharisees Wasted Their Opportunity (Luke 16:14-18)

Now Jesus's target becomes clear. Though he addressed his disciples, he was speaking to those listening in the shadows—the Pharisees.

> "The Pharisees, who loved money, heard all this and were sneering at Jesus." (Luke 16:14)

This seems paradoxical. The Pharisees were meticulous about the temple tax, tithing even their herbs. Money itself wasn't their failure. Their failure was something far graver.

### The Privilege They Squandered

The Pharisees—the leaders of Israel, inheritors of God's covenant—occupied a unique position. Through the prophets, God had declared:

> "I, the Lord, have called you in righteousness... I will keep you and will make you to be a covenant for the people and a light for the Gentiles." (Isaiah 42:6)

Israel was meant to be a light to the nations, a beacon of God's goodness. In the last days, the Gentiles would stream into Jerusalem, drawn by the radiance of God's people. This was Israel's calling—to bless the nations, to proclaim God's glory.

But Israel failed. The nation sank into the same political strife and idolatry as any other kingdom. They became no different from the world around them.

### Their Continued Blindness

And now, when Jesus himself walks among the Gentiles—the unclean, the sinners, the tax collectors, the women of the street—the Pharisees react with horror. "Why do you associate with such people?"

Jesus's response cuts deep:

> "You are the ones who justify yourselves in the eyes of men, but God knows your hearts. What is highly valued among men is detestable in God's sight." (Luke 16:15)

The Pharisees valued their own ritual purity and their reputation. They had forgotten why they were given privilege in the first place. They had received the Law and the Prophets, all pointing toward the Messiah and his mercy. But they failed to see it. They failed to pass it on.

### The Turning Point

> "The Law and the Prophets were proclaimed until John. Since that time, the good news of the kingdom of God is being preached, and everyone is forcing his way into it." (Luke 16:16)

The kingdom is now open to all. The unclean, the outsiders, the Gentiles—they are flooding in. But the privileged ones, the children of Israel who sat at God's table, refuse to enter. Their pride is too great. Their eyes too blind.

And Jesus adds, almost mournfully:

> "It is easier for heaven and earth to disappear than for the least stroke of a pen to drop out of the Law." (Luke 16:17)

The Law still stands. It still points to redemption. But they won't see it.

His final words about divorce echo this theme of broken covenant:

> "Anyone who divorces his wife and marries another woman commits adultery, and the man who marries a divorced woman commits adultery." (Luke 16:18)

It's a rebuke: the Pharisees have abandoned their first wife—God's Law—for a second wife, Mammon. Without even the decency of divorce, they've turned away from their calling.

## 4. Application: Don't Waste Your Opportunity

### A Broader Calling

As we consider how this applies to us, we must remember our different position. We are Gentiles, not the covenant people. We are the ones who didn't reject Jesus; we are in the kingdom. Yet the principle remains: **don't waste your opportunities.**

When you stand before God in eternity, you'll regret the chances you didn't take. The friend you didn't invite to hear the gospel because life got busy. The conversation you didn't have. You cared, but you were careless. You wasted the opportunity.

Looking at our community here in the Hills, I see a harvest field. I've considered what tools I have. I'm not particularly popular or athletic, and yes, my accent might seem unusual to some. But I'm one thing: I'm Asian, nerdy, I wear glasses. I can reach out to other Asian students—the study-focused ones who might listen to someone who understands their culture and their pressures.

I wonder if you'll join me. I see thirty Asians here who understand Asian culture, who speak the language, whose parents sound like me, and who have Asian friends who don't yet know Jesus. Some of you might feel awkward being Asian, but think about it: being Asian might be your greatest advantage for the kingdom's sake.

Of course, we all have different gifts. Different personalities. Different opportunities—where you live, the sports you love, the school you attend, the interests you share. **Don't waste them.**

### A Direct Challenge

There's a powerful image in Scripture: the privileged children of Israel sat at God's table but rejected the bread. They threw it to the floor. The dogs—the very Gentiles they despised—lapped it up and were satisfied.

The Jews wasted their opportunity to declare God's wonderful works to the nations. Because of this refusal, that privilege was taken from them.

But there is One who didn't waste his opportunity. The true Jew, the true Israelite in whom there is no deceit—Jesus himself—took that calling seriously. He went to the Gentiles of Galilee and proclaimed the year of the Lord's favor. He healed the blind, made the lame walk, brought comfort to the outcast. He became the light to the Gentiles that Israel refused to be.

**Fellow Gentiles: the kingdom stands open for you.** If you haven't entered, don't waste the opportunity of a thousand lifetimes. The Pharisees wasted theirs—they forgot to invite others, then forgot to enter themselves, and missed it altogether. Even when it was obvious that the kingdom had come, they couldn't swallow their pride. What a waste.

Don't let that be you. Don't waste this chance.
