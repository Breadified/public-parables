# Daniel 7:1-28

**Title:** The Son of Man
**Date:** 24 July 2011
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Introduction: The World as a Zoo

The world is a zoo—a place where wild, untamed forces roam. We've been journeying through the Book of Daniel, one chapter each week, and we've now reached chapter 7, a pivotal turning point in this remarkable book.

Chapter 7 marks several significant shifts:
- It concludes the Aramaic section (continuing through verse 28)—God's message to the international community
- From here onward, the language becomes increasingly strange and visionary
- The chronology breaks from the previous narrative structure—we're no longer following a straight timeline

Notice the timestamp in verse 1: we've jumped back to the first year of Belshazzar's reign, roughly ten years before the events of chapter 5.

---

## Part 1: Kingdoms Are Beastly (Verses 1-8)

### The Dream Interpreter's Dream

> In the first year of Belshazzar king of Babylon, Daniel had a dream, and visions passed through his mind as he was lying in bed. He wrote down the substance of his dream. (verse 1)

It's a remarkable reversal: for six chapters, Daniel has interpreted dreams for kings. Now, finally, Daniel himself receives the vision. It's like a surgeon discovering he has cancer and then undergoing surgery himself—Daniel is suddenly under the microscope.

Like the kings' dreams before it, Daniel's vision contains all the familiar elements: a timestamp, strange imagery, and a troubled response. Just as Belshazzar's face turned pale at the writing on the wall, Daniel's experience will shake him deeply.

### Four Beasts from Chaos

> Daniel said: In my vision at night I looked, and there before me were the four winds of heaven churning up the great sea. Four great beasts, each different from the others, came up out of the sea. (verses 2-3)

The imagery is striking. Four winds churning the great sea—imagine the chaos of a hurricane multiplied fourfold, a collision of forces creating turmoil. Out of this primordial chaos, four beasts emerge. But notice: they rise from the sea, not the land. This is significant.

The sea represents chaos, evil, and the place of unknown danger. In Scripture, the sea is where Godzilla comes from, where civilization gives way to forces we cannot control. This is why Revelation describes the redeemed creation as having no sea—not because we can't surf, but because there will be no more chaos, no more evil rising from the depths.

### The Four Kingdoms

> The four great beasts are four kings that will rise from the earth. (verse 17)

These beasts represent actual kingdoms that will dominate the world. Similar to Nebuchadnezzar's dream of the statue with its four layers, Daniel's vision reveals four successive kingdoms—each different, each powerful, each threatening.

**The First Beast—Lion with Eagles' Wings (Babylon)**

> The first was like a lion, and it had the wings of an eagle. I watched until its wings were torn off and it was lifted from the ground so that it stood on two feet like a human being, and the mind of a human was given to it. (verse 4)

The lion represents Babylon, symbolized throughout their culture—on the Ishtar Gate, in their art, in their very identity. The eagle's wings speak of swift dominion. But the vision shows the wings being torn away. This recalls Nebuchadnezzar's humiliation:

> He was driven away from people and ate grass like the ox. His body was drenched with the dew of heaven until his hair grew like the feathers of an eagle and his nails like the claws of a bird. (Daniel 4:33)

The beast is eventually conquered by the next power.

**The Second Beast—Bear with Ribs in Its Teeth (Medes and Persia)**

> And there before me was a second beast, which looked like a bear. It was raised up on one of its sides, and it had three ribs in its mouth between its teeth. It was told, Get up and eat your fill of flesh! (verse 5)

The bear, raised up on one side, represents the Medes and Persians. Cyrus, who would lead the Achaemenid empire, was part Mede and three-quarters Persian. The three ribs in its teeth symbolize conquered nations—Egypt, Lydia, and Babylon itself. The command to devour speaks of conquest and subjugation.

**The Third Beast—Leopard with Four Wings and Four Heads (Greece)**

> After that, I looked, and there before me was another beast, one that looked like a leopard. And on its back it had four wings like those of a bird. This beast had four heads, and it was given authority to rule. (verse 6)

The leopard is swift and agile—perfectly suited to represent Greece under Alexander the Great. At eighteen, Alexander began consolidating the Greek city-states. By 334 BC, with only 32,000 infantry and 5,100 cavalry, he crossed into Asia Minor. Remarkably, by 323 BC—when he died at just thirty-two years old—he had conquered the known world. He even died in the palace of Nebuchadnezzar II in Babylon itself. That's the speed of a leopard with wings.

The four heads represent what happened after Alexander's death: with no proper succession plan, his four generals divided the kingdom among themselves.

**The Fourth Beast—The Terrifying Unknown (verses 7-8)**

> After that, in my vision at night I looked, and there before me was a fourth beast—terrifying and frightening and very powerful. It had large iron teeth; it crushed and devoured its victims and trampled underfoot whatever was left. It was different from all the former beasts, and it had ten horns. While I was thinking about the horns, there before me was another horn, a little one, which came up among them; and three of the first horns were uprooted before it. This horn had eyes like the eyes of a human being and a mouth that spoke boastfully. (verses 7-8)

This fourth beast is unique—no earthly animal can describe it. It's more terrifying than all the others, with iron teeth and overwhelming power. It crushes, devours, and tramples with absolute dominion.

The ten horns represent ten kings—symbols of power and authority. But then a little horn arises, speaking boastfully, and uproots three of the first horns to establish its own power.

### What Do These Beasts Represent?

Scholars have proposed several interpretations:

**Option 1: The Seleucid Dynasty** - Some scholars identify the fourth beast with the Seleucid kings, particularly viewing the ten horns as Seleucid successors, with the little horn being Antiochus IV Epiphanes. History records that Antiochus IV did indeed remove three rivals (Heliodorus, Demetrius, and his young cousin Antiochus) to secure his throne. Notably, he later committed a terrible sacrilege in Jerusalem.

**Option 2: The Roman Empire** - Others identify the fourth beast as Rome, which dominated the ancient world for over 500 years in the West and another thousand years in the East until Constantinople fell. Rome's iron tools of war—chariots, shields, swords—conquered territories from Britain to the Middle East, from modern Russia to Africa.

### Why These Interpretations Matter

The specific identities matter in one way: Christians should carefully study history, archaeology, and prophecy. Serious scholarship is valuable. We shouldn't ridicule those who work through these questions, even if some theories seem speculative. 

Why? Because Christianity is founded on history and truth. The Bible is not vague, feel-good fiction. When God gives prophecy, it means something. The ancient Jews didn't include books in the Hebrew Scriptures arbitrarily—they included those that proved true over time. Daniel was recognized as prophetic precisely because it was proven accurate. We don't put fake news in the Bible.

**But in another way, the specifics don't matter as much.** The big picture is what matters: these are real, powerful kingdoms that rise and attack people—especially God's people. Whether we're talking about Babylon, Rome, or any other tyranny, the pattern is the same.

### The Evil Origin of Kingdoms

> In my vision at night I looked, and there before me were the four winds of heaven churning up the great sea. (verse 2)

Notice where these beasts come from: out of chaos and evil. Their origin is in turmoil, in forces opposed to order and goodness. Thomas Hobbes captured this in his image of the Leviathan—that powerful, fearsome beast of chaos. These kingdoms, rising from the sea of chaos, are inherently beastly.

**All kingdoms have the potential for great evil.** History demonstrates this soberly. Democide—deaths caused by governments—far exceeds deaths from plague or war. One researcher, after studying 8,000 reports of government-caused deaths, estimated 262 million victims of democide in the last century—six times more than battlefield deaths. Even compared to the Great Plague, which killed 25 million, government-inflicted deaths dwarf it.

This is why our forebears—Christians influenced by Scripture—were deeply suspicious of concentrated power. Over a thousand years, believers deliberately weakened government:
- Multiple tiers of government
- Separation of powers
- Democratic checks and balances

Even in America, this distrust is enshrined: citizens are armed precisely so they might one day defend themselves against their own government if necessary.

**Application for today:** We must not naively trust government to do the right thing. We must be vigilant. We must push back against injustice while we still can. As Australia departs from its Christian roots, government will increasingly become beastly—increasingly corrupt, violent, and destructive.

## Part 2: The Ancient of Days (Verses 9-11)

But the vision doesn't end with the beasts. There is an alternate vision—a counter-narrative that brings hope.

> As I looked, thrones were set in place, and the Ancient of Days took his seat. His clothing was as white as snow; the hair of his head was white like wool. His throne was flaming with fire, and its wheels were all ablaze. A river of fire was flowing, coming out from before him. Thousands upon thousands attended him; ten thousand times ten thousand stood before him. The court was seated, and the books were opened. (verses 9-10)

### God's Throne Room

"Ancient of Days" is an Old Testament term for God himself. As Isaiah declares:

> You are my witnesses, declares the Lord, that I am God. Yes, and from ancient days I am he. No one can deliver out of my hand. When I act, who can reverse it? (Isaiah 43:12-13)

God exists before time itself, before the beginning. He is eternal and unchanging. Now Daniel sees Him taking His seat of power—a scene that echoes Ezekiel's vision of the heavenly throne room.

The imagery is overwhelming: white garments like snow, wheels of fire, a river of fire flowing from His presence, multitudes beyond counting—thousands of thousands, ten thousand times ten thousand—attending before Him in reverence and submission.

### The Judgment

The court is seated. The books are opened. God is about to judge.

> Then I continued to watch because of the boastful words the horn was speaking. I kept looking until the beast was slain and its body destroyed and thrown into the blazing fire. The other beasts had been stripped of their authority, but were allowed to live for a period of time. (verses 11-12)

The kingdoms—even the terrible fourth beast—are judged and destroyed. Their authority is stripped away. They are thrown into blazing fire. But this destruction doesn't happen by accident or through human effort. It happens through God's chosen instrument.

## Part 3: The Son of Man (Verses 13-14)

> In my vision at night I looked, and there before me was one like a son of man, coming with the clouds of heaven. He approached the Ancient of Days and was led into his presence. He was given authority, glory and sovereign power; all peoples and nations of every language worshiped him. His dominion is an everlasting dominion that will not pass away, and his kingdom is one that will never be destroyed. (verses 13-14)

### A Figure from Heaven

Notice the contrast: where the beasts rose from the sea (chaos), this figure comes with the clouds of heaven. If the sea represents evil, the clouds represent blessing—the place where sustaining rain falls, where the sun shines, where provision comes from above.

"One like a son of man"—a human figure. Not an animal, not a beast, but a man. And remarkably, this figure approaches the presence of God Himself and is led into His very throne room.

### Jesus the Son of Man

This "Son of Man" concept becomes crucial in the New Testament. After Daniel, the next major occurrence of "Son of Man" appears in the Gospels—and Jesus claims this title for Himself.

> There will be signs in the sun, moon and stars. On the earth, nations will be in anguish and perplexity at the roaring and tossing of the sea. Men will faint from terror, apprehensive of what is coming on the world, for the heavenly bodies will be shaken. At that time they will see the Son of Man coming in a cloud with power and great glory. When these things begin to take place, stand up and lift up your heads, because your redemption is drawing near. (Luke 21:25-28)

This was fulfilled in Acts:

> After he said this, he was taken up before their very eyes, and a cloud hid him from their sight. They were looking intently up into the sky as he was going, when suddenly two men dressed in white stood beside them. "Men of Galilee," they said, "why do you stand here looking into the sky? This same Jesus, who has been taken from you into heaven, will come back in the same way you have seen him go into heaven." (Acts 1:9-11)

Jesus has ascended to power. He has been installed as king at the right hand of the Father. He is our exalted Lord.

**Application:** Is He your king? Obedience to God and disobedience to Jesus are not options—they're the same thing. We cannot separate them. Jesus is God incarnate, and we follow Him as our Lord.

### The Co-Regent

How does the Son of Man relate to the Ancient of Days? The answer is profound: the Son of Man is a co-regent with God.

Just as Belshazzar served as co-regent with his father Nabonidus (who was away in Tema for ten years), the Father rules with the Son. God has appointed a co-regent who shares His authority and glory.

This beautiful mingling of the Son of Man and the Ancient of Days is captured perfectly in Revelation 1:

> I turned around to see the voice that was speaking to me. And when I turned I saw seven golden lampstands, and among the lampstands was someone like a son of man, dressed in a robe reaching down to his feet and with a golden sash around his chest. The hair on his head was white like wool, as white as snow, and his eyes were like blazing fire. His feet were like bronze glowing in a furnace, and his voice was like the sound of rushing waters. In his right hand he held seven stars, and coming out of his mouth was a sharp, double-edged sword. His face was like the sun shining in all its brilliance. (Revelation 1:12-16)

Notice how the description combines attributes: the white hair like wool (from Daniel's Ancient of Days), the eyes like blazing fire, the voice like rushing waters. The risen Jesus bears the marks of both the human and the divine, the Son and the Father.

### The Kingdom That Never Fails

> He was given authority, glory and sovereign power; all peoples and nations of every language worshiped him. His dominion is an everlasting dominion that will not pass away, and his kingdom is one that will never be destroyed. (verse 14)

Unlike earthly kingdoms—which rise and fall, are conquered and destroyed—this kingdom is different. This dominion is everlasting. This kingdom will never be destroyed. All peoples, all nations, all languages will one day worship Him. His rule is absolute and eternal.

### The Holy People Receive the Kingdom

> But the holy people of the Most High will receive the kingdom and will possess it forever—yes, for ever and ever. (verse 18)

And here's the stunning promise: those who belong to God—His holy people—will receive the kingdom with Him. We will possess it forever. Just as the Son of Man receives authority and glory, so too will those who follow Him share in His reign.

Later in the vision, this promise is repeated with even greater clarity:

> As I watched, this horn was waging war against the holy people and defeating them, until the Ancient of Days came and pronounced judgment in favor of the holy people of the Most High, and the time came when they possessed the kingdom. (verses 21-22)

And again:

> The court will sit, and his power will be taken away and completely destroyed forever. Then the sovereignty, power and greatness of all the kingdoms under heaven will be handed over to the holy people of the Most High. His kingdom will be an everlasting kingdom, and all rulers will worship and obey him. (verses 26-27)

## The Struggle with Delay

> This is the end of the matter. I, Daniel, was deeply troubled by my thoughts, and my face turned pale, but I kept the matter to myself. (verse 28)

Daniel is troubled. Why? The answer lies in something the vision revealed but doesn't fully comfort: **delay**.

> The other beasts had been stripped of their authority, but were allowed to live for a period of time. (verse 12)

> He will speak against the Most High and oppress his holy people and try to change the set times and the laws. The holy people will be delivered into his hands for a time, times and half a time. (verse 25)

"A time, times and half a time"—3.5 years, or more broadly, a limited but still significant period. The beasts don't disappear immediately. The oppression continues. There is waiting. There is suffering before triumph.

### Our Fears About Delay

We all know the anxiety of waiting:
- A child forgotten at school, waiting for a ride that doesn't come
- Missing the bus or train, knowing you'll lose a job or miss an exam
- The fear that delay means abandonment

We're afraid of pain. And yet Paul could write: "To me, to live is Christ and to die is gain." How can we learn this peace?

### Jesus on the End Times

Jesus Himself spoke of delay and suffering before the end:

> Nation will rise against nation, and kingdom against kingdom. There will be great earthquakes, famines and pestilences in various places, and fearful events and great signs from heaven. But before all this, they will lay hands on you and persecute you. They will deliver you to synagogues and prisons, and you will be brought before kings and governors, and all on account of my name. This will result in your being witnesses to them. But make up your mind not to worry beforehand how you will defend yourselves. For I will give you words and wisdom that none of your adversaries will be able to resist or contradict. You will be betrayed even by parents, brothers, relatives and friends, and they will put some of you to death. All men will hate you because of me. But not a hair of your head will perish. By standing firm you will gain life. (Luke 21:10-19)

The pattern is clear: persecution comes before triumph. Suffering precedes vindication. But Jesus promises: not a hair of your head will perish. Standing firm, you gain life.

### For the Aramaic Readers

Daniel leaves his readers—the Aramaic-speaking exiles—with both clear warning and comfort. The kingdoms will continue to oppress God's people for a time. But God has not forgotten. The Ancient of Days sits on His throne. The Son of Man will be exalted. And the holy people will ultimately receive the kingdom.

Chapters 8-12 will provide additional comfort and detail for those who struggle with the timeline, with the delay, with the suffering that continues until Christ returns.

## Application: The Beasts Among Us

The world still looks like a zoo. Even now, powerful forces clash and demand our allegiance. Hong Kong, China, and countless other regions groan under beastly governments—the potential for oppression is real and present.

But we serve a God who sits above all kingdoms. We follow a King who has already been exalted to heaven and given all authority. And we are promised that we—His holy people—will one day possess the kingdom with Him.

**Therefore:**
- Don't naively trust earthly governments. Be vigilant and push back against injustice while you can.
- Don't lose heart in delay. God's timeline is not our timeline, but it is sure.
- Don't forget that you belong to the King who will never fall. Your ultimate allegiance is not to any earthly kingdom but to Jesus, the Son of Man, the exalted Lord.
- Stand firm. By standing firm, you gain life.
