# Genesis 14-15

**Title:** Yahweh — The Provider of His Own Promises  
**Date:** February 10, 2008  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Recap of Genesis 12

In Genesis 1-11, we encounter a continuous cycle of creation and un-creation. Adam exemplifies this pattern. But in chapter 12, God makes a decisive move—He introduces His ultimate solution to humanity's problem of sin and rebellion.

Abraham becomes the new hope. In Genesis 12:1-3, God promises Abram three essential things, easily remembered by the acronym **L.O.B.**:

- **L**and
- **O**ffspring  
- **B**lessings (to himself and the world)

Yet Abraham struggles with faith. He flees to Egypt, jeopardizing the promised land. He gives up his wife, threatening the promise of descendants. But God remains faithful. He brings Abraham and Sarah back to the land and repeats His promise—descendants like dust and land as far as the eye can see (13:16).

**Abraham is the new hope for a permanent solution to sin.** These two chapters reveal a fundamental truth: God is not merely a God who promises. He is the Provider of His own promises—He makes them happen.

## The War of Kings (14:1-12)

A conflict erupts between four kings and five kings. Here we encounter a suzerain-vassal treaty arrangement—a protection racket of sorts, common in the ancient world. A powerful king (suzerain) promises protection to weaker states (vassals) in exchange for tribute, resources, and military support.

Kedorlaomer of Elam plays the role of the powerful boss. For twelve years, the cities of Sodom, Gomorrah, and others paid him tribute. But in the thirteenth year, they rebelled. Now Kedorlaomer marches with his allied kings to punish the rebellion, conquering nations along the King's Highway as he travels south.

When the five rebel kings hear of the approaching army, they gather their forces and meet Kedorlaomer's four kings in the Valley of Siddim. The battle is fierce. Some of Sodom's men fall into tar pits. The rest flee to the hills. Kedorlaomer's forces plunder both cities and capture Lot, Abraham's nephew.

## God Provides Victory (14:13-24)

A survivor brings news to Abraham. When Abraham hears that Lot has been taken captive, he mobilizes. With only 318 trained men born in his household, Abraham pursues the enemy north to Dan. During the night, he divides his men for a surprise attack and routes the enemy, pursuing them as far as north of Damascus. He recovers all the plunder and brings back Lot and the other captives.

After his victory, Abraham encounters two figures on his return:

**Melchizedek, King of Salem**, appears suddenly—a mysterious figure whose name means "King of Righteousness." He brings bread and wine and blesses Abraham:

> Blessed be Abram by God Most High, Creator of heaven and earth. And blessed be God Most High, who delivered your enemies into your hand.

Melchizedek credits the victory to God. Abraham responds by giving Melchizedek a tenth of everything—an act of worship and gratitude.

In stark contrast, the **King of Sodom** offers Abraham all the recovered goods: "Give me the people and keep the goods for yourself." But Abraham refuses emphatically. He raises his hand and swears an oath to God Most High:

> I will accept nothing belonging to you, not even a thread or the thong of a sandal, so that you will never be able to say, "I made Abram rich." I will accept nothing but what my men have eaten and the share that belongs to the men who went with me.

This passage is about God as the provider of blessing. The contrast between Melchizedek and the King of Sodom is striking. Melchizedek represents God's blessing and provision. He brings sustenance and pronounces God's favor. The King of Sodom offers material wealth—but only human wealth.

Abraham's response reveals his faith. He refuses the king's offer with an oath. Why? Because he has already received blessing from God Most High, the Creator of heaven and earth. No human can compare with that source. It's like being offered a gift from the dollar store versus a gift from the finest store in the land—the worst from the best is always superior to the best from the worst. Abraham knows that God's provision far exceeds anything the world can offer.

## God Promises Offspring (15:1-6)

The chapter opens with God speaking to Abraham in a vision:

> Do not be afraid, Abram. I am your shield, your very great reward.

God's word here is rich. "Shield" in Hebrew carries a double meaning—it also signifies "provider" or "benefactor." After Abraham has just given away the plunder to the King of Sodom, God reassures him: I am your provider. Trust me with the **L**and. Trust me with the **O**ffspring. Trust me with the **B**lessings.

But Abraham doubts. He questions:

> O Sovereign LORD, what can you give me since I remain childless? The one who will inherit my estate is Eliezer of Damascus.

Abraham has no children. A servant will inherit everything. How can God's promise of offspring be fulfilled?

God responds with unmistakable clarity:

> This man will not be your heir, but a son coming from your own body will be your heir. Look up at the heavens and count the stars—if indeed you can count them. So shall your offspring be.

God takes Abraham outside under the night sky and calls him to count the stars. The impossibility of the task matches the impossibility of Abraham's current situation. Yet God's promise is absolute. Your descendants will be as countless as the stars.

## God Promises Land (15:7-21)

God then speaks again:

> I am the LORD, who brought you out of Ur of the Chaldeans to give you this land to take possession of it.

Abraham questions once more:

> O Sovereign LORD, how can I know that I will gain possession of it?

This time, God responds differently. He commands Abraham to bring animals for a covenant ceremony:

> Bring me a heifer, a goat and a ram, each three years old, along with a dove and a young pigeon.

Abraham obeys. He cuts the animals in two and arranges the halves opposite each other. The birds he leaves whole. As Abraham stands guard over the carcasses, birds of prey descend, but he drives them away.

As the sun sets, Abraham falls into a deep sleep. A thick darkness comes over him. And God speaks:

> Know for certain that your descendants will be strangers in a country not their own, and they will be enslaved and mistreated four hundred years. But I will punish the nation they serve as slaves, and afterward they will come out with great possessions. You, however, will go to your fathers in peace and be buried at a good old age. In the fourth generation your descendants will come back here, for the sin of the Amorites has not yet reached its full measure.

This is an ancient covenant ceremony—literally a "cutting" of a covenant. In Hebrew culture, when you make a binding promise, you "cut a covenant." The imagery is deliberate. The cutting of the animals symbolizes the fate of one who breaks the covenant. As Jeremiah later explains, whoever violates the covenant will be "cut" as these animals are cut.

What makes this covenant extraordinary is its nature. Typically, in a suzerain-vassal treaty, the weaker party—the vassal—must cut the covenant and walk between the pieces, symbolically placing himself under the curse if he breaks the agreement. But here, God—the powerful one, the suzerain—cuts the covenant with Abraham, the weak one, the vassal. God submits Himself to this binding obligation.

And notice: Abraham is asleep. He does nothing. God alone is active, walking between the pieces as a smoking firepot with a blazing torch:

> When the sun had set and darkness had fallen, a smoking firepot with a blazing torch appeared and passed between the pieces.

God does all the work. God makes the commitment. God places Himself under the curse if the covenant is broken. This is astounding.

> On that day the LORD made a covenant with Abram and said, "To your descendants I give this land, from the river of Egypt to the great river, the Euphrates—the land of the Kenites, Kenizzites, Kadmonites, Hittites, Perizzites, Rephaites, Amorites, Canaanites, Girgashites and Jebusites."

## Abraham Believes (15:6)

After witnessing this covenant ceremony, Abraham's response is profound:

> Abram believed the LORD, and he credited it to him as righteousness.

Abraham believed. He heard God's word and trusted it completely. He took God at His word. And this belief—this simple trust in God's character and promise—is counted as righteousness.

This is the essence of faith: to believe God at His word. When everything in your circumstances says "impossible," the best thing you can do is believe God. Like a child in danger whose only safety is to trust his mother completely, we find our righteousness not in what we do but in our trust in God.

This verse becomes foundational in the New Testament. Paul takes it up in Romans 4 as the model of salvation. There are no works to boast in. The only righteousness that matters is faith. God provides everything. We simply believe.

## The Heart of the Matter

Why are these chapters—with their battle narrative and covenant ceremony—included in Scripture? They illustrate a central theological truth:

**God is the Provider of His own promises.** He does not promise and then delegate the fulfillment to someone else. He does not promise and fail to deliver. When God promises, He is trustworthy. He does what He says.

In chapter 14, we see God's protection and blessing of Abraham—the odd military victory that should have been impossible, and the blessing pronounced by Melchizedek representing God's favor.

In chapter 15, God reaffirms the covenant explicitly, with an even stronger ritual. He updates and amends it, showing His continued commitment to Abraham.

In Genesis 12-25, this narrative arc unfolds: a new hope in Abraham, bits and pieces of blessing beginning to appear, reconfirmation of the land and offspring amid doubt, Abraham's attempts to fulfill the promise himself, and ultimately, God's provision of Isaac.

God does not leave Abraham to wonder. He provides the **L**and. He provides the **O**ffspring. He provides the **B**lessings.

And this pattern extends to us. God has provided the Promise—the hope of rescue and restoration. He has provided the Offspring—Jesus Christ, the ultimate seed of Abraham. He has provided His Body as a sacrifice for our sins. He is the Provider of His own promises.

Therefore, no one can claim God owes them. Just as the King of Sodom cannot say he made Abraham rich, we cannot claim that God's provision of salvation is our achievement. Salvation is entirely God's work. It is grace. We simply believe.

Let us believe Him.
