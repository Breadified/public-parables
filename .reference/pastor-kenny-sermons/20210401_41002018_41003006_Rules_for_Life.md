## Fasting: The Aim is to Feast

> 18 Now John's disciples and the Pharisees were fasting. Some people came and asked Jesus, "How is it that John's disciples and the disciples of the Pharisees are fasting, but yours are not?"

### The History of Fasting in Israel

Fasting—to abstain from food for a period of time—originated as a command in the law, particularly surrounding the Day of Atonement (Leviticus 16:29; Numbers 29:7). This was when sacrifices were made in the temple to atone for sin.

> Leviticus 23:27: "The tenth day of this seventh month is the Day of Atonement. Hold a sacred assembly and deny yourselves, and present a food offering to the Lord."

The phrase "deny yourself" came to be read as fasting. Over time, "the Fast" became synonymous with the Day of Atonement itself:

> Acts 27:9: "Much time had been lost, and sailing had already become dangerous because by now it was after the Day of Atonement."

Beyond the Day of Atonement, the Jewish people developed additional fasting practices. During the Babylonian exile, special fasts were held in the fifth and seventh months to mourn the destruction of the temple and the assassination of Gedaliah (2 Kings 25:25; Jeremiah 41:1–2):

> Zechariah 7:5: "Ask all the people of the land and the priests, 'When you fasted and mourned in the fifth and seventh months for the past seventy years...'"

After the exile, two more regular fasts were established: in the tenth month to commemorate the siege of Jerusalem, and in the fourth month to mark the city's final capture.

Beyond these prescribed fasts, the Jewish people also fasted for various reasons:

- **To grieve:** When mourning loss or tragedy
- **To repent:** Returning to God with sincere contrition
- **To petition:** Seeking God's help in difficult situations
- **To concentrate:** Clearing the mind for important decisions
- **As discipline:** Humbling oneself before God

The common thread throughout all these practices was clear: **fasting served to humble the self, bringing a person into the proper frame of mind for relating to God.**

### The Problem in Jesus' Day

By Jesus' time, fasting had become institutionalized and formalized. The Pharisees fasted twice weekly, as illustrated in Luke 18:12: "I fast twice a week; I give tithes of all that I get." What began as a sincere expression of repentance and devotion had become a religious performance—much like our modern "awareness days" and charitable ribbons. We've simply replaced one religious system with another.

The problem is not with individual charities or causes, but when we use these outward practices to judge ourselves and others as more or less godly. We create a false sense of righteousness based on external compliance. We tell ourselves we're "good people" because we wore a ribbon or donated to a cause, without genuinely examining our hearts or the actual impact of our actions. Some charities, according to research, deliver only 10% of donations to actual recipients—yet we feel virtuous.

### Jesus' Response: Purpose Over Performance

Jesus points them back to the purpose of fasting:

> 19 Jesus answered, "How can the guests of the bridegroom fast while he is with them? They cannot, so long as they have him with them."

The Old Testament uses the metaphor of God as a bridegroom coming to rescue Israel (Isaiah 62:5). God was separated from His people because of sin, but He would come and marry them—rescue and restore them. Jesus is saying: the bridegroom has arrived. This is not a time for fasting and mourning; this is a time for feasting and celebration.

The purpose of fasting was never to remain in mourning. Rather, fasting was meant to lead to eating, to restoration, to joy. Consider David: when his child was ill, he fasted and wept, pleading with God (2 Samuel 12:16–17). But when the child died, David rose up, washed himself, changed his clothes, went to worship the Lord, and then went home and ate (2 Samuel 12:20). His confidence in the resurrection allowed him to move from fasting to feasting.

> Zechariah 8:19: "This is what the Lord Almighty says: 'The fasts of the fourth, fifth, seventh and tenth months will become joyful and glad occasions and happy festivals for Judah.'"

### A Practical Illustration

There is a right time to fast and a right time to eat. Before a wedding, you may fast to fit into your dress or tuxedo. After the wedding, you enjoy a ten-course banquet. The timing makes sense. It would be absurd to reverse the order. Similarly, the prophecies have been fulfilled. Jesus is here, bringing good news to sinners. Now is the time to eat.

### Food and Modern Judgment

In our society, we have begun to judge one another based on food—what we eat, what we don't eat. Rules proliferate: vegetarian, cage-free, carbon-neutral, ethically sourced, locally grown. There's nothing wrong with good intentions, but the danger emerges when the message becomes: "If you don't eat like us, we don't want to eat with you."

When did our morality become based on nutrition? We wonder why we have eating disorders and anxiety in society—we've invented new rules for food that function exactly like ancient religious law. But food is just food. True piety is not defined by what we consume.

## The Bridegroom Taken Away

> 20 But the time will come when the bridegroom will be taken from them, and on that day they will fast.

There is something strange in the fulfillment of this prophecy. The bridegroom will be taken away—Jesus Himself predicts His death. To understand why people must fast after His departure, we must recognize what that death accomplishes.

The Day of Atonement—"the fast"—was when the lamb of God was to be sacrificed. Jesus dies to bring the exile to an end, to bring us back to the promised land, to rescue His people. His death fulfills the purpose of fasting itself.

Therefore, fasting after His departure carries new meaning. It becomes mourning for the bridegroom. This is our motivation as believers: we mourn because we await His return. We fast not from legalism, but from longing.

## Application: Fasting Today

Some argue that Christians should not fast because we live in the new age. The Day of Atonement has come and gone in Jesus. There are no more prescribed fasting days. This is true. If you fast to earn merit or establish righteousness before God, it accomplishes nothing—He fasted and thirsted for us so we don't have to. We are free.

> Colossians 2:16: "Therefore do not let anyone judge you by what you eat or drink, or with regard to a religious festival, a New Moon celebration or a Sabbath day."

Yet others observe that since Jesus has ascended to heaven and we await His return, the bridegroom is still "taken away" from us. Grief continues. Repentance continues. Discipline and humility continue. The early church fasted (Acts 13:1–3; cf. 2 Corinthians 6:5; 11:27).

### How We Fast Today

The beautiful truth is that we fast in ways we may not have recognized as fasting:

**When you stay at church during lunch, remaining in fellowship without eating**—perhaps because the food isn't suitable for your diet, or you're grieving, or finances are tight—you are fasting for the sake of others. You choose community over comfort.

**When you skip breakfast to arrive on time for youth group, or skip dinner to join Bible study after work**, you are fasting. You choose hunger for the sake of community because you value the people around you more than your own comfort.

**True fasting is fasting from self-centeredness.** True feasting is eating with humility, gladness, and joy.

Food does not define us. Godliness defines us. This is true piety. And we have much to learn from eating together regularly—God speaks through the simple act of sharing a meal.

## The Sabbath: The Aim is to Rest

> 23 One Sabbath Jesus was going through the grainfields, and as his disciples walked along, they began to pick some heads of grain. 24 The Pharisees said to him, "Look, why are they doing what is unlawful on the Sabbath?"

### Understanding the Sabbath

The Sabbath—the seventh day—was God's command to rest and cease from work. In Israel's history, this carried deep meaning. In Egypt, the Israelites were enslaved, working endlessly. The Sabbath represented freedom from that bondage. More than just physical rest, it meant freedom to serve God rather than Pharaoh.

> Leviticus 23:3: "For six days work may be done, but the seventh day is a Sabbath of complete rest, a day of sacred assembly. You must not do any work; wherever you live, it is a Sabbath to the LORD."

The Bible never specifies exactly which activities constitute "work," but it gives examples: gathering wood (Numbers 15:32–35) and lighting fire (Exodus 35:3). From these fragments, later Jewish tradition developed an elaborate framework—eventually 39 categories of prohibited work.

The Pharisees accused Jesus of violating the Sabbath by gleaning grain, which fell under the category of reaping and gathering. His response seems puzzling at first:

> 25 He answered, "Have you never read what David did when he and his companions were hungry and in need? 26 In the days of Abiathar the high priest, he entered the house of God and ate the consecrated bread, which is lawful only for priests to eat. And he also gave some to his companions."

### The Real Issue: Legalism vs. Purpose

At first glance, this appears to be Jesus saying, "I'm breaking the law because David broke it too." That's never a valid argument. The real point is more subtle.

The consecrated bread was set aside for the priests alone (Leviticus 24:9). Yet the high priest was free to share it with the hungry—to do good with what was at his disposal. Just because a rule says "priests shall eat this" doesn't mean the rule prohibits sharing it with those in need. We read rules too narrowly when we assume them to be exhaustive restrictions rather than primary assignments.

Similarly, the law told Israel to work six days and rest on the seventh. But it did not prohibit acts of mercy, healing, or necessity on the Sabbath. The Pharisees had over-read the law, turning it into legalism.

### The Principle: Sabbath Serves Humanity

> 27 Then he said to them, "The Sabbath was made for man, not man for the Sabbath. 28 So the Son of Man is Lord even of the Sabbath."

The Sabbath exists for human flourishing—for rest, for restoration, for being freed from endless toil to pursue what matters. Humans are not machines to be worked without ceasing. We are not units of economic productivity. We are bearers of God's image who need rest.

When Jesus heals on the Sabbath (Mark 3:1–6), He demonstrates that it is lawful to do good on that day:

> Matthew 12:11–12: "If any of you has a sheep and it falls into a pit on the Sabbath, will you not take hold of it and lift it out? How much more valuable is a person than a sheep! Therefore it is lawful to do good on the Sabbath."

Ceasing from work does not mean ceasing all activity. It means being freed from work in order to do good—to help, to heal, to show mercy.

### Jesus' Authority and the Pharisees' Response

The Pharisees watched Jesus closely, looking for a reason to accuse Him. When He healed the man with the shriveled hand:

> 3:1–5: "Jesus went into the synagogue, and a man with a shriveled hand was there... Jesus said to the man with the shriveled hand, 'Stand up in front of everyone.' He looked around at them in anger and, deeply distressed at their stubborn hearts, said to the man, 'Stretch out your hand.' He stretched it out, and his hand was completely restored. Then the Pharisees went out and began to plot with the Herodians how they might kill Jesus."

The Pharisees were so committed to their interpretation of the law that they plotted to kill the One who demonstrated God's power and mercy. Their stubborn hearts prevented them from recognizing the true purpose of God's law.

## Rules for Life: The Unifying Principle

Throughout these two passages, one principle emerges: **God's laws are not meant to constrain human flourishing—they're meant to enable it.**

- The Sabbath was made for humans, not humans for the Sabbath
- The consecrated bread was meant to support temple priests, but not to forbid sharing with the hungry
- Fasting was meant as an outward expression of repentance and humility, not as a tool to judge others' godliness

When the Pharisees elevated the letter of the law above its purpose, they became enslaved to rules rather than freed by them. Jesus came to restore the law to its true function: to lead us toward life, not death; toward compassion, not judgment; toward freedom, not bondage.

**The aim of fasting is to feast. The aim of resting is to do good. The true rules for life lead us to love God and love our neighbors.**
