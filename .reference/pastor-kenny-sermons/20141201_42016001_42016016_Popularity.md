# Luke 16:1-16

**Title:** Popularity

**Date:** 1 December 2014

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Introduction: The Universal Desire for Popularity

We all want to be popular. This desire shows up in countless ways: we want quality friends who can open doors for us, quantity of friends visible on social media, and we'll go to great lengths to achieve this—wearing the right clothes, spending money strategically, keeping up with trends, and adopting the right attitudes.

Remarkably, Jesus was also deeply interested in popularity. But His vision of popularity was radically different from what the world offers.

---

## Part 1: Gaining Earthly Friends (Luke 16:1-8)

### The Parable of the Shrewd Manager

Jesus told a story about a rich man whose manager had been wasting his possessions—doing nothing productive, squandering resources, not growing the business. Facing termination, the manager didn't give up. Instead, he devised a quick plan.

> "I know what I'll do so that when I'm removed from management, people will welcome me into their homes." (Luke 16:4)

The manager realized his survival depended on having friends. He needed people to support him. So he began calling in the master's debtors and offering them significant discounts—50% off one debt, 20% off another. On the surface, this looks like dishonesty. But look deeper: the manager was strategically generating favors.

This resembles how people in professional networks operate. When accountants lose their jobs, they reach out to clients they've served well in the past, knowing those relationships will support them in finding new opportunities.

### The Master's Surprising Praise

> "The master praised the unrighteous manager because he had acted astutely. For the sons of this age are more astute than the sons of light in dealing with their own people." (Luke 16:8)

This praise is stunning. The master commends the manager not for his dishonesty, but for his shrewdness—his ability to assess a difficult situation and act decisively to solve it. The manager faced crisis without despair; he used intelligence and strategy to find a solution.

### What "Astute" Means

Astute (shrewd) means having the wisdom to accurately assess situations and people, discerning what matters, and turning understanding to practical advantage. Jesus isn't praising dishonesty. Rather, He's highlighting a quality believers should possess:

> "Look, I'm sending you out like sheep among wolves. Therefore be as shrewd as serpents and as harmless as doves." (Matthew 10:16)

Use your intelligence to solve difficult problems. When you're in trouble, admit the truth and work to fix it. Be strategic about making friends—not through deception, but through genuine relationship-building and investment.

## Part 2: Friendship with God (Luke 16:9-15)

### The Real Friendship That Matters

> "And I tell you, make friends for yourselves by means of the unrighteous money so that when it fails, they may welcome you into eternal dwellings." (Luke 16:9)

Notice the parallel with the manager's story. The manager sought to be welcomed into homes; here Jesus speaks of being welcomed into eternal dwellings. This parable isn't merely about making earthly friends—it's about friendship that extends beyond this life.

### The Unavoidable Choice

> "No household slave can be the slave of two masters, since either he will hate one and love the other, or he will be devoted to one and despise the other. You can't be slaves to both God and money." (Luke 16:13)

There's no middle ground. We cannot serve two masters equally. We will love one and hate the other, or be devoted to one and despise the other. This is as absolute as being pregnant—there's no halfway point.

The problem surfaces when we prioritize material things over the Creator of those things. Money itself isn't evil, but when we make it our master instead of our means, we've reversed what God intends.

### The Warning About Faithfulness

> "Whoever is faithful in very little is also faithful in much, and whoever is unrighteous in very little is also unrighteous in much. So if you have not been faithful with the unrighteous money, who will trust you with what is genuine? And if you have not been faithful with what belongs to someone else, who will give you what is your own?" (Luke 16:10-12)

Becoming a Christian will cost you money. This isn't optional. Consider the practical implications:

- Attending church means sacrificing Sunday work (sometimes losing 2.5x pay)
- A lifestyle of generosity costs resources
- Supporting Bible ministries requires financial commitment
- Avoiding excessive career ambition costs money
- Attending retreats and camps demands investment
- Even building deeper friendships requires spending on meals together

Being realistic about these costs means:

- Rejecting the trap of "keeping up with the Joneses"
- Living simply; eating at home rather than constantly dining out
- Avoiding greed and materialism
- Spending money wisely and being trustworthy with resources
- Earning enough to support yourself without letting work consume your faith and family time

### God Sees What Money Cannot Hide

> "The Pharisees, who were lovers of money, were listening to all these things and scoffing at Him." (Luke 16:14)

The Pharisees let money come between them and God. They were devoted to money, not to God. This devotion shaped everything—they served money, not God.

> "You are the ones who justify yourselves in the sight of others, but God knows your hearts. For what is highly admired by people is revolting in God's sight." (Luke 16:15)

God cannot be bought. God cannot be tricked. God is not fooled by popularity or outward success. He sees what truly motivates us.

### The Danger of Social Media

Social media amplifies this ancient temptation. We curate carefully: posting fine dining photos, exotic vacations, carefully filtered images—all designed to gain likes and validation. We care more about people's approval than God's approval. We post content designed to stumble others spiritually, choosing quantity of likes over quality of character.

Yet social media isn't inherently evil. It can help us build genuine connections and spread truth. The key is discernment about how we use it.

## Part 3: Friendship with the Lost (Luke 16:16)

### Israel's Lost Opportunity

The Pharisees held a sacred responsibility. They were entrusted with wealth and position to bring God's good news to the nations. They were meant to be "light bearers," elevated in character and conduct, showing the world what God intended for humanity.

> "In those days, 10 men from nations of every language will grab the robe of a Jewish man tightly, urging: Let us go with you, for we have heard that God is with you." (Zechariah 8:23)

The Jewish people were meant to be so visibly blessed, so clearly marked by God's presence, that foreigners would desperately want to follow them into God's kingdom. Instead, they became no different from the nations around them—equally materialistic, equally divided, equally corrupt. They failed to be the people of light.

### The Reversal

> "The Law and the Prophets were proclaimed until John. Since that time, the good news of the kingdom of God is being preached, and everyone is forcing his way into it." (Luke 16:16, paraphrased)

When Jesus came proclaiming the good news—that the King had arrived, that salvation was at hand—the Jews hesitated. They calculated and debated. But the Gentiles? They forced their way in, desperate to enter.

It's like watching a real estate market: some investors question whether it's worth the price. Meanwhile, outsiders grab properties as fast as they can. The Pharisees vacillated while the lost rushed forward.

### Our Opportunity

We're not in Israel's position, facing rejection and judgment. But we inherit their calling: use our resources and influence to make friends with the lost.

Jesus calls us to two distinct friendships:

**First:** Be friends with God. Don't let money or earthly popularity damage that relationship.

**Second:** Be friends with the lost. Use your money, your resources, your influence, and yes, your popularity for their salvation.

### Practical Application

Don't waste the relationships and opportunities you have. When you're in heaven, you won't regret the money you spent. You'll regret the moments you could have invested more in someone's spiritual journey but didn't—not because you didn't care, but because life got busy.

**Use money strategically:** Buy lunch for an unbelieving friend. Pick them up (cover petrol costs). Get them a Bible or a thoughtful book about faith.

**If you have earthly popularity**, use it. Not for personal gain or to become a minor celebrity, but to create openings for people to hear about Jesus. You might lose some superficial popularity in the process, but gain eternal friends. Bring ten people around you and tell them about Jesus. Use your influence to open doors to the gospel.

**Use social media wisely:**

- Tag quality articles that point toward truth
- Invite friends to church; find people through these networks
- If you have courage, engage with controversial topics directly. Write for the undecided middle who haven't formed opinions yet
- Face confrontation gracefully; you're writing for those listening, not for those determined to resist

---

## Conclusion

We all desire friendship. We all want to matter and be welcomed. God invites us into a radical reorientation of this desire.

First, invest in friendship with Him. Guard that relationship jealously. Don't let money, status, or earthly popularity create distance between you and the One who matters most.

Second, use every legitimate tool—your resources, your talents, your charm, your communication ability, your emotional intelligence, your very popularity—to make friends with the lost. Any genuine friendship demands investment: time, conversation, appointments, meals, generosity, presence.

Younger Son in Luke 15 squandered his father's wealth. But the shrewd manager in this parable shows us a different use of earthly resources: as seeds sown for eternal friendships.

**Be astute. Be shrewd. Be shrewd in making friends with God and shrewd in making friends with the lost.**
