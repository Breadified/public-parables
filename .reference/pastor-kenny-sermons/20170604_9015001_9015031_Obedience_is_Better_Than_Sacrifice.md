# 1 Samuel 15:1-31

**Title:** Obedience is Better Than Sacrifice
**Date:** June 4, 2017
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction: The Culture of Sacrifice

From childhood, we're taught that sacrifice is essential for success. Whether you come from a migrant family starting over in a foreign land, or a student foregoing social life for academic achievement, or someone saving for a house by cutting daily luxuries—success demands sacrifice.

Margaret Court exemplifies this principle. She won 62 Grand Slam titles, more than any male or female player in history. The Margaret Court Arena in Melbourne Park honors her achievement. But her success required extraordinary sacrifice: working part-time at a tennis club as a child to earn court time, working as a receptionist to pay for training, training with men when others wouldn't, and sacrificing her entire teenage years—no parties, no social life—for tennis.

Success and honor require sacrifice.

Yet today, I want to say something counterintuitive: **obedience is better than sacrifice.**

This truth is illustrated through King Saul's account in 1 Samuel. We've been reading through 1 Samuel together, a book about Israel's search for a leader. Samuel the prophet wasn't the right leader. Last week, we received hints that Saul wasn't either. Today, we see confirmation.

## 1. Obedience is Good (1 Samuel 15:1-8)

God commands Saul through Samuel to attack the Amalekites and completely destroy all they possess:

> Samuel said to Saul, "I am the one the Lord sent to anoint you king over his people Israel; so listen now to the message from the Lord. This is what the Lord Almighty says: 'I will punish the Amalekites for what they did to Israel when they waylaid them as they came up from Egypt. Now go, attack the Amalekites and totally destroy all that belongs to them. Do not spare them; put to death men and women, children and infants, cattle and sheep, camels and donkeys.'" (1 Samuel 15:1-3)

This sounds terrible—even genocidal. But we must trust Scripture and do the hard work of understanding its context.

### The Amalekites' Long Pattern of Disobedience

The Amalekites had consistently attacked Israel across generations:

- Despite Abraham saving them (Genesis 14), they attacked Israel during the Exodus (Exodus 17:8) when the Israelites were most vulnerable, before reaching Sinai and without water.
- In Numbers 14:45, they attacked Israel again in the desert.
- In Judges 3:13, they joined the Moabites in attacking Israel.
- In Judges 6:3-4, they invaded whenever Israel planted crops, destroying all sustenance—sheep, cattle, and donkeys.

Generation after generation, the Amalekites waged war against Israel and against God. This wasn't a conflict Israel started. The Israelites never attacked the Amalekites outside the Promised Land during those 300 years between Moses and Saul. But the Amalekites never stopped attacking.

### A Justified Defensive War, Not Genocide

When a nation is "hell-bent on killing you" and refuses all peaceful options, what can you do? Like Nazi Japan in World War II—refusing surrender even as their allies fell, flying planes into Allied ships in suicide attacks—some enemies give you no choice.

God had declared this long before:

> Then the Lord said to Moses, "Write this on a scroll as something to be remembered and make sure that Joshua hears it, because I will completely blot out the name of Amalek from under heaven. Moses built an altar and called it The Lord is my Banner. He said, 'Because hands were lifted up against the throne of the Lord, the Lord will be at war against the Amalekites from generation to generation.'" (Exodus 17:14-16)

### God Desires Mercy, Not Bloodlust

God is not bloodthirsty but the opposite—He desires to show mercy. Deuteronomy 20:10-13 outlines the rules of engagement for warfare: offer terms of peace first. Only fight if the enemy refuses.

Saul observed these principles. Before attacking, he warned the Kenites (who had shown kindness to Israel) to leave:

> Saul went to the city of Amalek and set an ambush in the ravine. Then he said to the Kenites, "Go away, leave the Amalekites so that I do not destroy you along with them; for you showed kindness to all the Israelites when they came up out of Egypt." (1 Samuel 15:5-6)

The Amalekites had a way out. They could have renounced their identity and fled. When a city faces siege, people escape unless they choose to stay and defend it. The command wasn't genocide—it was: "If you don't surrender your identity as Amalekites and your opposition to Israel, you will be destroyed."

> Then Saul attacked the Amalekites all the way from Havilah to Shur, near the eastern border of Egypt. He took Agag king of the Amalekites alive, and all his people he totally destroyed with the sword. (1 Samuel 15:7-8)

**Application:** There are consequences for disobeying God and resisting His purposes. We've all been attacking God throughout our lives. Some of us have taken the Kenite option—realizing we can't win against God. But some still fight like the Amalekites. God has given us every good thing, yet we keep attacking Him and His people. If you continue this path, you face judgment. But it's not too late. Surrender to God. Ask for peace. He will treat you and your family with compassion and mercy.

> While we were God's enemies, we were reconciled to him through the death of his Son. Therefore, since we have been justified through faith, we have peace with God through our Lord Jesus Christ. (Romans 5:10; 5:1)

## 2. Obedience is Better Than Sacrifice (1 Samuel 15:9-35)

But God plays no favorites. The same rules that applied to the Amalekites apply to Israel and its king.

> Saul and the army spared Agag and the best of the sheep and cattle, the fat calves and lambs—everything that was good. These they were unwilling to destroy completely, but everything that was despised and weak they totally destroyed. (1 Samuel 15:9)

They didn't obey God's word. The consequence came swiftly:

> Then the word of the Lord came to Samuel: "I regret that I have made Saul king, because he has turned away from me and has not carried out my instructions." Samuel was angry, and he cried out to the Lord all that night. (1 Samuel 15:10-11)

Samuel confronted Saul:

> And he sent you on a mission, saying, 'Go and completely destroy those wicked people, the Amalekites; wage war against them until you have wiped them out.' Why did you not obey the Lord? Why did you pounce on the plunder and do evil in the eyes of the Lord? (1 Samuel 15:18-19)

### Why Saul Disobeyed

Saul's reasons reveal a pattern of spiritual failure:

**a) He wanted his own glory (v. 12)**

> Early in the morning Samuel got up and went to meet Saul, but he was told, "Saul has gone to Carmel. There he has set up a monument in his own honor and has turned and gone on down to Gilgal." (1 Samuel 15:12)

This was foolish. Samuel reminded him:

> Although you were once small in your own eyes, did you not become the head of the tribes of Israel? The Lord anointed you king over Israel. (1 Samuel 15:17)

Saul's military success came from God, not his own skill. Yet when Samuel arrived, Saul boasted:

> "The Lord bless you! I have carried out the Lord's instructions." (1 Samuel 15:13)

**b) He blamed others (v. 15, 21)**

> Saul answered, "The soldiers brought them from the Amalekites; they spared the best of the sheep and cattle to sacrifice to the Lord your God, but we totally destroyed the rest." (1 Samuel 15:15)

First he claimed credit; now he deflects blame. "I" becomes "they."

**c) He gave in to pressure (v. 24)**

> Then Saul said to Samuel, "I have sinned. I violated the Lord's command and your instructions. I was afraid of the men and so I gave in to them." (1 Samuel 15:24)

He feared the soldiers more than God. Perhaps they wanted to keep the livestock for food. Yet his fear of people led him to disobey God's clear command.

**d) His apology was false (v. 30)**

When Samuel announced that the kingdom would be torn from him, Saul said:

> "I have sinned. But please honor me before the elders of my people and before Israel; come back with me, so that I may worship the Lord your God." (1 Samuel 15:30)

Notice the pronouns. He asks Samuel to honor *him* but refers to God as "the Lord *your* God"—as if God were Samuel's, not his own. This isn't genuine repentance but reputation management. Saul cared what people thought, not what God thought.

**e) He never fully obeyed**

The deeper issue: Saul thought sacrifice could substitute for obedience. Look at his defense:

> "But I did obey the Lord," Saul said. "I went on the mission the Lord assigned me. I completely destroyed the Amalekites and brought back Agag their king. The soldiers took sheep and cattle from the plunder, the best of what was devoted to God, in order to sacrifice them to the Lord your God at Gilgal." (1 Samuel 15:20-21)

He planned to sacrifice the animals later at Gilgal. What's the difference between killing them now or then?

Everything. God didn't want a tale of plunder and conquest. This was about justice, not profit. By deciding for himself what was "good" to keep, Saul redefined God's purposes—just as Adam and Eve did in the garden when they decided for themselves what was good and evil.

> But Saul and the army spared Agag and the best of the sheep and cattle, the fat calves and lambs—everything that was good. (1 Samuel 15:9)

"I say it's good, so I won't destroy it." They didn't want to destroy the animals because of self-centeredness. In sacrifices, meat was often left for food. The term "pounce on the plunder" echoes 1 Samuel 14:32, when soldiers "pounced on the plunder" to eat.

In the end, Samuel had to do what Saul wouldn't:

> Then Samuel said, "Bring me Agag king of the Amalekites." Agag came to him in chains. And he thought, "Surely the bitterness of death is past." But Samuel said, "As your sword has made women childless, so will your mother be childless among women." And Samuel put Agag to death before the Lord at Gilgal. (1 Samuel 15:32-33)

### The Heart of the Matter

Samuel's verdict is eternal:

> "Does the Lord delight in burnt offerings and sacrifices as much as in obeying the Lord? To obey is better than sacrifice, and to heed is better than the fat of rams. For rebellion is like the sin of divination, and arrogance like the evil of idolatry. Because you have rejected the word of the Lord, he has rejected you as king." (1 Samuel 15:22-23)

Sacrifice itself is good—God delights in it:

> The Lord told Moses to give the Israelites the following laws about offering sacrifices: Bulls or rams or goats are the animals that you may burn on the altar as sacrifices to please me... The smell of the smoke from these sacrifices is pleasing to me. (Leviticus 15:1-3)

But God is not pleased when you disobey one law to follow another. The problem is legalism—following one command while avoiding another, usually for selfish reasons.

Scripture emphasizes this across both Testaments:

> To do righteousness and justice is more acceptable to the Lord than sacrifice. (Proverbs 21:3)

> Guard your steps when you go to the house of God. To draw near to listen is better than to offer the sacrifice of fools, for they do not know that they are doing evil. (Ecclesiastes 5:1)

> For I desire steadfast love and not sacrifice, the knowledge of God rather than burnt offerings. (Hosea 6:6)

> And to love him with all the heart and with all the understanding and with all the strength, and to love one's neighbor as oneself, is much more than all whole burnt offerings and sacrifices. (Mark 12:33)

> "Woe to you, scribes and Pharisees, hypocrites! For you tithe mint and dill and cumin, and have neglected the weightier matters of the law: justice and mercy and faithfulness. These you ought to have done, without neglecting the others." (Matthew 23:23)

### Illustration: The Child and Her Chores

It's like when one of my children skips her homework to play with her siblings, saying, "But you always told me to love my siblings—I'm doing that right now!" That's legalism. Following one law by breaking another, usually because the easier law is more appealing.

Saul chose to follow the law of sacrifices (which he could have done anytime and which God never commanded in this context) to avoid the obvious command: cleanse the land. Usually for a selfish motive—free food.

### Application Today

We make the same mistakes. It's hard to distinguish "sacrifice" from "obedience," but the point is that Christianity isn't compartmentalizing one law against another. It's all laws, all of life, every day.

Consider these temptations:

- Stealing from somewhere (disobeying one law) but planning to give it to church (obeying generosity)—disobeying God to give back to Him?
- Following the command to work and provide for family as an excuse to neglect training children or being present for them.
- Showing up to church and praying (religious duty) while ignoring the command to love and encourage each other (Hebrews 10).
- Being a "good person" who doesn't smoke or use drugs, thinking this entitles you to ignore God.
- Living like a pagan all week, then coming to church Sunday to "sacrifice" and feel absolved.

The lesson: don't stop sacrificing. Rather, understand that **sacrifice without obedience is useless. Sacrifice is what comes after obedience.**

## 3. Obedience and Sacrifice Together (The Gospel)

Ultimately, obedience is better than sacrifice because the sacrifice has already been provided:

> For it is impossible for the blood of bulls and goats to take away sins. Consequently, when Christ came into the world, he said, "Sacrifices and offerings you have not desired, but a body have you prepared for me; in burnt offerings and sin offerings you have taken no pleasure." (Hebrews 10:4)

There is only one who is both perfectly obedient and offers sacrifice righteously without one denigrating the other: the Lord Jesus Christ.

Since He has provided the sacrifice, all that remains for us is to obey. To put our faith in Him. The world says you must do, do, do. Christ says all is done.

### Living Out Obedience: Margaret Court's Example

Margaret Court is not popular these days. Though she was named ABC Sportsperson of the Year twice, received an Order of Australia, an honorary doctorate in Law, appeared on a stamp, and won the highest tennis honor—the Philippe Chatrier Award—she fell from favor.

She made a statement about not flying on Qantas because of their advocacy for same-sex marriage, expressing her Christian convictions. The media vilified her. They want to remove her from the tennis hall of fame, rename the Margaret Court Arena. They will likely succeed in disgracing the most decorated tennis player in history simply for speaking her faith.

But why did she do it?

Because Margaret Court is born again. After her tennis career, she became a Christian, a Bible teacher, and started a church. Unlike many church leaders, she uses her position to speak truth.

"But won't you lose everything you've worked for?" people ask. "Those sacrifices—weren't they wasted?"

Her answer: **Obedience is better than sacrifice.**

She's willing to give up all the world's trappings—her medals, her stadium, the glory and honor of worldly success—to gain honor from God. Can you glorify God through tennis? Not if it's at the expense of obedience.

As you begin a new week, may you be emboldened to obey God, whatever the cost.
