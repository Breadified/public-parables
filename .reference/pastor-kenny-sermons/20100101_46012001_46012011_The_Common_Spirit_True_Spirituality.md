# 1 Corinthians 12:1-11

**Title:** The Common Spirit: True Spirituality

## What Is True Spirituality?

We live in a world full of competing claims about what spirituality looks like. Is it the TV evangelist performing spectacular miracles? The meditating monk pursuing inner peace? The missionary who sacrificed everything? The person leading worship with emotional fervor and raised hands? The devoted churchgoer who attends every event and conference?

Even within Christianity, we see growing numbers calling themselves "Reformed Charismatics"—evangelicals who want the emotional intensity of charismatic worship, the kind we see at churches like Hillsong. But is this what true spirituality really is?

The Corinthian church faced this exact question. Corinth was a wealthy, multicultural trading hub on a thin strip of land connecting northern and southern Greece. With harbors on both sides opening to Asia and Italy, the city was a crossroads of commerce, people, and money—but also sexual immorality, idolatry, and pagan worship. It was greedy and hedonistic.

Yet there was a church there, planted by Paul himself (Acts 18). And they struggled deeply. Paul wrote to them:

> "Brothers, I could not address you as spiritual but as worldly—mere infants in Christ." (1 Corinthians 3:1)

The Corinthian church reflected the pagan culture around them. Sexual immorality, divisions, lawsuits against each other, incest, idolatry, greed so severe that people went hungry—the list was staggering. In reaction, some swung to the opposite extreme, teaching strict asceticism: no sex, the body is evil, food is evil.

Paul's response to all this centers on understanding what "spirituals" truly means.

## Understanding "The Spirituals"

When Paul writes in 1 Corinthians 12:1, the original Greek simply says "now about the spirituals"—not "spiritual gifts," but the adjective form, "spirituals." Greek often does this when the author assumes the reader already knows what is being discussed (in this case, responding to a letter from the Corinthians we no longer have).

The word could refer to spiritual persons or spiritual things. But I want to suggest what the spirituals are *not*, and then what they are.

### What the Spirituals Are NOT: Spiritual Gifts

One popular interpretation suggests "spirituals" refers to spiritual gifts—different abilities distributed among believers. The NIV translation tries to link "spiritual" and "gifts" together throughout these chapters.

However, there are good reasons to think this interpretation misses the mark:

- The term "spiritual gift" appears only once in the New Testament (Romans 1:11). Paul wanted to impart a spiritual gift to the Romans, which turned out to be the gospel. The phrase doesn't appear elsewhere.

- There's actually very little connection between "gifts" and "Spirit" in the New Testament. In 1 Corinthians 1:7, Paul says the Corinthians lack no spiritual gifts—yet there's no mention of the Spirit at all. In chapter 7, Paul discusses various gifts of life (marriage, singleness), but again, no mention of the Spirit.

- When Paul discusses the Spirit's work in chapter 2, he doesn't talk about gifts.

- Most importantly, gifts come from God or the Trinity as a whole, not from the Spirit alone. Verse 6 says, "There are different kinds of working, but the same God works all of them." Verse 11 says the Spirit "divides them to each one," but gifts themselves aren't exclusively the Spirit's domain.

The popular Christian culture's emphasis on "spiritual gifts" creates a false impression that gifts come from the Spirit alone—as if the Holy Spirit is the "Santa Claus of the Trinity." This emphasis has been amplified by the Charismatic movement and self-help mentality, and unfortunately reinforced by modern Bible translations.

**Application:** The focus on spiritual gifts, gifts of the Spirit, and being "gifted by the Spirit" puts the emphasis in the wrong place. The Spirit is not the Santa Claus of the Trinity. Thank the entire Godhead for your abilities!

### What the Spirituals ARE: Spiritual Truths

I believe "spirituals" refers to spiritual truths—specifically, the communication of spiritual truths to one another. The Corinthians weren't simply arguing over general spiritual issues or people or gifts. They were arguing about the speaking of God's Word itself: Who is allowed to speak? Who should we listen to? What if people disagree? What's the right order to speak God's words?

To understand this, we must first understand the word "Spirit" itself (Greek: *pneuma*). Literally, it means wind or breath. Pneumatology comes from this word—it relates to lungs, to breath. In Scripture, Spirit specifically means wind or breath that comes from God. Genesis 2 describes God breathing His Spirit into Adam. Genesis 8:1 speaks of God sending a wind upon the earth. The word describes the very breath of life from God.

Look at how Paul describes the Spirit in 1 Corinthians 2, where he contrasts the Spirit of the world with the Spirit of God:

| What Paul Did NOT Do | What Paul DID Do |
|---|---|
| Eloquence or superior wisdom (v.1) | Resolved to know nothing except Jesus Christ and Him crucified (v.2) |
| Not with wise and persuasive words (v.4) | But with a demonstration of the Spirit's power (v.4) |
| Not resting on men's wisdom (v.5) | But resting on God's power (v.5) |
| Not wisdom of this age (v.5) | We speak a message of wisdom among the mature (v.6) |
| | We speak of God's secret wisdom (v.7) |
| Received the spirit of the world (v.12) | But received the Spirit who is from God (v.12) |
| Words taught by human wisdom (v.13) | Words taught by the Spirit (v.13) |
| The man without the Spirit cannot accept things of the Spirit (v.14) | The spiritual man makes judgments and knows the mind of Christ (v.15) |

On the left, we see the wisdom that belongs to this world and this present age. On the right, we see God's wisdom—the gospel. And what is that gospel?

> "Christ crucified" (v.2)
> "Destined for glory" (v.7)—which Paul links to the crucified Lord of Glory
> "The message of the cross is foolishness to those who are perishing, but to us who are being saved it is the power of God." (1 Corinthians 1:18)

So the spirituals—that which is from the Spirit's breath—refers to the truth that comes from God: the gospel itself. And the communication of spiritual truths is the speaking of the gospel to one another.

Verse 2:13 confirms this: "We have not received the spirit of the world but the Spirit who is from God, that we may understand what God has freely given us. This is what we speak, not in words taught us by human wisdom but in words taught by the Spirit, expressing spiritual truths in spiritual words."

## Three Marks of True Spirituality

### 1. Don't Be Ignorant of the Spiritual Truths

> "Now about spiritual gifts, brothers, I do not want you to be ignorant... Therefore I tell you that no one who is speaking by the Spirit of God says, 'Jesus be cursed,' and no one can say, 'Jesus is Lord,' except by the Holy Spirit." (1 Corinthians 12:1, 3)

There's a false spirituality that equates the spiritual with ignorance. This is the spirituality of tarot cards, horoscopes, empty meditation, psychic readings—a spirituality that says "just feel your way to the truth" or "empty your mind of thought." It's anti-intellectual. Or it pursues spectacular signs and wonders, or emotionalism that sways people with touching music and passionate rhetoric.

Christian spirituality is not this. Paul explicitly says he does not want us to be ignorant. He tells us to *know*. This is why we learn together. This is why we study God's Word. This is important.

Christian spirituality is also not emotionalism. Emotions are good and helpful, but they are not the sign of true spirituality. You cannot simply feel your way into the kingdom of God.

### 2. The Gospel Is the Ultimate Spiritual Truth and Power of God

> "There are different kinds of gifts, but the same Spirit. There are different kinds of service, but the same Lord. There are different kinds of working, but the same God works all of them in all men." (1 Corinthians 12:4-6)

Every Christian, at the moment of conversion, has received the gospel and the Spirit of God. If you have believed that Jesus died for you and rose again, the spiritual truth lives in you. You are a spiritual person.

In discussions about spiritual gifts and spiritual power, this fundamental truth is sometimes forgotten—as though miraculous signs were more important than the gift of eternal life itself.

Years ago, a harmful heresy spread teaching about a "second baptism." The first baptism, they said, was water baptism when you became a Christian. The second baptism was a "baptism of the Spirit" that came later, making you powerful, confident, and able to speak with authority. You weren't a "real Christian" until this second baptism happened.

This is false. The Spirit comes to you when you become a Christian—when you first committed your life to trusting that Jesus died for you. There is no second baptism. Many Christians do experience a season of renewed commitment and deeper dedication to ministry, but that's not the Spirit entering them for the first time. That's a decision to take your existing faith more seriously.

Paul's emphasis here is clear: among Christians questioning who truly has the Spirit of God, Paul says you *all* have the gospel. Every Christian has the same Spirit. Period.

A prominent American Christian once said that Sydney Anglicans "only believed in Father, Son, and Holy Bible"—implying we don't have the Spirit because we lack emotional enthusiasm and charismatic fervor. But that's both silly and wrong. The Holy Bible is inseparable from the Holy Breath of God. Is the Word of God not the "Sword of the Spirit"? If you want the Spirit, He is in the Word of God.

The point is this: emotionalism is not what defines spirituality. Being emotional doesn't make you spiritual. Being passionate doesn't mean you're in the Spirit. That's merely the second baptism heresy dressed up in modern language.

All Christians share the common Spirit, whether emotional or reserved, charismatic or quiet. And that common Spirit brings the greatest gift of all—eternal life.

### 3. True Spirituality Confesses the Lordship of Christ

> "Therefore I tell you that no one who is speaking by the Spirit of God says, 'Jesus be cursed,' and no one can say, 'Jesus is Lord,' except by the Holy Spirit." (1 Corinthians 12:3)

If you're looking for a sign of true spirituality, don't look for emotion. Look for this: Does this person confess that Jesus is Lord?

This isn't a formula to recite. It's a wholehearted commitment to the gospel. The sign of true spirituality is whether a person is living under the Lordship of Christ.

The gospel proclaims that Jesus died and rose again to be Lord of the universe. If you don't believe Jesus is Lord over your life, you don't have the Spirit. You're not a Christian if you refuse to submit to His Lordship.

Now, I'm not saying Christians live perfectly. But those with the Spirit ought to *want* to follow Christ's Lordship, even when they stumble and make mistakes. Piety and emotionalism cannot compensate for a lack of obedience. Some people live one way on Sunday and another on Monday, or party on Saturday and worship on Sunday. Such people do not have the Spirit. The Lordship of Christ demands that we follow Him.

## True Spirituality in Our Church

Some might say our church isn't very spiritual. Why? Perhaps because we don't have formal liturgy or elaborate musical arrangements. Maybe because I don't come to the front, raise my hands, or cry and shout.

But that misses the point entirely. A place where true spirituality exists is one where:

- The Bible is read and proclaimed
- We speak the spirituals to one another
- We submit to the Lordship of Christ as His Word, through His Spirit, cuts to the bone of our being, convicting us of sin and our need to repent
- Day by day, members commit more deeply to God's agenda and God's values

I've spoken with some of you this year and asked you to sacrifice time, money, and effort for others and for the growth of this church. And you said *yes*. You said yes to more suffering, more commitment, more sacrifice. **This is true spirituality.**

To the rest of you: Will you commit to the Lordship of Christ? Will you submit to Him and follow His will? Will you love one another and care for God's mission?

As we do this together, this is where true spirituality lies.

## Closing Prayer

Common Spirit, common God, and common Lord—help us to obey You. Like the Corinthians, we have sins to repent of. Grant us grace to commit wholeheartedly to Your mission and to love each other, so that the world may know we are Your disciples.
