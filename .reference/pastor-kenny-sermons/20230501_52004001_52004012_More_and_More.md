**Title:** More and More
**Date:** 19 May 2023 (ARISE)

## Introduction: Ambition and Our Ultimate Goal

What does God want for our ambition? In a world that constantly pushes us toward "more and more"—more titles, more money, more possessions—we must ask what truly matters. Timothy Keller, who recently passed away, exemplified worldly success: a thriving church in New York City during a time of cultural hostility toward Christianity. Yet even his remarkable achievement raises the question: what ambitions should Christians really pursue?

Ambition itself isn't wrong. The danger lies in directing it toward the wrong things. Paul's letter to the Thessalonians addresses this head-on, calling us to redirect our ambition toward two areas: **holiness** and **love expressed through honest work**.

---

## 1. More Holy (Verses 1-8)

### Understanding Holiness

Paul begins with urgency: "We urge you in the Lord Jesus **to do this more and more**" (v. 1). The foundation is clear: "**It is God's will that you should be sanctified**" (v. 3).

The word "sanctified" (Greek: *hagios*) simply means "holy"—to be set apart, separated, or made different. We often imagine holiness as monks in convents, but biblically it means being **set apart for God's purposes**, like special chopsticks reserved only for the owner, not for common use.

### Historical Context: Israel's Model

When God led Israel out of Egypt and placed them among pagan nations, He made their distinctiveness clear:

- The nations worshiped many gods; Israel had one God
- The nations were chaotic; Israel had God's laws
- The nations sacrificed children; Israel protected life
- The nations practiced sexual promiscuity through fertility worship; Israel demanded sexual faithfulness

As Peter later wrote: "You are a chosen people, a royal priesthood, a holy nation, God's special possession" (1 Peter 2:9). We are **set apart**—no longer identified by external markers like food or clothing, but by our **behavior and character**.

### Sexual Holiness in Today's Culture

Paul specifies what holiness means: "**that you should avoid sexual immorality**" (v. 3). The Greek word *porneia* (sexual immorality) refers to any sexual intercourse outside of marriage—premarital sex, adultery, incest, or other perversions. The principle is simple: "**Each man should have relations with his own wife and each woman with her own husband**" (1 Corinthians 7:2).

Why is this our distinguishing mark today? In a post-Christian society, we no longer stand out by caring for the poor (secular governments do this) or protecting orphans and widows (institutions handle this). But **sexual ethics now distinguishes us from the world**.

We live in a culture saturated with sexual messages—every magazine, every TV show, every social media platform celebrates sexual freedom without commitment. The modern equivalent of Nike worship is the "Just Do It" mentality: follow your desires without ethical constraint. This mindset has created a world obsessed with sexual immorality.

### Being Set Apart: The Practical Call

Paul continues: "Each of you should learn to control **your own body** in a way that is holy and honorable, not in passionate lust like the pagans, who do not know God" (vv. 4-5).

**We must control our desires.** The world sees the opposite gender as prey, targets, conquests. We are called to something radically different.

#### The Problem with "Boyfriend" and "Girlfriend"

Kenny challenges young people about quasi-marriages—relationships that look and function like marriage without the commitment. Today's "boyfriend" or "girlfriend" typically means:

- An exclusive sexual partnership
- Inseparable presence (arriving and leaving together, spending every significant break together)
- Shared holidays and family involvement
- Merged identities (friends see them as one unit)
- Physical intimacy including foreplay and sex

**This is not friendship—this is a counterfeit marriage.**

Such relationships leave deep scars. Breaking up from a quasi-marriage isn't just losing a friend; it's losing a lover. Studies show that couples who have premarital sex are more likely to divorce than those who don't. These instruction aren't restrictive—**they're protective**.

#### The Simple Standard: Brother and Sister

Paul offers clarity in verse 6: "No one should wrong or take advantage of a brother or sister."

If you're not husband and wife, you're brother and sister in Christ. The answer to "How far can we go?" is straightforward:

- Coffee together? Yes, as siblings do
- Movies? Yes
- Holding hands? Only if you'd do it with your actual brother or sister
- Making out? **Never.**

There's an intermediate period called courtship or engagement, but **physical purity is non-negotiable**. Good brothers protect their sisters from predators and exploitation. Good sisters protect their brothers from temptation and flirtation. 

**Don't look at your brother or sister in Christ that way. Don't take advantage of them for your own gratification.**

## 2. More Love Through Honest Work (Verses 9-12)

### The Foundation: Love for One Another

Paul affirms the Thessalonians: "Now about your love for one another we do not need to write to you, for you yourselves have been taught by God to love each other. And in fact, you do love all of God's family throughout Macedonia. Yet we urge you, brothers and sisters, **to do so more and more**" (vv. 9-10).

True love—the kind that says no to sexual immorality—is what marks us as God's people. But Paul specifies what this love looks like in practice: **honest, dignified work.**

### "Make it Your Ambition to Lead a Quiet Life"

"Make it your ambition to lead a **quiet life**: You should mind your own business and work with your hands, just as we told you" (v. 11).

The word "quiet" is counterintuitive. It doesn't mean laziness or idleness. In context, it means **settling down and working**. Compare 2 Thessalonians 3:10-12: "The one who is unwilling to work shall not eat... Such people we command and urge in the Lord Jesus Christ to **settle down** and earn the food they eat."

**Quiet means diligent, faithful work without fuss.**

It means:
- Stopping busybodies behavior (gossiping, running around with useless information)
- Sitting down and actually doing the work
- Getting on with it faithfully despite obstacles
- Executing plans rather than endlessly planning
- Carrying on despite setbacks

Kenny illustrates with HSC procrastination: students stay "busy" sharpening pencils, checking the fridge, filling printer cartridges, texting friends, attending graduation ceremonies—anything but actually studying. **That's not quiet; that's distraction.** A quiet life is focused work.

### The "Follow Your Passion" Myth

One major obstacle to honest work is the modern mantra: **"Follow your passion."** Graduation speeches, career advice, and successful people's interviews all echo this. Steve Jobs famously said, "The only way to do great work is to love what you do. If you haven't found it yet, keep looking, and don't settle."

**But this advice is misleading.** Consider:

1. **What else would they say?** Commencement speakers can't tell graduates, "Only one of you will really succeed; most of you studied the wrong degree."

2. **Following passion leads to failure.** Telling 17-year-olds to follow passion means staying home playing video games, dreaming of social media fame, eating all day, or just going to the gym. **These aren't useful life goals.**

3. **Steve Jobs didn't follow his passion.** Before Apple, his passions were Eastern meditation, fruitarianism, calligraphy, and dance. He got a night-shift job at Atari to pay rent—*not to follow his passion*—and one thing led to another.

4. **Passion comes from mastery, not the reverse.** What successful people describe as passion is actually the result of working hard at something consistently for years. They became good at it, liked it, and it became part of their identity. **Passion follows hard work; it doesn't precede it.**

### Biblical Work: Love of Others, Not Self

The key difference: **passion is self-love; biblical work is love for others.**

> "Anyone who has been stealing must steal no longer, but must work, doing something useful with their own hands, **that they may have something to share with those in need**" (Ephesians 4:28).

We work to:
- **Provide for ourselves** (not burden others)
- **Share with those in need**
- **Support the gospel** and God's work
- **Give and be charitable**

This reframes work entirely. It's not about finding your dream job. It's a practical, functional responsibility.

### Application: Overcoming Work Resistance

In affluent societies, we become **too fussy about work**. We make excuses:

- "It's too dirty"
- "It's boring"
- "It's beneath my qualifications"
- "The boss is difficult"
- "The pay isn't enough"
- "It's too far to travel"

Sometimes the choice isn't between a good job and a better job—it's between **work and idleness**. In those moments, we must:

- Swallow our pride
- Overcome laziness and procrastination
- Put away fantasies that something better will come along
- **Just work.**

Timothy Keller exemplifies this. He had no intention of going to New York City—he was content in rural Virginia. But when his denomination struggled to plant churches there and others declined, **he went where the need was, not where his passion was.** He wasn't chasing ambition; he was answering a call.

## 3. Living to Please God (Closing Vision)

### What Really Matters

God isn't primarily concerned with:
- **What career you have** (He cares about your love for others)
- **What you wear or eat** (He cares about your holiness)

He is concerned with:
- **Your consistent godliness and integrity**
- **Not taking advantage of your brothers and sisters**
- **Upholding sexual purity and the exclusivity of marriage**
- **Keeping your eyes on what's right and avoiding what's wrong**

### The Hope: Jesus' Return

Paul's closing prayer captures the vision:

> "May the Lord make your **love increase** and overflow for each other and for everyone else... May he strengthen your hearts so that you will be **blameless and holy** in the presence of our God and Father **when our Lord Jesus comes** with all his holy ones." (vv. 12-13)

Three themes unite Paul's teaching:
1. **Love** that overflows to others
2. **Blameless and holy** character
3. **Readiness for Jesus' coming**

In a world obsessed with doing and having more and more, Paul calls us to pursue **more holiness, more love, and more faithful work**—not for ourselves, but as an offering to our God who is coming again.

---

**The Core Challenge:** What are your true ambitions? Are you striving for more money, more status, more pleasure? Or are you pursuing holiness, love for others, and faithful, humble work? The difference marks you as God's set-apart people in a watching world.
