# Numbers 20-21:13

**Title:** Seduced by Anger
**Date:** 9 June 2013
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Death of Leaders

Nelson Mandela's legacy reminds us that great leaders shape generations. In his autobiography, *The Long Walk to Freedom*, Mandela reflected on how the Church engaged both the spiritual and material needs of society, pioneering education and social progress. This is the kind of leadership that transforms communities.

Numbers presents us with a turning point: the death of Israel's great leaders.

> In the first month the whole Israelite community arrived at the Desert of Zin, and they stayed at Kadesh. There Miriam died and was buried. (Numbers 20:1)

Later, at the end of chapter 20, Aaron also passes away:

> Moses removed Aaron's garments and put them on his son Eleazar. And Aaron died there on top of the mountain. Then Moses and Eleazar came down from the mountain, and when the whole community learned that Aaron had died, the entire house of Israel mourned for him thirty days. (Numbers 20:28-29)

## A Recapitulation of the Past

The community's complaint in Numbers 20 echoes their earlier complaint in Exodus 17. Both involve a crisis without water; both show the people quarreling with Moses and questioning God's care. Why repeat this story?

Some scholars suggest plagiarism, but I believe this is a *recapitulation*—a strategic replay of past events. We are now in the fortieth year of Israel's journey. This is where the death sentence announced in Numbers 14 comes into effect: the first generation dies in the desert, making way for the new generation to inherit the Promised Land.

Just as a parent takes their child to revisit the places of their own childhood—the school they attended, the park where they played, the shop where they first met—God was allowing the new generation to experience what the old generation had faced. But with a crucial difference.

> Moses and Aaron went from the assembly to the entrance to the Tent of Meeting and fell facedown, and the glory of the LORD appeared to them. The LORD said to Moses, "Take the staff, and you and your brother Aaron gather the assembly together. Speak to that rock before their eyes and it will pour out its water." (Numbers 20:6-8)

Here God breaks the cycle. In the past, grumbling led to judgment, pleading, and mitigation. But this time, God simply says: get them the water. The new generation will experience God's protection as well as His testing.

### The Pattern in Jesus

In the New Testament, Jesus embodies this recapitulation perfectly. Where Adam sinned and humanity fell, Jesus—the new Adam—had to retrace those steps and do them better.

He came fully human: born, grew through childhood and adolescence, learned to read and write, faced suffering, made friends, experienced betrayal and persecution, and ultimately died. But He did all this without sin. Because of His perfect recapitulation of the human life, He is worthy to offer Himself as a ransom for those who did not live rightly.

> Just as the result of one trespass was condemnation for all men, so also the result of one act of righteousness was justification that brings life for all men. For just as through the disobedience of the one man the many were made sinners, so also through the obedience of the one man the many will be made righteous. (Romans 5:18-19)

Jesus walked the righteous life we should have lived.

## The Sin of Moses and Aaron

Yet something goes wrong. Despite God's instruction and the water flowing miraculously, Moses incurs judgment:

> But the LORD said to Moses and Aaron, "Because you did not trust in me enough to honor me as holy in the sight of the Israelites, you will not bring this community into the land I give them." (Numbers 20:12)

This seems harsh. God commanded Moses to get water, and Moses got water. What was his sin?

### Speaking to the Wrong Person

God's instruction was clear: "Speak to that rock before their eyes and it will pour out its water." But Moses did something different:

> He and Aaron gathered the assembly together in front of the rock and Moses said to them, "Listen, you rebels, must we bring you water out of this rock?" Then Moses raised his arm and struck the rock twice with his staff. Water gushed out. (Numbers 20:10-11)

Moses spoke to the *people* instead of the *rock*. This is significant. Throughout Scripture, the rock symbolizes God:

> He is the Rock, his works are perfect, and all his ways are just. (Deuteronomy 32:4)

By speaking to the rock, Moses would have been petitioning God—an act of humble dependence. Instead, he rebuked the people unnecessarily. God was dealing with this new generation differently; He was showing them mercy rather than judgment. By speaking harshly to the people, Moses failed to honor this new approach to divine mercy.

Moreover, his words betrayed a deeper issue. When he said "must *we* bring you water," Moses took credit that belonged to God alone.

### Raising the Hand in Defiance

There's another layer to Moses' sin:

> Then Moses raised his arm and struck the rock twice with his staff. (Numbers 20:11)

The phrase "raised his arm" is unusual. Striking inherently involves raising the arm, so why mention it explicitly? And why strike twice, when in Exodus 17 God said simply to strike once?

Throughout Scripture, "raising the hand" carries significance. It can mean triumph over enemies:

> Your hand will be lifted up in triumph over your enemies. (Micah 5:9)

It can also mean defiance or rebellion:

> Jeroboam son of Nebat rebelled against the king. (1 Kings 11:26)

In Numbers 15:30, sinning "with a high hand"—literally, raising the hand—means acting defiantly against God. Moses raised his hand not in humble petition but in a gesture of power and control. In that moment, he stopped trusting God and relied on his own authority.

The core issue was faithlessness. Verse 12 says: "Because you did not trust in me enough to honor me as holy." Moses forgot that this moment was about God's character, not his own leadership.

## A New Generation Requires New Leaders

Because Moses and Aaron rebelled, they forfeited entry into the Promised Land. Miriam had already died. Now Aaron's time came:

> At Mount Hor, near the border of Edom, the LORD said to Moses and Aaron, "Aaron will be gathered to his people. He will not enter the land I give the Israelites, because both of you rebelled against my command at the waters of Meribah. Get Aaron and his son Eleazar and take them up Mount Hor. Remove Aaron's garments and put them on his son Eleazar, for Aaron will be gathered to his people; he will die there." (Numbers 20:23-26)

The old leadership passed away. Eleazar, the new priest, received the mantle. The old patterns—marked by cycles of sin and judgment—were being removed to make way for something new.

## A New Victory

The new generation experiences a complete reversal of fortunes:

> When the Canaanite king of Arad heard that Israel was coming along the road to Atharim, he attacked the Israelites and captured some of them. Then Israel made this vow to the LORD: "If you will deliver these people into our hands, we will totally destroy their cities." The LORD listened to Israel's plea and gave the Canaanites over to them. They completely destroyed them and their towns. (Numbers 21:1-3)

Compare this to the previous generation:

> The Amalekites and Canaanites who lived in that hill country came down and attacked them and beat them down all the way to Hormah. (Numbers 14:45)

The old generation was defeated; the new generation is victorious. God is not abandoning them for Moses' failure. Instead, He's establishing a new beginning.

## A New Forgiveness

Even more remarkably, when the new generation stumbles—complaining about food and water—God doesn't respond with judgment. Instead, He offers a path to healing:

> The people grew impatient on the way; they spoke against God and against Moses, and said, "Why have you brought us up out of Egypt to die in the desert? There is no bread! There is no water! And we detest this miserable food!" Then the LORD sent venomous snakes among them; they bit the people and many Israelites died. The people came to Moses and said, "We sinned when we spoke against the LORD and against you. Pray that the LORD will take the snakes away from us." So Moses prayed for the people. The LORD said to Moses, "Make a snake and put it up on a pole; anyone who is bitten can look at it and live." (Numbers 21:4-8)

This is profoundly merciful. Yes, there is judgment—the snakes. But immediately, God provides a remedy. Anyone bitten can look at the bronze serpent and live.

Jesus points to this moment:

> Just as Moses lifted up the snake in the desert, so the Son of Man must be lifted up, that everyone who believes in him may have eternal life. For God so loved the world that he gave his one and only Son, that whoever believes in him shall not perish but have eternal life. (John 3:14-16)

The snake represents sin—it echoes the serpent in Eden who brought death into the world. We have been bitten by sin; death is the venom in our veins. But God lifts up the one who conquers sin. Look to the cross, and live.

## The Humility of Leadership

As Moses passes from the scene—forfeiting the Promised Land due to his moment of anger and pride—we see a crucial lesson about leadership. Even the greatest leaders are fallible. Even Moses, the most faithful servant of God, could be seduced by anger and forget to trust.

> For we know that our old self was crucified with him so that the body of sin might be done away with, that we should no longer be slaves to sin—because anyone who has died has been freed from sin. (Romans 6:6-7)

The old patterns must die. The old generation passes. The old leaders step down. But God's covenant continues. God's mercy persists. And in Jesus, we find the Leader who never fails, whose humility matches His power, and whose faithfulness is eternal.

Leaders come and go. But Christ remains the same.
