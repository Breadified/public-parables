# Ephesians 2:11-19

**Title:** Imagine a Church United

**Date:** 24 February 2019

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Wall of Separation

Throughout history, walls have functioned as powerful symbols of division—separating those on the outside from those within. In the first century, such a wall existed in the temple of Jerusalem. It wasn't a massive structure, but rather a low rail that could have easily been crossed. Yet its symbolic weight was enormous.

An inscription discovered from that era read: "No foreigner is to go beyond the balustrade and the plaza of the temple zone. Whoever is caught doing so will have himself to blame for his death which will follow."

This wall separated two groups of people: Jews and Gentiles. It represented the vast chasm between the circumcised (those who bore the covenant sign of Abraham) and the uncircumcised (those outside God's covenant community).

## The Jewish Privilege

Circumcision originated in Genesis 17, where God established His covenant with Abraham:

> Every male among you shall be circumcised. You are to undergo circumcision, and it will be the sign of the covenant between me and you.

This covenant brought immense privilege. As Paul writes in Romans:

> Theirs is the adoption as sons; theirs the divine glory and the covenants; theirs the giving of the Law, the temple worship, and the promises. Theirs are the patriarchs, and from them proceeds the human descent of Christ.

The Jews were nearer to God. Deuteronomy celebrated this distinction:

> What nation is so great as to have a god as near to them as the Lord our God is to us whenever we call on him?

If Israel obeyed God's commands, they would be set above all nations—their enemies would be defeated before them, and all peoples would see that they were called by the Lord's name.

## The Gentile Experience

By Jesus' time, the separation had become absolute. The Gentiles were considered "the great unwashed"—uncivilized barbarians. Jewish eating laws and ritual cleansing practices reinforced this division. A faithful Jew could not eat with a Gentile, could not touch one. The wall was not merely social; it was spiritual.

More importantly, the Gentiles could not approach God. As Numbers prescribed:

> Any outsider who comes near the sanctuary must be put to death.

The temple itself embodied this separation through multiple walls: walls for Gentiles, walls for ordinary Israelites, walls for priests, walls for the High Priest. As proximity to holiness increased, so did the barriers and the regulations required for entry.

## Separation and Despair

In Ephesians 2:11-12, Paul reminds the Gentile Christians of their former condition:

> You who are Gentiles by birth... were separate from Christ, excluded from citizenship in Israel and foreigners to the covenants of the promise, without hope and without God in the world.

This separation was not merely social alienation—it was homelessness. The Gentiles were stateless, like refugees stuck in limbo, neither able to enter nor able to return home. They were in "the far country," as Jesus described it in Luke 15—the place people go to distance themselves as far as possible from God the Father.

Without access to God came the loss of hope. This explains why those without God lived as the saying goes: "Eat and drink and be merry, for tomorrow you die." When separated from God, there is nothing to live for.

Many of us know the agony of separation—whether through distance, broken relationships, or death. When I first came to Australia, my hometown in Malaysia felt impossibly far away. I had dreams of visiting relatives, then waking to the reality that home was four years away. Some have experienced the devastation of a shattered relationship or the loss of a loved one. That agony of distance, that longing for reunion—this is how our separation from God feels.

Separation from hope is despair.

## The Solution: Brought Near by Blood

Yet this is not where the story ends. Paul continues:

> But now in Christ Jesus you who once were far away have been brought near by the blood of Christ.

The crucial word is "brought." This action is passive. We did not travel near by our own efforts. Christ brought us near. He did the work through the shedding of His blood.

Just as someone might send a ticket to a distant grandmother to invite her to a wedding, Christ has sent His blood as our ticket of admission. Hebrews describes this beautifully:

> Since we have confidence to enter the Most Holy Place by the blood of Jesus, by a new and living way opened for us through the curtain, that is, his body... let us draw near to God with a sincere heart and with the full assurance that faith brings, having our hearts sprinkled to cleanse us from a guilty conscience.

The blood of Christ is our passport. It is the acceptance of a free ticket. All we need do is receive it.

## One New Humanity

But Christ's work accomplishes something even greater:

> For he himself is our peace, who has made the two groups one and has destroyed the barrier, the dividing wall of hostility, by setting aside in his flesh the law with its commands and regulations. His purpose was to create in himself one new humanity out of the two, thus making peace.

Through the blood of Christ, a transformation occurs—from bloodlines to bloodline. Under Christ, all believers share one spiritual bloodline. Background becomes irrelevant. Jew and Gentile, rich and poor, slave and free—all become brothers and sisters in Christ, members of one spiritual family with one Father.

By this reconciliation, a new temple is being built. Not the old temple with its walls of hostility, but a living temple composed of believers themselves—unseparated, indivisible, united in Christ.

> He came and preached peace to you who were far away and peace to those who were near. For through him we both have access to the Father by one Spirit.

Therefore:

> You are no longer foreigners and strangers, but fellow citizens with God's people and also members of his household, built on the foundation of the apostles and prophets, with Christ Jesus himself as the chief cornerstone. In him the whole building is joined together and rises to become a holy temple in the Lord.

## The Nature of True Unity

**There is no place for racism among Christians.** The church is for all people.

Some might find it interesting that we are the Asian congregation at St. Matthew's. This name is not meant to exclude anyone. Rather, it signals to those of Asian background—who may have experienced unwelcoming attitudes elsewhere, whether intentional or accidental—that they will find a home here. They will find community, familiar food, a network of friends, perhaps even a cousin's cousin. There are no walls here.

Yet realistic boundaries exist. A church in Cherrybrook cannot easily welcome someone living in Penrith. Complete earthly unity in a single gathering is not the goal. What matters is **theological unity**.

Notice Paul's language in verse 16:

> In one body to reconcile both of them to God through the cross, by which he put to death their hostility.

We are not reconciled *into each other's body*, as in a merger. Rather, we are reconciled *in the one body of Christ*. Our oneness with each other depends entirely on our oneness with God.

It is not A and B merging together. Rather:
- A merges with Christ
- B merges with Christ
- Therefore, A and B are united

Too often we attempt to merge A directly to B—through dialogue, compromise, or policy. But fundamental reconciliation is ultimately a theological question: What sort of God do you believe in? Are you near or far from Him?

Consider marriages that struggle with unity. The real question is not "Do they communicate enough?" but "Are they theologically compatible?" You can discover this by reading Scripture together—not through dating, but through Bible study in small groups, youth groups, serving teams, churches, universities, and workplaces. You can observe how someone acts, ask their family and friends, speak with their pastor. You will quickly discover whether their mind and actions align with their stated beliefs.

**As each of us becomes more like Jesus, we find ourselves united more and more.** As we conform to Christ's character, we suddenly discover that we have so much in common. That is the nature of true unity—not because of labels, but because we share:

- The same Father
- The same Brother (Christ)
- The same salvation
- The same values
- And therefore, the same brothers and sisters

## The Physical Wall and Evangelism

This is why, paradoxically, physical walls may be necessary today. Not the spiritual wall—that has been destroyed through Christ's blood. But borders and immigration policy that allow time for evangelism, time to explain to the nations that the spiritual wall is gone, that access to Christ is available to all.

If everyone embraced Christianity, there would be no need for walls. Harmony Day would become Christian Day. But without theological unity, without a shared faith in Christ, the differences remain.

The wall, then, is not a permanent solution. It is a temporary boundary that buys time—time to evangelize, time to proclaim that Christ has torn down the dividing wall of hostility and made a way for all people to be brought near to God through His blood.
