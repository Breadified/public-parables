# Job 28:1-28

**Title:** Where Is Wisdom?

**Date:** 13 July 2012

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## The Paradox of Wisdom

Throughout history, humanity has made remarkable technological advances. We measure human progress through mining—from the Bronze Age to the Iron Age, the Industrial Age, and now the Silicon Age. Each era has lifted millions out of poverty and hunger. Yet despite our extraordinary capacity to find precious metals, gems, and rare materials buried deep in the earth, there remains one thing we cannot seem to find: true wisdom.

Job himself had reached the end of his tether. After countless debates with his friends, after wrestling with God, he found himself at his wit's end. He couldn't understand why he suffered unfairly. In a moment of clarity, Job asks the crucial question: *Where can true wisdom be found?*

---

## I. The Treasures We Can Find (Verses 1-11)

The opening of Job 28 paints a vivid picture of human ingenuity. Job describes miners who:

> *There is a mine for silver and a place where gold is refined. Iron is taken from the earth, and copper is smelted from ore.*

People go to extraordinary lengths to extract these precious resources. They venture into darkness, dangling on ropes into mineshafts far from human dwellings. The text notes that even birds of prey and lions cannot find these hidden places—yet miners discover them.

> *Mortals put an end to the darkness; they search out the farthest recesses for ore in the blackest darkness. Far from human dwellings they cut a shaft, in places untouched by human feet.*

Their eyes see what nature conceals. They tunnel through rock, bringing hidden things to light. This effort, this determination, this technological mastery—it represents something noble in human nature.

Mining is a gift to us. Every age of human progress has revolved around it. In Australia, we've been blessed with this wealth. Our billionaires are made from mining. We live in the first world because of it. But here's the stunning irony: **While we are technologically capable of finding almost anything, there's one thing we cannot seem to find.**

---

## II. The Treasures We Cannot Find (Verses 13-22)

Job shifts the focus dramatically. After describing human achievement, he declares:

> *But where can wisdom be found? Where does understanding dwell?*

This is not mere knowledge or qualification. Wisdom encompasses understanding, judgment, sensibility, discernment—the ability to know right from wrong and act accordingly.

> *No mortal comprehends its worth; it cannot be found in the land of the living. The deep says, "It is not in me"; the sea says, "It is not with me."*

**Wisdom is not on earth. Wisdom is not in the earth.** No matter how deep miners dig, wisdom is absent. It's not in the centre of the earth. It's not on offshore rigs. Even in our digital age, it's not hidden in the deepest recesses of the internet. You cannot Google search for it.

Furthermore, wisdom cannot be bought:

> *It cannot be bought with the finest gold, nor can its price be weighed out in silver. It cannot be bought with the gold of Ophir, with precious onyx or lapis lazuli. Neither gold nor crystal can compare with it, nor can it be had for jewels of gold. The price of wisdom is beyond rubies.*

Even Gina Rinehart, with all her billions, cannot purchase it. A millionaire cannot walk into a shop and say, "I'll take one wisdom, please." It's priceless—not because it's expensive, but because it's utterly unavailable through human commerce or effort.

The deepest irony of our age is this: **The more qualifications we acquire, the dumber we seem to become.** Consider the state of the world. Scientists cannot even agree, after a hundred years of research, whether common painkillers are good for us. Yet we celebrate our learning as if we're wiser than ever.

---

## III. God's Wisdom Revealed (Verses 23-28)

Here's where Job's despair transforms into revelation. Wisdom exists, but it's found in one miraculous place:

> *God understands the way to it and he alone knows where it dwells.*

God has exclusive knowledge of wisdom because God created it. And God didn't keep it hidden from us:

> *When he established the force of the wind and measured out the waters, when he made a decree for the rain and a path for the thunderstorm, then he looked at wisdom and appraised it; he confirmed it and tested it.*

Like a craftsman examining a prototype, God examined wisdom, confirmed it, and tested it. And then God revealed what wisdom actually is:

> *"The fear of the Lord—that is wisdom, and to shun evil is understanding."*

**Fear of the Lord is true wisdom.** Not terror, though some of us must begin there. This "fear" means awe, reverent respect, humble obedience, and submission to God—the recognition that God is Creator and we are not. It's the attitude that acknowledges our blindness: we are so foolish that when God Himself appeared in history, we killed Him.

## Why Education Starts with God

This principle changed the course of civilization. When we place God at the centre of learning, education flourishes. It was Christians who invented the printing press—Gutenberg's first printed book was the Bible. Every major intellectual institution—Harvard, Princeton, Cambridge—began as theological colleges. Public education in New South Wales was founded by Thomas Hassall, an Anglican minister. The first schools in Australia were started not by government, but by churches.

Even Sydney University, though secular, was founded by Reverend Dr. John Woolley with the principle that all knowledge rests on the fear of the Lord.

> *"Every school you see—public or private, religious or secular—is a visible reminder of the religion of Jesus Christ. So is every college and university."* —Dr. James Kennedy and Jerry Newcombe

Remarkably, 65% of Nobel Prize winners have been Christians.

**But as we depart from God and the Bible, institutions and people become increasingly foolish.** When Christ is removed from the centre, wisdom flees.

---

## The Central Truth: Christ Is Wisdom

The apostle Paul captures this in 1 Corinthians:

> *Where is the wise person? Where is the teacher of the law? Where is the philosopher of this age? Has not God made foolish the wisdom of the world? For since in the wisdom of God the world through its wisdom did not know him, God was pleased through the foolishness of what was preached to save those who believe. Jews demand signs and Greeks look for wisdom, but we preach Christ crucified. To those whom God has called, both Jews and Greeks, Christ is the power of God and the wisdom of God.*

Christ crucified—judgment is here, the old is gone and the new has come, gold and silver count for nothing. Fear of the Lord is paramount.

If you lack wisdom, the Bible tells us simply: ask God. James writes, *"If any of you lacks wisdom, you should ask God, who gives generously to all without finding fault, and it will be given to you."*

In the Bible we find discernment. There we first find knowledge. God as Creator and Christ as Redeemer become the foundation upon which all other knowledge rests. Science itself requires a Creator God of order. The fear of God is the foundation of all knowledge and understanding—an attitude of humility and open inquiry before the mysteries of the universe.

## A Challenge for You

To those approaching or sitting Year 12 exams: Completing Year 12 is a major achievement, especially given the resilience you've shown in the disrupted years of the pandemic. But consider this sobering reality.

All that tutoring. All the time. All the money spent on study sessions and past papers. Thousands of hours laboured for a single number that nobody will care about by next March.

**How much more, then, should we be willing to mine for wisdom?** The pursuit is hard—just as mining is hard. It's hard to stick with God. It's hard to come to church, to join campus groups, to keep reading the Bible. But like any miner seeking rare gems, perseverance yields the treasure.

If we're willing to dig as deeply for the fear of the Lord as we dig for HSC marks, we will find what the world cannot: true wisdom, the foundation of all genuine understanding.
