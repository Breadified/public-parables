# Zechariah 2:1-13, 5:1-4

**Title:** Home: Land, People, and God
**Date:** 26 October 2025
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Introduction

We're looking at the third vision in Zechariah's sequence of eight visions, which are structured as a chiasm—meaning the first and last visions relate to each other, as do the second and seventh, the third and sixth, and so on. Today we're exploring what true home looks like through three essential components: Land, People, and God.

---

## 1. Land (2:1-5)

> Then I looked up, and there before me was a man with a measuring line in his hand. I asked, "Where are you going?" He answered me, "To measure Jerusalem, to find out how wide and how long it is."

The vision presents a craftsman with a measuring line—the ancient equivalent of a measuring tape. In building, measurement is foundational. A skilled craftsman doesn't just possess tools; their value lies in knowing *where* to apply them. Without proper measurement, walls won't fit, doors won't align with frames, and structural problems compound.

Here, the man isn't measuring a single house but Jerusalem itself—preparing to rebuild the city's walls. But then something unexpected happens:

> While the angel who was speaking to me was leaving, another angel came to meet him and said to him: "Run, tell that young man, 'Jerusalem will be a city without walls because of the great number of people and animals in it.'"

The measuring becomes unnecessary. The city will be too large for walls.

### The Paradox of Walls

In smaller homes, walls are essential—without them, neighbors' properties spill into yours. But as homes grow larger, walls become less necessary. Rural mansions often have no walls at all; natural boundaries like creeks and forests define the property. An island requires no wall whatsoever.

This reflects a trend we see even today: Australian homes have more than doubled in size over the past 60 years, yet the average household size has decreased from 4.5 people in 1911 to 2.5 in 2024. We have more space with fewer people living in it.

Jerusalem's expansion represents prosperity and growth—a city so flourishing that traditional boundaries become irrelevant. But this raises a crucial question: How can a city without walls be safe?

> And I myself will be a wall of fire around it, declares the Lord, and I will be its glory within.

God Himself becomes the city's protection. The irony is profound: fire once destroyed Jerusalem during siege (as Nehemiah recalled), but now fire becomes its firewall—the very judgment that once threatened now shields the people. This is God's "original firewall."

We often take walls for granted in the West, protected by geography rather than architecture. But God's promise here is clear: Jerusalem will have both *spaciousness* and *safety*—because the Lord Himself stands as its protective wall.

## 2. People (2:6-9 and 5:1-4)

> Come! Come! Flee from the land of the north, declares the Lord, for I have scattered you to the four winds of heaven, declares the Lord. Come, Zion! Escape, you who live in Daughter Babylon!

God's call is an invitation home. The diaspora—Jews scattered throughout Babylon and beyond—are comfortable in exile. They have jobs, families, homes they've purchased. They've adapted to their circumstances. Yet God calls them back.

> For this is what the Lord Almighty says: After the Glorious One has sent me against the nations that have plundered you—for whoever touches you touches the apple of his eye—I will surely raise my hand against them so that their slaves will plunder them. Then you will know that the Lord Almighty has sent me.

The language "the Glorious One has sent me" is striking—God speaking of God sending Him. This anticipates the Trinitarian revelation more fully developed in the New Testament (see Psalm 110: "The Lord said to my Lord").

The phrase "apple of his eye" comes from the Hebrew word meaning "little man" or "pupil"—the most sensitive part of the eye. When something touches your eye, the reaction is immediate and violent. God's people are this precious to Him. Any nation that attacks them touches the most sensitive part of God's heart.

God promises that these nations will be destroyed, their plunder stripped from them. The implication is clear: *return to Me*. God desires a pure, covenant people, not those comfortable in spiritual exile.

### Justice in the Restored Community

Zechariah further develops this theme of justice in chapter 5:

> I looked again, and there before me was a flying scroll. He asked me, "What do you see?" I answered, "I see a flying scroll, twenty cubits long and ten cubits wide."

These measurements—twenty by ten cubits—are identical to the temple portico entrance (1 Kings 6:3). The scroll carries symbolic weight.

> And he said to me, "This is the curse that is going out over the whole land; for according to what it says on one side, every thief will be banished, and according to what it says on the other, everyone who swears falsely will be banished."

The two-sided scroll condemns thieves (sin in deed) and false witnesses (sin in word)—violations of the eighth and ninth commandments. Together, they represent the comprehensive judgment against all wrongdoing:

> The Lord Almighty declares, "I will send it out, and it will enter the house of the thief and the house of anyone who swears falsely by my name. It will remain in that house and destroy it completely, both its timbers and its stones."

The new Jerusalem won't merely be spacious and secure; it will be *just*. There will be no wrongdoing, no rebellion against God—only righteousness.

Remarkably, this righteousness extends beyond Israel:

> Many nations will be joined with the Lord in that day and will become my people.

The enemies themselves become God's people. This is the stunning reversal: those who once attacked God's people become part of His family.

## 3. God (2:10-13)

> Shout and be glad, Daughter Zion. For I am coming, and I will live among you, declares the Lord. Many nations will be joined with the Lord in that day and will become my people. I will live among you and you will know that the Lord Almighty has sent me to you. The Lord will inherit Judah as his portion in the holy land and will again choose Jerusalem. Be still before the Lord, all mankind, because he has roused himself from his holy dwelling.

The culmination of this vision is God's presence. Throughout the Old Testament, God's holiness made direct proximity to sinners impossible. But through Jesus, everything changes.

Jesus took upon Himself the "violent reaction" we deserved. He entered the most holy place once, not yearly like the high priests, paying for sin not through animal sacrifice but through His own blood. Jesus is the ultimate sacrifice, the one who takes away the sins of many and brings salvation.

Through faith in Jesus, we no longer approach God through temples and priests. We have full access to the Father through Christ's blood. We are adopted into God's family, called His children, made part of His household and temple.

The temple came to us in Jesus.

### The Ultimate Home

The vision finds its ultimate fulfillment in Revelation:

> Then I saw a new heaven and a new earth, for the first heaven and the first earth had passed away, and there was no longer any sea. I saw the Holy City, the new Jerusalem, coming down out of heaven from God, prepared as a bride beautifully dressed for her husband. And I heard a loud voice from the throne saying, "Look! God's dwelling place is now among the people, and he will dwell with them. They will be his people, and God himself will be with them and be their God. He will wipe every tear from their eyes. There will be no more death or mourning or crying or pain, for the old order of things has passed away." (Revelation 21:1-4)

In that new creation, we see:

- **Land**: A new Jerusalem, measured and prepared (Revelation 21:15)
- **People**: Every tribe and tongue (Revelation 7:9-10), with nothing impure permitted in the city (Revelation 21:8)
- **God**: Dwelling directly among His people (Revelation 21:3)

---

## Conclusion

Our earthly homes are temporary shelters—practical places to do God's work while we await our true home. When we miss out on an auction or struggle to find housing, we should remember: home is heaven. It's a place of infinite space, perfect safety, and—most importantly—God's presence.

The same principle applies to our churches. Five years ago, Arise began by praying for a space. Today, we continue that prayer. But we're not merely seeking real estate; we're asking God for a place to do His work while we wait for the ultimate gathering—when God Himself will dwell among us forever.

Come home to Jesus. There is nothing outside of Him for you—no other religion, no earthly pursuit—only Him. Return to Me, declares the Lord, and I will return to you.
