# Romans 6:1-23

**Title:** Alive to God

**Date:** 16 June 2013

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction: The Natural Question

Over the past two weeks, we've explored God's creation and redemption through Christ. But now a critical question arises: if Jesus has forgiven all our sins—past, present, and future—does that mean we can continue living in sin?

This logical question deserves a thoughtful answer. If Christ's death covers every sin we will ever commit, then logically it seems we should be free to sin as much as we want, knowing we're already forgiven.

Paul anticipates this exact question in Romans 6:

> What shall we say, then? Shall we go on sinning so that grace may increase? (Romans 6:1)

The word "abound" here connects to the previous chapter: grace abounds to cover our sins. So the natural follow-up becomes, "Can I abound in sin as well?"

Paul's answer is clear and emphatic:

> By no means! (Romans 6:2)

And he gives us two powerful reasons why we should not sin.

## Part 1: We Have Died to the Power of Sin

### Understanding Our Death with Christ

Paul's first argument is rooted in baptism—that powerful symbol of our conversion to Christ. When we became Christians, we underwent a radical transformation:

> All of us who were baptized into Christ Jesus were baptized into his death. (Romans 6:3)

Baptism represents our repentance and our decision to follow Jesus. It's our public declaration: "I will turn from sin and follow Christ." But more than that, it signifies something even deeper.

> We were therefore buried with him through baptism into death in order that, just as Christ was raised from the dead through the glory of the Father, we too may live a new life. (Romans 6:4)

On the day you became a Christian, something in you died. Your slavery to sin died. It no longer has power over you. It cannot kill you anymore.

Paul repeats this truth in different ways throughout this section:

> For if we have been united with him in a death like his, we will certainly also be united with him in a resurrection like his. (Romans 6:5)

> Now if we died with Christ, we believe that we will also live with him. (Romans 6:8)

> In the same way, count yourselves dead to sin but alive to God in Christ Jesus. (Romans 6:11)

### The Logic Is Inescapable

The question "Shall we sin?" is absurd once you understand this truth. We have died to sin. Why would you want to resurrect something that's dead?

Consider this illustration: Some of us have faced cancer—that terrifying diagnosis that forces you to confront your own mortality. You moved quickly to schedule surgery, endured the worry and pain, did everything necessary to remove that malignant growth from your body. That cancerous tissue, that life-draining abnormality, was removed and destroyed.

Now imagine someone asking you after the operation: "Would you like to frame a photo of your cancer and display it in your living room? Can we put it back in your body? Maybe in a different location? Perhaps it won't hurt as much this time."

What a ridiculous notion. And yet, this is exactly what we do when we knowingly return to sin after coming to Christ. Sin is that cancerous thing that was killing us from the inside. Jesus removed it. So why would we ever want it back?

### Remember What You're Leaving Behind

Think carefully about what sin brought you. Do you remember the tears, the sadness, the brokenness, the fights, the hurt it caused to those around you? The frustration, the disappointment, the hopelessness, the despair, the guilt? That sin made you die on the inside.

That's exactly why you went to Jesus in the first place. You knew you'd done wrong. You knew you couldn't fix it yourself. You knew you desperately needed to be changed. And so you repented.

The things that once seemed attractive to you should be disgusting now. Why would you be tempted to return to gossip, greed, stealing, cheating, anger, pornography, adultery? Why go back to serving false gods?

Brothers and sisters, Jesus has given you a second chance at life. There is nothing that sin can offer you that would be worth it. There are no promises sin makes that will ever be true. Sin only offers shame and death.

### Practical Response

So here's what we must do: struggle against temptation. Speak to someone about it, because sin hates to be exposed. Help each other keep sin dead. We have died to it; let's not resurrect it.

## Part 2: We Are Freed from Slavery to Sin

### A New Master

The second reason we should not sin is even more compelling: we have a new master. Paul brings up the earlier question again:

> What then? Shall we sin because we are not under the law but under grace? (Romans 6:15)

And he gives the same answer:

> By no means! (Romans 6:15b)

Paul uses the language of slavery to explain our new status:

> Don't you know that when you offer yourselves to someone as obedient slaves, you are slaves of the one you obey—whether you are slaves to sin, which leads to death, or to obedience, which leads to righteousness? But thanks be to God that, though you used to be slaves to sin, you have come to obey from your heart the pattern of teaching that has now claimed your allegiance. You have been set free from sin and have become slaves to righteousness. (Romans 6:16-18)

Here's the reality: we are all slaves to someone. We have two choices of masters.

The first master is Sin. He is a cruel boss. We were enslaved to him once, and we served him willingly—or at least, we had no choice.

> Just as you used to offer yourselves as slaves to impurity and to ever-increasing wickedness, so now offer yourselves as slaves to righteousness leading to holiness. (Romans 6:19)

When we were enslaved to sin, what happened? We were taken captive, tied up, held in chains, forced to serve. We were punished, abused, with no way to escape. And what did sin make us do? Wickedness. Evil. Things we're now ashamed of.

> When you were slaves to sin, you were free from the control of righteousness. (Romans 6:20)

> What benefit did you reap at that time from the things you are now ashamed of? Those things result in death! (Romans 6:21)

We got nothing from that slavery except shame and spiritual death.

### The Better Master

But then another master heard our cry. Another boss arose—one completely unlike Sin. This master is gentle and kind. His yoke is easy and his burden is light. Jesus defeated sin and freed us from its grip.

> But now that you have been set free from sin and have become slaves of God, the benefit you reap leads to holiness, and the result is eternal life. For the wages of sin is death, but the gift of God is eternal life in Christ Jesus our Lord. (Romans 6:22-23)

Sin is an evil master. It starts small—the first time you do something wrong, it controls you a little. The second time, you give it more ownership of your body. By the third time, sin becomes your master entirely. You want to escape but you can't. The compulsive behavior is too strong. You feel powerless.

But here's the beautiful truth: we were freed. We decided to leave that master and follow a new one. We died with Christ because Christ died for our sins. The rule of the old master is over.

### Why Return to Slavery?

Think about this practically. When I was a teenager, I worked at a grocery store for five dollars an hour. It was hard, dirty, dusty work. I had to lift heavy boxes, stand on my feet for six hours, do repetitive checkout work with no automation. It was poorly paid, boring, exhausting work.

If I had the choice, why would I ever go back to that job? Now that I have better opportunities, why would I return to that drudgery?

The same logic applies to sin. When you have God as your master, why would you go back to sin?

Yes, following God requires obedience. Yes, we are his servants. But his rule is infinitely better than serving sin. God is gentle, kind, and loves us. He died for us. What other master would do that for you?

> Therefore do not let sin reign in your mortal body so that you obey its evil desires. Do not offer any part of yourself to sin as an instrument of wickedness, but rather offer yourselves to God as those who have been brought from death to life; and offer every part of yourself to him as an instrument of righteousness. For sin shall no longer be your master, because you are not under the law, but under grace. (Romans 6:12-14)

Don't let sin control your body. Don't use your members as instruments of evil. Don't be like the Israelites who left Egypt but then longed to go back, forgetting how terrible slavery was. We forget how bad a master sin truly was.

To ask "Shall I sin?" is like asking "Shall I go back into slavery?" It makes no sense. You are no longer under sin's control.

### Using Our Bodies for Righteousness

Instead, let's serve God wholeheartedly. Let's offer every part of ourselves to him as an instrument of righteousness.

Let us use our eyes to see the needs of others. Let us use our mouths to speak wholesome words. Let us use our hands to help the weak and serve one another. Let our feet bring us to worship. Let our faces smile at each other, knowing that Jesus has died for each of our brothers and sisters.

### A Realistic Hope

This doesn't mean we'll live perfectly. We're on a slow journey of growth. We will stumble, we will be foolish, and we will sometimes forget. But here's the hope: we will quickly repent and get back on track with God.

And God will forgive you—not because you are sinless, but because his Son was sinless. His sinless Son died for us. When we remember that truth, logically we wouldn't want to sin anymore.

## Conclusion

We have died to sin. We have been freed from sin's power. Now let's live this new life, serving only God. This is what it means to be alive to God.
