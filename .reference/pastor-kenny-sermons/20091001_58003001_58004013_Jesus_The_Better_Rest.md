# Hebrews 3:1–4:13

**Title:** Jesus: The Better Rest
**Date:** 7 October 2009
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Keep Your Eyes on Jesus

As we begin, the writer of Hebrews calls us to fix our attention on Jesus. He is described in two essential ways:

- **Apostle** – the One sent from God
- **High Priest** – our human mediator and representative before God

The writer will show us that Jesus offers us rest, and we should place our complete confidence in Him to provide it. We have three reasons to keep our eyes on Jesus:

1. Jesus is greater than Moses
2. Israel missed out on God's rest
3. The rest is still to come

## 1. Jesus Is Greater Than Moses

> Jesus has been found worthy of greater honor than Moses, just as the builder of a house has greater honor than the house itself. For every house is built by someone, but God is the builder of everything. Moses was faithful as a servant in all God's house, testifying to what would be said in the future. But Christ is faithful as a son over God's house. (Hebrews 3:3–6)

### The Foundation: God Chooses to Relate Through His Mediator

To understand Jesus's superiority, we must first grasp the exceptional status God granted Moses. When Aaron and Miriam spoke against Moses, God called them to account publicly at the Tent of Meeting. God declared:

> When a prophet of the Lord is among you, I reveal myself to him in visions, I speak to him in dreams. But this is not true of my servant Moses; he is faithful in all my house. With him I speak face to face, clearly and not in riddles; he sees the form of the Lord. (Numbers 12:6–8)

This was extraordinary. While God sends His Spirit to other prophets through visions and dreams, Moses occupied an altogether different class. God spoke with Moses **face to face** – literally mouth to mouth, an element of profound intimacy. He spoke **clearly, not in riddles**, and Moses **saw the form of the Lord**. As Scripture confirms:

> The Lord would speak to Moses face to face, as a man speaks with his friend. (Exodus 33:11)

> Since then, no prophet has risen in Israel like Moses, whom the Lord knew face to face. (Deuteronomy 34:10)

God spoke to Moses as a friend.

### The Comparison: Servant Versus Son

The writer of Hebrews uses two powerful pictures to illustrate Jesus's superiority:

**First: The Builder and the House**

God is the builder of everything – including His people, whom He created and continues to sustain. The house represents us, God's building and His people. While both Moses and Jesus belong to this house, their roles differ fundamentally. Moses served as a faithful **servant** in the house; Jesus is faithful as the **Son** over the house.

**Second: The Difference Between Serving and Belonging**

This distinction carries profound implications:

- **A servant** has full access to every room – bedrooms, bathrooms, intimate spaces – but enters only to work: cleaning, serving, performing tasks. The servant's access is functional.

- **A son** has full access for comfort and belonging. He can relax in his parents' room, use their facilities not as a servant but as a family member. His access is relational and permanent.

- **A servant's role** is temporary and specific. **A son's role** carries permanent responsibility – managing the household, overseeing its affairs, bearing the weight of it 24/7.

Even though Moses was extraordinarily faithful – even serving as an example of faith later in Hebrews (chapter 11) – the comparison is not about Moses's faithfulness versus Jesus's faithlessness. Rather, it highlights that **even the greatest servant cannot compare to the Son**. When we say Jesus has greater honor than Moses, we are making an astounding claim.

### The Application: Enter the House as a Son

> And we are his house, if we hold on to our courage and the hope of which we boast. (Hebrews 3:6)

The privileges of belonging to God's house as His son or daughter are immense. We have access to the Master's room itself – direct access to God through Christ. But there is a condition: we must **hold on to our courage and hope**. We must remain faithful to Jesus.

## 2. Israel Missed Out: A Warning

> So, as the Holy Spirit says: "Today, if you hear his voice, do not harden your hearts as you did in the rebellion, during the time of testing in the desert, where your ancestors tested and tried me, though for forty years they saw what I did." (Hebrews 3:7–9)

### The Story of Unbelief

The writer now provides a sobering historical example. After leaving Egypt and receiving God's law at Mount Sinai, Israel advanced toward the Promised Land – a place of true rest from their slavery and wandering. But when scouts returned with reports of strong nations occupying the land, the people's hearts filled with fear.

Despite God's promise to defeat these nations just as He had defeated Egypt, Israel **hardened their hearts** and refused to enter. This moment of faithlessness was the climax of an entire journey marked by grumbling:

- Not enough bread
- Not enough water
- Not enough meat
- Longing to return to Egypt
- Fear of enemies

What was their response? When God finally said they would not enter His rest, they suddenly decided they wanted to go – but it was too late. They were defeated.

> That is why I was angry with that generation; I said, "Their hearts are always going astray, and they have not known my ways." So I declared on oath in my anger, "They shall never enter my rest." (Hebrews 3:10–11)

### The Consequences: Forty Years of Wandering

> Who were they who heard and rebelled? Were they not all those Moses led out of Egypt? And with whom was he angry for forty years? Was it not with those who sinned, whose bodies perished in the wilderness? And to whom did God swear that they would never enter his rest if not to those who disobeyed? (Hebrews 3:16–18)

An entire generation died in the wilderness, never entering the Promised Land. Only Joshua and Caleb – the scouts who trusted God – entered. The rest spent forty years wandering without rest, without home, without the fulfillment of God's promise.

### The Lesson: Guard Against Unbelief

The writer draws a direct application for us:

> See to it, brothers and sisters, that none of you has a sinful, unbelieving heart that turns away from the living God. (Hebrews 3:12)

Faithlessness expresses itself as grumbling and disobedience. We must guard against hearts that:

- Grumble about hardship and persecution
- Complain about the cost of following Christ
- Resist God's ways in search of easier paths
- Refuse to trust God when faith is tested

The antidote is not isolation but **community encouragement**:

> But encourage one another daily, as long as it is called "Today," so that none of you may be hardened by sin's deceitfulness. We have come to share in Christ, if indeed we hold our original conviction firmly to the very end. (Hebrews 3:13–14)

### How to Encourage One Another

**In community**: Rather than focusing inward on our own discontent, we turn outward to build up one another. True encouragement means pointing others to Jesus – not merely offering solutions or positive affirmation, but grounding them in Christ's faithfulness.

**In frequency**: The word "daily" is crucial. Encouragement cannot be occasional; it must be woven into the rhythm of our lives. This happens:

- In church gatherings (formal)
- In small groups (regular)
- In informal conversations with Christian friends
- Around the table at meals, through prayer and grace
- Whenever two or more believers meet

**In urgency**: The word "Today" reminds us not to delay. The opportunity to encourage is now, not at some future convenient moment.

## 3. The Rest Is Still to Come

> Therefore, since the promise of entering his rest still stands, let us be careful that none of you be found to have fallen short of it. For we also have had the gospel preached to us, just as they did; but the message they heard was of no value to them, because those who heard did not combine it with faith. (Hebrews 4:1–2)

### The Paradox: Past and Future Rest

Here is where the passage becomes subtle. The writer says the promise of rest "still stands" – yet Israel eventually did enter the Promised Land under Joshua, and David ruled over it. So why does the writer insist the rest remains future?

The answer lies in understanding what the **real rest** is. The physical land was a shadow of a deeper reality.

### Not the Physical Land, But God's Rest

> Now we who have believed enter that rest, just as God has said, "So I declared on oath in my anger, 'They shall never enter my rest.' " And yet his work has been finished since the creation of the world. For somewhere he has spoken about the seventh day in these words: "And on the seventh day God rested from all his work." (Hebrews 4:3–4)

The writer reinterprets the Psalm 95 quotation in light of Genesis. God's **rest** refers to the Sabbath rest God entered after creation – a rest from His work, eternal and complete. The Promised Land was a type of this greater rest. Those who refused to enter the land missed out not merely on geography, but on what it symbolized: **God's eternal rest**.

> Therefore God again set a certain day, calling it "Today," when a long time later he spoke through David, as was said before: "Today, if you hear his voice, do not harden your hearts." For if Joshua had given them rest, God would not have spoken later about another day. (Hebrews 4:6–8)

Even David's kingdom and Joshua's conquest were not the final rest. The writer points us beyond all earthly fulfillments to the **heavenly rest that is still to come** – the new creation itself.

### The Sabbath Reinterpreted

The ritual Sabbath (Saturday) was given to Israel as a sign and reminder of the bigger Sabbath – God's eternal rest. The Israelites were to rest one day weekly, remembering that God provides and that one day, complete rest would come.

But now that Jesus has come:

- He has confirmed that the true rest is heaven, God's new creation
- He is the Sabbath for humanity – our rest
- The old ritual Sabbath is fulfilled and no longer binding

We are not obligated to observe Saturday as Sabbath, nor does Sunday replace it as a sacred day. Rather, **Jesus Himself is our rest**. We are not to substitute old rituals with new ones, but to find our ceasing from labor in Him alone.

> There remains, then, a Sabbath-rest for the people of God; for anyone who enters God's rest also rests from his own work, just as God did from his. (Hebrews 4:9–10)

## 4. Therefore, Make Every Effort to Enter That Rest

> Let us, therefore, make every effort to enter that rest, so that no one will fall by following their example of disobedience. (Hebrews 4:11)

### Guard Your Heart in Daily Decisions

Making every effort to enter God's rest is not passive. It requires active faithfulness in the choices we face:

**In relationships**: If a romantic relationship – whether with a Christian or non-Christian – is pulling you away from faithfulness to Christ, end it. Do not stay hoping for change. The daily reality of marriage or partnership will either build you up or drag you away. Choose wisely, for these everyday moments add up to your spiritual trajectory.

**In work and study**: If your career or course demands so much that it crowds out encouragement, community, and your spiritual life – and this is not temporary but a permanent state – reconsider. Make every effort means prioritizing what truly matters. If you are wasting hours on distraction and entertainment when you should be studying or serving – stop. Redirect your life toward the goal.

**In community commitments**: Make every effort to participate in what strengthens your faith – whether formal church gatherings, Bible study groups, or informal friendships with other believers. Set your schedule accordingly. Build your life around staying the course with Jesus.

### Do Not Harden Your Heart

The history of Israel shows us the danger of a hardened heart. Small acts of disobedience – grumbling, wavering faith, choosing comfort over conviction – gradually harden us toward God. Guard against this. If you know something is wrong – whether it is dishonesty, moral compromise, or anything that pulls you from Christ – repent today. Do not let sin's deceitfulness convince you that you can coast.

### The Long View: Finish Strong

The Christian life is a race, and we must keep our eyes fixed on the finish line, not on how fast others are going or how much ground we have covered. Steven Bradbury, an Australian speed skater, won gold at the 2002 Winter Olympics – not by being the fastest, but by staying in the race while others crashed. After the race, he said: "I don't think I'll take the medal as the minute-and-a-half of the race I actually won. I'll take it as the last decade of the hard slog I put in."

Bradbury endured terrible injuries – his leg was sliced open by a skate blade, he lost four liters of blood, he broke his neck. Many times he was tempted to quit, thinking he had no chance of a medal. But he stayed focused. He finished.

Similarly, the Christian race is difficult. You may feel like you are at the back of the pack. You may not see the finish line. You may be tempted to give up. But do not. Keep your eyes fixed on Jesus – **the rock**, immovable and eternal. Do not waver. Do not harden your heart. Do not give in to faithlessness.

## The Word of God: Living and Active

> For the word of God is living and active. Sharper than any double-edged sword, it penetrates even to dividing soul and spirit, joints and marrow; it judges the thoughts and attitudes of the heart. Nothing in all creation is hidden from God's sight. Everything is uncovered and laid bare before the eyes of him to whom we must give account. (Hebrews 4:12–13)

When we encourage one another, we bring the word of God into the conversation. Not mere human advice or positive thinking, but **the word of God** – which works miracles not through spectacular healings or amazing feats, but by changing your mind. It exposes and transforms your thinking, persuading you that God's way is infinitely better than your own.

## Conclusion: Hold Firmly to Your Faith

> Therefore, since we have a great high priest who has gone through the heavens, Jesus the Son of God, let us hold firmly to the faith we profess. For we do not have a high priest who is unable to sympathize with our weaknesses, but we have one who has been tempted in every way, just as we are – yet was without sin. Let us then approach the throne of grace with confidence, so that we may receive mercy and find grace to help us in our time of need. (Hebrews 4:14–16)

You may feel tired from the work and struggles of life. You may long for rest. But do not confuse temporary relief with the **true rest** Jesus offers. The rest we anticipate is:

- **Eternal** – not temporary
- **Provided by Jesus alone** – not something we can earn or buy
- **For all who believe** – available to you now, fulfilled in eternity

Keep your eyes on Jesus. Do not harden your heart. Encourage one another daily. Make every effort to enter His rest. The finish line is ahead. Stay faithful until you reach it.
