**Title:** Fair-Weather Friends

## Introduction: The Proverb and Job's Situation

"A friend in need is a friend indeed"—this old saying captures what many of us hope for during difficult times. Yet the book of Job shows us what happens when friends fail us, even with good intentions.

Last week we learned that Job is a righteous man, yet he's experiencing tremendous suffering. He has lost his children, his wealth, and is afflicted with a severe skin disease. This challenges our basic understanding of how the world works: shouldn't good people prosper and evildoers suffer?

Today, Job's friends have come to console him. We meet:

> Eliphaz the Temanite  
> Bildad the Shuhite  
> Zophar the Naamathite  
> Elihu (who appears later)

For seven days and nights they sit with him in silence—which is actually the helpful part. But then they speak, and their words become a problem.

---

## 1. The Logic of Retributive Justice

Eliphaz offers what seems like sound reasoning. He argues from Job 4:7-8:

> "Consider now: Who, being innocent, has ever perished? Where were the upright ever destroyed? As I have observed, those who plow evil and those who sow trouble reap it."

**Eliphaz's Logic:** Either you are righteous and don't suffer, or you are a sinner and therefore you suffer. By this logic, since Job is suffering, he must have sinned.

### The Appeal of This Thinking

There's truth here. The world operates by cause and effect. We understand this principle naturally:

> Galatians 6:7-8: "Do not be deceived: God cannot be mocked. A man reaps what he sows. Whoever sows to please their flesh, from the flesh will reap destruction; whoever sows to please the Spirit, from the Spirit will reap eternal life."

> James 1:14-15: "Each person is tempted when they are dragged away by their own evil desire and enticed. Then, after desire has conceived, it gives birth to sin; and sin, when it is full-grown, gives birth to death."

There are real consequences to our actions. If you neglect your studies and play computer games instead, you will fail your exams. If you gamble, you'll lose money. If you smoke, you risk cancer. The world is ordered by cause and effect.

### A Deeper Problem

But here's what troubles me: as Christians, we sometimes operate as though this principle doesn't apply to our moral and spiritual lives. We plan meticulously for school and career—studying leads to success, which leads to college, which leads to a good job. We calculate cause and effect everywhere else. Yet in the Christian life, we often think: *I can do whatever I want, and God will somehow rescue me from the consequences.*

- We marry someone who doesn't share our values and are surprised when our children don't inherit our faith.
- We hand our kids over entirely to secular education and then wonder why they return as "Romans."
- We follow atheistic media and worldly values, expecting somehow our children will be Christian anyway.
- We avoid political involvement, assuming God will magically give us a good city to live in.
- We think we'll beat the odds, that statistics don't apply to us, that this one last time will be different.

Yet God created an ordered world with cause and effect. Einstein's definition of insanity applies: doing the same thing over and over and expecting different results. We cannot escape the basic structure of creation.

## 2. But Job Is Righteous

Here's the problem with Eliphaz's argument: **God already told us Job is righteous.** Look at what God says in chapters 1 and 2:

> Job 1:8: "Then the Lord said to Satan, 'Have you considered my servant Job? There is no one on earth like him; he is blameless and upright, a man who fears God and shuns evil.'"
>
> Job 2:3: "Have you considered my servant Job? There is no one on earth like him; he is blameless and upright, a man who fears God and shuns evil."
>
> Job 1:22: "In all this, Job did not sin by charging God with wrongdoing."
>
> Job 2:10: "In all this, Job did not sin in what he said."

Job himself testifies to his integrity:

> Job 6:24-30: "Teach me, and I will be quiet; show me where I have been wrong... Do you mean to correct what I say, and treat my desperate words as wind?... Would I lie to your face? Relent, do not be unjust; reconsider, for my integrity is at stake. Is there any wickedness on my lips? Can my mouth not discern malice?"

Job cries out: *Don't blame the victim here. Tell me where I've gone wrong!*

His friends respond like those who blame Charlie Kirk for being assassinated—suggesting he must have done something to deserve it, or that "both sides" are equally responsible. But that's victim-blaming. There is such a thing as a righteous person:

> 1 John 3:7: "Dear children, do not let anyone lead you astray. The one who does what is right is righteous, just as he is righteous."

We've become uncomfortable with this language in modern times. We want to believe everyone is equally culpable, equally sinful. But Scripture recognizes degrees of righteousness. Yes, all of us are sinners, infinitely sinful before God. But relatively speaking, there is a difference. That ten thousand difference between infinite sin and infinite sin minus ten thousand is what separates a civil society from chaos.

## 3. The Exception: God's Sovereignty

Yet here's the crucial limitation to Eliphaz's neat theology of retribution. While cause and effect is generally true, **there are times when it doesn't apply.**

### The Blind Man from Birth (John 9)

When Jesus encounters a man born blind, His disciples ask the question on everyone's minds:

> John 9:1-2: "As he went along, he saw a man blind from birth. His disciples asked him, 'Rabbi, who sinned, this man or his parents, that he was born blind?'"

Even the Pharisees later accuse him: "You were steeped in sin at birth; how dare you lecture us!"

But Jesus gives a stunning answer:

> John 9:3: "Neither this man nor his parents sinned. But this happened so that the work of God might be displayed in his life."

**There is a third cause for suffering beyond personal sin or parental sin: God's purposes.** Not everything that happens is because someone sinned. Sometimes God has other plans.

### Jesus Himself: The Ultimate Exception

And consider Jesus. He is innocent—perfectly righteous—yet He suffers undeservedly on the cross:

> John 9:4-5: "As long as it is day, we must do the work of him who sent me. Night is coming, when no one can work. While I am in the world, I am the light of the world."

From an earthly perspective, it looks like injustice—an innocent man executed. But something else is happening that we cannot see. The foundations of the temple are being shaken. The penalty for sin is being paid. **The way to God is being opened.**

Jesus Himself subjects Himself to undeserved suffering. This means God is not running the universe by karma—a system where you always get what you deserve. There is room for God's sovereignty. There are "acts of God," as insurance policies recognize—suffering that comes with no blame attached.

### The Gift of Grace

This is actually good news. If we eliminated God's sovereignty and everything operated by strict cause and effect, we'd live in a litigious nightmare. We'd always be looking for someone to sue, someone to blame for our misfortune. But a world with God's sovereignty allows for suffering without blame and, more importantly, **for grace—undeserved mercy.**

If we don't believe in undeserved suffering, we can't truly believe in undeserved grace. If our only response to hardship is "what have I done to deserve this?" then when good comes, we'll think "I must have done something right." We forget God.

> Hebrews 12:5-6: "My son, do not make light of the Lord's discipline, and do not lose heart when he rebukes you, because the Lord disciplines the one he loves, and he chastens everyone he accepts as his son."

Sometimes suffering is God's discipline and preparation for us—not punishment for sin, but training because He loves us.

## 4. Job's Friends: Unhelpful Comfort

Despite their seven days of silent presence, **Job's friends ultimately fail him** because they misunderstand God's sovereignty. They cannot hold together the reality of an ordered, moral universe with the reality of God's sovereign freedom.

### How to Be a Better Friend

Job's experience teaches us how to support those who suffer:

- **Listen and reflect back** what you hear without judgment
- **Distinguish between comfort and solutions.** Comfort is short-term presence; solutions are long-term answers. Sometimes there are no solutions—only a hand to hold and a shoulder to cry on.
- **Don't offer easy answers** or spiritual platitudes
- **Pray for them** rather than trying to explain their suffering

### The Better Friend: Jesus

Ultimately, earthly friends—however well-meaning—will disappoint us. But Jesus offers something more:

He knows what undeserved suffering is like. He has walked in our shoes. He doesn't theorize about suffering; He has experienced it. And most importantly, He stayed. He held on. He went through death itself and came out the other side.

As the hymn says: "Jesus loves me, this I know, for the Bible tells me so." Not because I deserve it, but because He does.

## Discussion Questions

1. What is grace? How can understanding grace help us understand undeserved suffering?
2. How can I be a better friend to those who are suffering?
3. How can Jesus' friendship help us in our sufferings? Have you received His offer of friendship?
4. Where have you experienced cause and effect in life? Where have you experienced grace—something good you didn't deserve?
