# Matthew 1:18-25

**Title:** The Birth of Hope
**Date:** 17 December 2011
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Significance of Names

Names carry weight. Some are frivolous—fruit names chosen on a whim, or the 2,500 babies named "Olympic" in China during 2008. But most names represent something deeper: either history or hope, often both.

History lives in names. We name our children after Winston Churchill, biblical characters we admire, or grandparents whose legacy we want to honor. Hope also lives in names. Chinese parents might give their daughters names meaning "add a brother," expressing their desires for the future. Some names—like "Harvard"—carry the dreams of entire families and cultures.

Jesus's names are no different. In his birth, we see both the culmination of history and the fulfillment of hope.

## Jesus: The Historical Messiah

> This is how the birth of Jesus Christ came about: His mother Mary was pledged to be married to Joseph, but before they came together, she was found to be with child through the Holy Spirit. (Matthew 1:18)

The Gospel of Matthew begins with a genealogy—forty names, forty generations stretching back to Abraham. On the surface, this seems like an odd way to start a story. Imagine opening an autobiography or essay with a list of names. It would be terrible writing. Yet Matthew is making a crucial point: Jesus is no random figure. He is part of a continuous line from Abraham's time to his own.

This genealogy shows us that God's promises to Abraham—land, people, blessings—find their answer in Jesus. The forty names might even echo the forty years Israel wandered in the wilderness, waiting for the promised land. The wait is over. Jesus has arrived.

**Why this matters for us:** Jesus is not a fairy tale. He is a real historical person with a real family line—a mother, a stepfather, grandparents, great-grandparents. We can verify his existence. Unlike Cinderella or Santa Claus, Jesus walked this earth in history.

## Jesus: The One Who Saves

> She will give birth to a son, and you are to give him the name Jesus, because he will save his people from their sins. (Matthew 1:21)

The name Jesus comes from the Hebrew Yoshua, which means "Yahweh saves." It's a functional name, like how "Teddy" suggests a wealthy guardian, or "Henry" means "ruler of the home." But while most names don't perfectly describe their bearers—not every Ted is wealthy, not every Kenny is particularly handsome—Jesus lives up to his name completely.

He doesn't just represent salvation; he *becomes* salvation. He fulfills his purpose by voluntarily laying down his life on the cross, bearing the agony of death so that we might be saved from our sins.

Consider this: I have two cousins in Malaysia, sisters. The older was dying from a blood disease and needed a bone marrow transplant. Unable to find a suitable donor, my aunt and uncle made an extraordinary decision. They conceived a second child specifically so she could be a match for her sister. My younger cousin was born for one purpose: to save her sister's life. The process was painful for both girls, but both survived and are healthy today.

Jesus was born to save. Not just one person, but the whole world. Not just for one lifetime, but for eternity. This Christmas, don't picture a cute baby in a manger. Picture instead someone born for the specific purpose of transplanting a new heart—Christ's heart—into your defective, sinful heart. And in doing so, bearing all the agony of death.

## Jesus: The Anointed King

Jesus is called "Christ," but this is not his surname. Christ is the Greek word for the Hebrew term Messiah, which means "the anointed one." In ancient Israel, oil was poured on leaders—kings like David, priests of Israel—to establish them. This was how Israel's kings were crowned, not with a crown on the head, but with oil.

Jesus the Christ means Jesus the King, the anointed one in the line of David. But here's what we must remember: he is not some cuddly baby confined to a manger. He is the adult King, seated at God's right hand. We must follow him and obey him.

## Jesus: God With Us

> All this took place to fulfill what the Lord had said through the prophet: "The virgin will be with child and will give birth to a son, and they will call him Immanuel"—which means, "God with us." (Matthew 1:22-23)

For those of us who are Christian, Jesus has yet another name: Immanuel. God with us.

Think about what this means. At that first Christmas, Jesus was one person among 150 million on earth. Yet if you had been there, with Mary's permission, you could have touched him, held him, been in his presence. God was physically present with humanity. And through Jesus, God remains with us still—not physically in the same way, but truly, genuinely present.

You are not alone. God is with you through Jesus.

## Your Name: Christian

> However, if you suffer as a Christian, do not be ashamed, but praise God that you bear that name. (1 Peter 4:16)

Just as Jesus bears meaningful names—Savior, Christ, Emmanuel—so do you. You bear the name Christian. Christ-ian. One who follows Christ.

Don't be ashamed of this name, even if it's unpopular in your class or among your friends. Bear it with pride. There are several ways you can live out this identity:

- Invite others to Christian groups at school, openly identifying yourself as Christian
- Let your friends know you're a Christian through your conversations
- Reflect a God-dependent attitude in how you speak—saying "God bless you," "Praise God," or "I'll pray for you"
- Put a Bible verse on your folder or a fish sticker on your pencil case
- Find other creative ways to let people know who you are

It might feel scary. But consider what your name represents: the weight of history—all those who have gone before you in faith. And the weight of hope—your service to God and his kingdom. You bear the most wonderful of all names. Christ has died for you. You will be rescued on the day of judgment. Christ is with you and rules over you with grace and compassion.

Live out your name.
