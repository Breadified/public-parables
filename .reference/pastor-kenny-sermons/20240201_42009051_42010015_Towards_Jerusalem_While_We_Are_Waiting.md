# Luke 9:51-10:15

**Title:** Towards Jerusalem: While We Are Waiting
**Date:** 4 February 2024
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## 1. Waiting for Judgment (9:51-56)

> As the time approached for him to be taken up to heaven, Jesus resolutely set out for Jerusalem. (9:51)

The Greek phrase "to complete the days of ascension" can refer both to Jesus' ultimate ascension after his resurrection and to his journey up to Jerusalem—a city positioned at higher elevation than the surrounding areas. The plural "days" suggests Luke is describing the journey itself rather than a single moment of ascension. Jesus literally "set his face toward Jerusalem" with resolute determination.

> And he sent messengers on ahead, who went into a Samaritan village to get things ready for him. (9:52)

To reach Jerusalem from Galilee, Jesus had to pass through Samaria. The Samaritans were considered half-Jews—descendants who had intermarried during Assyrian rule centuries before. This created deep religious divisions. The Jews rejected them as true Israelites, and the Samaritans developed their own religious tradition centered on Mount Gerizim rather than the Jerusalem temple (recall the conversation with the woman at the well in John 4).

> But the people there did not welcome him, because he was heading for Jerusalem. (9:53)

When the villagers discovered Jesus intended to go to Jerusalem—the temple that they rejected—they refused him hospitality. The disciples' response was predictable:

> When the disciples James and John saw this, they asked, "Lord, do you want us to call fire down from heaven to destroy them?" (9:54)

James and John, nicknamed "the sons of thunder," were fresh from their successes in Galilee. They wanted to invoke divine judgment, modeling themselves after the prophet Elijah. Nearly a thousand years earlier, Elijah had called fire from heaven to consume soldiers in Samaria (1 Kings 1:10). In the biblical symbolism, fire represents the appropriate response to sin and rebellion—the destruction of what is corrupted, reducing it to elements that might be reconstituted elsewhere. Yet Jesus rebuked them:

> But Jesus turned and rebuked them. Then he and his disciples went to another village. (9:55-56)

The disciples had misunderstood Jesus' purpose. At this time in history, Jesus came not to condemn but to save:

> For God did not send his Son into the world to condemn the world, but to save the world through him. (John 3:17)

> For I did not come to judge the world, but to save the world. (John 12:47)

**The total judgment has not yet come.** We live in a mixed world where good and evil coexist, awaiting the day of final reckoning.

This reality calls us to intellectual humility. We often want to see the world in black and white—wars ceased, bad people in jail, good people rewarded. Yet the world that awaits judgment is fundamentally ambiguous. Good and bad are intertwined in nearly every institution and choice. Political parties are mixed bags; corporations have both exploitative and beneficial aspects; geopolitical conflicts rarely have purely righteous sides.

When we encounter ambiguity, we cannot simply demand perfect purity from every institution. Most of our real choices are between the slightly better and the slightly worse option. Wisdom means recognizing this and consistently choosing what is more just, even when perfect justice remains elusive. We need tolerance for ambiguity and humility about our ability to judge.

If we want to improve the world, we must begin with ourselves and our relationship with God.

## 2. Come Follow Jesus (9:57-62)

> As they were walking along the road, a man said to him, "I will follow you wherever you go." (9:57)

Three brief stories illustrate what following Jesus truly demands.

> Jesus replied, "Foxes have dens and birds have nests, but the Son of Man has no place to lay his head." (9:58)

The man's promise to follow "wherever" Jesus goes meets a sobering reality. Animals have homes—foxes have dens, birds have nests—but Jesus has nowhere to sleep. If our Master is homeless, so will we be. Discipleship requires accepting homelessness and instability.

> He said to another man, "Follow me." But he replied, "Lord, first let me go and bury my father." (9:59)

When Jesus directly calls this second man, he offers a condition: he must first attend to his father's funeral. Burying one's father is a crucial expression of honoring parents. Yet Jesus responds:

> Jesus said to him, "Let the dead bury their own dead, but you go and proclaim the kingdom of God." (9:60)

This seems harsh, but the emphasis falls on one word: "first." Jesus is not forbidding funeral attendance or dishonoring parents (Genesis 50:5 shows Jacob requesting proper burial). Rather, he challenges the man's prioritization. Jesus stands before him personally, on a mission to Jerusalem. "Wait until later" is not a viable response when the Master himself is present.

> Still another said, "I will follow you, Lord; but first let me go back and say goodbye to my family." (9:61)

Again, the word "first" reveals the issue. Jesus is not commanding us to abandon our families. This is not the destructive approach of certain cults that sever all family ties. In Scripture, children have special responsibility to parents, and parents to children—responsibilities that remain regardless of circumstances.

But Jesus highlights a deeper issue rooted in the First Commandment:

> You shall have no other gods before me. (Exodus 20:3)

When push comes to shove, whose priorities do we follow? For some believers, families may not share their faith. Their worldly concerns, ambitions, and dreams may directly conflict with yours. At some point, you must live out Jesus' priorities rather than your family's. This serves not only your spiritual growth but ultimately theirs as well.

> Jesus replied, "No one who puts a hand to the plow and looks back is fit for service in the kingdom of God." (9:62)

The image of plowing captures the essence of discipleship. Jesus turns toward Jerusalem; disciples must face forward, not backward. Looking back means allowing other gods—distractions, earthly comfort, family expectations, bucket list dreams—to pull your focus.

We live comfortably with many tempting distractions: remote work allows us to live anywhere; discounts make consumption easy; we can delay major commitments indefinitely. Yet if we are serious about following Christ, these earthly things must not become our primary focus. Dreams are dreams. The present moment with Christ demands our whole heart.

## 3. Go Proclaim (10:1-15)

> After this the Lord appointed seventy-two others and sent them two by two ahead of him to every town and place where he was about to go. (10:1)

Earlier in Luke 9, Jesus sent twelve disciples to gather Jews from Galilee. Now he appoints seventy-two others—a number symbolizing the inclusion of Gentiles and Samaritans, the nations beyond Israel. They are sent ahead to prepare the way.

> He told them, "The harvest is plentiful, but the workers are few. Ask the Lord of the harvest, therefore, to send out workers into his harvest field. Go! I am sending you out like lambs among wolves." (10:2-3)

The harvest refers to the gathering before God's judgment (Isaiah 27:12). The workers are few for such urgent work. The disciples go as vulnerable lambs among wolves—they have no protection except the Lord's authority.

> Do not take a purse or bag or sandals; and do not greet anyone on the road. (10:4)

This instruction emphasizes urgency and speed. No time for extended greetings or social pleasantries. The echo here recalls Elisha's hasty mission (2 Kings 4:29): move quickly toward the goal.

> When you enter a house, first say, "Peace to this house." If someone who promotes peace is there, your peace will rest on them; if not, it will return to you. Stay there, eating and drinking whatever they give you, for the worker deserves his wages. Do not move around from house to house. (10:5-7)

Upon arrival, disciples offer peace. If accepted, they remain—developing relationships, accepting hospitality without seeking upgrades from house to house. The worker deserves sustenance for the work.

> When you enter a town and are welcomed, eat what is offered to you. Heal the sick who are there and tell them, "The kingdom of God has come near to you." (10:8-9)

The message is clear: "The kingdom of God has come near"—Jesus himself is coming to this town. The disciples are preparing the way, announcing his arrival.

### Those Who Accept

> The seventy-two returned with joy and said, "Lord, even the demons submit to us in your name." (10:17)

Their rejoicing at spiritual power receives a course correction:

> However, do not rejoice that the spirits submit to you, but rejoice that your names are written in heaven. (10:20)

The ultimate aim is not supernatural signs but the salvation of souls. Your name written in heaven—that is the true triumph.

### Those Who Reject

> But when you enter a town and are not welcomed, go into its streets and say, "Even the dust of your town we wipe from our feet as a warning to you. Yet be sure of this: The kingdom of God has come near." I tell you, it will be more bearable on that day for Sodom than for that town. (10:10-12)

> Woe to you, Chorazin! Woe to you, Bethsaida! For if the miracles that were performed in you had been performed in Tyre and Sidon, they would have repented long ago, sitting in sackcloth and ashes. But it will be more bearable for Tyre and Sidon at the judgment than for you. And you, Capernaum, will you be lifted to the heavens? No, you will go down to Hades. (10:13-15)

Jesus lists towns destroyed by God in history—their judgment will be worse than that of pagan cities like Sodom, Tyre, and Sidon. Those towns at least never received Jesus' personal ministry and miracles. The rejection of the kingdom carries dire consequences.

## Application: Our Role in the Harvest

We are workers in this harvest, sent one by one to proclaim the gospel. This is not a future task—it is our present calling:

- **At your workplace:** Speak of Jesus amid your colleagues' daily lives
- **At school:** Many schools have Christian groups or Scripture classes—these are your platforms
- **With friends and family:** What greater honor can you give unbelieving family than to tell them about Jesus and have their names written in the book of life?

Prepare the way for his return. Tell the gospel. The kingdom of God has come near.

---

## The Cost: Jesus Heads to Jerusalem

Why does Jesus resolutely set his face toward Jerusalem? Not for a new career, a summer break, sightseeing, or family reunions. Jerusalem is a place of death. He will be judged, sentenced, and executed there. Yet he does not turn back.

Our cities are not immune from judgment. Fire will come sooner or later. We do not see the fire now because Jesus pauses it—he sacrifices his life to buy us extra time. He pours out his blood to purchase our lives and extend mercy.

We must not waste the extra time he has given us. As we head toward Jerusalem, toward Easter, will you follow Christ resolutely without turning back? Will you prepare the way for his return by proclaiming the gospel?

Don't turn back. Go forward. The harvest is plentiful, and the workers are few.
