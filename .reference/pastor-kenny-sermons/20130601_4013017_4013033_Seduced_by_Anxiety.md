# Numbers 13:17-33

**Title:** Seduced by Anxiety

**Date:** 9 June 2013

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Understanding Phobia

A phobia is an irrational, persistent fear of certain situations, objects, activities, or persons. While phobias can seem odd—there are over 530 documented types—they represent real suffering for those who experience them. Behind each diagnosis is a person wrestling with genuine anxiety that can lead to powerlessness, hopelessness, and depression. Many of us struggle with some version of these fears, whether major or minor, and their accompanying symptoms of low confidence and difficulty functioning in society.

## Part 1: Finally Here (13:1-25)

After eleven days in the wilderness, the Israelites have overcome hunger, jealousy, and divine judgment. Moses finally sends them to explore Canaan with specific instructions.

> When Moses sent them to explore Canaan, he said, "Go up through the Negev and on into the hill country." (13:17)

Moses asks the spies to investigate three things:

**1. The People:** Are they strong or weak, few or many?

**2. The Land's Fertility:** What is the quality of the soil? Are there trees? The spies should bring back fruit as evidence.

**3. The Cities:** Are they fortified or unwalled?

The explorers conduct a thorough forty-day survey. The number forty appears throughout Scripture as a symbol of sufficiency—sufficient judgment in the flood, sufficient time for Jesus to prove His resurrection. So forty days represents adequate time for the spies to gather conclusive evidence.

They discover remarkable abundance:

> They came back to Moses and Aaron and the whole Israelite community at Kadesh in the Desert of Paran. There they reported to them and to the whole assembly and showed them the fruit of the land. (13:26)

They bring grapes so large that two men must carry a single cluster on a pole, along with pomegranates and figs. The land objectively flows with milk and honey—representing health, prosperity, security, and abundance. For people emerging from slavery and wandering the desert, this would be their version of heaven.

**The Promised Land as Heaven**

The Promised Land serves as an analogy for heaven itself—a place of:
- Full health and prosperity
- The complete absence of evil
- Healthy relationships
- No more conflict
- Perfect security

In contrast, this present earth is temporary, like a rental property where we live briefly before moving to our true home. Christians wisely store treasures in heaven, looking forward to what lasts eternally.

## Part 2: Unreasonable Fear (13:26-14:10)

Despite the objective good news, the spies' majority report is disappointing. They represent an unreasonable fear that seduces them and the people.

**They Discount the Good News**

> "We went into the land to which you sent us, and it does flow with milk and honey! Here is its fruit." (13:27)

Notice the dismissal: they reduce all evidence to a single Hebrew word—"fruit"—a mere grunt that brushes aside the overwhelming fertility. They fail to mention the pomegranates, figs, and grapes. More significantly, they omit Hebron entirely.

Hebron is emotionally and spiritually significant—the burial place of Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob, where God first promised the land to the patriarchs. To ignore Hebron is to avert their gaze from the tombs of their fathers and neglect God's promises. It's as though a military force entering battle refused to mention Gallipoli or their historical legacy—they silenced the language that could inspire confidence.

Instead, they emphasize only negatives:

> "But the people who live there are powerful, and the cities are fortified and very large. We even saw descendants of Anak there." (13:28)

The land of Canaan comprises three geographical zones: the coastal plain with many inhabitants, the hill country with sparse population, and the river valley. Moses explicitly directed them to the hill country—the safer route—but they explored everywhere at once, seeing all enemies simultaneously.

**The Exaggeration of Fear**

> "We can't attack those people; they are stronger than we are. ... The land devours those living in it. All the people we saw there are of great size. We saw the Nephilim there.... We seemed like grasshoppers in our own eyes, and we looked the same to them." (13:31-33)

Their fear causes them to look only at bad news, generating more anxiety and spiraling into despair. This is how unreasonable fear operates: it feeds on itself.

**Application: Recognizing Unreasonable Fear**

Many of us experience this pattern. When anxious, we seek more bad news. We ignore blessings and focus on potential dangers. Year 12 students fear exam marks as if this single number determines their entire future. University entrance feels final, yet most people change courses and careers multiple times. One person is not "the be-all and end-all"—whether a romantic interest or a career path.

Ask yourself: Are we fearing that we won't achieve a magic number to enter courses we'll probably abandon for jobs that may no longer exist? In doing so, we give up church, family, Bible reading, and God itself. This is unreasonable fear.

**The True Perspective**

The Israelites should have remembered their own experience. In Egypt, the plague of locusts—tiny grasshoppers—stopped the mighty Pharaoh. Yet grasshoppers assembled in swarms become an unstoppable force:

> "They are more numerous than locusts, they cannot be counted." (Jeremiah 46:23)
>
> "Locusts have no king, yet they advance together in ranks." (Proverbs 30:27)

The Israelites had been numbered and organized as an army—over 600,000 soldiers arrayed for conquest. As a unified body, they would have won.

The real danger wasn't the enemies. It was this: "We seemed like grasshoppers in our own eyes." Their success depended not on how they saw themselves, but on how God saw them. With God's tent in the middle of their camp, they were never alone.

Listen instead to Joshua and Caleb:

> "The land we passed through and explored is exceedingly good. If the LORD is pleased with us, he will lead us into that land, a land flowing with milk and honey, and will give it to us. Only do not rebel against the LORD. And do not be afraid of the people of the land, because we will swallow them up. Their protection is gone, but the LORD is with us. Do not be afraid of them." (14:7-9)

Success rested on one thing alone: "If the Lord is pleased with us" and "The Lord is with us."

At this crucial moment demanding trust, they feared and grumbled:

> "If only we had died in Egypt! Or in this desert! Why is the LORD bringing us to this land only to let us fall by the sword? Our wives and children will be taken as plunder. Wouldn't it be better for us to go back to Egypt?" (14:2-3)

**When Fear Tempts Us**

Some might fear staying Christian. In stressful moments, we doubt whether Jesus is real, fear what unbelieving friends think, or fear missing out on worldly pleasures. Yet we've seen the joy of knowing Jesus, the security and comfort of His friendship, the purpose and moral coherence He provides. This fear is unreasonable.

Others might fear becoming Christian—hesitating at the doorstep of faith due to anxiety about what they'll lose, social embarrassment, or difficulty ahead. Yet God says: "Do not fear. The whole assembly of faithful believers testifies that this is worth it. Trust God rather than fear others."

## Part 3: Reasonable Fear (14:11-45)

**The Judgment**

> "How long will these people treat me with contempt? How long will they refuse to believe in me, in spite of all the miraculous signs I have performed among them? I will strike them down with a plague and destroy them." (14:11-12)

God's anger burns at their unfounded fear. Yet Moses intercedes, appealing to God's reputation and character:

> "The LORD is slow to anger, abounding in love and forgiving sin and rebellion. Yet he does not leave the guilty unpunished. In accordance with your great love, forgive the sin of these people, just as you have pardoned them from the time they left Egypt until now." (14:18-19)

God grants forgiveness, but justice remains. The sentence: forty years in the desert—one year for each day they explored the land.

> "Not one of you will enter the land I swore with uplifted hand to make your home, except Caleb son of Jephunneh and Joshua son of Nun. Your bodies will fall in this desert. Your children will be shepherds here for forty years, suffering for your unfaithfulness, until the last of your bodies lies in the desert." (14:30-33)

Their fears literally came true—not from enemies, but from God Himself. They feared death by the sword; they died in the desert. They feared their children being taken as plunder; instead, their children inherit the Promised Land. They wanted to return to Egypt; God granted it through the long wilderness journey.

**The Real Fear**

While we have various anxieties, the ultimate fear is judgment itself—krinophobia. This is the fear that matters. But Christ's death has solved this. Through His forgiveness, the greatest fear is gone. Our earthly fears are temporary; Jesus will return to restore all things.

## Comfort and Application

Rather than being seduced by unreasonable fears, we should:

**Cast Our Anxiety on God**

> "Do not be anxious about anything, but in everything, by prayer and petition, with thanksgiving, present your requests to God. And the peace of God, which transcends all understanding, will guard your hearts and your minds in Christ Jesus." (Philippians 4:6-7)

> "Humble yourselves, therefore, under God's mighty hand, that he may lift you up in due time. Cast all your anxiety on him because he cares for you." (1 Peter 5:6-7)

**Remember the Promise**

We are soon entering the Promised Land. No fear can debilitate us when God is on our side. Don't be seduced into thinking life is worse without God or that you'll miss out. Grace teaches our hearts to fear God rightly, and grace relieves all other fears.

*Amazing grace, how sweet the sound, that saved a wretch like me. I once was lost but now am found, was blind but now I see. 'Twas grace that taught my heart to fear, and grace my fears relieved.*
