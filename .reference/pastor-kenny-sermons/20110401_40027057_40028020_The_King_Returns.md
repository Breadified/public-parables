# Matthew 27:57-28:20

**Title:** The King Returns

**Date:** 18 April 2011 (SMAC); 30 October 2020 (ARISE)

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Gospel's Great Reversal: Death to Life, Life to Death

We come to the end of Matthew's gospel, where the narrative structure reveals a beautiful chiasm. The passage moves from the disciples' response to Jesus's burial (27:57-61), through a conspiracy to deny the resurrection (27:62-66), to the central event—the resurrection itself (28:1-10)—then back through another conspiracy (28:11-15), and finally to the disciples' commission (28:16-20).

### The Resurrection: Life from Death

The women come to the tomb on the first day of the week, after the Sabbath—a Jewish lockdown that prevented them from visiting earlier. They come to mourn and, according to Jewish custom, to verify that Jesus was truly dead. In a culture where premature burial was a genuine concern, periodic visits to tombs served to ensure that death was real.

But then everything changes:

> After the Sabbath, at dawn on the first day of the week, Mary Magdalene and the other Mary went to look at the tomb. There was a violent earthquake, for an angel of the Lord came down from heaven and, going to the tomb, rolled back the stone and sat on it. His appearance was like lightning, and his clothes were white as snow. (Matthew 28:1-3)

The earthquake is not arbitrary—it echoes the shaking at Jesus's crucifixion, when tombs broke open and bodies were raised to life (27:51-52). Earthquake means resurrection. For those who bury their dead in caves or in the ground, the earth itself must move to bring forth the dead. The stone is rolled away not to let Jesus out, but to let us see that he is no longer there.

The angel's message parallels Jesus's own words:

> Do not be afraid, for I know that you are looking for Jesus, who was crucified. He is not here; he has risen, just as he said. Come and see the place where he lay. Then go quickly and tell his disciples: "He has risen from the dead and is going ahead of you into Galilee. There you will see him." (Matthew 28:5-7)

The women experience the transformation from fear to joy. They arrive in grief and leave running with both fear and overwhelming joy. Jesus himself meets them with the same message: "Do not be afraid. Go and tell my brothers to go to Galilee; there they will see me" (28:10).

This resurrection is far more than Jesus simply being alive. It proclaims that Jesus is now Lord and Messiah—king, ruler, prime minister, and God of our lives. Peter's words at Pentecost capture this:

> God has raised this Jesus to life, and we are all witnesses of it. Exalted to the right hand of God, he has received from the Father the promised Holy Spirit... Therefore let all Israel be assured of this: God has made this Jesus, whom you crucified, both Lord and Messiah. (Acts 2:32-33, 36)

**This is a message of hope.** If the dead can become living, then Jesus has the power to save. If Jesus remained dead, he would be useless—he could not rule over us, protect us from future trouble, or guide us. But the resurrection declares that Jesus will look after us.

As Peter wrote:

> Praise be to the God and Father of our Lord Jesus Christ! In his great mercy he has given us new birth into a living hope through the resurrection of Jesus Christ from the dead. (1 Peter 1:3)

In hopeless moments—at funerals, facing impossible circumstances, feeling helpless and anxious—we need not despair. We look forward to the resurrection. We don't face helplessness or anxiety alone. Our hope is in Christ.

Yet there is another side to this coin. The gospel that turns death to life also turns life to death.

### The Conspiracy: When the Living Become Dead

Not everyone believed. The chief priests and Pharisees, remembering Jesus's prediction that he would rise after three days, went to Pilate and requested a guard at the tomb:

> "Sir," they said, "we remember that while he was still alive that deceiver said, 'After three days I will rise again.' So give the order for the tomb to be made secure until the third day. Otherwise, his disciples may come and steal the body and tell the people that he has been raised from the dead. This last deception will be worse than the first." (Matthew 27:63-64)

Jesus is called a "deceiver"—a term reserved for Satan himself. The irony is sharp: in their fear of deception, they become the deceivers. When the guards report what happened, the chief priests bribe them:

> When the chief priests had met with the elders and devised a plan, they gave the soldiers a large sum of money, telling them, "You are to say, 'His disciples came during the night and stole him away while we were asleep.' If this report gets to the governor, we will satisfy him and keep you out of trouble." So the soldiers took the money and did as they were instructed. And this story has been widely circulated among the Jews to this very day. (Matthew 28:12-15)

Matthew addresses this rumor specifically because his Jewish audience would have encountered it. The skeptics offer alternative explanations: the disciples stole the body, the disciples hallucinated, the disciples were confused but later organized a massive movement that conquered the Roman Empire within three hundred years.

Yet there is a simple way to disprove the resurrection: produce the body. The Romans were the FBI, CIA, police, and military all in one. They had informants everywhere. Yet fishermen supposedly overcame armed soldiers, stole a heavy body, hid it from family and friends, and fooled their closest allies into believing Jesus had risen. It strains credulity.

As Arthur Conan Doyle wrote: "When you have eliminated the impossible, whatever remains, however improbable, must be the truth."

There is only one probable explanation: Jesus rose from the dead.

**The barrier to belief is not evidence. The barrier is our prejudices.** We are convinced there is no God, so we ignore the evidence—the miracle of life itself, God's laws that make living secure and possible, and the resurrection. We insist that nobody comes back from the dead.

But if there is a God capable of the miraculous, why can't we believe that in this one special instance—for his own Son, because Jesus deserved it—God raised him from the dead?

For the first time in the universe's history, our sins have been broken. For the first time, we have been reconciled to God. For the first time, we have been forgiven. For the first time, we no longer face death after death—we face life after death. This is worthy of proclamation through the resurrection.

The case for Christ is built on evidence, not on inventing conspiracy theories. Instead of skepticism, Jesus calls us to discipleship.

## Discipleship: Following the Risen King

Believing in the resurrection leads to discipleship. Jesus appears to the eleven disciples (and possibly five hundred others, according to 1 Corinthians 15) on a mountain in Galilee:

> When they saw him, they worshiped him; but some doubted. Then Jesus came to them and said, "All authority in heaven and on earth has been given to me. Therefore go and make disciples of all nations, baptizing them in the name of the Father and of the Son and of the Holy Spirit, and teaching them to obey everything I have commanded you. And surely I am with you always, to the very end of the age." (Matthew 28:17-20)

The disciples worship him—they bow before him—because he now holds all authority in heaven and on earth. As Lord and Messiah, he gives them their commission:

**Go and make disciples** of all nations by:
- **Baptizing them** in the name of the Father, Son, and Holy Spirit
- **Teaching them to obey everything** Jesus commanded

The disciples are called not just to believe, but to teach others to believe and obey. This obedience means submitting to Scripture itself, which is why we are called to know the Bible.

The path of discipleship is not easy. It is a road of suffering and persecution—carrying our cross, surrendering our work, our social lives, our popularity and comfort. 

Consider Joseph of Arimathea, a wealthy man and secret disciple:

> As evening approached, there came a rich man from Arimathea, named Joseph, who had himself become a disciple of Jesus. Going to Pilate, he asked for Jesus' body, and Pilate ordered that it be given to him. Joseph took the body, wrapped it in a clean linen cloth, and placed it in his own new tomb that he had cut out of the rock. He rolled a big stone in front of the entrance to the tomb and went away. (Matthew 27:57-60)

Joseph gave up his own place of death—his tomb, his final resting place, his retirement plan—to Jesus. This was not a small gesture. He sacrificed his place of burial, his ultimate security, as a symbol of the good news: Jesus takes our place of death so that we might live.

The resurrection brings us from death to life. Discipleship asks us to go through suffering, not to avoid it. Sometimes the easier path is simply to go through.

---

## Conclusion

As we emerge from our own lockdowns—our periods of spiritual darkness, our seasons of despair—may we find the resurrected Christ. May we recognize him as Lord and Messiah, as the one who holds all authority and all power. And may we follow him as disciples, teaching others to obey everything he has commanded, secure in his promise: "I am with you always, to the very end of the age."

The gospel turns death to life, and calls the living to follow.
