# Revelation 21:1-2
**Title:** Boy-Girl Relationships
**Date:** 27 September 2003
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction

Marriage is the created design for humanity. While we certainly have choices today about whether to marry, and while there are godly unmarried people, we are fundamentally made for marriage. It is the norm and the ideal.

I speak as someone newly married—just 1.5 months—so my experience is fresh. But more importantly, I want to ground this discussion in Scripture, understanding that earthly relationships are meant to reflect the heavenly reality of Christ's relationship with His church.

## The Heavenly Marriage

The ultimate reality of marriage is unveiled at the end of all things:

> Then I saw a new heaven and a new earth, for the first heaven and the first earth had passed away, and there was no longer any sea. I saw the Holy City, the new Jerusalem, coming down out of heaven from God, prepared as a bride beautifully dressed for her husband. (Revelation 21:1-2)

The new Jerusalem represents God's people—the church. And who is the bridegroom? Jesus Christ Himself.

> And I heard a loud voice from the throne saying, "Now the dwelling of God is with men, and he will live with them. They will be his people, and God himself will be with them and be their God." (Revelation 21:3)

John the Baptist understood this reality profoundly. When questioned about Jesus's growing following, John replied:

> A man can receive only what is given him from heaven. You yourselves can testify that I said, "I am not the Christ but am sent ahead of him." The bride belongs to the bridegroom. The friend who attends the bridegroom waits and listens for him, and is full of joy when he hears the bridegroom's voice. That joy is mine and it is now complete. He must become greater; I must become less. (John 3:27-30)

John describes himself as the friend of the bridegroom—like a best man at a wedding. His joy is complete, not in his own prominence, but in seeing the bridegroom appear. This is the pattern: as Jesus increases, we decrease.

Paul makes this connection explicit in Ephesians:

> For this reason a man will leave his father and mother and be united to his wife, and the two will become one flesh. This is a profound mystery—but I am talking about Christ and the church. (Ephesians 5:31-32)

Through Christ, all things have been brought together under one head. In Him, Christians have been chosen and brought together. Those dead in sin have been raised to life through the power of His resurrection. Our ultimate identity and destiny is to be the bride of Christ.

## Purpose: Purity Before the Heavenly Marriage

Since we are betrothed to Christ, we must live in purity now:

> Christ loved the church and gave himself up for her to make her holy, cleansing her by the washing with water through the word, and to present her to himself as a radiant church, without stain or wrinkle or any other blemish, but holy and blameless. (Ephesians 5:25-27)

Our present calling is godliness. We are being sanctified, prepared for our ultimate marriage to Christ. This means we are to be pure now—not eventually, but today. God's aim for us is sanctification (1 Thessalonians 4:3).

## Why Earthly Marriage?

If our true marriage is to Christ, why do earthly marriages matter?

Earthly marriage is a shadow of heavenly reality. It's a living illustration of Christ's relationship with the church. This is why the marriage covenant carries such weight and beauty. When Paul describes the relationship between husband and wife, he's not just giving social advice—he's describing the pattern of Christ and His church:

> Wives, submit to your husbands as to the Lord. For the husband is the head of the wife as Christ is the head of the church, his body, of which he is the Saviour. Now as the church submits to Christ, so also wives should submit to their husbands in everything. Husbands, love your wives, just as Christ loved the church and gave himself up for her to make her holy, cleansing her by the washing with water through the word, and to present her to himself as a radiant church, without stain or wrinkle or any other blemish, but holy and blameless. In this same way, husbands ought to love their wives as their own bodies. He who loves his wife loves himself. (Ephesians 5:22-28)

The purposes of earthly marriage are two-fold:

**Fellowship and Intimacy.** Marriage provides deep relational communion—a picture of the intimate fellowship we will have with Christ. The Song of Songs celebrates this husband-wife love as a reflection of Christ's love for His church.

**Godly Offspring.** Marriage is the context for raising children in godly homes. Family is the basic building block of society, designed from creation itself.

Yet we must keep perspective: earthly marriage, however good and proper, is ultimately only a shadow. Whether we marry or remain single, all believers will be united to Christ on the last day. That is the marriage that matters eternally.

## Boy-Girl Relationships: The Biblical Framework

### Before Dating

**What is God's role in relationships?**

God's role in boy-girl relationships is the same as His role in every other sphere of life: we are called to submit to Him and live godly in all areas (Romans 12:1-2). This includes how we relate to the opposite sex.

**How can young people stop being obsessed with BGR?**

Theologically, understand that life is far more than dating and romance. Your marriage to the Lamb is the bigger deal. Practically, keep busy, pursue other interests, stay engaged with life and community, and accept reality rather than fantasizing.

**To date or not to date: what's the point?**

The Bible doesn't explicitly address dating as we know it, but it does speak to courtship—a season of getting to know someone to determine suitability for marriage. If you're dating, ask yourself: Am I thinking about marriage? Dating should be purposeful, not merely recreational.

**How does the Bible view singleness?**

Singleness is not the norm. God's design from creation was for man and woman, husband and wife. However, the apostle Paul acknowledges that singleness can be appropriate, especially in times of crisis or present difficulty (1 Corinthians 7; Matthew 19:10-12).

**What is the gift of singleness?**

The "gift of singleness" mentioned in 1 Corinthians 7 refers to your life situation, not some special spiritual endowment. If you're unmarried, you have the gift of singleness; if married, you don't. It's simply Paul's way of saying: your current situation is what God has given you. Live faithfully in it.

**Parental pressure about marriage**

Handle this the same way you'd handle any disagreement with parents: with love, understanding, and respect. Honor your parents (Ephesians 6:1), but also understand that leaving and cleaving is part of God's design (Ephesians 5:31). As you mature and become ready for your own family, this naturally shifts.

**When are you ready for a relationship?**

Ask yourself: Am I ready for marriage? The answer determines readiness for dating. A relationship means being willing to give your life for another person. If you're not ready for that commitment, you're not ready to date.

**Does the Bible specify an age to start dating?**

No. But remember the principle of leaving and cleaving (Ephesians 5:31). Your readiness should be measured by maturity and intention toward marriage, not by age alone.

**Dating in high school**

Is it hard to wait? Yes. But ask honestly: Are you ready for marriage? If not, a high school relationship, however fun it seems, creates unnecessary risks. What starts as "just for fun" often develops emotional and physical entanglement that complicates both people's lives. The better question isn't "Can I have fun dating?" but "Am I ready to marry?"

### Friendship and Dating Boundaries

**When do friendship and dating cross over?**

The boundary is determined by the level of intimacy—emotional and physical. Be careful of slipping into an "unofficial" relationship. If you're spending significant time alone, having deep conversations about feelings, and touching physically, you're dating whether you've named it or not.

**How long should you know someone before asking them out?**

There's no magic number. It depends on the depth of interaction and genuine getting-to-know of the other person's character, faith, and temperament.

### Physical Attraction and Presentation

**Is it OK to notice people of the opposite sex?**

Yes. Being drawn to physical beauty is normal and not inherently sinful. The sin is in lustful thought and behavior. Treat the opposite sex with absolute purity and respect (1 Timothy 5:2).

**Should Christians actively seek a partner?**

Yes, marriage is a matter of freedom. Seeking a spouse isn't discontentment—it's faithfulness to God's design. Discontentment involves complaining, grumbling, refusing to accept reality after genuine effort, obsessive thoughts, and failure to trust God. Prayerful pursuit of marriage is not the same as anxious desperation.

**How should Christians present themselves?**

Modesty is the standard (1 Timothy 2:9). Be thoughtful about how your clothing and presence affect others, particularly young men who are visually stimulated in ways many young women don't fully realize. This isn't about shame; it's about honoring one another.

### Dating Non-Christians

**Can a Christian date a non-Christian interested in the gospel?**

No. Wait to see if they truly become a Christian first. Many people are interested in Christianity for various reasons. Get them saved first; don't blur the agenda. Show them that being Christian is crucial—don't date them in hopes of converting them.

**Already dating a non-Christian interested in Christianity?**

If they've become a Christian but you suspect it's only because of you, break up and see if the faith is genuine. Time and separation will reveal true conversion.

**A non-Christian asks you out**

Say no clearly. Don't say maybe or "I'll think about it." These responses create confusion and false hope. Be kind but decisive.

### Who to Date

**What qualities should you look for?**

A believer in Christ, of the opposite sex, and someone godly—someone who demonstrates the character of leading (if male) or submitting (if female) as described in Scripture. Look for someone pursuing godliness.

**Dating someone spiritually less mature than you?**

One person will always be ahead. If you're the more mature one, you'll have to lead, which creates an asymmetry. Think carefully about whether this dynamic works long-term. What does "mature" mean to you? Are they growing?

**Physical attractiveness**

Attraction matters, but remember that physical beauty withers. Don't make it your primary criterion. Look for beauty of character that lasts.

### During Dating

**How should brothers and sisters in Christ treat each other?**

Like brothers and sisters. With absolute purity (1 Timothy 5:2). If you're dating, maintain that same standard.

**What should Christian couples do together?**

Study Scripture together and pray together—publicly, not secretly. Talk meaningfully. Establish clear boundaries and stick to them.

**Preventing physical escalation**

The root issue is the heart, but environment matters tremendously. Do your relationship building in public places. Never be alone in private rooms. Don't use cars for private conversations—use them for transportation only. Be seen by others. Movies and isolated settings create temptation; community and accountability help you stay pure.

**How far is too far?**

You already know the answer. Sexual immorality includes foreplay—the Bible doesn't give you a detailed checklist because the principle is clear: nothing that arouses sexually before marriage. Check your motivation in every physical gesture. Holding hands might be innocent friendship, or it might be sexual in nature—you know which it is. As physical intimacy increases, it becomes harder to stop. The solution? Get married if you're serious, or maintain real boundaries if you're dating.

The seriousness of sexual sin cannot be overstated:

> Flee from sexual immorality. All other sins a man commits are outside his body, but he who sins sexually sins against his own body. (1 Corinthians 6:18)

> But among you there must not be even a hint of sexual immorality, or of any kind of impurity, or of greed, because these are improper for God's holy people. No immoral, impure or greedy person—such a person is an idolater—will inherit the kingdom of Christ and of God. (Ephesians 5:3, 5)

### Breaking Up

**Is there a Christian way to break up?**

Yes. The sooner, the better. Be honest and kind. Don't leave them hanging in uncertainty. Don't continue to spend time together afterward in ways that create confusion about whether you're still together. Make a clean break.

**Long-term relationships**

Think carefully about whether you're using dating as a permanent state rather than a purposeful season. If you're serious about each other, move toward marriage. If you're not, it's unfair to keep the relationship going indefinitely.

**When should you marry?**

Godliness is more important than financial security. You don't need a house, a high salary, or perfect timing. Many have married as laborers or on government support and thrived. What matters is commitment, godliness, and willingness to build life together.

### Other Issues

**Masturbation**

The Bible is silent on this directly. Some argue it's wrong because it often involves lust or self-gratification. Others note the Bible doesn't explicitly forbid it. My counsel: examine your own motivation honestly. If it's bound up with lust, it's sin. If it's about handling sexual desire in singleness, the discussion is more nuanced. But don't use silence as permission; use it as an opportunity to examine your heart before God.

## Summary: Our True Purpose

We are created for marriage—but the marriage that matters eternally is our union with Christ. Earthly marriage is a beautiful shadow of that reality, designed for fellowship, for raising godly children, and for reflecting Christ's love to the world. Whether we marry or remain single, we are called to purity and godliness now, as we wait for the marriage feast of the Lamb.

Let earthly relationships point us to that heavenly reality. Let them be marked by honor, purity, and the kind of love that mirrors Christ's own.
