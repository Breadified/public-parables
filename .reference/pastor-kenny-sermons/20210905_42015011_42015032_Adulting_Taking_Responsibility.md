**Title:** Adulting: Taking Responsibility

## Introduction: What Does It Mean to Adult?

The word "adulting" has become a verb in our millennial vernacular—a made-up word now legitimately adopted into everyday speech. It typically refers to a young person accomplishing something mundane that an ordinary adult would do, treating it as a genuine achievement: "Paid my bills on time; I've done my adulting for the day." Or: "I cannot adult today." The phrase captures a real difficulty: adulting is genuinely hard.

Our church, full of many millennials, understands this struggle deeply. We face challenges with work, relationships, marriage, and children. Our culture doesn't help—it embodies youth culture, not adult culture. Many of us have grown up largely on our own, parents overseas or absent or busy, figuring out what it means to adult without guidance. For first-generation Christians, the question becomes even more pointed: what does it mean to be a Christian adult?

This series explores adulthood through Scripture. Rather than a traditional lecture, I'm grounding these reflections in Jesus's parables, taking one element from each and launching into the topic. In this first sermon, we look at the Parable of the Prodigal Son—not to discuss Jewish and Gentile relationships, but to explore a son who doesn't want to be an adult.

> Heavenly Father, I praise you for a church full of many young souls who are ready to change the world. Help us to figure out how to adult for the glory of your son. In Jesus name.

## 1. Avoiding Responsibility (Luke 15:11-13)

> "There was a man who had two sons. The younger one said to his father, 'Father, give me my share of the estate.' So he divided his property between them."

This is strange. Inheritance normally comes after the father's death. Yet this father obliges, dividing his property and giving it to his son. Perhaps in the younger son's view, the father is as good as dead to him—he wants his future now.

On the surface, he looks like he's trying to grow up: leaving home, claiming independence, proclaiming his emancipation. But there's a fundamental contradiction. He says he hates his father and is running away, yet he's asking for the father's money. He wants the benefits of adulthood without its costs. He wants his future now without working for it.

The inheritance represents what one normally receives after a lifetime of working for the father—a lump sum to help you get going in life. He's unwilling to wait for that. Like a child, he wants it immediately.

> "Not long after that, the younger son got together all he had, set off for a distant country and there squandered his wealth in wild living."

His subsequent actions confirm he hasn't matured. He goes to a "distant country"—exotic, extravagant, where no voice of restraint exists. He engages in "wild living," doing whatever he wants. He "squanders" his wealth wastefully, never thinking of the future. This is the story of someone who didn't want responsibility. Rather than work diligently at his father's farm, he chooses wasteful wild living in a distant land, caring neither for others nor even for himself.

### What Is an Adult?

The essence of being an adult is taking responsibility.

A child lives for the now, caring only for immediate needs. Others take responsibility for the child—feeding, cleaning, entertaining. The child has no responsibility.

An adult must take responsibility—for self, for future self, and for others.

Legally, this distinction is crucial. A child cannot be held liable for contracts signed or crimes committed. They're not responsible, so their contracts aren't enforceable. They're protected from the consequences of their actions. An adult, by contrast, has the capacity and liability for contracts signed and crimes committed. The legal distinction reveals a behavioral one: children are irresponsible, selfish, inconsiderate, self-centered—or vice versa: they're selfish and self-centered because they lack responsibility.

Consider Galatians:

> "Now I say, as long as the heir is a child, he does not differ at all from a slave although he is owner of everything, but he is under guardians and managers until the date set by the father."

A child is under control, like a slave. Someone else is responsible for their well-being. The child is not responsible.

### The Problem: Extended Childhood

There are some good qualities about being a child. Jesus teaches:

> "Unless you are converted and become as little children, you will by no means enter the kingdom of heaven."

And Peter writes:

> "like newborn babies, long for the pure milk of the word, so that by it you may grow in respect to salvation"

Children possess humility and dependence, innocence and wonder. But predominantly in Scripture, childishness is portrayed negatively: foolishness, ignorance, lack of spiritual maturity, fearfulness, rudeness, bullying.

An American anthropologist observed: "our youth love luxury. They have bad manners, contempt for authority, disrespect for older people. Children nowadays are tyrants. They no longer rise when their elders enter the room. They contradict their parents, chatter before company, gobble their food, and tyrannize their teachers." This isn't surprising—it's expected of children.

Historically, we transition from sinful immaturity to righteousness and adulthood. The problem is that childhood itself is being artificially extended.

### The Artificial Extension of Childhood

Prior generations transitioned to adulthood around age 12-13 (marking puberty), with training beginning then. By age 20, they were independent: established in a trade, working unsupervised, married or about to be, with children started or imminent. You had to be ready—by age 20, you could die, but your bloodline would continue.

Our modern situation is drastically different. At age 20, we're just starting education to become productive workers. We're in our first entry-level position with no experience. We're broke and in debt with no assets. We're definitely not thinking about marriage and family. We're 6-12 years behind prior generations in actual adulthood.

This isn't your fault. It stems from the Industrial Revolution. Child labor exploitation (famously depicted in Dickens's *Oliver Twist*) prompted government intervention to ban all child labor and force children into schools. But this created an unintended consequence: school became an extended childhood. First primary school was compulsory, then high school. The "teenager" was invented—unknown to human history—and with it came teen culture, boredom, drug use, and the "teen industrial complex" of pop idols and body image obsession.

Dr. Robert Epstein calls this the "artificial extension of childhood." Education stretched from common 3-year degrees to 5-6 years, now requiring a Master's degree. People graduate in their mid-to-late twenties, marry in their thirties, have children in their mid-thirties. Everything is pushed back. We live longer, but we're choosing to be children longer.

The twenties—your most productive years—are spent still in training. The effect is that we have many excuses to avoid adult responsibilities.

## 2. Taking Responsibility (Luke 15:15-20)

> "So he went and hired himself out to a citizen of that country, who sent him to his fields to feed pigs."

He wakes up and takes responsibility. He moves. When in despair, the first step is simple: make your bed. It's the first achievement of the day.

He "hired himself"—found a job. Feeding pigs. It's not fancy. It's minimum wage work, maybe cash in hand for less. He's shoveling rubbish to feed himself. Wasted all his inheritance, demoralised, starting from scratch when he had it all. Riches to rags. How does he drag himself? It's amazing.

> "He longed to fill his stomach with the pods that the pigs were eating, but no one gave him anything."

Still hungry. It's not enough.

> "When he came to his senses, he said, 'How many of my father's hired servants have food to spare, and here I am starving to death! I will set out and go back to my father and say to him: Father, I have sinned against heaven and against you. I am no longer worthy to be called your son; make me like one of your hired servants.' So he got up and went to his father."

He accepts responsibility for his own actions. He decides to go back and make things right. He says sorry. He swallows his ego and pride.

### Taking Responsibility Without Blame

What's even more remarkable is what he *doesn't* do. He doesn't blame anyone. He doesn't blame his father (who gave him the inheritance), nor does he blame his current employer (who doesn't pay enough). Today, immaturity often wears the mask of critical race theory—the idea that everything is someone else's fault: they're racist, selfish, greedy, sexist. Never "it's my fault." When anything bad happens, we say "I'm oppressed."

Consider current debates. On vaccines: "I'm sick because everyone doesn't take vaccines." No. You take the vaccine, eat right, stay healthy, stock up on vitamins, get Vitamin D. You take responsibility for your health. Or: "I have no freedom because you oppress me." No. You're oppressed because you need a job from someone else. Create your own business. Buy shares in companies. Work for yourself. Take responsibility!

The chains of causation are long and convoluted. The circumstances we find ourselves in may not be of our making. But so what? We're adults. We have agency. We're not ignorant. You know the problem—go fix it, one small step at a time. If it's a big problem, set up the next generation to fix it too.

This contrasts sharply with today's pattern of collecting government money and blaming society for our problems.

> "For everyone belongs to me, the parent as well as the child---both alike belong to me. The one who sins is the one who will die."

You are a product of your past decisions, but you are at least the product of today and your future decisions. The more responsibility you take, the richer and more meaningful your life.

## 3. God Takes Responsibility (Luke 15:21-30)

> "But while he was still a long way off, his father saw him and was filled with compassion for him; he ran to his son, threw his arms around him and kissed him."

The father has been watching, waiting, scanning the horizon every day. When he sees his son, he's filled with compassion. He runs—culturally inappropriate. He kisses him strongly, repeatedly.

The son doesn't even get to speak his prepared speech. The father cuts him off:

> "But the father said to his servants, 'Quick! Bring the best robe and put it on him. Put a ring on his finger and sandals on his feet. Bring the fattened calf and kill it. Let's have a feast and celebrate. For this son of mine was dead and is alive again; he was lost and is found.'"

The best robe would belong to the father himself. The ring is a family signet ring—a symbol of reinstatement to sonship. Slaves didn't wear sandals, though they tied a master's sandals. The father says, "I won't receive you as a servant. I'll receive you only as a son." He slaughters the fattened calf for the whole village—he wants everyone to know. Becoming a Christian is a celebration.

He could have stoned him. The law allowed it (Deuteronomy 21:18-21 prescribes stoning for stubborn, rebellious sons who are gluttons and drunkards). Instead, he chooses grace. He treats him differently. God the Father assumes responsibility to welcome us back.

### The Older Son: Still a Child

But there's another son.

> "Meanwhile, the older son was in the field. When he came near the house, he heard music and dancing."

He's working hard. The party is loud. He calls a servant: "When did we organize this?"

> "'Your brother has come,' he replied, 'and your father has killed the fattened calf because he has him back safe and sound.'"

The older brother is not impressed. He never disobeyed orders, it's true. But listen to his words:

> "'Look! All these years I've been slaving for you and never disobeyed your orders. Yet you never gave me even a young goat so I could celebrate with my friends. But when this son of yours who has squandered your property with prostitutes comes home, you kill the fattened calf for him!'"

He calls himself a "slave" and speaks to his father as though he's an employer, not a father. The language is accusatory: "Look"—telling the father off. He feels stingy treatment: "never gave me a young goat." He talks as though the Father was not a father. But he's wrong.

> "'My son,' the father said, 'you are always with me, and everything I have is yours. But we had to celebrate and be glad, because this brother of yours was dead and is alive again; he was lost and is found.'"

The son is *always* with him. Everything is his. In fact, with the younger son's inheritance given away, he technically has two-thirds—everything. Yet he thinks if he keeps doing all the work, the father will be obligated to love him. If he does A, B, C, then the father must do X, Y, Z. He's trying to put God into his debt. "You owe me."

> "Now to the one who works, wages are not credited as a gift but as an obligation. However, to the one who does not work but trusts God who justifies the ungodly, their faith is credited as righteousness."

The father's inheritance is a gift. God's salvation is mercy. But the older son thinks like a laborer: work equals payment. He's still a child—sulking, throwing a tantrum, refusing to go in. The father must come out and plead with him. His childishness shows: he can't see past his own grievance to his brother's redemption. He can't celebrate because it doesn't serve his interests.

## Application: What It Means to Adult

### Take Personal Responsibility

You are responsible for your actions. You are responsible for your sin. When anything bad happens to you, your first question should be: "What's my responsibility here?" Not "Who's to blame?" but "What can I do?"

### Understand the Cost of Inaction

Life is risky. You could get sick going outside. You could have an accident driving. You could have a heart attack eating unhealthy food. Yes, life is risky. There is a cost to doing something.

But there is also a cost to *not* doing something—the opportunity cost.

> "Go, eat your food with gladness, and drink your wine with a joyful heart, for God has already approved what you do. Always be clothed in white, and always anoint your head with oil. Enjoy life with your wife, whom you love, all the days of this meaningless life that God has given you under the sun—all your meaningless days. For this is your lot in life and in your toilsome labor under the sun. Whatever your hand finds to do, do it with all your might, for in the realm of the dead, where you are going, there is neither working nor planning nor knowledge nor wisdom."

Life is short. We are mortal. Eat and drink. Enjoy your spouse. You cannot keep what you have—it's a sunk cost. The risk isn't something to do later; it wouldn't be risky then. Risk is something you take now.

### Build Stress and Resilience

We need stress the way our teeth need use and our immune systems need challenge. Coddling and swaddling our children—extending artificial childhood—fuels mental illness in teenagers. They've never learned to handle difficulty. Give them real responsibility. Let them fail. Let them face consequences. This is how adults are made.

---

## Conclusion: On Fathers and Responsibility

What makes a father a father is this: the buck stops with him. He is responsible. A leak in the roof. Food on the table. Children misbehaving. The father takes responsibility.

On this Father's Day, may we learn what it means to be an adult: to take responsibility for ourselves, for our futures, for others, and to receive with gratitude the mercy of our Heavenly Father, who runs toward us with open arms, ready to welcome us home.
