# Job 32:1-10, 33:1-33

**Title:** Suffering as Teaching, God's Future-Oriented Discipline
**Date:** 5 October 2025
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction: Who Is Elihu?

After Job's laments and dialogues with his three friends (chapters 3-31), a mysterious fourth friend enters: Elihu, son of Barakel the Buzite. He is young, has been listening silently the entire time, and now steps forward with a vital message the others have missed.

There is considerable debate about whether Elihu is a help or a hindrance. On one hand, he appears suddenly without introduction, seems to repeat the friends' arguments, speaks for six chapters without reply, and disappears from the epilogue. Some scholars suspect these chapters were added later. On the other hand, his genealogy connects him to Abraham's line; his Hebrew name ("he is my God") suggests divine association; he is not condemned by God as the three friends are; and he prepares Job for God's own appearance.

Most significantly, unlike the other friends, Elihu doesn't simply rehearse the retribution formula ("you sinned, therefore you suffer"). Instead, he introduces a radically different perspective: **God may use suffering as redemptive discipline and teaching**—a bridge to the gospel itself.

### Elihu as Foreteller

In Job 33:1-7, Elihu claims divine inspiration. He speaks of being made by God's Spirit, of words on his tongue from an upright heart. He repeatedly asserts throughout his speeches (32:8, 18-19; 33:4; 36:3-4; 37:16) that he is divinely compelled to speak—language echoing the prophets:

> "The spirit within me compels me; inside I am like a bottle-up wine, like new wineskins ready to burst" (Job 32:18-19)

This mirrors Jeremiah's experience:

> "His word is in my heart like a fire, a fire shut up in my bones. I am weary of holding it in; indeed, I cannot." (Jeremiah 20:9)

Elihu appears at a critical juncture—just before the LORD Himself speaks. He functions as a preparatory prophet, reframing the entire theology of suffering before God addresses Job directly.

## Core Message: God Speaks Through Suffering

### The Problem: Job's Misunderstanding

Elihu fairly quotes Job's complaint:

> "I am pure, I have done no wrong; I am clean and free from sin. Yet God has found fault with me; he considers me his enemy. He fastens my feet in shackles; he keeps close watch on all my paths." (Job 33:8-11)

Job's logic is simple: I am innocent, yet I suffer. Therefore, God is unjust.

### The Correction: The Creator-Creature Distinction

Elihu's first correction reintroduces a truth the friends missed:

> "But I tell you, in this you are not right, for God is greater than any mortal. Why do you complain to him that he responds to no one's words? For God does speak—now one way, now another—though no one perceives it." (Job 33:12-14)

God is under no obligation to explain Himself in words. The Creator-creature distinction matters:

- "It is the glory of God to conceal things" (Proverbs 25:2)
- "God is in heaven and you are on earth; let your words be few" (Ecclesiastes 5:2)
- "Shall the clay say to him who forms it, 'What are you making?'" (Isaiah 45:9)

Job assumes God must answer his charges. Elihu says: No. God speaks in **various ways**—and Job is simply not listening.

### God's Multiple Channels of Communication

How does God speak if not always in words?

> "God does speak—now one way, now another. In a dream, in a vision of the night, when deep sleep falls on people as they slumber in their beds, he may speak in their ears and terrify them with warnings." (Job 33:14-16)

God uses dreams, visions, and—crucially—**suffering itself** as a medium of divine instruction.

### The Purpose: Redemptive Discipline, Not Payback

Here lies Elihu's revolutionary insight. Unlike the friends who say "Suffering means sin—repent and it will end," Elihu reframes suffering as **fatherly discipline**:

> "To turn them from wrongdoing and keep them from pride, to preserve them from the pit, their lives from perishing by the sword." (Job 33:17-18)

The aim is **preventive and restorative**, not punitive payback.

Elihu describes a person brought low by illness, on a bed of pain, bones wasting away, unable to eat, near death itself:

> "Or someone may be chastened on a bed of pain with constant distress in their bones, so that their body finds food repulsive and their soul loathes the choicest meal. Their flesh wastes away to nothing, and their bones, once hidden, now stick out. They draw near to the pit, and their life to the messengers of death." (Job 33:19-22)

This describes Job's suffering precisely. Yet Elihu asks: What if this is not punishment but awakening?

## The Gracious Mediator: A Glimpse of Christ

In verses 23-28, Elihu introduces a stunning image—one many theologians see as foreshadowing Christ:

> "Yet if there is an angel at their side, a messenger, one out of a thousand, sent to tell them how to be upright, and he is gracious to that person and says to God, 'Spare them from going down to the pit; I have found a ransom for them—let their flesh be renewed like a child's; let them be restored as in the days of their youth'—then that person can pray to God and find favor with him, they will see God's face and shout for joy; he will restore them to full well-being." (Job 33:23-26)

Notice the language: **mediator**, **ransom**, **restoration**, **reconciliation**. What Elihu sketches, Jesus fulfills:

- He is "the one Mediator between God and man" (1 Timothy 2:5)
- He is "the Ransom who gives his life for many" (Mark 10:45)
- He is "the Interpreter of God" (John 1:18)
- He is "the Deliverer who rescues us from the domain of darkness" (Colossians 1:13-14)

The sufferer who receives this mediation repents and proclaims:

> "I have sinned, I have perverted what is right, but I did not get what I deserved. God has delivered me from going down to the pit, and I shall live to enjoy the light of life." (Job 33:27-28)

## Why Suffering? Four Reasons

Elihu provides a framework for understanding suffering that goes far deeper than mere cause-and-effect karma:

### 1. Warnings & Wake-Up Calls

Suffering arrests us when we drift toward destruction. Like speed cameras on a road—they warn the innocent to stay safe while catching the reckless. Suffering jolts us awake to reality: life is short, our health is fragile, and we drift into complacency.

### 2. Training Toward Endurance & Character

Scripture repeatedly teaches that suffering produces spiritual maturity:

> "Suffering produces perseverance; perseverance, character; and character, hope." (Romans 5:3-4)

> "The testing of your faith produces perseverance. Let perseverance finish its work so that you may be mature and complete, not lacking anything." (James 1:3-4)

Each breakthrough requires pain—like an athlete breaking through a fitness threshold. Growth under pressure is the Christian's destiny.

### 3. Guarding Against Sin & Producing Repentance

Suffering can break the grip of pride and sin, directing us back toward God:

> "A thorn was given me... to keep me from becoming conceited. When I am weak, then I am strong." (2 Corinthians 12:7-10)

### 4. Equipping Us to Minister to Others

Your undeserved suffering becomes a credential to help someone else through their pain. It teaches compassion born of experience, not theory.

## Practical Application: A Personal Story

Kenny shares a childhood memory that illustrates divine protection. As a young boy with severe myopia (inherited poor eyesight), he was playing near construction workers carrying rebar—long, sharp metal rods. He walked directly into one, but his thick glasses caused it to glance off rather than pierce his eye. He never removed his glasses after that.

The lesson: **Some suffering is for our good.** We don't always see the dangers we've been spared. We must look toward the bigger picture, toward the future God is preparing.

## The Call to Listen

Elihu's final appeal to Job is simple:

> "Pay attention, Job, and listen to me; be silent, and I will speak. If you have anything to say, answer me; speak up, for I want to vindicate you. But if not, then listen to me; be silent, and I will teach you wisdom." (Job 33:31-33)

In suffering, ask not only "Why?" but "What for?" What crooked path might God be straightening? Where is the call to trust, repent, reconcile, reorder?

## Looking Forward: Future Orientation

The title of this sermon is "Suffering as Teaching/Future Oriented." Elihu shifts Job's gaze from the present anguish to what lies ahead. The suffering isn't the end of the story—it's preparation.

What is God preparing you for in your grief? 

- To remember something you've forgotten?
- To build resilience for greater trials ahead?
- To minister to someone else who will face similar pain?

The ultimate answer points to Christ and resurrection. Suffering serves the gospel. As the apostle James writes to suffering Christians:

> "You have heard of the steadfastness of Job, and you have seen the purpose of the Lord, how the Lord is compassionate and merciful." (James 5:11)

In your trials, the Spirit of Jesus in your heart enables you to walk through trouble with patient faith—not because the suffering makes sense right now, but because you trust the One who is making it serve a purpose you will one day understand.
