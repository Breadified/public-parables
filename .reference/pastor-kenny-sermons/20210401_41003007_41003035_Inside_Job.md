## Who's In? The Formation of Jesus' Club

This week, we had an Archbishop election here in the Anglican church. It's a peculiar process—a closed synod where only ministers and synod representatives get to vote. There's something about clubs that naturally raises the question: who's in, and who's out?

We all join clubs throughout our lives. University clubs, sports clubs, cultural associations, professional organizations. Each one has membership requirements and a subscription to a set of common beliefs. I was once a bouncer for a dance club run by the Asian-Australian Youth Business Council. My job was to determine who could enter. I'd collect the money and manage the door—deciding who was in and who was out.

But today, the question Jesus poses is far more significant: **who's in Jesus' club?**

### A Collection of Unlikely Sons (3:13-19)

> Jesus went up on a mountainside and called to him those he wanted, and they came to him. He appointed twelve that they might be with him and that he might send them out to preach and to have authority to drive out demons.

This reads like a university recruitment drive. Jesus doesn't pick from the ruling class or the educated religious establishment. He picks a motley crew.

Let's look at who made the list:

**Simon (Peter)** – His name means "he has heard" in Hebrew, *Cephas* in Aramaic, but Jesus gave him the Greek name *Peter*, meaning rock or pebble—a foundation stone.

**James and John, sons of Zebedee** – Jesus called them "Boanerges," which means "sons of thunder." Some scholars suggest this might mean all bark but no bite. These were hotheads. Look at Luke 9:54: when people didn't welcome Jesus on the road to Jerusalem, James and John asked, "Lord, do you want us to call fire down from heaven to destroy them?" They wanted to be the greatest too—sitting at Jesus' right hand, according to Mark 22:24-30. Yet they would become "the greatest" in a way they didn't expect.

**Andrew and Philip** – fishermen.

**Bartholomew (Nathaniel)** – the name itself means "son of Tolmew."

**Matthew** – a tax collector who worked for the Romans, a collaborator with the occupiers.

**Thomas** – the twin.

**James son of Alphaeus** – "James the Less," the smaller one.

**Thaddaeus** – whose name means "child at the breast," or as some texts say, "child of the heart."

**Simon the Zealot** – an anti-Roman revolutionary, possibly a murderer.

**Judas Iscariot** – who would betray him.

These were the "twelve." None from the ruling class. None from the educated or religious establishment. Uneducated fishermen, hotheads, former swindlers, would-be murderers, and eventually a traitor. Peter keeps saying the wrong thing at the start of the list. Judas ends it.

But they had one thing in common: **they were reformed sinners who now followed Jesus.**

If this group had applied for the Archbishop position, I'm not sure how they would have fared at synod. The nominees there were described as charismatic, empathetic, wonderful preachers, godly in character, theologians, winsome, gifted administrators, hardworking, educated, media-friendly, with impeccable CVs.

Notice something about the list of apostles: there's a pattern of "sons of." Even Bartholomew—*bar* means "son" in Aramaic. This is a collection of new sons of God. As Romans 8:14-16 tells us:

> For those who are led by the Spirit of God are the children (sons) of God. The Spirit you received does not make you slaves, so that you live in fear again; rather, the Spirit you received brought about your adoption to sonship. And by him we cry, "Abba, Father." The Spirit himself testifies with our spirit that we are God's children.

By choosing these twelve, Jesus was condemning the old twelve—the twelve tribes of Israel under the old leadership. That leadership had failed. Jeremiah 23:1-3 captures God's judgment:

> "Woe to the shepherds who are destroying and scattering the sheep of my pasture!" declares the Lord... "I myself will gather the remnant of my flock out of all the countries where I have driven them and will bring them back to their pasture, where they will be fruitful and increase in number."

Jesus was appointing a new twelve. In Revelation 21:14, we see the fulfillment: "The wall of the city had twelve foundations bearing the names of the twelve apostles of the Lamb."

These twelve were appointed to preach and to drive out demons—to do what Jesus does. Yet they don't actually get sent out until chapter 6.

### Application: A Motley Crew Wins

Think of Hollywood movies. You have the motley crew—ragtag, disorganized—versus the organized military, everyone looking the same, perfectly aligned. In the movies, the motley crew always wins. So it was with the apostles. They were a strange and diverse people, yet they would change the world.

We are their direct intellectual descendants. Under their teaching, Christendom was born. And today, we too are a motley crew—a strange and diverse people. How many of us speak second languages? How many of us were born in different countries? One of us was even born in Thailand. We belong to different clubs and associations.

But we all belong to one club: **we are on the inside of Jesus' club.** And we invite everyone not yet in that club to join us.

## One Household: The Inside Job (3:22-30)

Unfortunately, while some joined Jesus' movement, others who should have did not. In verse 22, the teachers of the law who came down from Jerusalem made a shocking accusation:

> "He is possessed by Beelzebul! By the prince of demons he is driving out demons."

*Beelzebul* likely comes from "Lord of the High Place" or "Lord of the Flies." In 2 Kings 1:2, it referred to a Philistine deity. By Jesus' time, it meant the prince of demons—the lord of the air, or as one scholar suggests, even the lord of dung.

The teachers of the law had been spying on Jesus. They witnessed the scenes in verse 10 firsthand:

> For he had healed many, so that those with diseases were pushing forward to touch him. Whenever the impure spirits saw him, they fell down before him and cried out, "You are the Son of God."

The evidence was undeniable. Many healed. Demons cast out. All the witnesses fell before him and confessed, "You are the Son of God." So they couldn't deny the miracles. Instead, they attacked from another angle: *You're right that demons are being cast out everywhere, but you're doing it by the power of demons themselves. It's an **inside job**—Satan attacking his own kingdom.*

Jesus responds with a logical argument:

> How can Satan drive out Satan? If a kingdom is divided against itself, that kingdom cannot stand. If a house is divided against itself, that house cannot stand. And if Satan opposes himself and is divided, he cannot stand; his end has come.

Think about it. During World War II, imagine if Germany said, "Don't worry, folks. We're conducting an experiment where we blow up our own planes intentionally to show how powerful we are." Then planes and tanks are being destroyed everywhere. "We're just wasting a few more of our battleships and tanks to demonstrate our strength." That's absurd. Satan isn't in the business of destroying himself.

So demons being cast out everywhere, and the twelve appointed by Jesus to also cast out demons everywhere—is that Satan attacking himself? If so, how long can this continue? If Satan is dividing against himself from the inside, then his end has come.

But there's a different kind of inside job happening here:

> In fact, no one can enter a strong man's house without first tying him up. Then he can plunder the strong man's house.

The Greek emphasizes "plunder" twice: "No one is able, having entered the strong man's house, to **plunder** his goods unless first he binds the strong man, then he **plunders** his house."

Jesus isn't Satan defeating Satan. Rather, a stronger man—Jesus himself—has entered Satan's kingdom and is destroying it from within. He comes in, overwhelms Satan, ties him up, and plunders his house. What is he plundering? The "vessels" or "objects" of the house. As Romans 9:23 says, we are "objects of his mercy." As 2 Corinthians 4:7 puts it, "We have this treasure in jars of clay." The people freed from demon possession—God is taking back what is his, rescuing the captives from the kidnapper.

I'm not certain whether demon possession was exclusively a first-century phenomenon or whether it continues. But preaching the gospel is never merely an intellectual or emotional act. It's also profoundly spiritual, metaphysical, something out of this world. It destroys demonic thinking and deeds. A miracle happens every time anyone—whether a boy in the first century or you in the twenty-first century—for the first time calls Jesus "You are the Son of God."

### The Unforgivable Sin (3:28-30)

The fact that the teachers of the law cannot recognize this divine work is itself the truest sign of demon possession:

> Truly I tell you, people can be forgiven all their sins and every slander they utter, but whoever blasphemes against the Holy Spirit will never be forgiven; they are guilty of an eternal sin.

This is controversial. "Whoever blasphemes against the Holy Spirit will never be forgiven." It sounds serious, so every generation tends to put whatever it hates most into this category. In the past, murder, suicide, sexual sin. Today, whenever we hear, "There's a special place in hell for..." we're doing the same thing.

But notice verse 30 clarifies what Jesus means:

> He said this because they were saying, "He has an impure spirit."

The unforgivable sin isn't the sins we confess and repent of. The unforgivable sin is seeing all that Jesus does—seeing good being achieved, miracles happening, demons being cast out, the lost being found—and calling it evil. The sin is calling good evil.

The teachers of the law saw everything. They couldn't deny the miracles. So they said, "He does good because he is evil. He drives out demons by the power of demons." If that's your thinking—if you see the clear work of God and call it demonic—then nothing can be done. That's blasphemy of the Holy Spirit.

Friends, if you carry guilt from sins in your past—from things you've done wrong, from estranged relationships, from regrets—know this: **Jesus forgives all sins.** Not because our sin was trivial, but because his sacrifice was so great, so profound, so universal, that it covers all our sins, all our regrets, all our slander against each other and against him.

> I acknowledged my sin to You, and my iniquity I did not hide; I said, "I will confess my transgressions to the Lord"; And You forgave the guilt of my sin. (Psalm 32:5)

Look at verse 28 again:

> Truly I tell you, people can be forgiven all their sins and every slander they utter,

But the teachers of the law looked at Jesus' cross and his love for us. They saw his sacrifice. And they called it evil. How wrong as wrong can be can you get if you come to that conclusion?

Today, we have our modern Pharisees and teachers of the law who call good evil:

- In New South Wales, to pray quietly for the unborn outside an abortion clinic is a criminal act carrying 6-12 months in jail.
- If you want to be homosexual, a hundred associations and government funding move mountains to help you. But if you're a homosexual wanting to be Christian, the opposite happens—media, government, health officials condemn you as evil.
- You can preach violence—kill the police, steal from the rich, promote pedophilia. But to preach the gospel is to be called a bigot, an extremist, a terrorist. Jesus on the cross is called child abuse.

That's the spirit Jesus warns about: calling God's spirit the spirit of demons. We can all get it wrong sometimes, calling good wrong or wrong good. But it takes a special kind of person to do both fanatically.

## Getting Inside: Family Redefined (3:20-21, 31-35)

There's a puzzle here. While some joined Jesus, others who should have didn't:

> Then Jesus entered a house, and again a crowd gathered, so that he and his disciples were not even able to eat. When his family heard about this, they went to take charge of him, for they said, "He is out of his mind."

His family thought he was crazy. They came to seize him. Then later:

> Then Jesus' mother and brothers arrived. Standing outside, they sent someone in to call him. A crowd was sitting around him, and they told him, "Your mother and brothers are outside looking for you."
>
> "Who are my mother and my brothers?" he asked.

This is striking. His own family wasn't automatically in the club. Being born in a Christian family doesn't make you a Christian, just like being born in a garage doesn't make you a car.

Notice the contrast: the disciples, the motley crew, are inside. Jesus' mother and brothers are standing outside. Those expected to be insiders aren't quite so.

> Then he looked at those seated in a circle around him and said, "Here are my mother and my brothers! Whoever does God's will is my brother and sister and mother."

The entry requirement is simple: **do God's will. Follow Jesus.**

Not automatic entry through blood relation. Not through birth into a Christian family. The invitation stands open to everyone: "Whoever does God's will is my brother and sister and mother."

There are exclusive clubs in the world that money cannot buy into—the Waltons, the Rothschilds, the Royal Family. You have to be related. You have to be mother, brother, and sister. But Jesus offers something radical: **the chance to become his family.** To be his brother, sister, mother through faith and obedience.

The membership closes at judgment day. But for now, Jesus invites us. Join his club. Do God's will. Follow him. Become his family.

Heavenly Father, we praise you for adopting us as your children and making us heirs of eternal life. In your mercy, you have washed us from our sins and made us clean in your sight. Yet we still fail to love you as we should and serve you as we ought. Forgive us our sins and renew us by your grace, that we may continue to grow as members of Christ, in whom alone is our salvation. Amen.
