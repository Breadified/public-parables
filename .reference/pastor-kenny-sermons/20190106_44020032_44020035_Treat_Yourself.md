# Acts 20:32-35

**Title:** Treat Yourself

**Date:** January 6, 2019

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Introduction

As we enter a new year and return to work, it's natural to think about treating ourselves—taking time to relax, enjoy downtime, and indulge a bit. But how should we view treating ourselves as followers of Christ? Is it selfish? Is it wise? The apostle Paul offers us some helpful guidance on this question.

## It's Right to Treat Yourself

Work has legitimate rewards, and treating yourself is part of God's design. Paul himself exemplifies this principle:

> You yourselves know that these hands of mine have supplied my own needs and the needs of my companions. (Acts 20:34)

Paul worked hard—he was a preacher by day and a tentmaker by night, his hands growing callous from labor, his voice growing hoarse. He treated himself as a reward for this genuine hard work.

### The Principle of Rewarding Labor

This isn't a new idea. Scripture is full of it:

> This is what I have observed to be good: that it is appropriate for a person to eat, to drink and to find satisfaction in their toilsome labor under the sun during the few days of life God has given them—for this is their lot. (Ecclesiastes 5:18)

> The hardworking farmer should be the first to receive his share of the crops. (2 Timothy 2:6)

> The worker is worthy of his wages. (1 Timothy 5:18)

To treat yourself as a reward for labor is a principle woven into creation itself. It's cruel to ask someone to work for nothing—Israel experienced this in slavery, where work brought no advancement, no ability to get ahead, no freedom to build wealth.

### Boundaries on Self-Treatment

Of course, treating yourself has limits:

> I have not coveted anyone's silver or gold or clothing. (Acts 20:33)

Paul avoided greed, materialism, and putting his hope in things. We're also called to work diligently, not to laziness:

> If a man will not work, he shall not eat. (2 Thessalonians 3:10)

And we reject asceticism—the false piety that denies legitimate enjoyment:

> Every creation of God is good, and nothing that is received with thanksgiving should be rejected. (1 Timothy 4:4)

Life lived to the full includes properly enjoying God's gifts. Treating yourself, without greed or covetousness, is appropriate. It refreshes you for the work ahead.

## But There's Something Better: Giving

Yet as you treat yourself, consider the fuller picture of Paul's life and teaching:

> In everything I did, I showed you that by this kind of hard work we must help the weak. (Acts 20:35)

Paul didn't work only for himself. He worked so that he could help others. His hard work had a direction: **serving the weak.**

### The Command to Give

This isn't mere suggestion—it's a command wrapped in practical love:

> Make it your ambition to lead a quiet life... and work with your hands, so that your daily life may win the respect of outsiders and so that you will not be dependent on anybody. (1 Thessalonians 4:11-12)

> Anyone who has been stealing must steal no longer, but must work, doing something useful with their own hands, that they may have something to share with those in need. (Ephesians 4:28)

The weak include the sick, widows, dependents, children, our families, our neighbors, and our communities. Work isn't merely for personal gain—it's so we have something to share.

### Practical Application

As you return to work this year, remember: your labor helps others. They need you. You can make a real difference through your work. Consider:

- Providing for your family
- Taking a poorer friend to coffee or lunch
- Inviting lonely people to meals at your home
- Paying for gas to give rides to elderly neighbors who can't drive
- Supporting your church community so others might know Christ
- More radically: fostering children, underwriting youth groups, teaching at local schools

## It Is More Blessed to Give Than to Receive

This principle comes from Jesus himself, preserved in Paul's testimony:

> It is more blessed to give than to receive. (Acts 20:35)

This seems counterintuitive. When we give, we lose—or so it appears. But Jesus reveals a deeper truth: giving isn't a zero-sum game.

### The Gospel Economy

In the world's economy, if I give you ten dollars, I have zero and you have ten. But in God's kingdom, something different happens. When you give, both the receiver and the giver are blessed. It's as if blessing multiplies rather than divides.

Consider capitalism: we often think someone getting rich means someone else gets poor. But a dynamic economy grows the pie. Bill Gates became a billionaire not by stealing, but by creating an industry that enriched millions. We all benefited—we have products, employment, opportunity.

God is saying giving operates on this principle—it creates blessing rather than diminishing it.

### Why Giving Produces Blessing

**We are wired for it.** Science confirms what Scripture teaches. Research shows that regardless of income level, people who spend money on others report greater happiness than those who spend on themselves. When given bonuses, the happiness people experienced correlated not with the size of the bonus, but with how much they gave away. Even small acts of generosity—buying a friend coffee instead of yourself—increase happiness.

Neuroscience reveals why: when we give support to others, reward-related regions of our brain activate, including areas associated with connection and relationship. We're neurologically designed to find joy in giving.

**We gain eternal reward.** Paul speaks of those he ministered to as his "crown":

> What is our hope, our joy, or the crown in which we will glory in the presence of our Lord Jesus when he comes? Is it not you? (1 Thessalonians 2:19)

Those we've encouraged, served, and helped—they become our reward in heaven. Friendship and fellowship await us there.

**We don't calculate God's provision.** 

> Now I commit you to God and to the word of his grace, which can build you up and give you an inheritance among all those who are sanctified. (Acts 20:32)

We have an inheritance waiting. Don't be calculative about giving—God knows far more than we can calculate. He is no man's debtor.

## Living This Truth

As you enter this new year—this two-thousand-and-nineteenth year since our Lord's first coming—consider how to live this paradox:

**Assess your Christmas not by what you received, but by what you gave.** Did you give thoughtfully? Sacrificially?

**On your birthday, consider reversing the pattern.** Instead of receiving gifts and asking others to pay for your meal, what if you paid for everyone's meal instead?

**Struggling with loneliness?** Loneliness often comes from isolation. But when you invest in people—taking them out, inviting them to your home, showing them love through sacrifice—you gain friendship and fellowship. You communicate Christ's love.

**Make giving regular and intentional.** Don't leave it to discretionary impulse. Decide once a year where you'll serve, how you'll give, who you'll care for. Set up direct giving. Commit to regular gatherings where you serve—church, Bible study, meals with friends.

## The Promise

> Blessed are all who fear the LORD, who walk in His ways! For when you eat the fruit of your labor, blessings and prosperity will be yours. (Psalm 128:1-2)

Treat yourself by treating others. You might gain only your own small reward, but you might receive far more. When you look after others, God's blessing flows in ways you cannot predict or measure.

This is what it means to be truly blessed.
