# John 6:1-71

**Title:** The Bread of Life: Jesus Answers Our Questions
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction: Our Questions and the Bible's Answers

Today's society raises many questions about the Bible, and you likely have your own. One persistent question is: How can miracles happen? Haven't figures like Hume proven that miracles are scientifically impossible?

The feeding of the five thousand in John 6 is indeed a miracle. But here's what's remarkable: John doesn't spend his time defending the historical reality of the miracle itself. His real concern lies elsewhere. Rather than getting bogged down in the controversy of whether Jesus fed 5,000 people, John focuses on what Jesus claims about *himself* through His discourse after the feeding.

The sign itself is less important than its meaning. The more shocking thing isn't that the sign happened—it's *who* Jesus claims to be.

## The Sign: The Facts

> Jesus crossed to the far shore of the Sea of Galilee. A great crowd followed him, and the Jewish Passover was near. When Jesus saw them gathering, He fed 5,000 people with five small barley loaves and two small fish. Twelve baskets of fragments were left over.

The people were well and truly full. Yet the very next day, they track Jesus down, seeking Him out. What follows is a remarkable dialogue in which the Jews repeatedly misunderstand Jesus—and Jesus repeatedly clarifies what the sign really points to.

Notice John's strategy: He doesn't waste energy proving the miracle happened. Instead, he uses his gospel to explain Jesus's claims about Himself. The peculiar concern in John's gospel isn't *what* Jesus did, but *who* Jesus claims to be.

## The Dialogue: Four Questions and Three Grumblings

As you follow this exchange, try to place yourself among those Jewish listeners. What would you have thought?

### Question 1: Is Jesus an Earthly King?

The Jews ask Jesus, "Rabbi, when did you come here?" They've been looking for Jesus to make him king—and now they've found him. But Jesus has news for them:

> "I tell you the truth, you are looking for me, not because you saw miraculous signs but because you ate the loaves and had your fill. Do not work for food that spoils, but for food that endures to eternal life, which the Son of Man will give you. On him God the Father has placed his seal of approval."

Jesus is correcting a fundamental misunderstanding. He is *not* coming to establish a world power on earth. He is *not* here to eliminate hunger and sickness. He is *not* here to provide free food to the starving, no matter how much we might wish He were. Rather, He comes to bring an eternal kingdom and eternal food that endures forever.

**Application:** Some of us share this same misunderstanding. We might expect that believing in Jesus guarantees He will save us from hunger, sickness, sorrow, and failure. We might think faith brings worldly success—friends, health, wealth. But Jesus's kingdom is a heavenly kingdom, not an earthly one.

### Question 2: What Must We Do?

The disciples ask a logical follow-up: "What must we do to do the works God requires?"

Jesus answers simply:

> "The work of God is this: to believe in the one he has sent."

The disciples seem satisfied—yes, the prophets said a deliverer would come from God, and yes, we must believe in Him. But here's where the Jews continue to misunderstand: They don't grasp *how* Jesus is like Moses.

Moses led them out of slavery, gave them the Law, fed them manna in the wilderness for forty years, and brought them to the edge of the Promised Land. Surely a prophet like him would be at least as powerful, if not greater? This expectation leads them to their next question.

### Question 3: Are You Greater Than Moses?

> "What miraculous sign then will you give that we may see it and believe you? Our forefathers ate the manna in the desert; as it is written: 'He gave them bread from heaven to eat.'"

Jesus responds:

> "I tell you the truth, it is not Moses who has given you the bread from heaven, but it is my Father who gives you the true bread from heaven. For the bread of God is he who comes down from heaven and gives life to the world."

At this point, the Jews probably mishear Jesus. They still think He's a prophet offering better *physical* bread than Moses. They haven't grasped that He's saying the bread is not a *thing* but a *person*. So they eagerly ask: "Sir, give us this bread always."

But Jesus's next statement is where things become deeply uncomfortable.

## The Claims: Who Jesus Really Is

> "I am the bread of life. He who comes to me will never go hungry, and he who believes in me will never be thirsty. But as I told you, you have seen me and still you do not believe. All that the Father gives me will come to me, and whoever comes to me I will never drive away. For I have come down from heaven not to do my will but to do the will of him who sent me. And this is the will of him who sent me, that I shall lose none of all that he has given me, but raise them up at the last day. For my Father's will is that everyone who looks to the Son and believes in him shall have eternal life, and I will raise him up at the last day."

Jesus makes three massive claims here:

1. **Through Jesus you will never hunger** (spiritual fulfillment, not earthly provision)
2. **Jesus has been sent by God to perfectly complete His Father's will**, giving people eternal life
3. **Those who have eternal life are those who look to and believe in the Son**

**Application:** These are claims on which the truth of Christianity stands or falls. Other religions and worldviews are often comfortable with answers 1 and 2, but question 3—"Jesus is the only way to God"—is what pushes people out of their comfort zone. This is why the question "Who do you think Jesus is?" is so revealing when discussing faith with non-Christians. Even our question about miracles today depends on a particular view of Jesus.

It's like if your brother stood up at dinner and said he was God. You'd be shocked too.

## The Grumblings: Jewish Resistance

### Grumbling 1: "How Can He Say He Came From Heaven?"

> "At this the Jews began to grumble about him because he said, 'I am the bread that came down from heaven.' They said, 'Is this not Jesus, the son of Joseph, whose father and mother we know? How can he now say, "I came down from heaven"?'"

Jesus responds by pointing them to God's will:

> "Stop grumbling among yourselves. No one can come to me unless the Father who sent me draws him, and I will raise him up at the last day. It is written in the Prophets: 'They will all be taught by God.' Everyone who listens to the Father and learns from him comes to me."

Jesus makes a powerful argument:

- **It is the Father's will**, testified to by the Prophets themselves
- **If you believe the Prophets, you'll believe Jesus**
- **Jesus surpasses Moses** not by bringing earthly victory, but by offering bread that leads to eternal life—eternal victory where no one dies

And Jesus reveals the mechanism: "This bread is my flesh, which I will give for the life of the world."

### Grumbling 2: "How Can He Give Us His Flesh to Eat?"

> "Then the Jews began to argue sharply among themselves, 'How can this man give us his flesh to eat?'"

Jesus intensifies His statement:

> "I tell you the truth, unless you eat the flesh of the Son of Man and drink his blood, you have no life in you. Whoever eats my flesh and drinks my blood has eternal life, and I will raise him up at the last day. For my flesh is real food and my blood is real drink. Whoever eats my flesh and drinks my blood remains in me, and I in him. Just as the living Father sent me and I live because of the Father, so the one who feeds on me will live because of me. This is the bread that came down from heaven. Your forefathers ate manna and died, but he who feeds on this bread will live forever."

Each revelation is a bombshell. The Jews realize this isn't the powerful, victorious earthly king they expected. First, they learn He hasn't come to establish political dominance. Second, they discover He's going to die. Salvation, Jesus reveals, will come at a price.

### Grumbling 3: "This is a Hard Teaching"

> "On hearing it, many of his disciples said, 'This is a hard teaching. Who can accept it?'"

Jesus responds:

> "Does this offend you? What if you see the Son of Man ascend to where he was before! The Spirit gives life; the flesh counts for nothing. The words I have spoken to you are spirit and they are life. Yet there are some of you who do not believe."

The result is decisive:

> "From this time many of his disciples turned back and no longer followed him."

## The Faithful Few: Peter's Confession

Jesus turns to the Twelve: "You do not want to leave too, do you?"

Simon Peter answers with a confession that stands against all the confusion:

> "Lord, to whom shall we go? You have the words of eternal life. We believe and know that you are the Holy One of God."

Yet even here, darkness lurks. Jesus replies:

> "Have I not chosen you, the Twelve? Yet one of you is a devil!" (He meant Judas, the son of Simon Iscariot, who, though one of the Twelve, was later to betray him.)

## Conclusion

In this passage, we see Jesus not primarily defending the miracle, but clarifying His identity. The real scandal isn't that He fed 5,000—it's that He claims to be the bread of life, the eternal sustenance of the soul, the one sent by the Father, and the only way to eternal life.

The question isn't whether miracles are scientifically possible. The question is: Who is Jesus? And will we believe?
