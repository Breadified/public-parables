# Numbers 1:1-10:13

**Title:** In the Wilderness
**Date:** 2 June 2013
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## One Giant Camping Trip

When you take a large group camping together, three essential things come to mind: count the people to ensure everyone is there, set the rules about where to camp and how to live together, and most importantly—don't die.

The book of Numbers is precisely like that camping trip. God tells Moses to take a census in the Desert of Sinai, and the Israelites are living in the Tent of Meeting with specific instructions for how to order their camp. The three themes of Numbers mirror our camping essentials perfectly: counting the people, establishing the rules for living with God, and surviving the wilderness journey that lies ahead.

## 1. Numbers is About Numbers—Count the People (1:1-46)

> "Take a census of the whole Israelite community by their clans and families, listing every man by name, one by one." (Numbers 1:2-3)

The book is called Numbers because when the Hebrew scriptures were translated into Greek (a couple of centuries before Jesus), the fourth book received the Greek name *Arithmoi*—meaning calculation or numbers. The name is fitting, as the book is fundamentally about counting people.

### The Census

Moses and Aaron conduct a detailed census of all Israelite men twenty years old or older who are able to serve in the army. The count is repeated several times throughout the book—in the initial census, again in the camp arrangement of chapter 2, among the Levites in chapter 3, at the tabernacle offerings in chapter 7, and once more in chapter 26.

The final number reported is remarkable: 603,550 fighting men. Accounting for women, children, and the elderly, scholars estimate the total population at roughly 2.5 million people.

### Why Count?

**The Practical Reason:** These former slaves were transforming into a nation. They needed to know their military strength as they prepared to enter and take possession of the promised land. Every nation going into a foreign land would naturally count its fighting men.

**The Theological Reason:** The numbers testify that God is keeping His promises. The story of these numbers begins with Abraham:

> "Look up at the heavens and count the stars—if indeed you can count them. So shall your offspring be." (Genesis 15:4-5)

When Israel entered Egypt, God's family numbered only seventy-three people. Joseph was already in Egypt, second in command—what seemed like a high point. But then came slavery. Yet by the time the Israelites left Egypt, they had multiplied from 73 to 603,550 fighting men alone. God had kept every tribe alive through centuries of oppression and had caused them to increase.

The numbers remind us that God values what He counts. We count what matters to us—our children, our money, our possessions. God counts His people. In the same way that one lost coin causes the woman in Jesus's parable to search diligently and then rejoice with her friends, God takes joy in each person:

> "In the same way, I tell you, there is rejoicing in the presence of the angels of God over one sinner who repents." (Luke 15:10)

These numbers represent God's love. He remembers His promises. He has not abandoned His people.

## 2. Numbers is About God—Set the Rules (1:47-9:23)

> "The LORD spoke to Moses in the Tent of Meeting in the Desert of Sinai on the first day of the second month." (Numbers 1:1)

The phrase "The LORD said" appears approximately forty-four times in Numbers, serving as a literary device that joins sections together and emphasizes God's constant presence and communication with His people. This name—sometimes called "The Lord Said"—highlights that even in the seemingly mundane details of camp organization and ceremonial rules, God is speaking. His presence is here.

### The Tabernacle: A Place to Meet God

For the first time, the Israelites are living with God's presence among them. The Tent of Meeting is where God dwells and where He will speak with Moses. All camp activity must be organized around this central tent.

> "The Israelites are to camp around the Tent of Meeting some distance from it, each man under his standard with the banners of his family." (Numbers 2:2)

Imagine a campfire in the center of a wilderness camp. Everyone orients their tents around it, and no matter where you go—collecting water, visiting a friend, completing your work—you see the smoke rising from that central tent. It is a constant, visual reminder that God is here with you.

This is the fulfillment of what Jesus would later embody: God dwelling among His people.

> "The Word became flesh and made his dwelling among us. We have seen his glory, the glory of the One and Only, who came from the Father, full of grace and truth." (John 1:14)

Jesus tabernacled among us. Today, His Word dwells with us:

> "Let the word of Christ dwell in you richly as you teach and admonish one another with all wisdom." (Colossians 3:16)

This means our life, like Israel's, must be organized around Christ. When we gather on Sundays to hear God's Word preached, that is not the time to catch up on phones or prepare morning tea—it is the time to prioritize listening. Our church meetings, our one-to-one conversations, even our social gatherings should put Jesus at the center. We don't merely download our problems onto each other; we first let God download His truth to us, which puts our heads in the right perspective before we even speak.

### The Holiness of God and Living with Him

But living with God has profound implications. God is holy—completely set apart, pure, and separate from sin. This is why chapters 5 and 6 contain extensive instructions for purity.

The camp must be clean. Anyone with an infectious disease, a discharge, or who is ceremonially unclean because of contact with a dead body must be sent outside the camp:

> "Send away male and female alike; send them outside the camp so they will not defile their camp, where I dwell among them." (Numbers 5:2-3)

When someone wrongs another person and is unfaithful to the Lord, they must confess their sin and make full restitution, adding a fifth to what they owe. There are rules for those who make special vows as Nazirites—those who choose voluntary separation to the Lord by abstaining from wine and maintaining ritual purity.

The point is stark: it is dangerous to live with a holy God. We see this illustrated vividly in the story of Aaron's sons:

> "Nadab and Abihu, however, fell dead before the LORD when they made an offering with unauthorized fire before him in the Desert of Sinai." (Numbers 3:4)

Holiness requires that everything be done rightly. No ordinary person can simply approach the Tent of Meeting—only those who have been set apart can draw near.

### The Levites: A Buffer Zone

To make it possible for the Israelites to live safely in God's presence, God appointed the Levites as intermediaries. During the exodus from Egypt, the Levites alone had shown zealous devotion to God, and now they would serve a critical function.

> "Bring the tribe of Levi and present them to Aaron the priest to assist him. They are to perform duties for him and for the whole community at the Tent of Meeting by doing the work of the tabernacle." (Numbers 3:5-7)

The Levites served in three main functions:

**First, they served the temple:** They took care of all the furnishings, carried the tabernacle when the camp moved, set it up at each new location, and maintained it according to God's exacting specifications. The Kohathites carried the sacred items under careful covering. The Gershonites carried the curtains and materials. The Merarites carried the poles and scaffolding.

**Second, they literally lived between the people and God:** 

> "Moses and Aaron and his sons were to camp to the east of the tabernacle, toward the sunrise, in front of the Tent of Meeting. They were responsible for the care of the sanctuary on behalf of the Israelites. Anyone else who approached the sanctuary was to be put to death." (Numbers 3:38)

Like a buffer around a campfire that protects you from the flames without letting you get burned, the Levites acted as a protective barrier between the holy God and His people.

**Third, they were substitutes for Israel itself:** When God struck down the firstborn of Egypt, He spared those protected by the blood of the lamb. The firstborn of Israel were set apart as belonging to God, but instead of taking all of them, God took the entire tribe of Levi in their place:

> "I have taken the Levites from among the Israelites in place of the first male offspring of every Israelite woman. The Levites are mine, for all the firstborn are mine. When I struck down all the firstborn in Egypt, I set apart for myself every firstborn in Israel." (Numbers 3:11-13)

There were 22,273 firstborn sons but only 22,000 Levites, so money was collected to make up the difference. This was a tremendous blessing to Israel—their firstborn were redeemed by substitution.

The Levites blessed Israel with the priestly blessing:

> "The LORD bless you and keep you; the LORD make his face shine upon you and be gracious to you; the LORD turn his face toward you and give you peace." (Numbers 6:24-26)

### The Fulfillment in Christ

This pattern of substitution and mediation finds its ultimate fulfillment in Jesus. God gave up His firstborn—His only Son—for us. We have been bought by His blood:

> "Do you not know that your body is a temple of the Holy Spirit, who is in you, whom you have received from God? You are not your own; you were bought at a price." (1 Corinthians 6:19-20)

Jesus serves as our great High Priest and intermediary. He took the wrath of God upon Himself when we sinned. Because of His blood, we can now safely approach God's holiest place with no fear of consequences. We can come in prayer. We can know Him. We can dwell with Him.

## 3. In the Wilderness—Don't Die (10:1-13)

> "The LORD spoke to Moses: 'Make two trumpets of hammered silver, and use them for calling the community together and for having the camps set out.'" (Numbers 10:1-2)

The third name sometimes given to Numbers is "In the Wilderness," referring to the desert—not necessarily a barren wasteland, but the wilderness: the place in between. The Israelites have come from one place (Egypt) and are heading toward a destination (Canaan), and the wilderness is that in-between space. It is the journey itself.

By the end of chapter 10, after nineteen days of organizing the camp, after a year and two months since the exodus, and after roughly four hundred years since God's promises to Abraham, the Israelites are finally ready to move. The cloud lifts from the tabernacle:

> "On the twentieth day of the second month of the second year, the cloud lifted from above the tabernacle of the Testimony. Then the Israelites set out from the Desert of Sinai and traveled from place to place until the cloud came to rest in the Desert of Paran." (Numbers 10:11-12)

It all sounds wonderful. The trumpets sound. The camps set out in order. The people advance toward the promised land. But there is one critical instruction they must not forget: **don't die**.

### The Lesson We Must Learn

What happens next is recorded in Deuteronomy. Instead of taking eleven days to reach the promised land, the journey takes forty years. The people forget the key instruction. They forget that God dwells with them. They forget to trust Him. They forget the intermediaries set up for their protection. They forget the numbers that testify to God's faithfulness and the fulfillment of His promises. Instead, they believe God has cheated them.

There is a powerful parallel between their journey and ours. The Israelites were in their second year after rescue from Egypt. We live roughly two thousand years after Jesus's rescue of us. They were saved but had not yet entered the promised land. We are saved but have not yet reached heaven. Salvation has been accomplished, but it is not yet complete. This "now and not yet" defines the Christian life—we are on a journey toward our inheritance.

We, like them, are vulnerable to seduction by sin. We can easily forget God's presence and promises when tempted by:

- Food and basic instincts
- Anger and pride
- Fear
- Money
- Sex

Paul reminded the early church of this pattern:

> "These things happened to them as examples for us. Do not be conceited, and do not fall into temptation. No temptation has seized you except what is common to all. God is faithful; he will not let you be tempted beyond what you can bear. But when you are tempted, he will also provide a way out so that you can endure it." (1 Corinthians 10:11-13)

The book of Numbers was written not to condemn us, but to keep us. Can we learn from their wilderness journey and not be seduced by the promises of the world? Can we remember that God dwells with us, that we have been bought by the blood of Christ, that we are held safely by His intermediary work, and that He is faithful to complete what He has promised?

> "The LORD bless you and keep you; the LORD make his face shine upon you and be gracious to you; the LORD turn his face toward you and give you peace." (Numbers 6:24-26)

Amen.
