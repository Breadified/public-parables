# Ephesians 1:11-14

**Title:** No Longer Enemies  
**Date:** June 1, 2008  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Born Enemies

What comes to mind when you think of "born enemies"? Rivals like Paris Hilton and Lindsay Lohan, Manchester United versus Arsenal, or competing tech giants. But Paul had something far more significant in mind when he wrote Ephesians.

Last week we explored God's plan of reconciliation—all things brought under the headship of Christ. Paul was writing to an ancient world divided not just by the prehistoric feud between God and humanity, but by a profound division between Jew and Gentile. This was genuine, generational hatred. In writing Ephesians, Paul sought to explain to both groups that the gospel belonged equally to each of them.

## The Reconciliation of the Jews (v.11-12)

> In him we [as in we, Jews] were also chosen, having been predestined according to the plan of him who works out everything in conformity with the purpose of his will, in order that we, who were the first to hope in Christ, might be for the praise of his glory. (Ephesians 1:11-12)

Notice the shift in pronouns. Throughout Ephesians, Paul uses "we" to refer to all Christians. But in verse 11, "we" suddenly means *Jews specifically*, while verse 13 switches to "you"—referring to *Gentiles*. Paul is addressing two distinct groups.

The word translated "chosen" here is different from the one used in verse 4. This word means "inherited" or "apportioned by lot"—language drawn from Israel's history. In Old Testament times, land and inheritance were distributed among the tribes of Israel by lot. Since the land itself belonged to God, Deuteronomy 32:9 says: "For the Lord's portion is his people, Jacob his allotted inheritance." God's people *are* His inheritance.

This was a special designation reserved for Israel alone. The phrase "the first to hope in Christ" reinforces this: the Jews came first into the promises of Christ.

Being a Christian Jew was profoundly privileged. As Romans 9:4-5 reminds us:

> Theirs is the adoption as sons; theirs the divine glory, the covenants, the receiving of the law, the temple worship and the promises. Theirs are the patriarchs, and from them is traced the human ancestry of Christ, who is God over all, forever praised!

These Christian Jews received not just the promises themselves, but their fulfillment. Despite fierce community opposition, they believed. Like the tall poppies we Australians secretly admire even as we mock them, these believers inherited everything—and they didn't waste it.

## The Reconciliation of the Gentiles (v.13)

> And you also were included in Christ when you heard the word of truth, the gospel of your salvation. (Ephesians 1:13)

If Jews were the tall poppies—privileged, chosen, inheritors of centuries of covenants—then Gentiles were the underdogs. The ones who had no claim, no history, no right to expect anything.

Yet they received the *same grace* as the privileged Christian Jews.

Think of it like learning your neighbor won the lottery, then discovering they're including you in the prize. You don't deserve it, yet there you are, unexpectedly blessed.

This was radical in the first-century world. Jesus once told the Syrophoenician woman that the bread belonged to the children, not to "gentile dogs"—yet when she humbly asked for even the crumbs, He commended her faith (Mark 7). Peter, in a vision, saw unclean animals and heard God say, "Do not call anything impure that God has made clean" (Acts 11). The moment Gentiles received the Holy Spirit, Peter recognized that God had granted them "repentance unto life."

Yet in another sense, this shouldn't have surprised anyone. Abraham received a promise—not as a Jew (the nation didn't yet exist), but as a man of faith—that *all nations* would be blessed through his seed (Genesis 12:3). This promise predated the law that would later distinguish Israel from all others. The entire world was always meant to benefit. The gospel simply fulfilled what God promised from the beginning.

## Why Are They Equal?

Both Jews and Gentiles are equal because they share two fundamental realities.

### 1. They Share the Same Spirit

> Having believed, you were marked in him with a seal, the promised Holy Spirit, who is a deposit guaranteeing our inheritance until the redemption of those who are God's possession—to the praise of his glory. (Ephesians 1:13-14)

When Gentiles believed, they received the same Holy Spirit that marked the Jews. A seal indicates possession—like a brand on cattle or a name label on a drink bottle. The Spirit's seal marks us as God's possession. Both groups bear this mark equally.

**They have the same assurance.** The seal is a deposit guaranteeing our inheritance. The word "inheritance" echoes the language from verse 11—it's yours. In this world, we arrange ourselves in hierarchies based on looks, success, test scores, titles, occupations, wealth. One successful banker I know says money is just "a way to keep score." But the Christian inheritance isn't based on our achievements. There is no first class and second class in heaven. All Christians inherit equally.

Until the final redemption comes—when our physical bodies are redeemed—we are already possessed and preserved by God. If you've heard the word of truth and believed, you have the Spirit and the seal of guarantee. Your passage to heaven is secured. You need not be afraid.

**They have the same access to God.** This was made dramatically clear at Pentecost. When the Spirit came upon the Jews, signs and wonders accompanied it—marking a pivotal moment in history. But this wasn't the *only* coming of the Spirit. When Gentiles received the same Spirit with the same signs, it demonstrated that the Old Testament promise extended to them as well. They had the same access to God's presence.

### 2. They Share the Same Sin

Both Jews and Gentiles, though historically enemies, shared a common condition:

> As for you, you were dead in your transgressions and sins, in which you used to live when you followed the ways of this world and of the ruler of the kingdom of the air, the spirit who is now at work in those who are disobedient. All of us also lived among them at one time, gratifying the cravings of our sinful nature and following its desires and thoughts. Like the rest, we were by nature objects of wrath. (Ephesians 2:1-3)

Equal sin requires equal grace. Both groups heard the same gospel—God's Word, the power of God to transform hearts and minds. Regardless of race or background, those who believe this Word receive the same Spirit and the same access to forgiveness.

## Implications: No First Class or Second Class

The first implication is clear: **There are no first-class or second-class citizens in God's kingdom.**

Yet in our churches, different classes sometimes emerge. Long-time Christians may condescend to new believers, wielding their knowledge and tradition like weapons. Or new believers, full of zeal, may dismiss the wisdom of those who came before them. Some elevate those who experience miracles or supernatural phenomena as "more spiritual," as if a second blessing sets them apart. But miracles are not distinctly Christian—other religions testify to healings and wonders. Fire-walking, levitation, miraculous healings happen in many faiths. What matters is not the sign, but the Spirit.

The Spirit is the same. There are no tiers of Christianity, no elite class of more-spiritual believers. All who believe have equal access, equal assurance, equal inheritance.

## Our Real Unity: The Gospel, Not Proximity

A natural follow-up question: If Jews and Gentiles are truly united, must they all gather in one place?

The answer is no. Real unity is not about physical proximity—it's about unity in purpose, unity in mind, and above all, unity in the *gospel*. It's about being equal citizens of heaven.

Consider the impossibility of enforcing physical unity. What does "gathered together" even mean? Thirty believers from one congregation? A hundred from a combined group? Eight hundred from a parish? Three thousand from a city? Which groups count? The church across the road? Your school Christian group? Your university group? Must they all merge into one assembly to be "united"?

Thirty years ago, some churches attempted precisely this vision—forming the Uniting Denomination and World Council of Churches—hoping to bring everyone under one roof. It didn't work. Sharing a label doesn't create unity.

In fact, even in New Testament times, Jewish congregations remained largely Jewish and Gentile congregations remained largely Gentile. Paul visited the synagogues first, then the Gentile gatherings. Gatherings naturally form for historical and missional reasons. At Crossword, we are predominantly Asian because of our historical ties to the Cantonese congregation, and because we seek to reach Asian neighbors in our area. This isn't exclusivity—it's having a goal and understanding how people actually work.

But exclusivity has no place among us. If a Christian enters our doors—someone of a different race, a fresh immigrant, someone dressed differently—we *never* snub them. They are our brother. We are already one in Christ. If a non-Christian visitor comes, we welcome them too—not because we're united in the gospel (we're not), but because we've declared ourselves a public place of welcome, and we genuinely believe in welcoming strangers.

We are united—united in the word of truth, united by the same seal, united in a Spirit that transcends buildings and labels. Real unity is not found in bricks and stones or the labels we wear. It's found in the gospel.

## No Longer Enemies

Consider the conflict between Palestinians and Israelis today—sixty years of hatred, multiple generations of tit-for-tat revenge. "My grandfather's land was wrongly taken. My father lived here, I was born and raised here. Who's right?" That is the kind of born-enemies hatred that Jews and Gentiles experienced in Jesus' time. Romans versus Jews. Generational, deep, seemingly irreconcilable.

Yet Paul calls them to reconciliation: *because they are now brothers*. They share the same Father, the same inheritance.

Some of us may face similar enmity within the church—casual hatred or deep animosity toward other Christians. Paul's word to us is the same: **Be reconciled.**

There is no more Jew and Gentile, but Christians. We share the same Father and inheritance. We must forgive one another—not in the shallow sense of simply forgetting, but in the deep sense that our shared memory serves a purpose. As one author writes, "The proper goal of the memory of wrongs suffered—its appropriate end—is the formation of the communion of love between all people, including victims and perpetrators."

In light of our reconciliation with God, let us be reconciled to one another. We are no longer enemies. We are brothers and sisters, inheritors together of the grace of God.
