## The Beginning of Family and Relationships

Last week we explored the ordering of the cosmos—the creation of the universe itself. Today we turn to another beginning: the beginning of the family and human relationships.

> Genesis 2:4 This is the account of the heavens and the earth when they were created, when the Lord God made the earth and the heavens.

The phrase "account" literally means "the generations of" in Hebrew. This formula appears throughout Genesis—"these are the generations of Adam," "these are the generations of Noah," "these are the generations of Shem," and so on. Each time it introduces something new. In this passage, we encounter the beginning of the family—the beginning of relationships.

---

## 1. Loneliness is Not Good

> Genesis 2:18 The Lord God said, "It is not good for the man to be alone. I will make a helper suitable for him."

This statement is striking. Throughout the creation account in Genesis 1, God repeatedly declared His work "good," and after creating humanity, He called creation "very good." Mankind is not an afterthought or a plague upon the earth, but the pinnacle of creation—the world is made for us.

So it catches us off guard when God says, "It is not good" for the man to be alone. Loneliness is genuinely not good.

### The Loneliness Crisis

Our modern world bears this out. Surveys show that 20-50% of people always or often feel lonely. In Japan, over half a million people under 40 haven't left their homes or interacted with anyone for at least six months. Nearly half of Britons over 65 consider the television or a pet their main source of company. In Canada, 28% of households are solo; across the European Union, it's 34%.

The health consequences are severe. Scientists have found that loneliness leads to psychiatric disorders including depression, anxiety, schizophrenia, and hallucinatory delirium. Studies show that social isolation triggers cellular changes resulting in chronic inflammation, predisposing the lonely to heart disease, stroke, metastatic cancer, and Alzheimer's disease. One analysis pooling data from 70 studies following 3.4 million people found that lonely individuals had a 26% higher risk of dying—rising to 32% if they lived alone.

### The Root Problem: Individualism

Our culture tells us to prioritize ourselves above all else: "Pay yourself first," "You're the most important person," "The world revolves around your self-fulfillment." Under the influence of the self-esteem movement, we treat relationships like consumer goods. When friendships or marriages no longer serve our immediate needs, we simply discard them and upgrade to something better—changing relationships almost as quickly as we change our phones.

In contrast, God says: it is not good for man to be alone. Individualism and selfishness are simply not good. The solution lies in relationship and community.

## 2. The Helper

> Genesis 2:18 The Lord God said, "It is not good for the man to be alone. I will make a helper suitable for him."

Many misunderstand this passage, imagining Adam saying something like, "You complete me," or interpreting it as though the solution to loneliness is sexual companionship. But this misses the actual purpose entirely.

### The Context: Incompleteness and Work

To understand what kind of helper Adam needs, we must look at the beginning of Genesis 2:

> Genesis 2:5-7 Now no shrub had yet appeared on the earth and no plant had yet sprung up, for the Lord God had not sent rain on the earth and there was no one to work the ground. But streams came up from the earth and watered the whole surface of the ground. Then the Lord God formed a man from the dust of the ground and breathed into his nostrils the breath of life, and the man became a living being.

The world is incomplete. There are no shrubs, no plants, no rain—but there is groundwater. The problem is clear: without rain, someone is needed to bring water from the streams and irrigate the soil. That someone is man. He is placed in the garden to work:

> Genesis 2:8, 15 Now the Lord God had planted a garden in the east, in Eden; and there he put the man he had formed. The Lord God took the man and put him in the Garden of Eden to work it and take care of it.

His task is twofold: to work and to guard. To rule and subdue the wilderness, domesticating it into a garden. To maintain what has been cultivated. To civilize creation through obedience to God's statutes.

So when Adam experiences loneliness in verse 18, he's not experiencing social isolation in a modern sense. He's in the sense of "I'm carrying groceries in one hand and a baby in the other—someone, please open the door! I need help!" He needs a companion to accomplish this divine task.

### Why Not the Animals?

But here's the puzzle. God brings the animals to Adam:

> Genesis 2:19-20 Now the Lord God had formed out of the ground all the wild animals and all the birds in the sky. He brought them to the man to see what he would name them; and whatever the man called each living creature, that was its name. So the man gave names to all the livestock, the birds in the sky and all the wild animals. But for Adam no suitable helper was found.

Imagine it like a parade. A horse could help—it could carry water in huge tubs. A buffalo could drag a plow, creating irrigation channels from streams to fields. A donkey could work a water pump. Yet after creature after creature—perhaps hundreds or thousands—we find: "for Adam no suitable helper was found."

Here's the remarkable thing: statistically, another male would be more useful for heavy labor. Based purely on average strength, a second man would be more efficient. Yet none of the animals sufficed because of something deeper. The task wouldn't be completed in one lifetime. It would take many lifetimes. To fill the earth and subdue it would require not just two hands, but generations of helpers. The solution wasn't another worker—it was a wife with whom he could multiply.

### The Creation of Eve

> Genesis 2:21-23 So the LORD God caused the man to fall into a deep sleep; and while he was sleeping, he took one of the man's ribs and closed up the place with flesh. Then the LORD God made a woman from the rib he had taken out of the man, and he brought her to the man. The man said, "This is now bone of my bones and flesh of my flesh; she shall be called 'woman,' for she was taken out of man."

Literally, the word "rib" means "side"—suggesting togetherness, equality, and partnership. The wordplay in Hebrew is beautiful. The man calls her "isha" (woman) because she comes from "ish" (man). And his response is emphatic: "This one! At last! This one!" After all that disappointment—"not the one, not the one, not the one"—finally: "This ONE I can multiply with! This ONE I can fulfill God's commission with! This ONE we will extend the garden with!"

The name itself is revealing. "Woman" doesn't mean "woe-man" or carry any negative connotation. It's a celebration of complementarity and compatibility.

Eve is created as a helper for a divine task—not a sex slave, not a romantic cure for loneliness, but a partner in God's mission to fill the earth and bring forth generations who will steward creation.

## 3. The Nature of Marriage

> Genesis 2:24 That is why a man leaves his father and mother and is united to his wife, and they become one flesh.

### Exclusive

Marriage is exclusive—one man, one woman. Scripture repeatedly affirms this:

> 1 Timothy 3:2 Now the overseer of a church must be above reproach, faithful to his wife...

Research confirms this design. Studies show that monogamous marriage reduces male criminality by 35%. Districts with skewed sex ratios show dramatically higher murder rates. When polygyny is widespread, male parental investment decreases, and children's outcomes suffer significantly. Among the Dogon of Mali, children in polygynous households were 7 to 11 times more likely to die before age 10, despite equivalent per capita resources.

### Permanent

> Malachi 2:15-16 Has not the one God made you? You belong to him in body and spirit. And what does the one God seek? Godly offspring. So be on your guard, and do not be unfaithful to the wife of your youth. "The man who hates and divorces his wife," says the Lord, the God of Israel, "does violence to the one he should protect," says the Lord Almighty.

Divorce is always against God's purposes. The research is stark: children ordinarily develop best when reared by both a mother and a father, experiencing regular interaction with both genders' parenting techniques. Children who grow up without a father are five times more likely to live in poverty, nine times more likely to drop out of school, and twenty times more likely to end up in prison. They're more likely to experience behavioral problems, run away from home, or become teenage parents themselves.

Yes, sometimes life is tragic—the loss of a parent through accident. In those circumstances, adoption or remarriage becomes a loving response to tragedy. But choosing divorce intentionally subjects your child to harm from the outset. Divorce is not the end of the story—statistics are percentages, not destiny—but they show the healthier path.

### Gendered

Marriage is gendered: male and female. This is not arbitrary. Children need both mothers and fathers because they bring complementary strengths and parenting styles affecting language development, behavioral outcomes, academic performance, self-control, and healthy sexual identity formation.

The entire design of marriage centers on something foundational: procreation. You cannot have children without male and female complementarity. When gender is removed from the definition of marriage, the concept becomes undefined. Then why not two sisters, best friends, a team, a person and a dog, or a person and themselves? Everything becomes marriage, which means nothing is.

We have two options: either marriage means any emotional union (in which case we must grant it to all emotional ties), or marriage is defined by its conjugal nature—male and female united for life. If we choose the latter, this isn't discrimination. Everyone has equal access to marriage: find a willing partner of the opposite gender and commit. That's simply naming what marriage is.

Marriage exists for the creation and raising of children. It doesn't make sense for those who reject that purpose to demand entry into the institution. Just as a women's gym reserves space for women (not as discrimination, but as design), or the Olympics requires the best athletes, marriage has a nature and purpose.

## 4. Multiply: Putting Children Back on the Agenda

The Anglican Prayer Book's marriage service states:

> "It is not to be entered upon unadvisedly, lightly, or merely to satisfy physical desires; but prayerfully, with careful thought, and with reverence for God, duly considering the purposes for which it was ordained."
>
> "It was ordained for the procreation of children and that they might be brought up in the nurture and instruction of the Lord, to the praise of his holy name."
>
> "It was ordained so that those to whom God has granted the gift of marriage might live a chaste and holy life, as befits members of Christ's body."
>
> "And it was ordained for the mutual companionship, help, and comfort, that the one ought to have of the other, both in prosperity and adversity."

Notice the order. The purposes were once understood clearly. But something shifted. With the introduction of the contraceptive pill and widespread access to contraception in the 1960s, sex became separated from children. What was meant to be family planning became family *stopping*.

### The Modern Distortion

Today, we kill. About 30% of conceptions in developed countries end in abortion—a third of this congregation. For Christians, the numbers aren't much better. We delay. We dedicate our most fertile years to careers, having children when our fertility declines. We restrict. If blessed with children at all, we replace rather than multiply—two children means replacement, not multiplication.

Sometimes this cannot be helped. But in our culture, children have become a lifestyle choice. We want them as accessories to keep up appearances, as an experience to tick off a bucket list. We want the benefits without the costs. We want babies without letting them affect our budget, travel, or career. We subcontract their care to nannies and preschools while we pursue ourselves. And when we do invest, it's conditional: we'll pay for them if they achieve the grades or status we demand.

We see children as a problem—the "overpopulation argument." Yet on a modest lifestyle, we could fit the world's population in a space the size of New South Wales. But on an American lifestyle of consumption? Yes, that's unsustainable. The issue is our choices, not our numbers.

This same selfishness gives the same-sex marriage debate its seeming legitimacy. We removed children from the equation of marriage and emphasized friendship instead. If marriage is really just about two people's love and companionship, then why can't same-sex couples marry? But put children back into the equation—put back the actual purpose of marriage—and the question answers itself.

### A Better Vision

What if we put children back on the agenda? Not just the young unmarried and newly married, but particularly to young people: this is how you change society most effectively. How many people have you brought to Christ in the past decade? You could easily double that in the next decade by raising your own children as Christian disciples. 

The early church expanded not through superior organization or compelling arguments alone, but through outbreeding the empire. Early Christians didn't abort when everyone else did. They formed orphanages and adopted the unwanted children. While the empire gloried in child-free self-gratification, Christians simply got on with living and raising the next generation of believers. That's why Christianity came to dominate a pagan world.

This requires choosing to marry someone who will be a good mother or father—not a good travel companion or model, but someone genuinely suited to family life. Look at your church: who brings food to others without being asked? Who takes initiative to serve? Who isn't selfish? Who delights in children? And become that person yourself.

## 5. The Loneliness Problem Revisited

We must clarify something: marriage is not primarily the cure for loneliness. If that were its purpose, then Jesus, who was unmarried, would have suffered tragic isolation. And celibacy would be the worst possible condition.

Yet we see exactly this conclusion in modern thinking. A single Christian woman once wrote that she regards her life of abstinence as a "tragic existence," saying: "Is it any worse than that experienced by single Christians who fear growing old alone?" Some even argue that homosexual partnership might be "the lesser of two evils, the greater evil being enforced celibacy and the accompanying loneliness."

But Augustine quipped wisely: "If God had intended marriage for companionship, He would have ordained that two men would have married one another."

### God's Provisions for Loneliness

The Bible presents several remedies for loneliness, none of which require marriage:

**Jesus Christ Himself.** Our ultimate completion comes through marriage to Christ. The church is the bride of Christ. Jesus completes you—not a spouse.

**Friendship.** The highest expression of love Jesus mentions is not romantic:

> John 15:13-15 Greater love has no one than this, that he lay down his life for his friends... I no longer call you servants... Instead, I have called you friends, for everything that I learned from my Father I have made known to you.

No greater love than laying down your life for your *friend*—not lover, but friend.

**Sacrificial Love.** True completion comes through loving others:

> 1 John 4:11-12 Dear friends, since God so loved us, we also ought to love one another. No one has ever seen God; but if we love one another, God lives in us and his love is made complete in us.

The "You complete me" quote isn't about romance—it's about agape, sacrificial love.

**Family and Community.** The Psalmist's answer to loneliness:

> Psalm 68:5-6 A father to the fatherless, a defender of widows, is God in his holy dwelling. God sets the lonely in families.

When loneliness is specifically mentioned, the Lord's remedy is to put people "in families"—not necessarily in sexual relationships, but in belonging, security, and trustworthy relationships.

### Being Single vs. Being Alone

To be unmarried is not to be alone. One writer puts it beautifully:

> "To be single is not to be alone. If someone asks if you are in a relationship right now, your immediate response should be that you are in dozens. Our range of relational options are not limited to getting married. My relationships are not about how much friends should love me, they are about how much I get to love them. No single should ever expect relational impoverishment by virtue of being single. We should covenant to love people—to initiate, to serve, to commit."

You may be unmarried, but you are not single if you're covenanted to love others.

### Cultivating Real Friendship

We live in a hyper-connected yet deeply isolated age. Social media demands little of us. We can screen out those who challenge us. We put in appearances without showing up—physically present but mentally absent, scrolling through our phones. 

Don't sacrifice real conversation for mere connections. You don't need 50 Facebook friends sending generic birthday messages. You need one person who calls and genuinely asks how you are. Commit to your church. Form real friendships. Show up fully.

## 6. Marriage as Divine Partnership

> Genesis 2:25 Adam and his wife were both naked, and they felt no shame.

Nakedness without shame speaks to honesty, vulnerability, and the absence of pretense in the relationship. But it also speaks to purpose.

### Marry Well

Choose someone suited to family life and the mission of God. Ask yourself: Would this person be a good father or mother? Is there someone in your church who quietly serves others without recognition? Who brings food, cleans up, takes initiative? Choose that person. And become that person yourself—a Christian who genuinely seeks to build a family that serves God.

### The Purposes of Marriage

Don't enter marriage lightly or to solve personal problems. Don't use it as a backup plan ("Let's marry if we're single at 30"). If someone is good enough to marry, pursue it intentionally. Marriage is the most serious commitment you can make because it's a covenant to participate in God's work of filling and subduing the earth, raising generations of disciples.

If you don't want children, then honestly, you don't want sex, and you don't want to marry. And that's perfectly acceptable. In the New Covenant, Jesus blessed singleness. The line of promise is fulfilled in Christ; we're no longer under the curse of childlessness. Jesus Himself was unmarried, and Paul affirmed that remaining single for the kingdom's sake is good.

But if you marry, you're embarking on a grand project—not primarily for companionship or romantic completion, but to build a family that will serve God's purposes for generations to come.

---

## 7. The Solution to Loneliness: The Family

When we embrace God's design for marriage and family—exclusive, permanent, gendered, and child-centered—we address the loneliness epidemic at its root. The solution is not sex or romantic completion. The solution is belonging to something bigger than ourselves: a family, a community, the body of Christ.

**For those seeking marriage:** Choose wisely. Choose someone for the mission, not the feeling. Prepare yourself to be a godly father or mother.

**For those single:** Don't despair. You're not alone if you're covenanted to love your church family, your friends, your community. Your relational life need not be impoverished.

**For all of us:** Put children back on the agenda. Strengthen marriages. Form real friendships. Commit to community. Show up fully. Serve one another sacrificially.

This is how we change the world—not through individual self-fulfillment, but through families and communities devoted to God's purposes, raising generations of people who will carry forward His kingdom work for a hundred years beyond our lifetimes.
