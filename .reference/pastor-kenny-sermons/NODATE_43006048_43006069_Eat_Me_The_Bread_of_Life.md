# John 6:48-69

**Title:** Eat Me! The Bread of Life

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Bread: Source of Life

Bread is beautifully simple—just flour, water, yeast, and salt—yet it sustains billions of people worldwide. In biblical times, bread was so essential to life that it became synonymous with food itself. The one who provides bread for the family is called the "breadwinner" because bread quite literally gives life.

This is the foundation for understanding what Jesus teaches in John 6, where he had just miraculously fed five thousand people with only two loaves and five fishes. That miraculous sign points to something deeper. Jesus begins to explain its meaning, and we pick up his teaching midway through his discourse.

## I Am the Bread of Life

> "I am the bread of life." (John 6:48)

Jesus makes a stunning claim about his identity. But what does he mean? Is Jesus literally made of flour? No—this is metaphorical language pointing to something profound. To understand the metaphor, we look to the next verse.

## Bread That Sustains: The Manna Connection

> "Your forefathers ate the manna in the desert, yet they died. But here is the bread that comes down from heaven, which a man may eat and not die." (John 6:49-50)

Jesus reaches back to an event around 1462 BC during the Exodus. As Moses led the Israelites through the barren desert, God miraculously provided food called manna—flakes of bread-like sustenance, white like coriander seed, tasting like wafers made with honey. It appeared fresh each morning like dew on the ground.

Jesus invites his listeners to make a comparison: just as manna nourished the Israelites in that dry, empty wilderness, he now offers himself as the bread that sustains spiritual life. Both gifts come from God. Both provide what is needed for the journey. But there is a crucial difference.

## The Living Bread: A Bread That Defeats Death

> "I am the living bread that came down from heaven. If anyone eats of this bread, he will live forever. This bread is my flesh, which I will give for the life of the world." (John 6:51)

Here Jesus shifts from comparing himself to manna to something far more radical: he *is* the bread itself. More than that, he equates this bread with his own flesh—his body—which he will give for the life of the world.

The critical distinction is this: the Israelites ate manna and still died. Ordinary food can only sustain earthly life for a time. But Jesus offers something entirely different—bread that provides eternal life, bread that conquers death itself. He doesn't merely feed us and leave us to face mortality like everyone else. He gives himself—his very body—as a sacrifice for the life of the world.

## You Must Eat Me

> "I tell you the truth, unless you eat the flesh of the Son of Man and drink his blood, you have no life in you. Whoever eats my flesh and drinks my blood has eternal life, and I will raise him up at the last day. For my flesh is real food and my blood is real drink. Whoever eats my flesh and drinks my blood remains in me, and I in him." (John 6:53-56)

This language shocked his listeners—and it can shock us too. But Jesus isn't calling for cannibalism. In fact, literal consumption would be impossible: Jesus is in heaven now, and even if we could somehow eat his flesh physically, that would belong to the end times. 

So what does Jesus mean by "eat me"? The answer comes a few verses later:

> "The Spirit gives life; the flesh counts for nothing. The words I have spoken to you are spirit and they are life." (John 6:63)

This is the key to understanding the metaphor. Flesh counts for nothing; the Spirit gives life. And what conveys that Spirit and life? *Words*—Jesus's words. As Peter would later say to Jesus, "You have the words of eternal life" (John 6:68).

Think of it this way: a biography is words describing a person. If I spoke to you about myself, you would come to know me through my words—even if you'd never met me in person. This is how we encounter Jesus today. We haven't seen him with our eyes, but his words remain with us in Scripture.

When Jesus calls us to "eat" his flesh, he's calling us to consume his words. To make his teaching part of our very being. To digest Scripture so deeply that it becomes our spiritual DNA. Just as eating physical food makes it part of your body, believing Jesus's words—trusting them, living by them—makes Jesus himself part of you.

## A Call to Spiritual Nourishment

The application is urgent: Are you spending your life chasing food that doesn't last? Chasing the pizza at school, the experiences and material things that leave you hungry again within hours? A lifetime of such chasing leads only to more hunger.

But here is Jesus, offering bread that truly satisfies—his words and his truth. He invites you to taste and see that he is good. If you want to respond to that invitation, to truly eat the bread of eternal life, believe his words. Let Scripture become the foundation of how you think and live. Let the words of Jesus pump through your veins like blood, governing your neurons and synapses. Let your entire thinking be shaped by what Jesus teaches.

This is what it means to eat Jesus: to make his words the very essence of who you are.
