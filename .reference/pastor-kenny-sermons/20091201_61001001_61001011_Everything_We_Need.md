# 2 Peter 1:1-11

**Title:** Everything We Need
**Date:** 27 December 2009
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Overview

The central message of 2 Peter is profoundly simple yet transformative: **we have everything we need for life and godliness**. This is not a promise of abundance in possessions or circumstances, but rather an affirmation of spiritual sufficiency through the gospel of Jesus Christ.

## 1. Everything We Need (1:1-4)

### The Gospel as Complete Provision

> His divine power has given us everything we need for life and godliness through our knowledge of him who called us by his own glory and goodness. (1:3)

Peter uses elegant parallelism to show what we've been given:

- **Verse 2:** Grace and peace in abundance through the knowledge of God and Jesus our Lord
- **Verse 3:** Everything we need for life and godliness through our knowledge of him
- **Verse 4:** His very great and precious promises so that we may participate in the divine nature

The answer to "what have we been given?" is the **gospel itself**—the good news of salvation. Through Christ's death, we have received grace and mercy. Through his resurrection, we have peace with God. Through his promise, we have assurance of rescue from coming judgment. On the cross, Jesus took our place; we should have been there, but he exchanged places with us.

### Participation in the Divine Nature

When Peter speaks of participating in the divine nature (1:4), he means **becoming godly**—reflecting God's character and holiness, not acquiring supernatural power or a "God gene." It is not about the force within us or pantheism, but about increasingly embodying the nature of God's love, care, and character through the Holy Spirit's work.

### Application: Resisting Cultural Lies

In our materialistic, hedonistic world, this truth is countercultural. Every advertisement, every magazine, every billboard whispers that we lack something essential. We're told to buy more, experience more, accumulate more. During the Christmas season especially, retailers deliberately cultivate the belief that we cannot possibly have enough.

Yet Peter's radical claim stands: **the gospel is all we need for life.**

Of course, we should receive God's material blessings with gratitude. We live in a privileged country with genuine abundance. But we must distinguish between what we have and what we actually need. Many things we take for granted—the latest iPhone, the newest game, a boat—are not truly necessary. Even across the global church, believers in countries with scarce food, inadequate shelter, and insecurity still thrive in Christ.

As missionary Jim Elliott said, *"He is no fool to give up what he does not have to gain what he cannot lose."*

## 2. Through Everything We Need to Know (1:3)

### Knowledge as the Gateway

> Grace and peace be yours in abundance through the knowledge of God and of Jesus our Lord. (1:2)

Throughout this epistle, Peter emphasizes knowledge as fundamental to Christian life. The gospel comes to us through knowledge—through Scripture, teaching, study, and the testimony of those who know Jesus.

This is crucial: **God has not left us in darkness.** The gospel is not hidden in mystical visions or private revelations. God takes the initiative to reveal himself through his Word and through his people. As Jesus said, "If you know me, you know the Father."

### The Sufficiency of Scripture

That we know God through knowledge means several vital things for the church:

**Christianity is not anti-intellectual.** We are called to learn, to study, to know. It is entirely proper that churches have Bible studies, sermons, lectures, and Sunday schools. It is proper that church leaders are teachers. Our minds are not obstacles to faith but tools through which faith develops.

Yet this is increasingly under pressure. Many churches are tempted to dilute serious biblical teaching in pursuit of relevance or entertainment. We must ask: In our gatherings, how much time do we dedicate to genuine knowledge of God versus everything else? In reaching toward the contemporary and exciting, have we forgotten how people actually come to know God?

Some object that "God is bigger than knowledge and bigger than books," therefore constant study is somehow improper. They claim God reveals himself through dreams and other means, rendering knowledge secondary. But this attacks the sufficiency of Scripture itself.

### Living Out Knowledge During Seasons of Rest

During holidays and breaks, there's a temptation to let our biblical engines go cold, to park our minds in the garage. Don't do this.

Instead, use your leisure season to deepen knowledge:

- Read the Bible with your children—at breakfast, lunch, before sleep
- Reinterpret holidays not as mere escape but as **Christmas**—time to know Jesus better
- Work through sermons or passages you missed during the busier months
- Read challenging theological books you've been postponing
- Attend biblical conferences and summer schools
- Wrestle through theological questions you've wanted to tackle all year
- Renew or establish consistent Bible reading habits
- Attend your church's study gatherings

Keep your mind sharp, warm, and engaged. Your spiritual growth depends on it.

## 3. Therefore, We Have Everything We Need to Do (1:5-11)

### The Purpose: Life and Godliness

Knowledge is never merely intellectual. The gospel we know is meant to produce something: **life and godliness** (1:3). Godliness—being like God in character and holiness—is not reserved for the educated or privileged. Every Christian, regardless of education or circumstances, has the full knowledge needed to be godly.

### The Call to Diligent Growth

> For this very reason, make every effort to add to your faith goodness; and to goodness, knowledge; and to knowledge, self-control; and to self-control, perseverance; and to perseverance, godliness; and to godliness, brotherly kindness; and to brotherly kindness, love. (1:5-7)

Peter presents a list of qualities to cultivate—not in rigid sequence but with emphasis on constant addition and effort. The repeated command is to **make every effort** (1:5) and be **all the more eager** (1:10).

The purpose is clear: these qualities in increasing measure will keep us from being ineffective and unproductive in our knowledge of Jesus Christ (1:8). The problem is not that we lack knowledge of the gospel—we have it fully. The problem is that we fail to *use* it. We know the truth but don't let it change how we live.

### A Particular Focus: Brotherly Kindness

The list culminates in **love**, but brotherly kindness deserves special attention, particularly during seasons of disruption.

During holidays and breaks, it's easy for church community to fragment. Birthday parties, farewells, celebrations, outings with school friends, movies, and public holidays proliferate. One event means nothing, but they accumulate silently until suddenly you're absent half the summer—not intentionally, but by a thousand small yes's to less important things.

**Be intentional about your church community.** Don't use holidays as an excuse to become invisible. Look out for your weaker and lonelier brothers and sisters. Make attendance and punctuality a priority—not from religious obligation but from genuine care for others.

Many of us have never thought of church attendance this way: not as something we consume but as something we *give*. When you're absent, you're a stranger to newcomers; you have no opportunity to follow up with those seeking help; you cannot welcome others. When you're irregular, you forfeit your ability to be godly and loving in that community.

If you don't attend regularly now, make it a new commitment: **attend faithfully.** This is how you learn continuously and add to your knowledge.

### The Choice Before Us

The tension is real. We all have competing agendas:

**Our List:**
- Buy a TV
- Watch these movies
- Landscape my backyard
- Finish this computer game
- Get my license
- Get a part-time job
- Go on a holiday
- Watch Avatar

**God's List:**
- Add goodness
- Add knowledge
- Add self-control
- Add perseverance
- Add godliness
- Add brotherly kindness
- Add love

Many of us don't truly believe we have everything we need for godliness. We think:

- "I'd be godly if only I had more money"
- "If only I had more security"
- "If only I had more time"
- "If only my parents were different"
- "If only my church were better"
- "If only my friends were more supportive"

We're always waiting for that missing ingredient. But Peter calls us to something different: **a little less complaining, a little more resourcefulness.**

Like MacGyver with his pocket knife and duct tape, we may be stuck in difficult circumstances—materially limited, relationally complicated, time-constrained. Yet God has given us everything we need. We can be godly because we have knowledge of the gospel. Let's stop waiting for better conditions and start living with the wonderful provision already in our hands.

## Closing

It is the prayer of this teacher that as you enter a new season, these priorities would remain clear: you have everything you need. The gospel is sufficient. Knowledge of Christ is available. The power to be godly is already yours.

Use the time ahead not to escape into endless consumption, but to press deeper into what truly matters—knowing Jesus and becoming like him.
