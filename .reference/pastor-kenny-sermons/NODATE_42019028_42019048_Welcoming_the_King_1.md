# Luke 19:28-48

**Title:** Welcoming the King
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Best Welcome Ever

When we think about being welcomed, we imagine the finest experiences: a red carpet reception, a surprise party with all our friends, or loved ones greeting us at the airport after a long journey. Some of you have experienced the warmth of welcome here at Connect. There's something deeply human about feeling genuinely expected and valued when we arrive somewhere.

I remember once visiting a fancy hotel—not to stay, but simply to use the facilities. Yet even that brief visit made an impression. The marble sinks, the gold trimmings on the taps, the luxurious towels—everything communicated that I mattered. Someone was there simply to ensure I felt welcomed and important.

Today, we encounter the account of Jesus' arrival in Jerusalem—the most significant welcome of all.

## 1. The Expected King (Luke 19:28-35)

Throughout Luke's gospel, chapters 9-19 follow a journey theme that culminates here. Jesus arrives at the Mount of Olives, which overlooks Jerusalem, and something unusual happens.

He sends two disciples into a village with specific instructions:

> Go to the village ahead of you, and as you enter it, you will find a colt tied there, which no one has ever ridden. Untie it and bring it here. If anyone asks you, "Why are you untying it?" say, "The Lord needs it." (19:30-31)

This seems bizarre. You don't simply go into a town and take what you want. Imagine if Jesus told you today: "Go to Carlingford Village. Find the red Toyota Camry by the elevators. Drive it to me. If anyone questions you, just say the Lord needs it!"

Yet what Jesus predicted happened exactly. The owners asked, "Why are you untying the colt?"—their way of demanding, "Why are you taking my donkey?" And the disciples simply replied, "The Lord needs it." Remarkably, the owners gave it up without further resistance.

**Why does Luke tell us this seemingly strange incident?** Because Jesus was expected.

Think about how this works in everyday life: arriving at a hotel where they greet you by name, being shown straight to your seat at a restaurant, your favorite coffee shop knowing your order, or the airport attendant saying, "Welcome, Mr. Liew"—they recognize you because they're expecting you. In the same way, the village owner had set aside that colt, anticipating Jesus' arrival.

But the colt itself carries deeper significance. This isn't merely convenient transportation. The prophet Zechariah, writing 500 years before Jesus, prophesied:

> Rejoice greatly, Daughter Zion!
> Shout, Daughter Jerusalem!
> See, your king comes to you,
> righteous and victorious,
> lowly and riding on a donkey,
> on a colt, the foal of a donkey. (Zechariah 9:9)

We might think a donkey is a strange choice for a king—certainly not like we'd expect. But with Hebrew eyes, the donkey becomes clear: it's the animal of Israel's terrain, suitable for rocky, mountainous land. More significantly, the donkey throughout Israel's history was the elite's choice of transport and a sign of wealth, power, and rulership. In Judges 10:4 and 12:14, judges who rode donkeys ruled over multiple towns—the donkey was their symbol of authority, more like a Mercedes or BMW than a lowly beast of burden.

The context makes this even richer. In Zechariah 9:10, we read of a king bringing peace—not war horses and chariots, but peace:

> He will proclaim peace to the nations.
> His rule will extend from sea to sea
> and from the River to the ends of the earth.

**See the donkey, see the king.** The owners expected Him. The prophecy expected Him. Jesus is the Expected King.

## 2. The Expected Reception (Luke 19:36-38)

As Jesus approached Jerusalem, the people spread their cloaks on the road—a powerful symbolic act declaring Him king. Just as the disciples had laid their robes on the donkey as a saddle, now the crowd laid theirs on the ground before Him.

This wasn't new. When King Jehu was anointed centuries earlier, his followers did the same thing (2 Kings 9:12-13):

> They quickly took their cloaks and spread them under him on the bare steps. Then they blew the trumpet and shouted, "Jehu is king!"

It was the ancient Near Eastern version of a red carpet—a robe carpet honoring their king.

Then, the whole crowd of disciples began joyfully praising God in loud voices:

> Blessed is the king who comes in the name of the Lord!
> Peace in heaven and glory in the highest! (19:37-38)

**The King had arrived.** Donkey. Robes. Praise. This was the long-awaited reception, and they welcomed Him rightly.

This prompts us to reflect: **What is our reception of Jesus?** Do we welcome Him with open arms, submitting ourselves to His kingship and celebrating His arrival?

### Jesus Arrives Through His Word

Friends, Jesus comes to us through His word. The gospels are reliable records of real events, relived and renewed every time we read them. We celebrated His first coming last month at Christmas. The fact that God comes to us through documents is perfectly fitting—that's simply how history works.

Reality and truth extend far beyond our few years of existence. All civilization rests on passed-down history: genealogy, laws, constitutions, language, culture, values. We cannot live without receiving what previous generations have transmitted to us. History is the reality that life continues beyond our individual years.

**And reality is this: Jesus has come.**

If this is true, then we must receive His word with open minds and open arms. Throughout Luke's gospel, He speaks words of life to us:

- *Your sins are forgiven* (Luke 5)
- *Your reward is great* (Luke 6)
- *Your faith has made you well* (Luke 8)
- *Eat your fill* (Luke 9)
- *Rejoice, for yours is the kingdom* (Luke 10)
- *Here is the crown of life* (Revelation 2:10)
- *Well done, good and faithful servant* (Matthew 25:21)

This means we can be right with God. Peace can exist between Him and us.

This year, I encourage you to make a fresh commitment to Jesus and His word. Engage in personal Bible reading. Be part of a church community. If you're at university or TAFE, get involved in campus groups. This is a profound privilege in Australia where we have the gospel freely available in our own language.

But here's the critical point: our knowledge of Jesus and the Bible must keep pace with everything else we're learning. At university, you'll encounter sophisticated essays, brilliant analysis, world-class professors who are experts in their fields. What a tragedy if your knowledge of Christianity remains at Sunday school level—unable to engage thoughtfully with the high-level challenges your peers and professors will raise.

For 2,000 years, Christianity has been the most scrutinized belief system in the world, beginning with the Pharisees in the first century. Thoughtful answers exist to the difficult questions you'll face. But we must study. We must equip ourselves. We must welcome His word into our minds and hearts.

**Do you welcome His word?**

## 3. The Unexpected Reception (Luke 19:39, 47-48)

But here's where expectations shatter. Some receive Jesus differently.

> Some of the Pharisees in the crowd said to Jesus, "Teacher, rebuke your disciples!" (19:39)

The religious leaders didn't want Jesus paraded before the crowds. They wanted Him silenced. This animosity intensifies:

> Every day he was teaching at the temple. But the chief priests, the teachers of the law and the leaders among the people were trying to kill him. Yet they could not find any way to do it, because all the people hung on his words. (19:47-48)

This is shocking. The very people trained in Scripture, familiar with Zechariah 9 and Psalm 118, the ones who should have recognized their King—they wanted to destroy Him instead.

## 4. The Unexpected King (Luke 19:40-46)

When challenged to silence His disciples, Jesus responds:

> I tell you, if they keep quiet, the stones will cry out. (19:40)

This is extraordinary. Even if His followers were silent, the very stones would proclaim His kingship. He may be alluding to His earlier teaching: *Out of these stones God can raise up children for Abraham* (Luke 3:8). The point is clear: **Jesus' kingship is so obvious that even lifeless creation will acknowledge it.** He is King over people and King over creation, commanding waves, wind, and stones themselves.

Yet the religious leaders—living, breathing people—refuse to recognize Him. Dead stones will praise God; living people will not. Their refusal condemns them.

Jesus' response is grief:

> As he approached Jerusalem and saw the city, he wept over it and said, "If you, even you, had only known on this day what would bring you peace—but now it is hidden from your eyes. The days will come upon you when your enemies will build an embankment against you and encircle you and hem you in on every side. They will dash you to the ground, you and the children within your walls. They will not leave one stone on another, because you did not recognize the time of God's coming to you." (19:41-44)

Because they rejected Him, disaster would follow. Jesus came as their King, but they received Him as a foreign conqueror. The rude rejection of the religious leaders would result in siege, destruction, and death—not one stone left upon another.

**"You did not recognize the time of God's coming."**

### The Question for Us

For thousands of years, the ancient Jews with knowledge of Zechariah and the prophets said: "If only God would appear and tell me directly what He wants, then I will believe. If only I could see His face, touch Him just once, then I would follow."

He came. They ignored Him.

And we do the same today. Why doesn't God visit me personally? Then I'll know He exists and will believe.

But the answer is: **He did.** Two thousand years ago.

Some object: "God should appear individually to every human being. That would make belief reasonable." But that's unreasonable. God is not our personal servant who appears at everyone's convenience. And even if He did—consider what happened last time Jesus appeared. The majority wanted Him dead.

There may be some here still unconvinced. You're thinking: "God, show Yourself to me. Make me believe. Show me Your face if You're real."

Friends, **that is playing with fire.**

He has appeared. When He returns, it will be too late for those who have not repented. He will come and make you believe—but you'll be believing His anger and wrath. He will show His face, and it will be terrifying.

Don't test God. Don't delay. The word of God in English, in the language you read and write, is God speaking directly to you: **Recognize the time of your God's coming.**

## Welcoming the King

But what if you would welcome Jesus? What a different reception you will receive on His return!

Will you welcome Him?

Reception into His palace. He will welcome you.

**See the donkey. See the King.**

Jesus is the Expected King—both then and now. The question is: Will you recognize Him? Will you welcome Him? Will you respond to His coming with open arms and a joyful heart?
