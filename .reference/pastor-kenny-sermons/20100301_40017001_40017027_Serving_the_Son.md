## The Serving Son

### The Glorious Son (16:27—17:8)

> After six days Jesus took with him Peter, James and John the brother of James, and led them up a high mountain by themselves. There he was transfigured before them. His face shone like the sun, and his clothes became as white as the light. Just then there appeared before them Moses and Elijah, talking with Jesus. (Matthew 17:1-3)

The Transfiguration is a stunning revelation of Jesus's glory, yet it echoes an earlier encounter in Scripture. This passage parallels Exodus 24:9-18, where Moses ascended Mount Sinai and encountered God's glory. Both accounts share remarkable similarities:

- The "six days" mentioned in both passages (Matthew 17:1; Exodus 24:15-16)
- Three companions present
- A mountain as the setting
- The glory of God appearing in a cloud
- A voice from heaven
- Fear and reverence from those who witness

Yet Matthew shows us something profoundly different. This is not merely a theophany—a manifestation of God's power. This is the revelation of God's *Son*. The Father's voice declares: "This is my Son, whom I love; with him I am well pleased. Listen to him!" (17:5)

Where Moses entered a cloud and spent forty days and nights receiving the law, Jesus stands before them—God in the flesh, the living Word. There is no need to build a tabernacle; Jesus *is* the dwelling place of God among humanity. And where Moses brought tablets of stone, Jesus speaks directly to his disciples. He is the new Moses, calling them not to obedience of written commands, but to obedience of his person.

Yet this glorious revelation stands in stark contradiction to what Jesus himself has declared about his mission.

### The Suffering Servant (17:9-13; cf. 16:21-28)

As they descended the mountain, Jesus instructed his disciples: "Don't tell anyone what you have seen, until the Son of Man has been raised from the dead" (17:9). The disciples immediately questioned him about Elijah's role in the messianic age (17:10). Jesus clarified that Elijah had already come in the person of John the Baptist—and they had rejected him. Then came the sobering word:

> In the same way the Son of Man is going to suffer at their hands. (17:12)

This echoes what Jesus had taught them just days before:

> From that time on Jesus began to explain to his disciples that he must go to Jerusalem and suffer many things at the hands of the elders, chief priests and teachers of the law, and that he must be killed and on the third day be raised to life. (16:21)

The glorious Son, radiant with divine light in the presence of the Father's voice, did not remain on that mountain. He came down and walked toward Jerusalem—toward suffering, rejection, and death.

Jesus calls his disciples to the same path: "If anyone would come after me, he must deny himself and take up his cross and follow me" (16:24). The essence of discipleship is not power or status. It is *service*.

## Serve the Son

### Be Little (18:1-4)

The disciples came to Jesus with a question that reveals their deepest preoccupation: "Who is the greatest in the kingdom of heaven?" (18:1)

Jesus's answer was both surprising and instructive. He called a little child and placed the child among them, saying:

> Unless you change and become like little children, you will never enter the kingdom of heaven. Therefore, whoever humbles himself like this child is the greatest in the kingdom of heaven. (18:2-4)

Why the child? Because children embody three essential qualities for discipleship:

**They listen.** When called, a child comes to Jesus without hesitation or agenda. They respond to his voice.

**They believe.** Unlike adults burdened with doubt and calculation, children trust simply. They have faith.

**They are humble.** In first-century culture, childhood was not romanticized as it is today. Children were small, weak, and assigned the lowliest tasks—the menial work beneath the dignity of adults. They held no power and harbored no ambition for status.

The disciples, by contrast, were consumed with questions of rank and honor. Jesus calls them to relinquish this entirely and become like little children—lowly, trusting, attentive.

### Welcome the Little Ones (18:5-14)

In their humble state, the disciples are called to do something equally important:

> Whoever welcomes a little child like this in my name welcomes me. (18:5)

But there is a solemn warning attached:

> If anyone causes one of these little ones who believe in me to sin, it would be better for him to have a large millstone hung around his neck and to be drowned in the depths of the sea. (18:6)

The word translated "causes to sin" carries the sense of being a stumbling block—the same term Jesus uses in 16:23 when Peter rebukes him ("You are a stumbling block to me"). It means to place an obstacle in someone's path, to prevent them from entering the kingdom of God.

This theme reverberates throughout Matthew 17-18. In verse 27, Jesus speaks of not wanting to "offend" people—literally, not wanting to place a stumbling block before them. Throughout chapter 18, the emphasis falls on not hindering the "little ones"—not blocking them from the kingdom.

The principle is clear: **To serve the Son is to welcome the little ones. To welcome them is to refrain from being a stumbling block. To be little yourself is the prerequisite for serving others without pride or self-interest.**

#### Service: Not Blocking the Little Ones (17:14-23)

When Jesus and his disciples came down from the mountain, they encountered a man in desperate need. His son suffered from severe seizures and was in danger of falling into fire or water (17:15). The father had brought his son to the disciples, but they could not heal him.

Jesus rebuked the demon, and the boy was healed immediately. When the disciples asked privately why they had failed, Jesus replied: "Because you have so little faith" (17:20).

Notice what happened. The disciples lacked not magical ability, but *faith*—trust in God's power and mission. They were unable to serve the suffering child because their hearts were divided. They may have sought the power and prestige of healing, rather than being moved by compassion for the suffering boy. In their failure, they became a stumbling block—the father's son went without healing, blocked from restoration by disciples who were focused on themselves rather than on him.

This carries a sobering lesson for those of us who lead or serve in any capacity. When our motivation becomes self-interest, status, or the affirmation of others, we inevitably become a stumbling block to those we claim to serve. We prioritize our needs over theirs. We withhold our presence, our time, our genuine care—and in doing so, we prevent them from experiencing God's love.

#### Service Even Though the Sons Are Exempt (17:24-27)

The final example is subtle but profound. The temple tax collectors came to Peter and asked whether Jesus paid the two-drachma tax. Peter assured them that he did.

When Peter entered the house, Jesus addressed him with a penetrating question: "From whom do the kings of the earth collect duty and taxes—from their own sons or from others?" Peter answered, "From others." Jesus replied, "Then the sons are exempt" (17:25-26).

Jesus was asserting an important truth: as the Son of God, he was exempt from the temple tax—a tax paid to support his Father's house. He owed nothing.

Yet what did he do?

> Go to the lake and throw out your line. Take the first fish you catch; open its mouth and you will find a four-drachma coin. Take it and give it to them for my tax and yours. (17:27)

The Son paid a tax he did not owe. He served others—even at the cost of setting aside his own rights. He lowered himself, not because the law required it, but because love demanded it. His service was not motivated by obligation, but by a desire not to place a stumbling block before those who did not yet understand his identity.

This is the heart of kingdom servanthood: **to relinquish rights, to humble ourselves, to prioritize the comfort and spiritual growth of others over our own status or preferences.**

#### Service to the Lost (18:12-14)

> What do you think? If a man owns a hundred sheep, and one of them wanders away, will he not leave the ninety-nine on the hills and go to look for the one that wandered off? And if he finds it, I tell you the truth, he is happier about that one sheep than about the ninety-nine that did not wander off. In the same way your Father in heaven is not willing that any of these little ones should be lost. (18:12-14)

The shepherd leaves the ninety-nine to search for the one who is lost. This is the posture of kingdom service: a willingness to inconvenience ourselves, to redirect our attention, to exhaust ourselves in pursuit of those who have wandered. The shepherd does not remain with the comfortable majority. He seeks the vulnerable, the lost, the one who needs him most.

## Transforming into Servants

The question before us is personal and penetrating: **Do you welcome others?**

In many of our churches and fellowship groups, the weakest point is precisely here. The most common complaint from visitors is not theological error or boring sermons, but relational coldness: "No one talked to me" or "It felt too cliquey."

To welcome others requires something of us. It demands our presence, our attention, our willingness to be inconvenienced. Consider the example of a host at a party. The true servant arrives early and stays late, watching for guests who are arriving and making sure everyone is cared for. By contrast, the person who arrives late, mingles superficially, and leaves before cleanup has used the gathering for personal entertainment rather than serving others.

This applies directly to our church life, our Bible studies, our campus ministries. **If you want to serve and welcome others, you must be there**—not just physically present, but early and engaged, willing to stay late and help.

That commitment comes with a cost:

- You may need to catch an earlier ride to church, sacrificing sleep on Saturday nights
- You may miss sitting with your closest friends and preferred group
- You may never again enjoy sitting in your favorite section of the sanctuary
- You may never get to discuss your favorite topics and agendas, because you will be listening to the concerns and needs of others, trying to understand what makes them tick
- You may sacrifice being the life of the party, instead slowly moving through the room to find the quiet ones who don't quite fit in, speaking gently with them and praying with them in a corner

**But that's okay**—and it will be more than okay—*only if* you are the disciple Jesus calls you to be. Only if your service flows from love, not self-interest. Only if your service mirrors the Lord's own service.

The glorious Son morphed into humility. He showed us what true glory is: the laying down of power for the sake of others. He denied himself, carried his cross, and calls us to do the same.

The question remains: **Are you willing to be little for others?**
