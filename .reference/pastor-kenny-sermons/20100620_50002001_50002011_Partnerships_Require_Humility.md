# Philippians 2:1-11

**Title:** Partnerships Require Humility

**Date:** June 20, 2010

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## What Is Humility?

We often hear about humility as something we should pursue, yet it remains elusive. Consider the irony: if you claim to be humble, are you truly humble? If others perceive you as humble, are you performing rather than living it?

The confusion runs deeper. We might think humility means denying compliments, putting ourselves down, or deliberately underperforming so others can shine. Some wonder if it's possible to be both highly competent and genuinely humble. These questions reveal our misunderstanding of what the Bible actually commands.

**The crucial insight:** Humility is not about how you view yourself. Rather, humility is centered on how you view and treat other people. This distinction transforms everything.

---

## Humility in Partnership (Philippians 2:1-4)

Paul calls the Philippian church to think a certain way—to maintain this mindset consistently:

> If you have any encouragement from being united with Christ, if any comfort from his love, if any fellowship with the Spirit, if any tenderness and compassion, then make my joy complete by being like-minded, having the same love, being one in spirit and purpose. (Philippians 2:1-2)

He then makes the practical demand clear:

> Do nothing out of selfish ambition or vain conceit, but in humility consider others better than yourselves. Each of you should look not only to your own interests, but also to the interests of others. (Philippians 2:3-4)

### The Negative View: Self-Centeredness Is the Enemy

From the negative perspective, humility means rejecting:
- Selfish ambition
- Vain conceit
- Self-interest pursued at the expense of others

The common thread? **Self-centeredness.** This was precisely the problem Paul addressed in Rome, where some preached Christ "out of selfish ambition, not sincerely, supposing that they can stir up trouble for me while I am in chains" (Philippians 1:17). Though Paul rejoiced that Christ was preached, he made clear that the means—driven by selfish ambition—was unjustified.

The same disease infected Philippi. Paul had to plead with Euodia and Syntyche, two women who had served the gospel alongside him, to resolve their conflict (Philippians 4:2-3). Self-centeredness fractures partnerships.

### The Root of Evil

Why does this matter so urgently? Because self-centeredness is the source of every evil practice.

James makes this devastating connection:

> But if you harbor bitter envy and selfish ambition in your hearts, do not boast about it or deny the truth. Such "wisdom" does not come down from heaven but is earthly, unspiritual, of the devil. For where you have envy and selfish ambition, there you find disorder and every evil practice. (James 3:14-16)

He then traces the downstream effects:

> What causes fights and quarrels among you? Don't they come from your desires that battle within you? You want something but don't get it. You kill and covet, but you cannot have what you want. You quarrel and fight. You do not have, because you do not ask God. When you ask, you do not receive, because you ask with wrong motives, that you may spend what you get on your pleasures. (James 4:1-3)

### The Adam Pattern

The poem embedded in Philippians 2—often called the Christ-hymn—hints at Adam's failure to illuminate Christ's success. Both passages concern grasping:

> Who, being in very nature God, did not consider equality with God something to be grasped, but made himself nothing, taking the very nature of a servant, being made in human likeness. (Philippians 2:6-7)

Adam, created in God's image and given dominion over creation, faced a choice. God commanded him not to eat from the tree of knowledge. Yet the serpent whispered:

> You will not surely die. For God knows that when you eat of it your eyes will be opened, and you will be like God, knowing good and evil. (Genesis 3:4-5)

Adam grasped. He and Eve ate. From that moment, humans decided their own versions of good and evil, caring only for themselves. One act of selfish grasping introduced sin into the world.

### The Cascade of Self-Centeredness

When we abandon humility and embrace self-centeredness, a predictable pattern emerges:

**First,** we care only about what we want—our goals, our desires, our needs. Imagine a household where everyone pursues only their self-interest:
- Mothers stop cleaning because it brings no recognition
- Fathers abandon their families or neglect them for career advancement
- Young adults living at home expect to be served while investing nothing in family life
- Everyone abandons shared responsibilities the moment personal priorities (like exams) demand attention

Such a household couldn't function. Society itself would collapse if everyone acted this way.

**Second,** having isolated ourselves through self-centeredness, we become envious. Looking around at others, all we see are people undeservedly doing better than us. A student resents the peer who scored higher. A parent covets the neighbor's new television and their children's academic success. A young professional can't believe a rival was accepted into a competitive program. All of it stems from envy and pride.

**Third,** envy breeds entitlement and cruelty. We take pleasure in others' failures. We justify their misfortunes. We subtly rejoice when the "too good" person falls. This is the spiral James describes: where envy and selfish ambition flourish, "there you find disorder and every evil practice."

### The Positive View: Humility Is Service

If self-centeredness produces every evil, then its opposite—humility—is the root of every virtue.

From the positive perspective, humility means:
- Considering others better than yourself
- Looking to the interests of others
- The removal of self-centered pride

Here lies the paradox and the power: **we remove self-centered pride not by concentrating on ourselves, but by concentrating on others.** Like faith, humility points away from self—toward God and neighbor.

This transforms how we pursue humility. We don't achieve it by asking, "Am I feeling humble? Do I look humble? How can I appear humble?" That's performance, not virtue. Instead, we become humble when we serve. When we look to the interests of others, humility follows naturally.

James captures this beautifully:

> The brother in humble circumstances ought to take pride in his high position. But the one who is rich should take pride in his low [humble] position, because he will pass away like a wild flower. (James 1:9-10)

This seems paradoxical—take pride in humility? But James means this: a poor person can find dignity in knowing they serve God. A rich person finds true greatness not in wealth and status, but in using their resources to serve those in need. The rich person's humility comes from recognizing their ability to serve the helpless.

It's hard to serve when you believe you deserve to be served. Humility flourishes when we recognize that function and position exist for a purpose: to serve others.

## The Humility of Jesus (Philippians 2:5-11)

> Your attitude should be the same as that of Christ Jesus. (Philippians 2:5)

Paul then offers the supreme example—not merely a moral ideal, but the very nature of God displayed in human flesh.

### Jesus Refused to Grasp

> Who, being in very nature God, did not consider equality with God something to be grasped, but made himself nothing, taking the very nature of a servant, being made in human likeness. And being found in appearance as a man, he humbled himself and became obedient to death—even death on a cross! (Philippians 2:6-8)

Jesus possessed what Adam grasped for but did not have: actual equality with God. Yet he refused to treat this equality as something to cling to. Instead, he emptied himself, taking on the form of a servant.

This echoes the suffering servant of Isaiah:

> By oppression and judgment he was taken away. For he was cut off from the land of the living; for the transgression of my people he was stricken. (Isaiah 53:8)

Jesus humbled himself, became obedient—not as a reluctant victim, but as the one who freely chose to give his life. Even death. A criminal's death. The cross.

Paul captures the scandal elsewhere: "For you know the grace of our Lord Jesus Christ, that though he was rich, yet for your sakes he became poor, so that you through his poverty might become rich" (2 Corinthians 8:9).

### Humility Is Not Mere Feeling

Here we must be clear: **humility is not a feeling about yourself.** It's not that inner emotion of being small before God's majesty—though that experience has its place. Humility is not piety. It's not thinking lowly of yourself or performing meekness.

Humility is action. It is being a servant.

When we understand this, we stop trying to manufacture the feeling. We stop asking whether we feel humble enough or look humble enough. Instead, we ask: **What needs to be done? How can I serve?** In serving others, we naturally become humble.

### Humility Does Not Mean Inferiority

An objection often arises: "Doesn't accepting a subordinate role mean accepting inferiority?" No.

Jesus's identity as the triune Son proves this. The Son is eternally divine—"in very nature God." Yet the Son relates to the Father in a permanent structure of obedience and honor. The Father is the source; the Son receives from the Father; the Spirit is sent by both. This hierarchy has existed from eternity and will continue forever.

> At the name of Jesus every knee should bow, in heaven and on earth and under the earth, and every tongue confess that Jesus Christ is Lord, to the glory of God the Father. (Philippians 2:10-11)

Even in exaltation and vindication, Jesus points all glory to the Father. The Son does not trade places with the Father. The Son remains the Son—eternally, perfectly, gloriously.

Some struggle with this: "How can the three persons of the Trinity be equal if there's a hierarchy?" The answer is simple: hierarchy does not imply inferiority.

Consider a parent and adult child. The child owes the parent permanent honor and respect in a way the parent will never reciprocate. The relationship remains asymmetrical for life. The form of obedience changes as the child matures, but the obligation endures. Are parent and child unequal as human beings? Nonsense. We can have hierarchy without inferiority.

Our world confuses these categories. We think that a higher position makes you a better person. That a prestigious title, a higher salary, an expensive car, or selective school entrance proves your worth. We scramble for positions and rights that move us up the social ladder, imagining that rank determines value.

It's false. Things in life are functional. We occupy positions and roles to serve a purpose. The position is for service, not for personal elevation. Humility means accepting our function—whether high or low—and using it to serve.

## Humility in Practice

### Guard Against False Humility

There's no room for false humility. Too often we hear: "God is so great and I am so lowly—I cannot serve." Or: "I don't know enough to teach. I'm not worthy to serve."

Sometimes this is honest awareness of real inadequacy. But often it's an excuse. If you're ungodly, the solution is to seek advice and address the problem—not to hide behind false modesty. If you lack knowledge, the solution is to learn. If you doubt your gifts, remember that partnerships need all kinds of contributions, not just the prominent ones.

**Real pride** occurs when sinful humans aspire to God's status and refuse to acknowledge their dependence on him. This manifests as an unwillingness to serve: "I'm too good to serve. Let someone else do it." That's pride masquerading as humility.

### Serving in Every Partnership

As we participate in God's amazing plans for the gospel's advance, our partnerships require humility. Consider a winning sports team. The best teams aren't filled with the best individual players trying to outshine each other. They're filled with humble players—teammates willing to pass the ball to someone they know might score instead of them.

England's national football team has been filled with world-class individuals who played poorly together. Why? Because without humility, partnerships crumble. It's hard to pass to someone you think is better than you. It's hard to celebrate their success. It's hard to do the supporting work that makes their brilliance possible.

But with humility, something beautiful emerges. Players pass freely. Everyone positions themselves to give the ball carrier options. When a goal is scored, everyone celebrates—because they know the whole team made it possible.

**In families:** We need humble fathers and mothers and children who set aside selfish ambition to serve each other. Fathers, continue providing and staying present, even if it means declining the promotion that would keep you away from home. Do it out of service. Mothers, continue with the thousand thankless jobs at home, knowing they're done for the good of your family. Young people, don't abandon family responsibilities the moment exams arrive, as if the world revolves around you.

**In schools and workplaces:** You don't have to work only with the best to prove yourself. When you work alongside people from different backgrounds or skill levels, see it as an opportunity to serve and help them grow, not as a threat to your status.

**In ministry and leadership:** This hits me personally. I've studied Scripture extensively and have considerable ministry experience. My temptation is to look down on those with less knowledge when new ideas emerge or plans go wrong—to blame them rather than serve them. When things go well, I'm tempted to think highly of myself. But roles and positions exist to serve, not to justify looking down on others.

Not everyone leads from a formal position, and that doesn't make them inferior. We all have a part to play in the gospel's progress. A leader's humility means believing this deeply and acting on it.

### The Promise of Vindication

Here's the encouragement for those who embrace humble service:

> Therefore God exalted him to the highest place and gave him the name that is above every name, that at the name of Jesus every knee should bow, in heaven and on earth and under the earth, and every tongue confess that Jesus Christ is Lord, to the glory of God the Father. (Philippians 2:9-11)

God lifts up the humble. James writes:

> Humble yourselves before the Lord, and he will lift you up. (James 4:10)

This is not a formula for self-advancement—as if we become humble to get exalted. Rather, it's a promise that God honors those who serve. Our job is to work out our salvation with fear and trembling, knowing that God works in us to will and to do his good pleasure (Philippians 2:12-13).

We don't pursue vindication. We pursue service. And God—who sees all—will vindicate his humble servants in due time.

## Cultivating Humility

### Daily Practices

To weaken pride and cultivate humility, consider these practices:

**Always:**
- Reflect on the wonder of the cross of Christ

**Each day begins:**
- Acknowledge your dependence upon God
- Express gratitude to God
- Practice prayer, study of God's Word, and worship—consistently
- Use your commute to memorize and meditate on Scripture
- Cast your cares upon God, knowing he cares for you

**Each day ends:**
- Transfer all glory to God
- Receive sleep as a gift from God

**For special focus:**
- Study the attributes of God
- Study the doctrines of grace
- Study the doctrine of sin
- Laugh often—especially at yourself

**Throughout your days:**
- Notice evidences of grace in others
- Encourage and serve others daily
- Invite correction and respond humbly to it
- When trials come, respond with humility, not defensiveness

## Conclusion

Humility is not about feeling small or looking meek. It's not a performance of piety. **Humility is the posture of service to one another.**

When Paul calls us to "consider others better than yourselves" and "look to the interests of others," he's not asking for emotional self-abasement. He's calling us to a radical reorientation: stop thinking about yourself and start thinking about what others need. Stop asking how you can look good and start asking how you can serve well.

This is the humility of Jesus—not weakness, but strength poured out in service. Not inferiority, but eternal obedience to the Father. Not self-rejection, but self-forgetfulness in the joy of serving others.

As we face each other in our families, workplaces, churches, and communities, let's adopt that humble posture. Let's look for ways to serve. Let's do even the lowly jobs gladly, not asking what counts as humble work, but asking what needs to be done to make this place better—where people can hear God's Word and be encouraged.

This is how partnerships flourish. This is how the gospel advances. This is true greatness.
