# 1 Peter 5:1-14

**Title:** Pattern of Authorities: Elders  
**Date:** December 16, 2017

## Context: The Pattern of Earthly Authorities

Over the past weeks, we've been examining God's pattern of authorities throughout society. Three weeks ago we looked at government—how we're called to honor those in authority over us. Two weeks ago, we considered employers and masters, who also rightfully exercise authority in certain spheres. Last week, we discussed husbands and fathers, whose authority operates within the family.

What connects all these patterns? They represent different orders in society that God has established—and notice the word is plural. We're not under a single earthly authority, but under various authorities, each with its own responsibilities and domain. In fact, you could say the biblical principle of separation of powers comes directly from Scripture: power and jurisdiction are deliberately distributed so that no single authority can dominate or tyrannize, and the different powers keep each other in check.

This week we come to another divinely ordered institution: the church. It too has structure, hierarchy, and offices—and notably, the language used is about age: older and younger.

## 1. Leaders of the Flock (5:1-4)

> To the elders among you, I appeal as a fellow elder, a witness of Christ's sufferings and one who also will share in the glory to be revealed: Be shepherds of God's flock that is under your care, serving as overseers. (1 Peter 5:1-2)

Scripture uses several terms to describe church leadership, each with rich meaning:

### Shepherds

The shepherd is the leader, guide, protector, and feeder of the flock. In ancient Israel, the shepherd became the primary metaphor for leadership itself. The Prophet Ezekiel confronts shepherds who failed in their calling:

> The shepherds did not search for my flock but cared for themselves rather than for my flock. I am against the shepherds and will hold them accountable for my flock. I will remove them from tending the flock so that the shepherds can no longer feed themselves. (Ezekiel 34:1-10)

Ezekiel's vision promises a new shepherd—God himself will come. Jesus claimed this identity: "I am the good shepherd. The good shepherd lays down his life for the sheep" (John 10:11-12). True shepherds are willing to sacrifice for their flock's protection and care.

We don't use the word "shepherd" for church leaders today, but we use a related term: **Pastor**—from the same root as pasture. A pastor is one who leads the flock to green pastures. While this includes physical welfare, the primary task is theological. The pastor is the teacher. The church is the depository, sustainer, and school of truth and wisdom.

### Overseers

A second term in verse 2 is **overseers**—those who watch over, who provide oversight. From the Greek *episkopos* (from which we get "scope"), we derive the English word "bishop"—one who watches over and takes responsibility.

### Elders

Finally, **elders**—literally, the older people. Church leaders throughout Scripture are typically the older members of the community. Why? Because age typically brings wisdom, experience, and learning. Even those who aren't particularly smart or successful have made more mistakes than the young—and can at least tell you what *not* to do.

Older leadership reflects the natural order of God's design. The wise pass wisdom to the next generation. When they become frail or pass away, the younger take their place in a natural succession. This is how leadership should flow.

> You shall stand up before the gray head and honor the face of an old man, and you shall fear your God. (Leviticus 19:32)

It is a calamity when the young lead the old—we'll address this more fully in the second point.

### Interchangeable Titles

In practice, these terms—elders, overseers, bishops, shepherds, pastors—basically all describe leaders in the church. Different church traditions organize leadership differently:

- **Baptist churches** (which includes most Chinese churches): Pastor, elders, deacons, and the broader membership all share authority. This distributes power but can slow decision-making.
- **Anglican churches**: A structured hierarchy—Bishop, priests (presbyters), deacons—descending in authority. This top-down approach means good leadership creates rapid growth, but poor leadership causes everything to collapse quickly. The Anglican structure also separates power: wardens control finances and buildings, while the parish council manages ministry.
- **Presbyterian churches**: A middle path. No bishops, but a plurality of elders sharing authority.

All these titles are applied somewhat arbitrarily based on whatever structure a church establishes. They're fundamentally interchangeable—they all mean "leaders."

### Three Requirements for Leaders: The "Three W's"

Scripture gives us three essential qualities for church leadership:

#### 1. Willingly

> Not because you must, but because you are willing, as God wants you to be. (1 Peter 5:2)

Leadership must be voluntary. It's not a position to be forced into, demanded by guilt, or accepted against one's informed conscience. True church leadership happens because the leader genuinely wants to serve.

Why? Because forced or guilt-driven leaders eventually burn out. They become bitter, leave the church—often hating it in the end. The church doesn't compel service. God might call someone to serve, but the church itself must respect voluntary commitment.

#### 2. Wantingly (Not Greedy for Money)

> Not greedy for money, but eager to serve. (1 Peter 5:2)

In principle, there should be no "employees" in the church. The minister receives a stipend—a word meaning tax, pay, gift, alms, or small scholarship—to cover living expenses. Historically, the church owned land that was cultivated or rented out to provide the stipend.

Even today, clergy technically aren't employees under law. We fall under special provisions and unique tax rulings (stipends are taxable, unlike most scholarships). The principle behind this distinction is crucial: truth is bigger than money and cannot be influenced by financial considerations. The same applies to academia.

However, reality has shifted. Many churches have sold their capital and land. Now we operate paycheck to paycheck. When someone becomes unemployed, changes churches, or faces illness, the congregation suddenly faces financial crisis.

But greed isn't only about money. The original Greek allows for all forms of greed: greed for recognition, titles, grandiosity, popularity. Instead, leaders must be **eager**—willing and enthusiastic—to serve.

#### 3. Worthily

> Not lording it over those entrusted to you, but being examples to the flock. (1 Peter 5:3)

Leaders must not lord their authority over the congregation. They must not be greedy for power or status. No special titles are necessary—in fact, informal leadership often works best. The essence of church leadership is *character*: leaders must be examples of servanthood and suffering, not crowds seeking applause and titles.

The example is given in 1 Timothy 3:1: "Whoever aspires to be an overseer desires a noble task." Leadership is a task, not a status symbol.

### Application for Formal Leaders

For those with formal leadership responsibilities—whether leading the church, youth group, Sunday school, ISCF groups, committees, or backend teams:

- **Show up on time.** Your flock has been entrusted to you.
- **Be prepared.** Don't wing it.
- **Be trained.** Gain competence in your role.
- **Get your character check done.** It's serious business.
- **Prioritize ruthlessly.** There are many good things to do, but you must discern the best. People are counting on you.

### All of Us Are Elders to Someone

Yet not everyone has a formal leadership title. Here's the crucial truth: all of us (except the very youngest) are elders to someone.

> Teach what accords with sound doctrine. Older men are to be sober-minded, dignified, self-controlled, sound in faith, in love, and in steadfastness. Older women likewise are to be reverent in behavior, not slanderers. They are to teach what is good, and so train the young women... (Titus 2:1-2)

Parents here are informal leaders. Young adults are picking up your examples and your words. Young workers—the university students are watching how you spend your money, what you prioritize. University students—the high schoolers follow your example in studying, volunteering, and playing.

The question becomes: are you an example to the young? Consider:

- Crude joking and rudeness—they're watching.
- The movies you watch, the shows you enjoy—they're watching you, not just the screen.
- How you dress and carry yourself.
- The young are impressionable; they follow.

Some might think, "I'm just a kid. I've just graduated. I want to have fun." But you're not kids anymore. Just as adults once cleaned up after you, now it's time for you to clean up after the younger ones. It's time to grow up and take your place in adulthood.

> Though by this time you ought to be teachers, you need someone to teach you the elementary truths of God's word all over again. You need milk, not solid food! (Hebrews 5:12)

Some of us need to stop "mucking around." Getting married, starting a family, settling into life—these aren't distractions from living; they *are* living. Endless holidays, additional careers, more degrees, more jobs—at some point, it's time to grow up. You're older now. You're an elder. Much more is expected of you. The carefree days should be shortening, not lengthening.

And yes, get your Working With Children Check done. While I personally think it's more government red tape and an infringement on liberty, we submit to it because we're older than someone.

### The Reward

> When the Chief Shepherd appears, you will receive the crown of glory that will never fade away. (1 Peter 5:4)

What is this crown of glory? It's the people you've invested your time in—not just socially, but those you've read Scripture with, kept accountable, encouraged, and cared for with genuine tears. It's the fruit of your labor.

This glory never fades. I went to a prestigious school and proudly wore my jersey, wanting recognition—then lost it on the train. But the spiritual investment you make in people? That inheritance never fades.

Here's the practical reality: if you lead well, you inherit a God-fearing, parent-honoring son or daughter. If you lead poorly, you'll be stuck with a man-child or a complaining daughter for the rest of your life. You'll live with the result.

Build a strong youth group? It requires investment. Want a healthy church? It requires investment. This is the glory awaiting elders. This is the reward and the calling.

## 2. Submission and Humility (5:5-9)

> Young men, in the same way be submissive to those who are older. (1 Peter 5:5)

Just as we've established patterns of authority in government, employment, and family, so too in the church: submit to your leaders. This isn't dependent on how good they are (though that's certainly preferable). We submit to an office—a position appointed by God, not necessarily earned by merit.

> Have confidence in your leaders and submit to their authority, because they keep watch over you as those who must give an account. (Hebrews 13:17)

The young must learn to respect those older than them, even when those leaders are sinners who sometimes make mistakes. Yes, there are ways to address genuine wrongs in leadership. But in the meantime, respect the position.

> Do not rebuke an older man but encourage him as you would a father. (1 Timothy 5:1)

Don't make a difficult job harder than it already is. This Christmas, thank your leaders. Not the paid staff—we receive remuneration already. Thank the volunteers who give their time after long weeks of work or study, who sacrifice willingly. They deserve your gratitude and your respect.

But submission requires something the young find very difficult: **humility**.

### Humility as Strength

> All of you, clothe yourselves with humility toward one another, because, "God opposes the proud but gives grace to the humble." Humble yourselves, therefore, under God's mighty hand, that he may lift you up in due time. Cast all your anxiety on him because he cares for you. (1 Peter 5:5-7)

Notice the picture: humility is weakness—being under God's mighty hand, needing help, casting anxiety because you can't carry it alone. The humble person acknowledges their need for God and others.

Yet we associate youth with something entirely different. The young possess what seems like genuine strength:

- You are the tallest you will ever be.
- You are the strongest and fittest you will ever be.
- You are the most beautiful—hair most luxurious and plentiful.
- You are the peak physical manifestation of yourself.

> The glory of young men is their strength, but the splendor of old men is their gray hair. (Proverbs 20:29)

When you don't need anyone, submission isn't a priority. When you feel invincible, humility seems irrelevant.

### The Pride of Youth and the Devil's Opportunity

> Be self-controlled and alert. Your enemy the devil prowls around like a roaring lion looking for someone to devour. Resist him, standing firm in the faith, because you know that your brothers throughout the world are undergoing the same kind of sufferings. (1 Peter 5:8-9)

Pride and arrogance are characteristic of youth. And this is precisely where the enemy finds opportunity. The young assume their strength will continue indefinitely. If you've grown 5 inches a year, you'll keep growing forever. If you're getting stronger every year, tomorrow you'll be stronger still.

But that's not how life works. At the height of your physical powers comes the plateau—and then decline. Just when you think you're greatest, you've stopped growing. This is why the young often make catastrophic mistakes.

> Folly is bound up in the heart of a child, but the rod of discipline will drive it far away. (Proverbs 22:15)

Consider driver's licenses. In the past, at 18 you got a provisional license; after one year, you got your full license. Simple. Yet car crashes persisted. Today's regime is much tougher—not because education is failing, but because the problem isn't knowledge. It's *maturity*.

The young think they're invincible. They lack foresight. They can't think long-term; they only see immediate gratification. This is why the system now emphasizes supervised hours and time—not testing knowledge, but developing maturity.

Science confirms this: the frontal lobe—where consequential, long-term thinking develops—doesn't fully mature until around age 25. No coincidence that car insurance premiums drop around then either.

### A Culture That Despises the Old

Our Western culture doesn't value older people. We advocate for euthanasia and physician-assisted suicide to be rid of them. They're seen as burdens on healthcare, slow and inconvenient. Yet simultaneously, we love the young and celebrate their ascent to leadership.

But the young should not be leaders.

### The Exception Proves the Rule

Scripture acknowledges rare exceptions:

> Better a poor but wise youth than an old but foolish king who no longer knows how to heed a warning. (Ecclesiastes 4:13)

> Let no one despise you for your youth, but set the believers an example in speech, in conduct, in love, in faith, in purity. (1 Timothy 4:12)

These are exceptional cases—young people of remarkable wisdom. But the general pattern is clear. When young men without such wisdom ascended to power, disaster followed:

> Rehoboam rejected the advice the elders gave him and consulted the young men who had grown up with him... (1 Kings 12:8)

This led to the splitting of the kingdom.

When the young rule in general, Scripture treats it as judgment:

> I will make mere youths their officials; children will rule over them... People will oppress each other—man against man, neighbor against neighbor. The young will rise up against the old, the nobody against the honored... Youths oppress my people; women rule over them. My people, your guides lead you astray; they turn you from the path. (Isaiah 3:4-5, 12)

### Modern Western Folly

Yet modern Western society has done exactly this. We celebrate when the young become leaders—Kevin Rudd, Barack Obama, Justin Trudeau. We praise their youth as if it were an asset.

It's precisely in the interest of those who would do evil to have young leadership. Why? Because the young are easier to manipulate. They value the immediate over the future. They act on feelings rather than wisdom. They have no experience of how the real world actually works.

When the young rule, one of two things is happening: either the eldership is manipulating them (as in Mao's Cultural Revolution), or the eldership has failed entirely.

## 3. Learning Leadership Now (5:10-14)

> And the God of all grace, who called you to his eternal glory in Christ, after you have suffered a little while, will himself restore you and make you strong, firm and steadfast. (1 Peter 5:10)

Here's the call for the young: God is not asking for physical strength in your youth. He's asking for you to be strong in a different way—to be firm and steadfast. To be faithful people. To delay gratification. To suffer for the greater, longer-term good rather than shrink from responsibility. To learn now so you can become mature leaders of the future.

### How to Learn Leadership

Real change requires humility, not youthful passion. Consider the progression:

Before you dream of changing society, master small things:
- Help the teacher in class
- Join the school council
- Organize the school formal
- Volunteer for ISCF leadership

Before you take on politicians, do the groundwork:
- Join a party branch
- Help at elections
- Learn the actual policies

Before you protest the principal, engage the system:
- Attend school meetings
- Volunteer at working bees
- Join the P&C
- Get elected as P&C president

Before you complain to building superintendents, participate:
- Attend strata meetings
- Do work for them
- Be a faithful servant
- Get elected as committee chair

Before you become a church leader, serve diligently as a regular member. Be willing. Be wanting. Be worthy. Learn and gain experience.

This isn't deferring maturity—it's *accelerating* it. Get married. Start a family. Understand how the real world works. Learn to be leaders through time and experience. Before we can govern, we must learn to govern.

### The Need Is Great

As you can see from the prevalence of young leaders in government, business, and even churches, we desperately need mature leaders. The need is great. And you are the ones meant to fill it.
