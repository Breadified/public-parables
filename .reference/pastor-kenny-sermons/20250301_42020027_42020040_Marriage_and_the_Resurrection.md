# Luke 20:27-40

**Title:** Marriage and the Resurrection  
**Date:** 9 March 2025  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction: A Question About Marriage

To understand today's passage, it helps to know the historical context. King Henry VIII married Catherine of Aragon in 1509, but few realize she had previously been married to his older brother Arthur. They were betrothed at age three and married at fifteen. When Arthur died young, Henry VII arranged for the young Henry to marry Catherine to preserve the Spanish alliance—a practice known as levirate marriage.

A similar scenario plays out in today's passage. We're in Jerusalem, where the authorities are increasingly hostile toward Jesus. Over the coming chapters, this tension will escalate toward His crucifixion. The passage touches on some perennially controversial topics—politics and marriage—but they arise naturally from the Scripture itself.

## Part 1: The Sadducees' Disbelief in Resurrection

> "Some of the Sadducees, who say there is no resurrection, came to Jesus with a question." (Luke 20:27)

The **Sadducees** were one of the major sects within Judaism. Their distinctive belief, as the text tells us plainly, was that there is no resurrection.

Historical sources like Josephus and Origen suggest the Sadducees rejected the resurrection because they believed only in the five books of Moses (the Torah), which they claimed were silent on the matter. However, the evidence for this limited canon is mixed. Many scholars think the Sadducees accepted the entire Old Testament but were theological rationalists—they trusted human logic over God's revealed Word and accommodated their beliefs to contemporary culture. This mirrors modern theological liberalism, where people claim to be "progressing" while actually just following popular culture.

## Part 2: The Purpose of Marriage in God's Kingdom

The Sadducees construct their trap by quoting the law of levirate marriage from Deuteronomy:

> "Teacher, Moses wrote for us that if a man's brother dies and leaves a wife but no children, the man must marry the widow and raise up offspring for his brother." (Luke 20:28)

> "If brothers are living together and one of them dies without a son, his widow must not marry outside the family. Her husband's brother shall take her and marry her and fulfill the duty of a brother-in-law to her. The first son she bears shall carry on the name of the dead brother so that his name will not be blotted out from Israel." (Deuteronomy 25:5-6)

This law, called **levirate marriage** (from Latin *levir*, meaning brother-in-law), likely originated in the story of Judah and Onan (Genesis 38:8). The practice served two critical purposes:

First, it demonstrated commitment to the widow—the duty was to her as a widow, not merely to the dead brother or his father.

Second, it ensured the family name and inheritance continued. When the eldest son died, someone had to assume his role. This was more than sentiment; it was about preserving a greater purpose. Consider King Henry VIII: his parents and Catherine's Spanish parents arranged their betrothal at age three not for romance, but to forge a powerful political alliance.

For ancient Israel, the stakes were spiritual. When they entered Canaan after Egypt, the land was divided among tribes and clans. Each family received an inheritance and a mandate to take over the land from pagan nations and establish God's kingdom. This task was so important that even the brother-in-law was enlisted if the eldest son died.

### The Application to Us Today

We no longer live in ancient Israel. There is no earthly promised land to inherit, and levirate marriages are not part of our practice. Yet we have an equally important task.

Consider this truth from Scripture:

> "Children are a heritage from the Lord, offspring a reward from him." (Psalm 127:3)

The word "heritage" means *inheritance*—but the passage means something profound. Children are not merely gifts we receive; they are our inheritance that we pass forward. Through our children, we pass on our legacy.

This is reflected in our naming practices. Surnames exist to carry forward the family name across generations. The English aristocracy needed no surnames because they had such long dynastic memories—Henry the First, then centuries later Henry the Eighth. Americans preserve the practice in both first and surnames—George Bush, George W. Bush—a visible reminder that some work requires multiple generations.

Consider what it takes to accomplish great things:
- **A property in Sydney**: At least two generations to save and build
- **A lasting company**: Three generations to establish and stabilize
- **A nation**: Many generations to build
- **God's kingdom and taking the gospel to the world**: Hundreds and thousands of years

This is why we must pass our work to our children.

Yet today's culture teaches something very different. We tell our children: "Do whatever makes you happy" or "Make enough money so we can be comfortable." Then we're surprised when they want to be influencers or only think of themselves. We've trained them to live for the short term.

But imagine if we truly believed our purpose is to grow God's kingdom. Imagine if our children lived for the resurrection and the age to come—not as mere consumers but as servants in something far larger than themselves. If you genuinely believe this, you must ask: *How much time do I need to spend with them? How often should they be in youth group? How many children would I need to raise? How many grandchildren? What must I do now to accomplish this legacy?*

## Part 3: Jesus Affirms Marriage and Addresses a Hypothetical

The Sadducees, disbelieving in resurrection, attempt a *reductio ad absurdum*—a logical trap to prove the resurrection is absurd. They present a hypothetical scenario:

> "Now there were seven brothers. The first one married a woman and died childless. The second and then the third married her, and in the same way the seven died, leaving no children. Finally, the woman died too. Now then, at the resurrection whose wife will she be, since the seven were married to her?" (Luke 20:29-33)

They chose seven brothers—the biblical number of completion and perfection—to emphasize the complexity. Their argument: If resurrection is real, whose wife will she be in heaven? All seven? One? If you pick one brother, isn't that arbitrary and unfair when all seven died equally? If you say marriage doesn't exist in heaven, doesn't that contradict the law Moses gave?

The Sadducees believed none of these answers worked, therefore the resurrection must be false.

### Jesus's Answer: Marriage Is for This Age

> "Jesus replied, 'The people of this age marry and are given in marriage.'" (Luke 20:34)

Jesus affirms marriage directly. **"The people of this age"** refers to this side of heaven—this present era. In it, marriage and childbearing are normal and essential parts of life. The command still stands:

> "God blessed them and said to them, 'Be fruitful and increase in number; fill the earth and subdue it.'" (Genesis 1:28)

In this age, the goal is to become a father or mother. This doesn't mean everyone will achieve it (there are obstacles and heartaches along the way), but it is the general calling.

**Jesus affirms marriage because marriage is good.** Marriage is good because caring for children is good. Yet modern culture often teaches that marriage is burdensome—something to escape or endure.

## Part 4: Jesus Affirms the Resurrection

> "But those who are considered worthy of taking part in the age to come and in the resurrection from the dead will neither marry nor be given in marriage." (Luke 20:35)

This is the answer to the Sadducees' conundrum. In the age to come, there is no marriage. All the awkward questions about which husband claims the wife in heaven simply dissolve—because there is no marriage in the resurrection.

Notice what this means: It's not merely that wedding ceremonies won't happen in heaven, but that **all earthly marriages will cease**. This is why the traditional marriage vow says "till death do us part"—marriage is for this life, this age.

This doesn't diminish marriage; it clarifies its purpose. The purpose of marriage is **not primarily about love**, for there will be love in heaven. The purpose of marriage is **not primarily about friendship**, for there will be friendship in heaven. 

**What you cannot do in heaven is make children.** The number of the elect is fixed; no more souls will be added. Just as evangelism is an urgent task limited to this age, so is procreation. Do it now, before it is too late.

In the age to come, the children will be different. They will no longer be *our* children in the familial sense:

> "They can no longer die; for they are like the angels. They are God's children, since they are children of the resurrection." (Luke 20:36)

In this age, children are vulnerable—they sicken and die. But in the age to come, they are like the angels: immortal and eternal. They become children of God the Father, with Jesus as our brother. What were once our children become our siblings. We parent them well in this age so that in the age to come they will be our friends—and we are all married to Christ, the bridegroom.

### The Proof from Scripture

To silence the Sadducees' rationalism, Jesus appeals to the very source they claim to trust—the law of Moses:

> "But in the account of the burning bush, even Moses showed that the dead rise, for he calls the Lord 'the God of Abraham, and the God of Isaac, and the God of Jacob.' He is not the God of the dead, but of the living, for to him all are alive." (Luke 20:37-38)

Jesus refers to Exodus 3:6, where God reveals Himself to Moses:

> "I am the God of your father, the God of Abraham, the God of Isaac and the God of Jacob." (Exodus 3:6)

The crucial detail: God says **"I am"**—present tense, not "I was." This is the eternal present tense of God's being.

**The logic is clear:**
- **Hypothesis**: The dead rise and will be resurrected
- **Evidence**: Moses calls God "I am"—present tense—the God of Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob
- **Implication**: Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob must be alive now, not dead forever
- **Conclusion**: Since God is the God of the living, not the dead, these patriarchs are alive. Therefore, the dead do rise.

If Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob were permanently dead and could never exist again, God could not truthfully call Himself their God. But because He is the God of the *living*, these patriarchs must be alive—either in the present age or awaiting resurrection.

**What a comfort this is.** Death will not be the end of us. For those who grieve over loved ones, we will see our children again. God is not the God of the dead but the God of the living.

### The Response

> "Some of the teachers of the law responded, 'Well said, teacher!' And no one dared to ask him any more questions." (Luke 20:39-40)

Jesus's answer was so complete, so skillful, so rooted in Scripture itself, that even His opponents had to acknowledge it. The conversation ends not with debate but with amazed silence.

## Conclusion: Passing the Legacy

King Henry VIII had three children. When his first two sons died, his daughter Queen Elizabeth I would reign for 45 years. During her reign, she:
- Stopped the bloodshed between Catholics and Protestants
- Stabilized the Church of England as an independent church
- Defeated the Spanish Armada
- Ushered in England's Golden Age—the age of Shakespeare
- Began England's naval dominance and rise as a world power

The legacy passed on for another generation.

**May you pass on your legacy to the age to come.**

## Reflections on Marriage and Faith

Martin Luther captured the tension between reason and faith in his *Estate of Marriage* (1522):

> The world says of marriage, "Brief is the joy, lasting the bitterness." When natural reason looks at married life, she turns up her nose and says: "Must I rock the baby, wash its diapers, make its bed, smell its stench, stay up nights, take care of it when it cries, heal its rashes, and care for my wife, provide for her, labor at my trade? What, should I make such a prisoner of myself? It is better to remain free and lead a peaceful life; I will become a priest or nun."
>
> But what does Christian faith say? It opens its eyes and looks upon all these insignificant, distasteful duties in the Spirit, aware that they are all adorned with divine approval as with the costliest gold and jewels.
>
> It says: "God, because I am certain that you have created me as a man and have begotten this child from my body, I also know for a certainty that it meets with your perfect pleasure. I confess to you that I am not worthy to rock the little babe or wash its diapers, or to be entrusted with the care of the child and its mother. How is it that I, without any merit, have come to this distinction of being certain that I am serving your creature and your most precious will? O how gladly would I do so, for I am certain that it is thus pleasing in your sight."
>
> A wife too should regard her duties in the same light, as she suckles the child, rocks and bathes it, and cares for it in other ways. These are truly golden and noble works.
