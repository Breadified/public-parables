## Introduction: The Problem of Half-Truths

There's an old saying: "There are three kinds of lies: lies, damned lies, and statistics." But this deception extends far beyond statistics to any manipulation of information we see in politics, advertising, and media.

A half-truth is a deceptive statement that includes some element of truth. It might be partly true, wholly true but presenting only part of the whole story, or employing deceptive elements like improper punctuation or double meaning—especially when the intent is to deceive or evade blame. In advertising, companies use half-truths because outright falsehoods are illegal. They might claim "Nine out of ten doctors recommend our product," conveniently omitting that they only asked ten doctors, nine of whom work for the company.

As we enter Isaiah 36-37, we face a narrative filled with half-truths, and we must decide which truth we will believe.

## Historical Context

For the previous 35 chapters, Isaiah has warned and judged Israel and Judah for their idolatry, lack of trust, and disobedience. Yet interspersed throughout these judgments are messages of hope: God himself will come to save them if they repent and trust him again. Isaiah warns them not to entangle themselves with the nations through alliances or pacts—anything that shows a lack of trust in Yahweh.

Fast forward several decades to around 701 BC. Part of Isaiah's prophecy has been fulfilled. The Kingdom of Israel no longer exists. The Assyrians under kings like Shalmaneser and Sargon II have conquered it, dispersing the Northern Kingdom's population into foreign lands and assimilating the remnant with captives from other conquered states.

But Judah remains. King Hezekiah reigns, and the question before us is stark: Will Judah meet the same fate as Israel? Whom will Judah depend upon?

## The Field Commander's First Challenge

**Isaiah 36:1-3**

Sennacherib, king of Assyria, has conquered 46 cities of Judah, including the formidable fortress of Lachish. Now he turns his attention to Jerusalem, the most heavily fortified city in the region. He sends his field commander with a large army to demand surrender.

### The Commander's First Argument: Egypt Is Unreliable (36:4-9)

The field commander presents two reasons Jerusalem should surrender:

**"Tell Hezekiah: On what are you basing this confidence of yours? You say you have strategy and military strength—but you speak only empty words. On whom are you depending, that you rebel against me?"**

First, the commander claims Egypt cannot help them. He describes Egypt as "a splintered reed of a staff, which pierces a man's hand and wounds him if he leans on it." Though it appears strong, a reed is hollow. Lean on it and it breaks, wounding you with sharp splinters. This is Egypt's true nature: it looks powerful but cannot be trusted.

Then he offers an alternative: "Make a bargain with my master, the king of Assyria. I will give you two thousand horses—if you can put riders on them! How then can you repulse one officer of the least of my master's officials, even though you are depending on Egypt for chariots and horsemen?" The logic is devastating: if Jerusalem lacks riders for horses, what good is Egyptian help?

### The Commander's Second Argument: Yahweh Is on Assyria's Side (36:10, 7)

The commander's second claim is that Yahweh himself has authorized this conquest: "Furthermore, have I come to attack and destroy this land without the LORD? The LORD himself told me to march against this country and destroy it."

This claim contains truth. Yahweh has indeed called Assyria "the rod of his anger and the club of my wrath" (Isaiah 10:5), using Assyria to punish Israel and other nations. The commander continues by mocking Hezekiah's religious reforms: "And if you say to me, 'We are depending on the LORD our God'—isn't he the one whose high places and altars Hezekiah removed?" The field commander mistakes Hezekiah's destruction of syncretistic altars as sacrilege against Yahweh himself, and concludes that Yahweh is therefore angry with Judah and aligned with Assyria.

### Jerusalem's Fearful Response (36:11-12)

The officials respond with a request: "Please speak to your servants in Aramaic, since we understand it. Don't speak to us in Hebrew in the hearing of the people on the wall."

Aramaic was the diplomatic language of the ancient Near East; Hebrew was the common tongue. The officials want to suppress this message to prevent demoralizing the soldiers. Their fear reveals something important: what the field commander says contains truth. Egypt was indeed unreliable—Sennacherib had recently routed the Egyptian army. And yes, Yahweh had said he would use Assyria to punish Judah. But this is only a half-truth based on a false assumption.

The field commander sees only part of the story. While Yahweh is indeed using Assyria as his instrument of judgment, this does not mean Yahweh is on Assyria's side. Yahweh himself declares: "When the Lord has finished all his work against Mount Zion and Jerusalem, he will say, 'I will punish the king of Assyria for the willful pride of his heart and the haughty look in his eyes'" (Isaiah 10:12).

## The Field Commander's Second Challenge

**Isaiah 36:12-20**

The commander ignores the officials' request and addresses the soldiers directly, using crude language about siege famine: "Do not let Hezekiah deceive you. He cannot deliver you! Do not let Hezekiah persuade you to trust in the LORD when he says, 'The LORD will surely deliver us; this city will not be given into the hand of the king of Assyria.'"

### The Commander's Focus Shifts: Hezekiah Cannot Deliver (36:14-15)

Now the emphasis changes. The commander no longer argues that Yahweh supports Assyria. Instead, he attacks Hezekiah's credibility: Hezekiah cannot save you. His promises of deliverance are false.

### Sennacherib's Alternative Promise: Trust Him Instead (36:16-20)

The commander then offers an alternative salvation: "Make peace with me and come out to me. Then every one of you will eat from his own vine and fig tree and drink water from his own cistern, until I come and take you to a land like your own—a land of grain and new wine, a land of bread and vineyards."

These images of vines, fig trees, cisterns, grain, and wine represent the classic Israelite ideal: blessing, prosperity, peace, wealth, abundance, happiness, contentment, and security. Sennacherib positions himself as an exodus-like deliverer offering a new promised land.

To seal his argument, he cites empirical evidence: "Has the god of any nation ever delivered his land from the hand of the king of Assyria? Where are the gods of Hamath and Arpad? Where are the gods of Sepharvaim? Have they rescued Samaria from my hand? Who of all the gods of these countries has been able to save his land from me? How then can the LORD deliver Jerusalem from my hand?"

Again, a half-truth. It is true that Assyria's policy was to relocate conquered peoples to other lands. But Sennacherib omits the fuller truth: the Judeans would not go together but would be dispersed and assimilated. The Assyrians would dilute their cultural and racial heritage, just as happened in Samaria. There would be no more Jews fighting for Jewish lands—only Assyrians. The promise of a new land comes with cultural annihilation.

## Jerusalem's Response: Prayer and Faith

**Isaiah 36:21-37:4**

The response is silence. What could the officials say? The field commander had spoken truth—but only half-truth. Nothing could be done but mourn and call on the one who could truly act.

The people tear off their fine clothes and put on sackcloth as a sign of mourning. King Hezekiah does the same and goes to the temple of Yahweh. He sends word to the prophet Isaiah: "This is what Hezekiah says: This day is a day of distress and rebuke and disgrace, as when children come to the point of birth and there is no strength to deliver them. It may be that the LORD your God will hear the words of the field commander, whom his master, the king of Assyria, has sent to ridicule the living God, and that he will rebuke him for the words the LORD your God has heard. Therefore pray for the remnant that still survives."

Hezekiah faces total defeat and hopelessness. He describes the nation's crisis with a powerful image: like a birth with no strength to deliver. A people of God, but no strength to continue. The remnant he speaks of is what remains—46 cities have fallen; Jerusalem is alone.

---

## Yahweh's First Reply (37:5-7)

**"Do not be afraid of what you have heard—those words with which the underlings of the king of Assyria have blasphemed me. Listen! I am going to put a spirit in him so that when he hears a certain report, he will return to his own country, and there I will have him cut down with the sword."**

Do not be afraid. Sennacherib will return to his own land, and Yahweh will cut him down there.

---

## Sennacherib's Letter: A More Direct Challenge

**Isaiah 37:10-13**

Before Yahweh's promise can unfold, Sennacherib sends a letter containing all the elements of the previous conversation—but his attack becomes far more direct. He no longer suggests that Yahweh is on Assyria's side. Instead, he directly attacks Yahweh's power itself.

### Sennacherib's Claim: You Cannot Depend on Yahweh (37:10-13)

"Say to Hezekiah king of Judah: Do not let the god you depend on deceive you when he says, 'Jerusalem will not be handed over to the king of Assyria.' Surely you have heard what the kings of Assyria have done to all the countries, destroying them completely. And will you be delivered? Did the gods of the nations that were destroyed by my forefathers deliver them—the gods of Gozan, Haran, Rezeph and the people of Eden who were in Tel Assar?"

Sennacherib offers proof of Yahweh's impotence. He lists the gods of Mesopotamia his ancestors defeated and the Syrian gods he recently conquered. The logic is simple: he has defeated gods numerically and over a long period of time. Why would Yahweh be any different? Hezekiah, you cannot depend on Yahweh. Surrender.

---

## Hezekiah's Prayer of Faith (37:14-20)

**"Then Hezekiah received the letter from the messengers and read it. Then he went up to the temple of the LORD and spread it out before the LORD. And Hezekiah prayed to the LORD: 'O LORD Almighty, God of Israel, enthroned between the cherubim, you alone are God over all the kingdoms of the earth. You have made heaven and earth.'"**

Here is the heart of this passage. Hezekiah's response is prayer grounded in the character of Yahweh. He acknowledges the half-truth: "It is true, O LORD, that the Assyrian kings have laid waste all these peoples and their lands. They have thrown their gods into the fire and destroyed them, for they were not gods but only wood and stone, fashioned by human hands."

Hezekiah recognizes Sennacherib's deception. Yes, Assyria has defeated other gods—but those were merely wood and stone, crafted by human hands. It is like winning a little athletics competition and claiming to be world champion. You must compare like with like.

Yahweh is in a class entirely his own. He is not made by human hands; he made humanity. He made heaven and earth. He alone is God over all the kingdoms of the earth.

Therefore, Hezekiah can pray with righteousness: **"Now, O LORD our God, deliver us from his hand, so that all kingdoms on earth may know that you alone, O LORD, are God."**

---

## Yahweh's Reply: Complete Vindication (37:22-35)

### Jerusalem Will Mock Her Enemy (37:22-23)

**"The Virgin Daughter of Zion despises and mocks you. The Daughter of Jerusalem tosses her head as you flee. Who is it you have insulted and blasphemed? Against whom have you raised your voice and lifted your eyes in pride? Against the Holy One of Israel!"**

### Assyria's Crime (37:24-25)

Yahweh lists Assyria's arrogance. Not because these specific conquests define the crime, but because of Assyria's presumption that its success belongs to itself alone:

**"By your messengers you have heaped insults on the Lord. And you have said, 'With my many chariots I have ascended the heights of the mountains, the utmost heights of Lebanon. I have cut down its tallest cedars, the choicest of its pines. I have reached its remotest heights, the finest of its forests. I have dug wells in foreign lands and drunk the water there. With the soles of my feet I have dried up all the streams of Egypt.'"**

### The Truth Behind the Conquests (37:26-27)

Yahweh reveals what Sennacherib refused to acknowledge: **"Have you not heard? Long ago I ordained it. In days of old I planned it; now I have brought it to pass, that you have turned fortified cities into piles of stone."**

The victories Sennacherib claims as his own were ordained by Yahweh from ancient times. Assyria is merely the instrument of Yahweh's plan.

### Assyria's Judgment (37:28-29)

**"But I know where you stay and when you come and go and how you rage against me. Because you rage against me and because your insolence has reached my ears, I will put my hook in your nose and my bit in your mouth, and I will make you return by the way you came."**

Yahweh will humble Assyria as one leads a beast by a hook through the nose and a bit in the mouth. Sennacherib will return the way he came—in defeat.

### Jerusalem Will Be Delivered (37:30-32)

**"This will be the sign for you, O Hezekiah: This year you will eat what grows by itself, and the second year what springs from that. But in the third year sow and reap, plant vineyards and eat their fruit. Once more a remnant of the house of Judah will take root below and bear fruit above. For out of Jerusalem will come a remnant, and out of Mount Zion a band of survivors. The zeal of the LORD Almighty will accomplish this."**

Jerusalem will survive the siege. The remnant will flourish like a plant taking root and bearing fruit—a metaphor for restoration and growth.

**"Therefore this is what the LORD says concerning the king of Assyria: He will not enter this city or shoot an arrow here. He will not come before it with shield or build a siege ramp against it. By the way that he came he will return; he will not enter this city, declares the LORD."**

---

## The Fulfillment (37:36-38)

**"Then the angel of the LORD went out and put to death a hundred and eighty-five thousand men in the Assyrian camp. When the people got up the next morning—there were all the dead bodies! So Sennacherib king of Assyria broke camp and withdrew. He returned to Nineveh and stayed there. One day, while he was worshiping in the temple of his god Nisroch, his sons Adrammelech and Sharezer cut him down with the sword, and they escaped to the land of Ararat. And Esarhaddon his son succeeded him as king."**

The irony is stunning. Sennacherib, who mocked Yahweh's power, is slaughtered by his own sons in the temple of his favorite god. The question is answered conclusively: Whose God answers prayer? Whose God can protect? Hezekiah's Yahweh, not Sennacherib's gods. Sennacherib built an edifice of pretension—idols, cities, and half-truths—but it was Yahweh who ruled the world.

## Application: Whom Will We Trust?

The question that Hezekiah faced still rings true in our time: **"On whom are you depending?"**

In 2008, as in 701 BC, we are tempted by the half-truths of the world that distract us from dependence on the truth. We have a better King than Hezekiah—Jesus Christ—who defeated the armies of darkness and evil, conquered sin and death, and promises life to all who trust in him. Yet like the field commander and Sennacherib, the world offers us half-truths disguised as complete salvation.

### The Half-Truth of Academic Success

The HSC is promoted as if it will deliver us. Do well in this exam and you are set for life. You will get into a good course and a good career. This contains truth, but it is only half-truth.

Yes, the HSC is important. But it is no more important than many other things in life—studying at university, maintaining relationships, honoring our parents. Yet the effort some give to the HSC suggests that all our energy should concentrate on that one thing. We take tutoring, ignore chores for two years, neglect relationships, give up extracurricular activities, confine ourselves to our rooms, refuse to read anything unless it relates to our syllabus.

The danger is twofold. First, this lifestyle is unrealistic and unsustainable. Even if you excel in the HSC, what will you do at university? In work? In marriage? Continue with tutoring? The evidence shows that when learning becomes mere rote repetition, people become less educated, not more. They stop learning how to think.

Second, sometimes we give up things to pursue this one goal only to discover we have lost them forever. I know of a Year 12 student who gave up Christianity for a year to concentrate on his studies—no Bible, no church, no youth group. He did so well that he never came back. He became so dependent on his studies that he no longer felt he needed to depend on God.

### The Half-Truth of Romantic Love

Perhaps it is that special person you really like and whom you want to like you back. It is true that relationships are a good thing. It is wonderful that many of you are seeking and finding a future spouse, and of course we want someone we love with real passion.

But this is only half the truth. The whole truth is that marriage is hard. Raising a family is hard. Finding something in common beyond physical attraction is hard. Our culture—fed on Australian soaps and Hollywood fairy tales—promotes a half-truth masquerading as truth: romantic love will solve all your problems. And many are willing to do anything in dependence on this lie.

Here is a harder truth: if marriage is genuinely difficult, why compromise its foundation by pursuing relationships with those who do not share your ultimate commitment? Whether they call themselves Christian or not, what is the point of a romantic relationship with someone who does not believe there is a promised land in the future? What is the probability of a happy marriage where one partner depends on God and the other does not?

At best, you are mere acquaintances drifting through a relationship with superficial connection—until something real happens, like your partner refusing to let your children attend church or controlling how you spend money. At worst, you are both hypocrites, pretending to have something in common when you do not.

One survey found that Australian couples in stable relationships had sex an average of 1.84 times per week. That leaves a great deal of time for many other things. If all you have in common is physical attraction, your marriage will be profoundly boring—except for that twice a week, of course. Yet we select spouses primarily based on how they look and how much passion we feel, ignoring that lasting love requires shared values, especially a shared faith.

It is hard to believe this, just as it was hard for Hezekiah to believe that Yahweh's word could be trusted when facing the might of Assyria. It is equally hard for this generation to believe that romantic passion is not the whole truth.

### The Half-Truth of Home Ownership

Consider the appeal of the quarter-acre house with four bedrooms and a study in the suburbs, or perhaps an apartment in the city. It is true that we need shelter and that, over time, buying is cheaper than renting.

But this is only half-truth. The other truths are many:

- While average family size is decreasing (2.6 people), the size of our houses is increasing.
- We view homes not just as shelter but as investments, committing ourselves to pay them off as quickly as possible, then buying another one.
- To pursue the dream of home ownership, some will compromise their integrity. They work themselves to death. They neglect their families under the guise of "doing it for the kids." They cheat on tax returns to make another payment.
- There is an entire apparatus behind this dream: the best jobs require the best degrees from the best universities, which require the best UAI scores, which require the best Year 12 results, which require the best Year 11 results, which require enrollment in the best selective school, which requires entry at age 7. The rat race has begun—and childhood is lost.

The Great Australian Dream, popularized in the 1940s as a counter to socialist ideology and formalized by the Housing Industry Association in 1964, is itself a half-truth. It is very human to think this way. We all desire long-term security, and success in this world is one ingredient of that security. But we must ask: Is it worth what we give up?

### A Call to Perspective

I am not saying do your worst or that there is no place for hard work. Rather, we must keep proper perspective and track what we are giving up in pursuit of our dreams—and whether that cost is worth paying.

There are people in this room who have chosen not to follow the half-truths of the world. They have decided that life is not about wealth, happiness, or sex but about obedience to Jesus Christ. They have decided that life is not about security for self but about service to others. They have decided that life is not about accumulation but about contentment. They have decided that what makes a good husband or wife is their godliness, not their appearance or personality.

Such people may look like losers to the general population. But Jesus asks: **"What good is it for a man to gain the whole world, yet forfeit his soul?"**

The proper perspective is this: the promised land is not this chunk of dirt beneath our feet—neither the land of Israel nor the suburbs of Sydney. As we trace through the Bible, it becomes clear that the land toward which God directs us is kept in the future: a new heaven and new earth where sin will not reign, where tears will not flow, where righteousness dwells, and where God will dwell physically with us. That is the truth by which we must live.

## Conclusion: A Strategy of Prayerful Dependence

Like Hezekiah, we must choose to depend on Yahweh's truth rather than the half-truths of the world. Prayer represents our continual dependence on God to sustain and save us. When we find ourselves in trouble, convicted of our sinfulness and lack of dependence, let us call on our Father in heaven to forgive us and sustain us while we wait for his Son to return.

Let us pray according to our knowledge of God's character—that he promises us a future land, not a temporary one. Let us not be disappointed if he answers with "no." Let us pray that the Father will protect us from the deceit of Satan and the world, and grant us wisdom to see through the half-truths that surround us, that we might live fully devoted to Christ.
