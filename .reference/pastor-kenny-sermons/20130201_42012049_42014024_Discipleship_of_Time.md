# Luke 12:49–14:24

**Title:** Discipleship of Time
**Date:** 9 February 2013
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Getting the Time Right

Getting time right matters—both practically and spiritually. Civilizations throughout history have worked carefully to calculate the precise length of a year (365.25 days) because even minor variations accumulate over centuries and affect harvesting and planting. But there's a deeper spiritual principle at work: getting time right shapes the kind of person you become.

There's a fundamental divide in how the world's religions understand time. Eastern religions view time as cyclical—an endless wheel of rebirth where you keep recycling until you finally decide to get things right. Western religions, including Christianity, understand time as linear—moving inexorably toward a definite point: judgment. This isn't just theological trivia. It changes everything about how we live.

The urgency of understanding our present moment—recognizing the times we live in—becomes critical. Jesus calls his contemporaries to recognize what time it is in God's plan.

## 1. Time Has Run Out for the Jews (12:49–13:7)

### The Jews Do Not Know the Times (v. 56)

> Jesus said to the crowd: "When you see a cloud rising in the west, immediately you say, 'It's going to rain,' and it does. And when the south wind blows, you say, 'It's going to be hot,' and it is. Hypocrites! You know how to interpret the appearance of the earth and the sky. How is it that you don't know how to interpret this present time?" (Luke 12:54–56)

The Jews could read the weather perfectly—clouds meant rain, warm winds meant heat. But they were spiritually blind to the times they were living in. They could interpret atmospheric signs but not recognize that Jesus's presence marked the moment of judgment.

Jesus had come not with peace but with division—a radically different kind of division than Israel had known. Historically, Israel's great dividing line was between Jew and Gentile, marked by bloodline and circumcision. But Jesus introduces a new division: between those who repent and those who do not. This division cuts through families, through the old tribal boundaries. A son turns against his father, a daughter against her mother—not because of nationality, but because of their response to God's offer of mercy.

### The Jews Do Not Know How to Get Right with God (v. 57–13:5)

> "Why don't you judge for yourselves what is right? As you are going with your adversary to the magistrate, try hard to be reconciled to him on the way, or he may drag you off to the judge, and the judge turn you over to the officer, and the officer throw you into prison. I tell you, you will not get out until you have paid the last penny." (Luke 12:57–59)

The image is vivid and urgent: there's still time to be reconciled before the judgment becomes irreversible. The pathway to reconciliation is repentance—turning from your adversary and seeking peace while you still can.

The next passage drives this home with brutal clarity:

> Now there were some present at that time who told Jesus about the Galileans whose blood Pilate had mixed with their sacrifices. Jesus answered, "Do you think that these Galileans were worse sinners than all the other Galileans because they suffered this way? I tell you, no! But unless you repent, you too will all perish. Or those eighteen who died when the tower in Siloam fell on them—do you think they were more guilty than all the others living in Jerusalem? I tell you, no! But unless you repent, you too will all perish." (Luke 13:1–5)

Jesus addresses two tragedies—Galileans killed by Pilate and others crushed by a falling tower. The implicit question is whether suffering indicates exceptional sin. His answer shatters that assumption: no, they weren't worse than others. But the real message hits harder: suffering and death are not occasions to doubt God. Rather, they are God's megaphones calling us to repentance. They are invitations to get right with God while there is still time.

The criterion for salvation is not your ethnicity—whether Jew or Gentile. It is repentance. This alone divides the saved from the lost, families from families.

### The Jews Are Found Wanting (v. 6–7)

> "A man had a fig tree, planted in his vineyard, and he went to look for fruit on it, but did not find any. So he said to the man who took care of the vineyard, 'For three years now I've been coming to look for fruit on this fig tree and haven't found any. Cut it down! Why should it use up the soil?'" (Luke 13:6–7)

The owner has given the tree three years—the length of Jesus's ministry. He's looked for fruit and found none. Time to cut it down. The Jews have had their opportunity during Jesus's earthly mission. They were given everything: the law, the prophets, the knowledge of God. Yet they bore no fruit.

Paul catalogs their failure in Romans:

> "Now you, if you call yourself a Jew; if you rely on the law and brag about your relationship to God; if you know his will and approve of what is superior because you are instructed by the law; if you are convinced that you are a guide for the blind, a light for those who are in the dark, an instructor of the foolish, a teacher of infants, because you have in the law the embodiment of knowledge and truth—you, then, who teach others, do you not teach yourself? You who preach against stealing, do you steal? You who say that people should not commit adultery, do you commit adultery? You who abhor idols, do you rob temples? You who brag about the law, do you dishonor God by breaking the law? As it is written: 'God's name is blasphemed among the Gentiles because of you.'" (Romans 2:17–24)

They had knowledge without obedience, form without substance. No fruit.

This parable strikes a sobering note for those of us who claim a Christian heritage. How many Christians have grown up in faith—perhaps from families stretching back generations—yet produce little fruit? We can critique sermons, run churches, manage ministries. But do we bear fruit? Genuine spiritual fruit means giving up idols, sacrificing until it hurts, sitting with an unbeliever to share the gospel, longing for their salvation.

When knowledge becomes disconnected from service, when we mistake longevity for spiritual depth, when we confuse the form of godliness with its power, we become like that fig tree. The call is urgent: bear fruit that befits repentance. Move from maintenance mode into mission mode. Without a mission, churches become clubs—and who wants to join a club? Without active purpose, maintenance turns into apathy.

Why should God not destroy what bears no fruit?

## 2. Time for the Gentiles (13:18–14:24)

Because the Jews refused, the door opens for the Gentiles. In their recalcitrance, they forfeit the place prepared for them. The remainder of these chapters shows how God's kingdom becomes inclusive—open to all who will respond.

### The Kingdom Is Open to All (13:18–29)

> "What is the kingdom of God like? What shall I compare it to? It is like a mustard seed, which a man took and planted in his garden. It grew and became a tree, and the birds of the air perched in its branches." (Luke 13:18–19)

> "What shall I compare the kingdom of God to? It is like yeast that a woman took and mixed into a large amount of flour until it worked all through the dough." (Luke 13:20–21)

The kingdom grows from small beginnings and permeates everything. It's not exclusive—even the birds nest in its branches.

Then comes the shocking inversion:

> "Someone asked him, 'Lord, are only a few people going to be saved?' He said to them, 'Make every effort to enter through the narrow door, because many, I tell you, will try to enter and will not be able. Once the owner of the house gets up and closes the door, you will stand outside knocking and pleading, "Sir, open the door for us." But he will answer, "I don't know you or where you come from." Then you will say, "We ate and drank with you, and you taught in our streets." But he will reply, "I don't know you or where you come from. Away from me, all you evildoers!" There will be weeping there, and gnashing of teeth, when you see Abraham, Isaac and Jacob and all the prophets in the kingdom of God, but you yourselves thrown out." (Luke 13:23–28)

The tragedy is exquisite: they shared meals with Jesus. They heard him teach in their streets. Yet they never truly knew him. The patriarchs—Abraham, Isaac, Jacob—will be there. The prophets will be there. But they, the chosen people, will be cast out.

Yet God's table will be full:

> "People will come from east and west and north and south, and will take their places at the feast in the kingdom of God. Indeed there are those who are last who will be first, and first who will be last." (Luke 13:29–30)

The last become first. The Gentiles—those historically excluded—now sit at the table. The first—Israel—have lost their place.

### The Banquet Is Open to All (14:7–24)

The principle repeats in a parable about seating at a feast:

> "When he noticed how the guests picked the places of honor at the table, he told them this parable: 'When someone invites you to a wedding feast, do not take the place of honor, for a person more distinguished than you may have been invited. If so, the host who invited both of you will come and say to you, "Give this man your seat." Then, humiliated, you will have to take the least important place. But when you are invited, take the lowest place, so that when your host comes, he will say to you, "Friend, move up to a better place." Then you will be honored in the presence of all your fellow guests. For everyone who exalts himself will be humbled, and he who humbles himself will be exalted.'" (Luke 14:7–11)

Then Jesus shifts the focus from the guests to the host:

> "When you give a luncheon or dinner, do not invite your friends, your brothers or relatives, or your rich neighbors; if you do, they may invite you back and so you will be repaid. But when you give a banquet, invite the poor, the crippled, the lame, the blind, and you will be blessed. Although they cannot repay you, you will be repaid at the resurrection of the righteous." (Luke 14:12–14)

This sets up the grand parable:

> "A certain man was preparing a great banquet and invited many guests. At the time of the banquet he sent his servant to tell those who had been invited, 'Come, for everything is now ready.' But they all alike began to make excuses. The first said, 'I have just bought a field, and I must go and see it. Please excuse me.' Another said, 'I have just bought five yoke of oxen, and I'm on my way to try them out. Please excuse me.' Still another said, 'I just got married, so I can't come.' The servant came back and reported this to his master. Then the owner of the house became angry and ordered his servant, 'Go out quickly into the streets and alleys of the town and bring in the poor, the crippled, the blind and the lame.' 'Sir,' the servant said, 'what you ordered has been done, but there is still room.' Then the master told his servant, 'Go out to the roads and country lanes and make them come in, so that my house will be full. I tell you, not one of those men who were invited will get a taste of my banquet.'" (Luke 14:16–24)

The invited guests—those with property, oxen, new marriages (the respectable, the successful)—all decline. They prioritize their own interests. God becomes angry and invites instead the poor, the crippled, the blind, the lame. Those considered worthless in society's eyes fill the banquet hall. The originally invited guests never taste the feast.

This is not merely a political redistribution of honor. It's a theological statement: those who think they have time, who suppose they can always come later, forfeit the feast. Those who come immediately, even though society calls them broken, are honored.

## 3. Still Time for Mercy (13:8–9)

But in the midst of judgment, mercy breaks through. When the vineyard keeper hears the command to cut down the fig tree, he asks for one more year:

> "Sir, leave it alone for one more year, and I'll dig around it and fertilize it. If it bears fruit next year, fine! If not, then cut it down." (Luke 13:8–9)

There is still time. Even as judgment looms, mercy pleads for opportunity. Jesus does not answer—the question hangs in the air, unresolved. But what follows are stories of salvation granted even in the final moments.

### The Sick Daughter of Abraham (13:10–17)

> "On a Sabbath Jesus was teaching in one of the synagogues, and a woman was there who had been crippled by a spirit for eighteen years. She was bent over and could not straighten up at all. When Jesus saw her, he called her forward and said to her, 'Woman, you are set free from your infirmity.' Then he put his hands on her, and immediately she straightened up and praised God." (Luke 13:10–13)

A woman bent double for eighteen years is healed. But the synagogue ruler protests:

> "Indignant because Jesus had healed on the Sabbath, the synagogue ruler said to the people, 'There are six days for work. So come and be healed on those days, not on the Sabbath.'" (Luke 13:14)

Jesus's response is brilliant. He points out the hypocrisy: on the Sabbath, Jews untie their oxen and donkeys to lead them to water. They violate the Sabbath to care for animals. Should not this woman—a daughter of Abraham, bound by Satan for eighteen years—be loosed (the same Greek word) from her chains?

> "The Lord answered him, 'You hypocrites! Doesn't each of you on the Sabbath untie his ox or donkey from the stall and lead it out to give it water? Then should not this woman, a daughter of Abraham, whom Satan has kept bound for eighteen long years, be set free on the Sabbath day from what bound her?'" (Luke 13:15–16)

There is a divine necessity here. It is necessary to show mercy. It is necessary to liberate. The Sabbath was made for humanity's good, not humanity for the Sabbath's sake.

The application cuts deeply: we often reverse this. We tell the hurting to come back on Sunday. We go to funerals faithfully but skip weddings. Yet true discipleship means showing mercy at inconvenient times—sitting with an unbeliever at an awkward moment, sharing the gospel when it disrupts our schedule, caring when it costs us comfort.

### The Sick Jew (14:1–6)

The pattern repeats:

> "One Sabbath, when Jesus went to eat in the house of a prominent Pharisee, he was being carefully watched. There in front of him was a man suffering from dropsy. Jesus asked the Pharisees and experts in the law, 'Is it lawful to heal on the Sabbath or not?' But they remained silent. So taking hold of the man, he healed him and sent him away. Then he asked them, 'If one of you has a son or an ox that falls into a well on the Sabbath day, will you not immediately pull him out?' And they had nothing to say." (Luke 14:1–6)

Again, the logic is inescapable. Of course you would break the Sabbath to save your son or your ox. How much more a human being bound in suffering?

Even as judgment falls on the Jews, even as the door closes for them, mercy still breaks through for individuals. These are last-minute entries into salvation. Why? Because our God is a God of compassion:

> "The LORD, the LORD, the compassionate and gracious God, slow to anger, abounding in love and faithfulness, maintaining love to thousands, and forgiving wickedness, rebellion and sin." (Exodus 34:6–7)

It is God's nature to forgive. He will do anything possible to show compassion, to delay judgment, to offer mercy to those who will receive it.

And then this haunting passage:

> "At that time some Pharisees came to Jesus and said to him, 'Leave this place and go somewhere else. Herod wants to kill you.' He replied, 'Go tell that fox, "I will drive out demons and heal people today and tomorrow, and on the third day I will reach my goal." In any case, I must keep going today and tomorrow and the next day—for surely no prophet can die outside Jerusalem!'" (Luke 13:31–33)

Jesus would do anything. Though warned to flee, he continues healing and delivering for three more days before moving toward Jerusalem—toward the cross. He shows compassion even as he walks toward judgment. He will not give up until the very end.

> "O Jerusalem, Jerusalem, you who kill the prophets and stone those sent to you, how often I have longed to gather your children together, as a hen gathers her chicks under her wings, but you were not willing! Look, your house is left to you desolate. I tell you, you will not see me again until you say, 'Blessed is he who comes in the name of the Lord.'" (Luke 13:34–35)

The longing is palpable. Jesus desires their salvation with aching tenderness. But they were not willing.

## Discipleship of Time

Friends, it is now time for the Gentiles to be saved. Let us not repeat the mistakes of ancient Israel. We must not forget that to whom much has been given, much will be required. Our knowledge is meant to flow out in service, not in self-enrichment.

Most critically: time is linear. It is going somewhere—toward judgment. We do not have the luxury that Buddhism offers: infinite cycles, no urgency, no real consequence. We cannot keep recycling until we decide to get it right. 

For Christians, time is short. History is moving toward an end. This creates urgency and gravity in our mission. We are intolerant of false religion not out of arrogance but out of love—because the stakes are eternal and the time is running out.

This is the discipleship of time God calls for: a people who understand what age this is, who recognize that every one of the 365 and a quarter days we're given is precious. Not for accumulating wealth, for endless entertainment, for distraction. These days are meant for the gospel—for telling people about Jesus.

Get the time right. Know what time it is in God's plan. Repent in light of coming judgment. Bear fruit that demonstrates genuine faith. And use your time well—for the mission that alone gives it meaning.
