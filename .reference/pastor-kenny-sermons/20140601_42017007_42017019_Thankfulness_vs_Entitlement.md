# Luke 17:7-19

**Title:** Thankfulness vs. Entitlement
**Date:** 22 June 2014
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Problem of Entitlement

A few years ago, the term "First World Problems" emerged to describe the phenomenon of privileged societies taking for granted what is luxury elsewhere. We complain about forgotten earphones, gardeners mowing too early, or watermelons with too many seeds—problems that 99% of the world doesn't face. These anxieties stem from taking for granted the very things that make life comfortable.

This mindset extends beyond material complaints. It's about our attitude in asking for help and our attitude after receiving it. We live in a generation that mistakes generosity for obligation.

## God Does Not Owe Us

Jesus illustrates this principle through the parable of a master and servant:

> "Suppose one of you had a servant plowing or looking after the sheep. Would he say to the servant when he comes in from the field, 'Come along now and sit down to eat'? Would he not rather say, 'Prepare my supper, get yourself ready and wait on me while I eat and drink; after that you may eat and drink'? Would he thank the servant because he did what he was told to do? So you also, when you have done everything you were told to do, should say, 'We are unworthy servants; we have only done our duty.'" (Luke 17:7-10)

After a grueling day—plowing fields that turn over soil and bury weeds, or shepherding sheep across miles under the Middle Eastern sun—the servant naturally wants to rest. This is understandable. But the master hasn't eaten yet. The work isn't finished.

The servant is not doing the master a favor by preparing supper. He's simply doing his duty. The master owes no thanks for work that was required. The servant has merely completed his obligation.

We struggle to grasp this principle. We thank people for handing in assignments, giving money at the till, or completing basic tasks. But think of your teacher or boss—they don't thank you for turning in work you were supposed to complete. You've done what you were supposed to do. That's not heroic; that's your job.

This parable indicts a dangerous attitude: the belief that we're entitled to rest when work is incomplete, or that we deserve gratitude for doing what's required.

## The Entitlement Trap in Our Culture

As Australians, we struggle particularly with entitlement through systems like Centrelink. While welfare serves a genuine purpose—allowing those with more to help those with less—we've reframed it as an entitlement rather than generosity. We complain when benefits are questioned or insufficient, forgetting that no one has a right to someone else's generosity. A person who gives money on the street is not obligated to give more simply because someone asks.

We demand not just shelter but the right to own a house. We frame these as justice issues, but they're symptoms of a deeper problem: we've confused generosity with obligation.

## Rather, We Owe God

The parable invites us to see ourselves as servants and God as master. God does not owe us a spouse, health, wealth, or the fulfillment of our dreams. When we don't receive what we feel we deserve, we often rebel—skipping church because we got bad marks, or abandoning faith because we experienced hurt.

But the truth is inverted: we owe God.

Jesus begins this passage by addressing something critical:

> "Things that cause people to sin are bound to come, but woe to that person through whom they come. It would be better for him to be thrown into the sea with a millstone tied around his neck than for him to cause one of these little ones to sin. So watch yourselves. If your brother sins, rebuke him, and if he repents, forgive him. If he sins against you seven times in a day, and seven times comes back to you and says, 'I repent,' forgive him." (Luke 17:1-4)

Our duty includes telling others about Jesus and ensuring we don't block them from his forgiveness. This is our work—incomplete and ongoing. Yet we get tired. We think after forgiving once, we've done our part. We feel entitled to hold grudges.

But we who have been forgiven must forgive others, however many times they repent. This is not easy, and boundaries may be necessary. But we forgive much because we have been forgiven much. When we do forgive, we're not heroes deserving praise—we've simply done our duty.

## Duty and Thankfulness

We modern people recoil from the word "duty." It sounds forced, emotionless, the opposite of love. But duty properly understood is love in action. A mother caring for her child despite exhaustion, a soldier marching into battle despite fear—these are acts of duty rooted in love. Duty isn't forced because someone else forces it on us; it's when we're tempted to run but choose to love anyway.

Thankfulness transforms duty. Students in selective schools often work tirelessly not because they're forced, but because they're grateful. Their parents have given them an opportunity; they won't waste it. They've invested in tutoring; the students honor that investment.

God did not owe us anything. Jesus owed us nothing. Yet out of love, he gave us life, placed us in privilege, and gave us redemption and forgiveness on the cross. When we grasp this gift, duty becomes our natural response.

## The Example of the Ten Lepers

> "Now on his way to Jerusalem, Jesus traveled along the border between Samaria and Galilee. As he was going into a village, ten men who had leprosy met him. They stood at a distance and called out in a loud voice, 'Jesus, Master, have pity on us!' When he saw them, he said, 'Go, show yourselves to the priests.' And as they went, they were cleansed. One of them, when he saw he was healed, came back, praising God in a loud voice. He threw himself at Jesus' feet and thanked him—and he was a Samaritan. Jesus asked, 'Were not all ten cleansed? Where are the other nine? Was no one found to return and give praise to God except this foreigner?' Then he said to him, 'Rise and go; your faith has made you well.'" (Luke 17:11-19)

Ten received healing. Nine walked away as if it were owed to them. One—a foreigner, an outsider—returned in gratitude. Jesus affirmed him: "Your faith has made you well." Faith here is simply gratitude. A simple thank you was enough.

We have received far more than healing. We have access to the privileges of being sons and daughters of God—eternal life, forgiveness, a seat in the heavenly realms, the incomparable riches of his grace. What more could we possibly be given?

## Our Continuing Duty

We are a spoilt generation creating "first world problems" while the world is in desperate need:

- We won't commit to ministry because we haven't booked flights yet
- We skip service because we need to holiday in Europe or Canada
- We can't serve because we're on four months of university holidays
- We won't befriend the lonely because we don't like their food
- We won't attend church because our children might miss a birthday party

These aren't the problems of the first world at all. They're the problems of the last world—a world that has forgotten why it was saved.

The danger is thinking we've done our Christian duty. We've packed up chairs at church, so we don't need to do anything else. We've served once, so we're free to focus on career, travel, and retirement. We work ourselves into exhaustion, then escape into hedonism, then return to workaholism. Then comes retirement—golf all day, extensive overseas travel.

But look at John Chapman, a beloved Christian worker in Sydney. He retired from evangelism fifteen years before he died, yet his "retirement" meant constant speaking engagements across Australia. Up to his final days, his diary was full. When he couldn't keep an appointment, it wasn't because something came up or he was tired—he had died serving the Lord. He didn't retire; he simply moved to his final rest.

God calls us to keep serving. Not as drudgery, but as gratitude.

## The Choice Before Us

We face a choice: Will we be entitled, or will we be thankful?

The entitled demand rights, expect thanks, and rest when work remains. They've done their duty once and now deserve a break. They complain about welfare being called entitlements, yet live as if generosity is a right.

The thankful remember what they've been given. They serve not because they must, but because they've been rescued. They count their blessings and recognize the vast gulf between what they deserve and what they've received. That recognition compels them to keep serving, not as payment but as response.

There is no end to our duty in this world. The work continues. Let us have no more talk of entitlements and no more excuses.

We have been given much. Let us do our duty together.
