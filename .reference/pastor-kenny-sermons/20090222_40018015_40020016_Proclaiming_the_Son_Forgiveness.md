# Matthew 18:15–20:16

**Title:** Proclaiming the Son & Forgiveness
**Date:** February 22, 2009
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Understanding Forgiveness

Forgiveness matters because it reverses the irreversible—or at least, it attempts to. We've all experienced moments we desperately wish we could undo: the words that severed a relationship, the harsh decision we can't take back, the accident we can't reverse. The deepest horror is not being forgiven when we desperately need it. Forgiveness is the only path forward when our sins have created a chasm between us and others.

---

## 1. Unfaithful Israel (19:1–29)

### The Pharisees' Test on Divorce

> When Jesus had finished saying these things, he left Galilee and went into the region of Judea to the other side of the Jordan. Large crowds followed him, and he healed them there. Some Pharisees came to him to test him. They asked, "Is it lawful for a man to divorce his wife for any and every reason?" (Matthew 19:1–3)

The Pharisees ask about divorce—literally, "loosening" or sending away. Jesus responds by grounding marriage in God's original design, not in human convenience.

> "Haven't you read," he replied, "that at the beginning the Creator made them male and female, and said, 'For this reason a man will leave his father and mother and be united to his wife, and the two will become one flesh'? So they are no longer two, but one. Therefore what God has joined together, let man not separate." (Matthew 19:4–6)

Jesus teaches that marriage is a lifelong covenant. When God joins two people into one flesh, they belong to each other for life.

### Moses' Permission and Jesus' Exception

The Pharisees push back, referencing Deuteronomy 24 and Moses' allowance of divorce certificates. Jesus clarifies:

> "Moses permitted you to divorce your wives because your hearts were hard. But it was not this way from the beginning. I tell you that anyone who divorces his wife, except for marital unfaithfulness, and marries another woman commits adultery." (Matthew 19:8–9)

This exception clause—"except for marital unfaithfulness" (porneia)—appears only in Matthew. Why? Matthew likely includes it to explain Joseph's righteous response to Mary's apparent unfaithfulness before the Incarnation (Matthew 1:18–19). Joseph was being merciful, not breaking covenant. The exception preserves both the permanence of marriage and God's mercy toward those trapped in impossible situations.

### Application: Marriage as Lifelong Commitment

Marriage is for life. If separation occurs, remarriage is not permitted. This is difficult teaching, especially for those whose parents divorced. I don't judge those situations—I don't know the circumstances. But as you enter the season of dating and marriage, understand this: if you're willing to date someone, you must be willing to marry them. This is a lifetime commitment.

Our generation struggles with commitment. I've seen young marriages collapse within months, followed by another relationship soon after. There is no such thing as "dating again." You cannot simply trade in a failed marriage like a damaged car and move on. You've become one flesh—you cannot separate and pretend nothing happened.

If you're not willing to commit to someone for life, don't date them. Dating is not entertainment; it's discernment toward marriage. When you date, you're evaluating whether this person is worth your whole life.

### The Disciples' Response

> The disciples said to him, "If this is the situation between a husband and wife, it is better not to marry." Jesus replied, "Not everyone can accept this word, but only those to whom it has been given. For some are eunuchs because they were born that way; others were made that way by men; and others have renounced marriage because of the kingdom of heaven. The one who can accept this should accept it." (Matthew 19:10–12)

Jesus acknowledges that not everyone is called to marriage. Some are called to singleness for the kingdom's sake. Both are valid callings. This is similar to Christian commitment itself—you cannot commit to Christ and then bow out whenever you please, then come back when it suits you. You cannot be re-baptized. The commitment is permanent.

### The Rich Young Man: Contrast with the Children

Just before discussing the rich young man, Matthew shows us children being welcomed by Jesus:

> Then little children were brought to Jesus for him to place his hands on them and pray for them. But the disciples rebuked those who brought them. Jesus said, "Let the little children come to me, and do not hinder them, for the kingdom of heaven belongs to such as these." (Matthew 19:13–15)

The children come with openness and receptivity. Now contrast this with the rich young man:

> Now a man came up to Jesus and asked, "Teacher, what good thing must I do to get eternal life?" Jesus replied, "Why do you ask me about what is good? There is only One who is good. If you want to enter life, obey the commandments." The man said, "Which ones?" Jesus replied, "'Do not murder, do not commit adultery, do not steal, do not give false testimony, honor your father and mother,' and 'love your neighbor as yourself.'"

The young man responds: "All these I have kept. What do I still lack?"

> Jesus answered, "If you want to be perfect, go, sell your possessions and give to the poor, and you will have treasure in heaven. Then come, follow me." When the young man heard this, he went away sad, because he had great wealth. (Matthew 19:16–22)

Here is the tragedy: the young man has done everything right externally, but he refuses the one thing that matters—surrendering his heart completely to Jesus. This mirrors Israel's posture toward God. Israel kept the commandments outwardly but held back her heart. Her worship was half-hearted. She would not give up her idols and false gods to follow the true God wholeheartedly.

## 2. Israel Coming Last (19:30–20:16)

### The Promise of Judgment and Reversal

> Jesus said to them, "I tell you the truth, at the renewal of all things, when the Son of Man sits on his glorious throne, you who have followed me will also sit on twelve thrones, judging the twelve tribes of Israel. And everyone who has left houses or brothers or sisters or father or mother or children or fields for my sake will receive a hundred times as much and will inherit eternal life. But many who are first will be last, and many who are last will be first." (Matthew 19:28–30)

This is Israel's future: judgment. And Israel, once first among the nations, will come last.

### The Parable of the Workers in the Vineyard

Jesus illustrates this principle through the parable of workers hired throughout the day:

> "For the kingdom of heaven is like a landowner who went out early in the morning to hire men to work in his vineyard. He agreed to pay them a denarius for the day and sent them into his vineyard. About the third hour he went out and saw others standing in the marketplace doing nothing. He told them, 'You also go and work in my vineyard, and I will pay you whatever is right.' So they went. He went out again about the sixth hour and the ninth hour and did the same thing. About the eleventh hour he went out and found still others standing around. He asked them, 'Why have you been standing here all day long doing nothing?' 'Because no one has hired us,' they answered. He said to them, 'You also go and work in my vineyard.'" (Matthew 20:1–7)

When evening comes, the landowner pays the workers:

> "When evening came, the owner of the vineyard said to his foreman, 'Call the workers and pay them their wages, beginning with the last ones hired and going on to the first.' The workers who were hired about the eleventh hour came and each received a denarius. So when those came who were hired first, they expected to receive more. But each one of them also received a denarius. When they received it, they began to grumble against the landowner. 'These men who were hired last worked only one hour,' they said, 'and you have made them equal to us who have borne the burden of the work and the heat of the day.'" (Matthew 20:8–12)

The landowner responds:

> "But he answered one of them, 'Friend, I am not being unfair to you. Didn't you agree to work for a denarius? Take your pay and go. I want to give the man who was hired last the same as I gave you. Don't I have the right to do what I want with my own money? Or are you envious because I am generous? So the last will be first, and the first will be last.'" (Matthew 20:13–16)

The point is clear: the Gentiles, hired at the eleventh hour, will receive the same inheritance as Israel. There is no advantage to being first. All receive the kingdom equally through grace, not through works or seniority.

## 3. Forgiveness and Forgiving (18:15–35)

### The Process of Reconciliation

> "If your brother sins against you, go and show him his fault, just between the two of you. If he listens to you, you have won your brother over. But if he will not listen, take one or two others along, so that 'every matter may be established by the testimony of two or three witnesses.' If he refuses to listen to them, tell it to the church; and if he refuses to listen even to the church, treat him as you would a pagan or a tax collector." (Matthew 18:15–17)

Jesus establishes a clear pathway for reconciliation: first, direct conversation. If that fails, bring witnesses. If that fails, involve the church. The goal is always restoration, never condemnation.

### The Authority to Bind and Loose

> "I tell you the truth, whatever you bind on earth will be bound in heaven, and whatever you loose on earth will be loosed in heaven. Again, I tell you that if two of you on earth agree about anything you ask for, it will be done for you by my Father in heaven. For where two or three come together in my name, there am I with them." (Matthew 18:18–20)

"Binding and loosing" means proclaiming heaven's verdict. The church does not create God's judgment; it announces what God has already determined. But notice what the Pharisees had done: they were loosing (permitting divorce) what God had bound in heaven (one flesh). They were reversing God's judgment.

### How Many Times Should We Forgive?

> Then Peter came to Jesus and asked, "Lord, how many times shall I forgive my brother when he sins against me? Up to seven times?" Jesus answered, "I tell you, not seven times, but seventy-seven times." (Matthew 18:21–22)

Seventy-seven times means limitless, perpetual forgiveness. Jesus isn't asking for a mathematics lesson; He's asking for a transformed heart.

### The Parable of the Unmerciful Servant

> "Therefore, the kingdom of heaven is like a king who wanted to settle accounts with his servants. As he began the settlement, a man who owed him ten thousand talents was brought to him. Since he was not able to pay, the master ordered that he and his wife and his children and all that he had be sold to repay the debt. The servant fell on his knees before him. 'Be patient with me,' he begged, 'and I will pay back everything.' The servant's master took pity on him, canceled the debt and let him go." (Matthew 18:23–27)

Ten thousand talents—an incomprehensibly large debt. The servant cannot possibly repay it. Yet the master, moved with compassion, cancels it entirely. The servant is free.

> "But when that servant went out, he found one of his fellow servants who owed him a hundred denarii. He grabbed him and began to choke him. 'Pay back what you owe me!' he demanded. His fellow servant fell to his knees and begged him, 'Be patient with me, and I will pay you back.' But he refused. Instead, he went off and had the man thrown into prison until he could pay the debt." (Matthew 18:28–30)

The same servant, having been forgiven an impossible debt, shows no mercy to a fellow servant who owes him a pittance. A hundred denarii is nothing compared to ten thousand talents.

> "When the other servants saw what had happened, they were greatly distressed and went and told their master everything that had happened. Then the master called the servant in. 'You wicked servant,' he said, 'I canceled all that debt of yours because you begged me to. Shouldn't you have had mercy on your fellow servant just as I had on you?' In anger his master turned him over to the jailers to be tortured, until he should pay back all he owed. This is how my heavenly Father will treat each of you unless you forgive your brother from your heart." (Matthew 18:31–35)

The connection is devastating: if the king has loosed you from your debt, you must loose others from theirs. If you refuse to forgive, you reject the forgiveness you've been given.

### Application: The Age of Forgiveness

This is what we pray: "Forgive us of our sins, just as we forgive those who sin against us." We claim God's forgiveness while often refusing to extend it to others.

Israel did not follow the decrees of heaven. They would not forgive the nations. Neither do we always follow heaven's decrees.

**Where does this show up in our lives?**

- **Gossip and church politics.** Stop the chain mail of gossip. I hate chain mails—the false promises, the manipulation. Gossip spreads the same way: through repetition and betrayal. When you hear something negative about someone, do you pass it on, amplifying the pain? Or do you stop the chain?

- **Do we practice Matthew 18:15 and beyond?** Do we have the courage to go directly to someone who has wronged us, rather than talking about them to others? Do we have the courage to lead others toward reconciliation instead of being a sounding board for their grievances?

- **Do we proclaim the gospel or condemn people to silence?** When we refuse to forgive, we're refusing to loosen people from their chains. The gospel proclaims forgiveness and freedom. Are we proclaiming it, or are we imprisoning people in their shame?

## Proclaiming the Son

This entire section of Matthew—from 18:15 through 20:16—is about declaring Jesus as the one who forgives, the one who restores, the one who welcomes the last and the least.

### For Non-Christians

Some of you have spent time with us these past weeks. Nothing would give the angels in heaven—or us—more joy than to see you come to Jesus. You are the lost sheep. Time is fleeting. University will come, life will distract you, and you'll think "good enough" is enough. But it's not. The call is to follow the Son.

### For Christians

The distractions ahead are real: university will bring fun, friends, money, and a thousand things competing for your attention and allegiance. But the call remains: be intentional about following the Son.

- **Follow the Son.** Don't give up God for money, status, or comfort, like the rich young man.
- **Listen to the Son.** Hear what He's actually saying, not what you want Him to say.
- **Proclaim the Son and His forgiveness.** Tell others that they can be free from their debt, their shame, their isolation.

It has been a pleasure serving you these past months. I hope we meet again soon—around March 6th, if the Lord wills. When we do, I want to hear that you're going strong in your faith, still learning, still serving, becoming radical disciples of Jesus.

And if not in this lifetime, I'll rejoice when we meet in heaven. Make sure you end up there.

## Closing Prayer

On that last day, may we all be standing there—bruised by the world but still standing, because we stood firm in forgiveness, in mercy, and in the gospel of Jesus Christ.
