# Zechariah 2:1-13, 5:1-4

**Title:** What Home Looks Like
**Date:** October 26, 2025
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## A Vision of Home

We're examining the third vision in Zechariah's sequence of eight prophetic dreams, which follow a chiastic structure. Today we'll explore what home truly looks like through three essential components: land, people, and God's presence.

## 1. Land: Spacious and Safe

### The Measurement of Jerusalem

> I looked up, and there before me was a man with a measuring line in his hand. I asked, "Where are you going?" He answered me, "To measure Jerusalem, to find out how wide and how long it is." (Zechariah 2:1-2)

The vision opens with a builder's image—a man carrying a measuring line, the ancient equivalent of a measuring tape. In construction, measurement is everything. A craftsman's skill isn't just in driving nails; it's in knowing *where* to drive them. Proper measurements prevent walls from being crooked, doors from misaligning, and entire structures from cascading problems. This man isn't measuring a single house but the entire city of Jerusalem, determining its dimensions so that the wall around it can be rebuilt.

### A City Without Walls

But then an angel interrupts with unexpected news:

> "Run, tell that young man, 'Jerusalem will be a city without walls because of the great number of people and animals in it.'" (Zechariah 2:3-4)

The messenger tells the builder to abandon his measurements. Jerusalem won't need walls—it will be too large, too crowded to confine. The Hebrew word for "without walls" carries the sense of "open space" or "rural"—suggesting expansiveness rather than confinement.

This seems counterintuitive. A city without walls? Defenceless. Yet the Lord promises:

> "I myself will be a wall of fire around it," declares the Lord, "and I will be its glory within." (Zechariah 2:5)

There's beautiful irony here. The fire that once destroyed Jerusalem during siege will now protect it. God's fire becomes the firewall—the original and ultimate security system. The Jewish exiles would have understood this deeply: what once brought devastation now brings deliverance.

The principle is visible in modern architecture. Small houses require fences to keep neighbours' possessions from spilling over. Larger homes need fewer external walls. But sprawling rural estates need no walls at all—creeks, forests, and mountains become natural boundaries. When you own an island, you don't need a wall.

Australia itself demonstrates this principle. Our houses have grown dramatically. In the last sixty years, average home sizes have more than doubled, reaching 235.8 square meters in 2019-2020. Yet simultaneously, average household size has shrunk from 4.5 people per household in 1911 to just 2.5 today. We're building larger spaces with fewer people to fill them.

Jerusalem's vision is of a vast, secure city—not because of human engineering but because God Himself is its protection and glory. For the exiles in Babylon, this meant something precious: there is space for you, and there is safety.

## 2. People: A Call to Return

### Scattered Israel Invited Home

> "Come! Come! Flee from the land of the north," declares the Lord, "for I have scattered you to the four winds of heaven," declares the Lord. "Come, Zion! Escape, you who live in Daughter Babylon!" (Zechariah 2:6-7)

The "land of the north" and "Daughter Babylon" refer to the same reality—the diaspora Jews scattered in Babylonian exile. Some had been carried away by Babylon itself; others traced their displacement back two centuries to the Assyrian captivity. Now God issues an invitation: return.

This isn't a command born of anger but an invitation born of promise. The nations that plundered them will face justice:

> "For this is what the Lord Almighty says: 'After the Glorious One has sent me against the nations that have plundered you—for whoever touches you touches the apple of his eye—I will surely raise my hand against them so that their slaves will plunder them. Then you will know that the Lord Almighty has sent me.'" (Zechariah 2:8-9)

The language here is remarkable: "the Glorious One has sent me." The Lord speaking of one who has sent Him—this echoes the Psalm 110 refrain, "The Lord said to my Lord," suggesting a trinitarian mystery. The point, however, is clear: those who attack God's people attack the apple of His eye.

That phrase carries weight. The pupil of the eye—"the little man" in Hebrew—is the most sensitive part of the body. Touch someone's eye, and the reaction is instantaneous and violent. Dirt, a child's finger, any irritation triggers an immediate, reflexive response. The metaphor conveys God's fierce protectiveness. Strike at His people, and you trigger His righteous judgment.

Yet the exiles facing this message were comfortable. They had jobs in Babylon. They'd started families. They'd bought houses. The status quo was stable. God's call demands something radical: leave comfort for home.

> "Many nations will be joined with the Lord in that day and will become my people." (Zechariah 2:11)

Ultimately, this isn't only about the Jews. It's an invitation that transcends ethnic boundaries. The ancient enemies will one day be welcomed into God's family.

### The Curse Against Sin

The vision then pivots to chapter 5, where Zechariah sees a flying scroll:

> "I looked again, and there before me was a flying scroll. He asked me, 'What do you see?' I answered, 'I see a flying scroll, twenty cubits long and ten cubits wide.'" (Zechariah 5:1-2)

The dimensions—twenty by ten cubits (roughly 30 feet by 15 feet)—match exactly the entrance to the temple's portico described in 1 Kings 6:3. This is no random scroll but one sacred in its proportions.

> "This is the curse that is going out over the whole land; for according to what it says on one side, every thief will be banished, and according to what it says on the other, everyone who swears falsely will be banished." (Zechariah 5:3)

The scroll is double-sided, inscribing curses against two specific sins: theft and false oaths. Why these two? They represent sin in deed and sin in word—violations of the eighth and ninth commandments. Together, they symbolize the complete purification the new Jerusalem will require.

> "The Lord Almighty declares, 'I will send it out, and it will enter the house of the thief and the house of anyone who swears falsely by my name. It will remain in that house and destroy it completely, both its timbers and its stones.'" (Zechariah 5:4)

The city that is spacious and safe will also be righteous. There will be justice, purity, and freedom from wrongdoing.

## 3. God: The Presence That Transforms

### God Comes to Dwell

The vision crescendos with a promise of divine presence:

> "Shout and be glad, Daughter Zion. For I am coming, and I will live among you," declares the Lord. "Many nations will be joined with the Lord in that day and will become my people. I will live among you and you will know that the Lord Almighty has sent me to you. The Lord will inherit Judah as his portion in the holy land and will again choose Jerusalem. Be still before the Lord, all mankind, because he has roused himself from his holy dwelling." (Zechariah 2:10-13)

This is the climax: God will dwell among His people. He will return. 

In the Old Testament, God's holiness created distance from human sinfulness. Like the apple of the eye, His presence couldn't bear the touch of sin. But in Jesus, everything changes. Christ took upon Himself the violent reaction that sin deserves. Through His sacrifice, we are protected and purified, making it possible for God to dwell with us.

Today, God dwells with us through His Word and promises, writing His presence upon our minds and hearts. But the future holds something greater—a restoration of what was lost in Eden. God walking among us in visible, tangible communion.

The old temple system required a high priest to enter the Holy of Holies once yearly with sacrificial blood. Jesus did away with that system entirely, appearing once for all to take away sin—not through the blood of bulls and goats, but through Himself. He is the ultimate sacrifice, the final mediator, the bridge between heaven and earth.

Because of Jesus, we no longer need to approach God through intermediaries. We come with full confidence. We call Him Father. We're adopted into His family, made part of His household and living temple. We have full access through Christ's blood.

The temple came to us. And one day, it will be restored in fullness.

## Where Is Home?

### Heaven: The Final Home

> "Then I saw 'a new heaven and a new earth,' for the first heaven and the first earth had passed away, and there was no longer any sea. I saw the Holy City, the new Jerusalem, coming down out of heaven from God, prepared as a bride beautifully dressed for her husband. And I heard a loud voice from the throne saying, 'Look! God's dwelling place is now among the people, and he will dwell with them. They will be his people, and God himself will be with them and be their God.'" (Revelation 21:1-3)

In the vision of the new Jerusalem, we see all three elements converging: vast, spacious land (described again with specific measurements in Revelation 21:15); people from every nation, tribe, and tongue (Revelation 7:9-10); and supremely, the presence of God Himself—"no more death or mourning or crying or pain, for the old order of things has passed away" (Revelation 21:4).

Nothing impure will enter this city. All that defiles or deceives will be excluded. It will be safe, spacious, filled with God's glory, populated by the redeemed from all nations.

### Our Earthly Homes in Light of Eternity

Earthly homes are temporary shelters—practical spaces where we carry out God's work while we await that greater home. When you miss out on an auction or your property search doesn't pan out, remember what home really is. Your dream house is a rain shelter on a journey. Its purpose is to create space—space to invite friends, space for children to run and play, space to do God's work.

Similarly, church buildings serve a sacred function: gathering places where God's people assemble, invest in community, and prepare for heaven. Five years ago, when Arise Church began, we prayed for a space. Today, we still pray for a space. Not because a building defines us, but because space enables us to do God's work together.

Our true home awaits us—somewhere huge, somewhere safe, somewhere where God dwells. Until then, we steward the spaces we've been given, using them to love one another and point toward that eternal home where we will finally dwell with God Himself.
