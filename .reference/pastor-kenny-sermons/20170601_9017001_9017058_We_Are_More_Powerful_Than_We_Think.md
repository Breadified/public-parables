## The Stalemate: A War at Deadlock

The Philistines and Israelites faced each other across the Valley of Elah, each army occupying a hillside with a valley between them. In warfare, the high ground is crucial—whoever descends into the valley first loses that advantage. This geographical stalemate meant neither side could afford to make the first move.

To break the deadlock, Goliath proposed a solution: single combat. If Israel could field a champion to fight him one-on-one, the winner's nation would claim victory. It was a pragmatic solution that avoided unnecessary bloodshed, yet the stakes were absolute—enslavement or freedom for the entire nation.

> For forty days the Philistine came forward every morning and evening and took his stand.

For forty days, Goliath taunted Israel with his challenge. No Israelite warrior dared accept.

## David: Stronger Than We Think

### He Appears Weak

Everything about David screamed weakness:

- **The youngest of eight sons**, left behind while his three oldest brothers marched with Saul's army
- **A shepherd boy**, not a soldier—shepherding held low social rank in ancient Israel
- **Sent to bring provisions**: food, bread, and cheese for his brothers. He was the errand boy, the waterboy
- **His brothers despised him**, barely acknowledging his worth
- **No military experience**, which Saul emphasized: "You are only a young man, and he has been a warrior from his youth"

Even Goliath was unimpressed: "Am I a dog, that you come at me with sticks?" He saw only a young man, fair-skinned and handsome—words used disparagingly of men in Hebrew culture. A fair complexion suggested someone kept from labor and sun, sheltered rather than battle-hardened.

### But He Has Actually Grown

Yet time had passed since David was merely a child. While tending sheep, David had been quietly becoming strong. His father Jesse had grown old, suggesting years had passed. David had killed both a lion and a bear to protect his flock—feats requiring tremendous strength and courage.

> Your servant has been keeping his father's sheep. When a lion or a bear came and carried off a sheep from the flock, I went after it, struck it and rescued the sheep from its mouth. When it turned on me, I seized it by its hair, struck it and killed it. Your servant has killed both the lion and the bear.

Additional evidence of David's physical capability:

- **Saul offered him his armor**, and David tried it on. He didn't reject it because he was too small, but because he was unaccustomed to it—a matter of unfamiliarity, not weakness.
- **David wielded Goliath's sword after defeating him**, a massive weapon that required strength to handle.
- **He would later be called "a mighty man of valor, a man of war"** (1 Samuel 16:18)—a description earned through genuine strength and capability.

### He Possessed Both Strength and Strategy

David combined physical power with intelligence. His "good eyes"—a gift from childhood—became crucial. Rather than meeting Goliath in close combat, David chose a more advantageous approach:

> Then he took his staff in his hand, chose five smooth stones from the stream, put them in the pouch of his shepherd's bag and, with his sling in his hand, approached the Philistine.

A sling in skilled hands could deliver force equivalent to a small firearm. David used both brawn and brains, employing distance and precision where Goliath could only rely on strength.

### Most Importantly: God Was With Him

Yet David's true strength transcended physical prowess:

> David said to the Philistine, "You come against me with sword and spear and javelin, but I come against you in the name of the Lord Almighty, the God of the armies of Israel, whom you have defied."

David understood that the battle belonged to the Lord. His confidence wasn't arrogance—it was faith. He approached the conflict knowing he carried God's authority.

## Goliath: Weaker Than We Think

While Goliath appeared invincible, closer examination reveals significant weaknesses:

### Possible Gigantism

Goliath's extraordinary height—approximately nine feet—suggests he may have suffered from gigantism, a medical condition caused by excessive growth hormone from a pituitary gland tumor. This condition often produces a tragic side effect: tunnel vision. The growing tumor presses on the optic nerves, causing peripheral vision loss.

### Behavioral Clues

Several details support this theory:

- **"Come here," he called to David** rather than attacking from a distance. Poor vision would explain why he needed David closer.
- **He saw "sticks" instead of a sling and stones**, suggesting his vision was impaired or distorted.
- **He needed a shield bearer to guide him** until he got close enough to see clearly.
- **When David launched his stone, Goliath couldn't dodge it**—too slow, too blind, too reliant on a fighting style that required close proximity.

### Strategic Limitation

Goliath had one strategy: get close and overpower with brute force. When David refused to play by those rules, choosing distance and precision instead, Goliath had no effective response.

## The Application for Christians

### We Are Stronger Than We Think

Many Christians harbor a defeatist mentality. We feel besieged, outnumbered, weak against a hostile world. We think of ourselves as victims, powerless against the forces arrayed against us.

But this contradicts Scripture:

> Rejoice in the Lord always. I will say it again: Rejoice! Let your gentleness be evident to all. The Lord is near. Do not be anxious about anything, but in every situation, by prayer and petition, with thanksgiving, present your requests to God. And the peace of God, which transcends all understanding, will guard your hearts and your minds in Christ Jesus. (Philippians 4:4-7)

> I can do all this through him who gives me strength. (Philippians 4:13)

> No, in all these things we are more than conquerors through him who loved us. (Romans 8:37)

We are not helpless. We possess:

- **Spiritual resources**: God's presence and the Holy Spirit's empowerment
- **Moral principles**: the righteous decrees and laws of God
- **Physical advantages**: We live in one of the most resourced, educated, healthy societies in all of history
- **Collective strength**: unified in Christ

Yet we continue making excuses:

- "I can't change my neighborhood"
- "I can't influence government"
- "I can't earn more"
- "I can't evangelize"
- "I can't pass that exam"
- "I can't have more than one or two children"

### We Are Not Powerless

The problem isn't inability—it's perspective. We've been conditioned to see ourselves as weaker than we actually are.

### We Must Not Pretend to Be Smaller Than We Are

In response to persecution, many Christians try to make themselves invisible. We stay quiet. We hide our faith. We pretend to be pagans, hoping not to be noticed.

But this strategy fails. And it's not who we're called to be.

God takes us as we are, but He doesn't leave us that way—He transforms us. We grow stronger in faith, in purpose, in capability. To shrink from our calling, to pretend we're powerless, contradicts the work God has done in us.

## Christ: The Ultimate Victory

The David and Goliath narrative foreshadows Christ's triumph over evil:

- **Goliath's armor of scales** resembled a serpent—echoing Satan's form in Eden
- **Goliath taunted God's people for forty days**, paralleling Jesus's forty-day wilderness fast and testing
- **David defeated Goliath with a blow to the head**, fulfilling God's promise to Eve that the Messiah would crush Satan's head
- **David used Goliath's own sword** to finish the battle, as Christ uses His authority to overcome evil
- **The place of David's victory**, possibly remembered as Golgotha (Gol[iath of] Gath), became the place where Jesus's feet were pierced and Satan was ultimately defeated

Jesus is the "strong man" who entered enemy territory and bound Satan, plundering his house. Where David's victory was physical and temporary, Christ's victory is spiritual and eternal.

## The Call

God is not looking for perfection or prominence—He's looking for willingness.

David was nobody's first choice. But David was willing. He saw the insult to God's name and stepped forward when others cowered. God took that willingness and gave him strength—both physical and spiritual—to accomplish what seemed impossible.

The question for us is simple: **What's your excuse?**

We are stronger than we think. The enemy is weaker than he appears. God is with us.

Will you stand?
