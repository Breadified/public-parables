## Grit: The Foundation of Perseverance

Why do some people achieve their goals while others fail? Over a century ago, Francis Galton studied eminent figures across politics, sport, art, music, and science, concluding that high achievement results from three things: **ability** combined with **zeal** and the capacity for **hard labor**. When he shared this with his cousin Charles Darwin, Darwin replied that he had always believed that excepting fools, men do not differ much in intellect—only in zeal and hard work. When it comes to intelligence, we are pretty much the same. The difference is hard work.

More recently, psychologist Angela Lee Duckworth has coined a modern term for this: **grit**. As a teacher and later a professor at the University of Pennsylvania, she noticed that IQ was not a predictor of success in her students. What was predictive was grit—the combination of passion and perseverance, the ability to finish what you begin and to overcome setbacks. Her research across Ivy League undergraduates, West Point cadets, and national spelling bee finalists consistently showed that grittier individuals achieved more: higher GPAs, greater likelihood of completing difficult training, and sustained success across their chosen fields.

Duckworth concludes that perseverance may be as important, if not more important, than intelligence in determining success. Will Smith, one of Hollywood's highest earners, encapsulates this perfectly: "I've never really viewed myself as particularly talented. I've viewed myself as slightly above average in talent. And where I excel is ridiculous, sickening, work ethic." He continues: "The only thing that I see that is distinctly different about me is that I am not afraid to DIE on a treadmill. You might have more talent than me, you might be smarter than me, but if we get on a treadmill together, there are two things: 1. You are getting off first OR 2. I am gonna die—it's really that simple."

These are all modern names for **discipline**—and discipline is exactly what Paul addresses in Hebrews.

## God Disciplines His Children (Hebrews 12:4–11)

Throughout Hebrews, Paul urges the struggling church: persevere. Do not give up on God. In verse 4, we see their struggle:

> In your struggle against sin, you have not yet resisted to the point of shedding your blood.

The Hebrew Christians face a hard choice: whether to resist sin and suffering, even at great cost, or to fall away from their faith. Paul first rebukes them for not resisting strongly enough. Then, more importantly, he calls them to remember God's loving purposes in their struggle:

> And have you completely forgotten this word of encouragement that addresses you as a father addresses his son? It says, "My son, do not make light of the Lord's discipline, and do not lose heart when he rebukes you, because the Lord disciplines the one he loves, and he chastens everyone he accepts as his son." (Proverbs 3)
>
> Endure hardship as discipline; God is treating you as his children. For what children are not disciplined by their father?

The key insight: **life is one long training lesson by God the Father, done out of love**.

### Earthly Fathers and Discipline

We are all familiar with this pattern from our own childhood. From an early age, we are trained to do many things we initially dislike—using utensils, eating vegetables, waking on time, doing mathematics, exercising, attending school, doing chores, going to tutoring and swimming classes. Sometimes pain is involved: the naughty corner, scolding, physical punishment. As we grow older, the discipline changes form: no pocket money, being grounded, losing car privileges.

None of it feels pleasant at the time. Yet it stems from love. A loving parent knows that an undisciplined child becomes unable to function—unable to use a spoon, unable to wake on time, unable to count, unable to care for themselves. Discipline is what every loving parent does. Not disciplining is what bad parents do.

Verse 8 makes this stark:

> If you are not disciplined—and everyone undergoes discipline—then you are not legitimate, not true sons and daughters at all.

The picture is of the illegitimate child—the neglected child born outside wedlock, raised without a father's presence or training. Lack of discipline ruins a child. We see this in child poverty statistics across wealthy nations. The key predictor is not inequality or capitalism, but the presence of a stable mother and father. When 50% (in some countries, 75%) of children are born to single mothers, poverty and dysfunction cycle through generations. These children grow up untrained, disenfranchised, unemployed, and the cycle repeats. A child without discipline is commonly called "spoiled"—ruined, good for nothing.

For those still at home, you may not appreciate your parents' discipline now. But in time, you will thank them for it. It is part and parcel of their love.

### Comparison: Our Heavenly Father

> Moreover, we have all had human fathers who disciplined us and we respected them for it. How much more should we submit to the Father of spirits and live!

If we respect our earthly fathers for the discipline they instill in us, how much more ought we appreciate God the Father? We can recognize our human fathers' loving hand in their discipline. Then cannot we also see our heavenly Father's loving hand in our hardship and struggle? God is disciplining us for our good.

What is this good? Verses 10–11 reveal the contrast:

> They disciplined us for a little while as they thought best; but God disciplines us for our profit, in order that we may share in his holiness. No discipline seems pleasant at the time, but painful. Later on, however, it produces a harvest of righteousness and peace for those who have been trained by it.

Our earthly fathers did as they **seemed** best to them—their judgment was limited. God, with infinite knowledge, disciplines us for our profit and for us to share in his holiness. Earthly discipline is **temporary**—"a little while"—and seems painful. But God's discipline produces an **eternal harvest of righteousness and peace**.

The contrast is between appearance and reality, temporary and eternal. What seems sorrowful and painful in the moment actually trains us for holiness and produces peace. If this is true, how much more should we submit to God's discipline?

### Two Areas to Reset Our Expectations

#### Why Am I Suffering?

Often we, or those we love, become jaded with God over suffering in our own lives. One answer is that suffering results from sin—whether personal sin (the direct consequence of my actions), global sin (I suffer from others' greed even if I can't point fingers), or the natural state of a broken world groaning since Adam's fall.

But there is hope. Another complementary answer is that **God uses present sufferings as training for the Christian**. As the saying goes, "Pain is weakness leaving the body." Scripture offers its own versions:

> Our present sufferings are not worth comparing with the glory that will be revealed in us. (Romans 8:18)
>
> God works for the good of those who love him, who have been called according to his purpose. (Romans 8:28)
>
> No temptation has overtaken you except what is common to mankind. And God is faithful; he will not let you be tempted beyond what you can bear. But when you are tempted, he will also provide a way out so that you can endure it. (1 Corinthians 10:13)

No matter what happens, God promises we will be better trained because of it. Rather than view challenges negatively, remember: **God is Father. He is acting in love.** Under the safety net of heaven, every hardship becomes a chance to be holy and godly, to understand the love of Christ, to appreciate our lives on earth, and to serve others.

#### Two Real Examples

**John Piper** famously wrote "don't waste your cancer." Diagnosed with cancer, Piper saw his illness as a gift. It trained him to trust God more deeply, to seek comfort in God rather than odds, to contemplate death, to read Scripture more seriously, to take sin seriously (cancer destroys appetite for pride, greed, lust, hatred, laziness, impatience), and to reconsider life's purpose. He resolved to cherish Christ and witness to others. Piper viewed cancer not as a disability, but as a **training opportunity**.

**Nick Vujicic** was born in Australia with tetra-amelia syndrome—no arms or legs. At age eight, he struggled with God: "Why did you take my arms and legs away? Why not give me what everybody has?" His struggle was justified. But rather than despair, Nick reconciled with God and became thankful for what he does have. He submitted to God's will. Instead of asking God for a miracle, he became a miracle to others' salvation. He has a cheerful disposition, does everything 100%, is married, recently became a father, jumps out of airplanes, and swims. Because he has no arms and no legs, God has used him to bring approximately 200,000 souls to Jesus. Nick says: "What would you rather? I would rather have no arms and no legs temporarily on earth in order to reach someone else for Christ and spend eternity with them."

What might look like tragedy actually trained him for his calling. He could have been just another spoiled child, but God trained him for service.

## Be Disciplined (Hebrews 12:12–13:6)

Therefore, take the challenges of life onboard and be disciplined.

> Strengthen your feeble arms and weak knees. Make level paths for your feet, so that the lame may not be disabled, but rather healed.

This is a metaphor for our **spiritual arms and knees**. Stand up, hands raised, ready for God. Walk a straight path. Do not turn aside from the truth to false teaching, do not fall away.

### Spiritual Exercises: Daily and Weekly Habits

Exercise daily and weekly with God's word. Cultivate habits like Bible reading, prayer, speaking and thinking about God, and attending church. It may feel strange when new to the faith, but over time these habits build strength. Just as abdominal crunches build core muscles, these spiritual habits help keep your paths straight.

### See to It That No One Falls Short (Hebrews 12:14–15)

> Make every effort to live in peace with everyone and to be holy; without holiness no one will see the Lord. See to it that no one falls short of the grace of God and that no bitter root grows up to cause trouble and defile many.

Do not fall short of God's grace. Do not turn aside.

### See to It No One Forfeits (Hebrews 12:16–17)

> See that no one is sexually immoral, or is godless like Esau, who for a single meal sold his inheritance rights as the oldest son. Afterward, as you know, when he wanted to inherit this blessing, he was rejected. Even though he sought the blessing with tears, he could not change what he had done.

Esau, as the oldest son, had his inheritance guaranteed. But when he was tired and hungry, Jacob offered him a bowl of lentil soup for it. Esau disregarded his birthright, and when he later wanted it, it was too late. He faced regret for giving away something precious for something so small.

A modern parallel: In 2010, programmer Laszlo Hanyecz traded 10,000 bitcoins for two pizzas. At the time, bitcoins seemed worthless. But years later, those bitcoins were worth $7 million—a million dollars per slice. He gave away something precious for almost nothing. The regret must be immense.

**Heaven is worth the world. Never sell that inheritance.**

## You Have Not Come (Hebrews 12:18–21)

> You have not come to a mountain that can be touched and that is burning with fire; to darkness, gloom and storm; to a trumpet blast or to such a voice speaking words that those who heard it begged that no further word be spoken to them, because they could not bear what was commanded: "If even an animal touches the mountain, it must be stoned to death." The sight was so terrifying that Moses said, "I am trembling with fear."

This is Mount Sinai, 1,500 years before Jesus. It represents the old covenant—an earthly, fearful encounter with God's law. The contrast continues:

> At that time his voice shook the earth, but now he has promised, "Once more I will shake not only the earth but also the heavens." The words "once more" indicate the removing of what can be shaken—that is, created things—so that what cannot be shaken may remain. Therefore, since we are receiving a kingdom that cannot be shaken, let us be thankful, and so worship God acceptably with reverence and awe, for our "God is a consuming fire."

## For You Have Come (Hebrews 12:22–24)

> But you have come to Mount Zion, to the city of the living God, the heavenly Jerusalem. You have come to thousands upon thousands of angels in joyful assembly, to the church of the firstborn, whose names are written in heaven. You have come to God, the Judge of all, to the spirits of the righteous made perfect, to Jesus the mediator of a new covenant, and to the sprinkled blood that speaks a better word than Abel.

**You have come to heavenly property. You have come to salvation.** You have followed in the steps of the ancient heroes of old—to Christ, to the one who speaks a better word.

God speaks a better word than Abel. Abel's was the first death recorded. Jesus's death is the final death that will put an end to all deaths. As Hebrews 1:1–2 says:

> In the past God spoke to our ancestors through the prophets at many times and in various ways, but in these last days he has spoken to us by his Son, whom he appointed heir of all things, and through whom also he made the universe.

This word is now within us who live by faith. Hebrews 4:12 describes it:

> The word of God is alive and active. Sharper than any double-edged sword, it penetrates even to dividing soul and spirit, joints and marrow; it judges the thoughts and attitudes of the heart.

God's word is no longer outside us, cutting from afar. It penetrates our marrow, changing us for the better.

## Do Not Refuse the Speaker (Hebrews 12:25–29)

> See to it that you do not refuse him who speaks. If they did not escape when they refused him who warned them on earth, how much less will we if we turn away from him who warns us from heaven?

How much greater is the revelation in Christ? How much more punished we will be if we turn away. Christ has come. He has revealed all. He died for us. We are not to be spoiled brats who chuck back in God's face what he has given. We are to persevere despite hardship. We are to listen to God and his instruction.

**The character of a Christian is faithfulness, commitment—grit.** To be a disciple is to have discipline. Righteousness and holiness now demand self-control, generosity, compassion, and service—all requiring great willpower when confronted by the world's distractions.

## Living the Disciplined Life (Hebrews 13:1–6)

Chapter 13 outlines practices requiring discipline:

> **Keep on loving one another as brothers and sisters.**
>
> **Do not forget to show hospitality to strangers, for by so doing some people have shown hospitality to angels without knowing it.**
>
> **Continue to remember those in prison as if you were together with them in prison, and those who are mistreated as if you yourselves were suffering.**
>
> **Marriage should be honored by all, and the marriage bed kept pure, for God will judge the adulterer and all the sexually immoral.**
>
> **Keep your lives free from the love of money and be content with what you have, because God has said, "Never will I leave you; never will I forsake you." So we say with confidence, "The Lord is my helper; I will not be afraid. What can mere mortals do to me?"**

These practices require discipline. Yet many of us struggle with discipline in all areas of life.

### A Generation Without Discipline

For Generation Y (those born 1982–1999), lack of discipline has reached crisis point. Youth unemployment approaches 40% because employers avoid Gen Y—we cannot stick with commitments. We arrive late or not at all. We don't show up to interviews. We post our absence on Facebook instead of calling. We resign by SMS. When faced with difficulties, we complain and blame the government.

We are not even mastering basic prerequisites—half of university students don't finish their courses. We cannot cook, cannot iron a shirt. We exhibit little of the grit previous generations showed.

This flows into all life areas. We are not loving—how is breaking promises to be there or be on time loving to another person? Half our marriages end in divorce. And it flows to our Christian life: we become flaky, treating God as just another optional "maybe" button on the Facebook of life.

### Discipline at Church

Perhaps this is why our church emphasizes certain habits: turning up regularly, serving consistently and diligently, informing others if we cannot attend, apologizing with good reason. From outside, it can seem painful and legalistic. But what we are trying to do is **train in discipline—to keep promises we have made, to honor commitments**.

Your leaders act out of love. Church is voluntary, and we are saved by grace. But for those committed to the church, think of it like SES (State Emergency Service) volunteers. They are not paid. They have other jobs. But they commit to training, to responding to callouts, to team discipline. When there's a fire, they drop everything. They have committed, and it means sacrifice.

Similarly, if we're convinced of the need for others to know Christ, we should be equally committed to serving. Surely middle-aged volunteers can prioritize church as seriously as they do fighting bushfires.

## Now Is the Time (Hebrews 12 Conclusion)

As Woody Allen said, "80% of success in life is showing up."

We see improvements in the church: people committed to attending regularly, young people serving, some pursuing driving licenses, others finding work. We do this not just out of love for those organizing it or for those relying on you, not just to make ourselves more reliable and employable, but **for our spiritual life**.

If you cannot prioritize God's things as a student, how will you when working and your boss is chasing you? If you cannot do it while working, how will you with a young family—spouse to please, sick kids, mortgage to pay? If you cannot do it as student, worker, or parent, why would you change after a lifetime of alternate habits? You will spend retirement on golf, not God.

**NOW IS THE TIME TO GET DISCIPLINE.**

**THE PRESENT IS THE TIME TO GET DISCIPLINED.**

**PERSEVERE!**

For HSC students: prioritize God's things even now. For Year 11s: train yourselves now. Now is not the time to be complacent or spoiled. Now is the time to be trained for righteousness and holiness, for self-control and perseverance. Your formative years as a student are crucial. Good habits last a lifetime. Young families: these formative years determine not just your family, but whether you will be a Christian family with certain values, habits, and disciplines.

As Angela Lee Duckworth says: "If it's important for you to become one of the best people in your field, you are going to have to stick with it when it's hard."

Or as Will Smith says: "I am not afraid to die on that treadmill."

**You are in the race of your life. Finish it.**
