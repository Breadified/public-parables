# Psalm 18

**Title:** This Year, I Will
**Date:** 8 January 2011
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction: New Year Resolutions

As we enter a new year, we naturally make resolutions. We commit to eating less, exercising more, spending time with our children, being kinder to our parents. But the Bible encourages us to make a more profound resolution: to prepare ourselves for times of trouble.

The psalmist David knew these troubles intimately. Psalm 18 records his words during a season of severe distress—specifically, during his persecution by King Saul, a time of civil war documented in 2 Samuel 22. This psalm invites us to discover how God sustains us through our darkest seasons.

## 1. In Times of Trouble: God is Our Refuge

David opens with a declaration of dependence:

> I love you, O LORD, my strength. The LORD is my rock, my fortress and my deliverer; my God is my rock, in whom I take refuge. He is my shield and the horn of my salvation, my stronghold. I call to the LORD, who is worthy of praise, and I am saved from my enemies. (Psalm 18:1-3)

When distress overwhelms him, David cries out:

> The cords of death entangled me; the torrents of destruction overwhelmed me. The cords of the grave coiled around me; the snares of death confronted me. In my distress I called to the LORD; I cried to my God for help. From his temple he heard my voice; my cry came before him, into his ears. (Psalm 18:4-6)

### The Cosmic Scope of God's Rescue

David describes God's intervention in terms so grand they transcend his earthly battle. He compares his deliverance to three cosmic rescues: from earth itself, from heaven, and from the sea.

**The Earth Trembles:**

> The earth trembled and quaked, and the foundations of the mountains shook; they trembled because he was angry. Smoke rose from his nostrils; consuming fire came from his mouth, burning coals blazed out of it. (Psalm 18:7-8)

**The Heavens Respond:**

> He parted the heavens and came down; dark clouds were under his feet. He mounted the cherubim and flew; he soared on the wings of the wind. He made darkness his covering, his canopy around him—the dark rain clouds of the sky. Out of the brightness of his presence clouds advanced, with hailstones and bolts of lightning. The LORD thundered from heaven; the voice of the Most High resounded. He shot his arrows and scattered the enemies, great bolts of lightning and routed them. (Psalm 18:9-14)

**The Seas Are Exposed:**

> The valleys of the sea were exposed and the foundations of the earth laid bare at your rebuke, O LORD, at the blast of breath from your nostrils. He reached down from on high and took hold of me; he drew me out of deep waters. He rescued me from my powerful enemy, from my foes, who were too strong for me. (Psalm 18:15-17)

David's imagery captures the terror of his situation. Having grown up in Malaysia where the ocean is calm and protected, the idea of waves so powerful they expose the valleys of the sea seems almost incomprehensible—yet that is how overwhelming his troubles felt.

### The Victory That Follows

Verses 28-45 contain vivid war imagery, describing God's empowerment:

> You, O LORD, keep my lamp burning; my God turns my darkness into light. With your help I can advance against a troop; with my God I can scale a wall. As for God, his way is perfect; the word of the LORD is flawless. He is a shield for all who take refuge in him. For who is God besides the LORD? And who is the Rock except our God? It is God who arms me with strength and makes my way perfect. He makes my feet like the feet of a deer; he enables me to stand on the heights. He trains my hands for battle; my arms can bend a bow of bronze. (Psalm 18:28-34)

God grants David complete victory:

> You give me your shield of victory, and your right hand sustains me; you stoop down to make me great. You broaden the path beneath me, so that my ankles do not turn. I pursued my enemies and overtook them; I did not turn back till they were destroyed. I crushed them so that they could not rise; they fell beneath my feet. You armed me with strength for battle; you made my adversaries bow at my feet. You made my enemies turn their backs in flight, and I destroyed my foes. They cried for help, but there was no one to save them—to the LORD, but he did not answer. I beat them as fine as dust borne on the wind; I poured them out like mud in the streets. You have delivered me from the attacks of the people; you have made me the head of nations; people I did not know are subject to me. As soon as they hear me, they obey me; foreigners cringe before me. They all lose heart; they come trembling from their strongholds. (Psalm 18:35-45)

### Application: Our Troubles Are Real

We face our own troubles—some small, some enormous. Whether minor or major, they feel cosmic to us when we're in the midst of them. Just as David experienced his civil war as earthquakes and cosmic upheaval, we experience our trials as shattering events.

Who will rescue us? God will. We need not fear.

Though we cannot demand that God answer according to our preferences, His promise stands firm. He will care for us—if not in this life, then in the next. As Paul assures us:

> What, then, shall we say in response to this? If God is for us, who can be against us? He who did not spare his own Son, but gave him up for us all—how will he not also, along with him, graciously give us all things? Who will bring any charge against those whom God has chosen? It is God who justifies. Who is he that condemns? Christ Jesus, who died—more than that, who was raised to life—is at the right hand of God and is also interceding for us. Who shall separate us from the love of Christ? Shall trouble or hardship or persecution or famine or nakedness or danger or sword? No, in all these things we are more than conquerors through him who loved us. For I am convinced that neither death nor life, neither angels nor demons, neither the present nor the future, nor any powers, neither height nor depth, nor anything else in all creation, will be able to separate us from the love of God that is in Christ Jesus our Lord. (Romans 8:31-39)

## 2. I Am Righteous: The Paradox of Grace

David then makes a surprising claim:

> He brought me out into a spacious place; he rescued me because he delighted in me. The LORD has dealt with me according to my righteousness; according to the cleanness of my hands he has rewarded me. For I have kept the ways of the LORD; I have not done evil by turning from my God. All his laws are before me; I have not turned away from his decrees. I have been blameless before him and have kept myself from sin. The LORD has rewarded me according to my righteousness, according to the cleanness of my hands in his sight. (Psalm 18:19-24)

David declares God's reward structure:

> To the faithful you show yourself faithful, to the blameless you show yourself blameless, to the pure you show yourself pure, but to the crooked you show yourself shrewd. You save the humble but bring low those whose eyes are haughty. (Psalm 18:25-27)

### The Resolution of the Paradox

This language seems troubling. It suggests God rewards us based on our righteousness. Yet we know this contradicts the heart of the Gospel: we are saved by grace, not by our works (Ephesians 2:9). God loves us despite our sinfulness, not because of our goodness.

Moreover, David himself is not righteous by his deeds. His life is stained with blood. He takes multiple wives contrary to God's law. He commits adultery with Bathsheba and orchestrates the murder of her husband. How can David claim to be righteous?

The answer lies in understanding righteousness not as moral perfection, but as *trust in God*. David is righteous because he believes God. Notice what David does: In verse 2, he puts his trust entirely in God's protection. In verse 6, he cries out to God with confidence that God will rescue him.

**The definition of a righteous person is one who trusts in God.**

The writer of Hebrews confirms this understanding:

> In bringing many sons to glory, it was fitting that God, for whom and through whom everything exists, should make the author of their salvation perfect through suffering. Both the one who makes men holy and those who are made holy are of the same family. So Jesus is not ashamed to call them brothers. He says, "I will declare your name to my brothers; in the presence of the congregation I will sing your praises." And again, "I will put my trust in him." (Hebrews 2:10-13)

Those who are made holy—those who are righteous—are characterized by trust. Because of that faith in God, He considers them righteous. God treats the sinner as His friend.

### Application: The Heart of Christian Identity

This is the definition of a Christian: we are sinners who are considered righteous because of our trust in God. Because we are God's friends through faith, He protects and cares for us.

Are you a Christian? If you trust in God, the comfort of this psalm belongs to you as well.

## 3. Because Christ Is Righteous: The True Hero of the Psalm

Yet a question remains: How can God treat sinners as righteous without being blind to our sin? Is He ignoring David's murders? His adultery? His unfaithfulness?

No. God is not blind to our sin. What God sees when He looks at us is Jesus.

### Jesus as the Anointed Descendant

The psalm concludes with a forward-looking promise:

> The LORD lives! Praise be to my Rock! Exalted be God my Savior! He is the God who avenges me, who subdues nations under me, who saves me from my enemies. You exalted me above my foes; from violent men you rescued me. Therefore I will praise you among the nations, O LORD; I will sing praises to your name. He gives his king great victories; he shows unfailing kindness to his anointed, to David and his descendants forever. (Psalm 18:46-50)

That final verse hints at a descendant of David—one who will enjoy eternal favor. That man is Jesus, the anointed one, who defeats the enemies of sin and death, who brings every nation to bow the knee in worship.

### Jesus Is the Truly Righteous One

Strictly speaking, Psalm 18 is not about David. It is about Jesus. Jesus is the only man who can genuinely declare verses 19 onward. At His baptism, God the Father said, "This is the one in whom I delight" (Matthew 3:17).

But here is the gospel's wonder: this truly righteous man dies for the unrighteous. On the cross, He exchanged His righteousness for our unrighteousness. When God looks at us now, He no longer sees a sinner—He sees Jesus, who has forgiven our sins.

### Illustration: The Gift Exchange

Imagine a Christmas gift exchange game where everyone brings one present and places it under the tree anonymously. As the party unfolds, people choose gifts in turn. Sometimes you receive something worthless—expired chocolates, old soap. Rarely, you might find something valuable—movie tickets, an expensive book, a gift card.

Now imagine a variation: if you've selected a terrible gift, you can exchange it for someone else's better gift, leaving them with the dud.

In the Christmas party of life, we have lived our lives as a worthless present. Our lives are expired chocolates and old soap—tasteless, used up, worthless. Jesus, by contrast, is the valuable movie ticket. What He does on the cross is extraordinary: He takes our lousy, sin-stained lives and gives us His perfect life in exchange. He takes the dud; we receive the ticket to see God.

You can have this ticket by trusting in Jesus.

## Conclusion: This Year, I Will

What will be your New Year's resolution?

None of us knows what this year will bring. Perhaps goodness and blessing. Perhaps suffering and sorrow. Perhaps death in the family. Perhaps loss of employment. Perhaps crises that seem earth-shattering, heaven-falling, and sea-parting.

But make this resolution: **Trust in Jesus.**

Because He is righteous, and because He has given us His righteousness through faith, we need fear nothing. Whether in triumph or in trial, we can declare with David: "The LORD is my rock, my fortress and my deliverer."
