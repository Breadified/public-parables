# 1 Samuel 8:1-22

**Title:** The Kingship
**Date:** 21 May 2017
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Introduction

Samuel has grown old and, like many leaders contemplating their legacy, he appoints his sons as Israel's judges. However, this seemingly practical succession sets in motion a crisis that reveals something far deeper than a political problem—it exposes a fundamental rejection of God's kingship.

> "And the Lord told him: Listen to all that the people are saying to you; it is not you they have rejected, but they have rejected me as their king." (1 Samuel 8:7)

The question before Israel—and before us—is not merely whether human kings are a good idea. The real issue is whether we will trust God as our ultimate King, or whether we will seek security, control, and independence in other sources.

---

## Part 1: The Problem of Kingship

### The Flaw of Hereditary Rule

Samuel's downfall mirrors that of Eli before him. Both grew old and appointed sons as leaders—sons who did not walk in their fathers' ways.

> "When Samuel grew old, he appointed his sons as Israel's leaders. The name of his firstborn was Joel and the name of his second was Abijah, and they served at Beersheba. But his sons did not follow his ways. They turned aside after dishonest gain and accepted bribes and perverted justice." (1 Samuel 8:1-3)

This directly contradicts the clear instruction God gave for choosing judges:

> "Appoint judges and officials for each of your tribes in every town the Lord your God is giving you, and they shall judge the people fairly. Do not pervert justice or show partiality. Do not accept a bribe, for a bribe blinds the eyes of the wise and twists the words of the innocent." (Deuteronomy 16:18-19)

The core problem with hereditary kingship is devastating in its simplicity: **succession is based on bloodline, not character or godliness**. A prince can be wicked or righteous, but he will be king regardless. Godly merit is replaced by mere accident of birth.

When Gideon was tempted to establish a dynasty, he refused:

> "But Gideon told them, 'I will not rule over you, nor will my son rule over you. The Lord will rule over you.'" (Judges 8:23)

Gideon understood something crucial: hereditary kingship undermines the very structure God established. In God's design, judges ruled locally while God ruled federally over all Israel. There was a hierarchy of appeal—local judges handled disputes, the priests consulted on difficult cases, and ultimately, God's law was supreme.

A hereditary king eliminates this arrangement. Instead of looking to God when crisis comes, the nation looks to a single human ruler. **The choice is taken out of God's hands.**

### Imitating the Nations

The people's demand reveals their true desire:

> "They said to him, 'You are old, and your sons do not follow your ways; now appoint a king to lead us, such as all the other nations have.'" (1 Samuel 8:5)

But Israel was called to be fundamentally different:

> "From the rocky peaks I see them, from the heights I view them. I see a people who live apart and do not consider themselves one of the nations." (Numbers 23:9)

God had explicitly warned:

> "When you enter the land the Lord your God is giving you, do not learn to imitate the detestable ways of the nations there." (Deuteronomy 18:9)

Israel was to be distinct culturally, ethically, economically—in every aspect of life. They were a holy people, separate and set apart. Yet repeatedly throughout their history, they looked longingly at other nations and envied their ways, forgetting their unique calling.

The people's real concern was military security:

> "Then we will be like all the other nations, with a king to lead us and to go out before us and fight our battles." (1 Samuel 8:20)

This is understandable. Israel was a small nation surrounded by enemies. The judges provided leadership, but they did not maintain a standing army. Now the people wanted permanent military protection, a king with royal power to command an organized force.

Yet this desire stems from **spiritual distrust**. Throughout the book of Judges, God repeatedly delivered Israel without standing armies—indeed, often with miraculous intervention when human strength seemed impossible. The people's call for a king is not merely a political preference; it is a cry for independence from God.

> "And the Lord told him: Listen to all that the people are saying to you; it is not you they have rejected, but they have rejected me as their king." (1 Samuel 8:7)

### The Consequence: Slavery

God permits Samuel to warn the people what kingship truly entails:

> "This is what the king who will reign over you will claim as his rights: He will take your sons and make them serve with his chariots and horses, and they will run in front of his chariots. Some he will assign to be commanders of thousands and commanders of fifties, and others to plow his ground and reap his harvest, and still others to make weapons of war and equipment for his chariots. He will take your daughters to be perfumers and cooks and bakers. He will take the best of your fields and vineyards and olive groves and give them to his attendants. He will take a tenth of your grain and of your vintage and give it to his officials and attendants. Your male and female servants and the best of your cattle and donkeys he will take for his own use. He will take a tenth of your flocks, and you yourselves will become his slaves." (1 Samuel 8:11-17)

This is a devastating picture. The king will conscript their sons for war, forcing them into the infantry—the front line where they may die. He will centralize the economy, taking the productive parts for himself and his attendants. He will impose heavy taxation—a tenth of everything. He will take the best land, the best animals, the best people.

> "You yourselves will become his slaves." (1 Samuel 8:17)

This is not democracy or shared governance. This is feudalism—a system where your entire life exists to serve the king's welfare, his comfort, his enjoyment. You receive just enough to survive, just as a farmer feeds livestock to keep them productive.

And when the people cry out under this oppression, God will not answer:

> "When that day comes, you will cry out for relief from the king you have chosen, but the Lord will not answer you in that day." (1 Samuel 8:18)

They will have returned to Egypt—the very slavery from which God delivered them. **If you choose to be like other nations, you will experience what other nations experience: oppression and bondage.**

## Part 2: The Promise of Kingship

### God Permits What He Does Not Endorse

Despite the warning, the people refuse to listen. God, with apparent reluctance, grants their request:

> "But the people refused to listen to Samuel. No! they said. We want a king over us. Then we will be like all the other nations, with a king to lead us and to go out before us and fight our battles. When Samuel heard all that the people said, he repeated it before the Lord. The Lord answered, Listen to them and give them a king." (1 Samuel 8:19-22)

Why does God relent? There are several possibilities. One is that God gives them over to their sin, as Paul describes in Romans 1—allowing people to experience the natural consequences of rejecting Him. 

Another possibility, drawn from Deuteronomy, is more nuanced: God anticipated that Israel might spiral into such chaos and corruption that a strong hand would be necessary to restrain evil. The book of Judges ends with a repeated, haunting refrain:

> "In those days Israel had no king; everyone did as they saw fit." (Judges 21:25)

This phrase carries double meaning. If everyone were righteous and disciplined by the law of God written on their hearts, no external king would be needed. But when a society descends into chaos—idolatry, sexual perversion, murder—a centralized authority with coercive power becomes necessary to maintain order. A king can restrain evil through force.

### The King Unlike All Others

But here is where God's wisdom breaks through. In Deuteronomy, God had already anticipated this moment and provided a vision of kingship radically different from all other nations:

> "When you enter the land the Lord your God is giving you and have taken possession of it and settled in it, and you may say, 'Let us set a king over us like all the nations around us,' be sure to appoint over you a king the Lord your God chooses. He must be from among your fellow Israelites. Do not place a foreigner over you, one who is not an Israelite." (Deuteronomy 17:14-15)

God's king would not be like the kings of other nations. Instead:

- **The Lord chooses him** (not heredity or conquest)
- **He must be from Israel** (maintaining the nation's separation from foreign powers)
- **He will not accumulate horses for himself or make the people return to Egypt to get them** (no military alliances with Egypt)
- **He will not take many wives** (no political marriages binding him to foreign powers)
- **He will not accumulate great amounts of silver and gold** (no personal enrichment or corrupt taxation)
- **He will write for himself a copy of the law and read it all his days** (submission to God's word, not above it)
- **He will not consider himself better than his fellow Israelites** (a constitutional limitation on power)

This is a king unlike any earthly monarch—a king under the law of God, not above it.

### The Ultimate Fulfillment in Jesus

The rest of 1 Samuel traces the search for such a king, and Israel's failures with Saul, David, and their descendants. But the ultimate fulfillment comes in Jesus Christ:

> "Jesus went into Galilee, proclaiming the good news of God. The time has come, he said. The kingdom of God has come near. Repent and believe the good news!" (Mark 1:14-15)

Jesus is the King that God's law anticipated:

- He did not tax the citizenry; instead, he gave freely—bread and fish to the hungry.
- He rode not on horses but on a donkey, a symbol of peace rather than military might.
- Rather than sending men to their deaths in battle, he stood in front of them as their defender. In Gethsemane, when his enemies came to arrest him, he protected his disciples even as he allowed himself to be taken.
- He fought the true battle—against sin and death itself—suffering a lonely death on behalf of his subjects.

This King has risen and ascended to heaven's throne. Every knee will bow before Him. And we await His return to judge the living and the dead.

In that day, there will be no need for earthly government or human kings. The law of God will be written on every heart. Every person will be ruled by Christ's perfect justice and love. Until then, Christ alone is our King in everything.

## Application for Our Time

How does this ancient political crisis speak to us? On the surface, it seems to endorse the Republican argument: hereditary kingship is unjust; the people should choose their leaders; freedom of choice matters.

But the sermon goes deeper. As we look at our own modern governments:

- We see massive military machines engaged in wars far from home
- We see centrally planned economies where government decides which industries should thrive through subsidies and regulations
- We see government dictating education, telling citizens which schools to fund and which teaching methods are acceptable
- We see taxation far exceeding the 10% that Samuel warned about, even as public services are cut

In many ways, we have become the very slaves Samuel warned Israel against. We are socially pressured to conform to government-approved thought, politically correct speech, and a narrow range of acceptable views. **We are well on our way to slavery.**

But here is what must be understood: **This is not primarily a political problem. It is a theological problem.**

We treat government as the solution to our deepest problems. We look to government to solve medical crises, housing shortages, environmental disasters. We invest our hope in human institutions, forgetting that humans are limited, fallible, and prone to corruption.

Some blame economics, politics, demographics, education, or media for our troubles. These are symptoms, not causes. The root cause is that **as a nation, we have rejected God as king**. And so we replace Him with godless rulers and reap the consequences. When we act like other nations, we get treated like other nations.

Our solutions must be found in God and His word. That confidence has brought us out of barbarism and poverty. It will continue to do so if we would but trust it. **Our ultimate hope cannot rest in earthly kings or governments—only in Christ as our King.**

This does not mean the form of government doesn't matter. A godly king, a godly democracy, a godly constitutional monarchy—any of these can be good if led by those who fear God and seek His will. But a godless government, regardless of its structure, will produce oppression and suffering.

Interestingly, Queen Elizabeth II, in her 2011 Christmas message, captured something of this truth:

> "Although we are capable of great acts of kindness, history teaches us that we sometimes need saving from ourselves—from our recklessness or our greed. God sent into the world a unique person—neither a philosopher nor a general, important though they are, but a Saviour, with the power to forgive... It is my prayer that on this Christmas day we might all find room in our lives for the message of the angels and for the love of God through Christ our Lord."

A modern monarch acknowledging her need for a Savior, recognizing that Jesus Christ is the King she serves—perhaps there is something redemptive in that.

Our calling is clear: to seek first the kingdom of God and His righteousness, to submit ourselves to Christ as King, and to trust His word as our ultimate authority. Only then will we find true freedom, true justice, and true peace.
