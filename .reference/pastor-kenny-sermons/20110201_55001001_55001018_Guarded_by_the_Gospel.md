# 2 Timothy 1:1-18

**Title:** Guarded by the Gospel  
**Date:** 2 February 2011  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction: Culture and Conviction

Culture shapes us in powerful ways. Growing up in Malaysia, Chinese New Year involved deep rituals passed down through generations—sweeping before the celebration, wearing new clothes, visiting parents first thing in the morning for red packets, decorations on doors. Yet here in Australia, where fewer people observe these traditions, the cultural pressure feels lighter. We're freed from conformity and relax into it more easily.

Similarly, Christmas and New Year have their own cultural rituals here. But what happens when you're in a culture very different from Christianity? How should we live out our faith when the surrounding culture doesn't support it? This is the question Paul addresses in his letter to Timothy.

## The Letter's Context

Paul writes from prison—likely Rome—to Timothy, whom he describes as "my dear son" (v. 2). Their relationship was deeply personal. Paul had met Timothy on a missionary journey, taken him along, and even had him circumcised to make him acceptable to Jewish audiences. Now Timothy serves in Ephesus, and Paul addresses the real cultural pressures Timothy faces.

> 2 Timothy 1:1-2  
> Paul, an apostle of Christ Jesus by the will of God, according to the promise of life that is in Christ Jesus, to Timothy, my dear son: Grace, mercy and peace from God the Father and Christ Jesus our Lord.

## 1. Do Not Be Ashamed to Testify

### The Spirit We've Been Given

> 2 Timothy 1:7-8  
> For God did not give us a spirit of timidity, but a spirit of power, of love and of self-discipline. So do not be ashamed to testify about our Lord, or ashamed of me his prisoner.

Notice the progression in verse 7. The Greek word for "timidity" (deilia) means cowardice—a spiritual paralysis. Instead, God gives us three things:

- **Power** (dunamis)—strength and ability
- **Love** (agape)—genuine, self-giving care
- **Self-discipline** (swfronismos)—sound judgment and wise self-control

These aren't just emotional states; they're spiritual attitudes that shape how we live.

### The Gospel as God's Power

Paul wants Timothy to understand what he's really testifying about:

> 2 Timothy 1:8-10  
> But join with me in suffering for the gospel, by the power of God, who has saved us and called us to a holy life—not because of anything we have done but because of his own purpose and grace. This grace was given us in Christ Jesus before the beginning of time, but it has now been revealed through the appearing of our Savior, Christ Jesus, who has destroyed death and has brought life and immortality to light through the gospel.

The gospel is good news about Jesus Christ, our Savior. Notice what Paul emphasizes:

- Salvation is **not** earned by our deeds
- It flows from **God's sovereign election and purpose**, His grace given to us in Christ before time began
- It's revealed through Christ's appearing
- Christ has **destroyed death itself** and brought eternal life to light

This is the power behind all our testimony. Paul echoes Romans 1:

> Romans 1:16-17  
> I am not ashamed of the gospel, because it is the power of God for the salvation of everyone who believes: first for the Jew, then for the Gentile. For in the gospel a righteousness from God is revealed, a righteousness that is by faith from first to last, just as it is written: "The righteous will live by faith."

The gospel itself is the power—not our eloquence, not our preparation, not our perfect circumstances. It's the gospel.

### A Legacy of Faith

Paul reminds Timothy of something crucial—he didn't invent this faith:

> 2 Timothy 1:3-6  
> I thank God, whom I serve, as my forefathers did, with a clear conscience, as night and day I constantly remember you in my prayers. Recalling your tears, I long to see you, so that I may be filled with joy. I have been reminded of your sincere faith, which first lived in your grandmother Lois and in your mother Eunice and, I am persuaded, now lives in you also. For this reason I remind you to fan into flame the gift of God, which is in you through the laying on of my hands.

Timothy's grandmother and mother were converts to Judaism who became Christians when Christ came. His father was Greek—likely not a believer, possibly even opposing his family's faith. Yet Timothy has seen firsthand how the gospel transforms lives across generations. He has a legacy of conviction to live up to.

## 2. The Cost of Shame: Paul's Abandoned Friends

### Those Who Deserted Paul

In a culture with significant pressures against Christianity, some of Paul's so-called brothers abandoned him:

> 2 Timothy 1:15  
> You know that everyone in the province of Asia has deserted me, including Phygelus and Hermogenes.

Later, Paul names more:

> 2 Timothy 4:9-10, 16  
> Do your best to come to me quickly, for Demas, because he loved this world, has deserted me and has gone to Thessalonica. Crescens has gone to Galatia, and Titus to Dalmatia... At my first defense, no one came to my support, but everyone deserted me.

These weren't criminal associates abandoning a guilty man. Paul was imprisoned for his faith, and his friends abandoned him rather than face the social shame. It's like modern-day rejection—denied friendship, excluded from gatherings, isolated at work or school.

### Why Paul's Imprisonment Is Different

But here's what transforms the narrative:

> 2 Timothy 1:11-12  
> And of this gospel I was appointed a herald and an apostle and a teacher. That is why I am suffering as I am. Yet I am not ashamed, because I know whom I have believed, and am convinced that he is able to guard what I have entrusted to him for that day.

Paul isn't in prison because he's a criminal, a loser, or unpopular. He's **the Lord's prisoner**. He's there for a reason—a good reason. He's not ashamed because he knows his faith won't disappoint him. By faith, he entrusts his reputation, his freedom, his very life to God.

His body may be beaten in that prison, but all bodies perish one day. At least Paul has laid up treasures in heaven, where moth and rust cannot destroy them.

> 2 Timothy 4:17-18  
> But the Lord stood at my side and gave me strength, so that through me the message might be fully proclaimed and all the Gentiles might hear it. And I was delivered from the lion's mouth. The Lord will rescue me from every evil attack and will bring me safely to his heavenly kingdom. To him be glory for ever and ever. Amen.

Here is a man of unshakeable conviction—not timid, but powerful, loving, and self-disciplined.

## 3. Join Me in Suffering for the Gospel

### The Only Imperative

Look carefully at verse 8:

> 2 Timothy 1:8  
> But join with me in suffering for the gospel, by the power of God.

In the Greek, this is the only direct command (imperative) in this whole section. Everything Paul has said leads to this plea: **Join me. Stand with me. Don't be ashamed.**

### Examples of Courage Worth Following

Paul doesn't stand alone. Look at those who refuse shame:

> 2 Timothy 1:16-18  
> May the Lord show mercy to the household of Onesiphorus, because he often refreshed me and was not ashamed of my chains. On the contrary, when he was in Rome, he searched hard for me until he found me. May the Lord grant that he will find mercy from the Lord on that day! You know very well in how many ways he helped me in Ephesus.

Onesiphorus didn't ask, "What will people think?" He searched for Paul in Rome, publicly associating with a prisoner. And Luke, Mark, and others stayed loyal when it cost them socially.

## Overcoming Timidity: Practical Wisdom

### Where We Feel Most Ashamed

We can identify with Timothy's struggle. Consider different relationships:

**With our peers:** We're tempted to stay silent during conversations about faith. We worry about seeming like the preachy, boring one. We've tried witnessing before and feel we've exhausted the topic. We don't want to be the party pooper.

**With those older than us:** Witnessing to non-Christian parents, aunts, and uncles feels terrifying. We fear overstepping boundaries. We might get the language wrong. We can't seem to agree on anything anyway—why bother?

**With those younger than us:** We assume they're too shallow, too focused on exams and trivialities. They won't take us seriously. We'll seem weird.

All of these are attitudes of timidity—we're finding excuses, and when we can't find real ones, we invent them on behalf of others.

### The Three Attitudes Paul Gives Us

Rather than timidity, Paul calls us to cultivate three things (in reverse order):

**Self-discipline:** This is practice and perseverance. Learning to sing in front of others, playing an instrument, having conversations about faith—these skills develop through repetition, not talent. Start small. Be accountable. Keep improving. Lose a few friends. That's okay. You're building spiritual muscle.

**Love:** We care. We genuinely want people to know Christ because there is no other way to salvation. This isn't manipulation; it's authentic concern for their eternal destiny.

**Power:** Remember—the power is in the gospel, not in us. We don't need everything to be perfect. We don't need to have every answer memorized. We don't need the ideal environment or the perfect mood. The gospel works "in season and out of season," as Timothy will later read. Trust the power of the gospel, not your powers of persuasion.

### Rethinking Our Relationships

**Friends:** Things change over time. Someone you thought had closed their heart to faith may come back to it years later. Be ready to raise the topic again.

**Family:** When you're older, conversations with aunts and uncles become different. You're not the kid at the kids' table anymore. You can approach them as an adult, ask about their lives, share yours. These conversations are natural now.

**Younger people:** They're not as shallow as we think. God is maturing them. They might desperately long for an older person to mentor them spiritually. Don't underestimate what a simple conversation might mean to them.

**Strangers:** You don't need to be best friends to share your faith. Meeting someone for the first time is actually the perfect time to mention what matters most to you. When you introduce yourself, your name, your work or studies, your friends, you can naturally say, "I go to church," and ask about their life.

### The Real Risk

The real risk is looking foolish. We might become like those Christians we laugh about—the ones handing out flyers on O-Day, wearing daggy Christian t-shirts, standing in the quad telling their testimony. The ones at university who won't shut up about Jesus. The ones at work with Jesus screensavers. The youth leaders constantly having deep conversations.

But here's the truth: **Paul was one of those guys.**

And Paul is not ashamed.

## Conclusion: A Culture of Courage

Instead of ostracizing serious Christians in our circles, let's join them. Let's support our brothers and sisters when they do something countercultural—when they offer to pray, when they invite others to church, when they want to have honest conversations about Christ. Let's say yes to their invitations. Let's go to church together. Let's be honest when they ask how we're really doing.

We have not suffered to the point of shedding blood, as the writer of Hebrews reminds us. But even if we did, it would be suffering for the gospel—a worthy suffering. We would be Christ's prisoners, not just any prisoners. There's no shame in being the slave of Christ. We are guarded by the gospel.

Let us join Paul and Timothy, across generations, and testify about our Lord Jesus Christ. Not with timidity, but with power, love, and self-discipline. The gospel will not fail us.
