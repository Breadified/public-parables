# Joshua 6:1-6:22

**Title:** God's Justice in War  
**Date:** 2 July 2016  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction: Understanding God's Judgment

We encounter in Joshua's conquest of Jericho a passage that raises difficult questions about God's character. When we read about the destruction of an entire city, we inevitably ask: Is God cruel? Does He act like a bully? Does He play favorites? These accusations deserve thoughtful answers grounded in Scripture.

The context helps us understand. Joshua stands at the threshold of the Promised Land after forty years of wilderness wandering. God has already given this land to Abraham's descendants (Genesis 15), but centuries had passed. Now, as Israel enters Canaan, God executes judgment on a deeply wicked civilization through His covenant people.

## God's Judgment Is Not Genocide

> 20 When the trumpets sounded, the army shouted, and at the sound of the trumpet, when the men gave a loud shout, the wall collapsed; so everyone charged straight in, and they took the city. 21 They devoted the city to the Lord and destroyed with the sword every living thing in it—men and women, young and old, cattle, sheep and donkeys.

This passage certainly appears to describe total destruction. Yet several factors help us understand what actually happened here.

**Understanding Ancient Military Language**

First, we must read this text within its ancient Near Eastern context. Military narratives of that era used hyperbolic language for rhetorical effect. When modern sports commentators say they'll "demolish" and "annihilate" the opposition, we understand they're using conventional rhetoric, not describing literal genocide. Similarly, these descriptions likely emphasized total victory rather than literal annihilation of every living soul.

**Who Actually Died?**

Second, those killed were primarily combatants and those who chose to remain in the city. In the ancient world, the concept of prisoners of war didn't exist—defeated soldiers faced slaughter as a military necessity. The phrase "men and women, young and old, cattle, sheep and donkeys" describes the spectrum of anyone who remained to defend the city or support its military force, not necessarily a deliberate targeting of civilians and children.

Jericho may have been a military fortress rather than a domestic city. It likely housed soldiers, support personnel like Rahab (a prostitute who may have served the military), and others in the war effort. When the walls collapsed, everyone had the opportunity to escape—indeed, Rahab and her family fled to safety.

**Evidence Against Complete Genocide**

Third, the subsequent history proves that genocide did not occur. Scripture itself testifies that Canaanites remained throughout the land:

> Joshua 23:12 - But if you turn away and ally yourselves with the survivors of these nations that remain among you...

The text mentions "foreigners and survivors in the midst of Israel," showing that the conquest was not a systematic eradication campaign but a military operation to take a strategic fortress.

## God's Judgment Is Not Bullying

> 6:1 Now the gates of Jericho were securely barred because of the Israelites. No one went out and no one came in.
>
> 2 Then the Lord said to Joshua, "See, I have delivered Jericho into your hands, along with its king and its fighting men. 3 March around the city once with all the armed men. Do this for six days. 4 Have seven priests carry trumpets of rams' horns in front of the ark. On the seventh day, march around the city seven times, with the priests blowing the trumpets. 5 When you hear them sound a long blast on the trumpets, have the whole army give a loud shout; then the wall of the city will collapse and the army will go up, everyone straight in."

What's remarkable about this passage is how extraordinary and un-military it is. This is no ordinary siege. Look at the peculiar elements:

- **Seven priests** and **seven trumpets**—the number seven represents completeness
- **The ark of the covenant** leading the procession—the symbol of God's presence
- **Silence enforced**—the army cannot speak until the final shout
- **Marching in ritual formation**—this resembles a temple ceremony more than warfare
- **Seven days of circling**—spiritual preparation, not military strategy

This reads far more like a religious ceremony than a conventional military campaign. The Israelites weren't employing tactics of conquest or demonstrating military superiority. Instead, they were participating in a spiritual act of judgment.

**The Canaanites' Wickedness**

The key to understanding this isn't invasion and conquest, but divine judgment against a genuinely evil civilization. The Canaanites practiced human sacrifice to their gods Molech and Baal. Archaeological and biblical evidence shows they burned their own children alive in religious rituals. This wasn't a matter of cultural difference—it was systematic cruelty justified by false religion.

> Jeremiah 19:5 - They have built the high places of Baal to burn their children in the fire as offerings to Baal

God executed righteous justice against this evil people, just as He promised Abraham in Genesis 15:

> Genesis 15:16 - In the fourth generation your descendants will come back here, for the sin of the Amorites has not yet reached its full measure.

God waited four centuries, allowing the Canaanites time to repent. Their sin had to reach its full measure before judgment fell.

**Not a Pattern of Conquest**

Notice that Israel didn't continue conquering beyond Canaan. They weren't building an empire or expanding borders endlessly. They conquered the land promised to them and stopped. There was no Israeli empire, no emperor, no endless territorial expansion like other conquering powers. This was execution of God's specific judgment on a specific wicked nation, not a pattern of aggressive imperialism.

Richard Dawkins and others criticize religion for promoting conquest and forced conversion. Yet history shows that Christianity, as a general rule, didn't operate this way. The contrast with other religions and ideologies is stark. Even secular nations like the UN intervened in Korea to stop North Korean aggression—no one called that invasion. They recognized it as justice against an oppressor. Similarly, God's actions in Jericho were justice, not aggression.

## God's Judgment Is Not Favoritism

> 5:13 Now when Joshua was near Jericho, he looked up and saw a man standing in front of him with a drawn sword in his hand. Joshua went up to him and asked, "Are you for us or for our enemies?"
>
> 14 "Neither," he replied, "but as commander of the army of the Lord I have now come." Then Joshua fell facedown to the ground in reverence, and asked him, "What message does my Lord have for his servant?"
>
> 15 The commander of the Lord's army replied, "Take off your sandals, for the place where you are standing is holy." And Joshua did so.

Joshua's question reveals the third accusation: If God fights for Israel, doesn't He play favorites? Doesn't His support give Israel an unfair advantage?

The answer is profound: **God is not on anyone's side—people choose to be on God's side.**

The commander of the Lord's army doesn't say "I'm with you" or "I'm against your enemies." He says "Neither." This doesn't mean God is neutral or indifferent. Rather, it means God stands apart from human factions, aligned only with justice and righteousness. God doesn't choose teams; He calls people to join His team.

**The Principle: God Sides with Justice**

This is beautifully illustrated by history. Consider the Nazi regime, which inscribed on its belt buckles "Gott mit uns"—God with us. The Nazis believed God sided with them. But God was absolutely not with them. They were wicked national socialists who committed unspeakable atrocities: the Holocaust, murdering two-thirds of European Jewry, forced abortions to control the birth rates of "undesirables," euthanasia of the disabled. They weren't just terrorists; they were systematically evil. God was not on their side, no matter what they claimed.

**God's Impartiality Proven**

The clearest evidence that God doesn't play favorites appears later in Israel's own history. When Israel itself turned to wickedness—adopting the practices of Molech, burning their own children—God showed the same impartiality:

> 2 Kings 16:3 - He [King Ahaz] even sacrificed his own son in the fire, following the detestable ways of the nations

Centuries later, when Israel's sin reached its full measure, God brought the same judgment: destruction by the Assyrians and Babylonians, incredible bloodshed and exile. God demonstrated He is not on Israel's side just because they're Israel. He's on the side of justice.

**Who Is on God's Side?**

The answer to "Whose side is God on?" is not about nationality or ethnicity—it's about alignment with His character. The Canaanite prostitute Rahab demonstrates this beautifully. She was an outsider, an enemy, yet she alone among Jericho's inhabitants was saved:

> Joshua 6:22 - Joshua said to the two men who had spied out the land, "Go into the prostitute's house and bring her out and all who belong to her, in accordance with your oath to her."

Rahab believed in God. She chose His side. So while her entire nation fell under judgment, she and her household were rescued. God's position is clear: **It's not about which nation you belong to; it's about whether you belong to God.**

## The Hope of Salvation

As we face the reality of divine judgment, there is grace. God offers salvation to anyone willing to repent and trust Him. Like Rahab, we have an opportunity to choose His side before judgment comes.

> Revelation 19:11 - I saw heaven standing open and there before me was a white horse, whose rider is called Faithful and True. With justice he judges and wages war.

God's judgment will come in two stages. Christ came the first time to defeat Satan and secure our redemption. He will return to finish the work and establish His kingdom of perfect justice.

The question each person must answer is: Which side are you on? God's side means trusting in Jesus, accepting His grace, and aligning yourself with justice and righteousness.

## Conclusion: The Justice of War

Yes, war is brutal. The Korean War killed or wounded 2.5 million Koreans and 35,000 American soldiers. Yet we don't call it evil because it was righteous—a UN-led defense against tyrannical aggression. The war preserved South Korea. Without it, the South would resemble the North today: impoverished, enslaved under authoritarian rule, dependent on foreign aid with no freedom or democracy. Instead, South Korea became prosperous, free, and democratic—a testament to the value of that just war.

In this fallen world where evil exists, good people must sometimes defend themselves and others through war. God enacts judgment through nations and armies because sin demands a response. We don't call the Korean War evil. Neither was the war in Jericho.

One day, God will wage His final war to end all wars, establishing His perfect justice forever. On that day, the question for each of us remains: Whose side will you be on?
