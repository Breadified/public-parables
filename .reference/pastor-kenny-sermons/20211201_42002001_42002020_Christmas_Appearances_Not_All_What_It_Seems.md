# Luke 2:1-20

**Title:** Christmas Appearances: Not All What It Seems

**Date:** 19 December 2021 (ARISE); Draft from 2 January 2004 (ACC)

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## 1. Who Is in Control?

### The World Under Caesar's Rule

> In those days Caesar Augustus issued a decree that a census should be taken of the entire Roman world. And everyone went to their own town to register. (Luke 2:1-3)

Caesar Augustus commanded the most powerful empire on earth. He had consolidated absolute authority, eliminating rivals and establishing the *Pax Romana*—peace enforced through military might and the threat of punishment. Garrisons stood ready throughout the empire to suppress any rebellion.

Augustus reigned for 45 years, unchallenged and unquestioned. An imperial cult developed where the emperor himself was revered as divine—the "Son of God." In the eyes of the world, Caesar *was* god, and with that power he controlled everything.

The census itself demonstrated this total control. It was a registration—a cataloging of the entire known world under Caesar's dominion. He decreed it, his governors obeyed it, and all people submitted to it. This was absolute power organizing absolute order.

### Even God Seems Bound

> So Joseph also went up from the town of Nazareth in Galilee to Judea, to Bethlehem the town of David, because he belonged to the house and line of David. He went there to register with Mary, who was pledged to be married to him and was expecting a child. (Luke 2:4-5)

But here is where appearances deceive. Look at what happened: Joseph, Mary, and Jesus—the Son of God himself—all obeyed Caesar's decree. A pregnant Mary traveled to Bethlehem because a pagan emperor ordered a census.

On the surface, it appears that Caesar controlled even God. The supposedly all-powerful Caesar moved the pieces, and God's own family complied like any other subject of the empire.

### The Hidden Reality

But appearances can be deceiving.

Eight centuries before Caesar Augustus was ever born, the prophet Micah wrote:

> But you, O Bethlehem Ephrathah, who are too little to be among the clans of Judah, from you shall come forth for me one who is to be ruler in Israel, whose origin is from of old, from ancient days. (Micah 5:2)

God had spoken. The Messiah would be born in Bethlehem. Eight hundred years of prophetic certainty.

Here is the stunning truth: Caesar was not in control at all. He was merely an instrument in God's hand, unwittingly carrying out the eternal purposes of God. The emperor thought he was directing the world; in reality, he was moving Mary and Joseph to the exact location where God had determined His Son would be born.

Who has lasted? Caesar? We remember him in the month of August, perhaps—but who celebrates Augustus' birthday each year? The world has forgotten him. Yet Jesus, born in that insignificant moment, is celebrated around the world two thousand years later.

Despite all appearances, it is not Caesar but God who is in control of the world.

### The Comfort This Brings

This truth is profoundly comforting. Despite the appearance of weakness, despite corruption and pandemic, despite evil people wielding power—God has it all under control. The One who moved Caesar Augustus without his knowledge continues to move history toward His purposes.

## 2. Who Is Jesus?

### Why His Birth Appears So Humble

Yet looking at the circumstances of Jesus' birth, it is hard to believe He is truly King. Everything about it whispers defeat.

### Bethlehem: A Backwater Town

The location itself is humble. Bethlehem is described in Micah as "too little to be among the clans of Judah." If the clans of Judah went to war, Bethlehem would be left behind—too small, too weak, too insignificant to matter.

Even the name underwhelms. "Bethlehem" means "house of bread" in Hebrew. Imagine a royal birth announcement: *Born at Baker's Delight. Current residence: Breadtop.* Hardly befitting a king.

### The Birth Circumstances

> While they were there, the time came for the baby to be born, and she gave birth to her firstborn, a son. She wrapped him in cloths and placed him in a manger, because there was no guest room available for them. (Luke 2:6-7)

The manger tells us much. In a typical Palestinian house, the living quarters occupied the upper space while animals were kept in the lower section. When the guest room filled, there was no other option—the baby lay among the animals, wrapped in cloths, sleeping in a feeding trough.

Compare this with the birth of royalty in our modern world: exclusive hospitals, private suites, the finest physicians, first-class travel, and every luxury afforded to the precious child. Jesus' birth was none of these. It was humble, poor, and apparently inglorious.

### The Reception: Shepherds

> And there were shepherds living out in the fields nearby, keeping watch over their flocks at night. An angel of the Lord appeared to them, and the glory of the Lord shone around them, and they were terrified. (Luke 2:8-9)

The announcement came not to dignitaries or royalty but to shepherds—people at the very bottom of society's hierarchy. They were peasants who could not secure better employment, working night shifts tending sheep. When a king was born, tabloids would swarm, celebrities would gather, famous patrons would be announced. Instead, a divine announcement went out to strangers working in the dark.

It is as absurd as a royal birth notice going to someone working the midnight shift at a petrol station. Yet this is who received the first announcement of Jesus' birth.

### What Is Missing

Notably absent from Luke's account are the wise men and the star described in Matthew's gospel. There is no exotic caravan, no royal tributes, no supernatural sign marking the heavens. Luke deliberately emphasizes that this birth was not fancy. It was poor, plain, and humble.

Why come this way when He could have come as a king of earthly grandeur?

## 3. How the Heavenly Sees Past Earthly Appearances

But the angels saw what earthly eyes missed.

> But the angel said to them, "Do not be afraid. I bring you good news that will cause great joy for all the people. Today in the town of David a Savior has been born to you; he is the Messiah, the Lord. This will be a sign to you: You will find a baby wrapped in cloths and lying in a manger."
>
> Suddenly a great company of the heavenly host appeared with the angel, praising God and saying, "Glory to God in the highest heaven, and on earth peace to those on whom his favor rests." (Luke 2:10-14)

### What the Angels Recognized

1. **The City:** The angel called Bethlehem "the town of David"—connecting it to Israel's first and greatest king. What appeared to be an insignificant hamlet was actually a city of royal significance and prophetic destiny.

2. **The Sign:** The manger, which to earthly eyes spoke of poverty and degradation, the angel declared to be a sign from God. What seemed shameful was actually divinely appointed.

3. **The Identity:** Despite Augustus ruling Rome, the angel proclaimed Jesus to be the true "Messiah" and "Lord." One king sits on an earthly throne; the other is the eternal King.

4. **The Significance:** This was not an ordinary Palestinian birth but "good news" for all people on earth—a message of cosmic importance, not local embarrassment.

### The Heavenly Perspective

The angels were not deceived by earthly appearances. A "multitude of the heavenly host"—God's army—stood behind this newborn King. Where Caesar commanded legions of soldiers, Jesus had the hosts of heaven at His disposal. The military strength of Rome was nothing compared to the angelic army backing God's Son.

And this King would establish genuine peace. Not the *Pax Romana*—enforced through fear and the threat of punishment, a peace that never truly lasts—but real peace between God and humanity, flowing out from reconciliation with the Almighty.

### The Cost and the Crown

Thirty years later, this King would become poor in earthly life so that He might enrich others with eternal life. He would give His life as an atoning sacrifice, paying for the sins of the world. On earth, He appeared weak and destitute. But from heaven's perspective, He is the Savior.

This is why Christmas is celebrated around the world, two thousand years later. Not because of a star in the sky or the visit of wise men, but because a humble birth in Bethlehem marked the arrival of the One who would save humanity.

## 4. God's Way: The Pattern of Scripture

Why would God's Son come this way—poor, humble, born to humble parents in a humble town?

It reflects the consistent character of God. As Mary herself sang:

> He has brought down the mighty from their thrones and exalted those of humble estate; he has filled the hungry with good things, and the rich he has sent empty away. (Luke 1:52-53)

This is God's way. He chooses the humble over the proud, the weak over the mighty, the last over the first. He calls His people not from palaces but from fishing boats and tax offices. He does not work through earthly power but through divine purpose hidden in what the world despises.

---

## Application: Don't Be Deceived

The Christmas story calls us not to be deceived by appearances. The world's standard judges by earthly measures:
- Caesar appears to be god
- Jesus appears to be a loser (born in a backwater, in poverty, with a peasant reception)

But heaven's standard judges differently. By heaven's reckoning, Jesus is the Christ, the Savior, and the Lord. The one who rules eternally, not for 45 years. The one whose kingdom cannot be shaken.

In your own life, do not judge by earthly appearances alone. The world may appear to triumph; God's purposes continue. Weakness may seem to prevail; God's strength is perfected in weakness. Humble circumstances may seem insignificant; God often works there most powerfully.

Trust that beneath the surface of all things, God remains in control, working out His purposes with perfect wisdom and power.
