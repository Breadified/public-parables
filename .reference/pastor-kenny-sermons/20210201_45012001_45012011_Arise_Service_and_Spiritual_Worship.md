# Romans 12:1-11

**Title:** Arise - Service and Spiritual Worship

**Date:** 26 February 2021

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Welcome Back to Gathered Worship

After nearly a year of restrictions, we're grateful to gather in person once again. This is significant for our community—to be together, to share fellowship, and to build one another up.

While we await the day we can sing together, it's worth considering what worship truly means. Many assume worship is primarily about singing or performing religious acts. But Paul calls us to something deeper. Yes, Paul calls us to worship, but foremost, he calls us to **serve**.

## 1. Serve God Through Living Sacrifice (v.1)

> Therefore, I urge you, brothers, in view of God's mercy, to offer your bodies as living sacrifices, holy and pleasing to God—this is your spiritual act of worship.

### Understanding "Worship" (Latreia)

The word "worship" in English can be confusing because multiple Greek words are translated by this single English term. In Romans 12:1, Paul uses **latreia**—meaning "service in the temple" or "priestly service."

This is not *proskunew* (bowing down, as when the Magi worship Jesus), nor *eusebeia* (piety or religious devotion). Rather, latreia refers to the actual work of temple service—the maintenance, cleaning, and prescribed rituals performed by priests.

Just as Levitical priests didn't own land or work in fields but devoted themselves to temple service, Paul calls us to offer ourselves in ongoing service to God.

### From Dead Sacrifice to Living Sacrifice

In the Old Testament, priests offered dead animal sacrifices—bulls and goats—to atone for the nation's sins. These sacrifices were made redundant by Christ's perfect sacrifice. Jesus is the Lamb of God, whose blood once and for all covers our sin.

But the language of sacrifice continues. Paul invites us to offer our bodies as **living sacrifices**—not a one-time death, but a continual, daily offering. As Jesus taught, we must "take up our cross daily" and follow him. This is not a single martyrdom but an ongoing surrender of our desires, our ambitions, and our will to God's purposes.

Our lives are no longer our own.

### Sober Judgment About Our Role (v.3)

> For by the grace given me I say to every one of you: Do not think of yourself more highly than you ought, but rather think of yourself with sober judgment, in accordance with the measure of faith God has given you.

Here Paul warns against pride. We are called to assess ourselves honestly—not inflating our importance but recognizing what God has entrusted to us. "Much given, much expected." Each person has been given different capacities and opportunities.

For those who are older and more established in faith, there is a temptation to redirect energy toward your own responsibilities and comfort. But maturity calls us to be examples. Set the standard. Support those coming after you. You may not have the same vigor or capacity as the young, but your consistent presence and faithful witness matter immensely.

## 2. Serve One Another Through Your Gifts (v.4-8)

> Just as each of us has one body with many members, and these members do not all have the same function, so in Christ we who are many form one body, and each member belongs to all the others.

Christ is the Head. We are the body. And just as a physical body requires every member functioning together, the church requires each of us contributing our unique capacities to build up the whole.

> We have different gifts, according to the grace given us. If a man's gift is prophesying, let him use it in proportion to his faith. If it is serving, let him serve; if it is teaching, let him teach; if it is encouraging, let him encourage; if it is contributing to the needs of others, let him give generously; if it is leadership, let him govern diligently; if it is showing mercy, let him do it cheerfully.

### Gifts as Grace, Not Talent

In our narcissistic age, we often confuse "gift" with "talent"—innate ability. We imagine someone is "gifted" at music or preaching, treating gifts as static abilities we discover through self-assessment surveys.

But notice Paul's descriptions: "let him give **generously**," "let him govern **diligently**," "let him do it **cheerfully**." These adverbs suggest that sometimes we may not naturally excel at what we're called to do, or it may not come easily. Gifts aren't always about playing to our strengths.

Consider 1 Corinthians 7:7: "Each of you has your own gift from God; one has this gift, another has that." Paul speaks of singleness and marriage as gifts. But these aren't talents—they're circumstances, opportunities, seasons of life. The "gift" is what God has provided in those circumstances.

**The key shift:** Rather than beginning with yourself ("I have X talent, how can I use it?"), begin with the body's need: "What does the church need? What can I do to meet that need?" As you meet needs, you're exercising your gift.

### Opportunities Transform Into Gifts

Let's think practically:

- **See rubbish on the floor?** You have eyes to see, a back that can bend, hands to pick it up. You're exercising your gift.
- **Someone forgot their Bible?** You have one at home. You have a car. Bring a spare Bible and share it. You're exercising your gift.
- **Parking is limited?** You have good health and functioning legs. Park farther away, walk an extra distance, and free a closer spot for someone less able. You're exercising your gift.
- **A neighbor is sick, unemployed, or lonely?** What can you do? Think creatively and act.

Gifts are not fancy, upfront things (those represent perhaps 0.1% of church life). True gifts are ordinary, unseen service to one another. This is why service is repeatedly linked with humility and love.

### The Shift in Mindset

When you begin thinking this way—looking for opportunities to serve—the definition of "gift" expands infinitely. You discover that gifts are the most ordinary things: a willingness to help, the ability to listen, the strength to carry something, the presence to sit with someone in pain.

This transforms how we approach church and community life. Rather than waiting to be assigned a role based on our talents, we develop a constant awareness: *What needs doing? Can I do it? Will I do it?*

If you want to serve more formally, we have serving teams—essentially, small groups with a servant heart. These groups don't exist to be introverted communities focused only on themselves. True knowledge of God leads to service, to prayer for the lost, to other-centeredness, to love for the broader body.

## 3. Service Turns Gifts Into Love (v.9-11)

> Love must be sincere. Hate what is evil; cling to what is good. Be devoted to one another in brotherly love. Honor one another above yourselves. Never be lacking in zeal, but keep your spiritual fervor, serving the Lord.

### Church as Family Kitchen, Not Restaurant

Why do we serve one another? Because the church should function like a healthy family kitchen, not a restaurant.

In a restaurant, things are done *for* you. You receive service passively. But in a healthy family kitchen, everyone participates. Everybody contributes. There's shared responsibility, mutual care, and genuine concern flowing in all directions—out of love, not obligation.

This isn't the dynamic of one person (like a mother) doing all the work while others eat and leave. Rather, it's a community where everyone is invested in one another's wellbeing. Everyone serves. Everyone is served.

This is not just something we do at church gatherings. It's the rhythm of Christian life. It's how we live together, how we love together, how we bear one another's burdens.

### A Living Sacrifice

Remember: our spiritual worship is offering ourselves as living sacrifices. This is what Jesus did—he voluntarily laid down his life in service and love. We follow him by doing the same—not in grand gestures necessarily, but in daily, faithful presence with one another.

This is the calling that awaits us: to see needs and meet them, to exercise our gifts through service, and to let that service become an expression of sincere, sacrificial love for God and for one another.
