# Job 4:1-8; 6:14-30

**Title:** Fair-Weather Friends
**Date:** 6 July 2012
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## The Friends Arrive

When we face trials, we often turn to friends for support. Sometimes they offer genuine comfort—a different perspective, a listening ear, a shoulder to lean on. But other times, friends become sources of pain themselves. They fill the silence with unhelpful advice, platitudes masquerading as wisdom ("just study harder," "just snap out of it"), or solutions we've already exhausted.

Job faces this struggle now. After losing his children, his fortune, and his health to a devastating skin disease, three friends come to console him: Eliphaz the Temanite, Bildad the Shuhite, and Zophar the Naamathite. Their intentions are good, but their theology—and therefore their counsel—will prove deeply problematic.

---

## The Friends' Argument: Suffering Proves Sin

Eliphaz speaks first, and his logic is straightforward:

> **Job 4:7-8** "Consider now: Who, being innocent, has ever perished? Where were the upright ever destroyed? As I have observed, those who plow evil and those who sow trouble reap it."

The implication is crystal clear: either you are righteous and therefore you do not suffer, or you are a sinner and therefore you suffer. Since Job is suffering, Job must be a sinner. If he simply admits this, seeks repentance, and changes course, his suffering will end.

### The Truth Behind the False Argument

In one sense, Eliphaz is right. We truly are not as innocent as we think. Sin has corrupted the world. As Paul writes:

> **Romans 1:28-31** "Furthermore, since they did not think it worthwhile to retain the knowledge of God, he gave them over to a depraved mind, to do what ought not to be done. They have become filled with every kind of wickedness, evil, greed and depravity. They are full of envy, murder, strife, deceit and malice. They are gossips, slanderers, God-haters, insolent, arrogant and boastful; they invent ways of doing evil; they disobey their parents; they are senseless, faithless, heartless, ruthless."

We can trace much suffering back to sin—our own or others'. One choice leads to another. One sin cascades into consequences. This is the principle of retributive justice on a cosmic scale, working itself out across generations.

### Practical Application: Taking Responsibility

Eliphaz's call for Job to take responsibility does contain wisdom. If you spend all week playing video games instead of studying, you will fail your exams—and we should expect you to fail. That's the consequence of your choice. If you gamble, you'll go broke. If you smoke, you increase your cancer risk dramatically. These aren't injustices; they're the natural results of our choices.

True resilience means being "in tune with the reality of our part in what's gone wrong." It means accepting blame rather than playing the victim. When we stop blaming others and blaming circumstances, we discover our own power to change. Growing in maturity—in how we relate to others, in our patterns of communication, in our willingness to examine ourselves—creates a maturity-growing environment for everyone around us.

As one wise observer notes: "The first step in growing yourself up is a good dose of humility. You need to be prepared to look at yourself and give up putting energy into trying to change others or blame others or complain about others. And the benefit of beginning to look at yourself actually has a flow-on effect for others. Becoming a more mature person can impact a whole group—your family, your church, your work team."

## But Job Is Actually Righteous

Here is where Eliphaz's counsel collapses. We must listen to Job's own testimony:

> **Job 6:14-21** "Anyone who withholds kindness from a friend forsakes the fear of the Almighty. But my brothers are as undependable as intermittent streams, as the streams that overflow when darkened by thawing ice and swollen with melting snow, but that stop flowing in the dry season, and in the heat vanish from their channels. The caravans of Tema look for water, the traveling merchants of Sheba look in hope. They are distressed, because they had been confident; they arrive there, only to be disappointed. Now you too have proved to be of no help; you see something dreadful and are afraid."

Job compares his friends to seasonal streams—swollen with water in winter but completely dry when the desert traveler needs them most. Their presence brings no comfort; their counsel brings no help. They have abandoned him in his darkest hour.

And here's the crucial point: **Eliphaz is wrong about the diagnosis.** We know this from what God Himself revealed just chapters earlier:

> **Job 1:8** "Then the Lord said to Satan, 'Have you considered my servant Job? There is no one on earth like him; he is blameless and upright, a man who fears God and shuns evil.'"

God Himself has declared Job righteous. His friends don't know this, but we do. Their entire argument rests on a false premise.

Job pleads with them:

> **Job 6:24-30** "Teach me, and I will be quiet; show me where I have been wrong. How painful are honest words! But what do your arguments prove? Do you mean to correct what I say, and treat my desperate words as wind? You would even cast lots for the fatherless and barter away your friend. But now be so kind as to look at me. Would I lie to your face? Relent, do not be unjust; reconsider, for my integrity is at stake. Is there any wickedness on my lips? Can my mouth not discern malice?"

## When Retributive Justice Doesn't Apply

The disciples made the same mistake that Job's friends made. When they encountered a man blind from birth, they asked:

> **John 9:1-3** "As he went along, he saw a man blind from birth. His disciples asked him, 'Rabbi, who sinned, this man or his parents, that he was born blind?' Jesus answered, 'Neither this man nor his parents sinned, but this happened so that the work of God might be displayed in his life.'"

Jesus shattered their assumption. Sometimes suffering is not the direct result of personal sin. Sometimes it's not anyone's fault at all. Sometimes God has other plans.

This is not to say that retributive justice is false in general. Rather, it has limits. Not all suffering can be traced back to personal wrongdoing. Some afflictions exist to reveal God's work. Some trials are tests—artificially imposed situations designed to strengthen us, like a two-hour exam on material we've studied. The trial itself isn't a natural occurrence; it's created to consolidate learning and test understanding.

God disciplines us, as Scripture tells us:

> **Hebrews 12:10-11** "They disciplined us for a little while as they thought best; but God disciplines us for our good, in order that we may share in his holiness. No discipline seems pleasant at the time, but painful. Later on, however, it produces a harvest of righteousness and peace for those who have been trained by it."

## The Undeserved Suffering of Jesus

But there is something even more profound: **God Himself subjected Himself to undeserved suffering.**

> **John 9:4-5** "As long as it is day, we must do the work of him who sent me. Night is coming, when no one can work. While I am in the world, I am the light of the world."

Jesus came to do the Father's work—work that required humility, patience, pain, betrayal, and death on a cross. To most people in first-century Palestine, He was simply another innocent man being executed by Rome. What was the big deal?

But out of sight, cosmic foundations were shaking. The penalty for sin was being paid. The way to God was being opened. The light of the world was shining, even as the darkness seemed to prevail.

Here is the heart of the gospel: **Jesus is not retributive justice in action. He is grace in action.** He suffered what we deserved so we could receive what we don't deserve.

If you have never experienced His friendship, understand this: being a Christian means recognizing your own sin, lowering your pride, and asking God for forgiveness. It's free. It's undeserved. You cannot earn it.

Many people live as though everything good must be earned—as though, in the words of *The Sound of Music*, "Nothing comes from nothing, nothing ever could. Somewhere in my youth or childhood I must have done something good." But grace doesn't work that way. God loves you not because you've earned it, but because He chooses to.

---

## What This Means for Us

### Truth About Suffering

If there is such a thing as undeserved suffering in this world, then there is also such a thing as undeserved grace. Don't ask, "What did I do to deserve this pain?" Instead, ask, "What grace has God given me that I don't deserve?" An innocent Jesus died for us while we were still His enemies. Don't call out for your rights—call out for mercy.

### How to Be a Better Friend

Job's friends failed him not because they were malicious, but because their theology was incomplete. They saw only retributive justice and missed God's sovereignty. When we misunderstand how God works, our advice becomes cruel rather than kind.

How can we do better?

- **Listen and reflect.** Don't rush to solve. Simply hear what someone is saying and reflect it back in your own words.
- **Distinguish comfort from solutions.** Short-term comfort is different from long-term answers. Sometimes what someone needs is not advice but presence.
- **Accept that there are no easy answers.** Sometimes there is no solution at all—just a hand to hold and a shoulder to cry on.
- **Pray for them.** Intercede. Let them know they are held before God.

And if your friends still disappoint you, that's expected. Earthly friends, no matter how well-intentioned, will eventually let us down.

### The Better Friend

In the end, we cannot put our ultimate hope in earthly friends. We need a friend who will stick by us not just in good times but through the deepest suffering. Better still, we need a friend who truly understands what it's like to be in our shoes.

That friend is Jesus. He holds your hand. He cries with you. He has suffered what you're suffering. And His friendship is the one friendship that will never fail.
