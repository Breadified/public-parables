# Job 42:1-17

**Title:** The End of Suffering
**Date:** 2 August 2013
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## When Life Feels Stuck

Many of us find ourselves at an impasse. Perhaps you're experiencing a mid-life crisis, or even a quarter-life crisis. Maybe you're stuck—trapped by tragedy, relationship breakdown, or chronic illness. The dreams you held have slipped away or are slipping away as we speak.

In such darkness, we ask the deepest questions: **Where is God? Why is life so hard? Why such disappointment?** If God says he cares, why isn't he here? It feels as though either God doesn't exist, or if he does, he doesn't care about us. And we can't have faith in a God that doesn't exist, nor in a careless God.

The Book of Job addresses one of life's most profound questions. It's a tough topic that raises personal issues for us—but that's evidence that God is working in our lives.

## 1. God Speaks (Job 40:6-10)

### The Story of Job's Test

Job is a righteous man in Scripture—innocent, upright, one who fears God and shuns evil. Satan made a deal with God to test Job, accusing him that Job only trusts God because everything goes well. God allows Satan to strip away Job's wealth, his children, and his health. Will Job continue to trust?

Throughout his ordeal, Job cries out: Why suffering? He longs to meet with God, to see him and speak to him about these matters. And God speaks.

> Job 42:1-2: "I know that you can do all things; no plan of yours can be thwarted."
>
> Job 42:3: "You asked, 'Who is this that obscures my counsel without knowledge?' Surely I spoke of things I did not understand, things too wonderful for me to know."
>
> Job 42:4: "You said, 'Listen now, and I will speak; I will question you, and you shall answer me.'"

### Why Suffering? The Answers We Think We Want

When we ask "Why suffering?" we're asking for the cause. Science might answer: "Because of mutating cells" or "Because of your lifestyle choices." Theologically, we answer that suffering is the result of sin.

**The sin of Adam has cosmic consequences.** Sin makes the universe decay and move toward chaos. Nature doesn't work as designed. The sun is dying. Earthquakes and tsunamis occur. Rain falls where it shouldn't; drought strikes where it should rain. Nature runs rampant, killing all in its path.

> Romans 8:19-21: "The creation waits in eager expectation for the children of God to be revealed... that the creation itself will be liberated from its bondage to decay."

Further, sin affects humanity directly. The entrance of death creates selfishness. We abuse each other, lie to one another, hurt one another, break our promises. Little things lead to big things, and the repercussions reverberate for generations.

> James 1:14-15: "Each person is tempted when they are dragged away by their own evil desire and enticed. Then, after desire has conceived, it gives birth to sin; and sin, when it is full-grown, gives birth to death."

Suffering can be God's way to get our attention—but that's not the answer God gives here. Job has heard those answers and isn't satisfied.

### The Real Answer: "Are You God?"

God gives a different answer entirely: **Are you God?**

> Job 40:9-10: "Do you have an arm like God's, and can your voice thunder like his? Then adorn yourself with glory and splendor, and clothe yourself in honor and majesty."
>
> Job 40:11-14: "Unleash the fury of your wrath, look at all who are proud and bring them low... Can you bring justice to the world? Punish all the wrongdoers, save the innocent?"

Consider how we criticize a ball player, a driver, a politician. Are we aware of all the complexities they navigate? I do construction work at my house and ask my builders countless questions, suddenly convinced I'm an expert in concrete. We live 21, 30, 45, or 75 years and think we're experts in life. Are you like the immortal and eternal God?

**The answer seems to be:** Although we can have some answers, they will always be incomplete in our limited eyes. We are mortal and limited. God has intricate plans—in Job's case, a secret plan behind the curtain that Job doesn't know about. God orders the universe as he sees fit and doesn't have to explain his reasoning behind every decision.

Even if he did explain, we couldn't comprehend it. We'd keep asking why forever, like nodding politely while the builder's explanations go over our heads.

**God therefore asks us to trust him.** The answer is: God knows what he's doing.

Job accepts this. He understands now:

> Job 42:2-3: "I know that you can do all things; no plan of yours can be thwarted... Surely I spoke of things I did not understand, things too wonderful for me to know."

Rather than see imperfect explanations and conclude there must be an imperfect God, perhaps we should believe in a good God whose explanations to us are necessarily imperfect because of our limitations.

**Trust him because he loves us. This is proven.** Jesus on the cross. Blood flowed for us. The God of the universe lowered himself to be judged so that we don't have to be judged.

## 2. We Move On (Don't Let Suffering Stop You from Finding God)

### The Strange Repentance

Now that God speaks, Job grows in faith and is ready to move on.

> Job 42:5: "My ears had heard of you but now my eyes have seen you."

New understanding. But there's a strange verse 6:

> Job 42:6: "Therefore I despise... and repent in dust and ashes."

This confuses us. "Dust and ashes" are Jewish marks of mourning—like our wearing black. It's a sign of repentance, sorrow, and contrition. But Job is righteous! God himself says so:

> Job 1:8: "There is no one on earth like him; he is blameless and upright, a man who fears God and shuns evil."

Throughout his ordeal, Job has been righteous. God later confirms this:

> Job 42:7-8: "You have not spoken the truth about me, as my servant Job has... You have not spoken of me what is right, as my servant Job has."

So why would Job repent? Repentance only makes sense when you've done something wrong. You don't repent from righteousness!

### The Correct Translation

The Hebrew needs clarification. The word "myself" isn't actually in the original text. And "repent in" is better understood as "repent **from** dust and ashes." Job is saying: **"I despise and repent from dust and ashes."**

"Repent" means to turn aside, to reverse direction. Job is rejecting and turning away from mourning. He's been sitting in dust and ashes, lamenting to God, asking why suffering? God answered him. His position before was:

> Job 30:19: "He throws me into the mud, and I am reduced to dust and ashes."

He was in mourning, marked by suffering. But now God has spoken. Now Job is ready to move on from where he is.

### A Time to Stop Mourning

After disaster and death, it's normal to have a mourning period where relatives and friends grieve. Time stops for a while—no business, no pleasure, no life. No delicious food, no nice clothes. Many questions. But some of us grieve too long.

Queen Victoria's husband, Prince Albert, died in 1862. She declared the period of public mourning "the longest term in modern times." But she didn't just mourn for a year—she wore her widow clothes for the rest of her life until her death in 1901. Forty years in black, in mourning.

**Suffering can't be the excuse for everything.** It can't excuse ignoring God. Hardship and bitterness cannot be the last word. It can't excuse stopping our service to God or excuse our sins. There is a time for mourning, but there is also a time to move on.

**Job is ready to move on. He stops asking questions and trusts God.** He's in the middle of a test—and the purpose of a test is to pass it. He's done it. Now it's time to move on. We are to persevere through our sufferings rather than wallow in them.

> Hebrews 12:11: "No discipline seems pleasant at the time, but painful. Later on, however, it produces a harvest of righteousness and peace for those who have been trained by it."

### Moving Forward in Different Contexts

Some of us are stuck in mourning—over broken relationships, uncertainty about the future. We can't move on. But we must.

**Grieving over broken dating relationships:** Dating and breakups are the price you pay for free choice. Grieve but move on. You should have a list of eligible prospects. If a relationship doesn't work, move on to the next one. Before marriage, you can move on. After marriage, that's different.

**Grieving over failure:** Failed an exam? Didn't get into your first choice course or prestigious school? That's sad. For a while. But move on. Quit moaning about what could have been. God has other plans for you. You won't find out what those are if you stay in the mud.

**Grieving over our history:** Parents abused or abandoned you. You were born in another country. You were bullied. You never had tutoring. You came from a poor family. These are real, painful circumstances. But they don't mean you can't do other things.

**Grieving over suffering:** Yes, you're suffering. But suffering doesn't mean you stop moving forward. This is resilience—getting up to try again after you've been knocked down, and being willing to keep doing that.

Commit to your goals. Change what you can change—yourself. Trust God with the rest—with others, the world, and the future.

## 3. The End of Suffering (Job 42:8-17)

Job moves forward. He stops mourning and returns to normal life, just as it was in chapter 1.

### Job's Restoration

**He goes back to interceding for others.** Before, he petitioned for his children's sin. Now he prays for his friends' sins.

> Job 42:8-9: "Go to my servant Job and sacrifice a burnt offering for yourselves. My servant Job will pray for you, and I will accept his prayer."

**He eats with others again.** He's no longer an outcast.

> Job 42:11: "All his brothers and sisters and everyone who had known him before came and ate with him in his house. They comforted and consoled him over all the trouble the LORD had brought upon him, and each one gave him a piece of silver and a gold ring."

His community gives him material help to start again. And then—

**He goes back to being rich:**

> Job 42:10: "After Job had prayed for his friends, the LORD made him prosperous again and gave him twice as much as he had before."
>
> Job 42:12: "The LORD blessed the latter part of Job's life more than the first."

The blessings come in multiple dimensions:

- **Double the animals:** Fourteen thousand sheep, six thousand camels, a thousand yoke of oxen, and a thousand donkeys.
- **A beautiful family:** Seven sons and three daughters. The daughters are so beautiful they're named for their loveliness.
- **Double the wealth:** His daughters inherit alongside his brothers—so abundant is his wealth that even his daughters receive an inheritance.
- **Double the years:** 

> Job 42:16-17: "After this, Job lived a hundred and forty years; he saw his children and their children to the fourth generation. And so he died, old and full of years."

Twice the typical lifespan. He became a great-grandfather.

### God's Final Word Is Blessing

These blessings exceed what Job lost. They're not mere compensation but gestures of grace—undeserved blessings to match his undeserved suffering.

God's last word is to bless. God is in control. As Job said:

> Job 1:21: "The LORD gave and the LORD has taken away; may the name of the LORD be praised."

We know he cannot be thwarted. If he can give great suffering, he can also give great blessing. His promise to his elect is blessing.

### Jesus Spoke About Suffering Too

Jesus addressed suffering directly:

> Luke 13:1-5: When asked why certain Galileans suffered terribly, Jesus answered, "Do you think that these Galileans were worse sinners than all the other Galileans because they suffered this way? I tell you, no! But unless you repent, you too will all perish." He repeated this about eighteen people killed when a tower fell on them.

Not karma. Not because they were worse sinners. But grace—God speaking to us. God's answer to suffering in the world is: **Repent and trust me.**

C.S. Lewis wrote: "God whispers to us in our pleasures, speaks to us in our conscience, but shouts in our pains: It is His megaphone to rouse a deaf world."

### Application

God has spoken to you through Job's story. He provides comfort in suffering. Jesus didn't just speak—he acted. He got on the cross and paid the penalty for sin. He suffered so that our suffering might one day end permanently.

> Romans 8:18-21: "I consider that our present sufferings are not worth comparing with the glory that will be revealed in us. The creation waits in eager expectation for the sons of God to be revealed... that the creation itself will be liberated from its bondage to decay and brought into the glorious freedom of the children of God."

We see the end of suffering in Job's example. There is a greater inheritance waiting at the end of life for those who persevere.

> James 5:11: "As you know, we consider blessed those who have persevered. You have heard of Job's perseverance and have seen what the Lord finally brought about. The Lord is full of compassion and mercy."

Be patient. Trust God. Look forward to the end of the struggle—to the end of suffering for those in Christ.
