# Matthew 7:1-6

**Title:** Judging and the Good Life
**Date:** 18 November 2018
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Problem with Matthew 7:1

Matthew 7:1 is arguably the most quoted verse in the world—more popular even than John 3:16 or Genesis 1:1. And it's popular precisely because unbelievers keep using it against Christians.

> "Do not judge, or you too will be judged." (Matthew 7:1)

This verse gets weaponized in our cultural moment. When Christian schools recently sought religious freedom protections, the immediate backlash from the media and cultural elites was swift: "How dare you judge? Don't you know Matthew 7:1?" The irony, of course, is that the call comes from those who are doing the judging themselves—judging Christians for their beliefs.

The question Jesus is really addressing here isn't whether we should judge at all. It's *how* we judge. And to understand that, we need to walk through three movements: judge yourself, judge others carefully, and know when not to judge at all.

## 1. Judge Yourself (Remove the Plank)

The opening verses introduce a striking image:

> "For in the same way you judge others, you will be judged, and with the measure you use, it will be measured to you." (Matthew 7:2)
>
> "Why do you look at the speck of sawdust in your brother's eye and pay no attention to the plank in your own eye? How can you say to your brother, 'Let me take the speck out of your eye,' when all the time there is a plank in your own eye?" (Matthew 7:3-4)

The Greek word Jesus uses for "speck" and "plank" comes from the same root—they're made of the same material. A plank is far larger, a beam of wood. A speck is perhaps a splinter, like shrapnel from a power tool or a fragment of a broken contact lens. But notice: the measure we use on others is the measure God will use on us. God is consistent. There is one standard—not one for Christians and another for pagans, not one for one person and another for a different person. God's judgment is universal.

> "What may be known about God is plain to them, because God has made it plain to them. For since the creation of the world God's invisible qualities—his eternal power and divine nature—have been clearly seen, being understood from what has been made, so that people are without excuse." (Romans 1:19-20)

The problem comes when we judge others while being guilty of the same things ourselves:

> "You, therefore, have no excuse, you who pass judgment on someone else, for at whatever point you judge another, you are condemning yourself, because you who pass judgment do the same things." (Romans 2:1)

This is hypocrisy—being hyper-critical while ignoring our own failings. And we hate hypocrites, don't we? We hate people who lecture us on issues they themselves don't practice. Rich environmentalists who fly around the world on private jets lecturing about carbon footprints. Celebrities preaching feminism while enabling sexual abuse. Activists who want refugees but wouldn't welcome them into their own neighborhoods. Female activists who stayed silent about abuse rather than speaking up.

The solution is clear: **judge yourself first**. Take the plank out of your own eye. Get rid of hypocrisy.

> "Rid yourselves of all malice and all deceit, hypocrisy, envy, and slander of every kind." (1 Peter 2:1)

This applies everywhere we have influence—as parents, teachers, leaders. We cannot simply tell our children to be honest if we lie. We cannot tell them not to steal if we steal. We cannot tell them not to swear if we swear. They see everything; they hear everything.

> "You who teach others, do you not teach yourself? You who preach against stealing, do you steal?" (Romans 2:21)

If we want our children to believe God is important, we cannot merely say it. Our actions must demonstrate it. When we consistently drop church for sports tournaments or birthday parties, when we prioritize marathons and recitals over Sunday worship, our children learn the real message: God matters less than everything else. The hypocrisy is far more powerful than the words.

> "You, then, why do you judge your brother or sister? Or why do you treat them with contempt? For we will all stand before God's judgment seat." (Romans 14:10)

We must answer to God for our own conduct. So judge yourself first. Take the plank out.

## 2. Judge Others (With Wisdom and Humility)

But here's what's remarkable: verse 5 doesn't say "never judge others."

> "You hypocrite, first take the plank out of your own eye, and then you will see clearly to remove the speck from your brother's eye." (Matthew 7:5)

The language shifts. Once you've removed your own plank—once you can see clearly—you're actually *called* to remove the speck from your brother's eye. The Bible expects judgment. It demands it.

Jesus himself commands us to judge:

> "Judge for yourselves what I say." (1 Corinthians 10:15)
>
> "Judge for yourselves: Is it proper for a woman to pray to God with her head uncovered?" (1 Corinthians 11:13)

And later in Matthew:

> "Watch out for false prophets. They come to you in sheep's clothing, but inwardly they are ferocious wolves." (Matthew 7:15)

To identify a false prophet, you must judge his teaching. Jesus approves of this judgment. He also tells us:

> "Stop judging by mere appearances, but instead judge correctly." (John 7:24)

The problem isn't judging. The problem is judging *badly*—judging by appearances, by headlines, by our prejudices and tinted glasses. We don't go beyond the surface. We're not educated about the issues. We don't think long-term. We judge based on what we want to believe rather than what is actually true.

**Judge correctly means:**

- **Don't prejudge.** Read the best arguments from both sides, not just the caricatures. Assume reasonable people on both sides and engage with their strongest case.
- **Ask curious questions.** Chase down inconsistencies. It's easy to dismiss the other side with labels—"You must be a racist"—but it takes work to judge correctly.
- **Recognize the weight of the responsibility.** Judgment is not casual. It carries real consequences.

The speck in your brother's eye isn't trivial. It causes pain. A child with something in their eye screams. If not removed, it scratches the cornea, leads to infection, potentially to blindness. We judge because we love. The child who is disciplined and corrected—who is judged—is the child who is cared for. The neglected, unloved child is the one who was never judged.

So judge with humility. Judge understanding that your judgment is incomplete, that only God's judgment is final:

> "Do not pronounce judgment before the time, before the Lord comes, who will bring to light the things now hidden in darkness and will disclose the purposes of the heart." (1 Corinthians 4:5)

Judge correctly, out of love, with the hope of repentance. The church—fallible people calling each other into account—is a good thing:

> "Let us consider how we may spur one another on toward love and good deeds, not giving up meeting together... encouraging one another." (Hebrews 10:24-25)

## 3. Don't Judge (Know the Limits)

Yet there's a third category of judgment Jesus addresses:

> "Do not give dogs what is sacred; do not throw your pearls to pigs. If you do, they may trample them under their feet, and turn and tear you to pieces." (Matthew 7:6)

The "pearls" here likely represent wisdom and discernment—the very ability to judge well. The "dogs" and "pigs" represent those who reject truth, who are hostile to the gospel. The point is stark: sometimes don't bother. Some people will not receive correction. Some will use your judgment against you. When you offer wisdom to the unwilling, they may "turn and tear you to pieces."

This is different from the second category. With brothers and sisters in the faith, we judge to restore. But with those actively hostile to truth, there is a time to step back. Leave them to God's judgment. Don't waste your pearls.

## Humility and Mercy

The thread running through all three movements is humility. God's standard is one. We are all accountable.

> "Who are you to condemn someone else's servants? Their own master will judge whether they stand or fall." (Romans 14:4)
>
> "Judgment will be merciless to one who has shown no mercy; mercy triumphs over judgment." (James 2:13)

And a final warning against a critical spirit:

> "Do not complain, brethren, against one another, so that you yourselves may not be judged; behold, the Judge is standing right at the door." (James 5:9)

The good life—the life Jesus calls us to—requires honest self-examination, wise and loving correction of others, and the restraint to know when silence is better than words. It requires recognizing that we stand under the same God, measured by the same standard, and answerable to the same Judge.
