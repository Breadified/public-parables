# Luke 15:11-32

**Title:** The Parable of the Lost Sons
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction

Jesus tells a parable about two sons who become lost—but unlike those skilled at survival, these sons face something far more serious: the consequences of losing their relationship with their father.

Parables are more than stories; they make a point to those who listen. As we examine this teaching, we must pay attention not only to what happens in the story, but also to what it reveals about our own hearts and our relationship with God.

## The Younger Brother

### Lost His Sonship

> "There was a man who had two sons. The younger one said to his father, 'Father, give me my share of the estate.' So he divided his property between them." (Luke 15:11-12)

The younger son makes a shocking request: he asks for his inheritance before his father has died. This is far more serious than a simple loan. In his culture, asking for an inheritance while the father still lives is effectively telling him, "I wish you were dead so I could take what's mine."

Beyond the disrespect, the son is declaring something profound: "I don't want to be your son anymore." By this act, he surrenders his sonship entirely. The father, remarkably, does not refuse. He liquidates his assets to give his son what he demands.

### Lost His Wealth

> "Not long after that, the younger son got together all he had, set off for a distant country and there squandered his wealth in wild living." (Luke 15:13)

The son doesn't just leave—he flees to a distant country, as far from his father as possible. With freedom from parental guidance, he does what many young people with sudden wealth would do: he spends it recklessly on wild living. Without his father's presence to teach him wisdom about money, he wastes everything.

This illustrates a profound truth: when our parents guide how we use resources, they're not restricting us—they're teaching us wisdom. The younger son rejected that wisdom and paid the price.

### Lost in Desperation

> "After he had spent everything, there was a severe famine in that whole country, and he began to be in need. So he went and hired himself out to a citizen of that country, who sent him to his fields to feed pigs. He longed to fill his stomach with the pods that the pigs were eating, but no one gave him anything." (Luke 15:14-16)

Desperation forces the son into the lowest work available: feeding pigs. For a Jewish young man, this was culturally catastrophic. Pigs were ceremonially unclean animals; the very work defiled him spiritually. His situation had become so degrading that even the pigs ate better than he did.

The famine compounds his misery. What little money he might have scraped together became worthless as inflation destroyed any purchasing power. He had lost his sonship, his wealth, his dignity, and his place in his community. He was utterly alone.

### Lost His Pride and Found Repentance

> "When he came to his senses, he said, 'How many of my father's hired servants have food to spare, and here I am starving to death! I will set out and go back to my father and say to him: Father, I have sinned against heaven and against you. I am no longer worthy to be called your son; make me like one of your hired servants.'" (Luke 15:17-19)

In desperation, the son finally sees clearly. He recognizes his sin against his father and decides to return, not expecting restoration but hoping for employment as a hired hand—anything is better than this.

> "So he got up and went to his father. But while he was still a long way off, his father saw him and was filled with compassion for him; he ran to his son, threw his arms around him and kissed him." (Luke 15:20)

Instead of anger or demands for repayment, the father explodes with compassion. He runs to embrace his son while he's still covered in the filth of the pig sty. He doesn't wait for an apology; he doesn't lecture about wasted inheritance. He simply welcomes his son home with overwhelming love.

> "The father said to his servants, 'Quick! Bring the best robe and put it on him. Put a ring on his finger and sandals on his feet. Bring the fattened calf and kill it. Let's have a feast and celebrate. For this son of mine was dead and is alive again; he was lost and is found.'" (Luke 15:22-24)

The father restores everything: his clothing, his status, his place at the table. A great celebration erupts—not a grudging acceptance, but genuine joy that his son has come home.

## The Older Brother

### The Dutiful Son's Hidden Rebellion

> "Meanwhile, the older son was in the field. When he came near the house, he heard music and dancing. So he called one of the servants and asked him what was going on. 'Your brother has come,' he replied, 'and your father has killed the fattened calf because he has him back safe and sound.' The older brother became angry and refused to go in." (Luke 15:25-28)

The older son has been faithful—working in the field, obeying orders, staying home. Yet when he hears of his younger brother's return and the feast being held, he becomes furious. He refuses even to enter the house.

By rejecting the celebration, he disrespects not just his brother but his father. He shames the host by refusing to participate in the feast. The father must leave his own party to plead with his son to come inside.

### The Heart of an Employee, Not a Son

> "But he answered his father, 'Look! All these years I've been slaving for you and never disobeyed your orders. Yet you never gave me even a young goat so I could celebrate with my friends. But when this son of yours who has squandered your property with prostitutes comes home, you kill the fattened calf for him!'" (Luke 15:29-30)

The older son's response reveals the true problem: he has been "slaving" for his father, not serving as a son. He treats obedience as work deserving wages rather than as the natural response of a child to a parent.

Notice his tone: he addresses his father with "Look!"—the same disrespectful opening his younger brother used when demanding his inheritance. Though the older brother never left home, his heart has been far from his father all along. He kept the rules not out of love but out of a desire for reward.

The tragedy is clear: the older son wants to reduce himself to a hired servant, someone who works for payment. Meanwhile, his younger brother, having lost his sonship through sin, seeks restoration. But the older brother is voluntarily downgrading from sonship to employment.

### The Father's Response

> "The father said, 'My son, you are always with me, and everything I have is yours. But we had to celebrate and be glad, because this brother of yours was dead and is alive again; he was lost and is found.'" (Luke 15:31-32)

The father's answer exposes the older son's misunderstanding. Everything the father has is already his—not as wages for obedience, but simply because he is his son. If the older son wanted a celebration with friends, he could have asked. His father would have given it gladly, not as payment for obedience, but out of love.

The older son failed to understand what sonship means: we belong to our parents not because we've earned it through behavior, but because we are their children. Similarly, our obedience to God is not a transaction—it's the natural expression of being loved children of a loving Father.

## The Father's Character

### Slow to Anger

When the younger son essentially says, "I wish you were dead," the father doesn't strike him or cast him out. When the older son speaks arrogantly about "slaving" and demands a reward, the father doesn't lose patience. Instead, he responds with gentle explanation, not harsh judgment.

### Abounding in Love

The father's love is most visible in how quickly he forgives and restores. He sees his younger son from a distance and runs to him. He doesn't wait to hear excuses or demands anything in return—he simply welcomes him with embraces and kisses. 

Even to the older son, despite being treated as a servant rather than a father, he offers everything he possesses and pleads for him to come and celebrate.

## The Application: Who Are We?

### Younger Brother or Older Brother?

Jesus poses a question to all who listen: Which son are you?

Are you the younger brother—someone who has run from God, squandered your life on selfish pursuits, and felt the weight of living apart from your Father in heaven? Perhaps you've chased wealth, status, relationships, or pleasures, only to find yourself broken and desperate.

Or are you the older brother—someone who follows the rules outwardly, lives a respectable life, but harbors bitterness in your heart? You may think, "I've done what I'm supposed to do. Why does God allow suffering? Why do sinners seem to prosper?" Your obedience is really just work you're performing to earn God's favor, not the loving response of a child to a Father.

Perhaps you're both at different times in your life. Regardless, the parable tells us this truth: both types of sin separate us from God. The younger brother's rebellion and the older brother's bitter self-righteousness both break the relationship with the Father.

### The Choice Before Us

Just as the parable ends without telling us the older son's final decision, Jesus leaves us with a choice. Will you, like the younger son, acknowledge your lostness and return to your Father in heaven? Will you repent—turn around completely—and trust that God will receive you with the same overwhelming love this father showed?

Or will you, like the older son, remain outside, bitter and separated, refusing to believe that your Father's love is not based on your performance but on your identity as his child?

### God's Judgment and Grace

If we remain separated from God through our sin or our pride, we will one day face His judgment. But there is one way to be restored: through Jesus Christ himself.

Jesus came to do what the older brother refused to do—he went searching for the lost. Through his death on the cross, Jesus paid the penalty for all our sins and restored our relationship with God. He offers us sonship again, not as employees working for wages, but as beloved children of God the Father.

### The Invitation

Today, you face the same decision the two brothers faced. Will you:

- Repent of living your life without considering your relationship with God?
- Return to Jesus and trust in his death and resurrection for your sake?
- Accept him as your Lord and Savior, and allow God to be your heavenly Father?

Time is limited. One day, God will return to his house to the great heavenly feast. Those who have repented and returned will celebrate with him forever. Those who refused will be left outside.

The choice is yours. Repent today.
