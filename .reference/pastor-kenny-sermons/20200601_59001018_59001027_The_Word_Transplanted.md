# James 1:18-27

**Title:** The Word Transplanted
**Date:** 10 June 2020
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Wasted Knowledge

Twenty years ago, I graduated from university with an accounting degree. It was largely wasted knowledge—all theory and no practice. When I entered church ministry, that degree became completely useless. All those years of work and cramming felt like a loss.

But this raises a more important question: **What have we wasted in our knowledge of God?**

The challenge James presents is not merely intellectual. He calls us to treat Bible knowledge differently than academic knowledge. We cannot remain passive receivers of God's truth. Life's main game, as James puts it, is to become not just a *receiver* of God's word but a *doer* of it.

---

## 1. Listen to the Word

> Therefore, get rid of all moral filth and the evil that is so prevalent and humbly accept the word planted in you, which can save you. (James 1:21)

### The Gospel: The Good News

The word James speaks of is the gospel—the good news of Jesus's death and resurrection. As he writes:

> He chose to give us birth through the word of truth, that we might be a kind of firstfruits of all he created. (James 1:18)

This word of truth that comes to us is ultimately about Christ. As Paul reminds us:

> The faith and love that spring from the hope stored up for you in heaven and about which you have already heard in the true message of the gospel. (Colossians 1:5)

It is remarkable that knowledge of Jesus saves us. Not works. Not rituals. Not the perfect life. The gospel alone—the good news that our sins are paid for by Christ, not by us—saves us. But this word must be accepted. It must be planted within us.

### Accepting the Word: A Heart Transplant

The image of being "planted" evokes a seed taking root in soil. But James's meaning runs deeper. A heart transplant is a better illustration. The word of God is planted in us, yet it is fundamentally alien to our nature. We are moral filth; the word is pure. Without active acceptance, the body can reject this alien DNA.

This is why James continually encourages us to keep listening. We must maintain the transplanted heart. We must take the medicines that prevent our bodies from rejecting it.

As Peter understood:

> Lord, to whom shall we go? You have the words of eternal life. (John 6:68)

We must keep eating His words as frequently as possible, continually nourishing our belief in:
- Jesus as God
- His coming to save us
- His substitutionary death for our forgiveness
- His resurrection
- His return as judge of the world

Keep accepting this word. Remain a convicted Christian who believes this is the true word of God.

## 2. Be Quick to Listen

> Everyone should be quick to listen, slow to speak and slow to become angry, because human anger does not produce the righteousness that God desires. (James 1:19-20)

### Quick to Listen

Listening can be deceptively passive. Our eyes don't "switch off" like a light, but our ears can easily drift into background noise. "Quick to listen" means something active: to be ready, alert, and sensitized to God's word—like a voice-activated device, always prepared to respond.

Many of us who have been Christians for years, who have heard countless sermons and attended numerous Bible studies, can fall into a trap. We've heard it all before. The word becomes background noise. We zone out.

But notice: it is not the preacher's words that will change you. It is not the Bible study leader's words that will transform you. **It is God's word—Scripture—that has power.** You might ignore the talk or study around you, but the word itself stands before you and speaks. Do not let distraction become your excuse to leave unchanged. Read it. Listen to it.

### Slow to Speak

The slowness of speech can appear as a weakness. Moses himself complained:

> I have never been eloquent, neither in the past nor since you have spoken to your servant. I am slow of speech and tongue. (Exodus 4:10)

Yet there is wisdom here. Instead of being quick to listen, we are quick to judge, quick to boast, quick to condemn. When we encounter parts of Scripture that conflict with our culture, our worldview, or our friends' opinions, we judge the Bible itself. This is selective listening.

Be slow to speak against the word. Listen first. Justify your resistance later—if you still must. This applies equally in our relationships with one another. Practice reflective listening; check that you have understood the facts correctly.

There is even nobility in slowness of speech. Moses, slow in speech, became known as the most humble man in the world. Perhaps his stuttering served him well. It forced him to think twice before speaking. If English is our second language, or if we naturally speak slowly, perhaps that is not a weakness—it makes us more thoughtful, more careful. In arguments, our tendency is to appear smart. But slow down. Ask questions. Let curiosity, not offense, guide your response. Be slow in your texts. Take time to read; take even more time to reply.

### Slow to Become Angry

Why does anger matter in listening? James suggests that anger is often our defensive reaction when we dislike what we're hearing—especially when it convicts us personally. We live in an age of outrage culture, yet God's word will offend us:

> For the word of God is alive and active. Sharper than any double-edged sword, it penetrates even to dividing soul and spirit, joints and marrow; it judges the thoughts and attitudes of the heart. (Hebrews 4:12)

When we feel defensive, we often employ the strategy: "Attack is the best form of defense." We raise our voices, show our temper, shut our ears. But anger does not produce the righteousness God requires. If you remain defensive against God, can you expect to change?

Instead, God desires humility and teachability:

> These are the ones I look on with favor: those who are humble and contrite in spirit, and who tremble at my word. (Isaiah 66:2)

Let us receive God's criticism humbly, trusting that His correction is an expression of His love for us.

## 3. Do the Word

> Do not merely listen to the word, and so deceive yourselves. Do what it says. (James 1:22)

The true believer is interested in obedience, not mere listening. When we disobey God, we like to pretend we're listening while doing nothing at all—much like children who claim to have heard us but then forget.

### The Mirror Illustration

> Anyone who listens to the word but does not do what it says is like someone who looks at his face in a mirror and, after looking at himself, goes away and immediately forgets what he looks like. (James 1:23-24)

To glance in a mirror, see yourself clearly, then walk away and immediately forget—this is absurd. Yet this is exactly what listening without doing is like. We look, see ourselves clearly in God's word, then forget. We forget that we have a new heart. We forget that the word is implanted in us. We forget what we determined to do.

This describes a person too distracted, too forgetful, too double-minded. Such religion becomes merely a form of godliness while denying its power.

### The Blessed Doer

But there is a better way:

> But whoever looks intently into the perfect law that gives freedom, and continues in it—not forgetting what they have heard, but doing it—they will be blessed in what they do. (James 1:25)

God's law gives freedom. When you look intently into this law, you need not return again and again. You don't need to re-examine it obsessively. You simply do not forget, and you act. Then comes blessing—true happiness.

This is the difference between wasted and transformed knowledge. We must put our knowledge of God into action.

### Avoiding Self-Deception

We have a remarkable human capacity for self-deception. We convince ourselves that we are righteous when we are not. We make God in the image of ourselves. We tell ourselves that because we don't murder or steal, we must be acceptable to God. We are deluded.

When we forget our sin, we forget our great need for forgiveness. When we forget forgiveness, we forget how good Jesus has been to us. Let us stop talking about how good we are. Let us abandon pride in our piety.

## 4. Pure Religion Defined

> Those who consider themselves religious and yet do not keep a tight rein on their tongues deceive themselves, and their religion is worthless. Religion that God our Father accepts as pure and faultless is this: to look after orphans and widows in their distress and to keep oneself from being polluted by the world. (James 1:26-27)

### What Pure Religion Is Not

What is the point of claiming piety but showing no deeds? What is the point of claiming to listen but taking no action? Pure religion cannot be reduced to Sunday performance—sitting quietly, going through the motions, ticking boxes, engaging in polite conversation—if there is no change by Monday.

Nor is it excitement and planning without action: asking great questions, making earnest commitments, scheduling appointments, yet tomorrow nothing has changed.

Such religion is worthless. This redefines what we mean by religion entirely. We might expect James to speak of more prayer, more fasting, more rituals. Instead, he redefines religion as love for one another. As John reminds us:

> By this all men will know that you are my disciples, if you love one another. (John 13:35)

### What Pure Religion Is

**Control your tongue.** Your words reveal whether you have been transformed by God's word. Idle, cruel, or careless speech—especially in our digital age—betrays a religion that is merely theoretical.

**Look after orphans and widows.** In Bible times, these were society's most vulnerable. They had no welfare system to sustain them. Today, widows remain vulnerable, needing friends and genuine support—including wives whose husbands work far away. In our churches, we have spiritual orphans—young believers and brothers and sisters from non-Christian families who need mentorship, care, practical help with food or transport, guidance in discipline.

If you claim to be religious, let it show in your love for one another. Let your lives demonstrate how different you are from a world of rampant selfishness and wickedness.

The world listens and does nothing. The world looks in the mirror, walks away, and forgets. The world theorizes without action.

**The Christian must be different.**

---

## Conclusion: Do Not Waste

We have knowledge of God. We know the gospel. We have heard the word. We have been born again through it. Do not waste this knowledge as I wasted my accounting degree.

**Jesus has done what we cannot do. Now we must do what He calls us to do.**
