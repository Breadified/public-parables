# 2 Samuel 1:1-27

**Title:** The King is Dead
**Date:** August 2023
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Death of a Leader

When national leaders fall, reactions reveal our character. Recent headlines show people celebrating the demise of political opponents, yet this reveals something troubling. In Scripture, we see a different response—one that teaches us about honor, loyalty, and grief.

## 1. The King is Dead (1:1-4)

> After the death of Saul, David returned from striking down the Amalekites and stayed in Ziklag two days.

On Mount Gilboa, the Philistines defeated Israel. Saul, facing capture, fell on his own sword rather than suffer at his enemies' hands. Meanwhile, David remained in exile at Ziklag—hunted by Saul yet protecting Israel through his own warfare against the Amalekites. Despite being forced to flee, David remained loyal to God's kingdom and God's anointed king.

A young messenger arrives with the news, his torn clothes and dust-covered head signaling disaster.

## 2. Lament for the Anointed One (11-12)

> Then David and all the men with him took hold of their clothes and tore them. They mourned and wept and fasted till evening for Saul and his son Jonathan, and for the army of the Lord and for the nation of Israel, because they had fallen by the sword.

**David's Surprising Grief**

David's response to his enemy's death was profound grieving. He didn't celebrate; he tore his clothes, mourned, wept, and fasted. He even composed a lament ordered to be taught throughout Judah—a national day of remembrance, like Anzac Day, meant to honor the fallen and remember war's true cost.

This is shocking because Saul had hunted David relentlessly. Yet David understood something essential: **Saul was still the anointed one of God.** When David had the chance to kill Saul in a cave, he refused, saying:

> "The Lord forbid that I should do such a thing to my master, the Lord's anointed, or lay my hand on him; for he is the anointed of the Lord."

Once anointed by God's prophet, a leader remains under God's protection and deserves loyalty—regardless of personal grievance.

## 3. Judgment on Those Who Harm the Anointed One (5-10, 13-16)

The young messenger claims he killed Saul at Saul's own request—a different account from 1 Samuel's record of suicide. Most likely, the Amalekite saw Saul dead, claimed credit, and brought the crown and armband as "proof," expecting David to reward him for eliminating his rival.

> David asked him, "Why weren't you afraid to lift your hand to destroy the Lord's anointed?"
> Then David called one of his men and said, "Go, strike him down!" So he struck him down, and he died.

**Why Such Harsh Judgment?**

1. **His testimony condemned him:** He admitted killing God's anointed, fully conscious of what he'd done.

2. **Lack of conscience:** David had been guilt-ridden merely for cutting Saul's robe; this man felt no fear about killing a king.

3. **Circumstance is irrelevant:** Even if Saul requested it, there is no "good" euthanasia when taking God's anointed.

**The Shadow of Jesus**

Jesus is the ultimate Anointed One. Those who taste His goodness yet willfully reject Him face grave judgment:

> To their loss they are crucifying the Son of God all over again and subjecting him to public disgrace. (Hebrews 6:6)

## Application Today

**Be Loyal to Authority and Law**

> Submit yourselves for the Lord's sake to every human authority... Show proper respect to everyone, love the family of believers, fear God, honor the emperor. (1 Peter 2:13-17)

In modern government, loyalty is to the system—to law, constitution, and justice itself—not merely to a person.

**Be Loyal to One Another**

When disputes arise among believers, taking them before the world's courts damages the church's witness:

> If any of you has a dispute with another, do you dare to take it before the ungodly for judgment instead of before the Lord's people? (1 Corinthians 6:1)

Paul distinguishes criminal matters (which authorities should judge) from civil disputes. For broken promises and daily hurts, we should solve them within the family. Taking family grievances public is like airing dirty laundry before strangers—it proves we're dysfunctional.

When Christians attack each other publicly, we give enemies ammunition: "You preach love but can't even love one another."

**Leave Judgment to God**

> Or do you not know that wrongdoers will not inherit the kingdom of God? (1 Corinthians 6:9)

God will judge. Our calling is to remain loyal—to authority, to law, and supremely to one another as God's people. That is David's legacy.
