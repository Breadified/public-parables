# Ecclesiastes 1:1-18; 12:8-14

**Title:** The Limits of Wisdom

**Date:** August 16, 2018

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Search for Knowledge

Why do we pursue education? We attend university seeking knowledge—aiming to understand how the world fits together, to find meaning and satisfaction. Sydney University's charter captures this aspiration perfectly: the advancement of "religion and morality and the promotion of useful knowledge." We invest years and tens of thousands of dollars because we believe knowledge will make us better.

Yet the Bible offers a sobering perspective on this pursuit. The Book of Ecclesiastes, written by King Solomon—the wisest man on earth—provides crucial insight into the limits of human wisdom.

## The Limits of Knowledge (Ecclesiastes 1:1-18)

Solomon, the teacher and assembler of knowledge (the Hebrew word *Qahal* means "gatherer," from which we get "Ecclesiastes"), devoted himself entirely to seeking wisdom. He writes:

> I applied my heart to seek and to search out by wisdom all that is done under heaven... I have acquired great wisdom, surpassing all who were over Jerusalem before me, and my heart has had great experience of wisdom and knowledge.

Yet his stunning conclusion was this:

> It is an unhappy business that God has given to the children of man to be busy with. All is vanity and a striving after wind.

The Hebrew word *hevel*—translated as "vanity"—means something ephemeral and temporary, like breath or mist. Solomon discovered that knowledge itself is fleeting. Consider your own studies: you memorize everything the night before exams, then weeks later, where is that knowledge? Gone, like breath on a mirror.

This pattern repeats throughout our age. Scientific "knowledge" constantly shifts: one year butter is harmful, the next year it's beneficial. The food pyramid that guided nutrition for thirty years has been largely discarded. Despite all our accumulated knowledge, obesity continues rising. Knowledge proves unstable, constantly being revised and corrected.

Solomon recognized another truth:

> For in much wisdom is much vexation, and he who increases knowledge increases sorrow.

The modern age celebrated reason and empirical knowledge as salvation, believing we could think our way to heaven. Yet this pursuit led to catastrophe: World War I killed twenty million. World War II claimed eighty million. For all our scientific advancement, we became more efficient at killing one another. All that knowledge proved useless and wearisome.

Ecclesiastes presents an unrelenting cycle:

> A generation goes, and a generation comes, but the earth remains forever. The sun rises and goes down, and hastens to the place where it rises. The wind blows to the south and goes around to the north; around and around goes the wind, and on its circuits the wind returns. All streams run to the sea, but the sea is not full... All things are full of weariness; a man cannot utter it; the eye is not satisfied with seeing, nor the ear filled with hearing. What has been is what will be, and what has been done is what will be done, and there is nothing new under the sun.

We live in repetition. There is nothing genuinely new. Yet the modern age promised progress through knowledge. Postmodernism reacted against this, rejecting reason entirely in favor of instinct and passion. Now we shout slogans instead of having conversations, substituting force for persuasion. The postmodern era tells us to unlearn, to start from scratch.

But what confidence do they have that they'll succeed where knowledge failed? Especially when they don't address the fundamental problem:

> What is crooked cannot be made straight, and what is lacking cannot be counted.

The issue isn't knowledge itself—it's the knower. Sin corrupts everything we touch. We are fundamentally bent, like a car with a dent that's visible no matter how carefully we inspect it. Knowledge can extend life from sixty to eighty years, but we're still mortal. We still face death. The problem isn't fixable through accumulation of facts.

Furthermore, we don't know what we don't know. There is too much. We need an overall perspective—a framework for knowledge, an ethic to guide what we do with what we learn. Some academics deserve our attention; others don't. Some ideas should be adopted; others rejected. We need guidance.

## The Beginning of Wisdom (Ecclesiastes 12:13-14)

After exploring everything, Solomon offers his conclusion:

> The end of the matter; all has been heard. Fear God and keep his commandments, for this is the whole of man. For God will bring every deed into judgment, with every secret thing, whether good or evil.

*Fear God* is both the beginning and end of wisdom. In final analysis, we cannot find perfect answers to all our questions. Fear the Lord.

*Keep his commandments.* We can invent the atom bomb, but only the Bible can tell us whether we should use it. Knowledge tells us *how*; Scripture tells us whether we *should*.

This applies to everyone. Our inquiries are necessarily limited. God has not left us wondering in darkness about what to do. Instead of blind accumulation of knowledge, we're called to discern, to trust Him, and to follow His commands.

## The Beginning of Wisdom Revealed (1 Corinthians 1:18-19)

In these last days, God has revealed true wisdom:

> The word of the cross is folly to those who are perishing, but to us who are being saved it is the power of God. I will destroy the wisdom of the wise, and the discernment of the discerning I will thwart.

God came in human form to redeem mankind through the cross. By conventional wisdom, this is foolish: how can a dying man save anyone? Yet in God's wisdom—not man's—this is His masterpiece. The righteous died for the unrighteous, bringing us to God.

Jesus embodied true wisdom: He feared God and obeyed His commands wholeheartedly. He was perhaps the one out of a thousand—truly wise. The gospel calls us to the same wisdom: repent and believe.

Scripture provides the lens through which we read the world. It is the lamp by which we understand reality. Theology and science must go hand in hand. Education should advance both useful knowledge *and* religion and morality.

Don't opt out of Scripture. Opt in, and discover true purpose.
