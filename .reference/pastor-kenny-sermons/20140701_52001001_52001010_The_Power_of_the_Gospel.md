# 1 Thessalonians 1:1-10

**Title:** The Power of the Gospel
**Date:** 6 July 2014
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Paradox of Power

We often misunderstand what power really means. Power, at its simplest, is the ability to do things. But our cultural understanding of power is deeply flawed.

We see power displayed in large conferences that draw twenty thousand people, in prestigious speakers, in chart-topping music releases. We see power in influential Christians who move with ease through society, who have the right words, successful careers, functional families, and material blessing.

Then we look at our own lives and see we're no different from the rest of the world. Christianity seems to make no difference at all. We see the world's problems that desperately need power to solve them, yet we feel weak and unable to do anything. We think, "If only we had more power."

But Paul's letter to the Thessalonians reveals something startling: the gospel's power operates in ways entirely opposite to what the world calls power.

## A Powerful City

Thessalonica itself was a city of power and prestige. Its very name meant "Victory of the Thessalians"—a city that understood conquest and dominance. During the first century, it was the capital of one of Rome's four districts in Macedonia, later elevated to capital of all Greek provinces. Its harbor, built by Roman engineers, made it a center of commerce and influence.

Even today, Thessalonica remains Greece's second-largest city, home to the nation's largest university. In recent years it's been ranked as the world's fifth-best party city, included in National Geographic's top tourist destinations, and declared by the Financial Times as Europe's best mid-sized city for the future.

The Thessalonians were accustomed to power—the kind the world recognizes and celebrates.

This was the city Paul, Silas, and Timothy visited. And their visit brought power with it, though not the kind Thessalonica expected.

## The Gospel's Unexpected Power

Paul writes in verse 5:

> Our gospel came to you not simply with words, but also with power, with the Holy Spirit and with deep conviction. You know how we lived among you for your sake.

Paul spent only three weeks in Thessalonica before opposition forced him to flee (as recorded in Acts 17). In that brief time, he proclaimed to them the gospel—the good news of Jesus' death and resurrection. But these were no mere words. They came with power.

Yet this power expressed itself in three surprising ways that challenged everything the Thessalonians believed about strength.

### They Endured Suffering

> You became imitators of us and of the Lord; in spite of severe suffering, you welcomed the message with the joy given by the Holy Spirit. (v. 6)

The believers in Thessalonica faced tremendous persecution. Acts 17 describes such intense opposition that Paul had to be smuggled out of the city for his safety. Yet despite this severe suffering, they welcomed the gospel with joy.

This seems paradoxical. We don't associate suffering with power—suffering suggests weakness and vulnerability. Yet Paul says they received the gospel with power precisely because they believed despite the cost.

Think about it: Why would anyone suffer for something they knew was false? The Thessalonians' willingness to endure persecution demonstrated the authenticity and power of their faith. They weren't attracted to comfortable words; they were gripped by truth so real they would stake their lives on it.

Paul reminds them what this power looks like:

> Your work produced by faith, your labor prompted by love, and your endurance inspired by hope in our Lord Jesus Christ. (v. 3)

The power isn't miraculous in the sensational sense. It's the power of steadiness—the ability to keep working, to keep laboring, to keep enduring through the worst circumstances. This is the power that sustained them.

### They Imitated Christ's Suffering

The Thessalonians knew Paul's life intimately: "You know how we lived among you for your sake" (v. 5). And Paul's life was marked by suffering for Christ. He could tell them honestly:

> We had previously suffered and been insulted in Philippi, as you know, but with the help of our God we dared to tell you his gospel in spite of strong opposition. (2:2)

Paul wasn't asking them to do anything he hadn't done first. He was simply living out what he'd learned from Jesus himself.

Jesus carried his cross. Jesus suffered on our behalf. And now, as his followers, we also suffer for him:

> It has been granted to you on behalf of Christ not only to believe in him, but also to suffer for him. (Philippians 1:29)

Not merely to believe in him with our words, but to be willing to suffer for Jesus—to live for him in deeds, not just doctrine.

This doesn't mean we should seek out suffering needlessly or suffer for trivial reasons. But it does mean that genuine godliness naturally produces opposition. As Paul writes:

> Everyone who wants to live a godly life in Christ Jesus will be persecuted. (2 Timothy 3:12)

The more we separate ourselves from the world's values, the more the world will hate us and what we stand for. This isn't failure; it's the inevitable result of faithfulness.

So the power of the gospel is not supernatural spectacle. It's suffering power. It's the power to keep working, keep laboring, keep enduring. This is what it means to receive the Holy Spirit with joy—to be given deep conviction about our sin and absolute certainty of God's faithfulness.

Consider how differently the world and the gospel view generosity:

- **The world's model:** Become successful first, then you'll have the resources to be generous. More success equals more generosity equals greater godliness.
- **The gospel's model:** Be generous no matter what your circumstances. Generosity isn't determined by worldly success.

The world says, "I'll bless others when the world has blessed me."

The Christian says, "I'll bless others because the gospel has blessed me."

Don't resent the suffering that accompanies godliness. You're simply imitating your Master, who suffered at the hands of his enemies. The gospel is fundamentally a gospel of suffering—a gospel of delayed gratification where heaven is still to come and suffering is present now.

### They Spread the Gospel

> The Lord's message rang out from you not only in Macedonia and Achaia—your faith in God has become known everywhere. (v. 8)

Like a great bell tolling across the land, the news of the Thessalonians' faith rang out across northern and southern Greece. Paul writes this from Corinth in AD 52, just months after his visit. They had told their neighbors and friends. They gathered at synagogues to testify to the Jews. They did this despite fierce opposition. And their faith became known everywhere.

Here's the profound truth: **Evangelism springs from faithfulness, not from material power or social status.**

We sometimes hear that the best way to evangelize is through our social success. "When I become a lawyer or a doctor or a successful businessman, then others will see how great it is to be a Christian."

But if that's your reason for faith, you'll be disappointed. Real Christians are the ones who abandon prestigious careers to become missionaries. Christian lawyers often work in less glamorous, lower-paying positions so they have flexibility to serve their churches. Christians who earn good money often give it away. By the world's standards, they don't look very powerful at all.

Yet in God's eyes, this is profoundly powerful.

The gospel's testimony isn't "Look at me—my life is perfect, and you have problems, so become a Christian." It's rather, "I have problems too. So do you. Let's together be Christian and seek God's help."

Do others know you are a Christian? Does your faith ring out from you?

### They Served Their Living God

> They tell how you turned to God from idols to serve the living and true God, and to wait for his Son from heaven, whom he raised from the dead—Jesus, who rescues us from the coming wrath. (vv. 9-10)

The Thessalonians had turned from idolatry. Thessalonica was filled with temples to Greek gods—Nike, Zeus, and others. These gods represented power, strength, and wealth. Each was worshiped for different reasons, but they shared one essential characteristic: they were powerful. They might make mistakes, miscalculate, kidnap, wage wars among themselves, and commit adultery, but they were undeniably powerful.

The Thessalonians turned from these false gods to the truly powerful God—the living God who was raised from the dead.

But notice: their response wasn't passive waiting. It was active service.

> Whoever wants to become great among you must be your servant, and whoever wants to be first must be slave of all. For even the Son of Man did not come to be served, but to serve, and to give his life as a ransom for many. (Mark 10:42-45)

This inverts everything the world teaches about power. In the world's understanding, power means others serve you. A powerful, wealthy person has servants. The powerful Greek gods demanded worship but gave nothing in return.

Our God is utterly different. The Son of Man came to serve, not to be served. And his followers do the same:

- We serve without expecting return
- We practice humility
- We seek what is good for others, not ourselves
- We imitate the Son of Man

## A Powerful Model for Us

In conclusion, the power of the gospel lies not in flashy displays or impressive numbers. Real power isn't found in the most powerful export brands or in massive crowds.

**Real power lies in changed lives.** This is something no record, no statistic can measure. But we can see it—in each other's lives.

The gospel's power manifests in:
- Endurance through suffering
- Humbly telling others about Jesus so the message rings out
- Serving without expectation of return

Where is this power? It's here—in transformed hearts and minds. It's the power of the Holy Spirit to change wicked men and women into followers of Christ. It's the power that changed the Thessalonians, and it's the power available to us.

The Thessalonians became our model by imitating Paul, who imitated Jesus. We would do well to follow their example and, through them, follow our Master.
