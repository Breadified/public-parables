## 1. Shepherd God's Flock

### The Calling

> Be shepherds of God's flock that is under your care, serving as overseers. (1 Peter 5:2)

In ancient Israel, the shepherd was the primary metaphor for leadership. Peter addresses the elders—the *presbyters* and *overseers*—using these terms interchangeably. Leadership flows naturally from those with maturity and wisdom, though these leaders are often formally appointed, set apart from other mature believers to care for a specific flock.

You are not merely older individuals who naturally lead. You are *appointed* leaders—Connect leaders entrusted with the spiritual care of year 12s who come through this ministry. Lead with whatever maturity a university student can muster, modeling after the Chief Shepherd, Jesus Christ.

Consider Ezekiel's vision of Israel's failed shepherds (Ezekiel 34:1-10)—leaders who exploited the flock rather than caring for them. God promised a new shepherd would come. Jesus claimed this role:

> I am the good shepherd. The good shepherd lays down his life for the sheep. The hired hand is not the shepherd who owns the sheep. So when he sees the wolf coming, he abandons the sheep and runs away. (John 10:11-12)

Now, serving as overseers under the Chief Shepherd, we must shepherd with three essential qualities:

### Willingly

> Not because you must, but because you are willing, as God wants you to be. (1 Peter 5:2)

Leadership cannot be forced or done from guilt. It must be deliberate, a voluntary commitment made from informed conscience. This reflects Paul's principle about giving:

> Each person should give what they have decided in their heart to give, not reluctantly or under compulsion, for God loves a cheerful giver. (2 Corinthians 9:7)

The same applies to shepherding. Do it because you genuinely want to care for people, not because obligation drives you.

### Wantingly

> Not greedy for money, but eager to serve. (1 Peter 5:2)

The motivation matters. An eager overseer—one who *desires* this task—pursues a noble calling. Eagerness means putting genuine effort into the work, not requiring constant hype or external motivation. The gospel itself should compel you.

### Worthily

> Not lording it over those entrusted to you, but being examples to the flock. (1 Peter 5:3)

Avoid using leadership as a platform for private gain or wielding authority over those in your care. Instead, be an example through servanthood and sacrificial living. Don't seek titles, colored leadership badges, or status symbols—these distract from your actual task: shepherding the flock.

### The Real Distraction: Networks Over Flocks

Many distractions pull us from this task—ungodliness, idolatry, materialism. But there's a particular distraction emerging in our social age: we prioritize networks over congregations.

The Connect offers a unique opportunity: 130 new friends, all your age, from similar schools, with overlapping interests. It's the easiest way to make friends. But this can subtly distract you from what has been entrusted to you.

Consider what has changed for your generation. In previous decades, friendships were concentrated in the church. Your main social circle came from your congregation. Birthday parties and social gatherings were spread thin across the calendar—perhaps one or two 21st birthday celebrations a year. Now, with social networking, a single cohort generates 130 social invitations annually. It's mathematically impossible to maintain meaningful relationships with all of them.

Add to this the new social patterns around baptisms and church events. What used to be occasional Sunday celebrations now compete for consistent attendance. The financial costs have shifted too: where once the birthday person covered the meal, now guests pay for both themselves and the host—all while providing gifts. This financial reality pushes many young leaders toward part-time work, leaving even less time for their local flocks.

The result? We no longer think of our churches as our primary community. Church members become simply part of a larger, vaguer network. We're friends with a nebulous collection of people—who exactly? How often do we actually see them? Who keeps us accountable? Meanwhile, networks feel more exciting: same age, same schools, same interests. How can a congregation of 50 people—ranging from toddlers to the elderly—compete with that?

This shifts our loyalty. We stop caring for our local flock (as Paul describes in 1 Thessalonians 2:8). They're no longer our primary concern. Instead, we invest in networks of other Christian leaders, hanging with them because they understand our world and share our interests.

This attitude extends to campus ministry. We invest heavily there because campus ministries are growing and "need us." But we face a choice: both are good, yet both demand significant time.

**Three practical responses:**

First, recognize you can fit both into your life—one weekday, one weekend. But when ministries overlap or make competing demands (especially against Sunday mornings), you must prioritize. Your home church comes first.

Second, identify what has actually been entrusted to you. If you haven't been given a specific group or asked to care for particular people, then campus ministry is secondary to what you've committed to. Who are you specifically responsible for? A small group? Individuals to follow up with? If your youth group is entrusted to you but campus is not, then shepherd your youth. Don't abandon your group meetings—that's a poor example. Examine honestly: are you truly doing ministry there, or just attending a social scene?

Third, if you genuinely cannot do both well, choose one. If campus is truly primary, then commit wholeheartedly—even consider relocating to serve it properly. If you've committed to shepherd one flock and can only conscientiously care for one, then pick one and shepherd it faithfully. Two overlapping ministries become an excuse to be mediocre at both.

**Why does this problem persist?**

- Willingly issues? Perhaps guilt is driving you—you feel obligated to show up.
- Wantingly? Are you chasing two titles instead of one?
- Worthily? Does your sacrifice and example suffer because you're stretched too thin?

The Connect can become so exciting that we forget our actual responsibilities. We're called to encourage young believers to be stable Christians committed to a *fixed congregation*—not a nebulous network of Christians. Sometimes this means standing against the crowd. If the group wants to go drinking at a pub after Connect, you might need to make a stand, calling on whatever influence you have to say: "I don't think that's wise straight after an evening focused on our faith. It undermines what we've built here."

## 2. Participant in the Glory About to Be Revealed

### The Crown of Glory

> And when the Chief Shepherd appears, you will receive the crown of glory that will never fade away. (1 Peter 5:4)

What is this crown of glory? It's the people you've invested in—not socially, but spiritually. Those you've read Scripture with, kept accountable, encouraged and challenged, cared for with genuine concern. The fruit of your labor is your inheritance.

I learned this the hard way. I attended a prestigious school and was proud of my jersey—a symbol of belonging. But I lost it on a train. That glory faded. Earthly honors don't last.

But the people you shepherd? They endure. They're your eternal reward.

### Sowing and Reaping

> Whatever you sow today, you reap tomorrow. (2 Corinthians 9:6)

This principle applies everywhere. I see it with my own children. If I don't invest time in them, they become selfish and demanding. If I want them to be delightful, I must invest daily. I trained my late dog through consistent daily practice. The same applies to youth groups and congregations.

You want a thriving youth group? Invest.
You want a healthy church? Invest.
This invested flock is your glory.

### The Danger of Neglect

There's no such thing as leaving your church on the back burner while you train at campus. If you don't invest now, there will be no church worth returning to when you're older. You'll have abandoned something you once cared for.

Consider your actual commitments. Nobody asked you to care for 130 Connect people. Nobody requires you to attend every baptism. But your church leadership asked you to shepherd perhaps five high school boys or two high school girls. What happened to that promise? You committed to shepherding your youth group—where did that responsibility go?

### Flock Means People

Remember: a flock is composed of *people*. These are real individuals under your care—your year 7 follow-ups, the people you meet with one-on-one, the lives you're invested in. *They need you.* 

We often think of leadership as abstract—organizing networks, doing "stuff." But that's just trellis without vine growth. Real ministry happens through people.

### The Connect and Your Priorities

The Connect should never feel secondary to your church. You have spare capacity during these holidays—and each of you does, since these are vacation weeks with no school commitments. You have Monday afternoons from 4-6pm free. Nothing else runs then. Our leadership meetings happen only four times a year, and we skip Saturday night (usually party night anyway). You're not sacrificing much.

Remember: this training isn't just for The Connect. We hope it equips you back at your home church. We've talked about welcoming—I want that door always manned by a friendly face with a pen and name tag, ensuring no one stands alone. We've discussed God talk—understanding that every conversation matters. We'll explore more opportunities to meet people and disciple them (Matthew and discipleship).

## Conclusion

As you step into leadership—whether in The Connect or your local congregation—ask yourself: Am I shepherding willingly, wantingly, and worthily? Am I clear about what's been entrusted to me? Am I investing in the flock, or just managing networks?

The glory you'll carry into eternity isn't measured in social connections or titles. It's the spiritual fruit of lives you've touched, people you've shepherded, and the steady investment you made in their faith.

That glory never fades away.
