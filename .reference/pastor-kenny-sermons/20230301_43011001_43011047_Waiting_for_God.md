# John 11:1-47 (1-44)

**Title:** Waiting for God
**Date:** 19 March 2023 ARISE
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Being Late

We all know the consequences of being late: missing transport, failing to submit assignments on time, arriving too late to help someone in crisis. But in this passage, we encounter Jesus himself being late—dramatically, impossibly late.

As we journey through John's Gospel toward Easter, we meet a story that challenges our understanding of God's timing and His love.

---

## 1. The Impossible Death (1-16)

### The Sisters' Cry for Help

> Now a man named Lazarus was sick. He was from Bethany, the village of Mary and her sister Martha. (This Mary, whose brother Lazarus now lay sick, was the same one who poured perfume on the Lord and wiped his feet with her hair.) So the sisters sent word to Jesus, "Lord, the one you love is sick."

Bethany—the house of figs, a suburb of Jerusalem—is where this family lives. When Mary and Martha send word to Jesus, they appeal not just to His power but to His relationship with them: *"the one you love"* is sick. They aren't just calling a miracle worker; they're calling a dear friend.

And indeed, Jesus does love them:

> Now Jesus loved Martha and her sister and Lazarus.

If someone told you only this much—that Jesus loves these people and they've asked for His help—what would you expect? Of course He would help. It's like being best friends with a wealthy person who loves you: if you asked them for help with something urgent, they would drop everything. The expectation is obvious; the outcome is guaranteed.

### The Shocking Delay

But then something unexpected happens:

> When he heard this, Jesus said, "This sickness will not end in death. No, it is for God's glory so that God's Son may be glorified through it." So when he heard that Lazarus was sick, he stayed where he was two more days.

Jesus stays put. For two full days. The messenger had to travel a day to reach Jesus. Presumably Lazarus became ill right after the messenger left. So by the time Jesus arrives, four days will have passed since Lazarus died.

Two days might not sound like much—how long does it take to get dressed and call an Uber? But the sisters have sent a desperate message. They're letting Jesus know that someone He loves is seriously ill. This is urgent. Yet He waits.

### The Disciples' Confusion

The disciples are equally confused. When Jesus finally announces they should return to Judea, they object:

> "But Rabbi," they said, "a short while ago the Jews there tried to stone you, and yet you are going back?"

The danger is real. In the previous chapter, the Jewish leaders attempted to execute Jesus for blasphemy. Bethany is barely two miles from Jerusalem—less than three kilometers from where His enemies gather. Going back is walking directly into danger.

But Jesus is unafraid:

> Jesus answered, "Are there not twelve hours of daylight? Anyone who walks in the daytime will not stumble, for they see by this world's light. It is when a person walks at night that they stumble, for they have no light."

This echoes His teaching from chapter 9: there is work to be done while the light remains. This is daytime work—God's work. The timing is His, not ours. He has His own agenda.

### Why the Wait?

So why wait two days? Perhaps—symbolically, at least—to ensure Lazarus is truly dead. For Jesus, who works for eternal life, death is merely sleep. But for the disciples and the watching world, death must be unmistakable, undeniable. Four days in a tomb leaves no room for doubt.

When Jesus finally speaks plainly about what has happened, the disciples still don't understand:

> After he had said this, he went on to tell them, "Our friend Lazarus has fallen asleep; but I am going there to wake him up." His disciples replied, "Lord, if he sleeps, he will get better." Jesus had been speaking of his death, but his disciples thought he meant natural sleep.

In the middle of grief, in the middle of wrestling with danger and doubt, the disciples offer practical advice. Sleep is good for healing. Don't wake him up. Rest and recovery will help.

So Jesus speaks plainly:

> "Lazarus is dead, and for your sake I am glad I was not there, so that you may believe. But let us go to him."

This sounds harsh—glad He wasn't there? Yet it makes sense if the delay served to make the resurrection undeniable. But the disciples don't see it that way. Thomas's response reveals the despair:

> Then Thomas (also known as Didymus) said to the rest of the disciples, "Let us also go, that we may die with him."

Is Thomas being gung-ho or sarcastic? Probably sarcastic. *Great idea. Let's all go to Jerusalem and die together. What's the difference? We thought Jesus would save the people He loved from death. Apparently, that's not important to Him. So let's just all die.*

### The Lesson in Delay

This is the central tension: Why did Jesus allow Lazarus to die? The disciples expected one thing; Jesus did another. Jesus has His own timing. He isn't moved by our urgency, even when we appeal to His love.

Just as Jesus had His own timing about going to the Feast of Dedication—saying He wouldn't go, then going anyway—He has His own timing here. And the disciples must learn: you cannot boss around your millionaire best friend, even if He loves you and wants to help.

### Application: God's Apparent Lateness

God seems late. Terribly late. The world is overdue for judgment, overdue for fixing. These past years have worn us down: pandemic, inflation, economic crises, the stubborn persistence of seasons when we expect change. Winter itself refuses to end. And we're tired of waiting.

It hasn't been two days—it's been two thousand years since Jesus ascended. Many of us are sick of waiting. Maybe we're not, if we're young, healthy, comfortable, held in high regard by the world. But the rest of us? We've been waiting a long time.

What's taking so long? Doesn't God love us?

> The Lord is not slow in keeping his promise, as some understand slowness. He is patient with you, not wanting anyone to perish, but everyone to come to repentance. (2 Peter 3:9)

God is teaching us patience. And perhaps there is still more to learn. There is still too much pride in us. The Refiner's fire continues. We cannot jump the gun and take matters into our own hands. Like Lazarus, we need these two more days. God is waiting for us to die—to judgment day—so that His glory might be revealed.

God's love isn't the world's definition of love, where nothing negative ever occurs, where there's no rebuke or discipline. Love doesn't mean no suffering. Real love sometimes means delay. Sometimes it means allowing death.

## 2. The Impossible Resurrection (17-44)

### The Four Days

> On his arrival, Jesus found that Lazarus had already been in the tomb for four days.

The timeline is clear: a day for the messenger to reach Jesus, two days of waiting, a day to travel back. Which means Lazarus died just after the messenger left. By the time Jesus arrives, decomposition is well advanced—a detail Martha will pointedly remind us of.

Many Jews had come to comfort the sisters. Bethany was close enough to Jerusalem that mourners could easily visit. But there's a telling difference in how the sisters react to Jesus's arrival:

> When Martha heard that Jesus was coming, she went out to meet him, but Mary stayed at home.

Mary remains seated, grief-struck. Martha is active, moving toward Jesus. But even Martha's initial words carry accusation:

> "Lord," Martha said to Jesus, "if you had been here, my brother would not have died. But I know that even now God will give you whatever you ask."

There's a glimmer of hope in verse 22—*whatever you ask*—but it feels contingent, conditional. When Jesus responds, that hope fades:

> Jesus said to her, "Your brother will rise again." Martha answered, "I know he will rise again in the resurrection at the last day."

Martha knows the doctrine. The resurrection is a future event, abstract, distant—something she's learned in synagogue and Torah school. But not now. Not for her brother. Not in this moment.

### I Am the Resurrection

Jesus reorients her entire understanding:

> Jesus said to her, "I am the resurrection and the life. The one who believes in me will live, even though they die; and whoever lives by believing in me will never die. Do you believe this?"

Not *in the future*. Now. The resurrection age has arrived. It is present with you, in the person standing before you.

Martha's response—"Yes, Lord, I believe that you are the Messiah, the Son of God, who is to come into the world"—sounds like doctrine. It's the right answer, the confession Peter makes in Matthew's Gospel. But does she truly understand? She's speaking what she knows, but knowledge and belief are not yet one.

### Mary's Grief

Jesus then insists on seeing Mary:

> After she had said this, she went back and called her sister Mary aside. "The Teacher is here," she said, "and is asking for you." When Mary heard this, she got up quickly and went to him.

When Mary reaches Jesus, her words mirror Martha's but her body expresses what Martha could only confess doctrinally:

> When Mary reached the place where Jesus was and saw him, she fell at his feet and said, "Lord, if you had been here, my brother would not have died."

Same words. But Mary has come in tears and in grief. And Jesus responds to her tears with His own emotion:

> When Jesus saw her weeping, and the Jews who had come along with her also weeping, he was deeply moved in spirit and troubled.

What does "deeply moved" mean? The Greek word *embrimaomai* appears only a few times in Scripture, and its meaning is debated. Some interpret it as emotional—Jesus weeping from compassion. Some suggest groaning—the word contains *pneuma* (spirit/breath)—indicating deep anguish. Others suggest something closer to rebuke or indignation.

> Jesus wept.

Three words in Greek. But which kind of weeping? Some have suggested Jesus wept over Lazarus, but why weep when you're about to raise him in minutes? Others suggest He wept over the reality of death itself, or in sympathy with those mourning. But perhaps the most compelling interpretation: Jesus weeps in frustration.

Look at what follows:

> Then the Jews said, "See how he loved him!" But some of them said, "Could not he who opened the eyes of the blind man have kept this man from dying?"

The same question again: *Where were you, Jesus? Why didn't you prevent this?* 

Jesus hasn't wept over Lazarus's death—He's about to reverse it. He weeps over the stubborn inability of people to believe. The resurrection isn't some distant, theoretical thing. He's standing right there, *the Resurrection and the Life in person*, and still they don't see it. Still they ask, *Why didn't you save him?* Still their grief clouds the glory that's about to be revealed.

### The Moment of Glory

> Jesus, once more deeply moved, came to the tomb. It was a cave with a stone laid across the entrance. "Take away the stone," he said. "But, Lord," said Martha, the sister of the dead man, "by this time there is a bad odor, for he has been there four days."

The smell of death—literal, undeniable, four days of decomposition. Martha is being practical: the body is far gone. Opening the tomb will only confirm the horror of death.

But Jesus speaks of glory:

> "Did I not tell you that if you believe, you will see the glory of God?"

Back to verse 4: *This sickness will not end in death. No, it is for God's glory so that God's Son may be glorified through it.*

It was always about glory. About God's power revealed, God's kingdom demonstrated in the face of humanity's greatest enemy.

> So they took away the stone. Then Jesus looked up and said, "Father, I thank you that you have heard me. I knew that you always hear me, but I said this for the benefit of the people standing here, that they may believe that you sent me."

The prayer is for the witnesses. Everything Jesus does is to move people from theoretical faith to genuine belief.

> When he had said this, Jesus called in a loud voice, "Lazarus, come out!" The dead man came out, his hands and feet wrapped with strips of linen, and a cloth around his face. Jesus said to them, "Take off the grave clothes and let him go."

Lazarus emerges, bound in the wrappings of death—trapped, imprisoned by the grave clothes. And Jesus commands them to release him. *Loosen him. Let him go.* This is more than resurrection; it's liberation. Death had him bound, and now he is freed.

### The Disciples' Incomplete Faith

Here's the crucial point: the disciples believed in a theoretical resurrection. They'd learned it from Scripture. They knew Jesus could do miracles. But their actions revealed their doubt—not in His ability, but in His willingness and timing.

When Martha calls Jesus "late," when Mary weeps, when Thomas says "let us die together"—these aren't expressions of faith. They're expressions of doubt dressed in doctrine. The disciples will need to hold this story close in the coming weeks and months. Jesus is preparing them for persecution, for witnessing, for their own deaths. They need to know: the One who raised Lazarus after four days can raise them too.

This is why the raising of Lazarus appears 53 times in the catacombs where early Christians hid from persecution. As Charles Hill notes, *the story of Lazarus, with its revelation of Jesus as "the resurrection and the life," held obvious meaning for Christians in the face of death.*

### Application: Death and Resurrection

We are resurrected in Spirit today. Do we act like it? We have a better resurrection than Lazarus—one that will never require binding again, never return to the grave.

> He will revive us after two days; He will raise us up on the third day, that we may live before Him. (Hosea 6:2)

But listen carefully: *if you are not already in Christ, there will come a time of suffering.* Sickness. Death—of others and our own. Persecution. It will get worse before it gets better.

And your theories about resurrection will be tested. Do you *believe*—not intellectually, but with your whole life?

We think we can endure anything. But can we? We can't even live comfortably in a small house without modern plumbing—do you think you'll survive prison? We complain about church lunches—do you think you can survive on gruel? We were afraid of a new flu—are we ready for suffering?

Theory is different from practice. When it's a choice between the things of God and the things of men—what will you choose?

> I am the resurrection and the life. That's not life full of material abundance. That's not life full of experiences and vacations. That's life full of serving, of doing God's work. What do you choose?

## 3. The Impossible Messiah (45-57)

### The Plot Against Jesus

This resurrection of Lazarus is Jesus's last public sign. His public ministry is ending. From now on, He will spend intimate time with His disciples—they must understand what's coming.

And what's coming is death:

> Therefore many of the Jews who had come to visit Mary, and had seen what Jesus did, believed in him. But some of them went to the Pharisees and told them what Jesus had done. Then the chief priests and the Pharisees called a meeting of the Sanhedrin. "What are we accomplishing?" they asked. "Here is this man performing many signs. If we let him go on like this, everyone will believe in him, and then the Romans will come and take away both our temple and our nation."

The miracle that saves Lazarus seals Jesus's death warrant. The very sign that should convince them drives them to murderous plots.

### The Unwitting Prophecy

> Then one of them, named Caiaphas, who was high priest that year, spoke up, "You know nothing at all! You do not realize that it is better for you that one man die for the people than that the whole nation perish."

Caiaphas intends this as political calculation. Better to sacrifice one person than risk the nation. But he speaks more truly than he knows:

> He did not say this on his own, but as high priest that year he prophesied that Jesus would die for the Jewish nation, and not only for that nation but also for the scattered children of God, to bring them together and make them one.

It's better that He dies—but not for the Pharisees. For you and me. Not to save the nation from Rome, but to save all people from death and sin.

### The Hour Has Come

> So from that day on they plotted to take his life. Therefore Jesus no longer moved about publicly among the people of Judea. Instead he withdrew to a region near the wilderness, to a village called Ephraim, where he stayed with his disciples.

Time is accelerating. What took two years in the first twelve chapters of John will take less than two weeks in chapters thirteen through twenty-one. Jesus's final days are upon us.

> When it was almost time for the Jewish Passover, many went up from the country to Jerusalem for their ceremonial cleansing before the Passover. They kept looking for Jesus, and as they stood in the temple courts they asked one another, "What do you think? Isn't he coming to the festival at all?" But the chief priests and the Pharisees had given orders that anyone who found out where Jesus was should report it so that they might arrest him.

We are heading not just toward another Passover, but the final Passover. In Jesus, the prophecies of the saving King will be fulfilled. Time has slowed down. He no longer walks in public places, but spends precious, intense time with His own disciples—one on one, face to face. Time is short and He has much to tell them and show them.

The hour has come. Death and suffering await. Yet this is the book of glory! Through this final Passover, through this death and suffering, salvation opens for all—Jew and Gentile alike. There is no distinction. We don't need to change currency to believe in God.

Be not like the crowds of old who saw but did not believe. Hear these words of God the Father and believe.
