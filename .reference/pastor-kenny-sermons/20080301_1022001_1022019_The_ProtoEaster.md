# Genesis 22:1-19

**Title:** The Proto-Easter  
**Date:** 14 March 2008

## Understanding the Proto-Easter

When we think of Easter, we typically picture 33 AD—the crucifixion and resurrection of Jesus Christ. But I want to take you back much further, to what I call the Proto-Easter: a remarkable foreshadowing of Easter that occurred around 2000 BC, recorded in Genesis 22.

A prototype is the model before the final version—the beta test that works out all the bugs. Genesis 22 is God's prototype of the Easter story, and understanding it deepens our appreciation for what Christ accomplished on the cross.

## The Crazy Command (Genesis 22:2)

> "Take your son, your only son, Isaac, whom you love, and go to the region of Moriah. Sacrifice him there as a burnt offering on one of the mountains I will tell you about." (Genesis 22:2)

This command seems almost incomprehensible. For 25 years, God had promised Abraham a son who would give him descendants as numerous as the seashore—a people through whom God would address humanity's sin problem (the failures of Adam and Eve, the corruption after Noah, the pride of Babel). In fact, the entire biblical narrative hinges on finding the descendant who will solve the problem of sin.

So when God suddenly commands Abraham to sacrifice Isaac, the absurdity cuts deep:

- How can Abraham have millions of descendants if his only son is killed?
- The emphasis falls heavily on "your only son"—there is no other. Abraham is already 99 years old; it took him 40 years to reach this point with Isaac.
- Even more pointedly, Isaac is described as "whom you love." Abraham's hopes, his dreams, his entire future rest on this one beloved son.

**A Personal Illustration:** Many of you know this feeling. Your parents hold all their hopes in you—the promise of a doctor or lawyer in the family, the security of your future earnings, the big house where they'll live with you in comfort. You're on the right track, and the pathway seems clear and good. But what God is asking Abraham to do is essentially tell him to throw it all away. What kind of future is there in death?

Yet despite the incomprehensibility, Abraham obeys. He rises early, saddles his donkey, takes Isaac and his servants, and sets out for Moriah. On the third day, he sees the place in the distance.

## The Sacrifice Prepared

As they ascend the mountain together, Isaac asks a piercing question:

> "Father?" "Yes, my son?" Abraham replied. "The fire and wood are here, but where is the lamb for the burnt offering?" Abraham answered, "God himself will provide the lamb for the burnt offering, my son." (Genesis 22:7-8)

Abraham builds an altar, arranges the wood, and binds his son. He reaches for the knife. And just as he's about to take his son's life—

> "Do not lay a hand on the boy," the angel says. "Do not do anything to him. Now I know that you fear God, because you have not withheld from me your son, your only son." (Genesis 22:12)

Abraham! Stop!

God's command to stop reveals something profound: Abraham's faith is real. He trusted God even with the impossible, and that trust—that fear of God—is what God honors.

## God Provides the Sacrifice (Genesis 22:13)

Abraham's words echo back with new meaning. "God himself will provide the lamb," he had said to Isaac. And now:

> Abraham looked up and there in a thicket he saw a ram caught by its horns. He went over and took the ram and sacrificed it as a burnt offering instead of his son. (Genesis 22:13)

The ram becomes Isaac's substitute. Abraham calls the place "The LORD Will Provide," and that name stands to this day.

## The Proto-Easter and the Real Easter

Now you can see why I call this the Proto-Easter. The parallels are striking:

**The Similarities:**

- Isaac and Jesus are both sons—one of Abraham, one of the Heavenly Father
- Both are only sons
- Both are deeply loved by their fathers

> "For God so loved the world that he gave his one and only Son, that whoever believes in him shall not perish but have eternal life." (John 3:16)

**The Critical Difference:**

In Genesis, God stops the sacrifice. Abraham is spared the agony of losing his son. But in the New Testament, God does not stop. It cost God immeasurably to see His Son—His only Son, whom He loved—slain on the cross.

Yet the parallel deepens further: just as the ram was swapped for Isaac, so Jesus, the Lamb of God, was swapped for us. When we deserved judgment and death, God said STOP. He would provide the sacrifice Himself.

## What This Means

God provided not merely goats, lambs, or rams—the blood of animals is limited and insufficient. Instead, God provided Himself through His Son as the atoning sacrifice. This is the promised descendant of Abraham who solves the problem of sin, not by ignoring it but by removing its penalty. He dies once for all, a sacrifice sufficient for all who believe.

Just as punishment was about to fall on Isaac, God intervened with a substitute. Just as punishment falls on us, God has provided His own Son as our substitute. Jesus placed Himself on the cross, bearing what we deserve, so that we might be set free.

## The Application

**If God did not spare Himself or His Son:**

- John 3:16 is true: God loves the world with an incomprehensible, sacrificial love
- God loves you personally. He demonstrates this love in the most concrete way possible: "While we were still sinners, Christ died for us" (Romans 5:8)
- There is no more sacrifice required—not human effort, not religious works, nothing else. The debt is paid

**For Those Not Yet Christians:**

Thank you for keeping an open mind. I hope you've grasped how significant it was for God to send His Son for us. As God has loved you with the love of Genesis 22, do not withhold your love from Him. Repent and trust Him to deal with your sin. There is forgiveness available today. Please speak with me or another Christian if you'd like to know more.

**For Those Who Follow Christ:**

Ponder the significance of our Savior during this Easter season. Remember what Christ has done for you. We have died with Christ—we are convinced that one died for all, and therefore all died. We now live not for ourselves but for others. We live to tell people of this powerful Easter message. So go and do that. Brave the ridicule, insult, and persecution. If you prefer a less confrontational approach, invite people to Soul Purpose, where the Jesus of the Bible is presented clearly each week.

## The Resurrection Connection

There is one final similarity between the Proto-Easter and the First Easter. Isaac was, in a sense, brought back from the brink of death—metaphorically resurrected. But Jesus was truly resurrected. He died for our sins, conquered sin itself, defeated death, and now offers new life to all who believe.

This is the hope of Easter: not just a death that satisfied God's justice, but a resurrection that guarantees new life for those who trust in Him.
