# Numbers 13:1-14:45

**Title:** Seduced by Anxiety  
**Date:** 9 June 2013  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Understanding Phobia

A phobia is an irrational, persistent fear of certain situations, objects, activities, or persons. While some phobias may seem peculiar or even amusing on the surface, they represent real psychological struggles for those who experience them. Behind each diagnosis lies genuine suffering—anxiety that produces feelings of powerlessness and hopelessness, often leading to depression, lack of confidence, and difficulty functioning in society.

Many of us wrestle with anxieties in greater or lesser forms, experiencing symptoms that impact our daily lives. Understanding how fear works is essential to understanding our own hearts.

## Part One: Finally Here (13:1-25)

After eleven days of wilderness travel, the Israelites have overcome fears of hunger and survived God's judgment. They have arrived at the threshold of promise. The Lord commands Moses to send scouts from each tribal leader to explore Canaan—the land God is giving them.

> **Numbers 13:1-3**  
> The LORD said to Moses, "Send some men to explore the land of Canaan, which I am giving to the Israelites. From each ancestral tribe send one of its leaders." So at the LORD's command Moses sent them out from the Desert of Paran.

The Desert of Paran sits just before the promised land—the very region Jacob's descendants had left four hundred years earlier when traveling to Egypt. Now they return.

Moses gives them one clear instruction and poses three critical questions:

**The Instruction:** Go up through the Negev (the southern route—the direct path from Egypt to Canaan).

**The Three Questions:**
1. What are the people like? Are they strong or weak, few or many?
2. What is the land like? Is it good or bad? Is the soil fertile? Are there trees?
3. What are their defenses? Do they live in unwalled towns or fortified cities?

The scouts explore for forty days—a biblical symbol of sufficient time. Forty days of rain symbolizes sufficient judgment. Jesus appeared for forty days to prove His resurrection. This timespan indicates the scouts had ample opportunity to thoroughly investigate and draw conclusions.

Their report returns with concrete evidence of abundance: giant grape clusters, pomegranates, and figs. To the Israelites—starving in the wilderness, emerging from slavery—this represents heaven itself. 

**The Promised Land and Christian Hope**

The promised land prefigures the Christian's hope in heaven. Just as the Israelites anticipated a land flowing with milk and honey—prosperity, health, security—Christians look forward to an eternal home where evil is abolished, relationships are perfected, and war exists no more. This earthly existence is temporary, a wilderness, a waiting room between now and eternity.

We should treat this earth as a rental property, not a permanent home. We don't over-invest emotionally or financially in what is temporary. We endure small discomforts because we know our stay is brief. Christians store up treasures in heaven, fixing our hope on what lasts forever.

## Part Two: Unreasonable Fear (13:26-14:10)

Yet the scouts' report proves deeply disappointing. They distort the good news in three ways:

**First, they discount the fertility.** Though verse 27 acknowledges the land flows with milk and honey, they immediately brush aside this evidence. The Hebrew word for "fruit" (peri) carries weight, yet they minimize it rather than emphasize the pomegranates, figs, and grapes they themselves saw.

**Second, they exaggerate the negatives.** 

> **Numbers 13:28**  
> But the people who live there are powerful, and the cities are fortified and very large. We even saw descendants of Anak there.

Ironically, they violated Moses' instruction to travel through the hill country, where populations were smaller and settlements fewer. Instead, they surveyed the strongest regions—the Canaanite coast and the Jordan valley. They looked down when God instructed them to look straight ahead.

**Third, they fail to remember the past.** The scouts omit any mention of Hebron—an ancient city emotionally bound to Israel's history. Hebron is where Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob were buried, where God first promised the patriarchs inheritance of this very land. To speak of Hebron is to invoke the covenant promises themselves. Yet they remain silent, averting their gaze from the tombs of their fathers and the promises of their God.

They transformed language capable of generating faith into language that generated doubt. When only negative reports reach people's ears, anxiety spirals into despair.

**The Nature of Unreasonable Fear**

Our fears often work this same way. We focus on bad news while discounting good news. We spiral into despair and forget to count our blessings. This is unreasonable fear.

Consider Year 12 students facing HSC exams—terrified about marks, rankings, and ATAR scores. Much of this fear is unnecessary. A first-year university student noted something counterintuitive: while university exams are harder, they're less frightening than the HSC. Why? Because everything feels like it rests on one ATAR number.

But consider what's actually at stake. If you don't earn a university degree, you won't starve, become unemployed, or become homeless in Australia—not if you work diligently. If you don't get into your desired course, most students change courses or universities anyway. Even those who enter their dream courses often change them later. Most people change careers throughout their lives.

We fear missing a magic number for courses we probably won't pursue to enter jobs that may not exist. Meanwhile, we abandon what gives our lives meaning: church, family, Bible reading, God himself.

Learn to work hard and prioritize well—life only grows busier. But don't make a single mark, a single course, or a single relationship the be-all and end-all of your existence. Is this genuine danger or manufactured phobia?

**The Matter of Perspective**

The Israelites also lost perspective. They saw themselves as grasshoppers—insignificant creatures. Yet this image actually affirms God's promise.

> **Numbers 13:33**  
> We seemed like grasshoppers in our own eyes, and we looked the same to them.

The problem wasn't their size—it was their self-perception. "We seemed like grasshoppers in our own eyes." They saw themselves as useless, small, without God. They forgot God was with them. His tent stood in the middle of their camp.

But consider what Scripture says about grasshoppers. Locusts, when gathered together, form an unstoppable swarm—a military force. The Israelites had witnessed this firsthand during the Egyptian plagues. They had seen how tiny grasshoppers could devastate an entire land. Yet they forgot.

Moreover, the census in Numbers 1-10 revealed over 600,000 soldiers—an organized army marching toward their enemies. This army would have prevailed.

The scouts also misrepresented the threat. God, through Moses, guided them toward the safer regions. Like a coach encouraging someone to climb a dangerous height, God was saying: "Stay on the safe path; don't look down." But the Israelites looked down.

**The Faithful Minority**

In contrast, Joshua and Caleb offered a different report:

> **Numbers 13:30**  
> Then Caleb silenced the people before Moses and said, "We should go up and take possession of the land, for we can certainly do it."

> **Numbers 14:6-9**  
> Joshua son of Nun and Caleb son of Jephunneh, who were among those who had explored the land, tore their clothes and said to the entire Israelite assembly, "The land we passed through and explored is exceedingly good. If the LORD is pleased with us, he will lead us into that land, a land flowing with milk and honey, and will give it to us. Only do not rebel against the LORD. And do not be afraid of the people of the land, because we will swallow them up. Their protection is gone, but the LORD is with us. Do not be afraid of them."

Everything hinged on one reality: whether the Lord was pleased with them, whether God stood with them. If God fought for them, they would swallow their enemies. Fear was unnecessary.

**The Choice Before Them**

The Israelites faced a choice between faith and fear—and they chose fear. They wept, grumbled, and wished aloud they had died in Egypt. They talked of stoning Joshua and Caleb and appointing a new leader to take them back.

Here they stood at the doorstep of their promised land, yet they were too terrified to enter—despite God's overwhelming presence with them. They gave it all up out of cowardice.

**Application for Today**

In our weakest, most stressful moments, we may contemplate abandoning our faith. We fear what our secular friends will think. We fear Jesus might prove false. We fear missing out on worldly experiences. Yet we fear without cause, having examined only the downside and ignored the positives: the joy of friendship with Jesus, the security and comfort He provides, the fellowship of believers, a coherent source of purpose and morality.

Friends, don't fear. Heaven awaits. Jesus will return soon. We will make it.

Or perhaps you're on the threshold yourself—considering becoming a Christian. Friends have spoken to you. You're intellectually convinced. Yet you hesitate. Are you afraid of what you might have to give up? Afraid it might become difficult? Afraid of embarrassment? God says tonight: Do not fear. It will be well. Many here have faced tough seasons, yet we choose to trust God rather than fear others.

What we should truly fear is God Himself.

> **Luke 12:5**  
> But I will show you whom you should fear: Fear him who, after the killing of the body, has power to throw you into hell. Yes, I tell you, fear him.

## Part Three: Reasonable Fear—God's Judgment (14:11-45)

**The Consequence of Contempt**

God's response reveals righteous anger:

> **Numbers 14:11-12**  
> The LORD said to Moses, "How long will these people treat me with contempt? How long will they refuse to believe in me, in spite of all the miraculous signs I have performed among them? I will strike them down with a plague and destroy them, but I will make you into a nation greater and stronger than they."

God threatens to destroy the Israelites and start over with Moses' descendants. But Moses intercedes with a stunning petition.

**Moses' Plea**

Moses appeals on two grounds: God's reputation and God's character.

First, he reminds the Lord what the nations will say:

> **Numbers 14:13-16**  
> Moses said to the LORD, "Then the Egyptians will hear about it! By your power you brought these people up from among them. And they will tell the inhabitants of this land about it. They have already heard that you, O LORD, are with these people and that you, O LORD, have been seen face to face, that your cloud stays over them, and that you go before them in a pillar of cloud by day and a pillar of fire by night. If you put these people to death all at one time, the nations who have heard this report about you will say, 'The LORD was not able to bring these people into the land he promised them on oath; so he slaughtered them in the desert.'"

Second, he appeals to God's very nature:

> **Numbers 14:17-19**  
> "Now may the Lord's strength be displayed, just as you have declared: 'The LORD is slow to anger, abounding in love and forgiving sin and rebellion. Yet he does not leave the guilty unpunished; he punishes the children for the sin of the fathers to the third and fourth generation.' In accordance with your great love, forgive the sin of these people, just as you have pardoned them from the time they left Egypt until now."

**Forgiveness Granted, Justice Executed**

God responds with grace:

> **Numbers 14:20**  
> The LORD replied, "I have forgiven them, as you asked."

Yet justice must be served. The consequences are severe.

The ten scouts who spread the bad report are struck down by plague and die before the Lord. Only Joshua and Caleb survive—their faith rewarded.

> **Numbers 14:21-24**  
> Nevertheless, as surely as I live and as surely as the glory of the LORD fills the whole earth, not one of the men who saw my glory and the miraculous signs I performed in Egypt and in the desert but who disobeyed me and tested me ten times—not one of them will ever see the land I promised on oath to their forefathers. But because my servant Caleb has a different spirit and follows me wholeheartedly, I will bring him into the land he went to, and his descendants will inherit it.

The entire community faces sentence: forty years in the wilderness—one year for each day they explored the land. Every person twenty years old or older will die in the desert, unable to enter the promised land. Their children, whom they feared would become plunder, will instead inherit what they rejected.

> **Numbers 14:29-35**  
> In this desert your bodies will fall—every one of you twenty years old or more who was counted in the census and who has grumbled against me. Not one of you will enter the land I swore with uplifted hand to make your home, except Caleb son of Jephunneh and Joshua son of Nun. As for your children that you said would be taken as plunder, I will bring them in to enjoy the land you have rejected. But you—your bodies will fall in this desert. Your children will be shepherds here for forty years, suffering for your unfaithfulness, until the last of your bodies lies in the desert. For forty years—one year for each of the forty days you explored the land—you will suffer for your sins and know what it is like to have me against you.

**The Perfect Justice of God's Response**

Consider the poetic justice: They said they'd rather die in the desert—so they will. They said they didn't want to enter the promised land—so they won't. They feared death by the sword—that fear comes upon them. They worried their wives and children would become plunder—yet by their own rejection, they condemn their offspring to forty years of desert wandering.

They wanted to return to Egypt—so God turns them back toward the wilderness. They received exactly what they deserved, yet they also received what they didn't deserve: forgiveness, and a second chance through their children.

**The Real Fear: Judgment**

This passage confronts us with what we should truly fear: not social judgment, not missing out on experiences, not failure—but God's judgment. The reasonable phobia is judgment-phobia—reverent fear of the One whose displeasure means exclusion from His presence.

The Epistle to the Hebrews warns: the Israelites "were not able to enter because of their unbelief" (4:6,11). In fearing man's giants, they became giants of unbelief before God. Their faith, tested, failed.

## Conclusion: Trading Fear for Faith

We live surrounded by genuine fears: economic uncertainty, health crises, relational loss, aging, mortality. Yet these are temporary. Jesus will return and make all things new.

As Paul writes:

> **Philippians 4:6-7**  
> Do not be anxious about anything, but in everything, by prayer and petition, with thanksgiving, present your requests to God. And the peace of God, which transcends all understanding, will guard your hearts and your minds in Christ Jesus.

And Peter encourages:

> **1 Peter 5:6-7**  
> Humble yourselves, therefore, under God's mighty hand, that he may lift you up in due time. Cast all your anxiety on him because he cares for you.

No fear can ultimately debilitate us. God stands with us. Don't believe the deception that life is worse without Him, that you'll somehow miss out. Trading temporary peace for eternal security is always the wise trade.

As John Newton wrote:

> Amazing Grace, how sweet the sound,  
> That saved a wretch like me.  
> I once was lost but now am found,  
> Was blind, but now I see.  
> 'Twas Grace that taught my heart to fear,  
> And Grace my fears relieved;  
> How precious did that Grace appear  
> The hour I first believed.
