# 1 Peter 1:1-2, 2:4-12

**Title:** Living as Migrants

**Date:** 18 June 2023 (ARISE) / 23 October 2011 (SMAC)

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Strange Behaviors: A Cross-Cultural Perspective

When moving to a new country, we encounter behaviors that seem strange and incomprehensible. Migrants experience culture shock—everything from how football is played to footwear etiquette to eating customs differs dramatically. These peculiarities highlight how deeply culture shapes our understanding of normal.

But imagine aliens landing on Earth without any prior knowledge of human civilization. What would they observe about us?

- At traffic lights, they would notice we understand red means stop and green means go, yet yellow somehow means "go faster"
- They would observe that humans spend most of their time gazing at screens—laptops, iPads, monitors, televisions, phones—making LCD displays our primary relationships
- They would conclude that dogs are actually the master race on Earth, since humans dutifully follow them on walks and pick up after them

This thought experiment reveals something profound: Christians are like aliens dumped into another world. Peter describes the recipients of his letter as:

> To God's elect, exiles scattered throughout the provinces of Pontus, Galatia, Cappadocia, Asia and Bithynia, who have been chosen according to the foreknowledge of God the Father, through the sanctifying work of the Spirit, to be obedient to Jesus Christ and sprinkled with his blood: Grace and peace be yours in abundance. (1 Peter 1:1-2)

As we observe these Christians, what should we see? What makes them distinctly different from those around them?

## 1. Sacred on the Inside (2:4-8)

### We Are a Living Temple

Peter declares:

> You also, like living stones, are being built into a spiritual house. (2:5)

This image echoes the ancient Jewish temple—the place where God's presence dwelt on earth. The temple was made of stones, yes, but it was sacred because God himself inhabited it. Yet because of sin, God's glory departed from the temple (Ezekiel 10:4), and the temple was destroyed by Babylonian invaders. The Jews faced exile, and it took thousands of years before Jesus finally came.

When Jesus appeared, he startled the crowds by declaring that he himself was the true temple of God (John 2:19). His body became the dwelling place of God's fullness (Colossians 1:19). After Pentecost, Jesus promised his presence would dwell not in a building but within his people—"in the midst of them" (Matthew 18:20), "with them always" (Matthew 28:20), and "within his people" (John 14:17, 23). The people of God became the true temple, the place where God dwells (1 Corinthians 6:19).

Jesus had promised to bring a race of people from dead stones. When John the Baptist said people couldn't claim Abraham as their father just because of genetics, Jesus responded:

> Out of these stones God can raise up children for Abraham. (Matthew 3:9)

And so it came to pass. Peter encourages Christians to think of themselves as living stones being built into God's new temple:

> To be a holy priesthood, offering spiritual sacrifices acceptable to God through Jesus Christ. (2:5)

Imagine a human pyramid—that's the picture of the temple. The temple is not a building. It never was about walls and mortar. The people are the church. You could meet in a fancy building, a tent, a school hall, or a renovated house. What matters is that new living stones keep being added—the apostles, the early followers, and now you. The temple is us.

### Treat Each Other Sacredly

When we enter a sacred place—a temple—we treat it with reverence. In the Old Testament, one had to be ritually cleansed before entering. You brought a sacrifice. You were respectful. You took your shoes off. You wore clean clothes. The temple was sacred.

Now, Peter isn't saying you need a ritual shower before church or your finest clothes (though some of us could use a shave!). But treating each other as sacred means we care about what we wear, what is modest, what is respectful to others, what encourages good deeds rather than sinful desires. It means we ask ourselves: Is this loving?

Peter writes:

> Now that you have purified yourselves by obeying the truth so that you have sincere love for each other, love one another deeply, from the heart. (1:22)

And:

> Therefore, rid yourselves of all malice and all deceit, hypocrisy, envy, and slander of every kind. (2:1)

**Malice** is hatred—wishing harm on others.

**Deceit** is falsehood, hiding our sins. Yet exposure is the best way! It's beautiful when Christians confess sins to one another.

**Hypocrisy** is insincerity—hiding our sins while pretending to be sinless.

**Envy** is failing to rejoice in another's blessings. We become jealous of their marks, their opportunities, their looks, discounting what we ourselves have been given.

**Slander** is gossip and character assassination through speech.

When we treat each other as sacred, we stop treating people as disposable commodities—useful while convenient, then discarded when inconvenient. During exam season, holidays, or breaks, we drift away from our church family. But we should treat the church—the people—with utmost sacredness.

This becomes clear when we compare our commitment to church with our commitment to other things. Consider what we treat as sacred:

- Many of us have pastimes we guard jealously—favorite TV shows, computer games, golf, Facebook. A 19-year-old burglar was arrested because he couldn't resist checking his Facebook account before robbing a house, forgetting to log out. We check it compulsively throughout the day, no matter how busy we are.

But do we treat each other as sacred? Do we treat our gatherings—meetings of the living stones together—as sacred?

We would never tell our employer: "I slept in because I played computer games late" or "I have a group assignment due today." Yet we say this to church without hesitation. We wouldn't text our workplace "sorry, won't be coming" a few minutes before our appointment. We wouldn't casually arrive 20 minutes late. Yet we do this at church.

Consider the word "holidays"—it comes from "holy-days," sacred days. In the medieval world, people worked 7/52—no weekends, no rest. When the world became Christianized, holy-days were created so people could do what was sacred to them. What do we do on holidays? We get away from each other.

Family commitments to places like Hong Kong are understandable, but many long holidays seem designed to separate us from our church community. Yet you cannot love casually. You cannot flirt with this group, then another group, then yet another. You must stick to one. Church is like gym—if you interrupt the routine, all the benefits are lost. It might as well not have happened.

You are not to each other a convenience store—coming to get what you want and leaving. You are to each other the living stones of God, brick by brick added by God, cemented by the blood of Jesus. We are the temple of God. Treat each other sacredly. Christ died for each of you.

By your love and action toward those who aren't here, encourage them to return. Those of you present today have shown you understand this. Well done.

## 2. Separated from the Outside (2:9-12)

### A Chosen, Separate People

Peter reminds us:

> You are a chosen people, a royal priesthood, a holy nation, God's special possession, that you may declare the praises of him who called you out of darkness into his wonderful light. Once you were not a people, but now you are the people of God; once you had not received mercy, but now you have received mercy. (2:9-10)

This language echoes what God told Israel:

> Now if you obey me fully and keep my covenant, then out of all nations you will be my treasured possession. Although the whole earth is mine, you will be for me a kingdom of priests and a holy nation. (Exodus 19:5-6)

Just as the priests in Israel were separated from the common people, Christians are separated from the pagan world—particularly separated from their behaviors. Peter is calling us to be distinctly different:

> Dear friends, I urge you, as foreigners and exiles, to abstain from sinful desires, which wage war against your soul. (2:11)

Different religions teach people to abstain from different things—pork, beef, alcohol, modern technology. But for Christians, we abstain from sin itself. Sinful desires wage war against our soul. They are not good for us. As James writes:

> What causes fights and quarrels among you? Don't they come from your desires that battle within you? You desire but do not have, so you kill. You covet but you cannot get what you want, so you quarrel and fight. (James 4:1-2)

The tension we feel constantly—being torn between what we want to do and what we should do. Paul described this inner battle:

> I do not understand what I do. For what I want to do I do not do, but what I hate I do. For I know that good itself does not dwell in me, that is, in my sinful nature. For I have the desire to do what is good, but I cannot carry it out. (Romans 7:15, 18)

These fleshly lusts—greed, malice, and all the sins that flow from our nature—ruin us. They take us away from God's purposes and destroy our fellowship with one another.

### Live Good Lives Before the Watching World

Instead, Peter calls us to something better:

> Live such good lives among the pagans that, though they accuse you of doing wrong, they may see your good deeds and glorify God on the day he visits us. (2:12)

"Good lives" means morally honorable lives—lives marked by self-control, not letting fleshly desires take over. It means doing good works: being kind and considerate, putting others' needs before our own, standing for truth.

We live in a unique time. Australia has a Christian heritage, and there was a time when being like everyone else meant absorbing Christian culture. Migrants came and learned a Christian way of life. But this is eroding faster than we anticipated. Our children are being influenced by a radically different culture. A separation is needed—a radical decision to live differently.

Yet our problem is we are too much like the pagans. Our lifestyle is identical. We do the same things on Friday nights. We dress for the same reasons. We spend money on the same things. We study the same subjects. We have the same ambitions. We travel to the same places. We live for fleshly desires. We fear the same things. We share the same anxieties.

**When aliens visit, what would they notice?** If Jesus himself were to return and visit you, what would he see as important to you? Would there be any difference between your life and the life of those around you who don't believe?

The hope is that people would notice your genuine love for one another—that you love each other deeply from the heart with sincerity. That you don't engage in malice. That you're not apathetic to each other but actually care. That they would see you're truly committed to your church family—your fellow aliens in this world. That commitment to each other is sacred to you, just as the temple was sacred to ancient Jews.

This is what living as migrants means: being so distinctly shaped by allegiance to Christ and his people that our lives provoke the question—what do they believe that makes them so different?
