# Galatians 4:21–5:12

**Title:** Freedom  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## The Misunderstanding of Christianity

Many in the world view Christianity as restrictive and oppressive—a system of slavery to rules and regulations. This perception is ancient and persistent. Napoleon Bonaparte once remarked, "Religion is excellent stuff for keeping common people quiet." Nietzsche characterized Christian faith as "sacrifice of all freedom, all pride, all self-confidence of spirit; it is at the same time subjection, a self-derision, and self-mutilation."

But Paul writes to the Galatians with a radically different message: Christianity is not slavery. It is freedom.

## Christ Has Set Us Free

> **Galatians 5:1** — It is for freedom that Christ has set us free.

Jesus came to liberate us—not just from the penalty of sin, but from the burden of legalism and moralism. We are free from the idea that following rules accomplishes anything spiritually. As Scripture says, "Cursed is the person who follows laws" (Galatians 3:10). Salvation comes through faith, not through obedience to the law.

## Two Covenants: Two Ways of Life

Paul uses a powerful image to illustrate this freedom. He contrasts two covenants by pointing to Abraham's two sons.

> **Galatians 4:21–23** — Tell me, you who want to be under the law, are you not aware of what the law says? For it is written that Abraham had two sons, one by the slave woman and the other by the free woman. His son by the slave woman was born in the ordinary way; but his son by the free woman was born as the result of a promise.

### Slavery: The Law Covenant

> **Galatians 4:24–25** — These things may be taken figuratively, for the women represent two covenants. One covenant is from Mount Sinai and bears children who are to be slaves: This is Hagar. Now Hagar stands for Mount Sinai in Arabia and corresponds to the present city of Jerusalem, because she is in slavery with her children.

The law came 430 years after Abraham's promise, given to regulate the enslaved Israelites coming out of Egypt. Perhaps it was meant to train them, to establish structure for a people who had known only slavery. But the law was never meant to be a permanent condition for God's people—it was temporary, a means to an end.

### Freedom: The Promise Covenant

> **Galatians 4:26–28** — But the Jerusalem that is above is free, and she is our mother. For it is written: "Be glad, O barren woman, who bears no children; break forth and cry aloud, you who have no labor pains; because more are the children of the desolate woman than of her who has a husband." Now you, brothers, like Isaac, are children of promise.

Sarah represents the covenant of promise—God's word given freely, not earned through obedience. Like Isaac, we are children born of promise, not of human effort. This is our inheritance.

### The Opposition Between Two Ways

> **Galatians 4:29–31** — At that time the son born in the ordinary way persecuted the son born by the power of the Spirit. It is the same now. But what does the Scripture say? "Get rid of the slave woman and her son, for the slave woman's son will never share in the inheritance with the free woman's son." Therefore, brothers, we are not children of the slave woman, but of the free woman.

Just as Ishmael persecuted Isaac, so those bound to the law oppose those who live by faith. But we must not return to slavery. We are free—and freedom cannot coexist with legalism.

## Stand Firm in Freedom

> **Galatians 5:1** — Stand firm, then, and do not let yourselves be burdened again by a yoke of slavery.

Paul's exhortation is clear: guard your freedom jealously. Some were suggesting that believers needed to be circumcised and obey the entire law to be truly righteous. Paul responds with urgency:

> **Galatians 5:2–6** — Mark my words! I, Paul, tell you that if you let yourselves be circumcised, Christ will be of no value to you at all. Again I declare to every man who lets himself be circumcised that he is obligated to obey the whole law. You who are trying to be justified by law have been alienated from Christ; you have fallen away from grace. But by faith we eagerly await through the Spirit the righteousness for which we hope. For in Christ Jesus neither circumcision nor uncircumcision has any value. The only thing that counts is faith expressing itself through love.

The choice is absolute: you cannot have both Christ and the law as your basis for righteousness. To pursue justification through law is to sever yourself from grace.

## Running the Good Race

> **Galatians 5:7–12** — You were running a good race. Who cut in on you and kept you from obeying the truth? That kind of persuasion does not come from the one who calls you. "A little yeast works through the whole batch of dough." I am confident in the Lord that you will take no other view. The one who is throwing you into confusion will pay the penalty, whoever he may be. Brothers, if I am still preaching circumcision, why am I still being persecuted? In that case the offense of the cross has been abolished. As for those agitators, I wish they would go the whole way and emasculate themselves!

The Galatians were running well until false teachers "cut in on you." A small amount of legalism—"a little yeast"—works through the whole community, corrupting the gospel. Paul is confident the Galatians will see the truth, and those troublemakers will face judgment.

## What Freedom Actually Looks Like

Christianity is not a system of restrictive rules. Look at what Christians do:

- **Diet:** There are no rules. Believers are free to eat what they choose.
- **Clothing:** No regulations dictate how Christians must dress.
- **Worship:** There are no mandatory rituals or temples requiring specific practices.

Freedom in Christ means we are not bound by external rules and regulations.

Yet this does not mean the Christian life is immoral or lawless. Christianity has its own unique answer to the question: *How can we be righteous without rules?* The answer lies not in external constraint, but in the transformation of the heart through faith and love.

We are free—truly, genuinely free. And in that freedom, we discover what true righteousness looks like.
