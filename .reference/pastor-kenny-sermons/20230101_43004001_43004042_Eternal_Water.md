# John 4:1-42

**Title:** Eternal Water

**Date:** 22 January 2023

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## The Thirst for Living Water

Jesus arrives at Jacob's well in Samaria tired, hot, and hungry from his journey. When a Samaritan woman comes to draw water, he asks her for a drink—a seemingly simple request that carries profound spiritual weight.

But there's an immediate barrier. Jews and Samaritans were ancient enemies. Centuries earlier, when Assyria conquered the northern kingdom of Israel, they had scattered the Israelites throughout their empire and brought in foreign peoples to intermarry with them. The resulting mixed-race population became known as the Samaritans. When the southern kingdom of Judah later returned from Babylonian exile, they viewed the Samaritans as impure, idolatrous, and disloyal. The hostility ran deep on both sides.

So when Jesus asks for a drink, the woman's response carries the weight of this history: *"How can a Jew ask a Samaritan woman for a drink?"*

Instead of shame, Jesus offers her something greater.

> "If you knew the gift of God and who it is that asks you for a drink, you would have asked him and he would have given you living water." (John 4:10)

The woman is confused. She points out that Jesus has no bucket, and the well is deep. How could he possibly draw water greater than what Jacob himself drank? But Jesus speaks of something far different from physical water—something that addresses the deepest human thirst.

> "Everyone who drinks this water will be thirsty again, but whoever drinks the water I give him will never thirst. Indeed, the water I give him will become in him a spring of water welling up to eternal life." (John 4:13-14)

This is self-generating, eternal refreshment. Like those water-storing pellets for plants, this water sustains itself from within. Unlike the daily grind of fetching water from wells—a burden that defines life for many women in the developing world—this living water requires no endless repetition, no constant returning to the well.

The woman responds with longing: *"Sir, give me this water so that I won't get thirsty and have to keep coming here to draw water."* (John 4:15)

## Repenting to the Truth

Then Jesus does something unexpected. He tells her to go call her husband. She admits she has no husband—technically true, yet evasive. The full truth is far more complicated: she has had five husbands, and the man she's now living with is not her husband.

This may explain why she comes to the well at midday, the hottest part of the day, alone. Other women typically gather in the cool of morning or evening in groups. Her social isolation suggests a life marked by repeated failure and rejection.

Yet remarkably, when Jesus confronts her with the truth of her life, she doesn't deny it or run away. She admits it: *"What you have just said is quite true."* (John 4:17-18) This admission is crucial. She has come to the place where truth-telling becomes possible—and necessary.

The conversation then shifts to worship. She recognizes that Jesus is a prophet, and she raises the ancient dispute between Samaritans and Jews about the proper place of worship. But Jesus transcends both temples:

> "Yet a time is coming and has now come when the true worshipers will worship the Father in spirit and in truth, for they are the kind of worshipers the Father seeks. God is spirit, and his worshipers must worship in spirit and in truth." (John 4:23-24)

The word "worship" here means to bow down, to submit. The right temple is not a building in Jerusalem or on a Samaritan mountain. The right temple is truth itself—and Jesus is the truth incarnate.

When the woman says, *"I know that Messiah is coming,"* Jesus responds with stunning directness: *"I who speak to you am he."* (John 4:25-26)

Truth is essential to following Jesus. We cannot come to him while hiding from ourselves. We must acknowledge our failure, our sin, our desperate thirst for something real. Only then can we drink the living water.

## Telling Others the Truth

The woman's response is immediate and striking. She leaves her water jar behind—symbolic of her transformed priorities. The very tool she came with, the instrument of her daily burden, she now abandons. She has found something infinitely better.

She returns to her village and tells everyone: *"Come, see a man who told me everything I ever did. Could this be the Christ?"* (John 4:28-29) She doesn't hide her shame; she proclaims it. She invites others to meet the one who saw her completely and loved her anyway.

The result is extraordinary:

> "Many of the Samaritans from that town believed in him because of the woman's testimony... They said to the woman, 'We no longer believe just because of what you said; now we have heard for ourselves, and we know that this man really is the Savior of the world.'" (John 4:39-42)

What started as private shame becomes public testimony. What began with an encounter at a well becomes a movement of faith that breaks down the ancient barriers between Jew and Samaritan.

Meanwhile, Jesus's own disciples remain spiritually asleep. When Jesus says, *"I have food to eat that you know nothing about,"* they think he's talking about literal food (John 4:32-33). They're still thinking in terms of the temporal—hunger, thirst, physical sustenance. But Jesus speaks of eternal priorities:

> "My food is to do the will of him who sent me and to finish his work... Open your eyes and look at the fields! They are ripe for harvest... Even now the reaper draws his wages, even now he harvests the crop for eternal life." (John 4:34-36)

The work of sowing has been done. Jesus has done the hard work. Our task is to harvest—to tell others what Christ has accomplished. The barrier between Jew and Samaritan, between insider and outsider, has been broken. All we must do is speak the truth of what he has done: that he died for our sins, that he loves us, that he sacrificed himself for us.

## The Unfinished Request

There's a beautiful detail hidden in this passage. Remember how the story began? Jesus was thirsty. He asked for a drink. Yet throughout the entire narrative—as the Samaritan woman encounters him, as the disciples leave and return, as the village comes to him—Jesus never receives that drink.

His thirst goes unfulfilled until we fast forward to John 19, to the cross itself:

> "Later, knowing that all was now completed, and so that the Scripture would be fulfilled, Jesus said, 'I am thirsty.' A jar of wine vinegar was there, so they soaked a sponge in it, put the sponge on a stalk of the hyssop plant, and lifted it to Jesus' lips. When he had received the drink, Jesus said, 'It is finished.'" (John 19:28-30)

On the cross, Jesus drank judgment on our behalf. He drank the cup of God's wrath so that we would never have to. He went thirsty—thirsty for righteousness, thirsty for relationship with the Father—so that we could drink living water forever.

He is the eternal source. The reason we never thirst again is that he thirsted completely for us. And now we are free to think of eternal things, to leave behind our water jars, to tell others the truth of what he has done.
