# 1 Corinthians 1:1-18

**Title:** Church Divided  
**Date:** July 8, 2015  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Holy People (1-3)

> To the church of God in Corinth, to those sanctified in Christ Jesus and called to be his holy people, together with all those everywhere who call on the name of our Lord Jesus Christ—their Lord and ours:
>
> Grace and peace to you from God our Father and the Lord Jesus Christ.

Paul writes from Ephesus during his third missionary journey, addressing a church he planted years earlier in Corinth—a wealthy trading city known for sexual immorality, idolatry, and hedonistic excess. Yet he calls them "sanctified" and "holy."

This seems ironic. The Corinthian church reflected the pagan culture around them, struggling with sexual immorality, lawsuits, and idolatry. How could Paul call them holy?

### Called to Be Holy

The key word is "called." Our holiness comes not from our actual behavior, but from God's declaration of what we are in Christ. We are sinners day after day—we fall to temptation, lose our temper, commit wrongs. We don't deserve to be called saints.

But God does something remarkable: He calls us saints. This is part of justification—those He justifies, He also sanctifies. It's the future breaking into the present. No longer are we labeled sinners, but saints. We are right in God's eyes.

### The Title Swap

How can God truthfully call sinful people holy? Through a title swap accomplished at the cross:

- Jesus deserves to be called sanctified and holy—He is purely righteous, clean, and wholesome
- But at the cross, Jesus gave His title to us and took our title upon Himself
- Now He bears our shame and is despised and punished; we bear His righteousness

It's like swapping title deeds: Jesus owned a clean Mercedes while I owned a broken, dirty bicycle. He swapped the deeds with me. Now I own the clean car. Jesus got the broken one.

The Corinthians didn't deserve this. All they did was receive the swap. Neither do we.

### For Everyone

This calling extends to anyone. If you're not yet a believer, you don't have to miss out. God's calling is for all who accept that they need help, who receive Jesus's gift of forgiveness and call upon His name.

God does things in a unique way: join the team first, learn the skills after.

## Gifted (4-9)

> For in him you have been enriched in every way—with all kinds of speech and with all knowledge—
>
> Therefore you do not lack any gift as you eagerly wait for our Lord Jesus Christ to be revealed.
>
> He will also keep you firm to the end, so that you will be blameless on the day of our Lord Jesus Christ.
>
> God is faithful, who has called you into fellowship with his Son, Jesus Christ our Lord.

God doesn't just give us a new identity—He supplies the means to become that identity. We're enriched with "all kinds of speech and with all knowledge." These gifts—the ability to speak truth and to understand Scripture—confirm us as Christians and keep us firm until Christ returns.

### We Lack Nothing

The Corinthian church may have seemed lacking in many ways. Perhaps they had no particular ministry, couldn't cater to certain age groups, lacked music instruments. But they lacked nothing essential: the ability to support each other toward heaven.

This doesn't require formal ministry structures or special skills. All of us can talk to each other. All of us can ask: "How are you?" "How has being a Christian been this week?" "What did you learn from the Bible?" "How can I pray for you?" Then pray right there, even if it feels awkward.

When we give each other permission to do this, God uses all our kinds of speech and knowledge to confirm each other to the end.

### The Church Is Complete

The Corinthian church had everything needed because it was "the church"—not part of a church, but the complete body. The local church is a full expression of Christ's body with everything necessary for its members to reach heaven together.

"Church" means the gathered body—both the final heavenly assembly and the current earthly one. What they could accomplish together on Sunday mornings was enough. We might associate with other churches for various purposes, but to confirm we'll be blameless on the last day—that's something every church can do.

### Speak Truth in Love

From Ephesians 4:14-16: "Then we will no longer be infants, tossed back and forth... Instead, speaking the truth in love, we will grow to become in every respect the mature body of him who is the head, that is, Christ."

This is the first priority at church. The world has more money, more power, more friends than we do. What do we have? Each other—a bunch of sinners admitting our need and trying to do something about it.

Yet this weak ensemble is our own gift to ourselves, and it's complete.

## United (10)

> I appeal to you, brothers and sisters, in the name of our Lord Jesus Christ, that all of you agree with one another in what you say and that there be no divisions among you, but that you be perfectly united in mind and thought.

Paul seeks theological unity. The word "united" means "mended"—like fishing nets being repaired. Unity isn't about sameness in structure or appearance, but about being mended together in mind and thought.

We come from different backgrounds, presuppositions, experiences, and sins. True unity means learning to think together biblically, sharpening each other through Scripture.

### Theological Unity

Denominational differences exist for various reasons—historical, political, even theological. But real unity is when believers agree at the level of thought and mind about what the Bible teaches.

This isn't about relativism or "multiple truths." There is one truth. But as sinners, we don't have a monopoly on it. We must agree to disagree humbly, recognizing that further study and time may reveal which understanding is correct. Throughout church history, believers have disagreed on various issues—some resolved, some still debated—but the aim is always truth, not fatalism.

### The Tuning Fork

In our fallen state, diverse churches and denominations serve a purpose. Time will test which positions endure. But ultimately, what matters most is that we're all tuned to Christ.

A.W. Tozer said: "One hundred pianos all tuned to the same fork are automatically tuned to each other... So one hundred worshippers together, each one looking away to Christ, are in heart nearer to each other than they could possibly be were they to become 'unity' conscious and turn their eyes away from God to strive for closer fellowship."

When we agree in thought and mind, our differences become mere variations in how we pursue the mission together.

## Not Divided (11-17)

> My brothers and sisters, some from Chloe's household have informed me that there are quarrels among you. One of you says, "I follow Paul"; another, "I follow Apollos"; another, "I follow Cephas"; still another, "I follow Christ."
>
> Is Christ divided? Was Paul crucified for you? Were you baptized in the name of Paul?

Bad news about divisions had reached Paul. The Corinthians were fragmenting into factions aligned with different leaders.

Paul's response is pointed: Christ is not divided. He alone was crucified. He alone is the source of salvation and unity. The factions around human leaders miss the point entirely.

### Disagreement Is Not Division

Theological disagreement is healthy and necessary—it's how the church grows in understanding. But disagreement becomes division when we stop looking to Christ and start looking to human leaders or our own interpretations.

Throughout history, the church has navigated differences. Some turned out to be wrong (like liberalism, which has largely died in Western churches). Others revealed different angles of the same truth that aren't actually incompatible. Some involved weighing priorities differently—like infant versus believer's baptism, both seeking to protect different biblical truths (God's sovereignty versus human choice).

### Maintain the Common Center

The danger isn't disagreement—it's losing our common center in Christ. When we're all tuned to Him, disagreements remain mere differences in method or emphasis.

As a church, we aim for theological unity by letting Scripture be our constitution and diet. It's lovely to gather with people who believe the Bible is true, that it offers life through Jesus, and that we must listen to it.

But this journey won't always be smooth. We may become exasperated. In worst cases, we might have to agree to disagree. Yet even then, our aim is always truth—not fatalism or relativism, but humble submission to what Scripture actually says.

## Who Are You?

The church in Corinth—and the church today—is:

- **Holy people**: Called and sanctified in Christ, declared righteous despite our ongoing sin
- **Gifted**: Equipped with everything needed to support each other to heaven
- **United**: Bound together by common theological convictions about Christ and Scripture
- **Not divided**: Free to navigate disagreements while staying centered on Christ

The question Paul asks is the same we must ask: Who are you? Who are we?

The Eurozone faces crisis partly because the north and south are divided not just structurally, but in thought and mind—different ideas about work, government, responsibility. Their common currency and institutions can't overcome that fundamental division.

The church's unity is different. It rests not on external structures, but on being tuned to the same Christ. When we're unified in mind and thought around Scripture, when we're committed to God's truth over our preferences, we become what Paul calls us to be: holy, gifted, united, and not divided.

Will you commit to this? To seeking theological unity? To supporting each other with speech and knowledge? To looking away from human leaders and toward Christ alone?
