# 1 Peter 3:1-7

**Title:** Alien Submission
**Date:** 6 November 2011
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction: The S-Word

Submission is a dirty word in our culture. We think of Gaddafi and Saddam Hussein. We think of slavery and exploitation. We think of domestic abuse. If you're feeling uncomfortable reading this passage, that's natural. It's okay. But please stay with me a bit longer.

Last week we saw that Christians are a chosen people, a royal priesthood, a holy nation—called to declare the praises of God who brought us out of darkness into his wonderful light. This is evangelism. But what happens when life circumstances make it hard to declare God's praises? What if your boss prevents you? What if there's persecution? What if there's a power imbalance that silences you?

This is where Peter's teaching becomes crucial. How do powerless minorities declare their faith?

## Creating Opportunities to Declare (v. 12)

Peter suggests a unique solution:

> Live such good lives among the pagans that, though they accuse you of doing wrong, by seeing your good deeds they may glorify God on the day he visits us.

Rather than protest, revolution, or self-immolation, Peter calls us to live good lives.

### What is "the good life"?

The good life is not the Aussie Dream—good food, good entertainment, material success. It's not showing the world how excellent your god is through material prosperity. The good life means living moral lives.

Peter emphasizes this in verse 11:

> Dear friends, I urge you, as aliens and strangers in the world, to abstain from sinful desires, which war against your soul.

The pagans disagreed with Christian morality. They found it strange that Christians didn't join them in their excess (4:4). But Peter calls us to persist in good deeds despite slander, because pagans will eventually acknowledge them on judgment day. This is vindication—not now, but on the day God judges all things.

### Good Works Silence Ignorant Talk (v. 15)

> For it is God's will that by doing good you should silence the ignorant talk of foolish men.

When we live morally and interact with others honestly and humbly, we undermine false accusations. The slander loses its power.

### Good Works Create Opportunities for the Gospel (3:15)

> But in your hearts set apart Christ as Lord. Always be prepared to give an answer to everyone who asks you to give the reason for the hope that you have. But do this with gentleness and respect.

As our good deeds speak, people will eventually ask: "Why are you different?" This is your opening to share the gospel. The gospel itself—the good news that salvation is finished, not something you earn—saves people. Good works alone cannot save; they only create the opportunity for the words of gospel to be heard.

### Being Visibly Different Creates Conversation

We need to live in ways that provoke questions. If we're indistinguishable from the pagan world—same priorities, same ambitions, same entertainment, same values—why would anyone ask us anything?

Think about real examples:
- When you prioritize family over career advancement, taking time off for a wedding despite the cost
- When you have children and don't chase worldly success, people ask why
- When you're professionally successful but live simply, that shocks people
- When you celebrate Christ instead of cultural holidays, it's different

These differences naturally create conversations about your values. They're openings to talk about God.

## Submission to Authorities (v. 13-17)

### Why Submit?

> Submit yourselves for the Lord's sake to every authority instituted among men: whether to the king, as the supreme authority, or to governors, who are sent by him to punish those who do wrong and to commend those who do right.

Government authority comes from God. This echoes Romans 13—authorities are established by God to do good. Even if imperfect, government is better than anarchy.

### The Tension: When Authority Conflicts with Conscience

We naturally ask: What if the government persecutes Christians? What if it forbids faith? What if it demands sin?

In extreme cases—if an authority commands you to do what God forbids—the step is to appeal to higher authorities through proper channels, or to resign and accept punishment. Biblical examples like Daniel, his friends, Peter, and Paul show faithful resistance to ungodly commands, but always with an acceptance of consequences, never with retaliation or revolution.

However, Peter and his first readers are not yet in such extreme situations. We must be careful not to make exceptions the rule. If we qualify submission too heavily, submission becomes meaningless.

### The Principle: Don't Abuse Freedom (v. 16)

> Live as free men, but do not use your freedom as a cover-up for evil; live as servants of God.

We are freed in Christ (Galatians 5:1), but this freedom doesn't exempt us from earthly submission. The Reformer Martin Luther captured this paradox beautifully: "A Christian is perfectly free lord of all, subject to none. A Christian is a perfectly dutiful servant of all, subject to all."

This is the tension we must hold. We submit to office, not personality. We don't submit because the authority is nice or competent—we submit because God has placed them in that office.

### Show Proper Respect (v. 17)

> Show proper respect to everyone: Love the brotherhood of believers, fear God, honor the king.

## Submission to Masters (v. 18-25)

### Understanding Biblical Slavery

We rightly hate slavery as practiced in the 18th and 19th centuries—the kidnapping of Africans, the Atlantic trade, the horrors documented in shipping records. But we must not let that atrocity distort our reading of biblical slavery, which operated under strict guidelines.

Biblical slaves:
- Could not be abused or kidnapped (1 Timothy 1:10 classifies slave traders with murderers)
- Had legal protections (Exodus 21:20-21)
- Could acquire property and conduct business
- Were freed at the Year of Jubilee (at most seven years of bondage for Hebrews)
- Were often prisoners of war, debt-servants, or those who voluntarily sold themselves to escape poverty

Biblical slavery was harsh but regulated. It was more like the faithful servant in Matthew 24 who managed his master's talents, or Joseph in Egypt, or Daniel in Babylon.

### Submit Even to Harsh Masters (v. 18-20)

> Slaves, submit yourselves to your masters with all respect, not only to those who are good and considerate, but also to those who are harsh. For it is commendable if a man bears up under the pain of unjust suffering because he is conscious of God. But how is it to your credit if you receive a beating for doing wrong and endure it? But if you suffer for doing good and you endure it, this is commendable before God.

The point isn't that abuse is acceptable—it's that submission is to the office, not the personality. You submit to a harsh master the same way you submit to a good one. This prevents selective submission based on what we prefer.

What's commendable? Suffering unjustly while doing right. The world qualifies submission—submit until insulted, submit until it hurts, submit until you disagree. But true submission has a heart of submission, not just external compliance.

### The Example of Christ (v. 21-25)

> To this you were called, because Christ suffered for you, leaving you an example, that you should follow in his steps. He committed no sin, and no deceit was found in his mouth. When they hurled their insults at him, he did not retaliate; when he suffered, he made no threats. Instead, he entrusted himself to him who judges justly. He himself bore our sins in his body on the tree, so that we might die to sins and live for righteousness; by his wounds you have been healed.

Jesus didn't look for exceptions. He didn't retaliate against false accusations or demand his rights. He entrusted himself to the Father as judge. He didn't qualify his submission—he embodied it completely. And in doing so, he bore our sins and opened the way to righteousness.

### Application to Modern Work

We may not be slaves, but many of us are in a kind of bondage:
- Contract employment binding us to an employer
- Mortgages tying us to work
- Student debt requiring repayment
- Phone contracts, family obligations

We must not sin to compensate for unfair treatment. We don't steal office supplies to make up for unpaid overtime. We don't waste time on social media. We sympathize with our bosses—they too have pressures. When we become leaders, we understand their weight.

Even with an unfair boss, Christ is our example. We don't look for ways to retaliate. We initiate submission and honor. If asked to sin, we escalate through proper channels or resign. We don't rebel.

But we also create opportunities for evangelism. By living with integrity and honesty, we give others reason to ask about our faith. This is how the gospel advances in hostile environments.

## Application: The Danger and Value of Submission

For nearly two centuries since the Enlightenment, we've emphasized personal rights and freedom so much that authority itself has become suspect. Teachers are guides, not authorities. Parents are friends, not leaders. Police are service providers. Government helps us pursue our goals but shouldn't tell us what to do.

But if we don't understand authority, we won't understand God. If we teach our children to submit to no one but themselves, they won't submit to God. And we won't understand marriage—the subject of the next teaching.

Let us live good lives that create opportunities to declare the gospel. Let us submit where God calls us to submit. And let us remember that in doing so, we follow the example of Christ himself.
