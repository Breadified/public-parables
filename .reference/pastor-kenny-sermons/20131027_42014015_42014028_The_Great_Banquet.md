# Luke 14:15-28

**Title:** The Great Banquet
**Date:** October 27, 2013
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Setting the Stage

Jesus is at a Pharisee's house on the Sabbath, surrounded by religious leaders watching his every move. After healing a man with dropsy, Jesus tells several parables that challenge the religious establishment's understanding of the kingdom of God. The parable of the Great Banquet emerges from this tension-filled conversation.

> "When one of those at the table with him heard this, he said to Jesus, 'Blessed is the one who will eat at the feast in the kingdom of God.'" (Luke 14:15)

This statement reveals the assumption of the religious leaders: they believed they had a secure place at God's table simply by virtue of their position and lineage. They were confident in their invitation to the kingdom.

## The Parable Unfolds

Jesus responds with a parable that dismantles this false confidence. A man prepares a great banquet and sends invitations to prominent guests—exactly the kind of people who would expect such an honor.

> "A certain man was preparing a great banquet and invited many guests. At the time of the banquet he sent his servant to tell those who had been invited, 'Come, for everything is now ready.'" (Luke 14:16-17)

The banquet is ready. The preparation is complete. The host, generous and gracious, calls his guests to come and enjoy what he has prepared.

## The Shocking Refusals

But something unexpected happens—the invited guests begin making excuses:

> "But they all alike began to make excuses. The first said, 'I have just bought a field, and I must go and see it. Please excuse me.' Another said, 'I have just bought five yoke of oxen, and I'm on my way to try them out. Please excuse me.' Still another said, 'I just got married, and so I can't come.'" (Luke 14:18-20)

These aren't merely polite deflections. The excuses reveal a troubling priority: a field, oxen, and marriage all take precedence over the host's gracious invitation. Each guest chooses something temporal over something eternal. Each prioritizes the material world over the kingdom of God.

The underlying sin isn't rudeness—it's ingratitude and misplaced affection. These guests treat the host's generosity with indifference. They've been invited to a feast prepared by someone of means and dignity, yet they turn away for mundane business.

## The Host's Response

What does the host do? He doesn't withdraw his generosity. Instead, his servant reports the rejections, and the host responds with surprising action:

> "The master told his servant, 'Go out quickly into the streets and alleys of the town and bring in the poor, the crippled, the blind and the lame.'" (Luke 14:21)

This is radical. The rejected guests were respectable people with legitimate (or at least plausible) excuses. The invited replacements are society's outcasts—those without status, wealth, or social standing. Yet these are the ones who will eat at the master's table.

The parable teaches a fundamental truth about the kingdom of God: it's not reserved for the self-righteous or the comfortable. It's offered to those who recognize their need and respond to grace with gratitude.

## The Inclusive Yet Selective Kingdom

Notice the paradox: the kingdom is radically inclusive yet paradoxically selective. The master says, "Go out to the highways and byways and make them come in, that my house may be full" (Luke 14:23, paraphrase). There's urgency and completeness to God's invitation.

But this inclusivity has boundaries. Not everyone receives God's grace. Some reject it outright. Others refuse to respond. The parable suggests that while God's invitation is extended generously to all, only those who accept it—who come hungering and thirsting, recognizing their spiritual poverty—will sit at his table.

## Application for Us

Kenny's pastoral heart here is to challenge us on the same issue Jesus raises with the Pharisees: What are we treating as more important than our relationship with God? Are we, like the original guests, making excuses based on possessions, pursuits, and personal plans? Or are we among those who recognize our need and respond eagerly to God's invitation?

The parable doesn't condemn buying fields or oxen or getting married. These are legitimate activities. But they become sinful when they displace God from the center of our lives. The invitation to the kingdom is comprehensive and urgent. It demands a response—either acceptance or rejection. There is no neutral ground.

Those who come to God's table do so as beggars, acknowledging their spiritual poverty and hunger. They come not because they deserve it, but because they've been graciously invited and have chosen to accept. This is the scandal of grace that the religious establishment could never accept—that God would welcome the outcast and overlook the respectable who reject him.
