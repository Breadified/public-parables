# Luke 23:50-24:12

**Title:** The King Resurrected
**Date:** April 16, 2017
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## The Reality of Death

A few years ago, a young man in Sri Lanka attempted to set a world record for being buried alive. He was interred in a three-meter trench, sealed with wood and soil. When brought to the surface after several hours, he was unconscious and pronounced dead on arrival at the hospital. He had chased a record that didn't even exist—and it cost him his life.

Today we encounter another man buried in the earth. But unlike that tragic story, this man was buried *dead* and rose *alive*.

## Truth One: Jesus Was Truly Dead

Scripture records Jesus's burial with remarkable detail:

> There was a good and righteous man named Joseph, a member of the Sanhedrin, who had not agreed with their plan and action. He was from Arimathea, a Judean town, and was looking forward to the kingdom of God. He approached Pilate and asked for Jesus's body. Taking it down from the cross, he wrapped it in fine linen and placed it in a tomb cut into the rock, where no one had ever been placed. (Luke 23:50-53)

Joseph of Arimathea was a man of considerable influence—wealthy enough to own a rock-cut tomb, with fine linen at his disposal. This detail matters profoundly: **burial is the ultimate confirmation of death**. A person might faint or be resuscitated before burial, but burial declares finality. When we bury our loved ones, we acknowledge we will not see them again in this life. We do not dig them up. We lay them to rest in peace.

Death is the one absolute certainty of human existence. As George Bernard Shaw observed: "The statistics on death are quite impressive: one out of one people die." Regardless of wealth, status, era, or belief—all humans share this inescapable reality. We are all equal in mortality.

### Why Did Jesus Have to Die?

Death entered the world through sin. "The wages of sin is death" (Romans 6:23). All humanity stands under judgment because all have sinned against God's eternal will. But Jesus was different. He was perfectly righteous and deserved no death. Yet He died—not for His own sins, but for ours.

His death was a ransom. In those ancient stories where villains capture a hero's loved ones, the hero steps forward: "Take me instead. Let them go." Jesus made the ultimate exchange: His death for our redemption. He paid the price to free us from sin's grip.

## Truth Two: Jesus Truly Resurrected

The women who came to complete the burial preparations encountered something astonishing:

> They found the stone rolled away from the tomb. They went in but did not find the body of the Lord Jesus. While they were perplexed about this, suddenly two men stood by them in dazzling clothes. (Luke 24:2-4)

The disciples' reactions reveal genuine shock:

> But these words seemed like nonsense to them, and they did not believe the women. Peter, however, got up and ran to the tomb. When he stooped to look in, he saw only the linen cloths. So he went home, amazed at what had happened. (Luke 24:11-12)

They were not gullible or superstitious. They knew—with absolute certainty—that Jesus was dead. A man rising from the dead would have seemed as impossible then as it does now. Yet they changed their minds. Why? Because the evidence compelled them.

### The Evidence for Resurrection

**The Empty Tomb**

The angels posed a simple question: "Why are you looking for the living among the dead?" (Luke 24:5). If the resurrection were a fabrication, the opposition had a simple refutation: produce the body. The Romans knew how to execute. The chief priests could have silenced every witness. They only needed one corpse. Yet no body ever appeared.

**Fulfilled Prophecy**

Jesus Himself had predicted this event:

> The Son of Man must be betrayed into the hands of sinful men, be crucified, and rise on the third day. (Luke 24:7)

This wasn't a random miracle—it was the fulfillment of Jesus's own repeated promises and the pattern of Old Testament prophecy (the sign of Jonah: three days in the earth as Jonah spent three days in the great fish). The timeline is precise: Friday evening to Sunday morning, encompassing the Sabbath rest and the dawning of new creation.

### Addressing Skepticism

Some argue that extraordinary claims require extraordinary evidence. But this objection reveals a hidden bias. We accept extraordinary news regularly without demanding special proof—a black president, a reality-television star in the White House. We don't fly to verify every newsworthy event personally. Yet with resurrection, skeptics move the goalpost, demanding a higher standard of evidence precisely because they don't *want* to believe.

The resurrection doesn't require extraordinary evidence. It requires ordinary evidence: eyewitnesses, corroboration, internal consistency, the absence of alternative explanations. We don't need special proof to know when someone is alive—we see them. We don't need special proof when someone is dead—we attend their funeral. Why should we need more evidence when someone is alive again?

What is extraordinary isn't the evidence—it's the *implication*. If this is true, then Jesus has done more than pay our ransom. He has **conquered death itself**.

## Truth Three: Rejecting the Lie

The dangerous lie is that this resurrection doesn't matter. Some live as though this event has no relevance to how we should live.

But it changes everything.

### Resurrection Gives Us Hope

Without resurrection, everything is meaningless. Atheistic logic leads to despair: "Eat, drink, for tomorrow we die." Any blessing we enjoy ends in heartbreak. Any good we accomplish decays into obsolescence. When our lives pass—mere moments in an infinite cosmos—whatever mark we leave will be washed away like footprints in sand.

But Jesus's resurrection promises otherwise:

> For if, while we were enemies, we were reconciled to God through the death of His Son, then how much more, having been reconciled, will we be saved by His life! (Romans 5:10)

Christ did not merely die for our sins. He lives to intercede for us. His ongoing life covers not just our past failures but our present and future ones. We are not alone in our struggle; we have an Advocate before the Father.

The resurrection assures us that our lives have significance both temporal and eternal. Our investments in God's kingdom are not in vain. Because Jesus conquered death, we anticipate victory over the grave. His triumph gives us courage to endure tragedy and wisdom to cherish every good thing we experience. We can face our own death without fear.

Victor Frankl, a Holocaust survivor, observed that while people in the most horrific circumstances responded differently—some losing hope and taking their own lives, others praising God even walking toward certain death—there was one constant: *people cannot survive without hope for more than a minute*. Hope is not luxury; it is survival.

### Living in Light of Resurrection

The resurrection transforms how we live. We no longer live for hollow pursuits—the world record no one keeps, the wealth that perishes, the status that fades. We live for the resurrection.

> For the grace of God has appeared with salvation for all people, instructing us to deny godlessness and worldly lusts and to live in a sensible, righteous, and godly way in the present age, while we wait for the blessed hope and appearing of the glory of our great God and Savior, Jesus Christ. (Titus 2:11-13)

This means:
- Denying godlessness—living with God's values, not indifference to Him
- Denying worldly lusts—refusing to be consumed by endless acquisition and pleasure
- Living righteously and sensibly now—using our time, resources, and talents for eternal purposes rather than empty distractions

Don't be buried alive in this world, suffocated by possessions and pursuits that have no ultimate value. Instead, be alive—truly alive—in service to God. In God's eyes, there is no record for the richest person, the person with the most possessions. The greatest in God's kingdom is the one who serves.

### Why This Matters for Us

Christians gather each Sunday to remember this: that Jesus rose from the dead and that all of Scripture speaks of His betrayal, crucifixion, and resurrection. We remember. We encourage one another. We celebrate that our Savior lives.

This means we grieve differently at death. We don't mourn as those without hope. When we lay a loved one to rest, we can truly say "see you later" and mean it—for we will meet again in resurrected bodies, in a renewed creation where death has no dominion.

The resurrection is not merely a past event to commemorate. It is the foundation of our present assurance and our future hope. Christ is risen. Because He lives, we live. And death—for all its power—has been stripped of its final victory.

This Easter, take this truth to heart: the story doesn't end at the tomb. It begins there.
