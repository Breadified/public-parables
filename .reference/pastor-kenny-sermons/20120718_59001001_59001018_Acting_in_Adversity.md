# James 1:1-18

**Title:** Acting in Adversity
**Date:** July 18, 2012
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Trials Test Our Faith

Have you ever faced circumstances where your trust in Jesus and God's goodness felt shaken? Not necessarily persecution—just the ordinary trials of life. Death in the family. Unexpected tragedy. The loss of everything you hoped for.

I remember a friend from my first year at Bible college whose wife was pregnant with twins. The babies were due to be induced on a Saturday at 1 PM, but at noon, she began bleeding severely from the womb. They rushed to the hospital, but it was too late. Both babies died—suffocating from lack of oxygen as the placenta separated. An hour. Just one hour before they were due to arrive. Two lives, gone.

At their funeral, my friend described it in the eulogy this way: "A bandit came into my home, stole my babies, and left a puddle of blood behind." The deepest question burned in his heart: Why, God? His faith was being tested in ways most of us cannot imagine.

How are we to respond to trials, suffering, mistreatment, disasters, and tragedies? This is what James teaches us.

## 1. Enjoy Your Trials (James 1:2-5)

James writes something that seems almost absurd:

> Consider it pure joy, my brothers, whenever you face trials of many kinds.

Be happy? In disappointment, frustration, and sadness, James calls us to rejoice. This is possible for specific, life-transforming reasons.

### For Trials Produce Endurance

> Because you know that the testing of your faith develops perseverance. (1:3)

The trial itself is not the end goal—it is the means to an end. Testing produces perseverance: the capacity to trust God no matter what, to keep obeying Him regardless of circumstances.

We might wish God would simply transform us supernaturally, painlessly, instantly into His likeness. But God has chosen a different way. And this method is not strange when we look at life itself.

Consider how we identify quality. How do you know which batteries last longest? You test them. Testing separates the genuine from the counterfeit. So it is with faith. The trial tests and refines what is real in us.

### For Trials Lead to Maturity and Completeness

> Perseverance must finish its work so that you may be mature and complete, not lacking anything. (1:4)

There is a goal to the Christian life: to become like Jesus, mature and complete, lacking nothing. But we must persevere through trials to reach it.

Think of physical training. The most tempting moment to stop exercising is when it begins to hurt—precisely the moment when old muscle is burning away to make room for new, stronger growth. That's the whole point of the gym. To stop now would mean quitting before the work is done.

It's like completing a course of antibiotics. If you stop early, the medicine never achieves its full effect, and the illness returns. You must persevere to completion.

The question is: Do we persevere when times grow hard? We need resilience. And we must teach our children resilience.

### For God's Testing Leads to Life

> Blessed is the man who perseveres under trial, because when he has stood the test, he will receive the crown of life that God has promised to those who love him. (1:12)

The crown is an ancient symbol of victory. In the Olympic games, the victor received the crown. For the Christian, victory is eternal life—the crown of life is another word for salvation, for heaven itself.

Peter wrote:

> The God of all grace, after you have suffered a little while, will himself restore you and make you strong, firm and steadfast. (1 Peter 5:10)

**Application: Don't give up the crown of life.** If you are walking through a trial, persevere. You know it serves you. You will receive the crown.

Consider Joni Eareckson Tada. A quadriplegic for nearly fifty years following a diving accident, she has suffered from chronic pain and has recently been diagnosed with breast cancer. Yet she has said:

> Only God knows why I was paralyzed. Maybe He knew I'd be ultimately happier serving Him. If I were still on my feet, it's hard to say how things would have gone. I probably would have drifted through life—marriage, maybe divorce—dissatisfied and disillusioned. When I was in high school, I reacted to life selfishly and never built any lasting values. God refined me through suffering that I could never have been refined through comfort.

Trials expose our inadequacies and show us where we need God's attention most. John Calvin wrote: "If we are given gold, would we not test it by fire to determine its value? So God tests us with the fire of affliction, revealing the true quality of our faith." The trial reveals our shortcomings and humbles our pride.

### A Word for the Comfortable

Some of us are not facing particular hardship. This is especially true here in Australia. Life comes easier. Our exchange rate has risen 30% in eighteen months without us doing any extra work. We subcontract our responsibilities—childcare, cleaning—so life flows smoothly. We gratify our desires instantly: one click on an app, charged to the credit card.

We should be deeply grateful for God's blessings. But there is danger: we may not mature. We may not develop the strength that only difficulty builds.

Perhaps we should intentionally practice delayed gratification. Perhaps we should choose hardship sometimes, so we train ourselves in endurance.

### Building Perseverance in Community and Family

We must not only be steadfast ourselves—we must encourage one another. This is what the church exists for: to help each other remain Christian even through suffering. Sometimes those facing trials don't need solutions. They simply need to know someone cares and will listen.

We must teach our children perseverance. But in our eagerness to protect them, we often shield them from failure. When they won't study, we hire tutors. When exams approach, we excuse them from chores. Every time it gets hard, we let them skip it. But this teaches the opposite of perseverance. It teaches them that someone else will solve their problems. Then one day they face something we cannot fix—they give up. They face hardship as Christians and they abandon Jesus.

Let us be a tested people. On that final day, there are only two options: those who have persevered and receive the crown of life, or those fading away in the burning sun.

## 2. Do Not Test God (James 1:6-17)

Yet there is a reciprocal truth: while God testing us leads to life, testing God leads to death.

### How We Test God

> But when he asks, he must believe and not doubt, because he who doubts is like a wave of the sea, blown and tossed by the wind. That man should not think he will receive anything from the Lord; he is a double-minded man, unstable in all he does. (1:6-8)

When we ask God for wisdom, we must ask in faith—genuinely believing that God will do what is good for us. We are not to doubt.

Now, doubting here does not mean asking sincere questions: *What does this passage mean? Why did God allow this? Why, God?* These are not doubts. True doubt is suspicion of God's heart: *He's out to get me. God is evil. God doesn't care.*

Such a person is like waves tossed by the wind—double-minded, wavering between two masters, unstable in everything. You ask God for wisdom while secretly doubting His goodness. You're asking Him to help while questioning His character. That is testing God—and it will inevitably lead to death.

### The Path from Temptation to Death

> When tempted, no one should say, "God is tempting me." For God cannot be tempted by evil, nor does he tempt anyone; but each one is tempted when, by his own evil desire, he is dragged away and enticed. Then, after desire has conceived, it gives birth to sin; and sin, when it is full-grown, gives birth to death. (1:13-15)

When we face temptation, we want to blame God. It's His fault, so whatever evil comes from it must be His doing. But this thinking leads straight to death.

Compare two paths:

**Path to Life:** Trials → Perseverance → Maturity → Wisdom → Life

**Path to Death:** Temptation → Desire → Sin → Death

God is not evil. We are. Sin originates not from outside but from within us. God does not trap people in sin. God is good.

> Don't be deceived, my dear brothers. Every good and perfect gift is from above, coming down from the Father of the heavenly lights, who does not change like shifting shadows. (1:16-17)

God is consistent and never deceptive. He doesn't change His mind from merciful to cruel. He is not playing games with us.

### Application: We Must Not Use Trials as an Excuse for Sin

How do we today blame God and justify our sin?

- *It's not my fault I'm poor—so I'll be stingy and selfish.*
- *It's not my fault I'm in a terrible job—so I'll cheat the company.*
- *It's not my fault I'm lonely—God didn't provide a Christian spouse—so I'll marry an unbeliever.*
- *It's not my fault movies and software are expensive—so I'll download them illegally.*

These desires come from within us, not from God. Testing can never justify sin. When you sin, you have already failed in perseverance.

You cannot test God this way, because God's goodness has already been tested and proven.

### God's Goodness Has Already Been Tested

> Of his own will he brought us forth by the word of truth, that we should be a kind of firstfruits of his creation. (1:18)

By His own initiative, God sent His Son to redeem us from sin. He made us to be firstfruits of His creation. Instead of the path that leads from desire to death, we have been reborn to be firstfruits—the beginning of His new creation.

He has made us alive through the word of truth—the gospel of salvation.

God's goodness must never be doubted, because He has paid an enormous price to prove it:

> God demonstrates his love for us in this: while we were still sinners, Christ died for us. (Romans 5:8)

And Jesus not only suffered as we do—He actually persevered all the way through. Jesus Himself cried out, *My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?* Yet in obedience, He remained on the cross and died. He never wavered in His loyalties. He never doubted the Father's goodness. He endured every test through perfect obedience.

This is the God we must keep trusting. This is the God we must keep persevering for, no matter what circumstances surround us. This is the God I reminded my friend of in his darkest hour: Do not be deceived. Blessed is the one who remains steadfast under trial.

## Conclusion

Friends, trials and temptations lead us along one of two paths. One leads to joy, perseverance, wisdom, and life. The other leads to sin and death. The choice, moment by moment, is ours. Choose perseverance. Choose to trust the God whose goodness has already been proven through the cross. Choose life.
