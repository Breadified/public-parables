# 1 Corinthians 13:1-13

**Title:** Love: The Essential Foundation for All Gifts
**Date:** May 3, 2020
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## The Problem: Gifts Without Love Are Useless

> If I speak in the tongues of men or of angels, but do not have love, I am only a resounding gong or a clanging cymbal. (v.1)

Paul opens with a striking image. You can possess remarkable abilities—eloquent speech, prophetic insight, mountain-moving faith, radical generosity—yet without love, these gifts become mere noise. Like trivia that clutters the mind but changes nothing, the exercise of spiritual gifts apart from love profits no one.

### The List of Hollow Abilities

Paul catalogs abilities that seem powerful on their surface:

**Speaking Abilities (Tongues & Prophecy)**

Tongues here refers to languages—whether human or angelic. Interestingly, "angel" in Scripture often simply means messenger. Even supernatural angelic beings communicated in ordinary human languages (Hebrew and Aramaic) when they visited Abraham. The point is not mystical utterance but human communication.

Similarly, prophecy represents the ability to speak God's truth and penetrate mysteries and knowledge. Two of Paul's four examples involve speaking—highlighting how much we value eloquent and authoritative speech in our communities.

**Faith That Moves Mountains**

> If I have faith that can move mountains, but do not have love, I am nothing. (v.2)

This echoes Jesus's teaching: "If you have faith as small as a mustard seed, you can say to this mountain, 'Move from here to there,' and it will move. Nothing will be impossible for you" (Matthew 17:20).

But what is this power? Paul connects faith to speech: "Since we have that same spirit of faith, we also believe and therefore speak" (2 Corinthians 4:13). Faith is conviction that compels us to speak—not a magical ability, but conviction that leads to witness.

**Material Sacrifice**

> If I give all I possess to the poor and give over my body to hardship that I may boast, but do not have love, I gain nothing. (v.3)

Even radical self-giving—surrendering everything, enduring suffering—becomes worthless without love undergirding it. Paul himself knew this cost intimately: "I have labored and toiled and have often gone without sleep; I have known hunger and thirst and have often gone without food; I have been cold and naked" (2 Corinthians 11:27-29). Yet he warns against boasting in these sacrifices.

### The Verdict

All these abilities—beautiful, powerful, even heroic—amount to nothing without love:
- **"A resounding gong or clanging cymbal"** (noise without meaning)
- **"I am nothing"** (v.2)
- **"I gain nothing"** (v.3—literally "profitless," like a company that labors all year and shows no profit)

The critical ingredient that transforms gifts from useless performance into meaningful service is **love**.

## What Love Actually Is (v.4-7)

> Love is patient, love is kind. It does not envy, it does not boast, it is not proud. It does not dishonor others, it is not self-seeking, it is not easily angered, it keeps no record of wrongs. Love does not delight in evil but rejoices with the truth. It always protects, always trusts, always hopes, always perseveres.

This list is not exhaustive, but it reveals love's fundamental nature: **humble service and other-centeredness**. Love is not self-seeking. It prioritizes the good of the whole body, not individual exercise of gifts.

The mathematical reality is simple:

**GIFTS × LOVE = PROFIT**

But: **GIFTS × GIFTS × GIFTS × 0 (no love) = 0**

No matter how many gifts you accumulate, multiply them infinitely—without love, the result remains zero.

Conversely, even modest gifts combined with genuine love produce extraordinary results. God works wonders through small offerings when they flow from love.

## Love as the Foundation, Not the Supplement

Love provides the proper focus for our gifts. It answers the crucial question: **Does this exhibit thoughtful love?** Or is it merely the unrestricted, self-centered exercise of ability?

God gives gifts for the common good—not for you alone, not even for your friends. The body is more important than the individual exercise of your abilities.

### From "Gifts" to "Opportunities"

A subtle but important shift in thinking: Rather than starting with "What gifts do I have?", start with **"What opportunities exist? What needs are present?"**

High academic ability doesn't automatically mean you must pursue law or medicine. Maybe God wants you to teach underprivileged students, serve in politics, or pursue studies that fulfill you personally. The ability is a tool; your goals determine its use.

Similarly, ecclesiastical gifts are opportunities to serve, not entitlements to exercise. Just because you can sing beautifully doesn't mean you must lead worship. Just because you're gifted in administration doesn't mean the church needs you in that role.

**Examine the actual needs:** Does the congregation need two drummers? Does leadership neglect welcoming while prioritizing music? In our transition to online church during the shutdown, do we even need a band?

Look at needs first. Then ask who can fill them out of love.

### The Common Ministry Principle

No ministry in our congregation operates in isolation. Everything is interconnected through the body. When we exercise gifts without considering the whole, we create unnecessary conflict and division.

Remember our early days: with thirty people, ten led the music ministry while visitors were neglected. We shifted our focus—from "who's talented?" to "who needs welcome?" We trained people in hospitality, created inclusive fellowship over shared meals, and when costs rose, we cooked together. We started with love and then mobilized gifts to meet actual needs.

**You don't need permission to love.** You don't require committee approval to check on someone's wellbeing or invite a friend over. Restrictions and awkwardness shouldn't paralyze the simple act of caring for one another.

### A Practical Reorientation

When serving in the church, ask yourself:
- Who needs to hear the gospel?
- Who needs encouragement?
- Who needs someone to talk to?

Everyone can do this. You don't need a formal title or special ability. You have the gift of saying hello. You have the gift of speaking God's Word to others. You have email to invite someone to youth group.

Don't say you have no gifts. Open your mind to the roles and opportunities all around you. They all require love.

## Love Alone Endures (v.8-13)

> Love never fails. But where there are prophecies, they will cease; where there are tongues, they will be stilled; where there is knowledge, it will pass away. (v.8)

This is love's ultimate vindication: **gifts are temporary, love is eternal.**

One day our congregation will cease to exist. Not in three months, but eventually—when we've grown for generations, when we're all gone, when buildings are demolished and memories fade. Sermons will be forgotten. Deeds will be lost to history.

But love will remain.

Those who heard the gospel because we spoke it, who turned from idols to follow the living God—they will remain. Though people forget, **God will remember that we served out of love.**

> And now these three remain: faith, hope and love. But the greatest of these is love. (v.13)

## Jesus as the Perfect Expression of Love

When you struggle with this vision of love—when the list of patient, kind, humble, other-centered service feels overwhelming—remember: **Jesus embodies this love perfectly.**

Read the description again through the lens of Christ:

Jesus is patient. Jesus is kind. Jesus does not envy, does not boast, is not proud. Jesus does not dishonor others, is not self-seeking, is not easily angered, keeps no record of wrongs (because He paid for all wrongs). Jesus does not delight in evil but rejoices with truth. Jesus always protects, always trusts, always hopes, always perseveres.

As we fix our gaze on Jesus and remember what He has done for us, as we speak of His love to one another, we will begin to love others. Our small, seemingly pathetic efforts—our little gifts—will be transformed into gold when motivated by His love.

---

## The Call Forward

We are not going into an uncertain future merely to survive. We are stepping forward for glory—God's glory, won through love.

This is true spirituality: not the accumulation of talents or the exercise of impressive abilities, but humble service motivated by Christ's love for us and expressed toward one another.

Don't trivialize your opportunities. Make them count through love.
