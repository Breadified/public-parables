# Matthew 17:1-26

**Title:** Serving the Son
**Date:** 6 March 2010
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Transfigured Son

After six days, Jesus took Peter, James, and John up a high mountain by themselves. There, something remarkable happened—Jesus was transfigured before them. His face shone like the sun, and his clothes became as white as light.

The word "transfigured" carries special significance. It means to be transformed, morphed into something entirely new and glorious. Jesus didn't just change appearance—he revealed his divine nature in a way his disciples could witness.

This was no ordinary moment. Throughout his ministry, Jesus walked among crowds, healing and teaching. But at certain times, he withdrew with a smaller group to show them something deeper about who he truly was. This was one of those sacred moments on a mountaintop.

The significance is profound: this incident reminds us that Jesus is not merely a human superstar or moral teacher. His philosophy is so transformative that it has shaped modern civilization—our legal systems, our economies, our social welfare, our very freedoms all rest on Judeo-Christian principles. Even those who don't claim to be Christian benefit from living according to his teachings.

But Jesus offers far more than good philosophy. He is divine. He comes from God himself. He is Emmanuel—God with us. This transfiguration was a declaration of his divinity, not just another historical figure, but the glorious God himself in human form.

## Listen to Him

As the disciples watched, Moses and Elijah appeared, talking with Jesus. Peter, overwhelmed by the moment, spoke up: "Lord, it is good for us to be here. If you wish, I will put up three shelters—one for you, one for Moses and one for Elijah."

But even as he spoke, something more significant occurred. A bright cloud covered them, and a voice from the cloud declared:

> "This is my Son, whom I love; with him I am well pleased. Listen to him!"

This was direct instruction from God himself—an authoritative command from beyond this world. The language echoes the Old Testament: just as Moses received the law from God on Mount Sinai with clouds and divine presence, so now God affirms Jesus as the true lawgiver. "My Son" speaks of his heir and authority. "With him I am well pleased" assures us he is not a disappointing or wayward son. And most importantly: "Listen to him!"

Jesus is the new Moses, the one to whom all authority has been given. He supersedes the old law and the old covenant. We must hear him, obey him, trust him.

## Do Not Be Afraid

The disciples' reaction was terror. They fell facedown to the ground, overwhelmed by what they had witnessed. But Jesus came and touched them, speaking words of comfort: "Get up. Don't be afraid."

This command might seem puzzling. Why shouldn't they be afraid? Jesus as judge is indeed fearful and full of dread—he holds ultimate power and authority. But his instruction to "not be afraid" becomes clear as he continues:

> "Don't tell anyone what you have seen, until the Son of Man has been raised from the dead."

As they descended the mountain, the disciples asked about the prophecy that Elijah must come first. Jesus replied: "Elijah has already come, and they did not recognize him, but have done to him everything they wished. In the same way the Son of Man is going to suffer at their hands."

Here is the reason for courage: Christ suffered on our behalf. Yes, he is powerful and fearful as judge. But he has chosen to bear our punishment. He has offered himself as the sacrifice. Therefore, we need not fear him as we would fear a police officer when we've done nothing wrong—because our debt has been paid. His suffering was for us, not against us.

This is the comfort that casts out fear: the Son of God, in all his glory and power, has become our substitute and our Savior.
