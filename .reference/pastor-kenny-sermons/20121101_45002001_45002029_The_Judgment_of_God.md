# Romans 2:1-29

**Title:** The Judgment of God
**Date:** 11 November 2012
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction: The Contrast Effect

When we perceive the world around us, we often judge things by comparison. A white square appears gray when placed next to an even whiter square. This is the contrast effect—how we perceive things depends on what surrounds them.

Similarly, we tend to measure our own goodness by comparing ourselves to others. But Paul addresses a critical question in Romans: Are we comparing ourselves against the right standard? Are we judging ourselves rightly before God?

The early church faced significant tension between Jewish Christians, who had centuries of God's law and covenant, and Gentile Christians, who were new to faith. This same dynamic appears in churches today—between longtime believers and new converts. Paul writes Romans to remind both groups of a foundational truth: **God is fair.**

## Point 1: God Judges Impartially

> He will render to each one according to his works: to those who by patience in well-doing seek for glory and honor and immortality, he will give eternal life; but for those who are self-seeking and do not obey the truth, but obey unrighteousness, there will be wrath and fury. There will be tribulation and distress for every human being who does evil, the Jew first and also the Greek, but glory and honor and peace for everyone who does good, the Jew first and also the Greek. For God shows no partiality. (Romans 2:6-11)

God's judgment is absolutely fair. He renders judgment according to works—not status, not background, not how long someone has been a Christian. Every person will give an account for how they have lived.

### Two Eternal Outcomes

The passage presents a sobering reality: there are only two possible destinations—eternal life or eternal judgment from God. Jonathan Edwards wrote, "The manifold and continual experience of the world in all ages, shews that this is no evidence that a man is not on the very brink of eternity, and that the next step wont be into another world."

Every action we take now determines where we will spend eternity. This is not legalism—it is the impartial reality of God's judgment. Paul will later explain (in chapter 3) that salvation comes through Christ's work, not our works. But here, Paul establishes the foundational truth: all people stand equally before God's judgment.

### Application

- Whether you have been a Christian for decades or converted yesterday, God treats each of us without favoritism
- God looks at our lives and judges according to what we actually do
- The question becomes personal: When God examines your life, where do you stand in his justice? Do you seek God's glory and honor, or your own?

## Point 2: No External Status Saves Us

Paul now turns directly to the Jewish community. Why does he emphasize God's impartial judgment? Because some believed their status as God's chosen people would exempt them from judgment. Paul's argument is direct: **Just because you are a Jew does not save you from the judgment of God.**

### The Privilege of the Law

The Jewish nation held something no other nation possessed:

> What other nation is so great as to have their gods near them the way the LORD our God is near us whenever we pray to him? And what other nation is so great as to have such righteous decrees and laws as this body of laws I am setting before you today? (Deuteronomy 4:7-8, NIV)

In Genesis 17, God established an everlasting covenant with Abraham, marked by circumcision. Jewish children grew up knowing they were chosen—circumcision represented not just national identity, but spiritual election. The law wasn't arbitrary; it was the gift that let Israel live as God intended and teach the nations about the true God.

The Gentiles, lacking the law, were expected to see a righteous Israel and be drawn to worship Israel's God. But something went terribly wrong.

### The Hypocrisy

> But if you call yourself a Jew and rely on the law and boast in God and know his will and approve what is excellent, because you are instructed from the law; and if you are sure that you yourself are a guide to the blind, a light to those who are in darkness, an instructor of the foolish, a teacher of children, having in the law the embodiment of knowledge and truth—you then who teach others, do you not teach yourself? While you preach against stealing, do you steal? You who say that one must not commit adultery, do you commit adultery? You who abhor idols, do you rob temples? You who boast in the law dishonor God by breaking the law. For, as it is written, "The name of God is blasphemed among the Gentiles because of you." (Romans 2:17-24)

The Jews became hypocrites. They possessed the law but didn't follow it. They looked down on Gentile ungodliness while breaking the very laws they claimed to uphold. As a result, unbelievers saw Israel and rejected their God.

There's an English phrase: "familiarity breeds contempt." The Jews became so familiar with their covenant privilege that they showed contempt for God's law. They took their relationship with God for granted.

### Parallel Temptation Today

This same hypocrisy can sneak into modern churches. We might judge newer Christians for not attending every service while we attend regularly but spend the time outside on our phones. We look down on new believers for their cultural ignorance while we quietly break God's law through our behavior.

> Therefore you have no excuse, O man, every one of you who judges. For in passing judgment on another you condemn yourself, because you, the judge, practice the very same things... Do you presume on the riches of his kindness and forbearance and patience, not knowing that God's kindness is meant to lead you to repentance? But because of your hard and impenitent heart you are storing up wrath for yourself on the day of wrath when God's righteous judgment will be revealed. (Romans 2:1-5)

The Jews believed they would escape judgment because they possessed the law. Instead, they were storing up God's wrath. They forgot that God is the judge.

### Application

- External status means nothing before God—not church attendance, not theological correctness, not denominational belonging
- God's kindness is meant to lead us to repentance, not to give us false security
- Hypocrisy—teaching one thing while practicing another—is what provokes God's judgment most severely

## Point 3: Circumcision of the Heart

### The Gentile Response

Remarkably, the Gentile Christians grasped something the privileged Jews missed:

> So, if a man who is uncircumcised keeps the precepts of the law, will not his uncircumcision be regarded as circumcision? Then he who is physically uncircumcised but keeps the law will condemn you who have the written code and circumcision but break the law. (Romans 2:26-27)

The Gentiles, born without the law and without circumcision, were obeying in ways the Jews were not. In God's fair judgment, their obedience counted more than the Jews' empty circumcision.

### The True Jew

> For no one is a Jew who is merely one outwardly, nor is circumcision outward and physical. But a Jew is one inwardly, and circumcision is a matter of the heart, by the Spirit, not by the letter. His praise is not from man but from God. (Romans 2:28-29)

Being truly Jewish—truly belonging to God—is not about external markers. It requires an inner transformation so deep, so profound, that it changes the very essence of who you are. It is nothing less than a **circumcision of the heart**.

The name "Judah" comes from a Hebrew word meaning "praise God." The true Jew is the one who praises God not just in word but in their entire life—by the Spirit, not by mere rule-keeping.

Ultimately, Jesus is the true Jew. He alone perfectly praised God in word and deed. He alone sought God's glory rather than his own. He alone lived without hypocrisy. Only by faith in Jesus can we too become the true people of God.

### The Danger of External Religion

Many of us are tempted to treat Jesus as simply another set of rules to follow. A young man once asked me what it takes to make Jesus his king. He thought it meant adopting more practices—reading the Bible when he didn't want to, attending church out of obligation. To him, following Jesus felt like a burden of additional laws.

But that misses the point entirely. The solution is not more rules; it is a transformed heart. The danger for those of us who teach Scripture regularly is profound: we can speak biblical truth while our hearts remain unchanged. We can know the right answers without letting them transform us.

> For when Gentiles, who do not have the law, by nature do what the law requires, they are a law to themselves, even though they do not have the law. They show that the work of the law is written on their hearts, while their conscience also bears witness, and their conflicting thoughts accuse or even excuse them on that day when, according to my gospel, God judges the secrets of men by Christ Jesus. (Romans 2:14-16)

### Application

- Do you understand that you stand before a God who sees everything and will judge accordingly?
- Are you relying on external markers of faith—church membership, good works, theological knowledge—to save you?
- What you need is a circumcision of the heart by the Spirit: a fundamental transformation of your inner self
- Only faith in Jesus Christ, who alone perfectly obeyed and praised God, can bring that transformation
- Whether you are a new believer or have walked with Christ for decades, you stand in the same place—needing the same gospel of grace
