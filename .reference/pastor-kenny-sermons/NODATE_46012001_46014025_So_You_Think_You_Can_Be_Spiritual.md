## Part 1: Desiring Prophecy (1 Corinthians 14:1-25)

### Understanding Prophecy

When we speak of prophecy, we're talking about two dimensions: foretelling (predicting future events) and forthtelling (declaring God's truth in the present). In the context of Paul's letter, he's primarily concerned with forthtelling—speaking God's word into the community.

### Edification: The Heart of Spiritual Gifts

> For no one can lay any foundation other than the one already laid, which is Jesus Christ. (1 Corinthians 3:11)

The central question Paul raises is this: What is the purpose of spiritual gifts? The answer is edification—building each other up in the faith.

When Paul writes about prophecy, he's establishing a principle that flows throughout his teaching on spiritual gifts:

**Spirit → Edification through Prophecy**

This isn't about showy displays of spiritual power. It's about speaking truth in love—the kind of speech that constructs faith and strengthens the body of Christ.

> Speaking the truth in love, we will grow to become in every respect the mature body of him who is the head, that is, Christ. (Ephesians 4:15)

This principle weaves through Scripture:

- **"Speak the gospel"** (Romans 15:20)—making Christ known
- **"Word of grace"** (Acts 20:32)—words that strengthen and edify
- **"Words that build and plant"** (Jeremiah 1:10)—constructive speech that establishes faith

Therefore, desire prophecy—not for personal prestige, but because it builds up the body of Christ.

### The Problem with Tongues Without Interpretation

Paul isn't forbidding tongues. Rather, he's addressing a critical issue: when someone speaks in a tongue without interpretation, no one is edified. No one understands what's being said.

> Acts 2:4 All of them were filled with the Holy Spirit and began to speak in other tongues as the Spirit enabled them. Acts 2:5-11 Now there were staying in Jerusalem God-fearing Jews from every nation under heaven. When they heard this sound, a crowd came together in bewilderment, because each one heard them speaking in his own language... Parthians, Medes and Elamites; residents of Mesopotamia, Judea and Cappadocia, Pontus and Asia, Phrygia and Pamphylia, Egypt and the parts of Libya near Cyrene; visitors from Rome... Cretans and Arabs—we hear them declaring the wonders of God in our own tongues!

On Pentecost, tongues served a divine purpose: to communicate the gospel clearly to people from many nations. Each person heard the mighty acts of God in their own language.

**In church gatherings, intelligibility matters.** Don't do your own thing. The whole point is that others are built up, encouraged, and strengthened in their faith. If no one understands, nothing is accomplished.

Furthermore, speaking in uninterpreted tongues reverses the very good news of Pentecost. At Pentecost, barriers fell and people understood. In an uninterpreted tongue in church, barriers go up and understanding is lost.

The principle is clear: **The intelligent proclamation of the gospel—whether we call it prophecy or clear teaching—edifies the body of Christ.** This is what true spirituality looks like in practice.

## Part 2: Same Spirit, Different Gifts (1 Corinthians 12:1-11 & 12:7-31)

### What Is True Spirituality?

Before Paul addresses gifts, he clarifies what we're actually discussing. Are we talking about supernatural abilities? Special spiritual experiences? No. He's talking about **spiritual truths**—the deep things of God revealed through His Spirit.

> No one can say, "Jesus is Lord," except by the Holy Spirit. (1 Corinthians 12:3)

The ultimate test of true spirituality is this: Does it confess the Lordship of Christ? Does it honor Jesus as Lord?

And the ultimate spiritual truth and power of God isn't found in spectacular gifts—it's found in **the gospel**. The cross and resurrection of Jesus Christ is where God's power is most fully revealed.

### One Spirit, Many Gifts

Paul begins with a foundational truth: we have one Spirit, but that Spirit distributes gifts differently to each believer.

> All these are the work of one and the same Spirit, and he distributes them to each one, just as he determines. (1 Corinthians 12:11)

The gifts vary widely:
- Words of wisdom and words of knowledge
- Faith, gifts of healing, miraculous powers
- Prophecy, distinguishing between spirits
- Speaking in different tongues, interpretation of tongues

But here's what matters: **each gift comes from the same Spirit working with the same purpose—to build up the body of Christ.**

This raises important theological questions about how the Spirit continues to work today. Christians hold different positions:

- **Full Continuists** believe all gifts function in the church today
- **Cessationists** believe certain gifts (like tongues and prophecy) ceased with the apostolic age
- **Partial views** hold that some gifts continue while others have ceased

Regardless of where you land on these questions, the principle remains: the Spirit gives gifts for the common good, not for personal prestige.

### Same Body, Different Parts

Paul then moves to one of Scripture's most profound metaphors: the church as the body of Christ.

> Just as a body, though one, has many parts, but its many parts form one body, so it is with Christ. (1 Corinthians 12:12)

Within this body, every part is vital. No one can say to another, "I don't belong to the body." And conversely, no part can say, "I don't need you."

**The Problem of Exclusion:**

Some members feel like outsiders. They compare their gifts to others and think, "I don't have what they have. I don't belong here."

But listen: if everyone were an eye, where would the hearing be? If everyone were an ear, where would the sense of smell be? God has arranged the body, giving greater honor to the parts that lack it, precisely so that there's no division in the body.

**The Problem of Superiority:**

Conversely, some members look at others and think, "I don't need you. Your gift isn't as impressive as mine."

This is spiritual pride dressed up in the language of giftedness. If the eye says to the hand, "I don't need you," the body is crippled. The parts that seem weaker are indispensable. The parts that seem less honorable we treat with special honor.

### The Most Important Person in This Room

Here's Kenny's pastoral twist on Paul's teaching: Who is the most important person in this gathering?

**It's you. And the person next to you. And the person behind you.**

Not the pastor. Not the person with the most impressive gift. Not the prophet or the healer or the teacher. It's every single member of Christ's body, each with something vital to contribute.

True spirituality isn't about how gifted you are. It's about recognizing that you belong, that you're needed, and that your role—whatever it is—matters in the body of Christ.

## The Gospel: The Heart of Spirituality

Throughout these chapters, Paul keeps returning to one reality: the gospel is the ultimate expression of God's power and wisdom.

> Jesus is Lord. The Spirit confesses this. The gospel proclaims this. And when the church gathers, it does so to declare this truth in love.**

True spirituality, then, isn't found in spectacular displays or impressive gifts. It's found in speaking the truth of Christ in love, in recognizing that the Spirit works through many members for the building up of the one body, and in honoring every part of Christ's body as essential.

When you gather with God's people, ask yourself: Am I seeking to build others up? Am I recognizing that this person's gift matters, even if it looks different from mine? Am I confessing that Jesus is Lord in both word and deed?

That's what it means to truly be spiritual.
