# Romans 3:1-26

**Title:** The Faithfulness of God
**Date:** 17 November 2012
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Family Precedence in God's Plan

In families, older siblings often set the precedence for what younger siblings can do. If the older child can't drive until 21, neither can the younger ones. This reflects a parent's sense of fairness—treating children according to the same standard.

Something similar happens in God's salvation history. There's an established order: the Jews first, then the Gentiles. Paul emphasizes this pattern:

> For I am not ashamed of the gospel, because it is the power of God that brings salvation to everyone who believes: **first to the Jew, then to the Gentile.** (Romans 1:16)

> There will be trouble and distress for every human being who does evil: **first for the Jew, then for the Gentile.** (Romans 2:9)

> But glory, honor and peace for everyone who does good: **first for the Jew, then for the Gentile.** (Romans 2:10)

In God's salvation plan, there is indeed an order of precedence. But what happens when the "older sibling"—Israel—becomes a disappointment?

## God's Faithfulness Despite Human Unfaithfulness

### The Core Issue

> What if some were unfaithful? Will their unfaithfulness nullify God's faithfulness? (Romans 3:3)

In Romans 2, Paul left us with a problem: The Jews, despite having God's law and covenant privilege, did not follow God. They did not receive the blessing promised to them. If good news comes first through the Jew, what happens to the Gentiles?

Paul's answer is resolute:

> Not at all! Let God be true, and every human being a liar. As it is written: "So that you may be proved right when you speak and prevail when you judge." (Romans 3:4)

### God's Character Transcends Human Failure

This quote comes from David's confession of sin in Psalm 51:

> For I know my transgressions, and my sin is always before me. Against you, you only, have I sinned and done what is evil in your sight; so you are right in your verdict and justified when you judge. (Psalm 51:3-4)

Three critical truths emerge:

- **God is faithful to all His promises.** His character doesn't depend on human performance.
- **Every human being is a liar** when compared to God's truth and righteousness.
- **Because of this divine character, God remains faithful even when humans fail.** We give up when others disappoint us. God doesn't.

God is so faithful to His promises that He will even turn human sin into goodness. Sin does not stop His mercy. David's example reveals a way of forgiveness that doesn't depend on sacrificial systems—it depends on God's faithfulness to forgive.

### The Objection: Does Goodness Justify Sin?

> But if our unrighteousness brings out God's righteousness more clearly, what shall we say? That God is unjust in bringing his wrath on us? (Romans 3:5)

A perverted argument emerges: If sin demonstrates God's righteousness and brings Him glory, shouldn't we sin? Paul emphatically rejects this:

> Certainly not! If that were so, how could God judge the world? (Romans 3:6)

> Let us do evil that good may result? Their condemnation is just! (Romans 3:8)

**The principle:** Grace does not make sin less odious. God's faithfulness to save doesn't excuse our rebellion. The Jews, with their law, did not use their advantage to teach and practice righteousness. Yet God will not let this stop His plan. In fact, God is using Israel's rejection to open salvation to the Gentiles. Good is coming from it. But this does not excuse the Jews. God is righteous, and His judgment is just.

## Universal Sinfulness Removes All Privilege

### Everyone Is Equally Sinful

> What shall we conclude then? Do we have any advantage? Not at all! For we have already made the charge that Jews and Gentiles alike are all under the power of sin. (Romans 3:9-10)

Paul now shifts the argument. The order of privilege doesn't matter if everyone is equally sinful. He quotes the Psalms:

> "There is no one righteous, not even one; there is no one who understands; there is no one who seeks God. All have turned away, they have together become worthless; there is no one who does good, not even one." (Romans 3:10-12)

The emphasis is on universality: "no one," "everyone," "all." Everyone stands on level ground before God's judgment.

### Sin Corrupts Every Part of Our Being

Paul uses vivid anthropomorphism to show how sin pervades the whole person:

> "Their throats are open graves; their tongues practice deceit." "The poison of vipers is on their lips." "Their mouths are full of cursing and bitterness." "Their feet are swift to shed blood; ruin and misery mark their ways, and the way of peace they do not know." "There is no fear of God before their eyes." (Romans 3:13-18)

Every body part represents sin: throats, tongues, lips, mouths, feet, eyes. From suppression of truth in our speech flows all manner of wickedness. **Every part of our body sins.**

Interestingly, Paul draws these quotes from the Old Testament passages that originally contrasted righteous and unrighteous people. By weaving them together, Paul shows that both Jews and Gentiles are equally condemned.

### The Law Silences All Excuses

> Now we know that whatever the law says, it says to those who are under the law, so that every mouth may be silenced and the whole world held accountable to God. Therefore no one will be declared righteous in God's sight by the works of the law; rather, through the law we become conscious of our sin. (Romans 3:19-20)

When everyone acts out and fights each other, **no one gets anything.** The initial order of privilege no longer applies.

### A Practical Contrast

Consider how we view inequality in our world today. We often assume there was a pie to distribute, and someone stole from others. Therefore, the solution is government redistribution—socialism, communism.

But Scripture reveals a deeper truth: **there is no pie at all.** We're all ruined. The real question isn't "Who stole what?" but rather "How did any good even get made?" The surprise is that there's any goodness at all in a world of universal sinners.

Most worldviews assume people are naturally good, and then some become evil. But Scripture flips this: **Everyone is evil. The real question is, where does any goodness come from?** This reframes everything.

## God's Provision: The Faithful One

### Righteousness Apart from the Law

> But now apart from the law the righteousness of God has been made known, to which the Law and the Prophets testify. This righteousness is given through faith in Jesus Christ to all who believe. There is no difference between Jew and Gentile, for all have sinned and fall short of the glory of God, and all are justified freely by his grace through the redemption that came by Christ Jesus. (Romans 3:21-24)

This "manifestation" mirrors Romans 1:19—just as God's law was manifested to Adam and Israel, now the righteousness of God is made manifest in Jesus. It's a real-life revelation, leaving no excuse. We now, like Adam and Israel, know God directly.

### "Apart from the Law"

This phrase is critical. Medieval theology misunderstood it as meaning "beyond the law"—you must go above and beyond the syllabus to earn righteousness. This offers no hope.

But the correct meaning is different: **Jesus takes the place of the law.** It's not about achieving 99.95% by going above the requirements; it's about escaping the entire testing system and entering a new covenant. Out of the sea of wickedness, righteousness is now made known.

### The Faithfulness of Jesus

Romans 3:22 contains an important ambiguity: "through faith in Jesus Christ" could mean:

1. Faith *in* Jesus (our belief toward Him)
2. Faith *of* Jesus (His faithfulness toward us)

Both are true, but Paul emphasizes the second: **It is the faithfulness of Jesus who saves us.** Jesus is the faithful one.

The Jews were supposed to be God's people, leading the way to righteousness. But they failed. In their absence, God provides the True Jew—one who teaches the true way, who doesn't blaspheme God to the Gentiles. Jesus was the faithful Jew. He obeyed God in both deed and heart.

### The Mercy Seat

> God presented Christ as a sacrifice of atonement, through the shedding of his blood—to be received by faith. He did this to demonstrate his righteousness, because in his forbearance he had left the sins committed beforehand unpunished. He did it to demonstrate his righteousness at the present time, so as to be just and the one who justifies those who have faith in Jesus. (Romans 3:25-26)

The "mercy seat" refers to the Old Testament temple system: the Most Holy Place, the ark of the covenant, where God sat. For centuries, the Jews performed sacrifices and sprinkled blood on the mercy seat. Yet sin continued and worsened.

Now God presents Christ as the mercy seat. The sacrifice of Jesus demonstrates God's righteousness in two ways:

- **He shows justice:** His forbearance in the past (leaving old sins unpunished) is now vindicated. He can judge the world fairly.
- **He shows mercy:** He justifies those who have faith in Jesus, freely by His grace.

### Faith Excludes Boasting

> Where, then, is boasting? It is excluded. Because of what law? The law that requires works? No, because of the law of faith. For we maintain that a person is justified by faith apart from the works of the law. (Romans 3:27-28)

Since justification comes through faith, not works, there's nothing to boast about. You can't earn it. You can only receive it.

### One God, One Faith

> Or is God the God of Jews only? Is he not the God of Gentiles too? Yes, of Gentiles too, since there is only one God, who will justify the circumcised by faith and the uncircumcised through that same faith. Do we, then, nullify the law by this faith? Not at all! Rather, we uphold the law. (Romans 3:29-31)

The order has shifted. It's no longer "Jew first, then Gentile." Now it's simply one God justifying all by the same faith—both those from covenant background and those from outside it.

## Application

### For the Non-Christian

If you believe that we are equally sinful before God, that we are all liars compared to His truth, that we cannot go above and beyond the law to save ourselves—then act on this conviction.

If you are not a Christian, it's time to go to God and plead for His mercy. God will answer because Jesus has died for you. Do this today.

### Our Misguided Solutions

We think the answer to sin is more laws: more tax legislation, more regulations, more elections. This will fix our problem, we believe.

When God fixed the world, He didn't send more laws. **He sent a person—Jesus.** He became the sacrifice for our sins and provides righteousness apart from the law for us.

### The Path Forward

Instead of relying on external status—church attendance, theological correctness, religious family background—we need something far deeper. Like the Jews, we may rely on good works or our group identity. But none of these will save us.

The solution is a circumcision of the heart—a change so deep and fundamental that it transforms your very essence. This is the work of the Holy Spirit, not the letter of law.

**Let's follow our older brother, Jesus.** He is the faithful one who fulfills what we cannot, who justifies us by His grace, who transforms us from within.
