# Acts 6:1-7:60

**Title:** God With Us

**Date:** December 15, 2013

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## The Forgotten Widows

In those days, the Jerusalem church was growing—disciples multiplying as believers proclaimed Jesus and lived out their faith. But growth brought an unexpected problem.

> In those days when the number of disciples was increasing, the Grecian Jews among them complained against the Hebraic Jews because their widows were being overlooked in the daily distribution of food. (Acts 6:1)

The widows being neglected were among the most vulnerable in society. Widows—women whose husbands had died—faced complete destitution. In a culture where men were the breadwinners, they had no economic support, no social standing, no safety net. They were dependent on the community's charity for basic food.

The problem was compounded by culture and language. The "Grecian Jews" were Greek-speaking Jews from outside Jerusalem, people for whom Greek had been the primary language since Alexander the Great. They were newer arrivals to the Jerusalem church, different from the Hebrew-speaking, culturally rooted Jewish community. Language barriers and cultural outsider status likely made these widows easy to overlook—tragically, food distribution bypassed them entirely.

This was precisely the kind of neglect Jesus condemned. He warned against religious leaders who "devour widows' houses and for a show make lengthy prayers" (Luke 20:46-47). By contrast, God has always shown special love for widows. When Dorcas (Tabitha) died, "all the widows stood around [Peter], crying and showing him the robes and other clothing that Dorcas had made while she was still with them" (Acts 9:39). Dorcas had given her life to serving widows, and Peter raised her from the dead—a powerful statement about God's heart for those who care for the vulnerable.

## The Solution: Faithful Servants

Recognizing the problem, the twelve apostles called the whole church together with a wise proposal:

> "It would not be right for us to neglect the ministry of the word of God in order to wait on tables. Brothers, choose seven men from among you who are known to be full of the Spirit and wisdom. We will turn this responsibility over to them and will give our attention to prayer and the ministry of the word." (Acts 6:2-4)

The apostles weren't rejecting service—they were recognizing their primary calling and trusting others with theirs. They selected seven men, all with Greek names, to serve the Greek-speaking widows. This was intentional: cultural and linguistic match mattered for genuine care.

The church chose Stephen, Philip, Procorus, Nicanor, Timon, Parmenas, and Nicolas from Antioch. Notice what qualified them: they were "known to be full of the Spirit and wisdom" (Acts 6:3). The apostles prayed and laid hands on them, conferring their blessing and authority.

What does it mean to be "full of the Spirit"? In Acts, this phrase describes people whose character reflects God's character. Stephen is described as "full of faith and of the Holy Spirit" (Acts 6:5)—not just someone filled with dramatic spiritual power, but someone characterized by godliness.

The apostle Paul captured this when he wrote: "Do not get drunk on wine, which leads to debauchery. Instead, be filled with the Spirit" (Ephesians 5:18). The contrast is telling—being filled with the Spirit means living with self-control, not excess. It means embodying "love, joy, peace, patience, kindness, goodness, faithfulness, gentleness and self-control" (Galatians 5:22-23). This is the fruit of the Spirit—a way of life characterized by godliness.

Luke describes many such people this way: Barnabas was "a good man and full of the Holy Spirit and faith" (Acts 11:24). The disciples were "filled with joy and the Holy Spirit" (Acts 13:52). Even Dorcas was described as someone "always doing good and helping the poor" (Acts 9:36)—full of good works.

This quality can be used negatively too. Paul rebuked Elymas, saying: "You are full of all kinds of deceit and trickery" (Acts 13:10). The term describes a way of life, not a moment or feeling.

### What Spirit-Filled Leadership Looks Like

True Spirit-filled people are not those who perform piety loudly in public—who "stand in the middle of the street with eyes tightly shut, jumping up and down emotionally." Spirit-filled people are godly when they get home. They are honest on their tax returns. They treat their families with respect. They serve without counting the cost.

The apostles called these seven men to "wait on tables"—a phrase that captures something beautiful about Christian character. Good waiters exemplify this attitude:

- They are there when you arrive and present until you leave
- Their first answer to every request is "yes"—their heart is simply to serve
- They notice needs without being asked
- They don't measure themselves by their gifts or preferences; they see what's needed and do it

Consider the Nordstrom department store, famous for customer service. A customer walked in with two snow tires and asked for a refund. The clerk, working there only two weeks, saw the price on the tires, opened the cash register, and handed the man his money back. The catch? Nordstrom doesn't sell snow tires. They sell shoes, dresses, jewelry, cosmetics. The clerk said yes first and sorted it out later—because their heart was to serve.

This is what leaders embody. Great leaders sweep the floor. They buy the cutlery. They clean the toilets before church starts and after it ends. If someone is too good for the toilets, they're not good enough to lead. With training, such people—humble, willing, character-driven—become excellent leaders.

And remarkably, this attitude of service became foundational to Stephen's later ministry of the word.

> So the word of God spread. The number of disciples in Jerusalem increased rapidly, and a large number of priests became obedient to the faith. (Acts 6:7)

## Stephen's Witness

Soon Stephen began to do "great wonders and miraculous signs among the people" (Acts 6:8). Opposition arose from various synagogues—Freedmen, Cyrenians, Alexandrians, and people from Cilicia and Asia. They debated with Stephen, but "they could not stand up against his wisdom or the Spirit by whom he spoke" (Acts 6:10).

Unable to defeat him openly, they resorted to deception. They secretly persuaded men to testify falsely: "We have heard Stephen speak words of blasphemy against Moses and against God" (Acts 6:11). The accusations were twofold: Stephen spoke against (1) the holy place and (2) the law. They claimed he said "this Jesus of Nazareth will destroy this place and change the customs Moses handed down to us" (Acts 6:14).

Stephen's response—his defense before the Sanhedrin—addresses both accusations by turning them back on his accusers. He shows that the Jews themselves have consistently rejected God's representatives and God's word.

---

## The Temple: God's Presence Cannot Be Contained

The accusation claimed Stephen didn't recognize the temple's holiness. But Stephen takes his listeners through Israel's history, showing that God's presence was never confined to one building.

God appeared to Abraham "while he was still in Mesopotamia, before he lived in Haran" (Acts 7:2-3)—far from any temple. Abraham received no inheritance of land, yet God's glory appeared to him.

God was with Joseph in Egypt, blessing him with wisdom so he gained Pharaoh's favor. Israel grew in Egypt, experienced God's deliverance, and "was enslaved and mistreated four hundred years" before being freed to "worship me in this place" (Acts 7:6-7).

God spoke to Moses in the wilderness, in the flames of a burning bush at Mount Sinai: "Take off your sandals; the place where you are standing is holy ground" (Acts 7:33). Moses stood on holy ground not in a temple, but in a desert.

For most of Israel's history, God's presence dwelt in a portable tent—the tabernacle. Only centuries later did Solomon build the temple. But even then, Stephen reminds them:

> "However, the Most High does not live in houses made by men. As the prophet says: 'Heaven is my throne, and the earth is my footstool. What kind of house will you build for me? says the Lord. Or where will my resting place be? Has not my hand made all these things?'" (Acts 7:48-50)

God cannot be contained. You cannot contain God any more than you can contain the wind, stars, or sunshine. The entire heavens and earth are His dwelling place.

Yet Israel had made exactly this mistake. When Moses delayed on Mount Sinai, the people demanded: "Make us gods who will go before us" (Acts 7:40). They made a golden calf and "brought sacrifices to it and held a celebration in honor of what their hands had made" (Acts 7:41). In doing so, they repeated the fundamental error: trying to make God, to control God, to possess God through objects made by human hands.

By Stephen's day, the temple had become an idol—exactly what the people had warned against. The prophet Ezekiel had witnessed God's glory departing from the temple because of detestable idolatries happening within its walls. For 500 years, the temple stood essentially empty—untouched, like a mathematics textbook gathering dust over summer holidays. It didn't matter that they rebuilt it after exile, that Herod had recently renovated it. God was not there.

Until Jesus came.

> The Word became flesh and made his dwelling among us. We have seen his glory, the glory of the One and Only, who came from the Father, full of grace and truth. (John 1:14)

Jesus is the temple. He is the place where God's presence dwells. When Jesus rose and ascended to heaven, He didn't abandon us. Through His Spirit, Jesus indwells each believer: "Do you not know that your bodies are temples of the Holy Spirit, who is in you?" (1 Corinthians 6:19). By His Spirit and His word, we can commune with Him through prayer, through Scripture, through fellowship with one another.

This is the heart of Christmas—God coming to hang out with us, to be present with us, to dwell among us. And though Jesus has returned to heaven, we remain connected through His word, His Spirit, and our prayers. We experience His presence through fellowship with each other, for by His Spirit, Jesus lives in each of us.

## The Prophets and the Law: A Pattern of Rejection

Stephen's second accusation addressed the law and Moses. But again, he turns the tables: You accuse me of persecuting the law? Your own history proves you are the ones who reject God's representatives.

From the beginning, Israel rejected those God sent. The patriarchs were jealous of Joseph and sold him into slavery. Yet "God was with him and rescued him from all his troubles. He gave Joseph wisdom and enabled him to gain the goodwill of Pharaoh king of Egypt" (Acts 7:9-10). Joseph became ruler, but Israel didn't recognize God's hand in him.

Even more strikingly, Israel rejected Moses himself—the very lawgiver they claimed to revere. Moses was educated in all Egyptian wisdom and "was powerful in speech and action" (Acts 7:22). When he tried to help his fellow Israelites, they rejected him: "Who made you ruler and judge over us?" (Acts 7:27). Persecuted, Moses fled to Midian.

Yet God sent Moses back with authority:

> "This is the same Moses whom they had rejected with the words, 'Who made you ruler and judge?' He was sent to be their ruler and deliverer by God himself, through the angel who appeared to him in the bush. He led them out of Egypt and did wonders and miraculous signs in Egypt, at the Red Sea and for forty years in the desert." (Acts 7:35-36)

And Moses himself predicted another would come: "God will send you a prophet like me from your own people" (Acts 7:37). God gave the living words through Moses—instructions to receive the coming Messiah well.

But the pattern repeated:

> "But our fathers refused to obey him. Instead, they rejected him and in their hearts turned back to Egypt." (Acts 7:39)

Israel persecuted Joseph. Israel persecuted Moses. They persecuted Elijah, Amos, Jeremiah, and John the Baptist. The prophets—God's messengers—faced beatings, imprisonment, death.

And when the Messiah came—the one Moses predicted—they killed Him too.

Stephen's conclusion is devastating: "You stiff-necked people, with uncircumcised hearts and ears! You are just like your fathers: You always resist the Holy Spirit! Was there ever a prophet your fathers did not persecute? You even killed those who predicted the coming of the Righteous One. And now you have betrayed and murdered him—you who have received the law that was put into effect through angels but have not obeyed it" (Acts 7:51-53).

The irony is piercing. These accusers claim to defend Moses and the law, yet they have done precisely what Moses warned against: they rejected the Prophet like Moses, the very one the law pointed toward.

## The Face of Authority

When the Sanhedrin looked intently at Stephen before his testimony, "they saw that his face was like the face of an angel" (Acts 6:15). This isn't about innocent appearance or childlike innocence. Throughout Acts, when angels appear, they come with divine authority. The law was "put into effect through angels" (Acts 7:53)—heavenly messengers representing God's authority.

Stephen's presence radiated that same authority. Despite seeing this—despite recognizing the presence of divine authority in him—they refused to listen. Instead, they "covered their ears and, yelling at the top of their voices, they all rushed at him, dragged him out of the city and began to stone him" (Acts 7:57-58).

They treated him exactly as they had treated Joseph, Moses, and the prophets before him. The pattern held true to the end.

---

## The Grace of Martyrdom

But in Stephen's final moments, something extraordinary happened:

> But Stephen, full of the Holy Spirit, looked up to heaven and saw the glory of God, and Jesus standing at the right hand of God. "Look," he said, "I see heaven open and the Son of Man standing at the right hand of God." (Acts 7:55-56)

While his murderers raged, Stephen saw the very presence of God—heaven opened, the glory of God, Jesus standing. And in that vision, with stones raining down, Stephen prayed: "Lord Jesus, receive my spirit" (Acts 7:59). Then, falling to his knees as he died, he cried out: "Lord, do not hold this sin against them" (Acts 7:60).

His final words echoed Jesus on the cross: "Father, forgive them, for they do not know what they are doing." Stephen died forgiving his murderers, his gaze fixed on Jesus.

---

## The Message for Us

Today, a Christian is being killed every eleven minutes in more than 130 countries around the world. Persecution continues. The pattern Stephen described—rejection of God's messengers—repeats in our own time.

Yet Stephen's vision remains true for us as well. We are never alone. Even when the world rises against God's people, even when darkness threatens, Jesus stands at the right hand of God—alive, present, interceding for us. Through His word and Spirit, He is with us, just as He was with Stephen.

Our calling, like Stephen's, is to be full of the Spirit—full of the character of Christ, willing to serve, faithful in witness, and trusting in God's ultimate presence and victory.
