# 1 Peter 3:8–18
**Title:** Alien Confidence
**Date:** January 2012
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Living Differently in a Watching World

### Do Good (v. 11)

Peter begins with a clear instruction: "He must turn from evil and do good; he must seek peace and pursue it." But what does it mean to do good in practice?

The passage shows us in verse 8. We are to:
- Live in harmony with one another
- Be sympathetic
- Love as brothers
- Be compassionate
- And humble

In essence, this means looking out for each other and loving your neighbor as yourself. Critically, this applies even to those outside the faith—our non-Christian and pagan neighbors. In 1 Peter 2:12, Peter writes that we should "live such good lives among the pagans that, though they accuse you of doing wrong, they may see your good deeds and glorify God."

This seems strange at first. Pagans are outside the family, outside our immediate loyalties. We owe them nothing. Yet Peter calls us to look out for even them.

**Practical application:** Start with your literal neighbors. Learn their names. Take in their bins. Offer to watch their home when they're away. Invite them over. Small gestures like sharing fruit or flowers at holidays can renew these relationships.

### Repay Good for Evil (v. 9)

Christians are also called to do good even when others have done us harm. Verse 9 is striking: "Do not repay evil with evil or insult with insult, but with blessing, because to this you were called so that you may inherit a blessing."

The word "blessing" here—*eulogia* in Greek—literally means "a good word." It's about speaking well of others, expressing praise, not the typical sense of happiness (*makarios*). This ties directly to verse 10, where we're told: "Whoever would love life and see good days must keep his tongue from evil and his lips from deceitful speech."

Our speech must be different. Instead of repaying insults with insults, we repay with good words.

**The Problem:** We live in an age without censorship or restraint. Social media and the internet encourage us to say whatever we want without filters. We've absorbed a culture that views all restraint as oppression. But we are called to something higher—to bless rather than curse.

**On the road:** When a driver behind you honks aggressively or swears, your instinct is to respond in kind. But return with a gentle word instead. Perhaps they've had a terrible day. Proverbs 15:1 reminds us: "A gentle answer turns away wrath, but a harsh word stirs up anger."

**On the internet:** The anonymity of the internet allows deeply offensive comments to flourish. Some advocate for editors or censors to control this, but there's a better way: self-censorship. We must discipline ourselves to swap a good word for an evil word, a good word for an insulting word.

## Why Do Good? Six Reasons

### 1. It's Your Calling (v. 9)

We do good because it's who we are. Verse 9 says: "because to this you were called so that you may inherit a blessing." We were called by the gospel message itself. We have inherited a blessing—a good word from God. Chapter 1 reminds us of our great inheritance stored up in heaven. Having received a good word from God, we should likewise repay others with good words.

### 2. To See Good Days (v. 10)

We do good "in order to see good days." This isn't necessarily about eternal life—it's about this life here and now. Practically speaking, it's easier to live in peace than to be at war. Stress levels decrease. Good neighbors become treasured relationships.

### 3. God Is Looking Out for You (v. 12)

"For the eyes of the Lord are on the righteous and his ears are attentive to their prayer."

God is not distant. His eyes on the righteous means He's watching out for you—not to punish, but to protect and help. Like a mother watching over her baby, His oversight is for your good. Even if the outcome seems bad, He will swiftly take care of you. And it's not just His eyes—His ears are attentive to your prayers.

**The hardest thing about doing good:** Doing it quietly, with no one knowing, no one acknowledging, and sometimes even being punished for it. But God says: I know. When you don't gossip back at someone who spoke badly of you, when you look out for their best interest even as they cost you marks and satisfaction—God is watching. His eyes are on you. His ears are listening. When you feel discouraged, call out. Your Father sees and knows all.

### 4. God Is Judge (v. 12)

A more sobering reason: God is the ultimate judge. While His face is on the righteous, "the face of the Lord is against those who do evil." The "face of God" symbolizes His presence and often His judgment—as when Adam and Eve hid from God's face after their sin.

Judgment belongs to God alone, not to us. The way of the world is "an eye for an eye," but that principle in the Old Testament prescribed the *maximum* penalty, not the minimum. We are called to something different: to do good to those who don't deserve it, to do good to our enemies.

We love stories of revenge—the Count of Monte Cristo, TV shows about vigilante justice. But that's not our calling. Instead of revenge, we offer love. Instead of justice, we offer forgiveness. Where revenge escalates conflict, Christians stop the cycle. "The buck stops with me," you might say. Don't pass forward evil—pass on good.

### 5. Don't Fear What the World Fears (v. 14)

Verse 14 says: "But even if you should suffer for what is right, you are blessed. 'Do not fear what they fear; do not be frightened.'"

The world fears suffering for doing good. But Christians don't belong to this world or its fears. We're not frightened by earthly consequences because we know them to be temporary. Heavenly consequences are eternal.

Fear doesn't make us paralyzed—it redirects us. We're called to fear God, not man. We fear God (showing reverent awe), but we don't fear the world's disapproval.

**The deepest fear:** Often it's not physical danger but fear of missing out (FOMO). We fear that doing good will cost us opportunities—the one-off event instead of faithful attendance, generosity when we could save for ourselves, showing up for a friend when we'd rather be somewhere else. But FOMO stops us from doing right.

### 6. A Unique Opportunity for Witness (v. 15–16)

Here's the surprising silver lining: "But in your hearts set apart Christ as Lord. Always be prepared to give an answer to everyone who asks you to give the reason for the hope that you have. But do this with gentleness and respect, keeping a clear conscience, so that those who speak maliciously against your good behavior in Christ may be ashamed of their slander."

When you continue to do good despite no earthly reward, despite persecution, people will eventually notice. One day—maybe years away—someone will see your consistent life and ask, "Why do you live this way?" That's your unique opportunity to share about your Father in heaven who hears you and watches over you.

They will believe you because you've *lived it*. You're different from other Christians who talk more than they do. You actually do good with no expectation of earthly reward or respect. You're doing what's right because of God, not convenience.

**Imagine:** A friend comes to you wanting to gossip about a mutual enemy. You gently decline, explaining it's not right to gossip even about someone who's hurt you. You show sympathy, love, compassion, and humility. Who will that friend trust next time? Who will she come to with serious questions about life?

**For non-Christian parents:** You tell your parents you're a Christian, that they should believe in Jesus. They might dismiss you: "I taught you how to live; why should you teach me?" But then they watch you. You become the obedient child who keeps his word, the responsible one, the one who does good for the family with no reward. You wash dishes without being asked. You make peace at home. You sacrificially love your siblings. You bring harmony through humility and compassion, becoming a listening ear.

A unique opportunity will come. When it does, stay consistent. Don't switch to an arrogant mode that contradicts everything you've lived. Have something ready to share—perhaps 2 Corinthians 5:17, your testimony, or the very truths from 1 Peter 3.

## Jesus: The Ultimate Example (v. 18)

The final and deepest reason not to fear is Jesus Himself:

"For Christ died for sins once for all, the righteous for the unrighteous, to bring you to God. He was put to death in the body but made alive by the Spirit."

Jesus lived everything He asks of us. He lived in harmony with others. He was sympathetic, loving, compassionate, and humble. He never traded insult for insult. He went silently to His death, giving up His rights, His body, His last resort for revenge, His reward and recognition—all for the sake of doing good.

And what was He repaid with? Ridicule, insults, betrayal, disrespect, torture, death.

Yet He trusted His Father. He sacrificially loved the unrighteous. He suffered for doing good.

**And then what happened?** God saw. He exalted Jesus. The resurrection proved God's vindication. Jesus broke the cycle of hate, violence, and revenge. His act, once for all, shows us the power of love and brings us to God. He's authorized to say "Don't fear" because He walked the path first—and was glorified.

## The Calling: Alien Confidence

As you go about your week—school, work, daily life—remember this. The world is watching whether we truly believe what we say. Our consistent, quiet goodness, even in the face of ingratitude or persecution, is a powerful witness.

Do not fear to do good. Do not fear to suffer for what is right. God's eyes are on you. A unique opportunity awaits. And you follow in the footsteps of Christ Himself, who conquered death and now sits at God's right hand with all authorities in submission to Him.

This is the confidence of those who are aliens in this world—confidence rooted not in earthly security, but in the God who watches, the Christ who conquered, and the Spirit who sustains.
