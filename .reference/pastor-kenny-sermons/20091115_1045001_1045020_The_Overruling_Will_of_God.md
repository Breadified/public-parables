# Genesis 45:1–20

**Title:** The Overruling Will of God

**Date:** November 15, 2009

## Introduction: Can God's Plans Be Ruined?

We begin our lives with plans—students planning study schedules that get disrupted, travelers whose visa applications are denied, workers whose retirements are derailed by financial crises, parents hoping for sleep but getting awakened by two-year-olds, couples planning dream weddings only to face unseasonable heat. Life teaches us that our plans are fragile things.

But this raises a deeper question: Can God's plans be ruined? Specifically, can sin ruin God's plans to bless the world?

Genesis opens with God creating a very good world. Yet Adam and Eve did a foolish and sinful thing—they ignored God's one warning and brought the world into sin. Does sin thwart God's plan to have a place of goodness? Now, as we come to the end of Genesis, we must ask: Who rules? Will Genesis end with sin having the last word over God's plans?

Last week we looked at chapter 41, which showed Joseph's remarkable rise to second-in-command in Pharaoh's court. Now in chapter 45, Joseph's story comes full circle with a reunion with his brothers and father. In the first part of this teaching, we will see how in every event and situation, God is firmly in control. In the second part, we will explore what this means for us.

## 1. God Overrules Sin by Bringing Good Out of Evil

### Joseph's Testimony to God's Sovereignty

One of the most striking features of the Joseph narrative is Joseph's consistent testimony to God's control. Joseph is remarkably keen to attribute everything to God.

When interpreting Pharaoh's dream, Joseph says:

> I cannot do it, but God will give Pharaoh the answer he desires. (Genesis 41:15–16)

Later he declares:

> God has revealed to Pharaoh what he is about to do. (Genesis 41:25)

Joseph even names his children as declarations of God's sovereignty. His firstborn he names Manasseh ("God has made me forget all my trouble"), and his second son Ephraim ("God has made me fruitful in the land of my suffering").

Most significantly, when Joseph reveals himself to his brothers, he says:

> Do not be distressed and do not be angry with yourselves for selling me here, because it was to save lives that God sent me ahead of you. For two years now there has been famine in the land, and for the next five years there will not be plowing and reaping. But God sent me ahead of you to preserve for you a remnant on earth and to save your lives by a great deliverance. (Genesis 45:5–7)

Then, most powerfully:

> It was not you who sent me here, but God. He made me father to Pharaoh, lord of his entire household and ruler of all Egypt. (Genesis 45:8)

### God Controls Natural Events Through His Sovereignty

It's relatively easy for Christians to believe that God controls natural events—the weather, harvests, famines. We pray that exams will contain the questions we studied, acknowledging God's hand in what seems natural or uncontrollable.

### God Controls Human Free Will and Sin

But what about man-made events? What about the greed that causes financial crises, the overdevelopment that causes mudslides, the pollution that causes global warming? What about the free will of sinful people? Does God control the decisions of sinful men and women?

Here lies the remarkable truth: God overrules not just natural events but also the free-willed actions of sinful people. Joseph's brothers willfully captured him, tied him up, put him in a hole, then sold him into slavery for silver. Yet God, in His sovereignty, used their sin for His purposes. Sin does not disrupt God's plans—God uses it to accomplish them.

### The Pattern Throughout Genesis

This phenomenon of bringing good out of evil is not restricted to Joseph alone. It applies to all the key figures in Genesis, a narrative filled with deceivers:

- **Adam and Eve** tried to deceive God ("It wasn't me")
- **Abraham** deceived the Egyptians by pretending Sarah was his sister
- **Jacob** (whose name means "deceiver") deceived Isaac to steal Esau's birthright
- **Jacob's ten sons** deceived their father with goats' blood on Joseph's coat

Yet God brought good out of all these sinful actions, ultimately preserving a people and fulfilling His redemptive purposes.

### Joseph's Test of His Brothers

Even Joseph himself used deception. He tested his brothers—playing the role of an Egyptian official, accusing them of being spies, demanding they bring Benjamin, planting his cup in Benjamin's sack. While the method was wrong, the result was profound: his brothers revealed their true character.

Through years of living with guilt, through famine, through Joseph's deliberate testing, they repented. Listen to their own confession:

> Surely we are being punished because of our brother. We saw how distressed he was when he pleaded with us for his life, but we would not listen. (Genesis 42:21)

The tests brought about transformation. Through their ordeal, Joseph's brothers showed themselves changed:

- They trusted each other and did not accuse one another
- They did not abandon Benjamin
- They humbled themselves for Benjamin's sake
- They offered to become slaves themselves
- Judah offered himself as a substitutionary sacrifice to save Benjamin

God was using events—difficult, painful events—to accomplish repentance and transformation in their hearts.

### Application: Repentance Through Life's Difficulties

When we encounter difficult events in our lives, we should not disdain God for them. Rather, we should recognize that He uses them to bring about repentance.

Whatever your past is, whatever guilt you're living with, God has brought you to a point of repentance. As we reflect on the events of our weeks, months, and years, we must ask ourselves:

- Who are the people we have used for our own advantage?
- Who have we made fun of?
- What lies have we told?
- Who in need have we ignored?
- What did we do in secret that we knew was wrong?

We cannot escape the fact that no matter how holy we try to present ourselves, there is a cancer called sin buried inside us. We present ourselves as good and righteous while living in fear that someone will discover the truth. Yet we are wicked people who have done horrible things, no matter how skillfully we hide them.

What we need is to be forgiven. God wants you to change. He calls you to trust Him and follow Him.

### The Ultimate Example: The Cross

The ultimate example of God working through the evil actions of sinful people is the cross itself. Peter speaks of this mystery:

> This man was handed over to you by God's set purpose and foreknowledge; and you, with the help of wicked men, put him to death by nailing him to the cross. But God raised him from the dead. (Acts 2:23–24)

Our guilt, our sin, put Jesus on the cross. Yet God used the evil intentions of wicked people to accomplish our redemption. This is the pinnacle of God overruling sin to bring about good.

## 2. An Important Qualification: Not a License to Sin

Here we must be very careful. Romans 6 poses the critical question:

> What shall we say, then? Shall we go on sinning so that grace may increase? By no means! We died to sin; how can we live in it any longer? (Romans 6:1–2)

If God can bring so much good out of so much evil and deception, we might be tempted to think: "Let me do evil so that God can work mightily through it."

That would be a grave mistake.

### The Hindsight Principle

Joseph and the author of Genesis both have the benefit of hindsight. Joseph looks back at his life and, though filled with evil acts against him, sees how God has used it for his and his people's good. The author of Genesis looks back at Israel's history and sees, despite the nation's founders all being liars and deceivers, how God overruled their sin to bring about His good purposes.

We, too, may look back at our lives and see how God has used even our mistakes and the sins committed against us to make us better people. I can personally testify to this—past relationships that were sinful and dysfunctional taught me and awakened me to repentance in ways that shaped the man I am today. In hindsight, I can see God's hand working good.

Yet this does not mean looking forward that we should make those mistakes.

### The Cart Before the Horse

Some argue, for example, that since God has blessed interfaith marriages where a non-believer has eventually come to faith, it must be acceptable to marry a non-believer. But this argument puts the cart before the horse. This argument only works in hindsight. Yes, as we look back at our mistakes, we can see God overruling our silly decisions and bringing grace into them. He may graciously save us from the consequences of our sinful actions.

But that does not mean, looking forward now, that we should make that mistake. That would be sinning so that grace might abound.

### The Exception Is Not the Rule

Consider the story of Louise Sauvage, an Australian Paralympic and Olympic gold medalist. Born with severe spinal dysplasia, she endured over twenty operations before age ten. Yet she persevered and became one of the world's greatest wheelchair athletes.

Now imagine someone saying: "Louise Sauvage became a champion athlete. Therefore, let me break my legs so I can become a champion too."

That's absurd. We don't look to exceptions and use them as role models for our own lives.

The same principle applies to sin. Just because:

- Someone overcame drug and alcohol addiction to become successful
- Someone entered into an unbiblical relationship that worked out
- Someone was uncommitted to church for a year but became a godly pastor

...does not mean we should replicate those paths. God overruled sin in those cases, showing mercy and delivering people from consequences. But that doesn't mean we should do more sin or recommend it to others. That's mistaking God's grace for His permission.

## 3. In Hindsight, the Perfect Plan

When we understand God's sovereignty through the lens of the entire story—especially through Jesus—everything makes sense.

The disciples didn't understand what was happening when Jesus was arrested and crucified. They asked, "Where are you going? What's going on?" The prophets didn't understand why the Messiah would have to suffer and die. How could allowing the authorities to nail Jesus to the cross possibly be part of God's plan?

But with hindsight—with the resurrection and what followed—everything becomes clear. The cross makes complete sense.

Yet we must remember: sending Jesus to the cross was still wrong. We should not have done that. God's sovereignty does not mean we abandon doing the right thing.

### Illustration: The Castaway

A man is the only survivor of a shipwreck, washed up on a small uninhabited island. He cries out to God to save him and scans the horizon daily for help, but none comes. Exhausted, he manages to build a rough hut and places his remaining possessions inside it.

One day, after hunting for food, he returns to find his hut in flames. He is devastated with grief. But early the next morning, a ship draws near the island—and rescues him.

"How did you know we were here?" asks the captain.

"We saw your smoke signal," replies the captain.

The man didn't have all the pieces of the jigsaw puzzle. He couldn't see how the loss of his hut would lead to his rescue. But God could. And God used what appeared to be disaster for deliverance.

---

## Conclusion

God ultimately overrules sin—its operations and its consequences. He brings good out of evil. He uses natural events and human decisions, abundance and famine, suffering and joy, for His ultimate purposes.

When difficult events come your way, don't ask, "Why is God punishing me?" Instead, ask, "What is God calling me to repent of? How is He refining me?" Look back with hindsight and trust that He is weaving your story—even its painful chapters—into something good.

But looking forward, don't use His sovereignty as an excuse to sin. Trust Him. Follow Him. Repent. And believe that, like Joseph, like the disciples, like that castaway, you will one day see the full picture of God's perfect plan for your life.
