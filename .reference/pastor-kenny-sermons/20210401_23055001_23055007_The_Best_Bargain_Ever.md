# Isaiah 55:1-7

**Title:** The Best Bargain Ever  
**Date:** 17 April 2021  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Invitation: Free Food

> Come, all you who are thirsty, come to the waters; and you who have no money, come, buy and eat! Come, buy wine and milk without money and without cost. (Isaiah 55:1)

Isaiah addresses a people ruined by successive invaders—the thirsty and hungry. His offer is radical: come to the waters, buy wine and milk without money or cost. This is free food.

In ancient times, water was precious. The Middle East was dry and drought-gripped. There were no swimming pools, no ice cubes in drinks, no bathing readily available. People had no running water, no indoor plumbing. Water was harder to find than it is for us. When Isaiah offers water—and not just water, but wine and milk—he's offering something deeply valuable. Free.

To people worn down and starving, he says: come. Feast on the finest liquids at no cost. It's the bargain of a lifetime.

## The Problem: Worst Bargains

> Why spend money on what is not bread, and your labor on what does not satisfy? (Isaiah 55:2)

But now that we know real food exists for free, it's a double rip-off to waste money and energy on what doesn't satisfy. Have you ever paid full price for something broken? Ordered from a fancy restaurant and found tiny portions at double the price? Paid and walked away still hungry?

We've all experienced it: *paid and still not satisfied*.

## The Real Hunger: The Soul

> Listen, listen to me, and eat what is good, and your soul will delight in the richest of fare. Give ear and come to me; hear me, that your soul may live. (Isaiah 55:2b-3a)

Wait—Isaiah isn't actually talking about food. He's talking about the soul. That part of you that has moments of thoughtfulness and reflection. Your inner life.

Just as the body needs food, the soul needs food. And this hunger cannot be satisfied by physical comfort alone. We know this intuitively—we want something more than the daily grind. We know this empirically too.

Australia has never been wealthier, healthier, or more educated. Yet happiness eludes us. Every indicator has climbed—except one. Since the 1950s, people reporting unhappiness has risen 20%. Depression rates have increased tenfold—so common we even named the emotional state "emo." Suicide rates are up. Family breakdown is up.

We're among the world's richest nations (only Switzerland and Scandinavia beat us), yet we're unhappy. Not happy Jan.

Something is deeply wrong.

## The Promise: A Greater King

Into this spiritual hunger, Isaiah brings a promise from God:

> I will make an everlasting covenant with you, my faithful love promised to David. (Isaiah 55:3b)

God promises a covenant like the one made to David, the shepherd boy chosen to be king. David presided over a prosperous land flowing with milk and honey—water, wine, milk, oranges, pomegranates, bread, cheese. Peace and security.

But David is long dead. All the kings of Israel are dead, exiled by Babylon. So this promise points to another king—one who will come and take David's place, with influence greater than David ever had.

> See, I have made him a witness to the peoples, a leader and commander of the peoples. Surely you will summon nations you know not, and nations that do not know you will hasten to you, because of the LORD your God, the Holy One of Israel, for he has endowed you with splendor. (Isaiah 55:4-5)

The promise isn't just power over Israel, but leadership over all nations. Even nations you don't know will come. Not spiritual hunger satisfied by mere food, but by spiritual fulfillment itself.

## The Promise Fulfilled

Five hundred years later, Jesus appears. In Mark 6, his disciples face a crisis: thousands are hungry, it's late, and they suggest sending people away to buy food.

> Jesus said to them, "You give them something to eat." They said, "That would take eight months' wages! Are we to go and spend that much on bread?" He asked, "How many loaves do you have? Go and see." They found five loaves and two fish.

Then Jesus directed them to seat people in groups of hundreds and fifties—like a king organizing his court. He took the five loaves and two fish, gave thanks, broke them, and gave them to his disciples to distribute.

> They all ate and were satisfied, and the disciples picked up twelve basketfuls of broken pieces of bread and fish. The number of the men who had eaten was five thousand. (Mark 6:42-44)

Jesus is the greater David. He feeds thousands with abundance left over. This symbolizes who he truly is: the one who can feed your soul.

He can fill your desire for truth. Your longing for certainty. Your hunger for meaning. Your need for purpose. Your curiosity about what lies ahead. Most importantly, he can address your deepest need: forgiveness.

Deep down, we know there's a God. We know he's righteous, and we know we've fallen short. We've done wrong things to him, to each other, to ourselves. We feel it in our gut when distractions fade away.

If that's you, you don't have to be hungry anymore. Seek the Lord.

## Seek God While You Can

> Seek the LORD while he may be found; call on him while he is near. Let the wicked forsake his way and the evil man his thoughts. Let him turn to the LORD, and he will have mercy on him, and to our God, for he will freely pardon. (Isaiah 55:6-7)

This is a limited offer. Like all bargains, it won't last forever. It's near now. Repent—turn around from your way. Abandon the evil thoughts you cling to.

And here's the promise: God will have mercy. He will freely pardon. Freely forgive.

But notice—it's free for you, not for Jesus. Every bargain has a cost to someone. With this one, Jesus pays. The man who fed five thousand would soon give his life as a sacrifice for us. He went to the cross willingly, knowingly, paying a debt that should have been ours.

Because of him, it's free.

## Can You Afford NOT to Follow Jesus?

We worry about the cost of being Christian. Can I afford to follow Jesus? It means changing how I live. Giving up certain habits. Committing to a belief system. Handing my autonomy to God.

But here's the thing: it's not a cost—it's freedom from being ripped off.

Look at what the world offers:
- **Money**: Gina Rinehart mines silver endlessly, yet finds no satisfaction. She steals from her own children.
- **Education**: Half the university students change courses from ones they worked so hard to enter.
- **Travel**: We chase temples in Japan, churches in Europe, beaches in Bali. More and more. Never satisfied.
- **Achievement**: An Abbotsleigh girl scored 99.95 on her ATAR and sued the Board of Studies.
- **Stuff**: What's important today becomes trash kerbside the next day.

These things do not satisfy. We're fooled by advertising agencies, Hollywood, corporations, and politicians who want us as consumers so their profits grow. What a tragedy to spend your whole life chasing bread at twice the price, only to discover at the end it's not even bread—just empty effort yielding nothing.

The cost of Christianity—living as God intended, abandoning evil—is that you won't be ripped off. That's not a cost. That's wisdom.

Even if you think there's a price, it's small. We entrust our lives to strangers on whims all the time. We eat from KFC despite meningitis risks. We put our trust in a 15-year-old cashier simply because "it's probably okay." You can trust Jesus on the same basis—faith as small as a mustard seed—because the benefit far outweighs any cost.

I came to faith in Year 11 from an atheistic home. I wondered: what if this slim possibility was true? Life works this way—we don't verify our spouses' backgrounds before marriage, we don't check shopping centers for terrorists, we don't ask bus drivers for their licenses. We gather a little information and take a leap of faith. In friendship, this leap is confirmed and we take another. It's a relationship of trust.

With Christianity, we have far more than that. We have the best evidence from the ancient world: multiple attestation, coherent origin, philosophical strength. Better than other religions. Better than atheism.

And I've been a Christian for twenty years—the proofs only get better. For those wavering because the world's arguments seem strong: the world's arguments haven't changed, but our rebuttal improves. Every year brings new manuscripts confirming Scripture. More science reveals more of God's work—quantum physics, the intricacy of creation—things simplified at high school that were later used to "debunk" Jesus, but real science only strengthens faith.

The choice is clear: Come, all you who are thirsty. Come to the waters. Buy wine and milk without money and without cost. There's abundant food left over, and it's for you.

Can you afford to follow Jesus? The real question is: *how can you afford NOT to?*

This is the best bargain of your life—literally.
