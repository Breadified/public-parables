# Acts 21:1-40

**Title:** Paul and Jerusalem
**Date:** 7 August 2016
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Introduction

You might remember last year when we looked at Acts. The theme was "to the ends of the earth" from Acts 1 — Judea, Samaria, and the ends of the earth. After all that, we're back at the beginning again. Paul is on his way back to Jerusalem.

This is the end of Paul's third missionary journey. Many people have heard about Jesus. The gospel has gone out. As he passes through the Greek islands, you'll recognize some familiar names:

> After we had torn ourselves away from them, we put out to sea and sailed straight to Kos. The next day we went to Rhodes and from there to Patara. We found a ship crossing over to Phoenicia, went on board and set sail. After sighting Cyprus and passing to the south of it, we sailed on to Syria. (Acts 21:1-3)

Syria — where the fighting is today. A very different Middle East back then. The land of ancient Syrians, ancient Phoenicians, not Arabs.

When Paul returns to Jerusalem, people treat him very differently depending on who they are. We'll look at this through three perspectives: how Christians see Paul, how the religious leaders see Paul, and how the Romans see Paul.

---

## 1. How the Christians See Paul: Unity (21:15-26)

When Paul arrives in Jerusalem, the reception is warm:

> When we arrived at Jerusalem, the brothers and sisters received us warmly. The next day Paul and the rest of us went to see James, and all the elders were present. Paul greeted them and reported in detail what God had done among the Gentiles through his ministry. When they heard this, they praised God. (Acts 21:17-20)

Great news about the Gentiles! And the leaders at Jerusalem have their own report — great news about the Jews:

> Then they said to Paul: "You see, brother, how many thousands of Jews have believed, and all of them are zealous for the law." (Acts 21:20)

But there's a problem. A rumour is circulating about Paul:

> They have been informed that you teach all the Jews who live among the Gentiles to turn away from Moses, telling them not to circumcise their children or live according to our customs. What shall we do? (Acts 21:21-22)

Here's the thing. Paul's message to the Gentiles was that you don't have to become a Jew in order to be a Christian. For the Gentile who had nothing to do with Judaism, there was no requirement to adopt Jewish customs — they left their old pagan beliefs behind instead. You can see this in verse 25:

> As for the Gentile believers, we have written to them our decision that they should abstain from food sacrificed to idols, from blood, from the meat of strangled animals and from sexual immorality. (Acts 21:25)

But this is different for Jews. If you're a Jew, you don't leave Judaism behind — the circumcision (because you can't get uncircumcised) or the temple rituals. Christianity is the fulfilment of Judaism. Romans 1 speaks of what it means to be a true Jew.

So when Jews hear that Gentiles don't have to become Jews, they mishear it as "Jews mustn't be Jews anymore." Confusion.

The Jerusalem leaders propose a solution — show them that you are a Jew:

> There are four men with us who have made a vow. Take these men, join in their purification rites and pay their expenses, so that they can have their heads shaved. Then everyone will know there is no truth in these reports about you, but that you yourself are living in obedience to the law. (Acts 21:23-24)

This is a Nazirite vow — a commitment to serving the temple. Paul obliges:

> The next day Paul took the men and purified himself along with them. Then he went to the temple to give notice of the date when the days of purification would end and the offering would be made for each of them. (Acts 21:26)

### A Theological Note: The Unique Transition from Judaism to Christianity

The transition from Jew to Christian is unique. It's not the same as the transition from paganism to Christianity. And it's not the same as claiming Christianity evolved into something else.

People sometimes say: "Christians evolved out of Judaism, so why can't Islam or Mormonism be legitimate? They claim to be the true fulfilment of Christianity."

The difference is this: when you say you fulfil something, you actually respect the past.

Islam claims to come out of Christianity — Jesus is a prophet, Muhammad is just another — but then claims that the New Testament is full of errors.

Mormonism claims to come out of Christianity — Joseph Smith is just another prophet — but then claims the New Testament is full of errors.

Christianity is different. It claims to come out of Judaism. And it claims the Old Testament is totally true! In fact, in the first few centuries AD, Christians read and kept the Old Testament better than the Jewish people did. Christians would quote the Old Testament back to the Jews to show how Jesus fulfilled prophecy. Islam doesn't quote the New Testament back to us.

So Paul fulfils his Jewish obligations. Yet he still can't satisfy some.

## 2. How the Judaisers See Paul: A Threat (21:27-40)

> When the seven days were nearly over, some Jews from the province of Asia saw Paul at the temple. They stirred up the whole crowd and seized him, shouting, "Fellow Israelites, help us! This is the man who teaches everyone everywhere against our people and our law and this place. And besides, he has brought Greeks into the temple and defiled this holy place." (They had previously seen Trophimus the Ephesian in the city with Paul and assumed that Paul had brought him into the temple.) (Acts 21:27-29)

Their accusations:
- He teaches against the law — which he doesn't
- He brought a Gentile into the temple — which was forbidden. Ancient inscriptions warned: "No foreigner is to enter within the balustrade and embankment around the sanctuary. Whoever is caught will have himself to blame for his death which follows."

But he didn't do that either. They saw Trophimus in the city and assumed Paul brought him into the temple.

They saw Paul as a threat. But who's got time to think when the mob is raging?

> The whole city was aroused, and the people came running from all directions. Seizing Paul, they dragged him from the temple, and immediately the gates were shut... When Paul reached the steps, the violence of the mob was so great he had to be carried by the soldiers. The crowd that followed kept shouting, "Get rid of him!" (Acts 21:30, 35-36)

When Paul finally gets a word in:

> "Brothers and fathers, listen now to my defense." When they heard him speak to them in Aramaic, they became very quiet. (Acts 22:1-2)

Then Paul shares his testimony — his Jewish credentials, his training under Gamaliel, his zealous persecution of Christians. He recounts his encounter with Christ on the Damascus road:

> "About noon as I came near Damascus, suddenly a bright light from heaven flashed around me. I fell to the ground and heard a voice say to me, 'Saul! Saul! Why do you persecute me?' 'Who are you, Lord?' I asked. 'I am Jesus of Nazareth, whom you are persecuting,' he replied." (Acts 22:6-8)

He describes how Ananias restored his sight and commissioned him:

> "The God of our ancestors has chosen you to know his will and to see the Righteous One and to hear words from his mouth. You will be his witness to all people of what you have seen and heard." (Acts 22:14-15)

And then he mentions his vision in the temple:

> "Then the Lord said to me, 'Go; I will send you far away to the Gentiles.'" (Acts 22:21)

All hell breaks loose:

> The crowd listened to Paul until he said this. Then they raised their voices and shouted, "Rid the earth of him! He's not fit to live!" (Acts 22:22)

The word "Gentiles" was the trigger. Their very accusation was that Paul was telling Jews to be like Gentiles.

### A Word About Speech and Violence

You see, once upon a time we believed in this saying: "Sticks and stones may break my bones but words will never hurt me." It was taught in kindergartens from 1862 onwards — we don't retaliate with violence just because people call us names. Talking can never hurt anyone. Ideas and facts can never hurt.

That's why we have words. We all disagree — violently at times. The question is how to disagree. We can use that violence to fight with physical violence. Or we can use words. I'm not talking about words that incite violent hurt — the "he's not fit to live" stuff. But ideas are always acceptable. Freedom of speech exists, if not for its own merit, then as a better alternative to actual violence.

Think about trigger subjects today. Immigration debates. Climate change. Concerns about education programs. Political figures. We all jump up and down, ready to attack.

But wait — why follow the violent mob? Especially when that same mob has been wrong on so many things.

## 3. How the Romans See Paul (21:37-22:30)

The Romans rescue Paul from the mob. But they confuse him with someone else:

> As the soldiers were about to take Paul into the barracks, he asked the commander, "May I say something to you?" "Do you speak Greek?" he replied. "Aren't you the Egyptian who started a revolt and led four thousand terrorists out into the wilderness some time ago?" (Acts 21:37-38)

They thought he was a terrorist. Literally. The Jewish historian Josephus records that there was an Egyptian false prophet who gathered thousands of deluded followers and was ready to break into Jerusalem by force from the Mount of Olives.

Paul is not a terrorist.

Later, when the Romans are about to flog him:

> As they stretched him out to flog him, Paul said to the centurion standing there, "Is it legal for you to flog a Roman citizen who hasn't even been found guilty?" (Acts 22:25)

This changes everything:

> The commander went to Paul and asked, "Tell me, are you a Roman citizen?" "Yes, I am," he answered. Then the commander said, "I had to pay a lot of money for my citizenship." "But I was born a citizen," Paul replied. Those who were about to interrogate him withdrew immediately. (Acts 22:27-29)

## 4. How Paul Sees Himself (21:4-14)

> Paul answered, "I am a Jew, from Tarsus in Cilicia, a citizen of no ordinary city. Please let me speak to the people." (Acts 21:39)

Paul had a successful "career" among the Gentiles. Why go back to Jerusalem?

Throughout chapter 21, people try to dissuade him:

> Through the Spirit they urged Paul not to go on to Jerusalem. (Acts 21:4)

A prophet named Agabus came with a vivid warning:

> Coming over to us, he took Paul's belt, tied his own hands and feet with it and said, "The Holy Spirit says, 'In this way the Jewish leaders in Jerusalem will bind the owner of this belt and will hand him over to the Gentiles.'" When we heard this, we and the people there pleaded with Paul not to go up to Jerusalem. (Acts 21:11-12)

But Paul's response:

> "Why are you weeping and breaking my heart? I am ready not only to be bound, but also to die in Jerusalem for the name of the Lord Jesus." When he would not be dissuaded, we gave up and said, "The Lord's will be done." (Acts 21:13-14)

Paul — ready to die in Jerusalem for Jesus. His love for the Jews draws him back.

He's done all this work with the Gentiles. Although it was the rejection of the Jews that sent him to the Gentiles in the first place, now at the end of his third journey, he longs to return. He sets his face to Jerusalem.

Just like Jesus. Luke 9:51 says Jesus "set his face to go to Jerusalem." There's a deliberate parallel between Paul and Jesus in Luke-Acts.

> "And now, compelled by the Spirit, I am going to Jerusalem, not knowing what will happen to me there. I only know that in every city the Holy Spirit warns me that prison and hardships are facing me. However, I consider my life worth nothing to me; my only aim is to finish the race and complete the task the Lord Jesus has given me — the task of testifying to the good news of God's grace." (Acts 20:22-24)

It doesn't matter how others see him. In the end, what's important is how he sees himself — from God's perspective. He is but a servant. His job is to witness. To finish the task the Lord has given him.

Gentiles, Jews — it doesn't matter. Persecution is the normal life of the Christian.

### The Meaning of Witness

The term "martyr" comes from the Greek word *marturion* — "witness." To be a witness is to be a martyr.

Here's the thing about being a witness: you are called to disagree. Think about it — you don't need witnesses to stand for something everyone agrees on. No one calls witnesses to testify that the sky is blue. You bring a witness out when people disagree about the facts.

Christians are witnesses. We are the voices that challenge the existing status quo — the thing everyone agrees on, where if only you would keep quiet, then we would have peace.

But if you speak up, some will think you're a terrorist. They'll see you as a threat.

Who are you? That's the question each of us must answer. Not based on how others label us. But based on who God says we are — His witnesses, called to testify to the good news of His grace.
