# Romans 3:22

**Title:** The Translation of Romans 3:22: Understanding "Faith of Christ" vs. "Faith in Christ"

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Question at Hand

A fundamental translation question arises at Romans 3:22. While most English translations render the phrase as "through faith in Jesus Christ," the Greek text can be better translated as "through the faith of Jesus Christ."

This raises an important interpretive issue: Is Jesus Christ the object of our faith (we believe *in* Jesus), or is He the subject of faith (His own *faithfulness* to God's plan)?

The PTC commentary explains the distinction:

> God acted to save us through the faith/obedience of Jesus which led him to die for us (compare Romans 5:18-19; Philippians 2:8). The second part of the expression stresses that the benefits of God's saving actions through Christ are for all who believe.

## What's at Stake Theologically?

Before diving into the grammatical details, we must recognize that both translations are theologically sound. Scholar Douglas Moo, who favors "faith in Jesus Christ," acknowledges the alternative this way:

> No objection to this interpretation can be made on doctrinal grounds. God's righteousness comes to us only because Jesus was faithful in carrying out the commission that God the Father had given him.

So if both positions are doctrinally valid, what really matters here? It's a fine-tuning of our understanding—a sharpening of how we grasp the great work of God in salvation that stands at the very heart of Paul's concern as he writes to the Roman church.

## The Grammatical Issue: The Genitive Case

The translation question centers on the Greek genitive case in the phrase *pisteos Iesou Christou* (faith of Jesus Christ). The genitive case can describe multiple relationships between words, so determining which relationship Paul intends requires careful analysis.

Specifically, the question is whether this is an **objective genitive** (faith in Jesus) or a **subjective genitive** (faith of Jesus).

### Four Key Considerations

#### 1. Subjective vs. Objective Genitive in the New Testament

Grammatically, the subjective genitive is more common in New Testament usage because it naturally occurs in more types of constructions than the objective genitive.

When we examine "faith" with a personal genitive in the New Testament, the subjective genitive is the standard usage. For example:
- Mark 2:5: "the faith of them" (their faith)
- Romans 1:8: "the faith of you" (your faith)

This grammatical pattern suggests the subjective reading may be more natural.

#### 2. How "Faith" and "Jesus" Are Connected Elsewhere

The genitive connection between faith and Jesus in Romans 3:22 appears in other New Testament passages:
- Galatians 2:16, 20; 3:22
- Philippians 3:9
- Ephesians 3:12

Galatians 3:22 is particularly significant because it mentions "the faith of Christ" alongside "those who believe in him"—the same translation issue Paul faces in both Romans 3 and Galatians 3.

The resolution of this question isn't determined by grammar alone, but by considering the passage's context, the author's general word usage, and the broader theological issues being addressed.

#### 3. How "Faith" and the Genitive Are Used Nearby in Romans

The immediate context of Romans 3:22 provides important clues:

- **Romans 3:3**: "the faith of God" (subjective genitive) → commonly translated as "God's faithfulness" or "the faithfulness of God"
- **Romans 4:16**: "the faith of Abraham" (subjective genitive) → "Abraham's faith"
- **Romans 3:26**: *pisteos Iesou* → translated as an objective genitive ("faith in Jesus")

Paul uses both subjective and objective genitives within these few verses, showing he was comfortable with both constructions.

#### 4. Theological and Contextual Reasoning

Both translations yield biblical truth, but they emphasize different aspects of Paul's teaching.

**The Objective Genitive ("Faith in Jesus Christ")**

This translation emphasizes that God's righteousness comes *through* human faith. The verse stresses that God's righteousness is universally available to all who believe, without distinction.

This reading makes sense in context because:
- Paul has just argued in 1:18-3:20 that all people—Jews and Gentiles alike—stand guilty before God
- Now a righteousness from God has come, available to everyone on the basis of faith
- This translation aligns with Paul's typical use of *pistis* (faith) throughout Romans
- It leads naturally into 3:26 and Paul's continuing argument in 3:21-4:25, which emphasizes faith as the only way we are justified

**The Subjective Genitive ("The Faith of Jesus Christ")**

This translation emphasizes that God's righteousness comes *through Christ's faithfulness*, not through human effort.

The theological reasoning works like this:

In Romans 1:17, Paul declares: "For in it the righteousness of God is revealed from faith for faith, as it is written, 'The righteous shall live by faith.'"

The gospel Paul speaks of here centers entirely on Jesus—the One who fulfills God's Old Testament promises, the One who is God's saving intervention through His death and resurrection. God's righteousness is revealed in this gospel.

But before Paul can expand on this, he must demonstrate that there is no other path to righteousness apart from the gospel. He does this by showing humanity's universal sinfulness, which justly brings God's wrath (1:18-3:20).

Then in 3:21-26, Paul connects the gospel of Jesus Christ with God's righteousness.

How does this righteousness come? Not through the law (which is human activity), but through divine activity—through Christ's faithfulness to God's promises and plan. Our salvation flows from Christ's faithfulness, not from human effort. This faithfulness takes Christ to the cross, where He offers Himself as a propitiatory sacrifice for our sin.

Through Christ's death, we are justified before God—declared not guilty because we are redeemed by His blood.

How do we receive the benefits of Christ's work? Not by works, but by faith. This means there is no ground for boasting. When the text says "to all who believe," it stresses that the gospel's benefits are made available to all who embrace this gospel centered in Jesus Christ's work.

This theological reasoning is what persuades the study notes to adopt the subjective genitive as the preferred translation.

## A Note on the New Perspective and Romans 3:22

The "New Perspective on Paul" challenges the traditional understanding of justification by faith alone as the Bible's primary way of describing God's salvation. This alternative view raises questions such as:

- Is justification really at the center of Paul's thought?
- Are Paul's opponents advocates of legalism, or do they view law-keeping as necessary to maintain one's saved or covenantal status?
- Does Paul's doctrine of justification have more to do with the place of Gentiles in the church than with individual salvation?

Scholars like E.P. Sanders, J.D.G. Dunn, and N.T. Wright advocate versions of this new perspective.

It could be argued that the subjective genitive reading of Romans 3:22 lends support to the new perspective approach (emphasizing covenant faithfulness rather than individual faith). However, the subjective genitive translation should not be rejected or accepted based on its alleged theological implications. It must be argued for on grammatical and exegetical grounds alone.

As one scholar notes: *The alleged implications of a particular translation are not a necessary outcome of the translation itself, for the translation is still part of a context and an argument.*

---

The question of how to translate Romans 3:22 ultimately reminds us that careful study of God's Word—wrestling with the original languages, considering context, and thinking theologically—deepens our understanding of the magnificent work of salvation God has accomplished through Christ.
