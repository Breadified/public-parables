# Luke 22:24-30

**Title:** True Ministry is Servanthood  
**Date:** November 12, 2008  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Who is the Greatest?

The question of greatness has sparked debates throughout history—Alexander the Great or Genghis Khan, Kobe Bryant or Michael Jordan. But in Luke 22, we find the disciples of Jesus arguing this same question during their final meal together.

> Also a dispute arose among them as to which of them was considered to be greatest. (Luke 22:24)

Who deserves the title? Peter with his special access to Jesus? John and James, who secured places at Jesus's left and right? Andrew, who found Jesus before Peter did? The disciples' concern reveals a deeply human preoccupation with rank and status.

Jesus responds to this quarrel not with rebuke alone, but with a complete reversal of their values. His answer unfolds through two powerful contrasts that reshape how his followers should understand greatness.

## Waiting on Tables: Two Contrasts

### Contrast #1: Kings and Servants (vv. 25-26)

Jesus begins by describing how the world operates:

> The kings of the Gentiles lord it over them; and those who exercise authority over them call themselves Benefactors. (Luke 22:25)

In the kingdoms of this world, power is exercised for self-aggrandizement. Those who rule use their authority not to serve others, but to serve themselves—adorning themselves with titles and accolades. The British peerage system, baronetcies, even our modern Order of Australia—these exist so that power can dress itself in flattery. Authority becomes a vehicle for personal vanity.

But Jesus cuts against the grain of everything the disciples understand about power and status:

> But you are not to be like that. Instead, the greatest among you should be like the youngest, and the one who rules like the one who serves. (Luke 22:26)

This is not a suggestion but a command. The kingdom of Jesus operates on an entirely different economy. Among his followers, greatness is measured in reverse. The greatest is the youngest. The ruler serves like a servant.

In many cultures, this makes perfect sense. The youngest holds the lowest station. In Korean culture, in Chinese tea ceremonies, youth and servanthood go together. Jesus elevates this principle to the heart of kingdom living: true greatness belongs to those who humble themselves in service.

### Contrast #2: Guests and Servants (v. 27)

Jesus drives the point home with one more contrast:

> For who is greater, the one who is at the table or the one who serves? Is it not the one who is at the table? But I am among you as one who serves. (Luke 22:27)

On the surface, the answer is obvious. The guest reclining at the table occupies the position of honor and importance. In this very room, Jesus himself reclines at the table—he is the teacher, the master, the one they follow. He has called them; they have devoted their lives to him.

Yet Jesus identifies himself not with the honored guest, but with the servant. And his words are not empty rhetoric. Just before this meal, he spoke of his body being broken for them. In the coming hours, he will face betrayal, suffering, and crucifixion. The one who is Lord of all will wash feet, pour out his blood, and lay down his life.

This is who they are to become: people who put others before themselves.

## What Servanthood Really Means

Servanthood is not defined by class, occupation, titles, or pecking order. It is simply the commitment to look after another person's needs before your own. It means putting others first.

In retail, the principle is elementary: the customer comes first. A hotel manager doesn't begrudge the guest who complains about noise—he serves him. A waiter doesn't resent being treated poorly or going hungry himself; his primary concern is the person at the table. He cannot serve carelessly. A shop assistant knows that customers come first. Even the garbage collector serves his clients.

This principle applies equally to ministry. As a Bible study leader, you ask what will best help your group understand Scripture. You prepare diligently, as a cook prepares for customers. You think ahead—creating a comfortable space, providing refreshment, removing distractions—not because anyone asked, but because you genuinely care about their experience. You are a servant.

## Why Leadership Battles Make No Sense

This is precisely why fights over leadership positions are so absurd. Imagine arguing over who gets to change a baby's diaper:

"I want to be the one!"  
"No, I'm cleaning her today!"  
"Let me get my hands dirty and deal with the mess!"

Would anyone fight over a diaper bag? Especially volunteers who aren't paid? Yet the disciples quarrel over who is greatest. How strange that people want to fight over positions of service when everyone could simply serve.

This is even more peculiar because ministry doesn't require special titles or positions. Anyone can serve anywhere. Ministry is simply the practice of looking out for someone else's interests rather than perpetuating your own agenda. The disciples' argument reveals how backwards their thinking still is.

## The Disciples Affirmed: A Future Table Awaits

Despite their silly quarrel, Jesus responds to them with surprising affirmation:

> You are those who have stood by me in my trials. (Luke 22:28)

Here's the beautiful irony: the disciples have actually been serving all along. Peter and John prepared the Passover meal (v. 13). Like waiters and cooks, they made it possible for Jesus and the others to recline at the table. Their very presence at this meal demonstrates their commitment to Jesus through trials and uncertainty.

Because they have stood by Jesus, he makes them an extraordinary promise:

> And I confer on you a kingdom, just as my Father conferred one on me, so that you may eat and drink at my table in my kingdom and sit on thrones, judging the twelve tribes of Israel. (Luke 22:29-30)

Those who served in this life will sit beside Jesus in the next. They will eat at his table in the kingdom. They will rule with him. The servant becomes the honored guest—but only after embracing servanthood.

## Our Focus: The Heavenly Gathering

This future banquet is not mere fantasy or distant hope—it is the central focus of Christian ministry. We serve in this life so that others might join us at that table.

The theologians Donald Robinson and Broughton Knox of Moore College made a crucial observation about the word "church" in Scripture. When they studied how it was used, they discovered that the Bible primarily points to one heavenly gathering—the ultimate assembly of all God's people. When Scripture speaks of earthly churches, it simply describes groups of people actually meeting together. This insight—that we belong to one heavenly church but many earthly gatherings—fundamentally changes how we view our local communities of faith.

The danger is that earthly churches can become primarily focused on their own organizational interests. They develop hierarchies (formal or informal, simple or complex). With organization comes authority, which can mobilize resources, attract loyalty, and perpetuate itself. Authority itself is morally neutral—it can be used well or poorly.

But when authority is used to serve the organization's own interests, something has gone wrong. When the aim is to fill pews rather than help people, when newcomers are immediately asked for money, when those who leave are branded disloyal—these reveal an organization serving itself, not the heavenly church. When size becomes the only measure of success, the organization has lost its way.

Conversely, when power is used to serve the heavenly gathering, when the local church becomes a platform to point people toward the gospel and toward eternal communion with God, when our loyalty is finally to the heavenly church rather than to a denomination or brand—then authority becomes a tool for genuine servanthood.

If the heavenly church is truly our focus, we are freed to be authentic servants. We can recommend other churches, other preachers, other communities of faith without the burden of organizational loyalty. Like an independent mortgage broker recommending the best loan regardless of commission, we can genuinely recommend Philip Jensen or Mark Driscoll based on who would actually help the person.

Think about what happens after a meaningful conversation about Jesus. If your first response is "Come to our church," people recognize that you're interested in growing your organization, not in their souls. They know the moment they walk through your doors is the moment you'll stop caring.

A better approach: Be a servant. Genuinely look after their interests. Help them find their place in the heavenly church. If they live far away, acknowledge that someone else may be better positioned to minister to them. But if they're in your neighborhood, take responsibility for their spiritual care. And if your organization pressures you for numbers, remember your first loyalty is to them, not to your organization's growth.

## The Greatest Among You

In the end, true greatness in God's kingdom belongs to those who focus on others' needs rather than their own advancement. It belongs to those who wait on tables while looking forward to sitting at the future table. The greatest is the one whose agenda is someone else's wellbeing.

Go and do likewise.
