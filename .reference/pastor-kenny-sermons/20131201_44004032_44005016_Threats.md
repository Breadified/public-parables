# Acts 4:32–5:16

**Title:** Threats
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew
**Date:** December 1, 2013

## Why We Join Groups

We join groups for many reasons—to exercise, to change the world, to grow intellectually, to pursue our interests. But what makes a group worth joining? And what threatens a group's unity and mission?

## The Early Church's Radical Generosity

> All the believers were one in heart and mind. No one claimed that any of his possessions was his own, but they shared everything they had. With great power the apostles continued to testify to the resurrection of the Lord Jesus, and much grace was upon them all. (Acts 4:32–33)

This wasn't socialism or communism. Private property remained intact. Rather, the early believers understood themselves as **stewards and trustees**—holding their possessions not as ultimate owners, but as guardians for God's beneficiaries.

A trust is a legal arrangement where one person holds title to property for the benefit of others. In the same way, God is the ultimate owner; we are merely trustees of what He has given us. This stewardship extends beyond our immediate families to the whole community of believers, and beyond that, to those in genuine need.

The apostles taught this principle clearly:

> Remember this: Whoever sows sparingly will also reap sparingly, and whoever sows generously will also reap generously. Each man should give what he has decided in his heart to give, not reluctantly or under compulsion, for God loves a cheerful giver. (2 Corinthians 9:6–7)

**Giving is always voluntary**—but voluntary does not mean we are exempted from generosity. It means the decision and amount are ours, yet the call to care for others remains. How we exercise our stewardship reveals our priorities and what we truly value.

## The Story of Ananias and Sapphira: Deceit in the Church

### Filled by Satan (Acts 5:1–4)

A couple named Ananias and Sapphira joined this new movement. They seemed like decent people—they sold their property and brought the proceeds to the apostles' feet, just as Barnabas and others had done.

But there was a problem:

> With his wife's full knowledge he kept back part of the money for himself, but brought the rest and put it at the apostles' feet. (Acts 5:2)

When confronted by Peter, we learn the severity of their transgression:

> Then Peter said, "Ananias, how is it that Satan has so filled your heart that you have lied to the Holy Spirit and have kept for yourself some of the money you received for the land?" (Acts 5:3)

Satan filled Ananias's heart—not through demon possession, but by tempting him toward deception. The question becomes: what exactly was Ananias's sin?

Some might think it was simple greed or lack of generosity. But the property was his to keep or give as he chose. Peter himself acknowledged this:

> Didn't it belong to you before it was sold? And after it was sold, wasn't the money at your disposal? (Acts 5:4)

Ananias had complete freedom. He always controlled the money. The crime was not insufficient giving. **The crime was that Ananias claimed to give everything when he did not. He lied.**

When we perform acts of righteousness for appearance rather than genuine obedience, we sin. Jesus made this clear:

> Be careful not to do your acts of righteousness before men, to be seen by them... But when you give to the needy, do not let your left hand know what your right hand is doing, so that your giving may be in secret. Then your Father, who sees what is done in secret, will reward you. (Matthew 6:1, 3–4)

The call is not to look good before others, but before God. When we live merely for appearance, we lie—not just to people, but to God Himself.

### The Consequences: Lied and Died (Acts 5:5–10)

The consequences were immediate and severe:

> When Ananias heard this, he fell down and died. (Acts 5:5)

He was struck down by God—not by Peter's hand, but by divine judgment. About three hours later, his wife Sapphira came in, unaware of what had happened. Peter asked her to confirm the sale price, and she agreed with Ananias's lie. She, too, fell down and died.

> Peter said to her, "How could you agree to test the Spirit of the Lord? Look! The feet of the men who buried your husband are at the door, and they will carry you out also." At that moment she fell down at his feet and died. (Acts 5:9–10)

She had tested the Lord—gambling that He would ignore her deception. She was wrong.

## Why Such a Drastic Response?

The church was described as being "united in heart and mind." But **the greatest threat to the church is not external opposition—it is internal compromise**. A church united in deception is far more dangerous than a church facing outside attacks.

This story echoes an earlier pivotal moment in Israel's history: the sin of Achan at the beginning of the conquest of Canaan (Joshua 7).

Like Ananias, Achan kept for himself what was devoted to God, using the same Hebrew word for taking/keeping back. Like Ananias, Achan lied about it. And like Ananias, Achan was confronted and died. Joshua called on him to "give glory to the LORD" and confess, and Achan admitted his sin—coveting a beautiful robe, silver, and gold from the plunder.

The parallels are striking:
- Achan's sin occurred early in Israel's military progress in the Promised Land
- Ananias and Sapphira's sin occurred early in the church's gospel progress in Jerusalem
- Both serve as **warnings that deceit interrupts the victorious progress of God's people**

The lesson is profound: **internal godliness—or lack thereof—is the greatest threat to the church's mission.** Lying and hypocrisy deserve death, and it is only by the grace of Christ that we are not struck down immediately for our own deceptions.

## The Church's Witness is at Stake

When people look at the church, do they see transformed lives—or merely people who look and act exactly like the world? If believers spend money on the same tutoring classes, sports clubs, piano lessons, cars, and holidays as unbelievers, why would anyone conclude there is any difference?

> They will say, "The church is full of hypocrites." And they're right—the church is full of hypocrites, and we have room for more.

But the critique cuts deeper. If transformed lives are the greatest promotion for God, then untransformed lives are the greatest obstacle. When we claim to live not for this world yet our actions prove we are thoroughly worldly, when we tell others to trust Jesus but trust in government instead—we become stumbling blocks to our own mission.

**Our only answer is to live godly lives.** This means examining how we use our money, what we prioritize, how we steward God's resources. The testimony of the early church was powerful not because they were perfect, but because they lived differently—and the resurrection of Jesus was vindicated by their transformed conduct.

Marriage, too, bears witness. Ananias and Sapphira were a couple others might have emulated—they consulted each other, they were united in mind. But they were united in lies and deceit. For married couples, the temptation is to think that loving each other fulfills all our obligations. Yet a strong marriage should be like a strong tree under which others find refuge. When we furnish our homes, are we thinking only of ourselves, or of how others might be welcomed? When we buy a television, is it for our entertainment alone, or for community?

**A marriage that turns inward becomes a threat to the gospel. A marriage that opens outward becomes a sign of God's kingdom.**

## The Fear of God Produces Growth (Acts 5:11–16)

The immediate response to Ananias and Sapphira's death was fear:

> Great fear seized the whole church and all who heard about these events. (Acts 5:11)

On the surface, this seems counterproductive:

> No one else dared join them, even though they were highly regarded by the people. (Acts 5:13)

Who would want to join a group where lying is judged so severely? It sounds dangerous.

Yet something remarkable happened:

> Nevertheless, more and more men and women believed in the Lord and were added to their number. Crowds gathered also from the towns around Jerusalem, bringing their sick and those tormented by evil spirits, and all of them were healed. (Acts 5:14, 16)

The church grew precisely because it took sin seriously. People were drawn to a community where:
- Lies were not tolerated
- Integrity was non-negotiable
- Transformation was genuine, not pretended
- The word of God actually shaped and cut people, making them into "polished pieces of precious gem"

Those who had grown weary of hypocrisy wanted to join. Those who longed for something real, for a community that stood for something, came. Those who had tried to carry the burden of their own sin but were willing to face it honestly—they came. And ultimately, those who understood that one day Jesus would deal with their sins once and for all—they came.

## An Invitation

It is a great privilege that we have visitors each week. You are welcomed anytime. But I want to invite you not merely to visit, but to **join**. To bring your sin to God and deal with it. The promise of God is clear: **He will forgive you and accept you.**

Jesus has already borne the judgment for our deception. The guilt that should destroy us has been placed at the cross. And in His resurrection, we are declared forgiven and accepted.

That is the church worth joining.
