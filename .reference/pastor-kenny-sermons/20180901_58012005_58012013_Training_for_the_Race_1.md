# Hebrews 12:5-13

**Title:** Training for the Race
**Date:** 2 September 2018
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Understanding Pain

Pain is not merely tissue damage. Modern research reveals its complexity: soldiers with severe injuries sometimes feel nothing, while others with healed tissue experience persistent phantom pain. Elite athletes demonstrate this reality vividly—they push through pain that would stop ordinary people because they've trained their minds differently.

This letter to Hebrews addresses Christians struggling with their faith in a painful season. Persecution made following Jesus costly. Jesus hadn't returned. It hurt to be a Christian.

## Part 1: Earthly Pain (Verses 5-8)

> 5 And have you completely forgotten this word of encouragement that addresses you as a father addresses his son? It says, "My son, do not make light of the Lord's discipline, and do not lose heart when he rebukes you, 6 because the Lord disciplines the one he loves, and he chastens everyone he accepts as his son."

The author quotes Proverbs 3, drawing a powerful parallel between human and divine fatherhood. God's discipline feels like punishment sometimes—because it is painful—yet it reveals His love. The word "encouragement" (paraklesis) means to build us up.

> 7 Endure hardship as discipline; God is treating you as his children. For what children are not disciplined by their father?

**The Core Principle:** Discipline proves sonship. A child who receives no discipline is like an illegitimate child—neglected, without a father's investment. This reflects a uncomfortable theological truth: we are not born good. Contrary to modern philosophy, which assumes children are innately pure and society corrupts them, Scripture teaches that foolishness is bound up in the heart of a child. Loving parents discipline because they understand what undisciplined self-centeredness produces.

Consider the statistics: children without fathers are five times more likely to live in poverty, nine times more likely to drop out of school, and twenty times more likely to end up in prison. Fatherlessness is not primarily an economic problem—it's a discipleship problem.

> 8 If you are not disciplined—and everyone undergoes discipline—then you are not legitimate, not true sons and daughters at all.

Without discipline, you lack a father. This is the reality we must face.

## Part 2: Heavenly Comfort (Verses 9-11)

> 9 Moreover, we have all had human fathers who disciplined us and we respected them for it. How much more should we submit to the Father of spirits and live!

Here lies the crucial contrast: human fathers do "as they thought best"—limited wisdom, limited knowledge, sometimes mistaken judgment. Our heavenly Father, however, disciplines us with infinite wisdom and perfect knowledge for our actual good.

> 10 They disciplined us for a little while as they thought best; but God disciplines us for our good, in order that we may share in his holiness. 11 No discipline seems pleasant at the time, but painful. Later on, however, it produces a harvest of righteousness and peace for those who have been trained by it.

**The Transformation:** Earthly discipline is temporary and often imperfect. God's discipline aims at something eternal—holiness, righteousness, peace. While discipline always hurts in the moment, it produces fruit for those who submit to training.

John Piper, facing cancer, wrote "Don't Waste Your Cancer." Though cancer is never a gift in the ordinary sense, he transformed it into one by seeing it as an opportunity to trust God more deeply, to take sin seriously, to read Scripture more, to witness more faithfully. Cancer became his training ground.

**The Key Insight:** Pain is not the same as bad. Easy is not the same as good. Many of the best things in life require suffering. Elite athletes prove this—they've learned to think about pain differently than average people. They embrace it as part of the path to excellence.

## Part 3: Endure Pain (Verses 12-13)

> 12 Therefore, strengthen your feeble arms and weak knees. 13 Make level paths for your feet, so that the lame may not be disabled, but rather healed.

This is not merely about physical strength. The author calls for spiritual resilience—stand firm in your faith. Make your paths straight, meaning don't turn aside into false teaching or abandon your commitment. As Paul writes elsewhere, some have "turned away to follow Satan" and "turned aside to myths."

**The Long View:** In a corrupt world, there is pain of consequences. In a needy world, there is pain of service. In a tragic world, there is pain of grief. Enduring the pain you face now trains you for the next one. This is how spiritual athletes are made.

## The Discipline of Commitment

Our generation struggles with this. Young people often lack persistence—leaving jobs, skipping interviews, posting absences on social media instead of communicating honestly. This lack of discipline flows into marriage (half end in divorce), relationships (broken promises), and faith (treating God as an optional "maybe").

Yet our church's emphasis on showing up regularly, serving consistently, and keeping commitments isn't legalism—it's training. We're teaching what the State Emergency Service teaches its volunteers: voluntary commitment means discipline and sacrifice, not showing up when you feel like it.

> 80% of success in life is showing up. —Woody Allen

Research confirms this. Dr. Angela Lee Duckworth discovered that **grit**—the ability to persevere through hardship—is the single greatest predictor of success, more reliable than IQ. Grittier students earn higher GPAs. Grittier cadets stay in military service. Grittier spellers win spelling bees.

Francis Galton, studying eminent figures across history, concluded that high achievement requires **ability plus zeal plus the capacity for hard labour**. Charles Darwin replied that excepting fools, men differ mainly in zeal and hard work—not intellect.

## The Call to Persevere Now

This is critical: the habits you form now will shape your entire life. Students must prioritize the things of God not just for grades, but because spiritual discipline becomes your foundation. Young families must establish Christian habits that will sustain you through decades of pressure. If you can't maintain faith commitments when life is relatively simple, you won't maintain them when work, marriage, and family demand everything.

The 1968 Olympic marathoner John Akhwari fell hard at 19 kilometers, dislocating his knee. Medical staff urged him to stop. He limped on for over an hour, finishing last among 57 finalists. When asked why he continued, he said: "My country did not send me 5,000 miles just to start the race; they sent me to finish the race."

No one remembers the gold medalist that year. But Akhwari's name endures because he finished.

> "We know the statistics—that children who grow up without a father are five times more likely to live in poverty and commit crime; nine times more likely to drop out of schools and 20 times more likely to end up in prison." —President Barack Obama

**Now is the time to get discipline.** Now is the time for self-control and perseverance. These are your formative years. Build habits that will sustain you in righteousness and holiness. The race is long, and you must finish it.

**Persevere.**
