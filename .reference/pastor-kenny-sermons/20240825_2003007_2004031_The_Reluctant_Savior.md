# Exodus 3:7–4:31

**Title:** The Reluctant Savior  
**Date:** August 25, 2024  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Excuses: The Art of Delay

We all know how excuses work. You're sitting down to watch an important TV show when your mother calls: "Come wash the dishes!" 

You respond with a string of delays:
- "Wait—I'm at a really important part of the show!"
- "Just wait until the commercial break, Mom!"
- "Just five more minutes."
- "I have homework to do now."
- "I don't have gloves—how can I wash without gloves?"

This is what an excuse is: a time-delay strategy, hoping the person asking will eventually give up and walk away. It's not saying no directly; it's avoiding commitment through postponement.

When we come to today's passage, we encounter something very similar. Moses makes excuses repeatedly, and through his responses, God teaches us profound truths about who we are and who He is.

## Historical Context

Remember from last week: God's people had been enslaved for 400 years. They multiplied into millions, and Pharaoh enslaved them all. As a baby, Moses was saved and raised in Pharaoh's court.

But now 80 years have passed. In his first 40 years, Moses was an Egyptian prince. He killed an Egyptian and fled to Arabia (Acts 7:23). In his next 40 years, he married, had a son, and worked as a shepherd for his father-in-law, Jethro (Exodus 7:7).

Finally, God acted:

> I have surely seen the affliction of my people who are in Egypt and have heard their cry because of their taskmasters. I know their sufferings, and I have come down to deliver them out of the hand of the Egyptians and to bring them up out of that land to a good and broad land, a land flowing with milk and honey. (Exodus 3:7–8)

God called Moses through the burning bush and said:

> Come, I will send you to Pharaoh that you may bring my people, the children of Israel, out of Egypt. (Exodus 3:10)

## First Excuse: "Who Am I?" (Exodus 3:11–22)

Moses immediately responds:

> Who am I that I should go to Pharaoh and bring the children of Israel out of Egypt? (Exodus 3:11)

On the surface, this sounds like humility. Moses seems to be saying, "I'm nobody. There must be someone better suited for this great task."

But here's the irony: Moses was an Egyptian prince. He had royal connections, understood court protocol, and had freedom. If not him, who? Yet Moses frames it as self-doubt rather than genuine humility.

God's reassurance is direct:

> I will be with you. (Exodus 3:12)

This is the key insight. Moses is right—he is nobody. But that's precisely why God says, "I will be with you." The mission isn't about Moses's abilities; it's about God's presence.

God then provides a sign:

> This shall be the sign for you, that I have sent you: when you have brought the people out of Egypt, you shall serve God on this mountain. (Exodus 3:12)

God assures Moses with His own name:

> I AM WHO I AM. (Exodus 3:14)

The divine name YHWH means "I will be who I will be"—a circular, self-defining statement. God cannot be defined by anything external; He is self-existent and self-sufficient.

God elaborates further:

> The Lord, the God of your fathers, the God of Abraham, the God of Isaac, and the God of Jacob, has sent me to you. This is my name forever, and thus I am to be remembered throughout all generations. (Exodus 3:15)

What follows is an incredible array of details: God will visit the plagues, Pharaoh will resist at first, but eventually release the people with spoils. God has planned everything—even Pharaoh's response—in advance.

Notice the pattern: God repeats, "I will do this." Why? Because YHWH means "I will be"—God is the one acting, not Moses.

### Application: Confidence in God, Not Self

As Christians, we're called to humility:

> Put on then, as God's chosen ones, holy and beloved, compassionate hearts, kindness, humility, meekness, and patience. (Colossians 3:12)

We live in a narcissistic generation, raised like little emperors. We imagine our callings should be filled with accolades, medals, and recognition.

But the truth is harder: Most of us are ordinary. We are the wooden and clay vessels in the great house, not the golden ones (2 Timothy 2:20). We are like the foot soldiers in war—cannon fodder with a life expectancy measured in weeks, not the generals who win glory.

Yet here's the critical balance: This shouldn't paralyze us. The issue isn't whether we're confident in ourselves—it's whether we're confident in *God*.

We shouldn't be arrogant, but we also shouldn't be paralyzed by false humility. We need a third way: confidence in God.

As soldiers, we may be ordinary, but we trust the General knows what He's doing and that we will win the war.

> Come to me, all who are weary and are carrying heavy burdens, and I will give you rest. Take my yoke upon you, and learn from me; for I am gentle and humble in heart, and you will find rest for your souls. For my yoke is easy, and my burden is light. (Matthew 11:28–30)

> I can do all things through Christ who strengthens me. (Philippians 4:13)

The credit belongs to God. When we serve, it's not about us—it's about the need and the God who enables us to meet it. If God commands us to raise children or pursue a calling, He provides what we need.

## Second Excuse: "Who Are You?" (Exodus 4:1–9)

The elders might not believe him, Moses worries:

> They won't believe me or listen to my voice, for they will say, "The Lord did not appear to you." (Exodus 4:1)

So God provides three signs. First, the staff—the shepherd's rod Moses has carried:

> Cast it on the ground. It became a serpent, and Moses ran from it. But the Lord said to Moses, "Put out your hand and catch it by the tail"—so he put out his hand and caught it, and it became a staff in his hand. (Exodus 4:3–4)

The staff transforms from ordinary shepherd's tool into a miraculous instrument. This teaches us something profound: God doesn't discard us; He uses what we have.

The second sign is healing:

> Put your hand inside your cloak. When he took it out, behold, his hand was leprous like snow. And he said, "Put your hand back into your cloak." So he put his hand back into his cloak, and when he took it out, behold, it was restored like the rest of his flesh. (Exodus 4:6–7)

The third sign involves water from the Nile turning to blood (Exodus 4:9).

Some scholars suggest these signs target Egyptian gods—the serpent, the Nile—or perhaps they foreshadow the gospel: the serpent destroyed, disease healed by Christ's blood and water. Or perhaps they're simpler: God uses what's at hand to accomplish His will.

The key insight: Just as God transforms Moses's ordinary staff into an instrument of miracles, God transforms Moses himself—an ordinary, frightened man—into His tool.

> In a large house there are articles not only of gold and silver, but also of wood and clay; some are for special purposes and some for common use. Those who cleanse themselves from the latter will be instruments for special purposes, made holy, useful to the Master and prepared to do any good work. (2 Timothy 2:20–21)

**Application: Growth Mindset**

God operates with a growth mindset. He doesn't leave us as we are; He stretches and shapes us. Some of us have fixed mindsets:

- "I can only do this one ministry"
- "I can't—it's too hard"
- "I'm stuck"

The world teaches a fixed-pie mentality: resources are limited, static, unchangeable. But God teaches growth: With more people come more scientists, more innovation, more solutions.

Christianity is fundamentally a growth mindset. We're called to be transformed:

> Grow in the grace and knowledge of our Lord and Savior Jesus Christ. (2 Peter 3:18)

> Be transformed by the renewing of your mind. (Romans 12:2)

God doesn't abandon us or exchange us for better tools. He refines us, stretches us, and prepares us for His purposes.

## Third Excuse: "I Can't Speak" (Exodus 4:10–17)

Finally, Moses offers his last excuse:

> O my Lord, I am not eloquent, either in the past or since you have spoken to your servant, but I am slow of speech and of tongue. (Exodus 4:10)

Whether Moses had a stutter or simply lacked confidence is debatable. But notice the pattern: "Who am I?" → "Who are you?" → Back to "Who am I?" (Now it's my speech that's the problem.)

God's response cuts through the issue:

> Who has made man's mouth? Who makes him mute, or deaf, or seeing, or blind? Is it not I, the Lord? Now therefore go, and I will be with your mouth and teach you what you shall speak. (Exodus 4:11–12)

God has already given Moses everything:
- His presence
- His name
- Three miraculous signs
- The exact words to say (Exodus 3:14–17)

Moses doesn't even need eloquence—he can perform miracles! God created mouths and language. If He wished, He could make a donkey speak (as He later did in Numbers 22:28).

Yet God's endless reassurance didn't convince Moses. His heart revealed the truth:

> O my Lord, please send someone else. (Exodus 4:13)

### The Real Issue: Unwillingness

After all these excuses—false humility, doubt about God, concern for others' belief, and speech anxiety—what emerges? *Moses simply didn't want to go.*

Excuses are just that: delays hoping the person asking will leave. But God saw through Moses. He squeezed his heart, and what came out was stubborn resistance and lack of trust.

God grew angry:

> The anger of the Lord was kindled against Moses. He said, "Is there not Aaron, your brother, the Levite? I know that he can speak well. Behold, he is coming out to meet you, and when he sees you, he will be glad in his heart. You shall speak to him and put the words in his mouth, and I will be with your mouth and with his mouth and will teach you both what to do. He shall speak for you to the people, and he shall be to you as a mouth, and you shall be to him as God." (Exodus 4:14–16)

How different is Jesus's response to a far greater calling:

> Going a little farther, he fell on his face and prayed, saying, "My Father, if it be possible, let this cup pass from me; nevertheless, not as I will, but as you will." (Mark 14:35–36)

Jesus didn't make excuses. He acknowledged the difficulty, but ultimately submitted to His Father's will out of love.

### A Word of Compassion

Perhaps Moses's reluctance ran deeper. He had experienced trauma in Egypt—both as a fugitive from Pharaoh and as an outsider rejected by the Israelites themselves. Psychological barriers are real, and healing takes time.

We all carry wounds and obstacles. The goal isn't to dismiss them but to move through them. We don't need to be fearless; we just need to be willing.

> Receiving this challenge is ultimately how we grow. One step at a time, encouraged by progress, we move forward and embrace the difficulty. (Acts 7:22)

Moses went. He received help from Aaron, but God still sent him. The question each of us must answer is: **Are you willing?**

## The Willing Savior

Moses was a reluctant savior—he refused four times. But we don't depend on Moses for our salvation. We have a different Savior.

In Gethsemane, facing death itself, Jesus prayed:

> Father, if you are willing, remove this cup from me. Nevertheless, not my will, but yours be done. (Luke 22:42)

Jesus didn't want to suffer—He expressed His anguish and fear. But He didn't make excuses. Out of love, He willingly laid down His life.

Notice the difference: a reluctant savior versus a willing one.

All God asks of us is to be willing to be saved. If you don't trust Him now, that's the place to start.

After salvation, life doesn't become easy. We'll be like Moses—constantly tempted to find excuses for disobedience, breaking commitments, unfaithful in our promises.

God says, "Don't be selfish"—yet we are.  
God says, "Love one another"—yet we fail.

But thank God for Jesus—the *willing* Savior who rescues us from our sin and our excuses.
