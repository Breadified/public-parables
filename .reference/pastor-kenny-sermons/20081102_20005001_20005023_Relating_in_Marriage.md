# Proverbs 5:1-23

**Title:** Relating in Marriage
**Date:** November 2, 2008
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction: Why This Matters

A few weeks ago, we explored the Sermon on the Mount and discovered that Jesus was promoting wisdom. This is where the Law ultimately leads. As children, we live by rules—do this, don't do that. But as adults, life becomes more complicated. We face diverse situations that rules alone cannot address. We need wisdom: the application of God's law to love Him and one another across all circumstances.

This passage is particularly important because it addresses something we rarely hear discussed openly and that often runs counter to our culture's messages: marriage and sexuality. Some of what we'll discuss may feel awkward, so please feel free to laugh when appropriate. But I do want you to take the warnings seriously.

This teaching is also deeply relevant to most of us here—whether you're married, preparing for marriage, or have chosen singleness. I should note that I'm speaking from the perspective of a still-young man, married only five years. I'm not an expert, but my wisdom comes from Scripture, which is worth far more than my personal experience.

## Listen Carefully to Wisdom (5:1-2)

The passage begins with the father's urgent call: **Pay attention!**

> My son, pay attention to my wisdom,
> listen well to my understanding
> in order that you may maintain discretion
> and your lips may preserve knowledge.

The father addresses his son—a young man listening to his father's counsel. For us, this is our Father in heaven offering us His wisdom. The command is twofold: pay attention and listen well. To what? To wisdom and understanding. Why? So that we can live wisely—maintaining discretion through our actions and preserving knowledge through our words. Wisdom means knowledge and action working together in harmony.

## The Dangerous Words (5:3-4)

What is the specific danger the son must heed? It comes in the form of a warning about a stranger—an adulteress. Notice the vivid imagery centered on her mouth:

> For the lips of an adulteress drip honey,
> and her speech is smoother than oil;
> but in the end, her mouth is
> bitter as gall,
> sharp as a double-edged sword.

Her words are sensually attractive—like honey and smooth oil—but they hide something bitter and sharp. Imagine a razor-sharp sword cutting inside your mouth. This is the nature of temptation: it appears beautiful and smooth on the outside while concealing ugliness and pain within.

Think of free ice cream that looks delicious and creamy, only to discover it contains something repulsive. Or consider how cigarette companies package their deadly products in attractive wrapping, while pornography comes in glossy packages that conceal the exploitation and organized crime behind the industry. Temptation always looks good on the outside; it's the lips that draw us in.

## The Path to Destruction (5:5-6)

The seduction of the stranger leads inevitably to bitterness and pain. The next metaphors shift from mouths to feet and roads:

> Her feet go down to death;
> her steps lead straight to the grave.
> She gives no thought to the way of life;
> her paths are crooked,
> but she knows it not.

The stranger is a fool—she doesn't think, doesn't weigh her choices, and doesn't realize where she's heading. Her path is crooked and leads downward, toward Sheol. And critically, she is leading her listeners down that same path to ruin.

## Keep Far Away (5:7-10)

The father makes his second urgent appeal:

> Now then, my sons, listen to me!
> Do not turn aside from the speech of my mouth!

He's calling the son to listen to a different voice, to follow a different road:

> Keep to a path far from her!
> Do not go near the door of her house.

This isn't mere caution—it's a command to maintain distance. What happens if you ignore this warning?

> Lest you give your honor to others
> and your years to one who is cruel,
> lest strangers feast on your wealth
> and your toil enrich another man's house.

You lose your strength, your honor, your years, and your wealth. Everything you've built benefits someone else.

## The Regret That Comes Too Late (5:11-14)

Eventually comes the bitter reckoning:

> At the end of your life you will groan,
> when your flesh and body are spent.
> You will say, "How I hated discipline!
> How my heart spurned correction!
> I would not listen to the voice of my teachers
> or obey my instructors.
> I have come to the brink of utter ruin
> in the midst of the whole assembly."

This is the voice of someone who finally sees the cost. The emotional devastation, the financial ruin, the loss of reputation—these are the wages of infidelity. And the tragedy is that the discipline and correction were always available; he simply refused them.

I've observed more marriage breakdowns this year than ever in my life since returning to full-time ministry. Friends separated after just two years of marriage, already talking about moving on—as if that's possible. Another friend recently discovered his wife has a degenerative disease that will lead to paralysis. These are the moments when temptation hits hardest. When life is difficult, when our desires aren't being met, the pull to seek satisfaction elsewhere becomes intense.

But marriage vows aren't conditional on our feelings. We enter with both eyes open, committed to remaining that way. If you find yourself looking elsewhere, you need to ask yourself: what are you really doing? Why are you wasting time looking away?

## The Better Way: Satisfaction in Your Own Marriage (5:15-19)

The author doesn't end in judgment alone. He offers a beautiful alternative:

> Drink water from your own cistern,
> running water from your own well.
> Should your springs overflow in the streets,
> your streams of water in the public squares?
> Let them be yours alone,
> never to be shared with strangers.
> Let your fountain be blessed,
> therefore, rejoice in the wife of your youth!
> 
> A loving doe, a graceful deer—
> may her breasts satisfy you always,
> whenever you are tempted to go astray.

The metaphor shifts from a road leading downward to water sources—cisterns, wells, springs, fountains. The focus is on satisfaction. Find your thirst quenched at home. The imagery is deliberately sensual: not just water but breasts and milk. Where honey once turned to gall and oil to sword, here we find genuine, lasting satisfaction in the wife of your youth.

When you're tempted to seek your desires elsewhere, return to your own sources. This is wisdom about marriage: exclusivity, privacy, and constant renewal of devotion.

## The Final Warning (5:20-23)

The passage concludes with a stark reminder:

> Why be captivated by a stranger, my son?
> Why embrace the bosom of another man's wife?
> For a man's ways are in full view of the LORD,
> and he examines all his paths.
> The evil deeds of a wicked man capture him;
> the cords of his sin hold him fast.
> He will die for lack of discipline,
> led astray by his own great folly.

God sees everything. He weighs every path. The wicked are ensnared by their own evil; sin becomes cords that bind them. Folly—the opposite of wisdom—leads to death.

## Looking Deeper: The Greater Loyalty

This passage is about more than marital fidelity, though it certainly includes that. Remember the vivid language about "strangers" and "foreigners" appearing throughout. Remember that unusual detail in verse 10 about losing your wealth to foreigners. And remember who wrote this: Solomon, the author of Proverbs.

Solomon's own life illustrates the danger he's warning against. According to 1 Kings 11, Solomon loved many foreign women—Moabites, Ammonites, Edomites, Sidonians, and Hittites. Despite God's clear command not to intermarry with these nations, Solomon held fast to them in love. He had seven hundred wives and three hundred concubines. And his wives turned his heart after other gods. He followed Ashtoreth and Molech, abandoning his devotion to the LORD.

The deeper message of Proverbs 5 is this: don't stray from God. Don't, like Solomon, run into the arms of false gods and idols. As Revelation 2:4 says, don't forsake your first love. Find your desires fulfilled in eternal life, not in this temporal one.

There are countless temptations that threaten our inheritance—not just adultery or pornography, but materialism, selfishness, and self-importance. These tempt us away from eternal life. Jesus asked the question that cuts to the heart: "What good is it for a man to gain the whole world, yet forfeit his soul?" (Mark 8:36).

Don't fall for it. Choose wisdom. Choose loyalty to God. Choose to find your satisfaction in Him.
