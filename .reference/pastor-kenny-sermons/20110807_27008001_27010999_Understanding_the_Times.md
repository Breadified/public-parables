# Daniel 8-10
**Title:** Understanding the Times
**Date:** August 7, 2011
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## An Anxious Question

> Then I heard a holy one speaking, and another holy one said to him, "How long will it take for the vision to be fulfilled—the vision concerning the daily sacrifice, the rebellion that causes desolation, and the surrender of the sanctuary and of the host that will be trampled underfoot?" (Daniel 8:13)

Delay and uncertainty create anxiety in us. We feel this when we're stuck in traffic running late, when we don't know the outcome of something important, or when we're waiting to hear back from someone we care about. Daniel's voice in this passage carries that same anxious longing: "How long, O Lord?"

We're nearing the end of Daniel, and the focus of the book shifts dramatically here. In chapters 1-7, Daniel's visions centered on the kingdoms of the world and who truly holds authority. We saw the Son of Man given all power. But from chapter 8 onward, the perspective narrows—from the whole earth to a specific question: *What will happen to God's people?*

Significantly, the language switches back to Hebrew, the language of God's covenant people. This was a signal to ancient Jews that their salvation was drawing near—that deliverance was coming.

## A Vision Beyond Understanding

Daniel receives a vision filled with symbolic animals and numbers that perplex him:

> In the third year of King Belshazzar's reign, I, Daniel, had a vision, after the one that had already appeared to me. In my vision I saw myself in the citadel of Susa in the province of Elam; in the vision I was beside the Ulai Canal. (Daniel 8:1-2)

**The Ram and the Goat**

> I looked up, and there before me was a ram with two horns, standing beside the canal, and the horns were long. One of the horns was longer than the other but grew up later. I watched the ram as he charged toward the west and the north and the south. No animal could stand against him, and none could rescue from his power. He did as he pleased and became great. (Daniel 8:3-4)

> As I was thinking about this, suddenly a goat with a prominent horn between his eyes came from the west, crossing the whole earth without touching the ground. He came toward the two-horned ram I had seen standing beside the canal and charged at him in great rage. I saw him attack the ram furiously, striking the ram and shattering his two horns. The ram was powerless to stand against him; the goat knocked him to the ground and trampled on him, and none could rescue the ram from his power. (Daniel 8:5-7)

**The Broken Horn and Its Successors**

> The goat became very great, but at the height of his power his large horn was broken off, and in its place four prominent horns grew up toward the four winds of heaven. (Daniel 8:8)

> Out of one of them came another horn, which started small but grew in power to the south and to the east and toward the Beautiful Land. It grew until it reached the host of the heavens, and it threw some of the starry host down to the earth and trampled on them. It set itself up to be as great as the Prince of the host; it took away the daily sacrifice from him, and the place of his sanctuary was brought low. Because of rebellion, the host of the saints and the daily sacrifice were given over to it. It prospered in everything it did, and truth was thrown to the ground. (Daniel 8:9-12)

Daniel is bewildered. He needs help understanding:

> While I, Daniel, was watching the vision and trying to understand it, there before me stood one who looked like a man. And I heard a man's voice from the Ulai calling, "Gabriel, tell this man the meaning of the vision." (Daniel 8:15-16)

## The Interpretation: Media, Persia, Greece, and Beyond

Gabriel arrives to explain:

> "Son of man," he said to me, "understand that the vision concerns the time of the end." (Daniel 8:17)

He then reveals the meaning:

> "The two-horned ram that you saw represents the kings of Media and Persia. The shaggy goat is the king of Greece, and the large horn between his eyes is the first king. The four horns that replaced the one that was broken off represent four kingdoms that will emerge from his nation but will not have the same power." (Daniel 8:20-22)

**The History Behind the Vision**

The ram represents Media and Persia—with Persia (under King Cyrus the Great) ultimately dominant, much like one horn growing larger than the other.

The goat is Greece. The large horn is almost certainly Alexander the Great, who conquered the Persian Empire with extraordinary speed. He swept westward and then eastward all the way to India, where he died young. His vast kingdom then fragmented among his generals into roughly four kingdoms: Macedonia, Lydia (modern Turkey), Egypt, and Syria. The last two would prove most significant for God's people.

**The Little Horn: Antiochus Epiphanes**

The vision then focuses on a terrifying figure:

> "In the latter part of their reign, when rebels have become completely wicked, a stern-faced king, a master of intrigue, will arise. He will become very strong, but not by his own power. He will cause astounding devastation and will succeed in whatever he does. He will destroy the mighty men and the holy people. He will cause deceit to prosper, and he will consider himself superior. When they feel secure, he will destroy many and take his stand against the Prince of princes. Yet he will be destroyed, but not by human power." (Daniel 8:23-25)

Scholars identify this as Antiochus IV Epiphanes, one of the last Seleucid kings of Syria, who ruled around 167 BC. His actions against Jerusalem and the Jewish people were brutally specific:

While Antiochus was campaigning in Egypt, a rumor spread that he'd been killed. The deposed High Priest Jason gathered soldiers and attacked Jerusalem. When Antiochus returned, enraged by his military defeat, he attacked the city with fury. In three days, eighty thousand people were lost—forty thousand killed violently and forty thousand sold into slavery.

To consolidate his power, Antiochus outlawed Jewish religious practices and ordered the worship of Zeus instead. When the Jewish people refused, he sent armies to enforce his decree. The temple was desecrated, a Greek military citadel was established in the city, and observant Jews faced impossible choices: abandon their faith or face death.

Men could not keep the Sabbath or celebrate their traditional feasts. Women who circumcised their children were executed, thrown from city walls. Those who secretly gathered in caves to observe the Sabbath were burned alive.

This persecution sparked the Maccabean Revolt, which eventually led to Jewish independence—a prelude to the Roman rule that would follow.

Yet the vision promises:

> "The vision of the evenings and mornings that has been given you is true, but seal up the vision, for it concerns the distant future." (Daniel 8:26)

Daniel is prophesying about events 250 years in the future. **After persecution, there will be an end to the exile.**

## The Seventy Sevens

In chapter 9, Gabriel returns with a more expansive prophecy:

> "Seventy 'sevens' are decreed for your people and your holy city to finish transgression, to put an end to sin, to atone for wickedness, to bring in everlasting righteousness, to seal up vision and prophecy and to anoint the most holy." (Daniel 9:24)

Scholars debate what "seventy sevens" means—whether weeks, years, or symbolic sabbatical cycles—but the overall sense is clear: 490 years of God's plan unfolding.

> "Know and understand this: From the issuing of the decree to restore and rebuild Jerusalem until the Anointed One, the ruler, comes, there will be seven 'sevens,' and sixty-two 'sevens.' It will be rebuilt with streets and a trench, but in times of trouble. After the sixty-two 'sevens,' the Anointed One will be cut off and will have nothing. The people of the ruler who will come will destroy the city and the sanctuary. The end will come like a flood: War will continue until the end, and desolations have been decreed. He will confirm a covenant with many for one 'seven.' In the middle of the 'seven' he will put an end to sacrifice and offering. And on a wing of the temple he will set up an abomination that causes desolation, until the end that is decreed is poured out on him." (Daniel 9:25-27)

The timeline divides into periods: seven weeks for rebuilding, sixty-two weeks before the Anointed One comes, and one final week marked by covenant, the end of sacrifice, and abomination.

**Why So Much Detail?**

Some scholars have suggested Daniel must have been written *after* these events, since the predictions are so specific. But the book claims to be written by Daniel himself, written well before these occurrences. There's a genuine historical question here about the text's origins and dating.

But consider the purpose: God gives a detailed timetable not to alarm His people, but to comfort them. It's like a doctor explaining a medical procedure to a child—"First we'll clean the wound, then apply medicine, then you'll rest. It will hurt for a while, but it won't last forever." The doctor isn't trying to scare; he's providing a map through difficulty.

The message is clear: **Do not fear. Redemption is coming. The Anointed One—the Christ—is coming.**

## Understanding the Times Through Prayer

Daniel doesn't remain in ignorance. By chapter 9, he comes to understanding through prayer and Scripture:

> In the first year of Darius son of Xerxes (a Mede by descent), who was made ruler over the Babylonian kingdom—in the first year of his reign, I, Daniel, understood from the Scriptures, according to the word of the LORD given to Jeremiah the prophet, that the desolation of Jerusalem would last seventy years. So I turned to the Lord God and pleaded with him in prayer and petition, in fasting, and in sackcloth and ashes. (Daniel 9:1-3)

Daniel reads Jeremiah's prophecy about seventy years of exile and realizes that this period is nearing its end. He turns to prayer—not to demand or complain, but to confess, to acknowledge God's righteous character, and to make his petitions known.

**Three Elements of Daniel's Prayer**

*Recognize God's character:*

> "O Lord, the great and awesome God, who keeps his covenant of love with all who love him and obey his commands." (Daniel 9:4)

Daniel begins by acknowledging who God *is*—not a diminished deity who can be manipulated, but the fearsome, awesome King who governs all things and honors His covenant promises.

*Recognize the people's sin:*

> "We have sinned and done wrong. We have been wicked and have rebelled; we have turned away from your commands and laws. We have not listened to your servants the prophets, who spoke in your name to our kings, our princes and our fathers, and to all the people of the land. Therefore the curses and sworn judgments written in the Law of Moses, the servant of God, have been poured out on us, because we have sinned against you." (Daniel 9:5-6, 11)

Daniel doesn't excuse the exile as unjust. He acknowledges that God's judgment is righteous—the people *deserved* what they received because they rebelled against Him.

*Make the petition:*

> "Now, our God, hear the prayers and petitions of your servant. For your sake, O Lord, look with favor on your desolate sanctuary. Give ear, O God, and hear; open your eyes and see the desolation of the city that bears your Name. We do not make requests of you because we are righteous, but because of your great mercy." (Daniel 9:17-18)

Notice what Daniel does *not* do: He doesn't demand God's intervention as a right. He appeals to God's mercy, not to Israel's merits. He prays knowing that grace, not justice, is what the people need.

**How We Should Pray**

Daniel models a prayer life transformed by understanding Scripture and God's character. He doesn't pray with entitlement or demand his "rights." He prays with humility, acknowledging both God's sovereignty and his own unworthiness.

When we face anxiety—about our children, our parents, our health, our faith—we pray like Daniel did:
- Pray with Scripture, not mere emotion
- Pray acknowledging God's fearsome authority and righteous character
- Pray confessing sin rather than justifying ourselves
- Pray appealing to mercy, not rights
- Pray with thanksgiving, not entitlement

And we receive an answer not through mystical experiences, but through Scripture itself. Daniel read Jeremiah and understood. **God speaks to us through His Word.**

## The Now and Not Yet

When we pray "Your kingdom come," what awaits us? Peter addresses this in his second letter:

> First of all, you must understand that in the last days scoffers will come, scoffing and following their own evil desires. They will say, "Where is this 'coming' he promised? Ever since our fathers died, everything goes on as it has since the beginning of creation." (2 Peter 3:3-4)

The scoffers ask a fair question: Why the delay? The answer reveals God's patience:

> With the Lord a day is like a thousand years, and a thousand years are like a day. The Lord is not slow in keeping his promise, as some understand slowness. He is patient with you, not wanting anyone to perish, but everyone to come to repentance. (2 Peter 3:8-9)

> But the day of the Lord will come like a thief. The heavens will disappear with a roar; the elements will be destroyed by fire, and the earth and everything in it will be laid bare. (2 Peter 3:10)

**We Live Between Two Comings**

Jesus has already come once. The Anointed One was crucified for us. The Son of Man came in His incarnation, death, and resurrection. **Therefore, we are already living in the end times—the last days.**

His second coming will be sudden, like a thief in the night. There's no detailed timetable for it, not because God is keeping secrets, but because all the major prophecies have been fulfilled. We are in the final, climactic period of history.

**For Those Who Wait**

Like Daniel, we experience a slight delay too. But it's not truly a delay—it's the "final half-week," the brief remaining moment before Christ returns. For those suffering persecution, for those tempted to abandon faith, the promise stands: **Help is coming. Hold on just a little while longer.** It's a very short wait, though it may not feel that way.

Some of you have brought friends to faith. Others, like Danny, keep praying faithfully for family members who don't yet believe. Still others stand boldly for Christ at university, witnessing from a milk crate, so to speak. Follow that example.

**Live as people who know their King is coming.** Don't let Him catch you with your hand in the cookie jar. Be holy, be godly, be ready. The time is sooner than you think.
