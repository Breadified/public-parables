# Jonah 1:17-2:10

**Title:** Salvation is from the Lord
**Date:** July 31, 2015
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## A Near Drowning

I nearly drowned once, back when I was in Year 7 at Pennant Hills High—newly arrived in Australia and still finding my way. The school held its annual swimming competition at Galston Pool, a full 50-meter facility with depths of over 2 meters.

When asked if I could swim, I confidently said yes. I'd taken swimming lessons in the local children's pool, managing the breast stroke across its width. How hard could it be?

But stepping up to that Olympic-sized pool, surrounded by students diving and swimming freestyle across its length, reality hit differently. I'd never swum that far, never dived before, never been in water this deep. Everyone else seemed to know what they were doing, so I jumped in.

I lifted my arm to stroke and immediately sank like a stone.

Drowning isn't what you see on television. There's no time to raise your hand. Your lungs have no capacity to shout for help. You simply sink, silently, into the depths. It was only seconds before half my class jumped in to rescue me—embarrassingly, tiny Year 7s pulling me from the water. I spent the rest of that summer taking lessons in the children's pool.

Jonah needed swimming lessons too. He is drowning.

## Going Down: The Descent into Death

Read Jonah's own words:

> You hurled me into the depths, into the very heart of the seas; and the currents swirled about me; all your waves and breakers swept over me.

Immediately after being thrown into the sea, Jonah finds himself in the "depths," in the "very heart of the seas." The churning chaos gives way to crushing silence as he sinks deeper.

> The engulfing waters threatened me, the deep surrounded me; seaweed was wrapped around my head.

The imagery is one of being progressively buried—engulfed, surrounded, bound. Down he sinks until reaching the very bottom:

> To the roots of the mountains I sank down; the earth beneath barred me in forever.

The continental shelf drops away into the abyss. Jonah is literally sinking.

In fact, throughout the narrative, we see a consistent downward trajectory. In 1:3, Jonah "goes down" to Joppa. In 1:5, he "goes down" into the ship. And now, he sinks down to the roots of the mountains. From the moment he disobeyed God, he has been descending.

But this isn't merely a physical descent. This is spiritual death. Jonah's own prayer reveals it:

> From deep in the realm of the dead I called for help.

Sheol. The grave. The place of the dead. A place where he cries:

> I have been banished from your sight.

The fish becomes the continuation of death itself. There is a haunting parallel between the "belly of Sheol" and the "belly of the fish" (1:17). The great fish is less a rescue and more a coffin—a tomb in the sea. Because of his disobedience, Jonah goes down and down and down.

This is what Scripture teaches about the consequence of sin. As Paul writes:

> You were dead in your transgressions and sins... all of us also lived among them at one time, gratifying the cravings of our flesh and following its desires and thoughts. Like the rest, we were by nature deserving of wrath. (Ephesians 2:1, 3)

We are all going down deservedly to death.

### The Inevitable End

Consider the arc of human life. By senior high school, we reach our tallest height. From that moment, we begin our descent. Our spinal and knee cartilages disintegrate. Our spine bends. We stoop. Eventually we lower ourselves into a wheelchair. Then we lie bedridden. Finally, we're placed flat in our coffins, lowered deep into the ground. We decompose and become worm food, then fish food. The cycle continues.

Friends, this is our ultimate fate because of our collective guilt. This is the price of sin. God made us. He told us lovingly the best way to live. And we ignore Him. We blame Him. We curse Him. We run from Him. We sin against one another. What could God possibly do but let us descend to the bottom of the ocean?

## Going Up: Deliverance and Hope

But there is hope for us.

From his desperate prison, Jonah prays:

> In my distress I called to the Lord, and he answered me. From deep in the realm of the dead I called for help, and you listened to my cry.

Jonah had countless opportunities to pray but refused them. Now, with no other choice, from the very pit of death, he calls out. From the bottom, he looks up.

> I said, 'I have been banished from your sight; yet I will look again toward your holy temple.'

Jerusalem's temple stands high on a plateau. You must always look upward to see it—constant elevation toward the sacred. And again he cries:

> When my life was ebbing away, I remembered you, Lord, and my prayer rose to you, to your holy temple.

God's presence dwells in the temple. In the Old Testament, the temple is where the Lord hears the prayers of His people. And God answered. He listened to Jonah's cry.

### Salvation Comes from the Lord

The contrast is stark:

> The earth beneath barred me in forever. But you, Lord my God, brought my life up from the pit.

From death, Jonah rises. And in his deliverance, he makes a remarkable declaration:

> Those who cling to worthless idols turn away from God's love for them. But I, with shouts of grateful praise, will sacrifice to you. What I have vowed I will make good. I will say, 'Salvation comes from the Lord.'

And the Lord commanded the fish, and it vomited Jonah onto dry land.

### Application: Through Jesus, Our True Temple

There is only one way out of death: through God. We must cry out to Him to rescue us. We look upward toward His temple.

But the temple is no longer in Jerusalem. We pray to God through the true temple—Jesus Christ (John 2). Jesus is the temple that was destroyed, that lay in the belly of death for three days. And on that third day, He defeated death. He rose from the grave and ascended to heaven.

When we trust Jesus, God promises to lift us up to where He is seated:

> But because of his great love for us, God, who is rich in mercy, made us alive with Christ even when we were dead in our transgressions—it is by grace you have been saved. And God raised us up with Christ and seated us with him in the heavenly realms. (Ephesians 2:4-6)

If you haven't yet surrendered your life to Christ, if you're uncertain about your eternal destination, call out to God for help. He will answer you. That is the promise of salvation—it comes from the Lord.

Even though we deserve to go down, there is a way up. Put your trust in Him. Call upon Him. Begin your journey up.

## My Final Trip

That summer after my near-drowning, I made a decision. I would not end my school swimming years by going down. I had realized two things: I now lived on an island where swimming was essential, and I'd been humiliated. I resolved to learn properly.

I found a patient teacher who invested weeks in me. In those two weeks, I went from drowning to swimming. I was down, but someone lifted me up. Remarkably, I was the only one from that entire group to make the transition.

My final swim at that school was upward—across the pool and out onto dry land. And as a Christian, my final journey out of this world will also be upward. I will not be mere fish food. Through Christ, I will be seated in the heavenly realms.

Where are you going? Will your last trip be upward or downward?

Friends, call on the Lord.
