# Romans 7:14-25

**Title:** Fully Rely on God
**Date:** 23 June 2017
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## The Christian's Struggle with Sin (7:14-20)

Paul describes a universal experience that every believer knows intimately: the painful gap between what we want to do and what we actually do.

> We know that the law is spiritual; but I am unspiritual, sold as a slave to sin. I do not understand what I do. For what I want to do I do not do, but what I hate I do. (Romans 7:14-15)

This verse captures the heart of the struggle. We intend to obey—to honor our parents, to be generous, to love one another, to guard our minds—yet we find ourselves doing the opposite. We procrastinate. We give in to laziness, fear, selfishness, or weakness. It's like holding a remote control car that responds opposite to every command: turn left and it goes right; go forward and it goes backward. Something is fundamentally broken.

Paul goes deeper into the nature of this struggle:

> And if I do what I do not want to do, I agree that the law is good. As it is, it is no longer I myself who do it, but it is sin living in me. For I know that good itself does not dwell in me, that is, in my sinful nature. For I have the desire to do what is good, but I cannot carry it out. For I do not do the good I want to do, but the evil I do not want to do—this I keep on doing. Now if I do what I do not want to do, it is no longer I who do it, but it is sin living in me that does it. (Romans 7:16-20)

The picture is vivid: our mind—like a rider—knows where it wants to go, but our body—like an elephant—won't cooperate. Our sinful nature is not some external force possessing us; it is ourselves. We are our own worst enemy.

### Understanding Our Responsibility

This passage isn't claiming we're not responsible for our sin. Rather, it's saying that we are held captive by our own desires: our fear of missing out, our selfishness, our laziness, our weakness, our forgetfulness, our apathy. These aren't excuses—they're descriptions of the human condition apart from God's transforming power.

The solution requires a conversion of the mind first, while our bodies remain works in progress. As Paul cries out:

> What a wretched man I am! Who will rescue me from this body that is subject to death? Thanks be to God, who delivers me through Jesus Christ our Lord! So then, I myself in my mind am a slave to God's law, but in my sinful nature a slave to the law of sin. (Romans 7:24-25)

The answer is not self-help or willpower—it is to **fully rely on God**.

## The Christian's Victory Over Sin (7:21-8:2)

A natural question arises: if we're still struggling with sin, what makes us Christian? Why follow Jesus if we're not freed from this battle?

Paul provides the crucial distinction:

> So I find this law at work: Although I want to do good, evil is right there with me. For in my inner being I delight in God's law; but I see another law at work in me, waging war against the law of my mind and making me a prisoner of the law of sin at work within me. (Romans 7:21-23)

The defining difference between a Christian and those without Christ is not whether we sin—all people sin. Rather, it's about our desire and awareness. Christians are those who **don't want to sin anymore**. We've tasted the effects of sin; we've seen it brings death. We're struggling precisely because we want something better, even when the struggle is hard. We've become conscious of our wound and are seeking healing.

### The Misconception About Christians

There's a false image of Christianity in the world: Christians are supposedly "good people" who succeed through moral superiority, yet when the world sees our failures, they dismiss us as hypocrites and judgment-mongers.

But this passage reframes everything. We are not saved by our good works but by fully relying on God through Jesus Christ. We're not better people—we're wounded people who've found the hospital and now invite other wounded people to come get help. Our struggle doesn't disqualify us; it identifies us.

In the words of Martin Luther, reflecting on an ancient painting: one side showed calm, serene figures in fine clothes with halos—what the world assumed were the righteous. The other side showed desperate, struggling people crying out to God. Luther inverted this image. The true Christian in this lifetime is the one struggling—the one who realizes the depth of their guilt, who knows perfection still awaits them, who tries each day to live God's way, sometimes failing, sometimes succeeding.

The serenity of the world comes from doing whatever you feel like, with no standards but your own that you can change at will. The struggle of the Christian comes from pursuing something greater than yourself.

### Identifying as a Christian

If you're uncertain whether you're truly Christian, ask yourself: Are you struggling? Do you genuinely want to follow God? Then you are a Christian. Rely on God.

This is the mark of the true believer—not the absence of struggle, but the presence of desire. Like a student at school who succeeds not through complacency but through humility, realizing their need for help and persisting despite failures. Like those in Bible study who gather regularly to understand God's will and grow more godly each day.

If you're struggling, keep seeking God's Word. If you're new here, join us. The greatest strength is not having it all figured out—it's admitting you need help and turning to the One who can transform you.
