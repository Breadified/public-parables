# Luke 19:1-10

**Title:** Riches Found: How to be Rich — Rich in Humility
**Date:** 21 April 2024
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## 1. Seeking Jesus (1-4)

> 1 Jesus entered Jericho and was passing through. 2 A man was there by the name of Zacchaeus; he was a chief tax collector and was wealthy.

Jesus approaches Jericho, the last stop before Jerusalem—a city steeped in biblical significance. In ancient times, Jericho was the first city Israel conquered from the Canaanites, its walls famously falling to the sound of trumpets. By Jesus' day, it served as a crucial waypoint between the Jordan River and Jerusalem.

A man named Zacchaeus lived there. His name, derived from the Hebrew *zaccai*, means "pure" or "righteous"—an ironic designation for a man whose occupation placed him at the bottom of Jewish society. As a chief tax collector, Zacchaeus was doubly despised: he was a Jew working for Rome, a traitor collecting taxes from his own people and enriching himself through extortion. With Roman power backing him, he could demand whatever he wished. His wealth reflects not virtue but his position of corrupt authority.

Consider how people viewed tax collectors in Jesus' time:

> 7 All the people saw this and began to mutter, "He has gone to be the guest of a sinner."

The Pharisees were even more explicit:

> Luke 18:11 The Pharisee stood by himself and prayed: "God, I thank you that I am not like other people—robbers, evildoers, adulterers—or even like this tax collector."

Yet the tax collector himself understood his condition:

> Luke 18:13 "But the tax collector stood at a distance. He would not even look up to heaven, but beat his breast and said, 'God, have mercy on me, a sinner.'"

Despite his wealth and status, Zacchaeus was spiritually impoverished. His position of power had twisted him. He needed transformation.

> 3 He wanted to see who Jesus was, but because he was short he could not see over the crowd.

The Greek word for "short" carries multiple layers of meaning. It can mean literally small in stature, but also carries the sense of being young, immature, or—symbolically—falling short of God's favor. Like the rich young ruler before him, Zacchaeus was blocked from reaching Jesus. His shortcomings—both literal and spiritual—stood in his way.

Yet notice: he *sought* Jesus. This wasn't mere curiosity about a traveling rabbi. The Greek word for "seeking" echoes Jesus' own purpose, revealed later in verse 10: to "seek and save the lost." Zacchaeus, despite his desperation, was genuinely reaching out.

> 4 So he ran ahead and climbed a sycamore-fig tree to see him, since Jesus was coming that way.

With remarkable humility, this wealthy chief tax collector did something undignified. He ran. He climbed a tree like a child. He overcame his pride and social standing to position himself to encounter Jesus. His desperation broke through his dignity.

## 2. Welcoming Jesus (5-8)

> 5 When Jesus reached the spot, he looked up and said to him, "Zacchaeus, come down immediately. I must stay at your house today." 6 So he came down at once and welcomed him gladly.

What follows is remarkable. Jesus doesn't just see Zacchaeus—he knows his name. Jesus calls him to come down, and Zacchaeus obeys immediately. More than that, he welcomes Jesus with gladness. The transformation is already beginning.

This echoes the deepest promise of the gospel:

> Revelation 3:20 Here I am! I stand at the door and knock. If anyone hears my voice and opens the door, I will come in and eat with that person, and they with me.

To share a meal in that culture was to offer acceptance, forgiveness, and friendship. Jesus' decision to dine at Zacchaeus' house was radical—the religious leaders were appalled—but it was also the essence of grace.

> 8 But Zacchaeus stood up and said to the Lord, "Look, Lord! Here and now I give half of my possessions to the poor, and if I have cheated anybody out of anything, I will pay back four times the amount."

Here we see the fruit of encounter with Jesus: genuine repentance. Jewish law required that someone who had defrauded others make full restitution plus a twenty percent penalty:

> Leviticus 6:5 They must make restitution in full, add a fifth of the value to it and give it all to the owner on the day they present their guilt offering.

Zacchaeus exceeds the requirement dramatically. He pays back *four times* what he took—400 percent restitution instead of 125 percent. And he gives half his remaining possessions to the poor, echoing the rich young ruler whom Jesus had just challenged:

> Luke 18:22 When Jesus heard this, he said to him, "You still lack one thing. Sell everything you have and give to the poor, and you will have treasure in heaven. Then come, follow me."

Where the rich young ruler had turned away in sorrow, Zacchaeus responds with radical generosity.

Repentance is not merely feeling sorry. It means turning a new leaf, making amends, converting our hearts and our wallets. For many of us, the wallet is the last thing to be converted—we must yield our relationship with money, time, talents, and resources to God's kingdom purposes.

> Ephesians 4:28 Anyone who has been stealing must steal no longer, but must work, doing something useful with their own hands, that they may have something to share with those in need.

This is rich in deeds. This is rich in relationships. This is what it means to be truly rich.

## 3. Jesus Welcomes You (9-10)

> 9 Jesus said to him, "Today salvation has come to this house, because this man, too, is a son of Abraham. 10 For the Son of Man came to seek and to save the lost."

Salvation has come. Not simply to Zacchaeus as an individual, but to his entire household. And in calling him "a son of Abraham," Jesus restores his identity and his standing before God. He is no longer an outcast—he is part of God's covenant family.

Throughout the preceding chapters, Jesus has been teaching about the lost through parables. Now, in Zacchaeus, the parables become flesh and blood:

In the parable of the Lost Son, the father finally finds his wayward child. In Zacchaeus, we witness a literal *lost and found*.

In the parable of the Dishonest Manager, we meet a man who recognizes he is in trouble, takes initiative despite shame, risks everything by climbing a tree, and finds an unlikely friend to invite home. Zacchaeus is that man.

In the parable of the Rich Man and Lazarus, the rich man remains forever separated from Abraham by a great chasm. Yet here is Zacchaeus, wealthy and corrupt, now declared to be "a son of Abraham"—he has crossed the chasm that seemed impassable.

In sharp contrast to the rich young ruler, for whom Jesus declared it harder for a camel to pass through the eye of a needle than for a rich man to enter the kingdom, Zacchaeus somehow squeezed through:

> Luke 18:25 Indeed, it is easier for a camel to go through the eye of a needle than for someone who is rich to enter the kingdom of God." 26 Those who heard this asked, "Who then can be saved?" 27 Jesus replied, "What is impossible with man is possible with God."

From the parable of the Pharisee and the Tax Collector, we know what salvation looks like for a tax collector: humility. And Zacchaeus, despite his wealth and position, ran and climbed a tree without shame, like a little child.

And from the parable of the Ten Minas, Zacchaeus becomes the servant who uses his resources wisely, of whom the king declares: "Well done, good and faithful servant!"

With Zacchaeus' salvation, Jesus completes his teaching ministry. He now enters Jerusalem to fulfill what these parables have been promising. He goes to his death so that the "checks of salvation" he has been writing throughout his journey might finally be cashed out.

**He did this for Zacchaeus. And he did this for you.**

To those of us who have been saved—who have been given much through Christian heritage, faithful mentors, and God's sustaining grace—the challenge is clear:

> Matthew 3:8 Produce fruit in keeping with repentance.

Much has been given to us. Much is therefore expected. Where are the minas? Are we living for ourselves or for God's kingdom? Where do we need to repent and realign our resources, time, and talents toward eternal purposes?

To those who have not yet given their allegiance to Jesus, the word is urgent. The world offers many delights to the wealthy, and with enough money, you may not feel the sting of sin—until it's too late.

But Jesus offers what the world cannot: eternal life, objective truth, guidance in a turbulent world, forgiveness of sins, and reconciliation with your Creator.

Jesus beckons you now. Climb down from your tree. Invite him into your home. He came to seek and save the lost. He came to seek and save *you*.

God is merciful. If you seek him, he will answer.
