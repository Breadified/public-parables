# Amos 4:1–5:17

**Title:** Judgment on the Wealthy & God's Call to Repentance

**Date:** October 24, 2021 (ARISE); Draft November 22, 2015 (SMAC)

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## The Indictment: Cows of Bashan

> Hear this word, you cows of Bashan on Mount Samaria, you women who oppress the poor and crush the needy and say to your husbands, "Bring us some drinks!" (Amos 4:1)

God opens His case against Israel with a startling image. The wealthy women of the northern kingdom—living in luxury in Samaria and Bashan, the most fertile region—are compared to well-fed cattle. They grow fat through the oppression of the poor, literally crushing the vulnerable beneath them while demanding indulgence from their husbands. This is not merely economic inequality; it is systemic injustice wrapped in comfort and entitlement.

---

## The Shocking Command: Go and Sin

> "Go to Bethel and sin; go to Gilgal and sin yet more... Bring your sacrifices every morning, your tithes every three years. Burn leavened bread as a thank offering and brag about your freewill offerings—boast about them, you Israelites, for this is what you love to do," declares the Sovereign Lord. (Amos 4:4–5)

God's command sounds impossible—until we understand it as divine irony. He is not granting permission to sin. Rather, He is saying: continue in your false religion, your meaningless rituals, your performative worship. Go to the shrines at Bethel and Gilgal—places laden with history (where Abraham built altars, where Israel crossed the Jordan)—and corrupt them further with empty ceremonies.

This is God's way of exposing their hypocrisy. They bring sacrifices and tithes while their hearts remain hardened toward justice. They boast of their generosity while trampling the poor. Their religion has become a substitute for righteousness.

### False Religion in Our Time

The same pattern persists today, wearing different masks:
- **God as nature:** Climate activism replacing the Creator with creation
- **Scripture as social media:** News outlets and mainstream narratives becoming our authoritative voice
- **Ethics without God:** Sexual permissiveness divorced from moral restraint
- **The religion of the elite:** A sophisticated rebellion that feels enlightened

When we pursue these empty religions—whether ancient idolatry or modern alternatives—we are essentially saying: *Go and sin yet more.* We indulge in our preferred vices while congratulating ourselves on our values.

## The Pattern of Judgment: Suffering as God's Megaphone

> The Sovereign Lord has sworn by his holiness: "The time will surely come when you will be taken away with hooks, the last of you with fishhooks." (Amos 4:2)

The image is brutal. Like cattle led to slaughter with hooks through their noses, Israel will be dragged away into exile. But before that final judgment comes a series of escalating warnings:

> I gave you empty stomachs in every city and lack of bread in every town, yet you have not returned to me. (Amos 4:6)

> I also withheld rain from you when the harvest was still three months away. (Amos 4:7)

> Many times I struck your gardens and vineyards, destroying them with blight and mildew. Locusts devoured your fig and olive trees, yet you have not returned to me. (Amos 4:9)

> I sent plagues among you as I did to Egypt. I killed your young men with the sword, along with your captured horses. I filled your nostrils with the stench of your camps, yet you have not returned to me. (Amos 4:10)

> I overthrew some of you as I overthrew Sodom and Gomorrah. You were like a burning stick snatched from the fire, yet you have not returned to me. (Amos 4:11)

The refrain is relentless: **"Yet you have not returned to me."**

God sends famine, drought, agricultural devastation, war, plague, and near-total destruction. Each affliction is designed to awaken them, to call them back. Suffering, in God's economy, is not meaningless cruelty—it is His megaphone, speaking truth to those too deaf to hear His gentle voice.

### Pain as Gift

This truth offends our modern sensibilities, yet it is profound. Dr. Paul Brand, a missionary physician who dedicated his life to leprosy patients, discovered something counterintuitive: leprosy doesn't attack the skin—it attacks the nervous system. Leprosy victims lose their ability to feel pain, and because they cannot sense injury, they destroy their own bodies through constant, unnoticed damage. They lose fingers and toes not from the disease itself, but from the absence of pain's protective warning.

**Pain is a gift.** It alerts us to danger. It preserves us from greater harm.

When we experience suffering—poverty, loss, injustice, consequences of our choices—we face a choice. We can either:

1. **Learn from it** and turn back to God, or
2. **Harden ourselves further** and face escalating judgment

Some of us will only learn through pain. We are stubborn, arrogant, perhaps too intelligent for our own good. Rather than heed God's whisper, we require His shout. As parents know, sometimes a child must touch a hot stove to learn not to touch it.

## The Devastation: Israel Decimated

> Fallen is Virgin Israel, never to rise again, deserted in her own land, with no one to lift her up. (Amos 5:2)

> Your city that marches out a thousand strong will have only a hundred left; your town that marches out a hundred strong will have only ten left. (Amos 5:3)

The prophetic word is categorical: Israel's military might will be decimated. A thousand soldiers reduced to one hundred. Complete collapse. The nation that once conquered the promised land will be conquered herself. This is not speculation—it is historical fact. In 722 BC, Assyria invaded the northern kingdom and deported its people, effectively ending Israel's existence as a sovereign nation.

## The Alternative: Seek Good, Not Evil

Yet even in judgment, God offers a way out—not through more ritual, but through radical repentance:

> Seek me and live; do not seek Bethel, do not go to Gilgal, do not journey to Beersheba. For Gilgal will surely go into exile, and Bethel will be reduced to nothing. Seek the Lord and live. (Amos 5:4–6)

The choice is starkly presented. Abandon your false shrines. Stop seeking meaning in empty religion. Instead:

> Seek good, not evil, that you may live. Then the Lord God Almighty will be with you, just as you say he is. Hate evil, love good; maintain justice in the courts. Perhaps the Lord God Almighty will have mercy on the remnant of Joseph. (Amos 5:14–15)

This is not a complicated formula. It requires:
- **Seeking God himself,** not religious performance
- **Active pursuit of justice,** especially for the vulnerable in legal systems
- **Hatred of evil,** not merely disapproval
- **Love of good,** not mere tolerance of it

## The Paradox of Judgment

Here lies a tension we must sit with honestly: While God sent these afflictions as warnings, the Israelites' response was not change but hardening. Their suffering did not produce repentance. Why?

Romans 1 offers insight into this pattern:

> God gave them over in the sinful desires of their hearts to sexual impurity for the degrading of their bodies with one another. They exchanged the truth about God for a lie, and worshiped and served created things rather than the Creator... God gave them over to shameful lusts... God gave them over to a depraved mind. (Romans 1:24–28)

The phrase "God gave them over" is crucial. It describes God's judgment of allowing people to fully experience the consequences of their rebellion. Like someone continuing to smoke despite every warning, each refusal to turn back deepens the habit. God does not force repentance; He respects human choice, even unto destruction.

## Application: What Suffering Reveals

When hardship comes—and it comes to all of us—we must ask: What is God trying to tell me?

Every occasion of suffering is an invitation to return. Every consequence is a corrective mercy. Some will learn through gentle discipline. Others require the school of hard experience.

For those willing to listen, the path is clear:
1. **Confess sin** rather than defend it
2. **Return to God** rather than run from Him
3. **Seek justice** rather than comfort
4. **Love good** rather than merely avoid evil

For those who refuse, there remains only the trajectory of judgment—the inexorable movement toward the edge of the cliff, one step at a time.

Yet even in this dark passage, grace echoes: *Perhaps the Lord God Almighty will have mercy on the remnant of Joseph.* The word "perhaps" is not uncertainty on God's part. It is an invitation to those still capable of hearing His voice. The door remains open, even as judgment approaches.

---

## The Call to Courage

As we emerge from seasons of isolation and testing, we need courage—not the bravado of human strength, but the quiet resolve of those who walk with Christ:

> Courage to take one step at a time; today is my gift, not tomorrow.
> Courage to walk with Christ in my sight, through paths strewn with laughter and sorrow.
> The past won't have a hold on me. Through loss and pain my hope remains—the power of Christ at work in me.

We choose daily whether to turn back to God or continue in our rebellion. The choice is ours. The consequences are certain. The mercy is real for those who repent.
