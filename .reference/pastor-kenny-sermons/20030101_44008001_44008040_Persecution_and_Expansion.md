# Acts 8:1-40

**Title:** Persecution and Expansion
**Date:** 22 January 2003
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Persecution Begins

Stephen's martyrdom last week marked the first great trial for the early church. As Acts 8:1 tells us:

> And Saul was there, giving approval to his death. On that day a great persecution broke out against the church at Jerusalem, and all except the apostles were scattered throughout Judea and Samaria.

The apostles remained in Jerusalem to face the persecution directly, but the believers were scattered—like embers from a kicked fire that spread the flames to new places. Though Saul sought to destroy the church by dragging believers from their homes and imprisoning them, God used this very persecution to accomplish His purpose.

> Those who had been scattered preached the word wherever they went. (8:4)

The gospel could not be contained. Instead of being extinguished, it spread outward in all directions, fulfilling Jesus's promise in Acts 1:8 that His witnesses would reach Jerusalem, Judea, Samaria, and the ends of the earth.

## The Gospel Reaches Samaria

### Historical Context: A Divided People

Samaria represents the Northern Kingdom of Israel, which split from Judah after King Solomon's reign. The Northern Kingdom fell to the Assyrians in the eighth century BC, while Judah was later exiled by Babylon in the sixth century BC.

The crucial difference lay in the invaders' policies. The Babylonians removed Judah's leaders into exile but later allowed them to return intact under Persian rule. The Assyrians, however, followed a policy of intermarriage—deliberately mixing the Israelite population with other nations to eliminate national identity. The result was a hybrid people who no longer bore the name Israel but were called Samaritans, and they followed a hybrid religion mixing Abraham's faith with idolatry. They accepted the first five books of Scripture but rejected the prophets and worshipped at their own temple on Mount Gerizim.

This created deep animosity between Jews and Samaritans. Religious division combined with centuries of bad blood meant that Jews considered any contact with Samaritans unthinkable. Those traveling from south to north would deliberately detour across the river and back simply to avoid them. The Parable of the Good Samaritan and Jesus's encounter with the Samaritan woman at the well demonstrate how radical it was for Jesus to break these barriers.

Yet now, the gospel was reaching Samaria—the very people the Jews despised.

### Philip's Ministry in Samaria

Philip, one of the seven chosen to serve tables in the church, went down to Samaria and proclaimed Christ. His message brought remarkable signs:

> When the crowds heard Philip and saw the miraculous signs he did, they all paid close attention to what he said. With shrieks, evil spirits came out of many, and many paralytics and cripples were healed. So there was great joy in that city. (8:6-8)

The healings pointed to Jesus's fulfillment of Old Testament predictions. The great joy erupted because these despised, half-breed Samaritans were being welcomed into God's kingdom.

### The Apostolic Confirmation

When the apostles in Jerusalem heard that Samaria had accepted God's word, they sent Peter and John to confirm this remarkable development:

> When they arrived, they prayed for them that they might receive the Holy Spirit, because the Holy Spirit had not yet come upon any of them; they had simply been baptized into the name of the Lord Jesus. Then Peter and John placed their hands on them, and they received the Holy Spirit. (8:15-17)

This moment represents the second "booster rocket" propelling the gospel outward—the second of four great baptisms of the Holy Spirit that marked the expansion of the church according to Acts 1:8.

### A Deeper Significance: Reunion

Why did God choose Samaria, of all places, for the second great outpouring of the Spirit? It seems strange to pick a people so despised and numerically insignificant. Yet the answer lies in Old Testament prophecy.

Just as Pentecost had united Jews from every tongue and nation, this Samaritan outpouring reunited the divided house of Israel. The prophet Isaiah had long prophesied this reunion:

> In that day the Lord will reach out his hand a second time to reclaim the remnant that is left of his people from Assyria, from Lower Egypt, from Upper Egypt, from Cush, from Elam, from Babylonia, from Hamath and from the islands of the sea. He will raise a banner for the nations and gather the exiles of Israel; he will assemble the scattered people of Judah from the four quarters of the earth. Ephraim's jealousy will vanish, and Judah's enemies will be cut off; Ephraim will not be jealous of Judah, nor Judah hostile toward Ephraim. (Isaiah 11:11-13)

Just as the Berlin Wall once divided East and West Germany—dividing families where no division should exist—Israel and Judah had been torn apart. The reunion brought incomparable joy. The house of Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob was never meant to be divided. When brought together at last, it symbolized restoration and healing.

## Simon the Magician: The Danger of Spiritual Ambition

### The Rise of Simon

In Samaria, there lived a man named Simon who had long practiced sorcery and amazed the people:

> Now for some time a man named Simon had practiced sorcery in the city and amazed all the people of Samaria. He boasted that he was someone great, and all the people, both high and low, gave him their attention and exclaimed, "This man is the divine power known as the Great Power." (8:9-10)

Simon was the David Copperfield of the ancient world. Whether his powers were genuine supernatural abilities or clever illusions, the Scripture makes clear that sorcery itself was forbidden in Israel. The Old Testament contains repeated prohibitions:

> Do not allow a sorceress to live. (Exodus 22:18)

> Do not practice divination or sorcery. (Leviticus 19:26)

> Let no one be found among you who practices divination or sorcery, interprets omens, engages in witchcraft, or casts spells, or who is a medium or spiritist or who consults the dead. (Deuteronomy 18:9-11)

The underlying issue is this: Magic represents an attempt to manipulate elements or spirits apart from God's sovereignty. If such powers are genuine, they represent a rebellion against God's exclusive authority. If they are fraudulent, they represent charlatans deceiving the weak and gullible. Either way, they are forbidden.

God's guidance comes through His word alone—not through superstition, divination, or any other means. As Moses told Israel:

> The LORD your God will raise up for you a prophet like me from among your own brothers. You must listen to him. (Deuteronomy 18:15)

### Simon's Apparent Conversion

When Philip preached the gospel and demonstrated genuine signs of God's kingdom, Simon believed and was baptized:

> But when they believed Philip as he preached the good news of the kingdom of God and the name of Jesus Christ, they were baptized, both men and women. Simon himself believed and was baptized. And he followed Philip everywhere, astonished by the great signs and miracles he saw. (8:12-13)

Yet his heart remained unchanged. When Peter and John laid hands on the Samaritans to bestow the Holy Spirit, Simon's true nature was revealed:

> When Simon saw that the Spirit was given at the laying on of the apostles' hands, he offered them money and said, "Give me also this ability so that everyone on whom I lay my hands may receive the Holy Spirit." (8:18-19)

### Peter's Rebuke

Simon fundamentally misunderstood what he had witnessed. The gift of the Holy Spirit is not an ability to be purchased or manipulated. It is not repeatable at human command. Yet Simon's attempt to buy this power revealed his heart.

Peter's response was unsparing:

> May your money perish with you, because you thought you could buy the gift of God with money! You have no part or share in this ministry of the word, because your heart is not right before God. Repent of this wickedness and pray to the Lord. Perhaps he will forgive you for having such a thought in your heart. For I see that you are full of bitterness and captive to sin. (8:20-23)

The diagnosis was clear: Simon's heart was not right. He was full of bitterness and enslaved to sin. He sought power not to glorify God but to be great himself—to manipulate his audience just as he had done with his magic. Like Jezebel of old, who led Israel into idolatry and witchcraft, Simon represented the danger of spiritual pride and the pursuit of power for its own sake.

Yet Peter also held out hope: "Perhaps he will forgive you for having such a thought in your heart." Repentance remained possible, though the text leaves Simon's ultimate response ambiguous:

> Then Simon answered, "Pray to the Lord for me so that nothing you have said may happen to me." (8:24)

### A Warning for Leaders

This account serves as a cautionary tale for all Christian leaders. There is a constant temptation to seek position for the sake of advancement—to pursue leadership in great churches for the networking opportunities they provide, to use ministry as a platform for personal influence. Such conflict of interest reveals a heart not right before God.

Philip, by contrast, serves as the model. Unlike Simon, who sought to be great, Philip waited on tables in humble service.

## Philip and the Ethiopian Eunuch: The Outsider Welcomed

### Philip: A Prophet-Like Messenger

The narrative shifts now to Philip himself, and we discover he is far more than a table-waiter. An angel of the Lord directed him to an unusual encounter:

> Now an angel of the Lord said to Philip, "Go south to the road—the desert road—that goes down from Jerusalem to Gaza." So he started out, and on his way he met an Ethiopian eunuch, an important official in charge of all the treasury of Candace, queen of the Ethiopians. (8:26-27)

Philip's character and calling deserve closer attention. He was one of the seven chosen specifically because he was "full of the Spirit"—meaning his character aligned with the fruit of the Spirit. Now we see him characterized by rapid movement, divine direction, and miraculous transportation. Scholars have noted striking parallels between Philip and the prophet Elijah:

- Both are associated with Samaria
- Both are divinely directed to out-of-the-way places (Philip to the desert road; Elijah to Zarephath)
- Both move with supernatural speed (Elijah running before Ahab's chariot; Philip running to intercept the eunuch's chariot)
- Both are suddenly taken away by God's Spirit and found in another place (Philip appears at Azotus; Elijah taken to heaven in a whirlwind)

Like a new Elijah, Philip is divinely directed by God to accomplish His purposes. This connection is significant: Elijah anointed Jehu as king to destroy Jezebel and restore righteousness to Israel. Now Philip, in similar fashion, extends God's restoration to the ultimate outsider—the Ethiopian eunuch.

### The Eunuch: An Excluded Outsider

The eunuch represents one of Scripture's most poignant figures:

> So he started out, and on his way he met an Ethiopian eunuch, an important official in charge of all the treasury of Candace, queen of the Ethiopians. This man had gone to Jerusalem to worship. (8:27-28)

A eunuch was a castrated man, typically made so to serve a royal household faithfully. The eunuch in this account had achieved high position—he was treasurer of an entire nation. Yet despite his power and wealth, he was profoundly excluded from full participation in Israel's worship.

The law was explicit:

> No one who has been emasculated by crushing or cutting may enter the assembly of the LORD. (Deuteronomy 23:1)

This restriction aimed to preserve the holiness of God's people, yet it meant the eunuch could not enter the inner courts of the temple to offer sacrifice. He could journey to Jerusalem to worship—a remarkable commitment, given his situation—but only as a half-included participant, barred by his physical condition from the most intimate acts of worship.

On his way home, reading his Bible in his chariot, the eunuch was likely contemplating this very exclusion:

> And on his way home was sitting in his chariot reading the book of Isaiah the prophet. Then Philip ran up to the chariot and heard the man reading Isaiah the prophet. "Do you understand what you are reading?" Philip asked. "How can I," he said, "unless someone explains it to me?" (8:28-31)

### The Revelation: Jesus as the Suffering Servant

Philip climbed into the chariot, and the eunuch shared what he was reading:

> The eunuch was reading this passage of Scripture: "He was led like a sheep to the slaughter, and as a lamb before the shearer is silent, so he did not open his mouth. In his humiliation he was deprived of justice. Who can speak of his descendants? For his life was taken from the earth." (8:32-33)

These words from Isaiah 53:7-8 were reading directly to the eunuch's deepest pain. He asked Philip:

> "Tell me, please, who is the prophet talking about, himself or someone else?" (8:34)

Philip had the privilege of opening the eunuch's eyes to the gospel:

> Then Philip began with that very passage of Scripture and told him the good news about Jesus. (8:35)

Consider the revelation the eunuch received. The passage spoke of one who was led like a sheep to slaughter, who did not open his mouth in protest, who was deprived of justice, and who had no descendants. The eunuch had lived all his life contemplating this last phrase—"Who can speak of his descendants?"—because his condition meant he could never have children. His sadness was the sadness Jesus bore.

Yet there was redemptive meaning. Because the eunuch himself could not offer sacrifice in the temple—could never do so due to his defect—he needed someone else to stand in his place and offer on his behalf. Jesus Christ had done exactly that. His body, broken on the cross, became the sacrifice that atoned for the eunuch's sins and all his defects. Through Christ's sacrifice, the way into God's presence was opened to this excluded man.

### The Promise Fulfilled

If the eunuch had read further in Isaiah, he would have found words written directly to him:

> Let no foreigner who has bound himself to the LORD say, "The LORD will surely exclude me from his people." And let not any eunuch complain, "I am only a dry tree." For this is what the LORD says: "To the eunuchs who keep my Sabbaths, who choose what pleases me and hold fast to my covenant—to them I will give within my temple and its walls a memorial and a name better than sons and daughters; I will give them an everlasting name that will not be cut off." (Isaiah 56:3-5)

And beyond the eunuchs:

> And foreigners who bind themselves to the LORD to serve him, to love the name of the LORD, and to worship him, all who keep the Sabbath without desecrating it and who hold fast to my covenant—these I will bring to my holy mountain and give them joy in my house of prayer. Their burnt offerings and sacrifices will be accepted on my altar; for my house will be called a house of prayer for all nations. (Isaiah 56:6-7)

The eunuch was being invited not merely to external observation but to true covenant relationship with God. What the Levitical law had forbidden—his presence in the inner temple—Jesus himself now made possible. He would inherit the temple in a far greater sense than any Levite. He was accepted, welcome, named, beloved.

### Baptism: The Sign of New Life

As they traveled and came to water, the eunuch recognized the significance of what he had learned:

> As they traveled along the road, they came to some water and the eunuch said, "Look, here is water. Why shouldn't I be baptized?" And he gave orders to stop the chariot. Then both Philip and the eunuch went down into the water and Philip baptized him. (8:36, 38)

Baptism became the symbol of the eunuch's new understanding and his faith in Christ. He had gone to Jerusalem to worship but was turned away at the temple gates. But on the way home, the Scripture came alive. The one he had been reading about—the Servant who died without descendants—had opened a way for him into God's own presence. He was no longer excluded. He was accepted, baptized into the family of Christ.

> When they came up out of the water, the Spirit of the Lord suddenly took Philip away, and the eunuch did not see him again, but went on his way rejoicing. Philip, however, appeared at Azotus and traveled about, preaching the gospel in all the towns until he reached Caesarea. (8:39-40)

### The African Church Is Born

The eunuch's conversion carries significance far beyond one man's spiritual journey. According to church tradition, this Ethiopian eunuch returned to Africa and became instrumental in establishing the Christian faith in Ethiopia—making him widely regarded as the founder of the Ethiopian church. The gospel, spreading from Jerusalem through persecution, had reached North Africa. A man excluded from the Jewish temple became the instrument through which Christ's name spread across a continent.

## The Real Heroes of This Story

In this chapter, we encounter three types of spiritual leaders. Simon represents the danger of spiritual ambition divorced from genuine devotion—the pursuit of power and influence disguised as faith. His story warns us against the corruption that comes when we seek greatness for ourselves rather than God's glory.

Philip represents faithful, humble service. He waited on tables before he proclaimed the gospel. He moved at God's direction, not his own agenda. He humbled himself before the eunuch, running to his chariot and sitting with him to explain Scripture. There was no pretense, no desire to make himself great.

But the real hero of this account is the eunuch himself—the one with enough courage and humility to admit, "I don't understand. Will you help me?" He had been excluded his entire life. He had journeyed to Jerusalem seeking fellowship with God only to be turned away. Yet when the Scripture was explained to him, he believed. He didn't hesitate or demand proof. He saw water and asked, "Why shouldn't I be baptized?" That faith—that willingness to follow Jesus despite a lifetime of exclusion—that required real courage.

## A Message for Today

As you reflect on this chapter, consider where you stand. Perhaps you feel excluded from God's presence—by past sins, by circumstances beyond your control, by the limitations of your flesh. The eunuch's story speaks directly to you.

God's kingdom is not a club that excludes based on physical condition or social status. Christ has paid the price for every barrier. Every defect, every failure, every shame—all have been cleansed away by His atoning work. You are accepted. You are named. You have a place in God's temple.

If you are not yet a Christian, recognize the defects in your life that separate you from God. Be grateful that Christ has already paid the price to open the way. Believe and be baptized into His family.

If you are already a Christian, remember that you have been given a message worth spreading. The eunuch went on his way rejoicing, and this good news spread to an entire continent. You, too, have been chosen to bear witness to the nations—not because of your greatness, but because Christ has made you clean and accepted.

This is the gospel: that God welcomes the excluded, restores the divided, and transforms the despised into beloved children of His kingdom. This is a message worth spreading to others.
