# Hebrews 12:18-29

**Title:** Persevering at Church
**Date:** 9 September 2018
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Scary Mountain (Verses 18-21)

The opening verses of this passage describe Mount Sinai at the moment God gave the Law to Israel. This was no ordinary meeting. The author paints a terrifying scene: a mountain that could not be touched, burning with fire, shrouded in darkness, gloom, and storm. There was thunder, lightning, and a trumpet blast so loud that those who heard God's voice begged Him to stop speaking. The fear was so overwhelming that even Moses said, "I am trembling with fear."

### Understanding Mount Sinai

Mount Sinai was likely a volcano. The Arabian Peninsula, where this occurred, sits on a major tectonic plate surrounded by fissures and pressure points. Volcanoes existed there in biblical times, and the description—fire, smoke, the whole mountain trembling—fits a volcanic eruption perfectly.

But it wasn't the physical fire and smoke that terrified the people. It was the voice of God speaking His commandments. God's word carries weight that shakes the soul. This makes sense when we consider the relationship between fearing God and knowing His word. Throughout Scripture, to listen to God is to fear Him:

> Fear the Lord and keep his commandments, for this is the whole duty of man. (Ecclesiastes 12:13)

> The word of God is alive and active. Sharper than any double-edged sword, it penetrates even to dividing soul and spirit, joints and marrow; it judges the thoughts and attitudes of the heart. (Hebrews 4:12)

### The Purpose of God's Law

God's word is terrifying precisely because it judges us. When God's word is taught, we feel exposed. We hide. We become defensive. Yet this is intentional—the law was designed to create fear in order to prevent sin:

> Moses said to the people, "Do not fear, for God has come to test you, that the fear of him may be before you, that you may not sin." (Exodus 20:20)

### A Modern Application: The Fear of God's Comprehensiveness

Many today are uncomfortable with God's complete authority. In our cancel culture, we try to limit and censor God's word. Sometimes we do this passively—by avoiding Scripture altogether. If I don't read the Bible, I don't have to fear what it demands of me. Never approaching the mountain means never having to tremble.

Other times we call God's word "legalism"—a pejorative label meant to discredit comprehensive obedience. But legalism, properly understood, is not the problem the Pharisees had. Jesus didn't criticize them for following too many laws. Rather, the Pharisees selectively obeyed Scripture while nullifying it through their traditions. They invented loopholes—like the *korban* practice—that allowed them to escape the demands of honoring their parents. They chose to follow some commands while ignoring others.

> "You have a fine way of setting aside the commands of God in order to observe your own traditions!" (Mark 7:9)

The Pharisees' real sin was lawlessness—they picked and chose which parts of God's word applied to them.

### God's Word Is Comprehensive

We must not be afraid of God's word, but we do need to understand its scope. God's word addresses every dimension of life:

- We're happy when God speaks about church, prayer, Bible reading, and giving to church. These feel safe and manageable.
- But we often resist when God's word speaks about our work, dating, sexuality, lifestyle, finances, politics, and how we spend our time. We want a church/state divide, as if God rules only on Sunday while we rule Monday through Saturday.

This is a concession to secularism. We've allowed the atheists to set the boundaries, telling us: "Keep your religion in your church. Leave the rest of the world to us."

But God's word is plenary—it has something to say about every decision we make:

> Nothing in all creation is hidden from God's sight. Everything is uncovered and laid bare before the eyes of him to whom we must give account. (Hebrews 4:12-13)

> The word of God will stand forever. (Isaiah 40:8)

God speaks to every person, in every season, about every area of life. His word applies to school students, young adults, the unmarried, the married, fathers and mothers, grandparents. It addresses all life stages and circumstances.

To call this "legalistic" is misguided. We should study God's law carefully and live by it with dedication. This is good. We are not restraining God's word—we're honoring it.

Notice how the atheist's social justice agenda shows no such hesitation. They apply their ideology everywhere: mathematics, geography, physical education, television, food choices, showers, cultural celebrations. Everything. Why should Christians limit God's word to Sunday?

## The Joyful Mountain (Verses 22-24)

But you have come to a different place—Mount Zion, the heavenly Jerusalem. This is not a mountain you approach with dread but with joy. Here is the dramatic contrast the author makes:

### What We Have Received

Instead of fire and terror, we now approach God's presence with:

- **Thousands upon thousands of angels in joyful assembly.** The angels rejoice over one sinner who repents. They celebrated Jesus's birth with praises of glory and peace.
- **The church of the firstborn, whose names are written in heaven.** We are enrolled in heaven like students registered for school, like athletes selected for the Olympics. Our names are permanently recorded.
- **God, the Judge of all.** He is there—not to condemn us, but to justify us.
- **The spirits of the righteous made perfect.** Those who came before us have reached their completion, and we will be perfected together with them.
- **Jesus, the mediator of a new covenant.** His blood speaks a better word than Abel's blood cried out for justice. Abel's blood demanded vengeance; Jesus's blood offers redemption.

### Why We Need Not Fear God's Law

This is why we can approach God's word without fear. The law is no longer scary because its judgment has been satisfied in Christ. God will not beat us with the law. The new covenant means righteousness comes from Him, not from our obedience alone.

But this doesn't mean we're free from the law—it means we're free to follow the law. When something becomes possible without threat, we pursue it fully.

Imagine metered internet that charged per gigabyte. You'd be cautious about what you download, afraid of exceeding your limit. But with unlimited data, you're free to explore fully. You're even obligated to find out what's available.

The same applies to God's word. We now follow Him out of joy and transformation, not external compulsion. Jesus endured the cross for the joy set before Him. If a medicine cured cancer, we wouldn't take only half the dose out of balance. We'd take the full dose, shower ourselves in it, inoculate ourselves.

God's word cures us. It protects us. It comforts us. It's good for us. So we must be willing to let go of the sins that entangle us—FOMO, pride, selfishness, idolatry, sexual immorality. When we release what destroys us, our hands are open to receive what God offers: opportunities for service, good works He's prepared for us, godliness, compassion, children, gratitude.

### The Promise Lived Out in Practice

Your name is enrolled in heaven—you're part of God's celestial team. Don't play as amateurs. The rules of church—attend regularly, bring your Bible, participate, stay connected—aren't meant to burden you but to give you a starting point and community for the race.

The rules of dating—pursue marriage, choose a Christian partner, maintain purity—aren't God spoiling your fun. They keep you holy, protecting both you and your future spouse from sin.

The challenge to raise children young and work hard isn't meant to ruin your life but to give you fulfillment.

We use what is temporary to build what is eternal. We transform the mortal into immortality, dust into permanence, judgment into salvation.

Yes, it's hard. But hard doesn't mean bad. When we follow God, hard actually results in good. We do this for His glory.

## Do Not Refuse Him (Verses 25-29)

> See to it that you do not refuse him who speaks. If they did not escape when they refused him who warned them on earth, how much less will we, if we turn away from him who warns us from heaven? (Hebrews 12:25)

The stakes have escalated. In the old covenant, the Israelites refused to hear God directly and asked Moses to mediate. They missed God's full revelation. Yet even that refusal had consequences—they never entered the promised land because of unbelief.

How much more serious is it for us to refuse Jesus? He comes from heaven, not earth. He is not a mediator between us and God—He is God. We hear Him directly. There's no excuse of "Moses didn't explain it properly." The responsibility is entirely on us.

### The Shaking

The old covenant brought a shaking:

> At that time his voice shook the earth, but now he has promised, "Once more I will shake not only the earth but also the heavens." (Hebrews 12:26)

> The words "once more" indicate the removing of what can be shaken—that is, created things—so that what cannot be shaken may remain. (Hebrews 12:27)

God will shake everything that is temporary, everything that can be destroyed. What remains will be the eternal kingdom—His kingdom, unshakeable and permanent. This is why we must persevere and not refuse Him.

### The Response Required

> Therefore, since we are receiving a kingdom that cannot be shaken, let us be thankful, and so worship God acceptably with reverence and awe, for our "God is a consuming fire." (Hebrews 12:28-29)

We respond with gratitude, with reverence, with awe. God is holy, consuming, all-consuming in His demand for our allegiance.

## The Character of Perseverance

The passage concludes by pointing us to Hebrews 13, which lists concrete disciplines required of every Christian: loving one another, showing hospitality, remembering the suffering, honoring marriage, being content with what we have, maintaining faith.

These require discipline. To be a disciple is to have discipline. Yet our generation struggles with this. Many young people cannot persist at jobs, finish university courses, or maintain commitments. We resign via text message and avoid interviews. When difficulties arise, we blame others and demand entitlements.

This weakness flows into our Christian lives. We become flaky. God becomes optional, just another button on the Facebook of life that we might click or might not.

But consider the Olympic marathoner John Stephen Akhwari. Competing in Mexico City in 1968, he fell badly at 19 kilometers into the 42-kilometer race. His knee was apparently dislocated, his leg bleeding. Medical staff urged him to withdraw. Instead, he continued running—walking, limping, pushing forward. He finished over an hour behind the winner.

When asked why he didn't quit, he said: "My country did not send me 5,000 miles just to start the race; they sent me to finish the race."

We remember Akhwari. Almost no one remembers the gold medalist that year. We honor him because he finished despite the pain.

So it is with the Christian life. Our Father sent us to finish the race. We are to persevere through hardship, maintain our commitment, and listen to God without excuse. The character of a true Christian is grit—faithfulness, commitment, the willingness to see things through to the end.

This is not burdensome. It is glory.
