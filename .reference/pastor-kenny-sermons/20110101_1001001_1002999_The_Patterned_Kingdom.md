## Introduction: Beginning the New Year with Scripture

As we enter a new year, I want to encourage all of us—whether longtime Christians, new believers, or newcomers to our church—to deepen our knowledge of God through His Word. Rather than offering tips, I want to give you something more fundamental: a basic outline of the Bible that will serve as a road map, template, or pattern. When you understand the big picture, everything you read will make far more sense.

## One Coherent Message

The Bible is fundamentally different from other religious writings. While it contains proverbs like the Qur'an, it is not primarily proverbial—we cannot extract isolated verses and apply them without historical context. Although it contains stories like Eastern religions, these stories claim to be historical, not mythological. Though it is a diverse collection of books like Buddhist writings, it is not scattered but coherent and interconnected—more like the Harry Potter novels than episodes of The Simpsons.

This means we must read the Bible with a different skill than we use for other religious documents. We cannot pick random passages, chant them, and ignore context. Instead, we must read with grammatico-historical awareness—understanding the linear, unfolding story in light of historical context and cultural setting.

> We need to sit under God's Word and listen, not sit above it and interrogate it.

## The Kingdom of God: Three Essential Components

The unifying thread running through Scripture is the Kingdom of God. Every kingdom requires three things:

1. **Place** - A geographical location
2. **People** - Subjects to inhabit that place
3. **Rule** - The king's authority and presence

Consider the British Empire at its height: great place, great people, and great presence. The sun never set on their dominion. God's ideal life is similarly expressed as God's People in God's Place under God's Rule.

## God's Place (Genesis 1:1-2:14)

### Creation: Order and Perfection

> In the beginning God created the heavens and the earth. Now the earth was formless and empty, darkness was over the surface of the deep, and the Spirit of God was hovering over the waters. And God said, "Let there be light," and there was light.

Genesis 1 reveals that God created an ordered and perfect world. This stood in sharp contrast to the creation myths of ancient religions, which portrayed the world as chaotic, formed after cosmic battles between gods, created from the carcasses of defeated deities.

The orderliness of creation is evident throughout the text:

- The repetitive pattern—"And God said... and it was so... the first day... the second day"—shows creation's perfect order, never chaotic.
- The word "good" appears repeatedly, emphasizing creation's inherent goodness.
- In Jewish literature, the number 7 represents completeness. Genesis 1 is saturated with sevens: seven days, seven words in the first Hebrew sentence, and God mentioned 35 times (7×5), all signifying perfect completion.

### The Pattern of Creation Days

Most striking is how the creation days are arranged in a deliberate pattern:

**Days 1-3: Creating the Spaces**
- Day 1: Light and darkness (day and night)
- Day 2: Waters and sky (seas and atmosphere)
- Day 3: Land and vegetation (earth and plants)

**Days 4-6: Filling the Spaces**
- Day 4: Sun and moon (to fill day and night)
- Day 5: Sea creatures and birds (to fill waters and sky)
- Day 6: Land animals and humanity (to fill earth)

God first prepares the environments, then populates them with the creatures suited to inhabit them. This demonstrates the mind of a single, intelligent Creator—not random forces, but purposeful design.

### Rest and Blessing

> God saw all that he had made, and it was very good... By the seventh day God had finished the work he had been doing; so on the seventh day he rested from all his work. And God blessed the seventh day and made it holy, because on it he rested.

Creation culminates not in productivity but in rest. The garden—God's world—is pronounced "very good," representing ultimate blessing and peace.

> A river watering the garden flowed from Eden; from there it was separated into four headwaters. The name of the first is the Pishon... The name of the second river is the Gihon... The name of the third river is the Tigris... And the fourth river is the Euphrates.

God's world is material, real, and abundantly blessed. Christianity is not a mystical religion divorced from physical reality. God cares about place, land, and creation itself.

## God's People (Genesis 2:7-25)

### Humanity Created in God's Image

A kingdom without subjects is no kingdom at all. God's first act is to create humanity:

> The LORD God formed the man from the dust of the ground and breathed into his nostrils the breath of life, and the man became a living being.

Then God places him in the garden He has prepared:

> Now the LORD God had planted a garden in the east, in Eden; and there he put the man he had formed.

### Woman: The Suitable Helper

God recognizes that man alone is incomplete:

> The LORD God said, "It is not good for the man to be alone. I will make a helper suitable for him." Now the LORD God had formed out of the ground all the beasts of the field and all the birds of the air. He brought them to the man to see what he would name them... But for Adam no suitable helper was found.

God then creates woman from man's own body:

> So the LORD God caused the man to fall into a deep sleep; and while he was sleeping, he took one of the man's ribs and closed up the place with flesh. Then the LORD God made a woman from the rib he had taken out of the man, and he brought her to the man. The man said, "This is now bone of my bones and flesh of my flesh; she shall be called 'woman,' for she was taken out of man."

### Mankind's Elevation and Purpose

Unlike other ancient religions—which taught that humans were created from dead gods or as an afterthought to serve divine needs—Genesis teaches that humanity is made *by God himself* in His very image. Humanity is not created to serve the gods; rather, God *serves* humanity by providing food and blessing.

Day 6, when humanity is created, is the climax of creation. The description is lengthened, and God doesn't simply say "let there be man" but rather "let us make man in our image." This reflects humanity's profound significance: we are divine representatives, created to exercise God's careful rule over creation and carry out His purposes. Mankind is God's prime minister.

This teaches us something essential: **we are important to God**. The God of the universe cares for you personally and has given you a task. Through Jesus, the depth of God's love is fully demonstrated.

### Blessing Relationship

God gives humanity three gifts:

1. **A task to perform:** "The LORD God took the man and put him in the Garden of Eden to work it and take care of it."

2. **Freedom and abundance:** "You are free to eat from any tree in the garden"—provision abounds.

3. **A wonderful relationship:** "For this reason a man will leave his father and mother and be united to his wife, and they will become one flesh. The man and his wife were both naked, and they felt no shame."

No shame means perfect peace and goodwill—no barriers, no guilt, no secrets. This is true fellowship: honest, free-flowing, and transparent. Like the friendship between King Bertie and his commoner friend Lionel in *The King's Speech*—a relationship transcending social station, characterized by first-name basis intimacy and genuine care. There is no shame between the humans and between humanity and God.

## God's Rule, Presence, and Blessing (Genesis 2:15-17)

### The King's Command

No kingdom exists without a king. God is the central character—creator and sovereign ruler. He establishes His rule through command:

> The LORD God commanded the man, "You are free to eat from any tree in the garden; but you must not eat from the tree of the knowledge of good and evil, for when you eat of it you will surely die."

God's role as benevolent ruler is clear. His presence brings blessing. Even within a relationship of mutual love and respect, divine order and authority remain. We sometimes confuse equality with the absence of distinction, thinking that treating equals means we can be rude or disrespectful. But true friendship—like that between a king and commoner—maintains respect and order while transcending social distance.

God is the elevated sovereign, yet He relates to humanity as a friend. He is vice-regent over all creation, the ultimate authority. Humanity's purpose is to follow God and extend His careful rule under His authority.

## The Pattern of the Kingdom

In Genesis 1-2, we see the foundation of God's great pattern: **God's People in God's Place under God's Rule**.

As you continue reading Scripture, keep this pattern in mind. Ask yourself: What does this passage reveal about God's place? What does it tell us about His people? What does it show us about His rule and blessing? This framework will help you trace the consistent theme of the Kingdom throughout the entire Bible, seeing how every part fits into God's grand, coherent story.
