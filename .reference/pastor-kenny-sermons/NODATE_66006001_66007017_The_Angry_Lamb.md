# Revelation 6:1-7:17
**Title:** The Angry Lamb
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Understanding God's Response to Evil

How does God deal with evil in this world? Consider this provocative quote: "Evil will always triumph over good because good is dumb" (Dark Helmet, *Space Balls*). This irreverent statement masks a serious question about divine justice and God's patience with wickedness.

The colors we encounter throughout these chapters—green, red, black, and white—carry symbolic weight in Scripture. While we often attach cultural meanings to colors in our everyday lives, we must ask whether these symbols carry the same significance across different cultures and, more importantly, what John intends them to mean in the Revelation narrative.

## The Seven Seals: Divine Judgment Unfolding

The seals reveal the progressive outworking of God's judgment. What connects the first four seals is their portrayal of riders on horses, each bringing a different aspect of judgment to the earth. These are not random calamities but a coordinated unfolding of divine purpose.

The four horsemen represent distinct but related judgments:

**Seal 1 (White Horse):** Conquest and deception
**Seal 2 (Red Horse):** War and bloodshed
**Seal 3 (Black Horse):** Famine and economic hardship
**Seal 4 (Pale Horse):** Death itself

These riders derive their authority from Christ himself. This is the crucial truth: evil does not operate independently or beyond God's control. Even the forces of destruction serve God's purposes and remain subject to his sovereign will. What appears as chaos is actually ordered judgment.

## The Call of the Seals

Notice that verses 1, 3, 5, and 7 all contain the word "Come." Christ is calling forth these judgments. He opens the seals. He summons these horsemen. The repeated invitation emphasizes that this is not random destruction but purposeful, intentional judgment flowing from the throne of God.

## The Cry of the Martyrs

The fifth seal reveals those who have been slain for their faith—the martyrs whose blood cries out from beneath the altar. Their plea is direct and anguished: "How long, O Lord?" They ask for justice, for vindication, for God to finally judge those who persecute them.

God's response is patience and reassurance. They are given white robes—symbols of purity and victory—and told to rest until the number of their fellow servants is complete. Their suffering is not forgotten; it has meaning within God's larger redemptive timeline.

## The Sixth Seal: Cosmic Judgment

The sixth seal brings cosmic upheaval. The earth quakes, the sun turns black, the moon becomes like blood, and the stars fall from the sky. The language is apocalyptic and overwhelming. Is this just? Yes, because it flows from the holiness of God responding to human rebellion and wickedness.

But notice who hides: kings, generals, the rich, the powerful—those who have accumulated authority and wealth through oppression and injustice. They hide from the face of the One seated on the throne. They recognize, finally, that there is a reckoning.

## The Interlude: Seals and Revelation's Structure

Do chapters 6 and 7 follow one another in strict chronological sequence? The answer is no. Chapter 7 is an interlude—a pause in the action of the seals. John is not following a purely linear timeline but is using literary structure to reveal theological truth. The seals describe what happens in history; chapter 7 reveals what is true in heaven during that same time. This is apocalyptic literature, not newspaper reporting.

## The Two Multitudes

There is indeed a difference between the 144,000 sealed from the tribes of Israel (7:4) and the great multitude that no one can count (7:9). The 144,000 represent a specific, sealed community—Israel's elect, those marked with God's protection. The great multitude from every nation, tribe, people, and language represents the full scope of God's salvation purpose, extending to all peoples.

These are not rivals but complementary pictures of God's people: the particular elect of Israel and the universal redeemed community drawn from all nations.

## The Doxology Pattern

Verses 10-12 establish a beautiful liturgical pattern:
- Salvation belongs to our God and to the Lamb
- Praise, glory, wisdom, thanksgiving, honor, power, and might belong to him forever

This same pattern echoes throughout Scripture—this is the fundamental confession of the redeemed: that all good things, all power, all authority belong ultimately to God and the Lamb. Wherever the people of God gather, this acclamation rings out.

## The Promise to the Overcomers

Those who have washed their robes and made them white in the blood of the Lamb—these are the ones who have embraced Christ's redemptive death, who have identified themselves with his sacrifice. What is their destiny? They serve God day and night in his temple. They hunger and thirst no more. The Lamb shelters them, and God wipes away every tear from their eyes. They inherit a creation finally healed and whole.

## The Paradox of Washing in Blood

Here lies one of Scripture's most stunning paradoxes: robes are made white through blood. Ordinarily, blood stains and defiles. But the Lamb's blood cleanses. His death accomplishes what no amount of our own effort could achieve—perfect purity, complete redemption, total transformation.

## Our Seal and Our Inheritance

What does the seal of God guarantee us? It guarantees our inheritance. We are sealed, marked as God's possession, promised the full realization of all he has promised to those who belong to him. The seal is both protection and promise—a down payment on an eternal inheritance.

## Christ at the Center

Throughout all these judgments, all these upheavals, all this cosmic drama, who remains at the center, controlling all that happens? Christ. He opens the seals. He receives the worship of the redeemed. He is both Judge and Savior, both the Lamb slain and the Lion of Judah.

This passage strengthens our confidence in salvation because it assures us that history is not spinning out of control. Evil does not have the final word. God's purposes will be accomplished. Those marked by his seal will be brought safely to their inheritance. We are not victims of random chaos but participants in God's sovereign redemptive plan.

## The World's Need for Righteous Anger

As Bede Jarrett observed: "The world needs anger. The world often continues to allow evil because it isn't angry enough." God's anger in Revelation is not petulant or vindictive—it is the righteous response of a holy God to human wickedness and rebellion. This is the anger we see in the Lamb.
