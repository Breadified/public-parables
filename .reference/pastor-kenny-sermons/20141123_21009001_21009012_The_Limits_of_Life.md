# Ecclesiastes 9:1-12

**Title:** The Limits of Life
**Date:** November 23, 2014
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Universal Mortality: All Must Face Death

Ecclesiastes presents a sobering truth: death comes for everyone, regardless of status or righteousness. Solomon examines this reality across five categories of people—the righteous and wicked, the good and bad, the clean and unclean, those who offer sacrifices and those who don't, the good man and the sinner, those who take oaths and those who fear to.

> All share a common destiny—the righteous and the wicked, the good and the bad, the clean and the unclean, those who offer sacrifices and those who do not. As it is with the good man, so with the sinner; as it is with those who take oaths, so with those who are afraid to take them.

Whether you live righteously or wickedly, whether you achieve earthly success or fail, whether you're Hitler or Mother Teresa—death comes for all. Your moral character doesn't exempt you. Your accomplishments don't save you. The average lifespan in Australia is 82 years, and we all carry an invisible expiration date.

### Death Is Unpredictable

Beyond universality, death catches us by surprise. We cannot predict our final hour:

> The race is not to the swift or the battle to the strong, nor does food come to the wise or wealth to the brilliant or favor to the learned; but time and chance happen to them all. Moreover, no man knows when his hour will come: As fish are caught in a cruel net, or birds are taken in a snare, so men are trapped by evil times that fall unexpectedly upon them.

You could be a non-smoker living carefully, exercising regularly, getting checkups—and then you're hit by a car. Or like Brittany Maynard, a healthy 29-year-old suddenly receives a terminal cancer diagnosis. Life is cruel, brutish, and short. This unpredictability leads many to despair and consider ending their suffering.

## The Case for Continuing to Live

Yet even in his despair, Solomon offers reasons for hope. While acknowledging our common mortality, he insists:

> Anyone who is among the living has hope—even a live dog is better off than a dead lion!

### Five Reasons Why Living Is Better Than Death

**1. The Living Can Still Know and Contribute**

> The living know that they will die, but the dead know nothing; they have no further reward, and even the memory of them is forgotten. Their love, their hate and their jealousy have long since vanished; never again will they have a part in anything that happens under the sun.

Death removes you entirely from participation in this world. But while you live, even as a "dog" rather than a "lion," you can still contribute. A disabled child teaches parents unconditional love. A baby who lives only two days shows us the preciousness of life. Someone struggling with depression who persists in living serves the world through their very endurance. The elderly contribute wisdom—their memory unlocks the lessons of the past that help us understand the present.

**2. Your Presence Prevents Others' Deaths**

The greatest predictor of suicide is not poverty, psychology, or depression—it's knowing someone who has already taken their own life. When you persevere, you give permission to others to persevere. When you end your life, you plant a seed in others suggesting that suicide is an acceptable escape. The reverse is equally true: your decision to continue living tells your children, your friends, your community that life is worth living.

**3. You Are Made in God's Image**

However low you feel about yourself, you are not worthless. Human dignity doesn't depend on health status, capability, or economic productivity. It rests on bearing God's image.

**4. Pain Can Be Managed**

Modern palliative care is far better than it was. Pain can be relieved. Hospice allows people to continue living with dignity while managing suffering.

**5. We Cannot Know What We Might Yet Become**

According to 1 Corinthians 12:22, "The weaker parts are indispensable." None of us can truly know what we mean to others, or what our future self will experience. History and philosophy teach us to remember these mysteries and persevere, awaiting the sun.

## Five Practices for Those Who Feel Despair

If you are overwhelmed by meaninglessness, Solomon prescribes five practices:

**1. Eat and drink with gladness.** Even when food tastes like ash and you have no appetite, nourish yourself. This is God's favor on your obedience to live.

**2. Clothe yourself and maintain your appearance.** When depression keeps you in pajamas, force yourself to shower, dress, and step into the sunshine. Vitamin D and natural light restore hope.

**3. Enjoy relationships.** Whether your spouse is sick, or you're facing dementia together, whether you feel disconnected—invest in the people you love.

**4. Work with all your might.** Don't surrender to listlessness.

**5. Remember your purpose: Fear God and keep His commandments.** This is the whole duty of humanity. Life's meaning isn't found in our circumstances but in obedience to God.

## Why Not Euthanasia? A Christian Response

The case for euthanasia rests on three arguments: easing suffering, honoring autonomy, and freeing medical resources. These are emotionally compelling, but they mask a dangerous truth.

### Suffering Has Purpose

Yes, suffering is real. But suffering connects us to our humanity and teaches us compassion. Family members who nurse dying loved ones experience profound meaning and purpose. The weak are indispensable to the community because they teach us what love really means.

### Autonomy Masks Abandonment

True autonomy means independence. But euthanasia also means being left entirely alone—abandoned at the moment of greatest vulnerability. When doctors offer death as an option, they communicate an implicit message: "I agree your life is no longer worth living." This reinforces helplessness and hopelessness rather than alleviating it.

### The Slippery Slope

In the Netherlands, euthanasia began with the terminally ill. Now people are euthanized for depression, autism, anorexia, chronic fatigue syndrome, and simply being "tired of life." Belgium's euthanasia rates have increased 150% in five years. The elderly, the poor, and the disabled are increasingly vulnerable. When politicians consider euthanasia as a cost-cutting measure, as Australia's EPAC did in 2001, we've crossed into eugenics—the systematic elimination of "unproductive" lives.

### Doctors Are Corrupted

The medical profession exists to heal and do no harm. Asking physicians to also prescribe death creates an irresolvable conflict. A doctor cannot simultaneously be dedicated to saving life and ending it. Evil doctors flourish when euthanasia is legalized because how can we distinguish euthanasia from murder?

## Two Stories: Brittany Maynard and Lauren Hill

**Brittany Maynard** was a healthy 29-year-old who received a terminal cancer diagnosis. After researching treatment options, she moved to Oregon and ended her life with doctor-assisted medication on November 1, 2014. CNN called her "extraordinary." Magazines celebrated her "courage." Her story went viral—and subsequently influenced California's legislature to legalize physician-assisted suicide.

**Lauren Hill** was a 19-year-old college basketball player who also received a terminal brain cancer diagnosis—DIPG, with a two-year prognosis. But Lauren's story took a different path. With hospice care, she continued living. She continued playing basketball. She decided to raise money for pediatric cancer research instead of for her own death. She raised $1 million for cancer research with "The Cure Starts Now Foundation." When her life ended naturally on April 15, 2015, she was honored with a brick in the Indiana Basketball Hall of Fame inscribed: "LAUREN HILL—HERO."

Lauren was a Christian who prayed: "I didn't want to be another local story that disappeared. I prayed that God would keep me alive, and if He did, I would be the voice for little kids."

Lauren served. Lauren persevered. The media largely ignored her story because it didn't support the narrative of "death with dignity."

Brittany raised money for death. Lauren raised money for life.

## The Path Forward: Christian Hope

The deepest issue is this: We live as though this life is all there is, and as though we alone control its boundaries. But we don't. We didn't start our lives, and we shouldn't end them. Life is a gift from God.

Christians should not see the future as bleak. The resurrection of Christ—historically attested and theologically certain—proves there is life after death. Jesus didn't merely jump in front of death like a shield; He wrestled it down, fought it, and defeated it over three days, maiming and destroying it so it can no longer harm anyone.

Heaven means no more suffering, no more meaninglessness, no more tears, no more sorrow. Death was never God's intention for humanity. It was defeated by Jesus and will one day be eradicated entirely.

Until then, we can live with our circumstances today. We can live with meaninglessness. We can live with tragedy. Knowing this life is temporary and will not be the final judgment on us.

**To those not yet Christian:** If you're facing personal tragedy or seeing the tragedies of the world, consider Christ and the hope He brings. Talk to someone at church. They can tell you about the hope of heaven.

**To those who know Christ:** Remember that hope in heaven doesn't make us abandon this world. Quite the opposite. It's Christians who oppose euthanasia and suicide. It's atheists with nothing to look forward to beyond this life who most readily forfeit it. As Christendom grew, Western civilization moved from a culture of death to a culture of life. As the West abandons Christianity, we return to death—abortion, euthanasia, eugenics.

Being Christian means accepting that life is not entirely in our hands. Our time on earth is God's gift. We are called to persevere in service to one another.

## A Final Word on Suicide

If you struggle with suicidal thoughts: Please don't. The vast majority of people who attempt suicide later report deep gratitude for surviving. Those who jumped from the Golden Gate Bridge and lived reported immediately regretting their leap. People who overdose on pills call 911 moments later, wanting to live.

Suicide is impulsive. When intervention comes—when someone tells you to go home instead of jumping—most people do. They don't return to the bridge.

But there's more: When you take your life, you steal from your future self—the person who will feel better. And worse, you plant a seed in everyone who knew you, giving them a weapon against themselves they would never have had otherwise. The greatest predictor of your child's suicide isn't poverty or mental illness—it's whether they've lost someone to suicide. You would poison those you love.

We are not isolated individuals. We are born into community and relationship. We belong to one another. As the Talmud teaches: "Whoever saves one life, saves the world entire." Whoever ends their life wounds the world.

**Stay for us.** As one writer put it:

> Some part of you doesn't want to end it all. I'm talking to that part of you. I'm throwing you a rope. You don't have to explain it to the monster in you, just tell the monster it can do whatever it wants, but not that. Later we'll get rid of the monster. For now just hang on to the rope. I know this means a struggle from one second to the next. Know that the rest of us are in the room with you, going from one moment to the next in whatever condition you manage it. Sobbing and useless is a million times better than dead. Thank you for choosing sobbing and useless over dead.

Or, as Ecclesiastes puts it simply: A living dog is better than a dead lion.
