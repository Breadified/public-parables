# Job 42:1-17

**Title:** The End of Suffering
**Date:** 2 August 2013
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Why Does Suffering Matter?

Pain and suffering have always been woven into human history. Rain falls on both the righteous and the unrighteous; earthquakes and floods respect no borders. We experience perfect and defective births, timely and untimely deaths, joyful and sorrowful partings. All of us ride the roller coaster of emotion and season.

When we cry out to God for deliverance and hear only silence, we're left asking: Why? Why me? Why now? Why suffering?

This question becomes one of the greatest obstacles to faith. Many conclude that if God were truly good, He would not allow such pain. Others believe He simply doesn't care. Over the past weeks, we've examined why suffering exists through the lens of Job's story. Now, at last, we reach its end.

## The End of Questions

After forty long chapters of complaint and confusion, Job finally speaks his last word to the Lord:

> I know that you can do all things; no plan of yours can be thwarted. You asked, "Who is this that obscures my counsel without knowledge?" Surely I spoke of things I did not understand, things too wonderful for me to know. (Job 42:2-3)

The answer Job arrives at is this: **God knows what He is doing.** His knowledge is infinite; His plans are unstoppable; His ways are too wonderful for us to fully comprehend.

Then comes the pivot moment:

> My ears had heard of you but now my eyes have seen you. (Job 42:5)

Job moves from intellectual knowledge to personal encounter. He has met with God.

But there's a puzzling verse that follows:

> Therefore I despise myself and repent in dust and ashes. (Job 42:6)

Some struggle with this. How can the righteous Job—the very one God commended as blameless and upright—be repenting? The Hebrew carries ambiguity here. A better translation might be: "I reject and repent from dust and ashes." In other words, Job is ready to move on from where he is.

You see, Job had already been in mourning, lamenting on his sickbed:

> He throws me into the mud, and I am reduced to dust and ashes. (Job 30:19)

He was crying out to God in his anguish, demanding answers. But now that God has spoken, Job's season of mourning ends. He is ready to stop wallowing and start trusting again.

We see this illustrated in history. When Queen Victoria lost her beloved Prince Albert in 1862, she declared a period of national mourning. Members of the royal household wore black for a year. But the Queen did not stop there—she remained in mourning clothes for the rest of her life until her death in 1901. Forty years she spent dressed in black.

Job, by contrast, stops mourning. And in doing so, he begins to trust again.

This teaches us something vital: **suffering cannot be an excuse for everything.**

- It cannot excuse us from honoring God. Our hardships don't justify ignoring Him.
- It cannot excuse us from faith. Bitterness and doubt cannot have the final word.
- It cannot excuse us from service. We cannot use pain as a reason to abandon our calling.
- It cannot excuse us from repentance. Suffering doesn't give us permission to sin.

Yes, there is a time for mourning. But there is also a time to move forward. To rise from the dust and ashes and persevere.

Job was in the middle of a test. Behind heaven's curtain, God had declared to Satan that Job was a righteous man. Satan replied that Job only remained faithful because of God's protection and blessings. "Take away the hedge," Satan challenged, "and Job will curse You to Your face." But he didn't. Job passed the test.

The purpose of a test is to pass it. So now it was time to move on.

We are called to persevere through our sufferings, not to wallow in them. We are not to escape through sinful means or by abandoning life itself. God uses discipline to prepare us:

> No discipline seems pleasant at the time, but painful. Later on, however, it produces a harvest of righteousness and peace for those who have been trained by it. (Hebrews 12:11)

Think about this practically. Decluttering skills are learned in a small house, not a mansion. Patience is learned when you have children. Perseverance is learned when you desire something you cannot have. Resilience is built through small trials, not large ones.

If we cannot resist cursing God when we accidentally hit our thumb, how will we resist when we face real loss? If we struggle to study in the comfort of our homes during exam season, how will we maintain our faith when facing mental illness? If we collapse under a single failure, what happens when we lose our job? When family turns against us? When we have less money to give?

This is why we must strengthen our feeble arms and weak knees now, in small ways, so that we are ready for whatever comes.

## God Has Spoken

Why was Job ready to move on? Because **God has spoken.**

Throughout the book, Job's deepest longing was for an audience with God:

> I desire to speak to the Almighty and to argue my case with God. (Job 13:3)

> I myself will see him with my own eyes—I, and not another. How my heart yearns within me! (Job 19:27)

Job didn't ask for his possessions back. He didn't ask for his children to be restored (though God would do both). His repeated cry was for God to break His silence. He needed to know that God had not abandoned him.

And while God's answer didn't specifically explain the test (God never told Job, "This is a trial to prove your faithfulness"), the fact that God spoke at all was transformative. God spoke out of the whirlwind with power and majesty:

> Do you have an arm like God's, and can your voice thunder like his? (Job 40:9)

In previous weeks we learned that God remains in charge of all creation—even the leviathan. But now that God speaks directly to Job, Job's faith grows. His willingness to trust returns.

> My ears had heard of you but now my eyes have seen you. (Job 42:5)

We can work out answers to suffering through scientific precision and hindsight. We can trace a chain of causation: cancer develops because cells mutate and grow out of control; cells mutate because of exposure to certain carcinogens; we're exposed because of our habits. But the chain still begs the question: why?

In this, the world is ultimately silent. Karma offers no real answer. Random chance is unfulfilling. Superstition—stars, feng shui, fate—is foolish. These provide no comfort when we suffer.

But into this vacuum, **God has spoken.** He came as the man Jesus two thousand years ago. When people asked Him about suffering, He gave a striking answer:

> Do you think that these Galileans were worse sinners than all the other Galileans because they suffered this way? I tell you, no! But unless you repent, you too will all perish. (Luke 13:2-3)

A group of Galileans had suffered terribly at Pilate's hands—their blood mixed with their sacrifices. Why suffering? The answer wasn't karma or moral inferiority. The answer was: repent and get right with God.

Jesus told another story:

> Or those eighteen who died when the tower in Siloam fell on them—do you think they were more guilty than all the others living in Jerusalem? I tell you, no! But unless you repent, you too will all perish. (Luke 13:4-5)

Again, no specific explanation. But the same call: turn from sin and trust God.

This echoes Job's experience—no detailed answer, but the grace of God speaking to us. And what God ultimately says to us about suffering is this: **Repent and trust Me.**

As C.S. Lewis wrote: "God whispers to us in our pleasures, speaks to us in our conscience, but shouts in our pains: It is His megaphone to rouse a deaf world."

God has not left us without a word. Jesus came and did more than speak—He acted. He went to the cross and paid the penalty for sin. He suffered so that our suffering might one day end permanently.

> I consider that our present sufferings are not worth comparing with the glory that will be revealed in us. (Romans 8:18)

In Job's example, we see the possibility of the end of suffering.

## The End of Suffering

After Job prayed for his friends, God restored him. He returned to interceding for others, just as he had before. His friends came to eat with him, no longer treating him as an outcast. They gave him silver and gold rings—capital with which to rebuild his life.

> The LORD made him prosperous again and gave him twice as much as he had before. (Job 42:10)

**Double the possessions.** Job received fourteen thousand sheep, six thousand camels, a thousand yoke of oxen, and a thousand donkeys.

**Double the family.** He had seven sons and three daughters—the same number he'd lost, but now a second set. Some theologians suggest the original children were resurrected in heaven, while these represent his earthly family. Either way, Job's daughters were remarkably beautiful, and their father granted them an inheritance alongside their brothers—a sign of extraordinary wealth and blessing.

Normally daughters didn't inherit, not out of discrimination but because there often wasn't enough to divide. If an estate were split equally among many children, each would receive too little to be meaningful. But Job was so rich that even his daughters received a substantial inheritance.

**Double the lifespan.** Job lived to 140 years—twice the typical length of life mentioned in Psalm 90. More than that, he saw his children and their children to the fourth generation. He became a great-grandfather.

> And so he died, old and full of years. (Job 42:17)

Yet even with all this doubling, Job still died. He still aged, still grew sick, still had to leave everything behind.

The point is crucial: **Job's earthly blessing was incomplete.** His restoration, though genuine and generous, was not the final word. Even his doubled prosperity ended in death.

This is why we must look beyond this life. There is a greater inheritance waiting for those who persevere in faith. There is a crown waiting at the end of the journey for those who endure.

> Be patient, then, brothers, until the Lord's coming... As an example of patience in the face of suffering, take the prophets who spoke in the name of the Lord. As you know, we consider blessed those who have persevered. You have heard of Job's perseverance and have seen what the Lord finally brought about. The Lord is full of compassion and mercy. (James 5:7-11)

## A Word for Today

The challenge with this message is that we typically don't need it when we hear it. If you were truly sick, you wouldn't be here—you'd be in the hospital. If you were facing persecution, you wouldn't be sitting in this service—you'd be in hiding. We study Job's perseverance in times of relative comfort.

But that's precisely why we must absorb these truths now. We need to inoculate our hearts against doubt before the real trial comes. We need to build resilience through small hardships so that we're confident when major ones arrive.

The goal is to strengthen ourselves now so that when real trouble hits, we remember:

1. **God is in charge of both good and evil.** Nothing escapes His knowledge or control.
2. **We are called to endure.** Suffering is not an excuse to abandon faith or integrity.
3. **God shows us grace, not karma.** Hardship doesn't prove we're wicked; blessing doesn't prove we're righteous.
4. **God has spoken a word of comfort.** Through Jesus, through His Word, through His Spirit, He speaks to us.
5. **There is an end to suffering.** For those in Christ, the final word is not pain but glory.

Let us be patient. Let us strengthen our feeble arms and weak knees. And let us help each other persevere.
