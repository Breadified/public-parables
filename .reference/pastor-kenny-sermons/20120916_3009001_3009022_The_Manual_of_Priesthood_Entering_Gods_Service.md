# Leviticus 9:1-22; 10:1-5

**Title:** The Manual of Priesthood: Entering God's Service  
**Date:** September 16, 2012  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction: The Danger of Sacred Work

What is the most dangerous job in the world? I once worked as a bouncer at university dance parties. The venue was dark and noisy, I was collecting admission fees in Tupperware, and I was a skinny Asian man telling drunk teenagers they couldn't come in. If anyone had wanted to fight, I would have been helpless.

But in ancient Biblical times, the most dangerous job must have been to serve as a priest in Israel.

In Leviticus, God has given His people a manual for relationship with Him. Last week we studied the different offerings and sacrifices. Today we consider the men who would offer them—the Israelite priesthood, and the extraordinary preparation required to approach a holy God.

---

## 1. The Perfect Priest

### Consecration: Person and Place

God was establishing a new mediator. Moses had held this role, but now it passed to his brother Aaron and his sons. For this sacred work, God required a certain kind of man—a man who would be thoroughly prepared.

In Leviticus 8, we see the consecration process unfold in a pattern that repeats deliberately throughout the chapter:

**The Priest is Made Clean**

> Moses brought Aaron and his sons forward and washed them with water. He put the tunic on Aaron, tied the sash around him, clothed him with the robe and put the ephod on him. He also tied the ephod to him by its skillfully woven waistband. He placed the breastpiece on him and put the Urim and Thummim in the breastpiece. Then he placed the turban on Aaron's head and set the gold plate, the sacred diadem, on the front of it. (Leviticus 8:6-9)

Aaron was being dressed like someone suited up for a sacred task—every garment intentional, every detail prescribed.

**The Place is Made Clean**

> Then Moses took the anointing oil and anointed the tabernacle and everything in it, and so consecrated them. He sprinkled some of the oil on the altar seven times, anointing the altar and all its utensils and the basin with its stand, to consecrate them. (Leviticus 8:10-11)

The furniture, the altar, the utensils—everything that would come into contact with the sacrifice had to be purified. Throughout chapter 8, this pattern repeats: person is anointed, then place; person receives blood, then place; person is anointed again. The repetition is deliberate and purposeful.

It's like an operating theatre. The surgeon, the scalpel, the machines—everything is checked and rechecked for bandages, for needles, for any contamination. Everything that will touch the work must be prepared. So it was in God's tent. Everything had to be right for God.

### Ordination: Seven Days of Waiting

But the preparation did not end with one chapter.

> Do not leave the entrance to the Tent of Meeting for seven days, until the days of your ordination are completed, for your ordination will last seven days. (Leviticus 8:33)

The furniture had been cleansed. Now the priests themselves would remain in that sacred space for seven days—literally becoming part of the furniture, holding the sacrifice, waiting. The word "ordination" means "installation" or "filling of hands," suggesting their hands would be filled with this new responsibility.

Perhaps it was a kind of quarantine. My father once worked in a blood lab with petri dishes filled with horse blood jelly. They would swab whatever they wanted to test, place it in an incubator, and watch what grew. I wonder if God kept the priests in isolation for seven days simply to ensure they remained truly holy—to see if any sickness or contamination would show itself.

### The Final Check

On the eighth day came one last verification:

> Aaron came to the altar and slaughtered the calf as a sin offering for himself. His sons brought the blood to him, and he dipped his finger into the blood and put it on the horns of the altar. (Leviticus 9:8-9)

Only after all these preparations—the washing, the dressing, the anointing, the seven days of waiting, the final offering—could Aaron finally do what he was meant to do.

> Aaron then brought the offering that was for the people. Then Aaron lifted his hands toward the people and blessed them. And having sacrificed the sin offering, the burnt offering and the fellowship offering, he stepped down. (Leviticus 9:15, 22)

It was done. But why all this complication?

## 2. The Holiness of God Appearing

### The Danger is Real

Because it was genuinely dangerous to approach God.

> You must stay at the entrance to the Tent of Meeting day and night for seven days and do what the LORD requires, so you will not die; for that is what I have been commanded. (Leviticus 8:35)

Do this wrong, and you will die. This was not a metaphor.

### A Sobering Example

Tragically, we see this reality demonstrated immediately:

> Aaron's sons Nadab and Abihu took their censers, put fire in them and added incense; and they offered unauthorized fire before the LORD, contrary to his command. So fire came out from the presence of the LORD and consumed them, and they died before the LORD. (Leviticus 10:1-2)

Aaron's two sons took it upon themselves to offer fire that God had not commanded. Some suggest they were drunk. Others think they followed a pagan ritual. Perhaps they tried to burn their own sacrifice rather than wait for God's presence to act. Whatever their reasoning, it was contrary to God's explicit command.

And instantly, fire consumed them.

I think of a tragedy I heard about—two university students in Bankstown, trapped in an apartment fire five stories up. As the flames grew unbearable, they stood on the window ledge. We might ask: why not just endure it inside? But we forget how hot fires burn. At 1000 degrees, even aluminum fly screens melt. They had no choice. The priests were working in dangerous conditions. Five priests installed, and after eight days, two were dead.

### Why This Severity? God's Holiness

Moses explained the reason:

> This is what the LORD spoke of when he said: "Among those who approach me I will show myself holy; in the sight of all the people I will be honored." (Leviticus 10:3)

God is holy. God cannot stand sin.

But humans are sinful. Sin is pervasive—it contaminates everything. The mediators who approach God must be clean. Not just the priests, but the furniture and utensils as well. Past sin, present sin, future sin—all of it needed to be dealt with.

There is a vast difference between God and humanity that we often fail to appreciate. We tend to think we're just different in degrees. God has been around longer, so He's a little better than us. Give us enough time and education, and we'll be good too. But this fundamentally misunderstands God's nature.

God radiates such comprehensive moral purity, and such a powerful and absolute intolerance of sin, that when this reality is accurately perceived by a sinner, they cannot help but be overcome with terror.

### God Himself Will Appear

Yet the problem is inescapable: God Himself plans to appear.

> Take a bull calf for your sin offering and a ram for your burnt offering, both without defect, and present them before the LORD. Then say to the Israelites: Take a male goat for a sin offering, a calf and a lamb—both a year old and without defect—for a burnt offering, and an ox and a ram for a fellowship offering to sacrifice before the LORD, together with a grain offering mixed with oil. For today the LORD will appear to you. (Leviticus 9:2-4)

And when He did appear, the people witnessed something unforgettable:

> Moses and Aaron then went into the Tent of Meeting. When they came out, they blessed the people; and the glory of the LORD appeared to all the people. Fire came out from the presence of the LORD and consumed the burnt offering and the fat portions on the altar. And when all the people saw it, they shouted for joy and fell facedown. (Leviticus 9:23-24)

The glory of the LORD appeared as fire. This fire recalled Mount Sinai, when Moses went up to meet God on a mountain of fire. The fire consumed the offerings on the altar. When the people saw it, they shouted with joy—and then fell facedown in terror.

The thing we must realize is that God's holiness is dangerous and fearsome. God is so holy that anything that is not holy cannot survive His presence. Some of the most chilling accounts in the Bible show what happens when people show contempt for God's holiness. Remember Nadab and Abihu. God's holiness is fearsome, and He will not let it be defiled.

One day God will reappear to us all:

> By the same word the present heavens and earth are reserved for fire, being kept for the day of judgment and destruction of ungodly men. (2 Peter 3:7)

How will we fare? How can any of us stand before such holiness?

## 3. The Perfected Priest

### The Problem with Earthly Priests

As an act of mercy, God gave the Israelites a mediator. But the mediators themselves had a problem—they could not be truly holy. Aaron's own background was compromised. He had participated in making the golden calf. Later, through Malachi, God would rebuke the priests:

> If I am a father, where is the honor due me? If I am a master, where is the respect due me? It is you, O priests, who show contempt for my name. (Malachi 1:6)

The human priesthood could not do what was required. They could stay ceremonially clean for a little while, after purification ritual upon purification ritual, but this could only mitigate the problem of sin, not solve it. The high priest and the old temple sacrifices could at best slow down God's anger, pacify Him for a time, but not forever.

### Jesus: The Perfect High Priest

Out of mercy again, a second time, God sent another priest. Turn to Hebrews:

> Therefore, since we have a great high priest who has gone through the heavens, Jesus the Son of God, let us hold firmly to the faith we profess. For we do not have a high priest who is unable to sympathize with our weaknesses, but we have one who has been tempted in every way, just as we are—yet was without sin. Let us then approach the throne of grace with confidence, so that we may receive mercy and find grace to help us in our time of need. (Hebrews 4:14-16)

**The Similarity:** Like Aaron, Jesus is a man—one of the people. 

> Every high priest is selected from among men and is appointed to represent them in matters related to God, to offer gifts and sacrifices for sins. He is able to deal gently with those who are ignorant and are going astray, since he himself is subject to weakness. (Hebrews 5:1-2)

**The Difference:** But Jesus was without sin.

> This is why he has to offer sacrifices for his own sins, as well as for the sins of the people. But [Jesus] has no need to do this, since [he] was without sin. Therefore, [he has] gone through the heavens. Therefore we have an inside man to help us. (Hebrews 5:3, adapted)

Jesus was tempted in every way, yet He was without sin. Here is the man who never needed to offer sacrifices for Himself. Here is the man who fulfilled all of God's righteousness decrees. Look at how He accomplished this:

> During the days of Jesus' life on earth, he offered up prayers and petitions with loud cries and tears to the one who could save him from death, and he was heard because of his reverent submission. Although he was a son of God, he learned obedience from what he suffered; and, once made perfect, he became the source of eternal salvation for all who obey him. (Hebrews 5:7-9)

Jesus—submission, obedience, suffering, becoming perfect, becoming the source of eternal salvation.

### No More Mediators But Christ Alone

Therefore, there is no need for any other mediator:

> For there is one God and one mediator between God and men, the man Christ Jesus, who gave himself as a ransom for all men. (1 Timothy 2:5-6)

Not the person upfront in the church. Not the pope. Not the one administering communion. Some might quote 1 Peter in response:

> But you are a chosen people, a royal priesthood, a holy nation, a people belonging to God, that you may declare the praises of him who called you out of darkness into his wonderful light. (1 Peter 2:9)

This is true, but it refers to another aspect of priesthood—the teaching of the law, which we see in Malachi. That still remains. But the offering of sacrifices? That is finished. There is only one man who can take us directly into the presence of God: Jesus Himself.

---

## Conclusion

> Let us then approach the throne of grace with confidence, so that we may receive mercy and find grace to help us in our time of need. (Hebrews 4:16)

We are enjoying a nice roof. Thank goodness none of us had to go up there and construct it. Thank God. Getting us an appointment with God was a dangerous job. But Christ has done it. He has made a way. We can approach the holy God with confidence—not because we are holy, but because our mediator is perfect, sinless, and eternally sufficient.
