# Numbers 1:1-10:13

**Title:** In the Wilderness
**Date:** 2 June 2013
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## One Giant Camping Trip

When you go camping with a large group—especially with children—three essential things come to mind. First, you count the people to ensure everyone is present. Second, you establish rules: where to camp, where the bathroom area is, where shoes go. Third, and most importantly, you don't die.

The book of Numbers mirrors this camping trip perfectly. It addresses these same three concerns: counting the people, establishing rules for living with God, and the Israelites' journey through the wilderness without perishing.

---

## 1. Numbers is About Numbers: Count the People (1:1-46)

The name "Numbers" comes from the Greek translation of the Hebrew scriptures, where this book was called *Arithmoi*—arithmetic or calculation. The name fits perfectly, as this book contains extensive counts and numbering.

> The LORD spoke to Moses in the Tent of Meeting in the Desert of Sinai on the first day of the second month of the second year after the Israelites came out of Egypt. He said: "Take a census of the whole Israelite community by their clans and families, listing every man by name, one by one. You and Aaron are to number by their divisions all the men in Israel twenty years old or more who are able to serve in the army." (Numbers 1:1-3)

The counting appears throughout Numbers: the tribes are listed in chapter 2 (with their camp arrangement), the Levites are counted in chapter 3, another census occurs in chapter 26, and even the offerings to the tabernacle are counted in chapter 7.

### Why Count?

**Practical Reason**

The Israelites were transitioning from a slave people to a nation. They had begun paying taxes (Exodus 30:12 onwards) and were preparing to enter and take possession of a foreign land. Naturally, they needed to know how many fighting men they could field for war.

**Theological Reason: God's Promises Are Being Fulfilled**

> All the Israelites twenty years old or more who were able to serve in Israel's army were counted according to their families. The total number was 603,550. (Numbers 1:45-46)

This remarkable number—603,550 fighting men—reminds us that God keeps His promises. The story begins in Genesis with the promise to Abraham of land, offspring, and blessing. From Abraham came Isaac, then Jacob, then Jacob's twelve sons. A famine brought them to Egypt, where they were eventually enslaved. At the end of Genesis, when they entered Egypt, God's family numbered around 73 people, with Joseph as second-in-command.

The rest of Exodus records their miraculous rescue from slavery. In Leviticus, they camp at Mount Sinai to receive God's laws. By Numbers, they've been there about a year. Yet now: 603,550 fighting men—likely representing about 2.5 million people total when including women, children, and the elderly.

These numbers demonstrate that God has preserved Israel through slavery. Not one tribe has perished; instead, God has multiplied the people from 73 to 600,000+.

**We Count What We Value**

These numbers might seem tedious to modern readers, but they carry profound significance. As humans, we count what we value. A parent at a shopping center counts their children constantly. We track our money, our possessions, our collections. God counts His people. To be numbered among the redeemed, to have one's name in the book of life—this is a blessing. These numbers represent God's love for His people. He values you and accounts you precious.

## 2. Numbers is About God: Set the Rules (1:47-9:23)

### God is Here

> The LORD spoke to Moses in the Tent of Meeting in the Desert of Sinai... (Numbers 1:1)

The phrase "The LORD said" appears approximately 44 times in Numbers. This repetition serves a literary function—joining sections together—and emphasizes an essential truth: **God is present with the Israelites through His speech.**

God works through His Spirit in speech. Even the portions of Numbers that seem dry are part of His Spirit's work. We read them because they are God's word to us. Reading the entire counsel of God means eventually encountering books that may not be our favorite or personal style, yet they remain vital.

God's continued speech demonstrates His grace. Though the people rebel, God keeps speaking. Indeed, silence would signal judgment; speech demonstrates ongoing relationship. Just as a relationship ends when communication ceases, God's voice assures the Israelites of His abiding presence.

### The People to Live Around God

> The Israelites are to camp around the Tent of Meeting some distance from it, each man under his standard with the banners of his family. (Numbers 2:1-2)

The Tent of Meeting is where God dwells—where He will speak with Moses for the people. The instructions for building this tent (given at the end of Exodus and repeated here) are meticulous because every detail symbolizes God's holy presence. Like a wedding where every element matters, the tent reflects the importance of properly approaching God.

Living with God for the first time, the Israelites organize themselves around His dwelling place. Their camps form a structured arrangement:

> On the east, toward the sunrise, the divisions of the camp of Judah are to encamp under their standard... His division numbers 74,600... (Numbers 2:3-4)

Imagine this arrangement: You wake and see smoke from the tent. As you collect water, the tent reminds you God is near. Visiting a friend, you turn a corner and see it again. The tabernacle becomes a constant, visible reminder: *God is here with us.*

But living with God demands something of us.

### The People Must Be Pure

God is holy. Chapters 5 and 6 contain extensive instructions—all prefaced with "The LORD said"—addressing purity in the camp.

Chapter 5 deals with camp cleanliness: handling dead bodies and contamination (as in Leviticus), and addressing adultery. Chapter 7 focuses on cleansing the tent itself. The overall impression is clear: proximity to God carries danger.

> Nadab and Abihu, however, fell dead before the LORD when they made an offering with unauthorized fire before him in the Desert of Sinai. They had no sons; so only Eleazar and Ithamar served as priests during the lifetime of their father Aaron. (Numbers 3:4)

Holiness defines God. Everything must be right. Moreover, ordinary people cannot simply approach the tent—there are severe consequences.

### The Buffer Zone: The Levites as Intermediaries

To help the people approach God safely, God chose the Levites to serve as intermediaries. In Exodus, they were the only tribe zealous for God. Now they are set apart for service.

> The LORD said to Moses, "Bring the tribe of Levi and present them to Aaron the priest to assist him. They are to perform duties for him and for the whole community at the Tent of Meeting by doing the work of the tabernacle..." (Numbers 3:5-7)

One-thirteenth of the male population—substantial numbers—were dedicated to tabernacle service. You might think all these men were needed for war, but God established a different priority. There is another war: the battle within us against sin and against God's holiness.

The Levites lived between the people and the sanctuary, serving as a buffer—like a protective zone around a campfire, neither so close as to burn nor so far as to lose the warmth.

> Moses and Aaron and his sons were to camp to the east of the tabernacle, toward the sunrise, in front of the Tent of Meeting. They were responsible for the care of the sanctuary on behalf of the Israelites. Anyone else who approached the sanctuary was to be put to death. (Numbers 3:38)

The Levites also served a substitutionary function. When God struck the firstborn in Egypt during the Passover, He set apart all Israel's firstborn for Himself. Yet because of the blood of the lamb that protected Israelite homes, God substituted the Levites for these firstborn sons:

> I have taken the Levites from among the Israelites in place of the first male offspring of every Israelite woman. The Levites are mine, for all the firstborn are mine. When I struck down all the firstborn in Egypt, I set apart for myself every firstborn in Israel. (Numbers 3:12-13)

There were 22,000 Levites but 22,273 firstborns, so money was collected to make up the difference (Numbers 3:44-51).

## 3. In the Wilderness: Don't Die (10:1-13)

The third aspect of Numbers concerns the journey itself. The name "In the Wilderness" (or desert) describes the space between places—the journey from one destination to another. The wilderness isn't necessarily barren; it's the in-between, the place of transition.

By the end of chapter 10, after nineteen days in the book's timeline, the Israelites are ready to depart. It has been about a year and two months since the Exodus—roughly 400 years after God's promises to Abraham.

> The LORD said to Moses: "Make two trumpets of hammered silver, and use them for calling the community together and for having the camps set out. When both are sounded, the whole community is to assemble before you at the entrance to the Tent of Meeting..." (Numbers 10:1-3)

When the trumpets sound, it signals movement. The people begin their journey with hope—a future in their own land, celebrations and feasts ahead. Each trumpet blast reminds them they are moving toward home.

> On the twentieth day of the second month of the second year, the cloud lifted from above the tabernacle of the Testimony. Then the Israelites set out from the Desert of Sinai and traveled from place to place until the cloud came to rest in the Desert of Paran. They set out, this first time, at the LORD's command through Moses. (Numbers 10:11-13)

It all sounds promising. Except they forget the central instruction: **don't die**.

They forget that God dwells with them. They forget to trust Him. They forget the buffer of intermediaries set up for their protection. They forget the numbers—the evidence of God's fulfilled promises. They believe God has cheated them.

The book of Deuteronomy records what happened next:

> These are the words Moses spoke to all Israel in the desert east of the Jordan... (It takes eleven days to go from Horeb to Kadesh Barnea by the Mount Seir road.) In the fortieth year, on the first day of the eleventh month, Moses proclaimed to the Israelites all that the LORD had commanded him concerning them. (Deuteronomy 1:1-3)

What should have taken eleven days took forty years. Over 13,000 days in the wilderness—lost, seduced by sin.

## Application: Our Journey

The book of Numbers was written not merely for Israel but for us:

> These things happened to them as examples and were written down as warnings for us, on whom the fulfillment of the ages has come. So, if you think you are standing firm, be careful that you don't fall! No temptation has seized you except what is common to man. And God is faithful; he will not let you be tempted beyond what you can bear. But when you are tempted, he will also provide a way out so that you can stand up under it. (1 Corinthians 10:11-13)

There is a striking parallel between their journey and ours. Israel was two years from their rescue—we are roughly 2,000 years from ours. They were saved but not yet at the promised land; we are saved but not yet in heaven. Salvation is accomplished but not yet completed. This "now and not yet" characterizes the Christian life.

Just as Jesus dwelled among the Israelites in the tabernacle, He dwells with us today:

> The Word became flesh and made his dwelling among us. We have seen his glory, the glory of the One and Only, who came from the Father, full of grace and truth. (John 1:14)

> Let the word of Christ dwell in you richly as you teach and admonish one another with all wisdom, and as you sing psalms, hymns and spiritual songs with gratitude in your hearts to God. (Colossians 3:16)

Our community gathers around Christ. When His word is preached, we prioritize listening. In our homes, we read Scripture. On Sundays, we focus on His word—not catching up on phones or even good tasks like preparing food. In our meetings, we ensure Christ is central. When we gather one-to-one, we don't merely download our problems; more importantly, we let God download into us first, gaining right perspective before we speak.

Like the Levites, Jesus serves as our mediating priest:

> Do you not know that your body is a temple of the Holy Spirit, who is in you, whom you have received from God? You are not your own; you were bought at a price. Therefore honor God with your body. (1 Corinthians 6:19-20)

Jesus took God's wrath upon Himself when we sinned. Because of His sacrifice—His blood shed for us—we can approach God safely. We can enter His presence without consequence. We can pray, know Him, and dwell with Him.

The journey continues. Like Israel, we travel toward our inheritance. Will we learn from their example? Will we avoid being seduced by sin and the world's empty promises—by food and comfort, by anger and pride, by fear, by money, by lust?
