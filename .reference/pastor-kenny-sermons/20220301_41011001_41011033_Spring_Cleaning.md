# Mark 11:1-33

**Title:** Spring Cleaning
**Date:** 20 March 2022
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Jesus Comes to Save—and to Judge

The crowds lining the road to Jerusalem are crying out "Save us!"—*Hosanna* in Hebrew, a prayer rooted in Psalm 118. They sense that Jesus might be the one who can deliver them from their enemies. But as we read through this passage, we discover that Jesus has come not only to save, but first to judge.

### The King's Authority (11:1-10)

> Those who went ahead and those who followed shouted,
> 
> "Hosanna!"
> 
> "Blessed is he who comes in the name of the Lord!"
> 
> "Blessed is the coming kingdom of our father David!"
> 
> "Hosanna in the highest heaven!"

The word *hosanna* combines two Hebrew roots: *hos* (save) and *anna* (we pray). The crowd is literally crying, "Save us, we pray!" They're quoting Psalm 118:25-26, a psalm sung in the context of foreign enemies pressing in on all sides.

Jesus fulfills a specific prophecy as He enters Jerusalem. When the disciples bring Him a young colt—"which no one has ever ridden"—Mark is deliberately triggering our memory of Zechariah's vision:

> Rejoice greatly, Daughter Zion! Shout, Daughter Jerusalem! See, your king comes to you, righteous and victorious, lowly and riding on a donkey, on a colt, the foal of a donkey. I will take away the chariots from Ephraim and the warhorses from Jerusalem. (Zechariah 9:9-10)

The symbolism is profound. A king riding a donkey instead of a war chariot signals peace, not conquest. He comes in humility, not military power.

The people recognize this. They spread their cloaks on the road—an act of coronation that echoes the moment when Jehu was proclaimed king (2 Kings 9:13). They cut branches from the fields and wave them, forming a living carpet for their king. His kingly authority is evident in His command to the disciples: "The Lord needs it." With a word, He sets events in motion.

Yet as Jesus enters the temple that evening, something peculiar happens. Despite the crowds, despite the hosannas, He simply looks around and then leaves. It's an anticlimax—the red carpet moment fades to silence.

### The King Comes to Judge (11:11-21)

The next morning reveals Jesus' real purpose. He is hungry and sees a fig tree in the distance. Though it's not the season for figs, the tree displays leaves—which would normally indicate fruit. Jesus approaches looking for food and finds nothing. His response is jarring: "May no one ever eat fruit from you again."

This seems unfair. Mark himself notes it wasn't the season for figs. But the point isn't about the tree—it's about what the tree represents. The strange phrase "it was not the season for figs" is best understood as explaining why the tree has leaves at all. Even though it's not the season, there are leaves—false promise without substance. Jesus curses it as a metaphor for Israel and her leaders: all appearance, no fruit.

As Jesus enters the temple, He immediately begins driving out the merchants and money changers. These weren't simply greedy businessmen; they were serving a function during Passover. Pilgrims from across the world needed to exchange foreign currency, and animals were necessary for sacrifice. Yet Jesus sees corruption at the heart of it all.

His indictment is based on Scripture: "My house will be called a house of prayer for all nations" (Isaiah 56:6-7). The temple's purpose, rooted in Solomon's dedication prayer (1 Kings 8:29-30), was to be a place where all people could come before God. Instead, it has become "a den of robbers"—a phrase from Jeremiah (7:9-11) describing a place where criminals hide behind the appearance of religion.

The leaders of Israel were meant to be servants to the nations, yet they exploited them instead. The temple—designed as an inclusive place of prayer—had been perverted into a system of greed. The illustration Kenny offers is apt: imagine if the Royal Easter Show, once devoted to agriculture, became nothing but commercial entertainment. That's the fundamental error here.

In response, the chief priests and teachers of the law "began looking for a way to kill him, for they feared him, because the whole crowd was amazed at his teaching" (11:18). Jesus has exposed their corruption, and they cannot tolerate it.

### Understanding the Fig Tree

The fig tree's withering becomes clearer when we understand Old Testament symbolism. In Micah's vision of the Messianic age, everyone sits "under their own vine and under their own fig tree" (Micah 4:4)—an image of peace and prosperity. But Jeremiah warns of judgment: "The nations will devour your harvests and food... they will devour your vines and fig trees" (Jeremiah 5:17).

The fig tree becomes an illustration of Israel's spiritual condition. It's all leaves and no fruit—appearance without substance, promise without fulfillment.

Consider what Jesus knew as He approached Jerusalem. Mark tells us earlier that He expected to die: "We are going up to Jerusalem, and the Son of Man will be delivered over to the chief priests and the teachers of the law... they will condemn him to death" (10:33-34). He wasn't expecting much. It was not the season for hope.

But then He saw the leaves. He saw the crowd waving palm branches, heard them crying "Hosanna!" For a moment, there was hope—perhaps there were still some faithful ones, some who genuinely sought Him. Yet when He arrives at the temple, His hope is dashed. There is no fruit. The leaders are corrupt. The temple is defiled. Down to its roots, the tree is diseased.

### The King's Offer of Salvation (11:22-25)

What follows seems to come from nowhere. After condemning the temple and cursing the fig tree, Jesus suddenly says: "Have faith in God."

The disciples are afraid. They've witnessed Jesus' power over nature and His judgment of the corrupt religious establishment. What might He do to them? So Jesus reassures them with a promise: "Truly I tell you, if anyone says to this mountain, 'Go, throw yourself into the sea,' and does not doubt in their heart but believes that what they say will happen, it will be done for them" (11:23).

The temple sits on Mount Moriah. This is an invitation to see beyond the corrupted institution—to pray toward the true temple, which is Christ Himself. When we pray with genuine faith toward God, He promises to hear and to forgive.

> Therefore I tell you, whatever you ask for in prayer, believe that you have received it, and it will be yours. (11:24)

This isn't a promise about cars or school grades. It's grounded in Solomon's prayer at the temple's dedication: "Hear from heaven, your dwelling place, and when you hear, forgive" (1 Kings 8:30). The first prayer God guarantees to answer is the prayer for forgiveness. Ask and you shall receive. Seek and you shall find.

But there's a condition: "When you stand praying, if you hold anything against anyone, forgive them, so that your Father in heaven may forgive you your sins" (11:25).

Standing in prayer symbolizes readiness to go seek reconciliation. Forgiveness isn't just a feeling we nurture in private prayer—it's an action we pursue in our relationships. We cannot ask God for forgiveness while holding grudges against others.

### The Hidden Question

The passage concludes with the authorities challenging Jesus: "By what authority are you doing these things?" (11:27-28). Jesus responds with a counter-question about John's baptism, and the leaders, trapped by fear of the crowd, refuse to answer.

Neither will Jesus tell them directly. But the answer is implicit: He comes with the authority of God Himself. He judges the corrupt. He cleanses the defiled. And He offers salvation to those who believe.

## Application

Jesus came first as judge—to expose corruption, to cleanse the temple, to declare that words without fruit are worthless. But He came also to save. The same passage that reveals His judgment offers His mercy: forgiveness for those who ask, reconciliation offered to those who forgive.

As you stand in prayer, consider: Are you holding anything against anyone? Are you merely displaying leaves—the appearance of faith without the substance of obedience and love? Or are you bearing fruit?

The clearer passages of Scripture interpret the less clear. When facing a difficult text, keep reading until something clearer helps explain what's puzzling. *Scripture interprets scripture.* This principle guides our understanding of even the strangest details in God's Word.
