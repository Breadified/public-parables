# Zechariah 12:1-13:2

**Title:** The Day of the Lord: A Good Day and a Bad Day
**Date:** 20 December 2020
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Introduction: What Day Are You Looking Forward To?

We look forward to many special days throughout the year—exam results, Christmas celebrations, family reunions, New Year's Day. But there is another day that deserves our attention: the Day of the Lord. This is a bittersweet day, both good and bad, and Zechariah prophesies about it with remarkable detail and spiritual depth.

Zechariah wrote in the 6th century BC, and his book divides into two clear movements. The first eight chapters focus on rebuilding the temple after exile. The remaining chapters shift our perspective from the physical building to what happens when God returns to His temple—the coming Day of the Lord.

---

## Part One: A Good Day (12:8-9)

### Divine Protection and Transformation

On the Day of the Lord, God promises protection and empowerment to His people:

> On that day the Lord will shield those who live in Jerusalem, so that the feeblest among them will be like David, and the house of David will be like God, like the angel of the Lord going before them. On that day I will set out to destroy all the nations that attack Jerusalem.

The imagery here is striking: a complete reversal and elevation. The weakest will be strengthened to warrior-king status; the royal house will possess divine power. This is not mere military victory but a fundamental transformation through God's supernatural intervention.

### Jerusalem as an Immovable Rock

Zechariah uses two powerful metaphors to describe Jerusalem's indestructibility:

**The Cup That Pours Out Poison** (12:2)

> I am going to make Jerusalem a cup that sends all the surrounding peoples reeling.

The enemies see Jerusalem as a prize to be conquered—like a cup of wine ready to drink. But when they breach the walls, what flows out is not sweet nectar but poison. Those who attack reeling, stumbling, gasping for air, brought low by what they thought would be easy conquest.

**The Immovable Rock** (12:3)

> On that day, when all the nations of the earth are gathered against her, I will make Jerusalem an immovable rock for all the nations. All who try to move it will injure themselves.

Imagine discovering that a small stone you kicked is actually the exposed part of a massive foundation rock. The harder you kick, the greater your own injury. This is the irony of God's protection: the enemy's own strength and weapons become instruments of their defeat.

### God's Eyes on Judah

> I will keep a watchful eye over Judah, but I will blind all the horses of the nations.

Horses were the war machines of ancient armies, particularly those of Egypt. They had superior vision—360-degree sight with magnified perception. But on that day, God strips away this advantage. The horses panic, bucking their riders, running into one another. Their greatest strength becomes their greatest weakness.

This echoes the exodus:

> Sing to the Lord, for he is highly exalted. Both horse and driver he has hurled into the sea. (Exodus 15:21)

By contrast, God is not blind. His watchful eye remains on His people, protecting and preserving them.

### Clan Unity Through Divine Strength

> The clans of Judah will say in their hearts, 'The people of Jerusalem are strong, because the Lord Almighty is their God.'

Even the surrounding clans recognize the source of this strength—not military prowess or clever strategy, but the presence of the Lord Almighty.

### Fire Consuming Fire

> On that day I will make the clans of Judah like a firepot in a woodpile, like a flaming torch among sheaves. They will consume all the surrounding peoples right and left, but Jerusalem will remain intact in her place.

The invaders bring fire as a weapon of siege. But whatever they use against God's people returns upon them with greater force. The fire meant to destroy becomes the means of judgment against the attackers. It's a pattern we see in every story: the villain's own weapon becomes the instrument of their downfall.

God has a sense of irony woven into His justice.

## Part Two: A Bad Day (12:10-13:1)

### Mourning for the Pierced One

The good day comes only through a bad day:

> They will look on me, the one they have pierced, and they will mourn for him as one mourns for an only child, and grieve bitterly for him as one grieves for a firstborn son. On that day the weeping in Jerusalem will be as great as the weeping of Hadad Rimmon in the plain of Megiddo.

The phrase "the one they have pierced" is deliberately ambiguous. Zechariah may be referring to himself, the prophet who was martyred (Luke 11:50). But more likely, this passage points forward to someone else.

### A Shadow of King Josiah

The historical echoes are striking. King Josiah, the last good king of Judah, was young and righteous. He reformed the temple, removing idols and fighting for the kingdom. Yet:

> While Josiah was king, Pharaoh Necho king of Egypt went up to the Euphrates River to help the king of Assyria. King Josiah marched out to meet him in battle, but Necho faced him and killed him at Megiddo. (2 Kings 23:29)

Archers pierced him:

> Archers shot King Josiah, and he told his officers, "Take me away; I am badly wounded." So they took him out of his chariot, put him in his other chariot and brought him to Jerusalem, where he died. (2 Chronicles 35:23-24)

The people mourned greatly:

> All Judah and Jerusalem mourned for him. Jeremiah composed laments for Josiah, and to this day all the male and female singers commemorate Josiah in the laments. (2 Chronicles 35:24-25)

### The Ultimate Fulfillment: Jesus Christ

But Zechariah prophesies of someone greater. History reveals a figure who matches this description far more perfectly:

- Young, in His 30s
- Righteous, cleansing the temple of corruption
- He entered Jerusalem not on horses but on a donkey
- He was God's only begotten Son
- He was pierced—not by arrows, but by crucifixion and spear

> When he had received the drink, Jesus said, "It is finished." With that, he bowed his head and gave up his spirit... Instead, one of the soldiers pierced Jesus' side with a spear, bringing a sudden flow of blood and water. These things happened so that the scripture would be fulfilled: "Not one of his bones will be broken," and, as another scripture says, "They will look on the one they have pierced." (John 19:30, 34, 36-37)

The blood and water that flowed from His pierced side would become:

> On that day a fountain will be opened to the house of David and the inhabitants of Jerusalem, to cleanse them from sin and impurity. (13:1)

This is not a literal fountain in Jerusalem, but the cleansing fountain that flows from Christ's sacrifice. His piercing cleanses us from sin.

### The Response of Witnessing

Those who saw understood:

> The centurion, seeing what had happened, praised God and said, "Surely this was a righteous man." When all the people who had gathered to witness this sight saw what took place, they beat their breasts and went away. A large number of people followed him, including women who mourned and wailed for him. (Luke 23:47-48, 27)

He gave up His spirit so that we might receive the Spirit. He was pierced so we wouldn't have to be. His mourning becomes our joy.

## Part Three: The Repentance of the Leadership (12:12-14)

### The Houses That Mourn

> The land will mourn, each clan by itself, with their wives by themselves: the clan of the house of David and their wives, the clan of the house of Nathan and their wives, the clan of the house of Levi and their wives, the clan of Shimei and their wives, and all the rest of the clans and their wives.

Who are these figures? Some scholars suggest they represent the kingly line (David and Nathan, Solomon's brother) and the priestly line (Levi and Shimei). Others propose they were members of David's own court—the leaders and officers of his reign.

More intriguingly, these names trace through the genealogy of Jesus Himself. Luke's genealogy of Jesus runs through David, Nathan, Levi, and Shimei—the very line that would produce the Messiah.

The mourning of these houses is deeply personal: they mourn one of their own. The genealogy that produced the Messiah also produced His rejection and death. Christmas points to what Jesus came to accomplish for us, and it didn't come cheaply.

### A Day That Points to Grace

The good day comes only after the bad day. Safety comes only through the piercing. Protection comes only through sacrifice.

---

## Conclusion: Christmas Points to the Cross

As we celebrate Christmas, we celebrate the birth of the One who came to be pierced for us. The comfort of a Babe in a manger is inseparable from the cup of suffering He would drink and the spear that would pierce His side.

On the Day of the Lord, enemies will be destroyed and God's people will be exalted. But that exaltation was purchased by piercing. That safety was won through sacrifice. That joy rose from mourning.

Christmas didn't come cheap. It came at the cost of a cross.

> Behold, this Child is appointed to cause the rise and fall of many in Israel, and to be a sign that will be spoken against, so that the thoughts of many hearts will be revealed—and a sword will pierce your soul as well. (Luke 2:34-35)
