# Zechariah 3:1-4:14

**Title:** A New Leader
**Date:** November 20, 2016 (SMAC); November 22, 2020 (ARISE)
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction: Two Leaders?

America's recent leadership transition involves more than just a popular vote—electors, certifiers, legislators, and governors all play a role. Some have imagined novel scenarios: Scott Adams envisioned two presidents, with half the country following one leader and half another, or one inaugurated while another awaits a Supreme Court decision months later.

A similar complexity exists in Israel's restoration. As Zechariah reveals his visions, we discover that God envisions two leaders rebuilding Jerusalem and the temple after the exile from Babylon.

Zechariah's opening message is clear: "Return to me and I will return to you." God calls the Jews, freshly returned from captivity under the Persian empire, to rebuild both the temple and Jerusalem. The question becomes: who will lead this restoration?

Today we examine the fourth and fifth visions of Zechariah, beginning with chapter 4, since these dreams are not presented chronologically.

## Vision 1: The Lampstand and Two Olive Trees (4:1-14)

### The Lampstand: God's Presence

> Then the angel who talked with me returned and woke me up, like someone awakened from sleep. He asked me, "What do you see?" I answered, "I see a solid gold lampstand with a bowl at the top and seven lamps on it, with seven channels to the lamps." (4:1-2)

The lampstand symbolizes God's presence among His people:
- **One lampstand and one bowl** represent the one God
- **Seven lamps** symbolize completeness (seven is the number of perfection throughout Scripture)
- **Seven channels** fed oil to keep the lamps perpetually burning, representing God's ever-constant presence

This imagery draws directly from the temple instructions in Exodus. Solomon later completed this menorah for the first temple. In the New Testament, John reveals that the seven lampstands represent the seven churches—God's presence continues among His people through His body.

### Two Olive Trees: Two Anointed Leaders

> Also there are two olive trees by it, one on the right of the bowl and the other on its left. (4:3)

The angel explains the mystery later:

> These are the two who are anointed to serve the Lord of all the earth. (4:14)

The word "anointed" (messiah) literally means "oiled"—a play on words, since olive oil flows from these trees through golden pipes to feed the lampstand. These two olive trees represent two people anointed by God to rebuild the temple and Jerusalem.

### Zerubbabel: The Civic Leader

The first anointed leader is Zerubbabel, the governor of Judah, appointed by the Persian king. God speaks directly to him:

> This is the word of the Lord to Zerubbabel: Not by might nor by power, but by my Spirit, says the Lord Almighty. What are you, mighty mountain? Before Zerubbabel you will become level ground. (6:6-7)

Zerubbabel oversees the entire construction project—laying the foundation and completing the temple:

> The hands of Zerubbabel have laid the foundation of this temple; his hands will also complete it. Then you will know that the Lord Almighty has sent me to you. (4:9)

God even warns against despising this work as "a day of small things":

> Who dares despise the day of small things, since the seven eyes of the Lord that range throughout the earth will rejoice when they see the chosen capstone in the hand of Zerubbabel? (4:10)

Zerubbabel is a descendant of the royal line—his grandfather was Jehoiachin, the last king of Judah before exile.

### Joshua: The Spiritual Leader

> Then he showed me Joshua the high priest standing before the angel of the Lord, and Satan standing at his right side to accuse him. (3:1)

The second leader is Joshua, the high priest. His name recalls the great Joshua of old who led Israel into the promised land—this new Joshua will re-lead God's people back to their spiritual home. As high priest, he heads the temple and represents the people before God.

The prophet Haggai, a contemporary of Zechariah, confirms both leaders:

> The Lord stirred up the spirit of Zerubbabel son of Shealtiel, governor of Judah, and the spirit of Joshua son of Jozadak, the high priest, and the spirit of the whole remnant of the people. They came and began to work on the house of the Lord Almighty. (Haggai 1:14)

## The System: Separation of Powers

At first glance, two leaders seems problematic. Leadership theory typically demands one leader—two power centers invite chaos. Yet God deliberately establishes this structure with one political leader (Zerubbabel, representing the Persian authority) and one spiritual leader (Joshua, representing the people).

This reflects a profound biblical principle: **the separation of powers**.

This doctrine did not originate in England or America. The Jewish people, thousands of years ago, understood that concentrated power breeds tyranny. By distributing authority between political and spiritual spheres, neither leader could dominate completely.

### Why Separation of Powers Matters

History teaches us that concentrated power corrupts. As power consolidates, tyranny becomes inevitable. The inefficiency of separated powers—the gridlock, the checking and balancing—is actually a **feature, not a flaw**. It protects the people.

We see this principle validated even in modern governance:
- Executive vs. judicial vs. legislative branches
- Local vs. state vs. federal governments
- Multiple competing centers of authority

When one entity has all the power and all the guns, no people-based check can stop tyranny. But when government faces government, genuine accountability emerges.

The alternative—unified government under one leader—sounds efficient until that leader turns corrupt. History shows the pattern: one good king, followed by ten bad ones. Even the United States, designed with separation of powers, faces constant tension between branches. Yet this tension protects liberty.

## The Problems: Three Crises

Yet Zechariah's vision of two leaders faces serious obstacles.

### Problem 1: The Curse on Zerubbabel's Line

Zerubbabel descends from Jehoiachin, one of Judah's last kings before exile. But there is a curse upon this line:

> As surely as I live, declares the Lord, even if you, Jehoiachin son of Jehoiakim king of Judah, were a signet ring on my right hand, I would still pull you off. (Jeremiah 24:24)

God promises:

> Record this man as if childless, a man who will not prosper in his lifetime, for none of his offspring will prosper, none will sit on the throne of David or rule anymore in Judah. (Jeremiah 23:30)

If no descendant of Jehoiachin can be king, how can Zerubbabel serve as governor? How can the cursed line lead God's people?

### Problem 2: The Priest Cannot Be King

The second problem is equally troubling. Joshua, the high priest, is anointed as king:

> Take the silver and gold and make a crown, and set it on the head of the high priest, Joshua son of Jozadak. Tell him this is what the Lord Almighty says: Here is the man whose name is the Branch, and he will branch out from his place and build the temple of the Lord. It is he who will build the temple of the Lord, and he will be clothed with majesty and will sit and rule on his throne. And he will be a priest on his throne. And there will be harmony between the two. (6:11-13)

Yet Scripture explicitly forbids priests to be kings. The tribes of Israel had specialized roles:

> The Levitical priests—indeed, the whole tribe of Levi—are to have no allotment or inheritance with Israel... The Lord is their inheritance, as he promised them. (Deuteronomy 18:1-2)

Priests inherit no land, hold no dynastic power. Kings come from Judah:

> The scepter will not depart from Judah, nor the rulers staff from between his feet, until he to whom it belongs shall come. (Genesis 49:10)

Furthermore, kings cannot serve as high priests. Aaron's descendants alone hold that office. When King Uzziah attempted to burn incense—a priestly function—God struck him with leprosy:

> It is not right for you, Uzziah, to burn incense to the Lord. That is for the priests, the descendants of Aaron, who have been consecrated to burn incense. (2 Chronicles 26:18)

Yet here, in post-exilic restoration, Joshua the priest wears a crown. Why would God violate His own laws?

### Problem 3: The Leader Is Filthy

The deepest problem emerges in the first vision. Joshua stands before God, but he is clothed in filthy garments:

> Now Joshua was dressed in filthy clothes as he stood before the angel. (3:3)

Satan stands at his right side, accusing him:

> Satan standing at his right side to accuse him. (3:1)

God calls him a "burning stick snatched from the fire"—rescued from destruction, but barely. A filthy priest cannot represent the people before God, much less before the world. His corruption disqualifies him.

Yet God does not abandon Joshua in his filthiness. The Lord rebukes Satan:

> The Lord said to Satan, The Lord rebuke you, Satan! The Lord, who has chosen Jerusalem, rebuke you! Is not this man a burning stick snatched from the fire? (3:2)

Then God acts decisively to cleanse him:

> The angel said to those who were standing before him, "Take off his filthy clothes." Then he said to Joshua, "See, I have taken away your sin, and I will put fine garments on you." Then I said, "Put a clean turban on his head." So they put a clean turban on his head and clothed him, while the angel of the Lord stood by. (3:4-5)

God removes Joshua's filthy garments, takes away his sin, and clothes him in pure priestly vestments symbolizing holiness and renewal. He receives a clean turban—new garments for a new beginning.

God then renews the covenant:

> The angel of the Lord gave this charge to Joshua: "This is what the Lord Almighty says: If you will walk in obedience to me and keep my requirements, then you will govern my house and have charge of my courts, and I will give you a place among these standing here." (3:6-7)

God promises that Joshua can govern the temple and dwell among God's messengers—but only if he remains obedient. This echoes the original covenant: obedience brings blessing.

## The Solution: The Branch

These tensions—the curse, the broken law, the sinfulness—point to an impossible situation. Yet Zechariah hints at resolution:

> Listen, High Priest Joshua, you and your associates seated before you, who are men symbolic of things to come: I am going to bring my servant, the Branch. See, the stone I have set in front of Joshua! There are seven eyes on that one stone, and I will engrave an inscription on it, says the Lord Almighty, and I will remove the sin of this land in a single day. In that day each of you will invite your neighbor to sit under your vine and fig tree, declares the Lord Almighty. (3:8-10)

The mixing of names and roles in chapter 6 suggests something profound: God will one day unite these two offices in a single person. One leader who is both king and priest, both political and spiritual authority.

This finds its ultimate fulfillment in Jesus Christ.

### Jesus: The Perfect King and Priest

Five hundred years later, Jesus emerges as the one leader who fulfills both roles and solves all three problems.

**Jesus is King:** He descends from the line of Judah, not cursed but blessed. He sits at God's right hand, anointed as the Messiah—the chosen King.

**Jesus is Priest:** Though not from the tribe of Levi, He serves as a priest in the order of Melchizedek—a higher priesthood that transcends the old tribal system:

> For it is clear that our Lord descended from Judah, and in regard to that tribe Moses said nothing about priests. And what we have said is even more clear if another priest like Melchizedek appears, one who has become a priest not on the basis of a regulation as to his ancestry but on the basis of the power of an indestructible life. For it is declared: "You are a priest forever, in the order of Melchizedek." (Hebrews 7:14-17)

**Jesus is Sinless:** Where every priest from Aaron onward failed, where every king from Saul to Zedekiah fell into sin, Jesus stands alone:

> Such a high priest truly meets our need—one who is holy, blameless, pure, set apart from sinners, exalted above the heavens. Therefore he is able to save completely those who come to God through him, because he always lives to intercede for them. (Hebrews 7:26, 7:25)

In Jesus, justice and mercy harmonize perfectly. He rules justly as King while mediating for us as Priest. The two roles, impossibly separated in the old covenant, find their perfect expression in one person.

## Application: Who Is Your Leader?

The restoration under Zerubbabel and Joshua was incomplete. A divided leadership, though better than tyranny, still reflects the brokenness of a fallen world. We live in an age of multiple leaders—political, spiritual, cultural—and this fragmentation is often necessary to prevent tyranny.

But the proliferation of leaders also means we never have one we can fully trust. World leaders come and go. They become corrupt. They lose office. They fail us.

Yet there is one Leader who does not:

- Never gets voted out
- Never succumbs to dementia or corruption
- Always acts for our ultimate good
- Reigns both now and forever

**The question is not merely: who is leading your country, your church, your community?**

**The deeper question is: who is your ultimate leader?**

Jesus is the promised Branch—the sinless Priest who removes our sin in a single day. He is the perfect King who will one day reign over all. In Him, the separation of powers gives way to the perfect unity of authority and love.

We long for a unified government because we dream of finding a wonderful leader. But history proves that good leaders turn corrupt, and corrupt leaders bring tyranny. The solution is not better human leaders.

The solution is Jesus.

Let us follow God's chosen Leader.
