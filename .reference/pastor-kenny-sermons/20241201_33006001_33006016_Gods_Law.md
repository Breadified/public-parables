# Micah 6:1-16

**Title:** God's Law
**Date:** 8 December 2024
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## The Courtroom Drama

Micah 6 presents God's case against His people as a legal proceeding—a courtroom drama. The prophet invokes the mountains and hills as witnesses while God brings charges against Judah for covenant violation.

## Subpoena (6:1-2)

> Listen to what the Lord says:
>
> "Stand up, plead my case before the mountains;
>
> let the hills hear what you have to say.
>
> Hear, you mountains, the Lord's accusation;
>
> listen, you everlasting foundations of the earth.
>
> For the Lord has a case against his people;
>
> he is lodging a charge against Israel.

The opening imperative summons the parties to court with legal terminology familiar to any courtroom: "hear ye, hear ye—court is now in session." God positions creation itself as witnesses to His complaint against His people. The Lord stands as prosecutor, plaintiff, and judge, bringing charges against Judah, the southern kingdom.

## The Accusation (6:3-5)

> My people, what have I done to you?
>
> How have I burdened you? Answer me.

God poses this question not to gather information but to expose the nature of the accusation: **Israel had become weary of the Lord—they viewed God as burdensome.** Like ungrateful children who take an aging parent for granted, Israel grew impatient and abandoned their covenant responsibilities. They stopped performing the duties they owed to God.

### God's Evidence

> I brought you up out of Egypt
>
> and redeemed you from the land of slavery.
>
> I sent Moses to lead you,
>
> also Aaron and Miriam.

God rehearses His faithful acts: liberation from slavery, provision of leadership through Moses, Aaron, and Miriam. The evidence is overwhelming that God had done nothing to weary Israel. Rather, He had been faithful and gracious throughout their journey.

> Remember
>
> what Balak king of Moab plotted
>
> and what Balaam son of Beor answered.
>
> [Remember your journey] from Shittim to Gilgal,
>
> that you may know the righteous acts of the Lord.

Shittim was Israel's final encampment in the wilderness after leaving Egypt (Numbers 33:49). Gilgal was their first settlement after crossing the Jordan into the Promised Land (Joshua 4:19). Between these two places, much happened.

When Balak, king of Moab, feared Israel's military victories, he hired the seer Balaam to curse them. Four times Balaam refused—Israel was blessed by the Lord. But when direct cursing failed, Moab tried a different strategy. At Shittim, they enticed Israel into sexual immorality and worship of Baal:

> While Israel was staying in Shittim, the men began to indulge in sexual immorality with Moabite women, who invited them to the sacrifices to their gods. The people ate the sacrificial meal and bowed down before these gods. So Israel yoked themselves to the Baal of Peor. And the Lord's anger burned against them. (Numbers 25:1-3)

Even when enemies could not curse Israel, they could be seduced into idolatry. Yet despite this disobedience, God remained faithful. Over and over in the wilderness—manna and quail from the sky, water from the rock, defeat of their enemies—God demonstrated His faithfulness. And over and over the people complained, rebelled, and disobeyed. Micah reminded them of this pattern to call them to repentance before judgment fell again.

## Israel's Defence (6:6-7)

> With what shall I come before the Lord
>
> and bow down before the exalted God?
>
> Shall I come before him with burnt offerings,
>
> with calves a year old?
>
> Will the Lord be pleased with thousands of rams,
>
> with ten thousand rivers of olive oil?
>
> Shall I offer my firstborn for my transgression,
>
> the fruit of my body for the sin of my soul?

Israel responds to God's indictment with its own rhetorical questions, implying that the accusation is unfounded. Yet their defense reveals a fundamental misunderstanding of what God desires.

Notice the intensification of their offers: from the best animal sacrifices (calves a year old) to exaggerated quantities (thousands of rams, ten thousand rivers of oil) to the desperate abomination of child sacrifice. Their underlying assumption: **these things will constitute an adequate defense.**

But is this what God really wants?

> Does the Lord delight in burnt offerings and sacrifices as much as in obeying the Lord? To obey is better than sacrifice, and to heed is better than the fat of rams. (1 Samuel 15:22)
>
> For I desire mercy, not sacrifice, and acknowledgment of God rather than burnt offerings. (Hosea 6:6)

Israel had converted the sacrificial system—designed to deal with sin—into a means of avoiding obedience and mercy. They thought external ritual could substitute for internal transformation.

## God's Rebuttal (6:8)

> He has shown you, O mortal, what is good.
>
> And what does the Lord require of you?
>
> To act justly and to love mercy
>
> and to walk humbly with your God.

This verse crystallizes God's requirement. He has already shown Israel what is good through Moses (justice and law), Aaron (mercy and the temple system), and their shared history. Now He makes explicit what He requires: **three inseparable virtues.**

**To act justly** means to uphold right and wrong—to administer justice based on law, not favoritism. This reflects the substance of the Ten Commandments.

**To love mercy** means to give freely what others do not deserve. Mercy is the opposite of justice; where justice gives people what they deserve, mercy gives them what they don't. This finds expression in forgiveness and charity.

**To walk humbly with your God** means to move through life in humility before the Lord—not merely performing external acts, but maintaining a posture of submission and reverence.

Jesus fulfills all three requirements: He fulfills the demands of justice (sin must be punished) through His death on the cross, and He fulfills the requirements of mercy through grace extended to us.

## The Verdict (6:9-12)

> Listen! The Lord is calling to the city—
>
> and to fear your name is wisdom—
>
> "Heed the rod and the One who appointed it.
>
> Am I still to forget your ill-gotten treasures, you wicked house,
>
> and the short ephah, which is accursed?
>
> Shall I acquit someone with dishonest scales,
>
> with a bag of false weights?
>
> Your rich people are violent;
>
> your inhabitants are liars
>
> and their tongues speak deceitfully.

The verdict focuses on concrete injustice in the marketplace. Israel's wealthy had grown rich through deception: using false scales, dishonest weights, and corrupt dealings. The people had become violent and deceitful to accumulate wealth.

### Justice vs. Social Justice

An important distinction must be made: **the justice condemned here is not "social justice" in the modern sense.**

The rich in verse 12 are judged not because they are rich, but because they are violent and cheat. That is true justice—addressing actual wrongs.

"Social justice," by contrast, often means that because one group seems poorer and another richer, we should take from the wealthy and redistribute to engineer society according to our preferred image. But if wealth was gained through legitimate means—if no crime was committed—then taking it by force is itself an injustice. It breaks the laws "Do not steal" and "Do not covet."

True justice requires identifying and punishing actual wrongdoing. It cannot manufacture crimes to justify redistributing wealth.

### Mercy and Justice: Two Different Virtues

We rightly sympathize with those who are poor or underprivileged. But solving their plight is not a matter of justice—it is a matter of mercy. **Mercy is to give someone something they do not deserve.** Forgiveness is mercy. Charity is mercy. Helping someone in need is mercy.

Mercy can only be freely given; it cannot be compelled. When you demand that someone else show mercy, you have destroyed the very nature of mercy itself.

This Saturday's letterboxing event and Sunday's meal for international students exemplify true mercy. We pool resources and volunteer time not because the students owe us anything or because we owe them something by right. We do it freely, recognizing that many students are alone in a foreign country—some poor, some far from home—and we have opportunity to express Christian compassion.

## The Problem: Double Lives (6:9-12)

The Jews in Micah's day were living double lives. Outwardly, they appeared as God-fearers with elaborate sacrifices. Inwardly, they lacked justice, mercy, and humility before God.

We face a similar temptation. Many of us call ourselves evangelicals—people who love the gospel and the Bible. We spend time studying Scripture, attending conferences, learning exegesis and hermeneutics. We read the Bible, read books about the Bible, and talk about the Bible.

**But sometimes, that is all we love.** Where is justice? Where is mercy?

> Religion that God our Father accepts as pure and faultless is this: to look after orphans and widows in their distress and to keep oneself from being polluted by the world. (James 1:27)

We can have a form of godliness while denying its power:

> Having a form of godliness but denying its power. (2 Timothy 3:5)

Sometimes we confuse loving what is right in the Bible with simply being right. As though intellectual correctness is sufficient.

### A Challenge to Action

The knowledge we possess is not a source of pride; it is a gift meant to help us serve others better. **The more you know, the more you ought to do.** If we think ourselves wiser than others, we should demonstrate that wisdom through greater service, not through criticism.

Evangelicals have a distinguished history as activists—Wilberforce and the Puritans engaged in justice not just in families and churches, but in society, politics, and business. Yet today we risk becoming mere theorists, spouting correct answers while our transformed lives remain invisible.

Some of us talk enthusiastically about evangelism and mission, yet rarely engage international students or serve beyond our comfort zones. Do our actions match our words?

Some of us celebrate God-given gifts and the call to glorify God, yet we only serve when there is recognition. We serve when friends are watching, when it is visible and upfront. But if serving means working with a "nobody" alone, where no one knows, suddenly we are busy. Is that for God's glory or our own? **Walk humbly.**

Some of us speak eloquently about the gospel's forgiveness, yet we nurture grudges and withhold forgiveness from those who hurt us. **Show mercy.**

Some of us champion biblical justice, yet when political issues arise at church, we object and wonder why we are discussing politics instead of the Bible. **Do justice.**

God's definition of true religion is not exhausted in reading the Bible correctly. He is creating us not to be theoretical Christians, but to take action.

## Punishment (6:13-16)

> Therefore, I have begun to destroy you,
>
> to ruin you because of your sins.
>
> You will eat but not be satisfied;
>
> your stomach will still be empty.
>
> You will store up but save nothing,
>
> because what you save I will give to the sword.
>
> You will plant but not harvest;
>
> you will press olives but not use the oil,
>
> you will crush grapes but not drink the wine.

The punishment contains poetic justice: just as Israel's outward religious observance lacked substance, so their external prosperity will lack substance. They will go through the motions of life—eating, storing, planting, harvesting—but find no satisfaction or lasting benefit.

> You have observed the statutes of Omri
>
> and all the practices of Ahab's house;
>
> you have followed their traditions.
>
> Therefore I will give you over to ruin
>
> and your people to derision;
>
> you will bear the scorn of the nations.

The reference to Omri and Ahab—kings known for leading Israel into idolatry—shows that the people have embraced evil practices and traditions. The consequence is inevitable: destruction and national shame.

## The Call to Transformation

The same requirement stands before us:

> He has shown you, O mortal, what is good.
>
> And what does the Lord require of you?
>
> To act justly and to love mercy
>
> and to walk humbly with your God.

This is not a call to mere intellectual assent. It is a summons to transformed living—to alignment between what we profess and how we actually live. The knowledge we have been given creates responsibility. Our claim to love God's Word obligates us to obey it, not merely to understand it.
