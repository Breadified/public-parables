# Matthew 26:47–27:26

**Title:** Betraying the King  
**Date:** 4 April 2011 (Revised 19 October 2020)  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## The Violence of Betrayal

Few experiences cut as deeply as betrayal. When someone you've trusted turns against you, it strikes at the heart of human relationship. We've all witnessed it in the public sphere—leaders stabbed in the back by their closest allies, overnight transformed from power to irrelevance. But the pain of betrayal is never merely political; it's deeply personal.

Today we step into Jesus's sandals as he faces a devastating series of betrayals—not just from individuals, but from his own people, his nation, and those who should have protected him.

---

## 1. Betrayed by His Disciples

### Judas's Kiss (26:47–54)

> While he was still speaking, Judas, one of the Twelve, arrived. With him was a large crowd armed with swords and clubs, sent from the chief priests and the elders of the people. Now the betrayer had arranged a signal with them: "The one I kiss is the man; arrest him." Going at once to Jesus, Judas said, "Greetings, Rabbi!" and kissed him.

The scene unfolds in the Garden of Gethsemane during Passover—a crowded time in Jerusalem when pilgrims flooded the city seeking accommodation. Many camped in gardens like Gethsemane, making it difficult to identify individuals in a crowd.

Judas had agreed to betray Jesus for thirty pieces of silver. Notice his greeting: he calls Jesus "Rabbi"—teacher. In Matthew's gospel, only Jesus's enemies address him this way. The kiss, a gesture of affection, becomes an instrument of betrayal.

> Jesus replied, "Do what you came for, friend." Then the men stepped forward, seized Jesus and arrested him.

There's a striking gentleness in Jesus's response. He doesn't protest or resist. He moves toward his destiny with clarity.

### The Disciples' Failure (26:51–56)

One of Jesus's companions instinctively draws his sword and strikes the servant of the high priest, cutting off his ear. War erupts—not the tanks-and-planes kind, but the warfare of weapons drawn against weapons. Yet Jesus immediately stops it:

> "Put your sword back in its place, for all who draw the sword will die by the sword. Do you think I cannot call on my Father, and he will at once put at my disposal more than twelve legions of angels? But how then would the Scriptures be fulfilled that say it must happen in this way?"

The disciples fundamentally misunderstand Jesus's mission. They expect him to fight. A Roman legion consisted of six thousand soldiers; twelve legions would total seventy-two thousand—representing all of Israel's people coming to protect their king. Jesus had the power. He had the right to self-defense. Yet he refuses.

> "Am I leading a rebellion, that you have come out with swords and clubs to capture me? Every day I sat in the temple courts teaching, and you did not arrest me. But this has all taken place that the writings of the prophets might be fulfilled."

Jesus is not claiming that war itself is always wrong. He commends centurions and officers of war. Self-defense against evil is legitimate and godly. Controlled violence against violent evil is acceptable. God is Lord of Hosts—the God of armies. But here, now, Jesus will not fight because his mission is the cross. He has already prayed, "Take this cup from me," yet now he sees God's will clearly: this is how the world will be saved, how God and humanity will be reconciled.

The disciples cannot see beyond the immediate moment. They cannot perceive the gospel.

> Then all the disciples deserted him and fled.

Fearful of the authorities, they scatter into the mountains—exactly as Jesus had predicted. All abandon him.

### Peter's Denial (26:69–75)

Even the most loyal falls away. Peter follows Jesus at a distance to the courtyard of the high priest, curious, concerned—or perhaps both. But when a servant girl recognizes him:

> "You also were with Jesus of Galilee," she said. But he denied it before them all. "I don't know what you're talking about," he said.

The accusation comes again from another servant girl: "This fellow was with Jesus of Nazareth."

Again Peter denies, now with an oath: "I don't know the man!"

A third time, bystanders confront him: "Surely you are one of them; your accent gives you away."

> Then he began to call down curses, and he swore to them, "I don't know the man!"

Notice the escalation. First, a simple denial. Then an oath reinforcing the lie. Finally, curses. In the original Greek, the pronouns suggest Peter may not be cursing himself, but cursing Jesus—perhaps even his existence, their friendship, everything Jesus has done for him.

This is what self-preservation looks like. Peter curses:
- The Jesus who called him and his brother from their fishing nets in the Sea of Galilee
- The Jesus whose miracles he witnessed
- The Jesus the multitudes recognized as "the prophet of Galilee"

He swears by heaven and earth that he does not know this man. In the courtyard of the judges, he commits perjury against the one he loves.

> Immediately a rooster crowed. Then Peter remembered the word Jesus had spoken: "Before the rooster crows, you will disown me three times." And he went outside and wept bitterly.

Peter's tears are the tears of someone who has betrayed himself.

## 2. Betrayed by His Nation

### The Jewish Leaders' Trial (26:57–68)

> Those who had arrested Jesus took him to Caiaphas the high priest, where the teachers of the law and the elders had assembled.

The high priests, teachers of the law, and elders formed the Sanhedrin—Israel's governing council. These were the nation's leaders, called to protect their people from injustice and pagan influence. Instead, they betray the very one they should serve.

The trial follows a pattern: false evidence, false trial, sentencing, then abuse.

**False Evidence:**

> The chief priests and the whole Sanhedrin were looking for false evidence against Jesus so that they could put him to death. But they did not find any, though many false witnesses came forward. Finally two came forward and declared, "This fellow said, 'I am able to destroy the temple of God and rebuild it in three days.'"

**The False Trial:**

> Then the high priest stood up and said to Jesus, "Are you not going to answer? What is this testimony that these men are bringing against you?" But Jesus remained silent. The high priest said to him, "I charge you under oath by the living God: Tell us if you are the Messiah, the Son of God."
>
> You have said so, Jesus replied. But I say to all of you: From now on you will see the Son of Man sitting at the right hand of the Mighty One and coming on the clouds of heaven.

To sit at the right hand of God is to share God's authority and power—to be equal with God. Jesus's answer is theologically explosive. It is also true. But the council seizes on it as blasphemy.

> Then the high priest tore his clothes and said, "He has spoken blasphemy! Why do we need any more witnesses? Look, now you have heard the blasphemy."

**The Verdict and Violence:**

> "What do you think?" "He is worthy of death," they answered. Then they spit in his face and struck him with their fists. Others slapped him and said, "Prophesy to us, Messiah. Who hit you?"

Jesus receives the abuse in silence. This fulfills Isaiah's prophecy of the suffering servant:

> I offered my back to those who beat me,
> my cheeks to those who pulled out my beard;
> I did not hide my face from mocking and spitting.

### The Roman Governor's Trial (27:1–19)

The pattern repeats with Pilate. The leaders present their case:

> Early in the morning, all the chief priests and the elders of the people made their plans how to have Jesus executed. So they bound him, led him away and handed him over to Pilate the governor.

Pilate, as Roman governor, controlled taxes, law and order, and the power of death. To maintain empire, Rome installed governors in each province. The leaders now seek his permission to execute Jesus.

**Another False Trial:**

> "Are you the king of the Jews?" asked Pilate. "You have said so," Jesus replied. When he was accused by the chief priests and the elders, he gave no answer.

Jesus maintains silence before his accusers—sometimes wisdom speaks louder than words. False trials thrive on propaganda and noise. Sometimes the most powerful response is refusal to engage.

**Pilate Recognizes Innocence—But Acts Unjustly:**

> For he knew it was out of self-interest that they had handed Jesus over to him.

Pilate understands the politics. He sees through the charges. Yet:

> While Pilate was sitting on the judges seat, his wife sent him a message: "Don't have anything to do with that innocent man, for I have suffered a great deal today in a dream because of him."

Even Pilate's wife recognizes Jesus's innocence. But Pilate, facing pressure from the crowd, abdicates responsibility:

> When Pilate saw that he was getting nowhere, but that instead an uproar was starting, he took water and washed his hands in front of the crowd. "I am innocent of this man's blood," he said. "It is your responsibility!"

The irony cuts deep: "I am innocent"—while condemning an innocent man to death. Justice is not my problem. Doing the right thing is not my problem. Dealing fairly is not my problem.

## 3. Betrayed by His Own People (27:20–26)

### The Choice of Barabbas (27:15–26)

Pilate attempts to save face through a custom: releasing a prisoner chosen by the crowd. At that moment, he has in custody a notorious prisoner named Jesus Barabbas.

> "Which one do you want me to release to you: Jesus Barabbas, or Jesus who is called the Messiah?"

The irony is stark: Jesus versus Jesus. One is a well-known criminal; everyone knows who Barabbas is. The other is the innocent Son of God.

> But the chief priests and the elders persuaded the crowd to ask for Barabbas and to have Jesus executed.

The crowd is swayed. When Pilate asks again:

> "Which of the two do you want me to release to you?" they answered, "Barabbas." "What shall I do, then, with Jesus who is called the Messiah?" "Crucify him!" they shouted. "Why? What crime has he committed?" Pilate asked. But they shouted all the louder, "Crucify him!"
>
> All the people answered, "His blood is on us and on our children!"

They choose the wrong Jesus. The wisdom of crowds can be the blindness of mobs. Democracy without truth becomes tyranny. Yet through this terrible wrong, God works salvation.

**The Substitution:**

This scene embodies the gospel itself. Jesus faces judgment; Barabbas goes free. Jesus faces judgment; a criminal walks away. Jesus faces judgment; we go free.

> Then he released Barabbas to them. But he had Jesus flogged, and handed him over to be crucified.

### Jesus Identifies with Sinners (27:38–57)

Jesus protests only once during the Jewish trial:

> "Am I leading a rebellion, that you have come out with swords and clubs to capture me?"

More literally: "Am I a robber?" He's treated like a criminal, hunted like a thief.

Next week we'll see Jesus die alongside robbers. Yet the real robbers were those in the temple courts. Jesus himself had called the temple "a den of robbers." He was betrayed by that den of robbers. But he dies for that den of robbers.

The accusation that "his blood be on us and on our children" was meant as a curse. But the blood is not on them. As we'll see, the blood is on Jesus. He absorbs it. He bears it.

## The Heart of the Matter

Jesus did not betray us. It is we who betray him.

We are like the crowds who welcomed Jesus with "Hosanna!" when he entered Jerusalem. But when the tables turn, when times grow difficult, when we don't get what we want, when we hunger for material things and power, when we seek approval from the powerful, when we fear those with authority—we betray him.

We deny him. We curse him. We choose Barabbas.

Yet Jesus does not respond as Pilate responded, distancing himself and declaring, "Not my problem." Instead, Jesus takes responsibility for us. Out of love, he identified with sinners so that he could redeem them. He moves toward the cross not in defeat, but in redemptive purpose.

If ever you doubt the goodness of God, look to this: Jesus died for those who betrayed him. He died for you.
