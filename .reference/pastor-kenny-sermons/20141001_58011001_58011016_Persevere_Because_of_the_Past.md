# Hebrews 11:1-16

**Title:** Persevere: Because of the Past

## 1. Faith is Seeing the Unseen

### Understanding Faith

The author of Hebrews opens with a definition of faith that goes far deeper than our modern understanding:

> Now faith is the reality of things hoped for and proof for what we do not see. (Hebrews 11:1)

The Greek word *hupostasis* (being/reality) appears five times in the New Testament, three times in Hebrews. When we say faith is the "reality of things hoped for," we mean faith is substantial—it has weight and objective existence. Similarly, *assurance* here means "proof" or "demonstration"—the kind of conviction that comes from evidence.

Faith, therefore, is not primarily about how we *feel*. It's about what is actually true. We don't have confidence *because* we have faith; we have confidence *because* we recognize the reality of what God has promised.

### What Faith Is NOT

In modern usage, faith often means inner personal conviction—"I just know," or "I feel assured." This makes faith entirely subjective, dependent on our emotional state. But this gets the relationship backwards.

When we say "I don't have faith," we often mean "I'm not feeling confident right now." But our feelings are unreliable indicators of truth. A chair holds you up whether you feel confident or terrified. The chair's structural integrity—the objective reality—determines whether it will support you, not your emotional state.

### What Faith IS

Faith is **based on evidence**, looking at what is real and true, and then **acting in obedience to that reality** even when we cannot yet see the full outcome.

Think of it this way: When you board a plane, you don't half-board it. You commit 100% because you trust in the evidence that planes fly safely. You believe like a mustard seed but you act like a tree. Once you determine there is sufficient evidence, you commit fully.

Jesus taught us this principle: "Believe in me like a mustard seed of faith, but live like a great tree—with complete commitment." There is no middle ground in obedience.

### The Unseen Becomes Seen

How do we know the unseen will actually appear? Look at creation itself:

> By faith we understand that the universe was formed at God's command, so that what is seen was not made out of what was visible. (Hebrews 11:3)

The invisible became visible through God's creative word. What is not yet appearing will become seen. This is the nature of reality—the future becomes the present. Before long, the exams arrive, the school year ends, Christmas comes around again. Have faith in the unseen; it will appear.

And without faith, it is impossible to please God:

> Anyone who comes to him must believe that he exists and that he rewards those who earnestly seek him. (Hebrews 11:6)

### Living on Sufficient Evidence

We rarely have 100% certainty about anything important—there's never time for complete proof. Sufficient evidence means "no more objections that I can reasonably raise, given my timeline and situation."

Think about choosing to marry someone. You don't need to know every detail of their future or even their entire past. You need sufficient evidence that they are trustworthy, and then you commit 100%.

The same is true with our faith in God. We have seen:
- The historical evidence of Jesus's birth, life, death, and resurrection
- Two thousand years of testimony from believers who persevered through opposition
- The reliability of Scripture

Once you've examined this evidence and reached your conclusion, don't keep second-guessing yourself. Has the evidence changed? Then why should your faith change? Just because you don't feel excited, or because life is tough, or because you're struggling with sin—none of these mean that God does not exist.

When doubts creep in, return to the objective evidence. Don't let the media or skeptics shake your foundation with accusations that have no substance. Go back to what Christ has objectively done for you.

## 2. The Ancients' Vision: A Heritage of Faith

### Seeing Without Receiving

The heroes of faith listed in this chapter all died without receiving the fulfillment of God's promises:

> All these people were still living by faith when they died. They did not receive the things promised; yet they only saw them and welcomed them from a distance, admitting that they were foreigners and strangers on earth. (Hebrews 11:13)

**This is the paradox**: They didn't receive what they hoped for, yet they *saw* it. They saw it from a distance. They didn't know exactly what it would look like, but they trusted God and moved forward anyway.

This is the kind of people God is looking for. Not people who wait until everything is certain and visible. People who, based on sufficient evidence, step forward into the future God promises.

### The Testimony of Their Lives

**Enoch** saw the unseen and was taken up without experiencing death (11:5). **Noah** was warned about a flood that had never occurred, in a place where it had never rained—and he built the ark anyway in holy fear and obedience, while everyone ridiculed him (11:7).

**Abraham** heard God's call and went, not knowing where he was going (11:8). He lived as a stranger in the promised land his entire life, never settling because he was *awaiting the city with foundations, whose architect and builder is God* (11:10). Even Sarah, though past childbearing age, received strength to bear a child because *she considered him faithful who had made the promise* (11:11).

**Moses** refused to be known as Pharaoh's son. Why? Because *he was looking ahead to his reward*. He regarded *disgrace for the sake of Christ as of greater value than the treasures of Egypt* (11:26). And this was centuries before Christ came in the flesh—but Moses saw Him by faith.

### The Cost of Faith

Some of these heroes saw dramatic victories:

> Through faith conquered kingdoms, administered justice, and gained what was promised; who shut the mouths of lions, quenched the fury of the flames, and escaped the edge of the sword. (Hebrews 11:33-34)

But many others suffered:

> Others were tortured, refusing to be released so that they might gain an even better resurrection. Some faced jeers and flogging, and even chains and imprisonment. They were put to death by stoning; they were sawed in two; they were killed by the sword. They went about in sheepskins and goatskins, destitute, persecuted and mistreated—the world was not worthy of them. (Hebrews 11:35-38)

Whether they experienced triumph or suffering, **they were all commended for their faith, even though none of them received what had been promised** (11:39). Their faith was real and pleasing to God, not because the outcome was always positive, but because they trusted in God's character and persevered.

### The Promise of Perfection

How were they made perfect? Through Jesus:

> Fixing our eyes on Jesus, the pioneer and perfecter of faith. (Hebrews 12:2)

Perfect means "reaching the goal." All these ancients were looking forward to the arrival of Christ. The land, the better country, descendants, the prepared city, the blessing—all pointed to Jesus. They have been made complete only through Him, the fulfillment of all God's promises.

## 3. Fix Our Eyes on Jesus

### Our Advantage

We are in a privileged position compared to those ancients. Jesus said:

> Truly I tell you, among those born of women there has not risen anyone greater than John the Baptist; yet whoever is least in the kingdom of heaven is greater than he. (Matthew 11:11)

Why? Because we know what they only saw in shadow:
- Jesus's name
- When He came
- How He came
- What He did
- How He actually saves us through His sacrifice

We have less excuse for faithlessness than they did. They had faith in a future they could not see. We have faith in history—the death and resurrection of Christ are not future hopes but completed facts.

> For the joy set before him he endured the cross, scorning its shame, and sat down at the right hand of the throne of God. (Hebrews 12:2)

Jesus Himself is our supreme example. Though divine, He limited His knowledge to that of a man. He didn't know when judgment day would come. In Gethsemane, His fear was real. But He said, "Not my will but yours," and entrusted Himself to God's loving hands based on the evidence of Scripture. Now, with hindsight, He sits at the right hand of God's throne.

### Enduring Opposition

Jesus endured opposition from sinners. He faced mockery, rejection, betrayal, and death. And now we read:

> Consider him who endured such opposition from sinners, so that you will not grow weary and lose heart. (Hebrews 12:3)

If Jesus faced opposition and persevered, so can we. We may encounter skepticism or even persecution. But we have their example—and His example—to strengthen us.

### Running the Race

Therefore:

> Since we are surrounded by such a great cloud of witnesses, let us throw off everything that hinders and the sin that so easily entangles. And let us run with perseverance the race marked out for us. (Hebrews 12:1)

We are not running alone. We are surrounded by a cloud of witnesses—Abraham, Noah, Moses, all the faithful ones who have gone before. They testify that Christ has come, that the fulfillment of the ages has reached its climax. And they also testify, through their perseverance across the centuries, that Christ will come again.

This is like a relay race. The baton has been passed from generation to generation for over four thousand years:

- From Abraham to Isaac to Jacob to Joseph
- Through Moses, Joshua, the Judges, Samuel, and David
- To the apostles after Jesus's resurrection
- Through the early church fathers, through the dark ages and the Protestant Reformation
- Through the Puritans and evangelicals to Sydney Anglicans
- Through countless faithful teachers and pastors
- And now to us

For some reason, this precious baton has landed in our hands. We who are undeserving of His grace, who sometimes felt we were watching from the sidelines—we have been handed this great truth. **We must run.** And we must make sure we pass it on.

## 4. Passing the Baton: Our Responsibility

The heritage we've received comes with obligation. From Noah's example:

> By his faith he condemned the world and became heir of the righteousness that is in keeping with faith. (Hebrews 11:7)

And Abraham received the same inheritance:

> By faith Abraham, when called to go to a place he would later receive as his inheritance, obeyed and went. (Hebrews 11:8)

Something profound was passed down: *the righteousness that is in keeping with faith*. With that inheritance comes the obligation to maintain it and pass it on.

### To Your Friends

Don't let the gospel die with you. Invite your friends to church. Try to tell them about Christ. This year, audit yourself: How many friends did I try to share the gospel with? How many did I invite to church? Ruth meets in December on weeknight—a chance for small groups to discuss apologetics together.

### To Your Church

The church exists, in part, to pass down this inheritance to the next generation. Take Sunday school and youth ministry seriously. These aren't optional programs; they are the means by which we fulfill our obligation to the rising generation.

### To Your Children

If you have children, understand that raising them in faith is a twenty-year training course. It's not just about piano lessons or academic subjects. It's about making sure they become heirs of the righteousness that is in keeping with faith—so that they learn to see the unseen and persevere through opposition.

- Just as you make sure they read their novels, make sure they read their Bibles.
- Just as you ensure they go to school, ensure they go to Sunday school.
- Just as you cultivate respect for their teachers, cultivate respect for their spiritual leaders.
- Just as you invest in their education, invest in their spiritual life.
- Just as you care for their physical health and buy them glasses so they can see physically, help them to truly see spiritually—to understand that what we see on earth is not all that is real. There is a future coming, and that is what truly matters.

Their lives depend on seeing this future. And they, in turn, are to tell their friends.

### The Urgency

We are only one generation away from extinction. This baton has been passed for over four thousand years. From Jesus to the apostles, through the early church fathers and apologists, through the dark ages, the Reformation, the Puritans, evangelicals, and modern teachers—the chain is unbroken.

If we do not pass this on, the chain breaks with us.

---

## Conclusion: Persevering Because of the Past

The ancients persevered because they had faith in a future they could not see. We persevere because we look back at history and see what God has done. We have the testimony of:

- The fulfilled life, death, and resurrection of Jesus
- Two thousand years of believers who fought the good fight and passed the baton on
- The Word of God, preserved and reliable
- The evidence that the promises of God are true

You are part of this chain. You are following in the footsteps of your spiritual ancestors. You have been given this precious inheritance not by accident, but by God's sovereign design.

So take up the mantle of responsibility. See the unseen future that Jesus promises. Persevere through opposition, knowing that a great cloud of witnesses surrounds you. And pass the baton to the next generation with all the strength and faith you can muster.

The race is yours to run. Run it well.
