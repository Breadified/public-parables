# 1 Corinthians 12:7-31

**Title:** The Common Body: We Need Each Other

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Who Is the Most Important Person in This Room?

When we think about importance—in movies, in organizations, in churches—we naturally think of the person at the front, the speaker, the chairperson, the one with the biggest title or the most obvious talents. But Paul's answer to this question is radically different. The most important person in this room is the person next to you.

## Same Spirit, But Different Gifts

### The One Spirit (v. 7)

> Now to each one the manifestation of the Spirit is given for the common good. (1 Corinthians 12:7)

The word "manifestation" means announcement or disclosure. This is not many different revelations—it's one message disclosed in different ways. Paul is telling us that all Christians have received the same gospel message: **Jesus is Lord**. We have been baptized by one Spirit into one body and have all drunk of that one Spirit.

Yet this one Spirit distributes gifts as He determines (v. 11). It's the same Spirit working in all of us, dividing the gifts according to His wisdom.

### The Different Gifts (v. 8-10)

> To one there is given through the Spirit the message of wisdom; to another the message of knowledge by means of the same Spirit; to another faith by the same Spirit; to another gifts of healing by that one Spirit; to another miraculous powers; to another prophecy; to another distinguishing between spirits; to another speaking in different kinds of tongues; and to still another the interpretation of tongues. (1 Corinthians 12:8-10)

Paul lists a variety of gifts. What these gifts are—and whether they continue today—depends largely on our theological perspective:

**Full Continuists** believe the miracles of Acts continue in the church today. They point to "Jesus Christ the same yesterday and today and forever" and expect ongoing signs, wonders, healings, visions, and tongues. Yet this view gained prominence mainly in the 20th-century Azusa Street revival. Has God been silent for nearly two thousand years before that?

**Cessationists** maintain that miraculous gifts belonged to the apostolic era. The apostles laid the foundation of the gospel; the Bible was completed; their special role ended with them. Therefore, while God can work miracles, they are not normative today, nor are they given through special persons. These gifts either no longer apply, or we reinterpret them as natural abilities—wisdom as insight, prophecy as excellent preaching, tongues as speaking other languages, healing as being a physician.

**Partial Continuists** occupy the middle ground, often because they simply aren't sure. Experience shapes their view: those who've experienced the supernatural lean toward continuism; those who haven't lean toward cessationism.

#### What I Think

Here's my honest perspective:

1. **We don't know for certain.** Even Christian writers in 400 AD admitted they weren't sure what these gifts were. We should approach with humility.

2. **Based on context**, I believe these are primarily speaking gifts—prophecy, knowledge, interpretation, discerning spirits—operating while Acts was still unfolding around AD 50.

3. **The miraculous was never normative.** Throughout Scripture, miracles were linked to specific salvation events and were exceptional, not everyday occurrences.

4. **Experience doesn't determine theology.** Even if you've had an experience, that doesn't make it normative for the church.

5. **Consider what the Bible emphasizes as God's power.** The gospel itself is the power of God—the proclamation that Jesus is Lord transforms us.

6. **God is a God of order, rationality, and knowledge.** He's not a God of whatever is weird and wonderful. An antibiotic from brilliant pharmaceutical minds reflects God's order; a coincidence interpreted as miraculous reveals our confusion about what truly glorifies God.

7. **God may give different gifts today** than in the first century, but the principle remains: these gifts are given **for the common good**, to build each other up, to serve one another.

**This is the key:** To achieve the common good, we need each other.

## Same Body, But Different Parts

### The One Body (v. 12-13, 20, 27)

> The body is a unit, though it is made up of many parts; and though all its parts are many, they form one body. So it is with Christ. (1 Corinthians 12:12)

A human body is a cooperative unit. Hormones, enzymes, blood, organs, and muscle all work together in harmony. So it is with Christ's body—and that body is us, right here, whenever Christians gather.

> For we were all baptized by one Spirit into one body—whether Jews or Greeks, slave or free—and we were all given the one Spirit to drink. (1 Corinthians 12:13)

Water baptism was a Jewish ritual of washing, a sign of repentance and identification. But Paul is not talking about water baptism here. In the early church, some divided over this very issue: "I was baptized into Paul; I was baptized into Apollos" (1 Corinthians 1:12-13). Paul cuts through this: **Spirit baptism is what counts.**

Spirit baptism means your identification with Christ as Lord. When you believed the gospel, you were united with Him through His Spirit. The Spirit of truth is your master. This is a spiritual unity far deeper than any ritual or church membership.

> Now you are the body of Christ, and each one of you is a part of it. (1 Corinthians 12:27)

We are one body. We belong to each other. We are vital to each other.

### The Many Parts That Are Vital for the Body (v. 14-26)

#### "I Do Not Belong!" (v. 15-19)

> If the foot should say, "Because I am not a hand, I do not belong to the body," it would not for that reason cease to be part of the body. And if the ear should say, "Because I am not an eye, I do not belong to the body," it would not for that reason cease to be part of the body. (1 Corinthians 12:15-16)

A hand seems more important than a foot—it's functional, versatile, presentable. An eye seems more important than an ear—we admire eyes; who compliments someone's ears? So the foot might say, "I don't belong." The ear might say, "I'm not important enough."

But this is foolish thinking:

> If the whole body were an eye, where would the sense of hearing be? If the whole body were an ear, where would the sense of smell be? But in fact God has arranged the parts in the body, every one of them, just as he wanted them to be. (1 Corinthians 12:17-18)

God has placed each part exactly where it belongs, in perfect proportion. Every part is important—even the little pinky toe. When you stub your pinky toe, the pain is disproportionate to its size because it's actually crucial for balance.

**Application:**

Many of us identify with "I do not belong":

- **The newcomer**: This is your first time here. The setting feels strange. You don't know anyone. You don't belong here.
- **The non-Christian**: Someone pressured you to come. These Christians are so different from you. You definitely don't belong.
- **The person struggling with illness**: Physical or mental, you never quite fit anywhere. You fear people will discover your condition and judge you. You don't belong.
- **Parents**: You're the only adult here at times. Everyone else seems to have their people. You don't belong.
- **The person from a different background**: Different ethnicity, language, culture. You're clearly out of place. You don't belong.
- **The young person**: Everyone here is so much older and bigger. No one notices you. You don't belong.
- **The person with a secret sin**: Everyone else seems so godly. You're struggling. If they found out, they'd judge you. You don't belong.

We all feel like minorities, even in a minority church. I once showed a chart of our congregation's age distribution—it was perfectly normal. Yet the oldest person saw all these young people behind them and felt out of place. The youngest person saw all these adults and felt out of place. We all feel disenfranchised when we compare ourselves to the majority, even when there is no majority.

But look to your unity in Christ, and you will see that you are valuable:

- **Newcomers teach us how to love strangers.** How will we learn to be like the Good Samaritan if we never receive strangers?
- **Those who are sick teach us compassion.** How will we learn to show love and concern if everyone is healthy?
- **Those who frustrate us make us better people.** They refine us, teach us patience, help us understand God's kingdom values.
- **The elderly bring wisdom.** They carry experience and perspective we need.
- **Even those not yet believing help us.** You ask tough questions that keep us honest. You prevent us from becoming a cult.

God has given us to each other **to teach us how to be one body. How to love each other.**

So please: Don't say "I do not belong." You do. Come to church, to our gatherings. Be encouraged and encourage others. When hard times come, the first thing we often abandon is fellowship with Christians—we fear judgment or feel ashamed. I'm sorry if that's happened to you. This is why I'm deeply committed to making church a safe, caring place. But don't disappear on us. Don't pull out. **We need each other.**

#### "I Don't Need You!" (v. 21-26)

> The eye cannot say to the hand, "I don't need you!" And the head cannot say to the feet, "I don't need you!" (1 Corinthians 12:21)

This is the attitude of the strong—those doing well in life, those with obvious gifts, those who feel self-sufficient. "I can study the Bible alone. I can attend a conference elsewhere. I don't need this church. I'm doing fine spiritually without you."

You're right. You don't need us. But look at what Paul says next:

> On the contrary, those parts of the body that seem to be weaker are indispensable, and the parts that we think are less honorable we treat with special honor. And the parts that are unpresentable are treated with special modesty, while our presentable parts need no special treatment. But God has combined the members of the body and has given greater honor to the parts that lacked it, so that there should be no division in the body, but that its parts should have equal concern for each other. (1 Corinthians 12:22-25)

The reason you are strong is **to help the weaker parts.** You have strength so you can serve those who lack it. "The parts we think are less honorable we treat with special honor"—this doesn't mean hiding them away. It means helping them, honoring them, caring for them.

Many of us neglect church because we think we don't need it. We pack our Sundays with birthday parties, holidays, social events, work, camps, university meetings, recitals, baptisms, confirmations, and weddings. There's no time for each other. And our unspoken attitude is, "You're not important enough for me to prioritize. I'll see you in a couple of weeks, or maybe the week after. What we do for each other feels like a favor we're doing."

But consider this: While you don't need us and I'm genuinely happy for your strength and stability, **we still need you.** We cannot replace you. The foot cannot suddenly become a hand in your absence. The ear cannot spontaneously turn into an eye. The whole point of a body is that **the parts are not interchangeable.**

> If one part suffers, every part suffers with it; if one part is honored, every part rejoices with it. (1 Corinthians 12:26)

**Consider what kind of church we want to be:**

- Do we want to be a place where people can confess their sins to each other? That requires regular attendance and trust built over time.
- Do we want to be a place of learning? That requires consistency and continuity.
- Do we want to pray for each other? That requires commitment, stability, safety, and security.

I've seen churches where no one knew who would show up week to week. Different prayer partners every Sunday. Different dynamics. Teaching and worship varied wildly. You couldn't challenge each other or really know how people work. Disagreements meant nothing because you wouldn't see them again anyway.

**Relationships don't form in a state of flux.** People need consistency to be vulnerable, to open up, to grow.

> so that there should be no division in the body, but that its parts should have equal concern for each other. (1 Corinthians 12:25)

**Here's the question:** Do you want the parts to have equal concern for each other? Or do you want concern when you feel like it, when it fits between work, studies, and your social calendar?

If you are strong, **show up to help.**

## The Most Important Person in This Room

When the credits roll at the end of a movie, we see hundreds of names. Key grips. Gaffers. Best boys. Best girls. Every role matters. A key grip supervises the crew moving lights, tracks, cranes, and scenery—it's skilled, essential work. A gaffer is the head electrician. These are the people who make the film possible, yet most of us never knew these roles existed.

**The most important person in this room is the person next to you. The person you haven't talked to. The stranger. The one you assume doesn't matter.**

We are all important to each other. We need each other for the proper functioning of this body.

### Two Calls

**To those who feel they don't belong:** Give it another chance. You are valuable, even if we fail to look after you well. Take the risk and come back.

**To those who think they don't need us:** There are people taking the risk to be here, to be vulnerable, to participate. Don't fail them. Show up. Use your strength to serve.

### Imagine

Imagine a congregation where no one cares about anyone else. Everyone is self-indulgent. People speak whenever they want. At mealtime, everyone rushes in, grabs their share, and forgets about others. A newcomer walks in—what happens? You ignore them, hoping someone else will eventually reach out. No one prays together because, honestly, why would they? No one will ask you how it went next week. No one shares deeply because you're all still strangers. Musicians do their own thing. The sermon is random. People talk over each other. No one knows when meetings start or end. Why would anyone ever be vulnerable here? Why would anyone ever share?

This is what happens when the eye says, "I don't need you." It's monstrous. **This was the Corinthian church.**

### Now Imagine This Instead

Imagine a place where we hear God's word faithfully each week—not just from the pulpit, but from each other as we share what we've learned from our Lord, as we share our struggles and temptations and difficulties. Where we hear each other and offer help and prayer. Where outsiders come in, hear God's word, and see how love operates here.

**This is what it means to be truly spiritual.** Not through extraordinary gifts or dramatic experiences, but through committed, sacrificial love for one another.

We all have a role in helping each other. And this relationship needs **commitment and regularity.**
