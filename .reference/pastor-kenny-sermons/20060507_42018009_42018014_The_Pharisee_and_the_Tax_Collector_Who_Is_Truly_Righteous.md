# Luke 18:9-14

**Title:** The Pharisee and the Tax Collector: Who Is Truly Righteous?
**Date:** May 7, 2006
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Setup

Jesus tells this parable to those who were confident of their own righteousness and looked down on everyone else. The scene is set in first-century Jerusalem, under Roman occupation, at the temple where people came to pray and offer sacrifices to God.

> Two men went up to the temple to pray, one a Pharisee and the other a tax collector. (Luke 18:10)

## The Good Guy (Or So He Seems)

The Pharisee represents what we'd call a respectable person today—morally upright, well-educated, from a good family. He's done nothing criminally wrong. He helps with charity. He's the kind of person we'd admire in any society.

Listen to his prayer:

> "God, I thank you that I am not like other men—robbers, evildoers, adulterers—or even like this tax collector. I fast twice a week and give a tenth of all I get." (Luke 18:11-12)

His credentials are impressive:
- He avoids the sins of others
- He disciplines himself through fasting, not once but twice weekly
- He gives generously, tithing a tenth of his income

**The problem:** He knows he's good, and he thanks God for it. He's confident in his own righteousness.

## The Bad Guy

The tax collector, by contrast, is despised. In those days, tax collectors were seen as traitors—Jews who worked for Rome, collecting tributes. They were hated by the Romans for not being Roman, and hated by their own people for collaborating with the occupiers. Worse, they became wealthy through extortion and corruption, placing them in the same category as robbers and adulterers.

The tax collector wouldn't even approach the temple properly. He stood at a distance, unable to lift his eyes to heaven. His prayer was simple and desperate:

> "God, have mercy on me, a sinner." (Luke 18:13)

He beat his breast in genuine remorse. Here is someone who knows exactly what he is.

## Understanding Sin

The word sin isn't just about breaking rules—it's deeper. Sin describes our rebellion against God. It's not simply doing bad things; it's the orientation of the heart against Him. Whether your sin is small or great, whether you've done one thing wrong or many, rebellion against God is rebellion.

## The Shocking Twist

Here's what Jesus says:

> "I tell you that this man, rather than the other, went home justified before God. For everyone who exalts himself will be humbled, and he who humbles himself will be exalted." (Luke 18:14)

The tax collector—the corrupt, extortionate, sinful man—went home *justified*. Right with God. Friends with God.

At first, this seems unfair. The Pharisee has done so much good. Surely his moral achievements count for something? And the tax collector—surely mere humility and a plea for mercy can't erase years of corruption?

But that's the point Jesus is making: **all are guilty before God**. Whether your sins are small (like the Pharisee's self-righteousness) or great (like the tax collector's extortion), you're still a sinner. One sin against God is enough. The difference isn't in the amount of sin; it's in the response to it.

The Pharisee's fatal flaw was his confidence. He trusted in himself, in his works, in how little bad he had done. He looked down on others. The tax collector, by contrast, humbled himself and asked for mercy.

## The Question for Us

Who do you relate to? Are you more like the tax collector—aware of your brokenness, desperate for God's grace? Or more like the Pharisee—confident in your own goodness, comparing yourself favorably to others?

If you see yourself as the tax collector, there's good news: Jesus was raised to life and now rules over the universe. He can show mercy to those who humble themselves and ask for it.

If you're more like the Pharisee, don't lose hope. Jesus can forgive you too—but not while you're trusting in yourself and your good works. Forgiveness requires the same humble admission the tax collector made: that you are a sinner in need of mercy, not a righteous person in need of recognition.
