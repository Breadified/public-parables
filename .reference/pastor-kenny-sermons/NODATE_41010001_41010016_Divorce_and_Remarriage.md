# Mark 10:1-16

**Title:** Divorce and Remarriage  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Opening

This is a sensitive and deeply personal topic. Many in our congregation are personally touched by divorce—either having gone through it themselves or feeling its impact as children of divorced parents. Different Christian traditions approach this passage differently, which creates genuine theological disagreement among faithful believers about what Scripture permits.

We cannot avoid this teaching. But I want to be clear: I am not here to judge anyone. We are simply seeking to understand what God's Word says.

---

## 1. Exemptions? (10:1-5)

> 10:1 Jesus then left that place and went into the region of Judea and across the Jordan. Again crowds of people came to him, and as was his custom, he taught them.

After coming down from the mountain where he taught about discipleship—that the last shall be first—Jesus encounters the Pharisees with a test question.

> 2 Some Pharisees came and tested him by asking, "Is it lawful for a man to divorce his wife?"

The word "divorce" carries weight. In Greek, it means to loosen or release; in Hebrew, to cut. The Pharisees are asking about the legality of separation and what grounds permit it.

> 3 "What did Moses command you?" he replied.
>
> 4 They said, "Moses permitted a man to write a certificate of divorce and send her away."

They point to Deuteronomy 24 as their justification. Let's look at what that passage actually says:

> Deuteronomy 24:1 If a man marries a woman who becomes displeasing to him because he finds something indecent about her, and he writes her a certificate of divorce, gives it to her and sends her from his house, 2 and if after she leaves his house she becomes the wife of another man, 3 and her second husband dislikes her and writes her a certificate of divorce, gives it to her and sends her from his house, or if he dies, 4 then her first husband, who divorced her, is not allowed to marry her again after she has been defiled. That would be detestable in the eyes of the Lord.

### Understanding Deuteronomy 24

This is case law, not a command endorsing divorce. The Pharisees have taken verse 1 literally and built their entire theology around it, forgetting the context and the law's actual purpose.

Notice what the law actually forbids: a man cannot remarry his ex-wife after she has been with another man. Why? The law treats this as a form of defilement, placing it on the same moral level as adultery (compare Leviticus 18:20 and Numbers 5:13-14). The formal certificate of divorce served to protect the woman by providing legal proof that she was no longer bound to her first husband—without it, she risked being accused of adultery, a capital crime under Jewish law.

The phrase "something indecent" (Hebrew: *ervat davar*, literally "nakedness of a thing") likely refers to obnoxious conduct, not physical appearance. Scholar Jeffrey Tigay notes that the phrase "finds in her" appears elsewhere in Scripture to mean finding fault with someone's behavior or conduct.

### The Real Purpose

The original command was extremely limited: don't remarry your divorced spouse. That's it. The restriction itself reveals something important—Moses was not promoting divorce; he was controlling it.

By biblical times, two schools of thought had emerged. The strict interpretation (Shammai's school) permitted divorce only for sexual unfaithfulness. The permissive interpretation (Hillel's school) allowed divorce for any reason—if a wife displeased her husband in any way.

I believe the first divorce in Deuteronomy 24 actually refers to breaking off an engagement before consummation—similar to Joseph's situation with Mary (Matthew 1:19). Marriage involved stages: betrothal, vows, and consummation. Breaking an engagement was different from divorcing a consummated marriage. The second marriage described in verses 2-4 was consummated, which is why reconciliation to the first husband would be considered defiled.

But what happened over time? The exemption became an expansion. A case law meant to handle an exception became a general permission. What began as a narrow restriction became a loophole for divorce on nearly any grounds.

**Jesus sets the record straight:**

> 10:5 "It was because your hearts were hard that Moses wrote you this law," Jesus replied.

Divorce was never God's intention. The Mosaic law is like another regulation in Scripture—the command about not bringing money from prostitution into the temple (Deuteronomy 23:18). That command doesn't approve prostitution; it restricts and controls it, acknowledging it as an existing evil. Similarly, the divorce law doesn't promote divorce; it mitigates its harmful effects while people's hearts remain hard.

The command exists not to promote what is good, but to restrain what is evil. People were already divorcing. The law was introduced to prevent the worst consequences of that sin.

## 2. One Flesh (10:6-8)

> 6 "But at the beginning of creation God 'made them male and female.'
>
> 7 'For this reason a man will leave his father and mother and be united to his wife,
>
> 8 and the two will become one flesh.' So they are no longer two, but one flesh.

Jesus points us back to the beginning—to God's design in creation. The sexual binary is not cultural; God made them male and female. A man leaves his parents (who themselves are male and female) and unites with his wife (female). Sex is binary because procreation requires both.

### The Reality of "One Flesh"

We modern romantics hear "one flesh" and think of a beautiful metaphor—emotional and spiritual union, the chemistry and connection that makes us feel complete. But I think we've forgotten what this phrase actually means biologically.

Your body is your own. Each system—respiratory, cardiovascular, digestive, renal—functions independently. You don't need someone else's lungs to breathe or their heart to pump your blood. There is practically no organ in your body that isn't independently functional.

**Except one.**

Your reproductive system exists for one purpose: to create new life. But here's what's remarkable—that organ, by itself, is completely useless. It is only half of the equation. For life to be produced, you need one male and one female. Not male and male; not female and female. They can never produce life together.

When a man and woman unite in the act of marriage—barring sickness or intervention—a miracle occurs. A new life is created. A profound union has happened that is literally biological, not merely metaphorical. This is what "one flesh" means.

### For Young People Considering Marriage

It's dangerous to build a marriage on feelings and chemistry alone. Feelings change; chemistry fades. Instead, ask yourself: *Will this person be a good parent to my children?*

That's the real question. Not "Do I enjoy gaming with them?" or "Can we travel together?" The primary aim is finding someone who will be a good mother or father. Appearance, athletic ability, impressive career titles—these are nearly irrelevant to being a good parent. What matters is a servant heart, willingness to sacrifice, faithfulness to promises, integrity, and commitment to serving the next generation.

This is what makes a good husband and wife. This is true one flesh—commitment not based on passing emotions, but on the profound biological and spiritual union that marriage creates.

## 3. No Divorce (10:9-16)

> 9 Therefore what God has joined together, let no one separate."

There is no divorce. God has joined them together, and no human should separate what God has united.

This raises immediate questions. What if there's abuse? What then?

Separation may be necessary in extreme cases, and Paul speaks of temporary separation by mutual agreement for prayer (1 Corinthians 7:5). But separation is not the same as divorce, and divorce is not the same as remarriage. One can separate without remarrying—just as one flesh cannot simply become two separate fleshes again.

> 10 When they were in the house again, the disciples asked Jesus about this. 11 He answered, "Anyone who divorces his wife and marries another woman commits adultery against her. 12 And if she divorces her husband and marries another man, she commits adultery."

Why is remarriage adultery? Because you remain one flesh with your first spouse. How can you become one flesh with another? That would be committing adultery—joining yourself to someone else while still bound to another.

Some people think divorce is a reset button, like losing a car in an accident and buying a new one. But marriage is not a car. Divorce is like losing your legs. You cannot simply move on and get a new pair.

If the marriage was not productive, perhaps people think they can return to where they started. But what about the child? The child doesn't reset. The child carries the brokenness forward.

### The Impact on Children

> 13 People were bringing little children to Jesus for him to place his hands on them, but the disciples rebuked them. 14 When Jesus saw this, he was indignant. He said to them, "Let the little children come to me, and do not hinder them, for the kingdom of God belongs to such as these. 15 Truly I tell you, anyone who will not receive the kingdom of God like a little child will never enter it." 16 And he took the children in his arms, placed his hands on them and blessed them.

The juxtaposition is deliberate. After teaching about divorce, Jesus welcomes the little children and tells us the kingdom belongs to them.

### For Married Couples

The temptation is real. After months or years of difficulty, people think, "It's time to move on. I'm done." But if marriage is for life, there is no such thing as dating again. It's not like changing cars. It's more like losing your legs.

Even when the other party is unfaithful, even when it feels impossible, you stay committed. You separate if necessary for safety, but you don't remarry. You wait and pray for their repentance.

This sounds harsh. Life for the rest of your life, no matter what? Even if the other person is unfaithful? Yes. And we do this for the little ones.

### The Empirical Reality

Research consistently shows that parental divorce is one of the strongest social predictors of negative outcomes for children:

- Parental separation significantly impacts children's long-term emotional wellbeing and educational performance
- In a longitudinal U.S. study, children from divorced families died almost five years earlier on average than children from intact families
- Parental divorce during childhood was the single strongest social predictor of early death
- Financial stress compounds the problem—going from one household to two makes both poorer, even with government support
- Father absence represents long-term grief for children
- Research suggests non-biological parents are 17 times more likely to commit fatal assault against a child than biological parents
- Intergenerational effects show that parents who experienced physical abuse or neglect in childhood are 2-5 times more likely to abuse their own children

We are not bound by statistics, but we are shaped by them. We do everything we can to make our children successful: the best schools, tutors, enrichment activities, church attendance. But the one thing that would make the real difference—the one thing that accounts for about 90% of the outcome—is keeping your marriage intact.

You might hate your spouse's guts. But staying together for the sake of the kids is commendable. That's what your children want. Not elaborate vacations or expensive educations. They want their mother and father together.

## Application

When our hearts grow hard, we look for loopholes. In recent generations, the exemptions have expanded until divorce for any reason is legally possible. We've moved from "divorce with restrictions" to "no-fault divorce"—meaning there is no such thing as faithfulness in marriage under the law. We're trigger-happy.

But when we understand that divorce is a tragedy—something to mitigate, not endorse—everything changes. The question becomes not "Can I get out of this marriage?" but "How do I honor the covenant I've made? How do I model faithfulness to my children?"

Marriage is permanent. That's God's design. That's the good news—and the hard news—of the gospel.
