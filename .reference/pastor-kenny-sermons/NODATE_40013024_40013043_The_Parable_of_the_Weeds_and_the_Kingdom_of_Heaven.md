# Matthew 13:24-43

**Title:** The Parable of the Weeds and the Kingdom of Heaven  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Understanding the Kingdom of Heaven

When we talk about a kingdom—say, Camelot—we're describing a place under the rule of a king. King Arthur ruled Camelot; similarly, the kingdom of Heaven is the realm where God reigns. Christians believe Jesus is the King who rules His kingdom right now. But as we'll see in this parable, the kingdom doesn't look like we'd expect it to.

## The Parable of the Weeds

> "The kingdom of heaven is like a man who sowed good seed in his field. But while everyone was sleeping, his enemy came and sowed weeds among the wheat, and went away. When the wheat sprouted and formed heads, then the weeds also appeared." (Matthew 13:24-26)

Jesus explains this parable later:

> "The Son of Man is the one who sowed the good seed. The field is the world, and the good seed stands for the people of the kingdom. The weeds are the people of the evil one, and the enemy who sows them is the devil." (Matthew 13:37-39)

**What are tares?** The weeds mentioned here—called "tares" in some Bible versions—are a type of weed that closely resembles wheat. The crucial difference: when eaten, tares can cause poisoning and even death. In a field, they're the last thing a farmer wants.

## Why Are There Weeds?

The servants of the farmer naturally ask: "Why are there weeds in your field? Didn't you plant good seed?" This question echoes our own doubts about God's judgment. Sometimes we, like those servants, think we know better than God. We question His decisions for our lives when we're not satisfied. But here's the reality: we are infinitely inferior to God, the Creator of the universe. Do we really know better than He does?

The answer to why weeds exist comes in verse 25: **while everyone was sleeping**. This "sleeping" has a dual meaning—it could be physical rest, but more importantly, it represents spiritual slumber. When we are spiritually asleep, we become vulnerable to Satan's work. 

> "His enemy came and sowed weeds among the wheat." (Matthew 13:25)

This is the state of our world. It's full of corruption—murder, domestic violence, bullying, racism, and countless other evils fill our newspapers and neighborhoods. The world appears to be a kingdom of weeds, not heaven.

## God's Patient Answer

The servants ask the obvious question: "Do you want us to go and pull them up?" When something bad is around us, our instinct is to remove it immediately—like swatting an annoying mosquito. Similarly, we ask Jesus: "If you're the King and this is your kingdom, why don't you clean up this mess right now?"

But Jesus gives a surprising answer:

> "No, because while you are pulling the weeds, you may root up the wheat with them. Let both grow together until the harvest. At that time I will tell the harvesters: First collect the weeds and tie them in bundles to be burned; then gather the wheat and bring it into my barn." (Matthew 13:29-30)

**Why not pull the weeds immediately?** There are two crucial reasons:

1. **The risk of mistake.** Separating weeds from wheat prematurely could damage the good crop. Just as it would be impossible to accurately divide people based on preferences during a school assembly, it's impossible for us to judge who truly belongs to God's kingdom.

2. **God's patience and mercy.** By allowing both to grow together, God is reassuring us. He's being absolutely certain there's no mistake, and more importantly, He's giving non-Christians time to accept His Word and put their faith in Him. He's offering everyone a chance to repent and believe before He judges.

## What This Means for Us

Because plants take time to grow, patience is required to see results. This parable teaches us that **God is patient**. He won't act rashly or jump to wrong conclusions as we so often do. He takes His time, allowing His Word to grow within our hearts and giving all of us a chance to repent and believe in Him before judgment comes.

But here's our responsibility: because God doesn't judge immediately, we must guard against Satan, who seeks to destroy the good seed planted within us.

**The central question:** Do you allow God's Word to grow in your heart? Just as last week's parable asked, "What kind of soil are you?"—this week we ask the same. The answer should be the fourth type of soil: the receptive heart that accepts God's Word, withstands trials and opposition, and grows deeper in Jesus Christ.

## The Net and the Final Harvest

The parable of the net shows us that the kingdom of Heaven doesn't discriminate. The net is cast to catch all fish—everyone and anyone can be part of God's kingdom. But inclusion in the kingdom now doesn't guarantee inclusion in heaven forever.

> "First collect the weeds and tie them in bundles to be burned; then gather the wheat and bring it into my barn." (Matthew 13:30)

There will come a harvest time—a final judgment. The bad fish will be separated from the good and cast away, preventing any defect in the bad to spread to the good. Just as Jesus warned, being exposed to the kingdom of Heaven doesn't automatically grant you entry into Heaven.

## A Call to Evaluate Your Life

It's time to ask yourself honestly: **Are you truly a Christian?**

- Are you Christian merely because your parents are?
- Are you Christian just because you attend church on Sunday?
- What about the rest of the week—how do you live Monday through Saturday?

A true Christian is one who follows Jesus wholeheartedly as their King. Will you obey that King as befits a subject of His kingdom?

## Two Final Words

**If you have committed to following Jesus:** That's wonderful! Even though the world doesn't look like much of a kingdom of Heaven right now, there will come a time when the weeds are taken away. Be patient as God is patient. You will be gathered and brought safely into the barn—into God's eternal presence and protection.

**If you have not committed to following Jesus:** Be warned. Harvest time is coming. Jesus, the King and Master, will return and separate the weeds from the wheat. Make sure you are not among those cast away when that time comes. Reach out to friends and family—invite them into God's kingdom while there is still time.
