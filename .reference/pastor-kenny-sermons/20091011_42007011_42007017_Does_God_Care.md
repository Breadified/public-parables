# Luke 7:11-17

**Title:** Does God Care?
**Date:** October 11, 2009
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## The Scene: A Widow's Deepest Loss

> Soon afterward, Jesus went to a town called Nain, and his disciples and a large crowd went along with him. As he approached the town gate, a dead person was being carried out—the only son of his mother, and she was a widow. And a large crowd from the town was with her.

Here we encounter a moment of profound human tragedy. A widow has lost her only son. In the context of ancient Jewish society, this was catastrophic—not merely emotionally, but economically and socially. A widow without a son had no protector, no provider, no future security. She was completely alone.

The text emphasizes she is a widow *and* that this was her *only* son. The repetition matters. This woman has suffered ultimate loss.

## Jesus' Response: Compassion in Action

> When the Lord saw her, his heart went out to her and he said, "Don't cry."

Jesus doesn't simply observe the funeral procession from a distance. When he sees her, *his heart goes out to her*. This is not clinical detachment. This is genuine, felt compassion. He tells her not to cry—not as a dismissal of her grief, but as a promise that something is about to change.

Then comes the remarkable act:

> Then he went up and touched the coffin, and those carrying it stood still. He said, "Young man, I say to you, get up!" The dead man sat up and began to talk, and Jesus gave him back to his mother.

Notice the intimacy of this miracle. Jesus touches the coffin—an act that would have made him ceremonially unclean under Jewish law. He doesn't stand back and command from a distance. He enters into the funeral, he touches death itself, and he raises the young man to life.

The young man doesn't just return to consciousness—he "began to talk," showing full restoration. And Jesus "gave him back to his mother." The emphasis is not primarily on the miracle as a display of power, but on restoration, on reunion, on answering a mother's deepest need.

## The People's Recognition

> They were all filled with awe and praised God. "A great prophet has appeared among us," they said. "God has come to help his people."

The witnesses understand what has happened. A great prophet has appeared—someone through whom God acts. More than that, they recognize in this act a statement about God himself: *God has come to help his people.*

This is the core truth of the passage: In Jesus, God demonstrates that he is not distant, not indifferent to human suffering. He cares. He intervenes. He restores.

## The Message Spreads

> This news about Jesus spread throughout Judea and the surrounding country.

The story doesn't end in Nain. It spreads. People hear what Jesus did for a widow and her son, and the news travels. This is what a genuine encounter with God's compassion produces—it cannot be contained. It moves people to share what they've witnessed.

## Application: Does God Care?

The question posed by this sermon's title finds its answer here. Yes, God cares. Not in some abstract theological sense, but concretely, personally, redemptively. He sees our deepest losses. He responds with compassion. He acts to restore what has been broken.

In your own seasons of loss and grief, remember: the God revealed in Jesus is the God who sees you, who is moved by your pain, and who works toward your restoration.
