# Matthew 9:35–10:42

**Title:** Pray and Go  
**Dates:** February 15, 2009 (Crossword); May 4, 2025 (Arise)  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Goal: Teaching and Healing (9:35)

Jesus is on a mission to proclaim God's kingdom. This mission unfolds through two core activities—teaching and healing—which appear together throughout Matthew's Gospel.

> Matthew 9:35 Jesus went through all the towns and villages, teaching in their synagogues, proclaiming the good news of the kingdom and healing every disease and sickness.

This formula appears consistently. Earlier in Matthew 4:23, just before the Sermon on the Mount, Matthew uses the identical pattern:

> Matthew 4:23 Jesus went throughout Galilee, teaching in their synagogues, proclaiming the good news of the kingdom, and healing every disease and sickness among the people.

Since then, Jesus has continued this work—the Sermon on the Mount (chapters 5–7) presents his great teaching on the kingdom, while chapters 8–9 showcase his healing power in action.

### Understanding the Kingdom

When Jesus teaches about God's kingdom, he's announcing a political reality: **the King has come**. This isn't abstract theology—it's a call to allegiance and obedience.

The kingdom of heaven is fundamentally about Jesus, the King. His arrival means judgment is coming and new allegiances are required. The question becomes: Will you submit to his rule?

We didn't experience a change of government yesterday, but we did experience one 2,000 years ago at Jesus' resurrection. The authorities of this world were defeated. We live now in a caretaker mode before the final inauguration of the new King. Things are already being set in motion, even if they haven't been completed.

We live in 2025 AD—anno Domini, "the year of the Lord," marking Christ's ongoing rule over history.

### The Power of God's Word

The healing accounts in chapters 8–9 reveal something profound: **the word of God has an effect**. Things change for the better wherever God's word touches creation.

When Jesus spoke with authority—the pure, unfiltered word of God straight from his mouth—people changed instantly. Sickness became health. Poverty became abundance. Hunger became fullness.

Today, this transformation continues, though mediated through our imperfect words and actions. Because we are sinful, we continually invent new ways of doing evil, creating new diseases and spiritual strongholds. Change takes longer now, but it still happens.

Consider the evidence: When minds are freed from guilt and shame, mental illness diminishes. When we live for a higher power and calling, anxiety levels decrease. When we abandon sensuality and live with sobriety and self-control, our physical bodies improve. When we practice generosity and honest work, we grow wealthier. When we believe in the God of order rather than superstition, we become wiser.

Our choices about time, food, and thought patterns affect our well-being. Societies flourish when they follow the law of God. Christian kingdoms have historically been healthier places. This isn't instantaneous or perfect, but the pattern is undeniable.

> John 10:10 The thief comes only to steal and kill and destroy; I have come that they may have life, and have it to the full.

The converse is also true: when societies abandon God's word, they deteriorate. Fewer doctors emerge. Murders increase. The further people drift from Christ, the less healthy society becomes—and increasingly, the more demonic it grows.

### Jesus' Motivation: Compassion

> 36 When he saw the crowds, he had compassion on them, because they were harassed and helpless, like sheep without a shepherd.

Jesus' teaching and healing flow from compassion, not harshness. Some wrongly contrast compassion with truth-telling, but teaching truth is deeply compassionate. A prescription given in love is an attempt at care.

True teaching always involves judgment—distinguishing right from wrong. But judgment is not the same as judgmentalism. Judgment says, "This is wrong, and here's the truth." Judgmentalism says, "Not only are you wrong, but I'm better than you."

Kenny's illustration captures this beautifully: We are all beggars. Some of us just know where the bread is. That doesn't make us better. We teach not because we're superior, but because we care for people's growth—not to dominate them, but out of love.

### The Condition of the Crowds

The crowds Jesus saw were "harassed and helpless"—literally, troubled and dispersed. The imagery of dispersed sheep is crucial.

A flock's strength lies in unity. When sheep stay together, predators can't single out individuals. Zebras' stripes aren't for camouflage—they blend the herd together so no one gets targeted. Sheep form a sea of white, and when predators approach, the flock's coordinated movement confuses and deters the threat. Safety comes in numbers.

But when dispersed, sheep become vulnerable. A single sheep separated from the ninety-nine has no protection. Without a shepherd, they're defenseless.

The Israelites in Jesus' day were precisely this—scattered and leaderless. Some were exiled across the Middle East. Those in Jerusalem were harassed politically by the Romans and their tax collectors, and religiously by Pharisees imposing legalistic rules of their own invention. They had leaders who exploited rather than protected them.

Ezekiel described this condition centuries earlier:

> Ezekiel 34:3–6 These leaders ate the curds, clothed themselves with wool, and slaughtered choice animals, but did not take care of the flock. You have not strengthened the weak or healed the sick or bound up the injured. You have not brought back the strays or searched for the lost. You have ruled them harshly and brutally. So they were scattered because there was no shepherd, and when they were scattered they became food for all the wild animals. My sheep wandered over all the mountains and on every high hill. They were scattered over the whole earth, and no one searched or looked for them. Therefore, God says, "I myself will search for my sheep and look after them."

Jesus is the fulfillment of that promise. He is the shepherd who comes, teaching and healing by his word, gathering the scattered flock for their good.

## The Command: Pray and Go (9:37–10:1)

### The Problem and the Prayer (9:37–38)

The task Jesus described—reaching all towns, healing every disease—reveals an impossible situation:

> 37 Then he said to his disciples, "The harvest is plentiful but the workers are few.

The harvest is overflowing with abundance. But workers are few, inadequate for the task. How can this be accomplished?

Jesus' solution seems counterintuitive:

> 38 Ask the Lord of the harvest, therefore, to send out workers into his harvest field.

We might expect Jesus to say, "Go into the harvest field!" Instead, he says to pray first. This isn't responsibility-shifting or virtue-signaling. It reflects a fundamental doctrine: **God's sovereignty**.

Prayer expresses our dependence on God. We acknowledge that he is in charge and knows all our needs before we ask them. But prayer is also how we verbally express our trust in him. We trust him first, not our own efforts.

This reorients us completely. We don't approach the task by asking, "What can I do?" Instead we ask the Lord of the Harvest for workers. We seek God's blessing first. We consult him. We ask permission from the most powerful one.

### Why Prayer Matters

Prayer is powerful, but not because prayer itself is inherently powerful. You could pray to Buddha with perfect sincerity and see no results. **Prayer is powerful because God is powerful.**

Remarkably, even the unbelieving world understands this. In Australia, new abortion laws make it illegal to pray with another person with the intent that they change their mind. Non-believers and atheists who claim not to believe in prayer don't want you praying! They're terrified by an elderly grandmother quietly praying outside an abortion clinic—so terrified they're willing to jail her.

The world recognizes prayer's power more than many Christians do. We often dismiss prayer as talking to the ether, wasting breath on someone who already knows everything. But the world knows better. It fears prayer because it knows prayer works.

The question is: Do you? Do you truly depend on the Lord Almighty to judge all according to his standards? Do you believe all will ultimately submit to him? Have you understood that you have only one audience that matters—God alone?

> Ask the Lord.

### The Answer: Go (10:1)

**And immediately the prayer is answered.**

> 10:1 Jesus called his twelve disciples to him and gave them authority to drive out impure spirits and to heal every disease and sickness.

Prayer isn't passive resignation. It's not "pray and pat yourself on the back." It's **pray and then go**. We move from being on our knees to being on our feet.

Jesus, who is the Lord of the Harvest, calls and sends workers (apostles means "sent ones"). He gives the disciples the same authority he himself has—to proclaim the kingdom, drive out demons, and heal every disease and sickness.

> 10:7–8 As you go, preach this message: "The kingdom of heaven is near." Heal the sick, raise the dead, cleanse those who have leprosy, drive out demons. Freely you have received, freely give.

The disciples receive the same task and authority as Jesus. They go with his power and commission.

## The Cost of Going (10:16–20)

### A Strange Command

Here comes a strange observation. Jesus just sent the disciples into the harvest field, but then says something unsettling:

> 10:16 I am sending you out like sheep among wolves. Therefore be as shrewd as snakes and as innocent as doves. 17 Be on your guard; you will be handed over to the local councils and be flogged in the synagogues. 18 On my account you will be brought before governors and kings as witnesses to them and to the Gentiles. 19 But when they arrest you, do not worry about what to say or how to say it. At that time you will be given what to say, 20 for it will not be you speaking, but the Spirit of your Father speaking through you.

Jesus came to save harassed and helpless sheep, yet here he's purposely sending them into danger: "I am sending you out like sheep among wolves!"

This is dangerous work. The disciples will be handed over to councils and flogged. They'll be brought before governors and kings for trials. They'll be arrested, punished, jailed, their possessions confiscated—and one day, some will be killed.

Why would Jesus say this?

### The Meaning Behind the Danger

First, this points to true apostleship. If the apostles receive authority like Jesus, they must also be willing to share his sufferings:

> Matthew 10:24–25 The student is not above the teacher, nor a servant above his master. It is enough for students to be like their teachers, and servants like their masters.

More fundamentally, this points to Jesus himself. Jesus is ultimately the sheep among wolves. He's the one handed over to councils and flogged, brought before governors and kings (Pilate and Herod). He bears witness to the Father in his words. He is the worthy worker, the one who ultimately goes into danger for the harvest.

### The Difference Jesus Makes

Yet there's a crucial difference between the scattered sheep and the sheep Jesus sends.

Before Jesus came, the crowds were harassed and dispersed. They were sheep without a shepherd, vulnerable and alone. But when Jesus sends his disciples out, they are no longer weak and scattered. He has gathered them together. As he said, "I will build my church." They are united and together, helping one another. They run as a pack, so the wolves become confused.

Most importantly, **they are no longer sheep without a shepherd**. They have a leader. They are empowered and never alone.

### A Call to Courage

Never doubt which side of history you're on. We are those who are harassed, but we are never defeated.

Christianity is good for the world. Hold your head high as you proclaim the word of God and address the many ailments that afflict our society marked by sin.

The harvest is plentiful. The workers are few.

**Will you be among the few who together will reap the harvest?**

Those of you involved in outreach, serving teams, youth leadership, Bible study—**you are workers in God's harvest field**. Those of you who welcome visitors, fill out comment cards, invite others to lunch—**you are workers in God's harvest**. Even those here for the first time—your role matters too.

We asked the Lord of the Harvest for more workers. **You are the answer to our prayers—both present and future workers**.
