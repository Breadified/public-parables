# Ecclesiastes 5:1–6:12

**Title:** The Search for Meaning: The Lure of Wealth
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew
**Date:** 11 May 2014

## Structure of the Passage

The Teacher presents a carefully balanced structure exploring dissatisfaction and the futility of wealth:

- **Section A (5:8-12 / 6:7-9):** Dissatisfaction through various proverbs about wealth and desire
- **Section B (5:13-17 / 6:3-6):** Two contrasting illustrations—a man who hoards wealth only to lose it, and a man with many children and long life who cannot enjoy his prosperity
- **Section C (5:18-19 / 6:1-2):** A striking contrast between something good the Teacher has observed (enjoying one's labor as God's gift) and something evil (possessing wealth but being unable to enjoy it)
- **Section D (5:20):** The affirmation that God gives joy to those who accept their lot

**Key Structural Parallels:**
- References to sight/seeing: 5:11 and 6:9
- References to family: 5:14 and 6:3
- References to coming and going (birth and death): 5:15 and 6:4
- References to darkness: 5:17 and 6:4
- Notice the complex relationship between God as giver (5:19) and God as withholder (6:2)

## The Problem of Dissatisfaction (5:8-12)

The Teacher begins by observing systemic injustice. When you see the poor oppressed and justice denied in a district, do not be surprised. There is a hierarchy of corruption—one official answers to a higher one, and others stand above them still. Even the king profits from the fields through taxation.

This structural reality creates a condition of perpetual dissatisfaction:

> Whoever loves money never has money enough;
> whoever loves wealth is never satisfied with his income.
> This too is meaningless.

As goods increase, so do those who consume them. What benefit does wealth bring to its owner except the ability to feast his eyes upon it?

This creates a striking irony: **The rich man's abundance prevents him from sleeping, while the laborer sleeps sweetly whether he eats little or much.** Those consumed by wealth find no rest.

## The Illustration: Wealth Hoarded and Lost (5:13-17)

> I have seen a grievous evil under the sun:
> wealth hoarded to the harm of its owner,
> or wealth lost through some misfortune,
> so that when he has a son
> there is nothing left for him.

The Teacher reflects on the ultimate futility of accumulation:

> Naked a man comes from his mother's womb,
> and as he comes, so he departs.
> He takes nothing from his labor
> that he can carry in his hand.

This is the reality we must face: **All his days he eats in darkness, with great frustration, affliction and anger.** He toils for the wind—and dies having gained nothing he can keep.

The statistics of modern life bear this out. Wealth is far more fleeting than we imagine. Among the Fortune 400—the wealthiest Americans—only about one percent appeared on the list for all seventeen years between 1992 and 2008. There is considerable churn at the top; the 0.1 percent is not the same group from year to year.

Even more striking: **78 percent of NFL players—earning $310,000 to $460,000 annually, placing them in the top 1 percent of American income—declare bankruptcy within two years of retirement.** About 70 percent of lottery winners spend or lose all their winnings within five years. Easy come, easy go.

## The Illustration: Abundance Without Enjoyment (6:3-6)

> A man may have a hundred children and live many years;
> yet no matter how long he lives,
> if he cannot enjoy his prosperity and does not receive proper burial,
> I say that a stillborn child is better off than he.

The Teacher presents a man with every advantage—a large family, a long life—yet he cannot enjoy what he possesses. **In such a case, the stillborn child, who comes without meaning and departs in darkness, has more rest than he does.** Even living a thousand years twice over means nothing if you cannot enjoy your prosperity. All go to the same place.

This challenges our assumptions about having children. We sometimes think of children primarily as retirement security, but that misses the mark. The true reason to have children is to raise the next generation of Christians—the same reason we evangelize.

## Dissatisfaction Revisited (6:7-9)

> All man's efforts are for his mouth,
> yet his appetite is never satisfied.

The Teacher drives home the point: **What advantage has a wise man over a fool? What does a poor man gain by knowing how to conduct himself before others?**

> Better what the eye sees
> than the roving of the appetite.
> This too is meaningless,
> a chasing after the wind.

We live in a consumer society where materialism has become pervasive. We confuse material goods with significance and safety. New gadgets offer a way to overcome the boredom that possessions inevitably bring. The scoreboard becomes material: number of TVs versus number of eyeballs, number of sofas versus number of people to sit on them.

But this is the fundamental error: **We are material beings and need material things to live, so we cannot be anti-material. But materialism—the belief that material goods are all that ultimately matters—is like hoarding sand, sticks, and stone.** Whether we hoard sophisticated sand (silicon in processors), sticks (furniture), or stone (bricks and mortar), the anxiety remains. Why the worry?

Even the extraordinarily wealthy discover this truth. Arnold Schwarzenegger once reflected, "Money doesn't make you happy. I now have $50 million but I was just as happy when I had $48 million."

## Something Good Observed (5:18-20)

Yet the Teacher has also observed something beautiful:

> Then I realized that it is good and proper for a man to eat and drink,
> and to find satisfaction in his toilsome labor under the sun
> during the few days of life God has given him—
> for this is his lot.

This acceptance of our lot—to work and serve, even when our daily tasks seem boring—is itself a form of obedience.

> Moreover, when God gives any man wealth and possessions,
> and enables him to enjoy them,
> to accept his lot and be happy in his work—
> this is a gift of God.

The key word is **enjoy**. The issue is not work itself but the ability to rejoice in it. And notice what follows:

> He seldom reflects on the days of his life,
> because God keeps him occupied with gladness of heart.

When God grants both the ability to work and the ability to enjoy that work, He fills the person's heart with joy—so much so that they don't spend their time anxiously analyzing their life.

## Something Evil Observed (6:1-2)

But there is a dark inverse to this gift:

> I have seen another evil under the sun, and it weighs heavily on men:
> God gives a man wealth, possessions and honor,
> so that he lacks nothing his heart desires,
> but God does not enable him to enjoy them,
> and a stranger enjoys them instead.
> This is meaningless, a grievous evil.

**There are no guarantees.** God may grant wealth and abundance but withhold the ability to enjoy it. The goods themselves become a curse. And the stranger—perhaps an heir, perhaps simply circumstances—ends up benefiting from what this man cannot use.

## The Impossibility of Knowledge (6:10-12)

The passage concludes with an acknowledgment of human limitation:

> Whatever exists has already been named,
> and what man is has been known;
> no man can contend
> with one who is stronger than he.
>
> The more the words,
> the less the meaning,
> and how does that profit anyone?
>
> For who knows what is good for a man in life,
> during the few and meaningless days he passes through like a shadow?
> Who can tell him what will happen under the sun after he is gone?

We cannot fight against what is greater than ourselves. We cannot achieve meaning through endless words and arguments. And we cannot know the future or what will happen to our legacy after we die.

## The Central Tension

The Teacher has identified the fundamental paradox of material life: **If dust is the end, is life meaningless or meaningful?**

On one hand, everything passes away. Work yields no permanent gain. Wealth cannot be carried beyond death. Possessions become somebody else's. All is like chasing the wind.

On the other hand, there is something better than despair: **to fear God and obey His commandments.** If meaning is found in relationship with God rather than in material accumulation or human achievement, then life becomes meaningful even in its brevity and uncertainty. The same God who gives wealth and possessions also gives the ability to enjoy them—and that ability, grounded in awe of Him, is the true gift.

**For Further Reflection:**

Compare this passage with the words of Paul in 1 Timothy 6:7-10, which echoes the Teacher's insights: "For we brought nothing into this world, and it is certain we can carry nothing out. And having food and raiment let us be therewith content. But they that will be rich fall into temptation and a snare, and into many foolish and hurtful lusts, which drown men in destruction and perdition. For the love of money is the root of all evil."

The Teacher's conclusions anticipate the New Testament call to contentment and the recognition that true riches are found not in possessions but in the fear of the Lord.
