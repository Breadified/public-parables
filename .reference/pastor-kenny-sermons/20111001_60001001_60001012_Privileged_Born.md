# 1 Peter 1:1-12, 18-25

**Title:** Privileged Born
**Date:** 16 October 2011
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Born Privileged

> Praise be to the God and Father of our Lord Jesus Christ! In his great mercy he has given us new birth into a living hope through the resurrection of Jesus Christ from the dead, and into an inheritance that can never perish, spoil or fade. This inheritance is kept in heaven for you, who through faith are shielded by God's power until the coming of the salvation that is ready to be revealed in the last time. (1 Peter 1:3-5)

Peter writes to Christians scattered across the Roman provinces—exiles with no earthly home, no land, no security. They face sporadic persecution from the empire that should have granted them privilege. Yet Peter begins by reminding them of something far greater: they are born privileged.

### The Language of Inheritance

Throughout Scripture, inheritance means land. God promised Abraham a great land, a great people, and great blessing (Genesis 12:7). Moses led the Israelites into their promised inheritance (Exodus 15:17). The Levites were told they had no inheritance in the land—not that they lacked God's blessing, but that they were landless, set apart to serve the temple (Numbers 18:20).

Peter speaks to a landless people using the language of land. His audience—scattered, temporary, without property or security—needed to hear that they possessed something infinitely more valuable than earthly real estate.

### An Imperishable Inheritance

Peter describes this inheritance in stark terms: it can never **perish, spoil, or fade**. There is no inflation that erodes its value, no thief that can steal it, no decay or obsolescence. Unlike earthly wealth—vulnerable to economic collapse, natural disaster, and the ravages of time—this inheritance is eternally secure, protected in heaven.

For those struggling with property affordability, mortgage payments, or financial insecurity in this world, Peter offers radical hope: the real property worth investing in is not here. It is in heaven, where it cannot be taken away.

> Do not store up for yourselves treasures on earth, where moths and vermin destroy, and where thieves break in and steal. But store up for yourselves treasures in heaven, where moths and vermin do not destroy, and where thieves do not break in and steal. (Matthew 6:19-20)

## Born of the Imperishable Word

How can we—creatures of earth—inherit a heavenly land? We must be born of something different. We must be born again.

> For you have been born again, not of perishable seed, but of imperishable, through the living and enduring word of God. (1 Peter 1:23)

Christians are born not of earthly soil (jus soli—the right of the land) but of eternal blood (jus sanguinis—the right of kinship). We are born not of place but of relationship, not of geography but of redemption.

### The DNA of Our Birth

Our spiritual birth comes through the gospel—the good news that Jesus lived the godly life we could not live, was crucified to pay for our ungodly lives, and rose from the dead to promise the same resurrection to all who believe. We are redeemed not with perishable things like silver or gold, but with the precious blood of Christ, a lamb without blemish or defect (1 Peter 1:18-19).

We come to faith in many ways—through our parents, through friends, through church, through powerful spiritual experiences, through intellectual conviction. Yet all of us are born through the same imperishable source: the living and enduring word of God. This word testifies to Christ's life, death, and resurrection. Through it, we become citizens of heaven by blood.

When we believe this word and belong to it, we become imperishable. Death no longer holds us. We are resurrected.

## Rejoicing in Suffering

Peter's readers face real trials. Yet he calls them to rejoice:

> In all this you greatly rejoice, though now for a little while you may have had to suffer grief in all kinds of trials. These have come so that the proven genuineness of your faith—of greater worth than gold, which perishes even though refined by fire—may result in praise, glory and honor when Jesus Christ is revealed. (1 Peter 1:6-7)

It is possible to be sad and joyful simultaneously: sad today because of present trouble, joyful for tomorrow because it brings salvation. Suffering, paradoxically, proves and refines our faith more than wealth could ever do.

### Faith Without Sight

Many of us are frustrated that we have not sensorially encountered God—no miracles, no visions, no direct experience. Peter anticipates this as normal:

> Though you have not seen him, you love him; and even though you do not see him now, you believe in him and are filled with an inexpressible and glorious joy, for you are receiving the end result of your faith, the salvation of your souls. (1 Peter 1:8-9)

Not seeing Jesus or witnessing miracles is the common Christian experience. Yet we love him, believe in him, and are filled with inexpressible joy. We are privileged, and there is nothing to be ashamed of in this invisible faith.

## The Living Word: Past, Present, and Future

The prophets of old spoke of the grace coming to us. They searched intently to understand when and how the Messiah would come, predicting his sufferings and the glories that would follow—though they did not know his name was Jesus.

> It was revealed to them that they were not serving themselves but you, when they spoke of the things that have now been told you by those who have preached the gospel to you by the Holy Spirit sent from heaven. Even angels long to look into these things. (1 Peter 1:12)

The word spoken long ago is living in the present for us. And it will continue living into the future:

> All men are like grass, and all their glory is like the flowers of the field; the grass withers and the flowers fall, but the word of the Lord stands forever. (1 Peter 1:24-25)

## Guarding Against Two Errors

### Error One: Denying What Scripture Says

Some approach the Bible by first proclaiming ignorance—claiming ancient laws are unclear or obsolete. Then they appeal to non-biblical authorities: modern culture, scientific studies, personal experience. Finally, they declare confidently that we should abandon traditional interpretations for new ones.

The rules for reading Scripture are clear:

- Scripture interprets Scripture (not external authorities)
- When in doubt, stick to the conservative reading

The gay marriage debate illustrates this pattern: declare the Bible unclear, cite external authorities (cultural change, sympathetic friends), then confidently overturn long-standing Christian teaching. Instead, we must let Scripture speak for itself.

### Error Two: Adding to What Scripture Says

While some deny what the Bible says, others add to it. They acknowledge gaps in biblical guidance—which subject to study, whom to marry, what to buy—and attempt to fill these gaps with extra words from God: visions, dreams, voices, "words of knowledge."

Yet we must be cautious. How do we know the voices we hear are truly God's and not our own desires or the influence of peers? The Bible is always relevant. If it doesn't address a particular question, perhaps we are meant either not to know or to work it out ourselves through prayer, wisdom, and humble discernment.

Rather than seeking new revelations, we should:

- Study Scripture more closely and regularly
- Pray that we read the Bible rightly
- Approach it listening to what it says rather than making it say what we want
- Ask for wisdom to discern what is already there
- Ask for repentance to apply what we discover
- Ask for strength to obey, even when difficult

The Bible is our precious passport in a foreign land. Keep it close—physically, mentally, spiritually. Bring it to church and Bible studies. Read it in context. Take notes. Let it continually remind you that the Lord is good.

## Living as Aliens

We are born of another world. We do not belong to this earth. Therefore, we must not act like its natives.

### Do Not Assimilate

> Therefore, prepare your minds for action; be self-controlled; set your hope fully on the grace to be given you when Jesus Christ is revealed. As obedient children, do not conform to the evil desires you had when you lived in ignorance. But just as he who called you is holy, so be holy in all you do; for it is written: "Be holy, because I am holy." (1 Peter 1:13-16)

Holy means set apart. We are born of different DNA—the imperishable word, the blood of Christ. We must not assimilate to the world's values and behaviors.

This applies concretely: Do not marry those who do not share your faith. Do not pursue relationships alien to your calling. The younger among us feel intense peer pressure to conform; the older among us, less so. Yet all of us must resist the constant pull toward cultural assimilation.

### Work Hard

> Since you call on a Father who judges each man's work impartially, live your lives as strangers here in reverent fear. (1 Peter 1:17)

We are strangers in this world, not home. We work—not for earthly accumulation, but to send treasure home to heaven, to advance the gospel, to live in reverent acknowledgment that God judges our work.

### Crave Pure Spiritual Milk

> Therefore, rid yourselves of all malice and all deceit, hypocrisy, envy, and slander of every kind. Like newborn babies, crave pure spiritual milk, so that by it you may grow up in your salvation, now that you have tasted that the Lord is good. (1 Peter 2:1-3)

Newborn babies cry for milk instinctively. We who are born again should crave the spiritual milk of God's word with the same urgency. Through the written word, we continue to taste that the Lord is good. Nothing else can satisfy this hunger.

---

## Conclusion

You are alien born. You are born not of this earth but of the eternal word. You are born of the blood of Christ. In his great mercy, he has given you new birth into a living hope through the resurrection of Jesus Christ from the dead.

Live as one born to another kingdom.
