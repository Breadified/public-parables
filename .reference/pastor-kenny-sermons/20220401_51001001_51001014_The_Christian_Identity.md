## Introduction: Who Are You?

Imagine a stormy night on the Mediterranean Sea. A fishing boat cuts through waves and rain when the crew spots a shadowy figure in the water. They pull him aboard, discovering a man with bullet wounds in his back. As they tend his injuries, he awakens with dangerous combat skills, speaks multiple languages fluently—yet has no memory of who he is.

This is the premise of *The Bourne Identity*, where an amnesiac CIA operative must rediscover his complicated past. Today, as we look at Colossians 1 and begin our series on Christ Supremacy, we face a similar question: **As Christians, what is our identity? Who are we?**

## Point 1: Fruit of the Gospel

### Hearing and Responding

Paul begins Colossians with thanksgiving:

> We always thank God, the Father of our Lord Jesus Christ, when we pray for you, because we have heard of your faith in Christ Jesus and of the love you have for all God's people. (Colossians 1:3-4)

Paul and Timothy have heard wonderful reports about the Colossians from Epaphras—specifically about their faith and love. But these virtues spring from something deeper:

> Your faith and love spring from the hope stored up for you in heaven and about which you have already heard in the true message of the gospel. (Colossians 1:5)

The faith and love Paul commends isn't accidental; it's the product of hearing and truly understanding the gospel message. This becomes clear in verses 6-8:

> In the same way, the gospel is bearing fruit and growing throughout the whole world—just as it has been doing among you since the day you heard it and truly understood God's grace. You learned it from Epaphras, our dear fellow servant, who is a faithful minister of Christ on our behalf, and who also told us of your love in the Spirit. (Colossians 1:6-8)

Here's the pattern: the Colossians **heard** the gospel from Epaphras, **truly understood** God's grace, and **responded** with faith and love. This illustrates what it means to be a Christian:

**A Christian is someone who has heard the gospel and responded to it rightly.**

### The Gospel: From Darkness to Light

But what exactly is the gospel? The Greek word *evangelion* simply means "good news." Paul defines it in verses 12-14:

> Giving joyful thanks to the Father, who has qualified you to share in the inheritance of his holy people in the kingdom of light. For he has rescued us from the dominion of darkness and brought us into the kingdom of the Son he loves, in whom we have redemption, the forgiveness of sins. (Colossians 1:12-14)

The gospel presents two kingdoms: the **dominion of darkness** and the **kingdom of light**—the kingdom of God's Son.

By nature, all humanity belongs to the dominion of darkness. Since Adam and Eve's first rebellion against God, we have all inherited their pattern: we disobey God's laws, pursuing our own way instead of our Maker's way. We lie, steal, and hold idols in our hearts that displace God himself. In doing so, we condemn ourselves as enemies of God, enslaved to our own sinful desires.

> People loved darkness rather than light, for their deeds were evil. (John 3:19)

The verdict? Guilty. The punishment? Death and eternal judgment.

> People are destined to die once, and after that to face judgment. (Hebrews 9:27)

But this is only half the picture. The gospel is good news because God, in His love for us—His enemies—has made a way to rescue us.

**Two thousand years ago, God sent His Son Jesus to walk the earth in perfect obedience.** Yet He willingly died a criminal's death on the cross for us. In that moment, all of humanity's sins throughout history were placed upon Him. His death paid the price we deserved for our rebellion. All of God's righteous wrath was poured out on His only Son so that He would become a sacrifice for sinners.

Anyone who believes and trusts in the finished work of Jesus on the cross will be forgiven. God will no longer be angry with them.

The word *redemption* is crucial here. In ancient times, redemption meant paying a ransom to free a slave or prisoner from bondage. **When Jesus died on the cross, His blood paid for your release from the dominion of darkness, from enslavement to your sinful nature. He brought you into His kingdom of light.**

> For you were once darkness, but now you are light in the Lord. Live as children of light. (Ephesians 5:8)

Rather than facing eternal death in hell—which we earned through rebellion—we become co-heirs with Jesus to an inheritance we didn't deserve: eternal life in heaven.

From darkness to light. From death to life. Not earned, not deserved—but **won** through the death and resurrection of Jesus Christ, as planned by God the Father.

### Application

This is the Gospel. If you've never heard it before, you have heard it now.

Are you a fruit of the gospel? Do you have faith like the Colossians did?

If you are not yet a Christian, I urge you to consider what has been done for you. Right now, you remain in bondage to darkness and your own sinful ways. Repent. Believe in what Jesus has done for you. Trust that forgiveness of sins comes not from what you can do, but from what Christ has already **done**.

> All those the Father gives me will come to me, and whoever comes to me I will never drive away. (John 6:37)

Repent, believe, and start living as a follower of Jesus. As a Christian. As fruit of the gospel.

## Point 2: Growing Fruit

But is that the full picture? Are Christians simply saved souls waiting passively for Jesus to take them to heaven? Not according to Paul.

Paul doesn't stop after giving thanks for the Colossians' conversion. Instead, he remains actively invested in their growth, like a farmer carefully tending a young fruit tree:

> For this reason, since the day we heard about you, we have not stopped praying for you. We continually ask God to fill you with the knowledge of his will through all the wisdom and understanding that the Spirit gives. (Colossians 1:9-10)

Paul prays for knowledge. The Colossians have begun to know God—they've heard the gospel and responded—but that's only the start. As Christians, we must keep growing in our knowledge of God's will, asking the Holy Spirit to give us wisdom and understanding of how God wants His people to live as revealed in His Word, the Bible.

**We cannot remain as unripened fruit. We must be fruit that grows.**

### Illustration: From Prison to Purpose

This week, I watched a clip about a prisoner hired by chef Gordon Ramsay. While Ramsay is known for harsh criticism, he also has a softer side. Seeing potential in a prison kitchen worker, he offered the young man a job after his release.

How would that man respond? With gratitude and growth. Once released, Andrew Insley worked tirelessly, grateful for his second chance. He refined his culinary knowledge so thoroughly that he was eventually head-hunted to become a chef at The Roast, a prestigious London restaurant.

### Application

In the same way, becoming a Christian begins a life of gratitude and growth. We must continually develop our knowledge of God and what He requires of us.

Simply attending church on Sunday to hear a sermon isn't enough. **You must open your Bible at home. Pray that God would help you grow in understanding His Word.**

Be hungry for God's Word. Seek deeper knowledge of who He is.

- Join a Bible study if you're not already part of one
- Watch sermons and read articles online—we're blessed to have resources our grandparents could only dream of accessing
- Seek recommendations after service or leave a comment card

There is no excuse for stagnation. **Don't be stagnant. Grow.**

## Point 3: Bear Fruit

Knowledge alone is insufficient. Our knowledge must not be useless trivia but practical—like a professional skill used every day. **What we know must bear fruit in tangible ways within our lives.**

> That you may live a life worthy of the Lord and please him in every way: bearing fruit in every good work, growing in the knowledge of God. (Colossians 1:10)

As we grow in knowledge of God, this must be accompanied by bearing fruit—good works. We must strive to live a life worthy of our Lord and pleasing to Him in every way.

This involves cultivating core Christian virtues—faith, hope, and love. But it also means growing new believers. Flicking back to verse 6, we see that the gospel is bearing fruit and growing around the world. When we join Jesus' kingdom, we join this global mission: helping the gospel bear fruit in others, just as it has borne fruit in our own lives.

**Bearing good works necessarily involves sharing the good news of Jesus with others.**

### Illustration: The Strawberry

It's fascinating how God has designed plants with an inbuilt survival mechanism—fruit naturally bear new fruit. Consider a strawberry: look at how many seeds it contains! Each seed can grow into a whole new plant, multiplying your harvest.

**Christians are the same. We are fruit designed to bear new fruit.**

### Application

You've heard the gospel. Now, are you seeking to bear good fruit?

If your vision for life after receiving the gospel is merely a suburban house and European vacations every two years, you have missed the point entirely. **You were not saved into a life of comfort—you were saved to do good.**

We are men and women of Christ serving the kingdom of God's Son. We must spend and exhaust our lives in service to the preaching of the gospel, so that Christ's message continues to save others and grow them to maturity.

**Don't waste your life. Do good.**

**Preach the Gospel to non-believers in your life.** How can people receive the gospel unless they hear it preached? If you can't remember the last time you shared Christ with a non-believer, that's a good indication it's been far too long.

**Be the first to welcome newcomers on Sunday**, showing them the love that was first shown to us. Don't arrive late expecting someone else to do it—**you** step up and do good.

**Encourage your brothers and sisters in the faith.** Spur one another on so we grow not only in numbers but in the depth of our faith, love, and hope.

Do good. Bear good fruit.

This won't always be easy. We live in a broken, sinful world, and a lifetime of good works will be exhausting. But let verse 11 encourage you:

> Being strengthened with all power according to his glorious might so that you may have great endurance and patience. (Colossians 1:11)

God Himself will give you the strength you need to keep serving Him. He promises to sustain you with **all power** according to His glorious might—not just some power, but all power.

**Keep pressing on. Even when it's hard. Bear good fruit.**

## Conclusion: The Christian Identity

We began with a question: What is our identity as Christians? What makes us who we are?

Looking through Colossians 1, we've discovered that **Christians are fruit of the gospel**—those saved and transformed by the good news of Christ's death and resurrection.

With the gospel in our hearts, we are to continue growing in knowledge of God, putting that knowledge to good use. We are to be fruit that create new fruit, giving our lives to the proclamation of the Gospel message.

**This is the Christian Identity.**
