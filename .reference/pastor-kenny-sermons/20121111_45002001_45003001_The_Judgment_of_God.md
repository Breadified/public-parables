# Romans 2:1–3:1

**Title:** The Judgment of God
**Date:** November 11, 2012
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction: The Contrast Effect

When we view things, we rarely judge them in isolation. We judge them relative to what surrounds them. This is the contrast effect—how we perceive colors, qualities, even people depends entirely on the context around them. The same white square appears black when surrounded by whiter squares. Similarly, we determine our own goodness by comparing ourselves to others.

Paul addresses this directly in Romans. He asks the Jewish people—God's chosen nation—a piercing question: Are you comparing yourselves against the right standard? You may look good compared to the Gentiles, but how do you look before a holy God?

## Part One: God Judges Impartially

> He will render to each one according to his works: to those who by patience in well doing seek for glory and honor and immortality, he will give eternal life, but for those who are self-seeking and do not obey the truth but obey unrighteousness, there will be wrath and fury. There will be tribulation and distress for every human being who does evil, the Jew first and also the Greek, but glory and honor and peace for everyone who does good, the Jew first and also the Greek. For God shows no partiality. (Romans 2:6–11)

The fundamental truth Paul establishes is this: **God judges each person according to their works, not their status.**

God will render judgment to each one. This judgment is not biased, not influenced by favoritism, not swayed by national privilege or birthright. God does not favor one nation over another, one individual over another. He is completely and utterly just.

The passage presents two possible outcomes: eternal life or eternal fury from God. There is no middle ground. Every action we take now determines where we will spend eternity. As Jonathan Edwards wrote, "The manifold and continual experience of the world in all ages shows that this is no evidence that a man is not on the very brink of eternity, and that the next step won't be into another world."

This is not legalism. Paul is not saying that salvation comes through our works. Rather, he is establishing that all people—regardless of background—stand on level ground before God's judgment. We cannot use our heritage, our learning, our religious membership as an excuse. We cannot say, "Tomorrow I will do better." The Scripture is clear: **He will render to each one.**

## Part Two: No External Status Saves You

Why does Paul labor to prove God's impartial judgment? Because he is addressing a specific audience: the Jewish people. And his argument is simple and devastating: **Just because you are a Jew, God's chosen nation, this will not save you from God's judgment.**

Notice that in verses 6–10, Paul deliberately repeats "the Jew first and also the Greek." He is saying that judgment falls on both, in that order. The privilege of being first to receive the law brings first responsibility in judgment.

### Going for the Jugular

Paul's strategy here is brilliant. In a good debate, the strongest debater doesn't attack side issues—he takes the opponent's best argument and destroys it. This is exactly what Paul does.

The Jews' greatest claims were two: **the Law** and **circumcision**. These were not superstitions to them. They represented something profound—a covenant with God, a national and spiritual identity.

In Genesis 17, God made an eternal covenant with Abraham. To be circumcised was to be marked as God's chosen. The Law was not mere rules; it was the embodiment of knowledge and truth. To grow up Jewish was to know that you belonged to God's people in a unique way.

Paul attacks this directly:

> You, then, who teach others, do you not teach yourself? You who preach against stealing, do you steal? You who say that people should not commit adultery, do you commit adultery? You who abhor idols, do you rob temples? You who boast in the law, do you dishonor God by breaking the law? For, as it is written: "God's name is blasphemed among the Gentiles because of you." (Romans 2:21–24)

Is Paul saying every Jew steals and commits adultery? No. He is making a more devastating point: **The very things you preach against, you practice.** You have the law sitting on your bookshelf, you bear the mark of circumcision, you boast of your privilege—but you break the law. And in doing so, you make God's name a laughingstock among the nations.

This echoes verses 1–5: When you judge others while doing the same things, you condemn yourself. You may think you will escape God's judgment because of your status, but instead you are storing up wrath against yourself.

As the English phrase says, "Familiarity breeds contempt." The Jews were friends with God, entrusted with His word—and they treated it lightly.

### Application

Consider this: What external markers do we use to assure ourselves that we are God's people?

- That we attend church week after week?
- That we have grown up in a theologically sound church?
- That we have the "right" doctrine?

But here is the hard truth: **These things mean nothing without obedience.** A gym membership has value only if you actually exercise. Church membership has value only if you participate. Possessing a Bible has value only if you read it. Reading it has value only if you do it.

Being Jewish was good only if you followed the law. Being Christian is good only if you follow Christ.

## Part Three: You Must Be Circumcised of Heart

### The Problem Restated

God judges equally. There is no external status—whether Jewish birthright, the law, circumcision, or church membership—that exempts you from His judgment. Everything will be examined. Everything will be known.

Do we truly understand this? Jonathan Edwards posed a haunting question: "Almost every natural man that hears of hell flatters himself that he shall escape it; he depends upon himself for his own security; he flatters himself in what he has done, in what he is now doing, or what he intends to do."

Are we like this? Do our hearts trust that somehow we will be okay?

### The Solution: Circumcision of the Heart

> No, a person is a Jew who is one inwardly; and circumcision is circumcision of the heart, by the Spirit, not by the written code. Such a person's praise is not from other people, but from God. (Romans 2:28–29)

Here is the solution—paradoxical and extreme: **To be a true Jew, to truly belong to God, your heart must be circumcised.**

The real Jew is not defined by descent or external marks. It is the one whose heart has been changed by the Spirit, whose very essence has been transformed. The real circumcision is not physical; it is internal, written on the heart, worked by the Spirit.

This points to something even deeper than the law: **Obedience must come from the heart, not from external compulsion.** The law points toward Christ, who alone can write His will upon our hearts and give us the desire to obey.

### The Danger of External Religion

Many of us who teach the Bible face a particular danger: we can teach truth while remaining unconvinced. What is true in our heads remains distant from our hearts. We adopt religion as another set of rules—another burden to bear—rather than a transformation of the heart.

When Jesus becomes king, it is not simply about adopting more practices. It is about a heart so changed that obedience flows from love, not obligation. This is what the Spirit does. He writes God's law on our hearts so that we desire to obey, not because we must, but because we love.

### God Shows No Favoritism

The implications are staggering:

- **For the Gentile who obeys the law:** Even though you have no birthright, no circumcision, no privileged background, God sees your obedience and honors it (Romans 2:26–27).

- **For the Jew who breaks the law:** Your circumcision becomes as though it never happened. Your privilege becomes your shame (Romans 2:25).

> The one who is not circumcised physically and yet obeys the law will condemn you who, even though you have the written code and circumcision, are a lawbreaker. (Romans 2:27)

God is not arbitrary. He is not malicious. He gives each person exactly what they deserve according to His law. Whether you are a new Christian or have followed for years, God treats each of us as His children without favorites—but He looks at how we live. He judges according to truth, not appearance.

### The True Jew

Interestingly, the name Judah comes from the Hebrew word meaning "to praise." A true Jew—whether born Jewish or a Gentile Christian—is one whom God praises. And ultimately, the only true Jew is Jesus Christ Himself. He alone perfectly praised God in word and deed. He alone sought God's glory and honor. He alone was no hypocrite. He alone obeyed perfectly.

It is only by following Jesus that we too can become what the name Jew truly means: those whom God praises.

## Conclusion: The Call to Obedience

God's judgment is real. It is coming. It is impartial. No status, no heritage, no external mark can save you from it.

What you need is not more rules but a transformed heart. A heart that loves God so deeply that obedience becomes the natural overflow of that love. A heart circumcised by the Spirit, marked not by flesh but by faith in Jesus Christ.

Do you seek God's glory, honor, and eternal life? Or are you storing up wrath against yourself through disobedience?

The choice before us is clear. Know the truth. Now do it. For God judges not what we say we believe, but what we actually obey.
