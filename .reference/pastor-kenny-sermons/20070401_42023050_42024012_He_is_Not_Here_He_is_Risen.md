# Luke 23:50-24:12

**Title:** He is Not Here, He is Risen
**Date:** 3 April 2007 (Easter)
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Why Is the Resurrection Important?

When the apostles first proclaimed the gospel in Acts, their central message wasn't primarily that Jesus died for our sins—though that was certainly implied. Rather, they proclaimed that Jesus had risen from the dead. This raises a crucial question: Why is the resurrection so significant?

### What the Resurrection Is NOT

We often assume the resurrection proves God's power or demonstrates that Jesus is God. We might think it gives us spiritual confidence when other religions challenge our faith. After all, our God came back to life! Surely this must be the ultimate miracle that proves Christianity is true.

But this reasoning contains a critical flaw: **the resurrection by itself is not that remarkable.**

Other religions have accounts of people returning to life. Reincarnation teaches that everyone will come back to life eventually—no big deal. In popular culture, we see characters like Neo in *The Matrix* or the cheerleader in *Heroes* come back from death, yet we don't conclude they're divine. Even within Scripture, people were healed and raised from the dead—Lazarus, the widow's son, Jairus's daughter—but we never conclude that these individuals must be God simply because they were resurrected.

More tellingly, the religious leaders of Jesus's day saw the empty tomb themselves, yet they still refused to believe. So why would we think that news of the resurrection would convince skeptics? They might simply say, "Interesting, so what?"

The resurrection is something special and unusual, yes. It might indicate that someone is powerful or exceptional. But by itself, it doesn't prove divinity or compel repentance.

Jesus himself addressed this in the parable of the rich man and Lazarus:

> There was a rich man who was dressed in purple and fine linen and lived in luxury every day. At his gate was laid a beggar named Lazarus, covered with sores and longing to eat what fell from the rich man's table. Even the dogs came and licked his sores. The time came when the beggar died and the angels carried him to Abraham's side. The rich man also died and was buried. In hell, where he was in torment, he looked up and saw Abraham far away, with Lazarus by his side. So he called to him, "Father Abraham, have pity on me and send Lazarus to dip the tip of his finger in water and cool my tongue, because I am in agony in this fire."
>
> But Abraham replied, "Son, remember that in your lifetime you received your good things, while Lazarus received bad things, but now he is comforted here and you are in agony. And besides all this, between us and you a great chasm has been fixed, so that those who want to go from here to you cannot, nor can anyone cross over from there to us."
>
> He answered, "Then I beg you, father, send Lazarus to my father's house, for I have five brothers. Let him warn them, so that they will not also come to this place of torment." Abraham replied, "They have Moses and the Prophets; let them listen to them." "No, father Abraham," he said, "but if someone from the dead goes to them, they will repent."
>
> He said to him, "If they do not listen to Moses and the Prophets, they will not be convinced even if someone rises from the dead." (Luke 16:19-31)

Notice the crucial principle: **If you do not believe the Old Testament—the Law and the Prophets—then the resurrection means nothing to you.** Even if someone were to rise from the dead before your eyes, you would not be convinced. Conversely, if you do not believe the Old Testament, you do not truly believe in Jesus.

### What the Resurrection Actually Is

The resurrection is far more significant than merely a great miracle or proof of divine power. To understand its true importance, we must look at what Luke's Gospel itself tells us.

## The Context: Jesus's Death and Burial

Jesus has died. Joseph of Arimathea, a member of the Sanhedrin who had not consented to Jesus's crucifixion, asked Pilate for his body. The women from Galilee—Mary Magdalene, Joanna, Mary the mother of James, and others—witnessed where he was laid. They observed the burial rites: the linen wrappings, the tomb, the Sabbath rest. On the third day, they returned with spices and perfumes to anoint his body, a final act of honor and care for the dead.

Then they encounter two men in bright clothes at the tomb. But these messengers don't begin with "He's over there" or introduce themselves. Instead, they say something peculiar:

> He is not here; he has risen! Remember how he told you, while he was still with you in Galilee: "The Son of Man must be delivered into the hands of sinful men, be crucified and on the third day be raised again." (Luke 24:6-7)

The command is clear: **Remember.** Remember his words. Remember what Jesus predicted.

## Three Reasons the Resurrection Matters

### 1. The Resurrection Fulfills Jesus's Own Words

Throughout Luke's Gospel, from chapter 9 onward, Jesus repeatedly predicts his death and resurrection:

> The Son of Man must suffer many things and be rejected by the elders, chief priests and teachers of the law, and he must be killed and on the third day be raised to life. (Luke 9:22)

> The Son of Man in his day will be like the lightning, which flashes and lights up the sky from one end to the other. But first he must suffer many things and be rejected by this generation. (Luke 17:24-25)

> He will be handed over to the Gentiles. They will mock him, insult him, spit on him, flog him and kill him. On the third day he will rise again. (Luke 18:32-33)

Jesus's predictions were not invented on the spot. They were anchored in Scripture. As Jesus himself said:

> Everything that is written by the prophets about the Son of Man will be fulfilled. (Luke 18:31)

> It is written: "And he was numbered with the transgressors"; and I tell you that this must be fulfilled in me. (Luke 22:37)

### 2. The Resurrection Fulfills Old Testament Prophecy

The passages Jesus referenced come from Isaiah and the Psalms. Consider Isaiah 53, the great prophecy of the suffering servant:

> Who has believed our message and to whom has the arm of the LORD been revealed? He grew up before him like a tender shoot, and like a root out of dry ground. He had no beauty or majesty to attract us to him, nothing in his appearance that we should desire him. He was despised and rejected by men, a man of sorrows, and familiar with suffering. Like one from whom men hide their faces he was despised, and we esteemed him not.
>
> Surely he took up our infirmities and carried our sorrows, yet we considered him stricken by God, smitten by him, and afflicted. But he was pierced for our transgressions, he was crushed for our iniquities; the punishment that brought us peace was upon him, and by his wounds we are healed.
>
> We all, like sheep, have gone astray, each of us has turned to his own way; and the LORD has laid on him the iniquity of us all. He was oppressed and afflicted, yet he did not open his mouth; he was led like a lamb to the slaughter, and as a sheep before her shearers is silent, so he did not open his mouth.
>
> By oppression and judgment he was taken away. And who can speak of his descendants? For he was cut off from the land of the living; for the transgression of my people he was stricken. He was assigned a grave with the wicked, and with the rich in his death, though he had done no violence, nor was any deceit in his mouth. (Isaiah 53:1-9)

Yet in the midst of this account of suffering, hope breaks through:

> Yet it was the LORD's will to crush him and cause him to suffer, and though the LORD makes his life a guilt offering, he will see his offspring and prolong his days, and the will of the LORD will prosper in his hand. (Isaiah 53:10)

The language here speaks of life—prolonged days, offspring, the prosperity of God's will. When this prophecy was first written, it spoke of a servant who would live and endure. Jesus, of course, did not have offspring in the natural sense, nor did he continue life as ordinary mortals do. But the principle is unmistakable: **He lives. He is alive. He has risen.** 

God's plan all along was that Jesus would rise again. God would vindicate him and prolong his days. He would not remain in the grave eternally but would be raised to life.

### 3. The Resurrection Declares That We Are Forgiven and That Jesus Is Lord and Judge

When we understand that Jesus is the suffering servant described in Isaiah 53—the one who "had no beauty or majesty to attract us," who "was despised and rejected by men," who "grew up like a tender shoot out of dry ground"—then we also understand that **He took up our infirmities and carried our sorrows. He was pierced for our transgressions and crushed for our iniquities. We are healed by his wounds, and his punishment brings us peace. The LORD has laid on him the iniquity of us all.**

The resurrection declares that this work is complete. **Our sins are forgiven.**

But there is more. The Old Testament prophesies not only of a suffering servant but of God the King himself coming in the last days to reign and to judge. The resurrection reveals that Jesus is this risen Messiah—now the Lord of the earth. He will judge the world as King. The time of judgment is here.

In the parable of the rich man and Lazarus, we see this division made manifest: some stand on one side of an uncrossable chasm, while others stand on the other. This is the reality the resurrection establishes.

Moreover, the resurrection means that the kingdom has finally arrived. Joseph of Arimathea had read Isaiah and the Law and the Prophets. He believed them and waited for the promised one. When Jesus was crucified, Joseph's hope wavered—perhaps this wasn't the one after all. But with the resurrection, all is confirmed: **Yes, he is! The kingdom is finally here. There is no more waiting.** The consolation of Israel has come. As Isaiah proclaimed, "Comfort, comfort, O Israel; your sins are paid for." It is fulfilled.

## Your Response

The resurrection means that Jesus is the King and Judge of his kingdom. He sits ruling and judging now. And he offers forgiveness to all who turn to him in repentance.

The resurrection is not a mere miracle or demonstration of power or an inspiration for belief. It is the fulfillment of the Old Testament—the Law and the Prophets. If you believe them, the resurrection means you are forgiven. If you do not believe them, you are being judged by the Lord of the universe.

**The resurrection means that the time of belief and repentance is now.**

Luke shows us various responses to this reality. The apostles initially dismiss the women's report as nonsense. Peter runs to the tomb and, seeing the grave clothes, walks away wondering to himself what has happened. But the women remember. They recall what Jesus said. They rush to tell others the good news. And as they remember all that Jesus had explained concerning Moses and the Prophets—all of Scripture—their eyes are opened. They understand.

May you too remember what Jesus has spoken. May your eyes be opened to understand that this is the hour of repentance before the King and Judge of the universe. May you accept the forgiveness he freely offers.

Repent today.
