# Romans 14:1–15:6

**Title:** Serve the Weak

**Date:** 22 June 2024 (ARISE) / 17 March 2013 (SMAC)

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## When Christians Disagree

Throughout church history, believers have faced numerous controversies and disagreements over matters of practice and conscience. Some disputes have centered on theology itself—whether Christ's blood was divine or human, the nature of the Holy Communion, or the proper interpretation of Scripture. Others have focused on church practice: which day to meet, how to dress, whether to use music, drums, or organs in worship.

This passage addresses a particularly tender area: how strong and weak believers should relate to one another when they disagree on matters that Scripture hasn't definitively settled.

---

## 1. Accept the Weak—Do Not Despise

> Accept the one whose faith is weak, without quarreling over disputable matters. One person's faith allows them to eat anything, but another, whose faith is weak, eats only vegetables. (Romans 14:1-2)

### Who Are the Weak and the Strong?

The Roman church contained both Jewish and Gentile believers, each bringing their own backgrounds into the fellowship.

**The weak** appear to be believers struggling with their conscience about certain freedoms. They had been trained from childhood in strict observance—dietary laws, Sabbath observance, festival days. For a Jewish Christian, this wasn't merely habit; it was the foundation of spiritual discipline built over generations. The weak might also include Gentiles who had formerly worshipped idols and still felt the pull of that past, unable to eat meat that had been sacrificed to those idols without guilt.

**The strong** are those who understand that Christ has fulfilled the law's requirements. They recognize with Paul that:

> As one who is in the Lord Jesus, I am fully convinced that no food is unclean in itself. (Romans 14:14)

Since Jesus declared all foods clean, and since idols are nothing in the presence of Christ's lordship, meat is simply meat—neither more nor less special than ordinary food.

### The Problem: Contempt, Not Discernment

> The one who eats everything must not treat with contempt the one who does not, and the one who does not eat everything must not judge the one who does, for God has accepted them. (Romans 14:3)

Here Paul addresses something subtle yet serious: the strong believer's tendency to look down on the weak one. When someone says, "Why are you living in the past? Don't you understand the gospel? Where is your faith in Christ?"—that's contempt dressed up as spiritual challenge.

But Paul also warns the weak not to judge or condemn those who exercise their freedom. Both attitudes are wrong because God has accepted both kinds of believers.

### What Does "Judge" Mean?

This seems contradictory—Paul uses the word "judge" and then says don't judge. But look carefully at the context:

> Who are you to judge someone else's servant? To their own master, servants stand or fall. And they will stand, for the Lord is able to make them stand. (Romans 14:4)

Paul distinguishes between two kinds of judging:

**Not this kind:** Discernment. Jesus himself called us to judge and discern:

> Why don't you judge for yourselves what is right? (Luke 12:57)

Peter and John told their opponents: "You be the judges!" (Acts 4:19). Paul tells the Corinthians they should be wise enough to settle disputes among themselves (1 Corinthians 6:5) and to judge sensible teaching (1 Corinthians 10:15).

Christians are expected to be discerning, to distinguish truth from falsehood, trustworthy people from untrustworthy ones. Being Christian doesn't mean you've surrendered your brain in the name of tolerance.

**But avoid this kind:** Contempt. The kind of judgment Paul forbids is despising, looking down upon, condemning someone for something that isn't actually sin. The weak one hasn't sinned by maintaining their stricter conscience. The strong one hasn't sinned by exercising their freedom.

When you contemn someone, you treat them as worthless. You've decided they don't deserve your respect. That's what Paul forbids.

### A Matter of Conscience

> One person considers one day more sacred than another; another considers every day alike. Each of them should be fully convinced in their own mind. (Romans 14:5)

Each believer must work out these disputable matters for themselves, in relation to God:

> So whatever you believe about these things keep between yourself and God. Blessed is the one who does not condemn himself by what he approves. (Romans 14:22)

This doesn't mean "keep it secret." It means you must settle these convictions between yourself and God. Your ultimate accountability is to him alone, not to the church, not to your pastor, not even to other believers.

> For we will all stand before God's judgment seat... So then, each of us will give an account of ourselves to God. (Romans 14:10b, 12)

God has no grandchildren, only children. You cannot hide behind the church, your leaders, your parents, or your friends. You are directly accountable to your Creator and Lord.

## 2. Look After the Weak—Do Not Stumble Them

But understanding your freedom is not the end of the story. Knowledge must be paired with love.

> Therefore let us stop passing judgment on one another. Instead, make up your mind not to put any stumbling block or obstacle in the way of a brother or sister. (Romans 14:13)

The strong believer must exercise their freedom with care. Yes, food is morally neutral. Yes, you have the right to eat anything. But when your weak brother or sister is present—someone whose conscience is more sensitive—restraint becomes an act of love.

### The Conscience as a Guard

Paul makes a crucial point:

> But if anyone regards something as unclean, then for that person it is unclean. (Romans 14:14b)

If someone with a tender conscience violates what they believe to be right, they sin—not because the action itself is sinful, but because they've gone against their conscience. To act against your conscience is to damage that final line of defense against true sin.

> But whoever has doubts is condemned if they eat, because their eating is not from faith; and everything that does not come from faith is sin. (Romans 14:23)

Think of conscience as the yuck factor—that internal reflex that says "this is wrong." We don't want to desensitize that reflex in a weaker believer. We want to help them develop an informed and properly trained conscience, not destroy the conscience they have.

### Practical Applications

**Alcohol and gambling.** These are morally neutral acts in themselves. But they are also perennial vices in our society, destroying families and causing untold suffering. For someone recovering from addiction, the context matters enormously. A church gathering where alcohol flows freely might trigger relapse. A social event centered around gambling could lead a recovering gambler back into bondage.

The strong believer might say, "I can drink responsibly. There's nothing wrong with a glass of wine." Theologically true. But is this the best place to exercise that freedom? Will this build up your brother or sister?

That's why churches thoughtfully avoid alcohol and gambling at their events—not out of legalism, but out of love for those whose consciences are still healing, whose habits are still being reformed.

**Modesty in dress.** There's no biblical law governing how you must dress. Your body, your choice. But if you dress immodestly, you make it harder for others—especially those struggling with lust—to keep their thoughts pure. The defense that "one guy isn't affected" misses the point entirely. He's the strong one. We're looking after the weak.

**Clubbing and other venues.** Nothing is inherently wrong with dancing or darkness. But put together alcohol, low lighting, and loud music all designed to lower inhibitions and self-control—and you've created an environment where most people will stumble. Think bigger picture than your own freedom.

### The Spirit of Self-Denial

> We who are strong ought to bear with the failings of the weak and not to please ourselves. Each of us should please our neighbors for their good, to build them up. For even Christ did not please himself... (Romans 15:1-3)

This is the measure of Christian maturity: not how much you can do, but how willing you are to lay aside your rights for the sake of others' growth.

> If your brother or sister is distressed because of what you eat, you are no longer acting in love. Do not by your eating destroy someone for whom Christ died. (Romans 14:15)

Christ died for that struggling believer. Their faith is precious to him. When you needlessly place a stumbling block in their path, you're working against the very redemption Christ purchased.

> For the kingdom of God is not a matter of eating and drinking, but of righteousness, peace and joy in the Holy Spirit. (Romans 14:17)

The kingdom isn't won or lost over food choices. It's about right relationships, internal peace, and the joy that only God's Spirit brings. Build toward those realities.

### The Goal: Mutual Edification

> Let us therefore make every effort to do what leads to peace and to mutual edification. Do not destroy the work of God for the sake of food. All food is clean, but it is wrong for a person to eat anything that causes someone else to stumble. (Romans 14:19-20)

Every time you choose restraint out of love, you're building up the body of Christ. Every time you refuse to use your freedom in ways that might harm a weaker believer, you're investing in their spiritual growth.

## 3. Remember: Our Judgments Are Provisional

One final caution: our judgments are always preliminary. We judge by external appearance and limited information. God judges hearts and motives.

> God does not judge by external appearance. (Galatians 2:6)

> You judge by human standards; I pass judgment on no one. But if I do judge, my decisions are right, because I am not alone. I stand with the Father, who sent me. (John 8:15-16)

> Therefore judge nothing before the appointed time; wait till the Lord comes. He will bring to light what is hidden in darkness and will expose the motives of men's hearts. (1 Corinthians 4:5)

We see dimly. We know only part of the story. We make snap judgments based on one sentence, never realizing the context or struggle behind someone's position. Be humble. Don't be proud that you're not struggling with what troubles your brother. Don't be hypocritical—if you find it hard to show restraint, what about the person next to you who finds it even harder?

Be slow to judge, especially if you're naturally critical. Ask more questions. Listen longer. Realize you might be caricaturing someone's position without understanding it fully. That person answers to God, not to you.

---

## A Call to Unity and Hope

> Accept one another, then, just as Christ accepted you, in order to bring praise to God. (Romans 15:7)

Christ didn't wait for you to get your act together before welcoming you. He accepted you while you were still weak, still struggling, still learning. That's the model for how we treat one another.

> May the God of hope fill you with all joy and peace as you trust in him, so that you may overflow with hope by the power of the Holy Spirit. (Romans 15:13)

The pathway forward isn't harsh judgment or stubborn insistence on your rights. It's learning to walk together in hope, each one growing in faith, each one bearing with the weaknesses of the other, all of you together moving toward the maturity that only Christ can bring.
