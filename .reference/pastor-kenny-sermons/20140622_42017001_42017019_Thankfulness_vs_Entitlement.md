# Luke 17:1-19

**Title:** Thankfulness vs. Entitlement  
**Date:** June 22, 2014  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction: First World Problems

We live in an age of entitlement. The term "First World Problems" captures this perfectly—those of us in developed nations take for granted luxuries that 99% of the world considers unimaginable:

- I can't sleep in because my gardener is mowing the lawn
- There are too many seeds in my watermelon
- I'm forced to watch Netflix in standard definition

These aren't real problems. They're symptoms of taking for granted what we actually have. And this attitude doesn't just affect how we view the world—it bleeds into how we relate to God.

---

## Part 1: God Does Not Owe Us (Luke 17:7-10)

Jesus illustrates this through a parable about a master and servant:

> "Suppose one of you had a servant plowing or looking after the sheep. Would he say to the servant when he comes in from the field, 'Come along now and sit down to eat'? Would he not rather say, 'Prepare my supper, get yourself ready and wait on me while I eat and drink; after that you may eat and drink'? Would he thank the servant because he did what he was told to do? So you also, when you have done everything you were told to do, should say, 'We are unworthy servants; we have only done our duty.'"

The servant has labored all day in the field—plowing the soil, tending the sheep, working under the Middle Eastern sun until muscles ache and blisters bleed. He feels entitled to rest and a meal. It's only fair, right?

But the master hasn't eaten yet. The servant's work isn't finished. And when the work is truly complete, there's no thanks required—the servant has merely done his duty.

**This challenges our cultural instincts.** We live in a society that calls welfare "entitlements." We complain when government benefits aren't generous enough, yet these are acts of generosity from taxpayers, not rights we possess. Can a beggar demand that a gift be larger? No—because generosity cannot be obligatory.

**Applied to God:** We treat our relationship with God the same way.

- "God owes me a beautiful spouse"
- "God owes me health, wealth, and my dreams"
- "If I don't get what I want, I'll rebel against Him"

But this is backwards. **God does not owe us anything.** We are the servants. God is the Master.

---

## Part 2: Rather, We Owe God (Luke 17:1-6)

The context reveals what our duty actually is. Jesus begins with a sobering warning:

> "Things that cause people to sin are bound to come, but woe to that person through whom they come. It would be better for him to be thrown into the sea with a millstone tied around his neck than for him to cause one of these little ones to sin."

This doesn't mean blocking people from Jesus's forgiveness. In Luke 18, the disciples try to prevent children from coming to Jesus, and He rebukes them sharply. "Let the little children come to me." To cause others to stumble means to keep them from encountering Christ and remaining trapped in their sin.

**What is our duty then?**

1. **Tell others about Jesus.** Evangelism is not optional; it's our ongoing work.

2. **Support the church** through time and finances, whose primary purpose is to point people to Jesus and teach His Word.

3. **Pray for others** and intercede for their salvation.

And we grow tired. We want to rest. But our work on this earth is incomplete.

### The Scandal of Unforgiveness (Luke 17:3-4)

Jesus also addresses forgiveness as our duty:

> "If your brother sins, rebuke him, and if he repents, forgive him. If he sins against you seven times in a day, and seven times comes back to you and says, 'I repent,' forgive him."

Forgiveness is hard. We carry wounds from betrayal, hurtful words, broken promises. When we finally bring ourselves to forgive, we expect to rest from that forgiveness. We think one act of mercy should suffice.

But forgiveness is unlimited—"seven times" in Scripture means without limit. We don't get to hold grudges. We don't get to say, "I've forgiven once; that will do."

**Why?** Because we have been forgiven much. We who have received Christ's unlimited forgiveness must extend it to others. When we do forgive, we're not heroes deserving praise. We've simply done our duty.

### The Problem Isn't Lack of Faith (Luke 17:5-6)

The disciples respond with desperation:

> "Increase our faith!"

And Jesus replies:

> "If you have faith as small as a mustard seed, you can say to this mulberry tree, 'Be uprooted and planted in the sea,' and it will obey you."

The issue isn't the size of faith. A tiny mustard seed worth of faith is enough. **The issue is our willingness to do our duty, and a simple thank you is all that matters.**

## Part 3: Being Thankful (Luke 17:11-19)

Jesus encounters ten men with leprosy. They cry out:

> "Jesus, Master, have pity on us!"

He tells them:

> "Go, show yourselves to the priests."

And as they go, they are healed. But notice what happens next:

> "One of them, when he saw he was healed, came back, praising God in a loud voice. He threw himself at Jesus' feet and thanked him—and he was a Samaritan. Jesus asked, 'Were not all ten cleansed? Where are the other nine? Was no one found to return and give praise to God except this foreigner?' Then he said to him, 'Rise and go; your faith has made you well.'"

Only one returned. A Samaritan—an outsider, a foreigner. And to this one, Jesus says something remarkable: "Your faith has made you well."

The other nine were physically healed. But this one—the one who returned in gratitude—was saved. Wholeness came through thankfulness.

### Understanding Duty Through the Lens of Gratitude

We struggle with the word "duty." It sounds forced, joyless, the opposite of love. We prefer romance to obligation, emotion to logic.

But this is a misunderstanding.

**Duty is what we do when we are tempted to run but we love anyway.** When a mother presses forward despite exhaustion to care for her child, is there no love? When a soldier marches into battle despite fear, is there no love? Duty is not the absence of love—it's love expressed through commitment.

Watch students in selective schools. What drives their extraordinary work ethic? Often, it's thankfulness. "My parents sacrificed for this opportunity. I don't want to waste it. They've spent money on tutoring—I want to honor them."

**This is the key:** God did not owe us anything. Yet Jesus lived out perfect duty—and did it out of love. He gave us life, placed us in abundance, granted us redemption and forgiveness through His death on the cross.

Our response should be gratitude. And gratitude transforms duty from drudgery into joy.

## The Antidote to Entitlement: Count Your Blessings

We are a spoiled generation. We create "first world problems" while the world suffers:

- "I can't be generous because I need to buy another phone"
- "I can't commit to mission work because I'm booking flights to Korea"
- "I can't serve in ministry because I'm skiing in Canada"
- "I don't have time for outreach—I only get four months off university"
- "I won't visit that lonely person because I don't like the food they serve"

These aren't first world problems. **These are last world problems.**

We have been rescued by God. We have access to privileges beyond measure: eternal life, complete forgiveness, a seat in the heavenly realms, the incomparable riches of God's grace expressed in His kindness to us in Christ Jesus.

What can we do in response? A little service. It is nothing compared to what we've been given.

---

## Conclusion: Two Types of People

We can be grateful servants or entitled complainers. We can recognize our duty and live it out, or we can demand more than we deserve.

The best antidote to entitlement is simple: count your blessings.

And then, with grateful hearts, let us do our duty. Not out of compulsion, but out of love. Not waiting for thanks, but living as unworthy servants who have merely done what we were told.

Our work on this earth is not finished. God wants us to keep serving—in evangelism, in forgiveness, in generosity, in showing up and showing care. John Chapman, a beloved Christian worker, didn't retire to play golf. He died serving the Lord, still speaking and sharing Christ until his final days.

**So let us have no talk of entitlements and no more excuses.** We have a duty. Let's go do it together.
