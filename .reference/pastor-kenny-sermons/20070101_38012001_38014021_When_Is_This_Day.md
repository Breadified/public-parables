# Zechariah 12:1–14:21

**Title:** When Is This Day?  
**Date:** 21 January 2007  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Introduction: The D-Day Illustration

When we speak of waiting for an appointed day, few historical moments capture this longing better than D-Day—June 6, 1944. The Allies had endured years of careful preparation, and millions of people across Europe were waiting for the day of liberation. The Russians, exhausted from bearing the weight of the Eastern front since 1942, called for Western intervention. The occupied French yearned to be freed from German oppression. Even the Germans knew this day would come and fortified their defenses accordingly. General Eisenhower himself waited on the eve of the invasion, originally planning for June 5 before weather forced a one-day delay.

When the meteorologists spotted a break in the clouds, Eisenhower gave the order. On the morning of June 6, Operation Neptune began—the largest seaborne invasion in history. Some 156,000 troops landed in Normandy, supported by 11,590 aircraft. The naval armada included 6,939 vessels of various kinds. By June 11, over 326,000 troops had been landed along with supplies and equipment. Within months, Paris was liberated and the tide had turned decisively.

Even Hitler recognized that D-Day was decisive. He understood that while Germany might retreat in the East and survive, a successful Western invasion meant catastrophe. Indeed, less than a year later, on May 7, 1945, Germany surrendered unconditionally.

But before that day came, everyone was waiting. Everyone asked: *When is this day?*

### Israel's Waiting

In post-exilic times, Israel asked the same question. They waited for a day when God would return to them and bless them abundantly. They waited for a day when their enemies would cease mocking them and be defeated. They waited for a day when sin would be no more.

The book of Zechariah speaks to this waiting. Chapters 1–6 present a series of eight visions, each one God's promise that He will return one day. The remaining chapters (7–14) provide us with increasingly detailed information about what that future day will look like and when it will come—much like how military planners learned more and more details about D-Day as the invasion approached: the 9th Division, Normandy, 1944, June 6th.

## Part 1: The Characteristics of That Future Day

### Jerusalem Will Be Secure

> I am going to make Jerusalem a cup that sends all the surrounding peoples reeling. Judah will be besieged as well as Jerusalem. (Zechariah 12:2)

God promises that Jerusalem will become a cup of overwhelming judgment to those who drink from it. But more than that:

> On that day, when all the nations of the earth are gathered against her, I will make Jerusalem an immovable rock for all the nations. All who try to move it will injure themselves. (Zechariah 12:3)

Jerusalem will become immovable, like an unmovable rock. Those who attempt to move it will hurt only themselves.

> On that day I will strike every horse with panic and its rider with madness. I will keep a watchful eye over the house of Judah, but I will blind all the horses of the nations. (Zechariah 12:4–5)

Notice the contrast: God will watch over Judah with care while blinding the horses of the nations. Horses, in ancient warfare, represent military might and the capacity for war. This speaks of a day when warfare itself will cease.

### Jerusalem Will Have Great Leaders and People

> On that day I will make the leaders of Judah like a firepot in a woodpile, like a flaming torch among sheaves. They will consume right and left all the surrounding peoples, but Jerusalem will remain intact in her place. (Zechariah 12:6)

The leaders of Judah will be powerful and effective, yet the city itself will remain intact and secure.

God also promises something remarkable about equality:

> The LORD will save the dwellings of Judah first, so that the honor of the house of David and of Jerusalem's inhabitants may not be greater than that of Judah. On that day the LORD will shield those who live in Jerusalem, so that the feeblest among them will be like David. (Zechariah 12:7–8)

Here we see that while there is a priority given to David's house, the weakest person in Jerusalem will be elevated to the strength and stature of King David himself. This is a vision of fundamental equality—where no one person dominates over another, and therefore no one will be in a position to become corrupt through unchecked power.

### The Removal of Sin and False Prophets

> On that day a fountain will be opened to the house of David and the inhabitants of Jerusalem, to cleanse them from sin and impurity. (Zechariah 13:1)

When God comes to dwell, sin departs. He promises a public fountain—accessible to all—for the cleansing of guilt and uncleanness.

> On that day, I will banish the names of the idols from the land, and they will be remembered no more. I will remove both the prophets and the spirit of impurity from the land. (Zechariah 13:2)

Idolatry will be completely forgotten. False prophets and the spirit that motivates deception will be removed entirely. The passage continues with a stark picture of zero tolerance for deception:

> If anyone still prophesies, his father and mother, to whom he was born, will say to him, "You must die, because you have told lies in the LORD's name." When he prophesies, his own parents will stab him. (Zechariah 13:3)

The seriousness here is profound. There will be no room for lies spoken in God's name. Even a false prophet's own parents would reject him completely.

> Every prophet will be ashamed of his prophetic vision. He will not put on a prophet's garment of hair in order to deceive. He will say, "I am not a prophet. I am a farmer; the land has been my livelihood since my youth." (Zechariah 13:4–5)

False prophets will abandon their pretense entirely, preferring to be known as ordinary farmers rather than continue in their deception.

### The Result: True Peace

When the city is secure from external threats, when all people are treated with fundamental equality, and when sin and lies have been removed, what do you have? Peace. Complete wholeness. A society without corruption or warfare.

This future king is described earlier in Zechariah:

> Rejoice greatly, O Daughter of Zion! Shout, Daughter of Jerusalem! See, your king comes to you, righteous and having salvation, gentle and riding on a donkey, on a colt, the foal of a donkey. I will take away the chariots from Ephraim and the war-horses from Jerusalem, and the battle bow will be broken. He will proclaim peace to the nations. His rule will extend from sea to sea and from the River to the ends of the earth. (Zechariah 9:9–10)

Note what is absent: chariots, war-horses, battle bows. The king comes not in military might but on a donkey—a symbol of peace. His peace will extend across the entire world, from sea to sea.

### Application: What Day Are You Waiting For?

What is it that you genuinely long for? What day do you wait for in your deepest wishes? Many of us long for things the world offers: a new device, a certain lifestyle, a particular person, material security. These things have their appeal, but are they what we truly need?

Or do we long for genuine peace? For God to dwell among us? For truth to rule? For justice to prevail? For the end of sin itself?

We live in a world that offers counterfeits of peace—Pax Romana, the peace of empires built on power and dominance. Nations like Australia experience relative peace, but often only because they align with the right superpowers, not because true justice prevails globally. These earthly peaces are temporary and fragile.

But some of you are waiting patiently, enduring through difficult circumstances. I want to encourage you: keep doing so, knowing that your wait will not be in vain. The day is coming.

## Part 2: Suffering Comes First

Yet Zechariah's vision of the future day comes with a cost. Before we experience the fullness of that peace, there must be suffering.

### The Pierced One and Mourning

> I will pour out on the house of David and the inhabitants of Jerusalem a spirit of grace and supplication. They will look on me, the one they have pierced, and they will mourn for him as one mourns for an only child, and grieve bitterly for him as one grieves for a firstborn son. (Zechariah 12:10)

Who is this pierced one? God Himself declares it: "They will look on me, the one they have pierced."

> On that day the weeping in Jerusalem will be great, like the weeping of Hadad Rimmon in the plain of Megiddo. (Zechariah 12:11)

This reference calls to mind King Josiah, the reformer king who rediscovered the Law after centuries of neglect. He died tragically in battle against Egypt. The mourning for Josiah was immense—a great national loss. Similarly, the mourning for this pierced one will be profound, mourned by every family and clan throughout the land.

### The Shepherd Is Struck

> Awake, O sword, against my shepherd, against the man who is close to me! Strike the shepherd, and the sheep will be scattered, and I will turn my hand against the little ones. In the whole land, two-thirds will be struck down and perish; yet one-third will be left in it. This third I will bring into the fire; I will refine them like silver and test them like gold. They will call on my name and I will answer them; I will say, "They are my people," and they will say, "The LORD is our God." (Zechariah 13:7–9)

The shepherd—God's chosen one—will be struck. The sheep will be scattered, as if returning to exile. But of the remnant that remains, God will refine them like precious metals in fire. Through suffering comes purification and restoration of the covenant relationship.

### The Pattern of Redemptive Suffering

Peace comes at a price. This was true of D-Day—the liberation of Europe cost nearly 10,000 casualties on that single day. It was true for Jesus, who experienced suffering before resurrection and glory. It is true for every Christian.

> We also rejoice in our sufferings, because we know that suffering produces perseverance; perseverance, character; and character, hope. And hope does not disappoint us, because God has poured out his love into our hearts by the Holy Spirit, whom he has given us. (Romans 8:3–5)

> In bringing many sons to glory, it was fitting that God, for whom and through whom everything exists, should make the author of their salvation perfect through suffering. (Hebrews 2:10)

Understanding that suffering has a purpose—that it produces character and refines us like precious metals—makes our current difficulties more bearable. We are not suffering meaninglessly. We suffer with purpose.

## Part 3: The Day Is Actually Now

Here is where Zechariah becomes profoundly christological: the day has already come. The day finds its fulfillment in Jesus and the cross. This is how the New Testament interprets Zechariah.

### The Donkey: Zechariah 9:9 Fulfilled

The donkey passage would have been familiar to any Jewish reader anticipating the Messiah:

> Rejoice greatly, O Daughter of Zion! Shout, Daughter of Jerusalem! See, your king comes to you, righteous and having salvation, gentle and riding on a donkey, on a colt, the foal of a donkey. (Zechariah 9:9)

Matthew saw this fulfilled when Jesus entered Jerusalem:

> As they approached Jerusalem and came to Bethphage on the Mount of Olives, Jesus sent two disciples, saying to them, "Go to the village ahead of you, and at once you will find a donkey tied there, with her colt by her. Untie them and bring them to me. If anyone says anything to you, tell him that the Lord needs them, and he will send them right away." This took place to fulfill what was spoken through the prophet: "Say to the Daughter of Zion, 'See, your king comes to you, gentle and riding on a donkey, on a colt, the foal of a donkey.'" (Matthew 21:1–5)

Jesus is the king riding on the donkey. He is riding toward death—toward the cross.

### The Shepherd Is Struck: Zechariah 13:7 Fulfilled

The prophecy about striking the shepherd was fulfilled in Jesus:

> When they had sung a hymn, they went out to the Mount of Olives. Then Jesus told them, "This very night you will all fall away on account of me, for it is written: 'I will strike the shepherd, and the sheep of the flock will be scattered.' But after I have risen, I will go ahead of you into Galilee." (Matthew 26:30–32)

Jesus is the shepherd who is struck. The disciples are the sheep who scatter. And yet, He promises resurrection and restoration.

### Understanding "Mountains" in Light of Zechariah 14

This realization—that Zechariah is fulfilled in Jesus—unlocks passages that otherwise seem contradictory or difficult to understand. Consider Matthew's words about faith and moving mountains:

> He replied, "Because you have so little faith. I tell you the truth, if you have faith as small as a mustard seed, you can say to this mountain, 'Move from here to there' and it will move. Nothing will be impossible for you." (Matthew 17:20)

Many have understood this to mean: *If you have enough faith, God will do anything you ask.* But this interpretation misses the deeper christological meaning. We must read it in light of Zechariah 14:

> On that day his feet will stand on the Mount of Olives, east of Jerusalem, and the Mount of Olives will be split in two from east to west, forming a great valley, with half of the mountain moving north and half moving south. (Zechariah 14:4)

Zechariah promises that on the Day of the Lord, one will stand on the Mount of Olives, and the mountains themselves will split and move. This is cosmic, cataclysmic redemption. It speaks of a great exodus from bondage and a return to the new Jerusalem.

Jesus is saying to His disciples: your faith is not in yourselves or in your own power to believe. Your faith is in *the coming Day that I am about to fulfill*. You are believing in the Messiah moving mountains. You are believing in the Day—the Day of the cross. When you ask in alignment with this Day, your prayer will be answered, because you are asking for what God is already accomplishing.

> If you have faith and do not doubt, not only can you do what was done to the fig tree, but also you can say to this mountain, 'Go, throw yourself into the sea,' and it will be done. If you believe, you will receive whatever you ask for in prayer. (Matthew 21:21–22)

The promise is: believe in D-Day. Believe in the cross. That is the true Day that changes everything.

## Part 4: Application for Us

For the disciples, faith was directed toward a future event—the Day they awaited. But we have a different posture: we put our faith in the *past*. The Day has been accomplished.

Jesus has stood on the mountain. He has shaken it. A great exodus is occurring, not just from Egypt, but from sin and condemnation to righteousness and salvation. A return from exile—the spiritual exile of separation from God—to the new Jerusalem.

We trust in that past Day. We believe that all is done. The victory has been accomplished. We do not need new prophecies about Iraq or nuclear war or political upheaval. We need to believe what God has already done through the cross.

### We Have Won, But the Battle Continues

This is not to say there is no future. There is. But our future is merely an extrapolation of what has already been accomplished in the past. To understand the future, we must look to the past.

It is like D-Day. Once the Allies landed and established a beachhead at Normandy, the outcome of the war was essentially decided. Yet Hitler fought on for nearly a year. The formal surrender came on May 7, 1945, but the war's outcome was determined on June 6, 1944.

Similarly, Satan does not yet realize he has been defeated. He continues to fight. But we who believe in Jesus know the truth: *we have already won*. If you are a Christian, you stand on the winning side.

We are simply waiting for the final formalities—the full manifestation of what Christ has already accomplished.

### Look to the Cross

Look forward to the cross. The cross is our atonement. The cross is our victory over Satan. The cross is where the penalty of sin was paid. The cross is where God demonstrated His justice, satisfying His wrath against sin so that we might be reconciled to Him.

It would be foolish to return to slavery after being liberated. It would be as absurd as a French citizen continuing to speak German and salute Hitler after being freed from Nazi occupation. Yet this is what happens when Christians, having been freed by Christ's work on the cross, return to patterns of sin.

Keep trusting in Jesus' victory on the cross. That victory defeats Satan, strips him of his power to condemn you, and secures your future. Though Satan can still hurt you, he cannot ultimately harm you. You belong to the winning side.

---

## Conclusion

Zechariah offers us a vision of the future Day—a day of complete peace, of God dwelling with His people, of the removal of sin and the triumph of justice. We have traced that vision through its development in chapters 12–14, and we have seen how the New Testament finds its fulfillment in Jesus Christ and His cross.

As you continue reading Zechariah and the other post-exilic prophets, may you see in them the same Christ who was pierced for our transgressions, who was struck so that we might be gathered, and who accomplished on the cross the Day we have been waiting for.
