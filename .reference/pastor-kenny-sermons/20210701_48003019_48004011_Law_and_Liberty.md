# Galatians 3:19–4:11

**Title:** Law and Liberty  
**Date:** 25 July 2021  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Why Do Christians Value Freedom?

Christians appear disproportionately drawn to causes of freedom—from Sydney protests to Hong Kong demonstrations to American political movements. This pattern isn't coincidental. In Hong Kong, where Christians comprise only 12% of the population, they played an outsized role in the 2020 protests. Christian leaders like Cardinal Joseph Zen, Baptist minister Chu Yiu-Ming, Joshua Wong, and Agnes Chow were at the forefront. Even the movement's name—"Occupy Central with Love and Peace"—bears the unmistakable mark of Christian thought. Similarly, in America, Christians dominate the landscape of freedom-focused movements, from libertarian movements to civil rights causes.

Perhaps Galatians can help us understand why believers have such a deep, instinctive commitment to freedom.

## The Purpose of the Law (3:19)

In the previous passage, Paul established that the law cannot justify us before God. This raises a natural question: **If the law cannot make us righteous, why was it given at all?**

Paul offers three answers.

### 1. The Law Restrains and Reveals Sin

> 3:19 Why, then, was the law given at all? It was added because of transgressions until the Seed to whom the promise referred had come.

The phrase "because of transgressions" can be read two ways. The first reading suggests the law was given to *cause* sin—to make transgression visible and multiplied.

This interpretation finds support throughout Scripture:

> Romans 5:20 The law was brought in so that the trespass might increase. But where sin increased, grace increased all the more.

> 1 Corinthians 15:56 The sting of death is sin, and the power of sin is the law.

> Romans 7:13 Did that [law] which is good, then, become death to me? By no means! Nevertheless, in order that sin might be recognized as sin, it used what is good to bring about my death, so that through the commandment sin might become utterly sinful.

The law is designed to expose sin. It tells us we're doing wrong—often about things we didn't even realize were violations. The law is fundamentally *for the ignorant*. When someone moves to a new country and learns the laws, they discover behaviors they never knew were wrong. The law teaches.

But here's the troubling part: for the rebellious, the law becomes an invitation to transgression. A teenager forbidden from touching something suddenly finds that thing irresistible—not because it's inherently appealing, but because the prohibition creates the desire. The law gives sin its power.

This is the paradox: while the law intends to restrain sin, sin hijacks the law to create *more* sin. It's like setting a trap to catch a thief, only to find the thief has learned to operate within the trap's design. As Martin Luther observed, the law's ultimate function is to condemn us—to make us so aware of our guilt that we cry out to Christ for salvation.

**The second reading**: The law was added *because of* transgressions—to *protect* from sin.

In this view, the law is fundamentally protective. "Do not murder" protects people from being murdered. "Do not steal" protects property and social order. The law doesn't just restrain through punishment; it *retrains* society through teaching. When laws change, they reshape what future generations believe is acceptable or unacceptable.

Consider marriage law changes. Some reasoned: "People are already doing what they want; changing the law won't make a practical difference." But they missed the point. Laws aren't just about the present generation—they train future generations. Those advocating for legal changes understood this principle well. Why push so hard for change if it makes no practical difference? Because they're thinking of the future.

> Romans 7:12 So then, the law is holy, and the commandment is holy, righteous and good.

**The law is good.** But its goodness is limited. While the law intends to limit sin, sin exploits it. The law's intention and the law's actual effect have been corrupted.

> Romans 8:3 For what the law was powerless to do because it was weakened by the flesh, God did by sending his own Son in the likeness of sinful flesh to be a sin offering.

> Romans 7:11 For sin, seizing the opportunity afforded by the commandment, deceived me, and through the commandment put me to death.

This creates a vicious cycle: sin exists, so we add laws to restrain it. But sin hijacks those laws to create more sin. So we add more laws. It's like trying to control a cane beetle infestation by introducing cane toads—you've only created a new problem. Or like adding divorce laws to limit marital conflict, only to find yourself creating hundreds of new laws governing custody, property division, and bedroom specifications.

Some laws are necessary for society. But more laws does not guarantee a better society. Living together requires countless unstated rules that are impossible to codify. Each new law has unintended consequences that give sin new avenues for havoc.

> 3:22 But Scripture has locked up everything under the control of sin, so that what was promised, being given through faith in Jesus Christ, might be given to those who believe.

> 3:23 Before the coming of this faith, we were held in custody under the law, locked up until the faith that was to come would be revealed. 24 So the law was our guardian until Christ came that we might be justified by faith.

The law locks us down. But compulsion is not the solution to righteousness.

> 3:21 Is the law, therefore, opposed to the promises of God? Absolutely not! For if a law had been given that could impart life, then righteousness would certainly have come by the law.

### 2. The Law Is Temporary

> 3:23-24 Before the coming of this faith, we were held in custody under the law, locked up until the faith that was to come would be revealed. So the law was our guardian until Christ came that we might be justified by faith.

The words "custody" and "guardian" describe *temporary* roles. A custodian holds responsibility until it's relinquished. A guardian cares for a child until they reach adulthood. These roles have expiration dates.

> 4:1-2 What I am saying is that as long as an heir is underage, he is no different from a slave, although he owns the whole estate. The heir is subject to guardians and trustees until the time set by his father.

In ancient legal systems, children couldn't hold legal title to their own property because they couldn't take responsibility for it. Even today, minors cannot have certain legal responsibilities. If property belongs to a child in equity, someone else must hold legal title and sign contracts on their behalf. Until maturity arrives, the child remains effectively enslaved to guardians.

> 4:3 So also, when we were underage, we were in slavery under the elemental spiritual forces of the world.

These "elemental spiritual forces" (stoicheia in Greek)—the basic principles and worldly laws—kept God's people in slavery until maturity.

> Colossians 2:20-23 Since you died with Christ to the elemental spiritual forces of this world, why, as though you still belonged to the world, do you submit to its rules: "Do not handle! Do not taste! Do not touch!"? These rules, which have to do with things that are all destined to perish with use, are based on merely human commands and teachings. Such regulations indeed have an appearance of wisdom, with their self-imposed worship, their false humility and their harsh treatment of the body, but they lack any value in restraining sensual indulgence.

The law imperfectly and chaotically protects us until the real solution arrives. It's like enduring hardship until hope comes—like a student sleeping poorly and eating junk food through exam season, surviving as best as possible until the ordeal ends. The law is a temporary measure for those not yet mature.

### 3. The Law Points to Christ

> 4:4 But when the set time had fully come, God sent his Son, born of a woman, born under the law, 5 to redeem those under the law, that we might receive adoption to sonship.

> 4:6-7 Because you are his sons, God sent the Spirit of his Son into our hearts, the Spirit who calls out, "Abba, Father." So you are no longer a slave, but God's child; and since you are his child, God has made you also an heir.

We have reached maturity in salvation history. Christians have come of age. We no longer need laws to supervise our conduct—what time we come home, what we eat, how we dress. When we reach adulthood, we can spend our inheritance.

> 4:8-9 Formerly, when you did not know God, you were slaves to those who by nature are not gods. But now that you know God—or rather are known by God—how is it that you are turning back to those weak and miserable forces? Do you wish to be enslaved by them all over again?

We no longer need the law to guilt us into righteousness. We already know we are sinful. We've solved that problem through Christ, who took the penalty of the law for us.

> 4:10-11 You are observing special days and months and seasons and years! I fear for you, that somehow I have wasted my efforts on you.

Why return to empty rituals once designed to restrain sin but no longer necessary? It would be strange for an adult to surround themselves with childhood rules: "Don't touch the hot kettle," "Ask permission to use the toilet," "Sharpen your pencil only at designated times." 

Similarly, if the coronavirus were to disappear, would we continue wearing masks out of residual fear? That would be tragic—not for epidemiological reasons, but because we'd remain enslaved to a temporary measure long after its purpose had ended.

> 3:25-29 Now that this faith has come, we are no longer under a guardian. So in Christ Jesus you are all children of God through faith, for all of you who were baptized into Christ have clothed yourselves with Christ. There is neither Jew nor Gentile, neither slave nor free, nor is there male and female, for you are all one in Christ Jesus. If you belong to Christ, then you are Abraham's seed, and heirs according to the promise.

We are free. Not because external authorities have given us permission, but because the law is now written on our hearts and minds.

> Hebrews 10:16 "This is the covenant I will make with them after that time, says the Lord. I will put my laws in their hearts, and I will write them on their minds."

> Jeremiah 31:33 "This is the covenant I will make with the people of Israel after that time," declares the LORD. "I will put my law in their minds and write it on their hearts. I will be their God, and they will be my people."

Law was never the ultimate solution. A changed heart and mind—transformed by the gospel—is the solution. In salvation history itself, we see the progression: from 613 laws under Moses, to two great commandments under Jesus, to no external laws in heaven (though right and wrong remain). The law restrains; the gospel transforms.

> John 8:32 Then you will know the truth, and the truth will set you free.

## Freedom as a Christian Conviction

This theological understanding of the law explains why Christians instinctively value freedom. It's not incidental to our faith—it flows directly from the gospel.

When the gospel takes root in society, the need for external compulsion diminishes. People persuaded in heart and mind to love God and neighbor don't need laws forcing them to do so. Teaching suffices where compulsion becomes unnecessary.

In Western Christian nations, the reopening of the Bible 500 years ago during the Protestant Reformation unleashed a broad liberation: freedom of conscience, freedom of religion, freedom of nations, freedom of speech, universal suffrage (which functions only in morally educated peoples), increasing economic freedoms, and expanding personal liberty. This wasn't coincidental—it was the fruit of gospel transformation.

But the reverse is also true. As nations abandon the gospel, they necessitate ever more laws to restrain escalating evil. That is not how it should be.

As Christians, we should not rejoice at increasing laws. Where possible, we should advocate for freedom—not naively, but from a deep conviction that changed hearts produce better societies than proliferating rules.

Some Christians have understood this instinctively. Lebanese Christians came forward in recent protests against redefinitions of marriage and changes to abortion laws that not only legalize certain practices but criminalize speaking against them. They were being consistent. They asked for no special privileges—only freedom for all. They were being Christian.

For those troubled by legal expansions but fearful of government, peer pressure, or circumstances: remember, *we are the Protestants*. Not with outrage, but with courage, we should speak for freedom.

## The Two Covenants (4:21–31)

> 4:21-26 Tell me, you who want to be under the law, are you not aware of what the law says? For it is written that Abraham had two sons, one by the slave woman and the other by the free woman. His son by the slave woman was born according to the flesh, but his son by the free woman was born as the result of a divine promise. These things are being taken figuratively: The women represent two covenants. One covenant is from Mount Sinai and bears children who are to be slaves: This is Hagar. Now Hagar stands for Mount Sinai in Arabia and corresponds to the present city of Jerusalem, because she is in slavery with her children. But the Jerusalem that is above is free, and she is our mother.

Paul uses Abraham's household as a metaphor for two covenants: the covenant of law (represented by Hagar, the slave woman) and the covenant of promise (represented by Sarah, the free woman). The law produces slaves; the promise produces free children.

> 4:27-31 For it is written: "Be glad, barren woman, you who never bore a child; shout for joy and cry aloud, you who were never in labor; because more are the children of the desolate woman than of her who has a husband." Now you, brothers and sisters, like Isaac, are children of promise. At that time the son born according to the flesh persecuted the son born by the power of the Spirit. It is the same now. But what does Scripture say? "Get rid of the slave woman and her son, for the slave woman's son will never share in the inheritance with the free woman's son." Therefore, brothers and sisters, we are not children of the slave woman, but of the free woman.

We are children of the free woman—of promise, not law. Our identity is not determined by legal compliance but by our relationship with Christ. The law may have once been our guardian, but maturity has arrived. We are free.
