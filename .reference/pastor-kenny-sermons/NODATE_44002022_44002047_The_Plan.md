# Acts 2:22-47

**Title:** The Plan  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## God's Accreditation of Jesus

> Fellow Israelites, listen to this: Jesus of Nazareth was a man accredited by God to you by miracles, wonders and signs, which God did among you through him, as you yourselves know. (Acts 2:22)

Jesus came with divine credentials. The miracles, wonders, and signs performed through him were not incidental—they were God's public testimony to his identity and authority.

## God's Plan and Foreknowledge

> This man was handed over to you by God's deliberate plan and foreknowledge; and you, with the help of wicked men, put him to death by nailing him to the cross. But God raised him from the dead, freeing him from the agony of death, because it was impossible for death to keep its hold on him. (Acts 2:23-24)

This is the heart of the gospel: Jesus' death and resurrection were not accidents caught God by surprise. They were part of God's eternal plan from the beginning. Yes, human wickedness played a role, but God's sovereignty encompassed all of it. And crucially, death could not hold him. The resurrection proves that no power—human or demonic—can ultimately resist God's purposes.

## The Old Testament Confirms the Resurrection

Peter now turns to David's prophecy, which he applies to Jesus:

> I saw the Lord always before me.
> Because he is at my right hand, I will not be shaken.
> Therefore my heart is glad and my tongue rejoices;
> my body also will rest in hope,
> because you will not abandon me to the realm of the dead,
> you will not let your holy one see decay.
> You have made known to me the paths of life;
> you will fill me with joy in your presence. (Acts 2:25-28, quoting Psalm 16:8-11)

The key point: David himself died and was buried, his tomb still standing in Jerusalem. But David was also a prophet who knew God's promise—that one of his descendants would sit on his throne eternally. David understood that this promise could only be fulfilled through resurrection. So when David spoke these words, he was speaking prophetically of the Messiah's resurrection, not his own.

> God has raised this Jesus to life, and we are all witnesses of it. (Acts 2:32)

Peter's claim is staggering: "We saw him. We know he rose." This is eyewitness testimony to the resurrection—the ultimate accreditation of Jesus as Lord and Messiah.

## The Ascension and the Holy Spirit

> Exalted to the right hand of God, he has received from the Father the promised Holy Spirit and has poured out what you now see and hear. (Acts 2:33)

After his resurrection and ascension, Jesus received the Holy Spirit from the Father and poured it out on the church. What they were experiencing at Pentecost—the tongues, the power, the boldness—was Jesus' gift to his people. This was the fulfillment of Old Testament promises.

> For David did not ascend to heaven, and yet he said,
> 
> "The Lord said to my Lord:
> Sit at my right hand
> until I make your enemies
> a footstool for your feet." (Acts 2:34-35, quoting Psalm 110:1)

Again, Peter appeals to David's own words. David acknowledged that someone greater than himself—his "Lord"—would sit at God's right hand. This is the Messiah.

## The Verdict: Jesus is Lord and Messiah

> Therefore let all Israel be assured of this: God has made this Jesus, whom you crucified, both Lord and Messiah. (Acts 2:36)

This is the conclusion of Peter's entire argument. The one you killed is now revealed as your Lord and your Messiah. The resurrection is God's decisive declaration of Jesus' identity.

**Important note:** The resurrection of Jesus is the accreditation of *his* resurrection and his unique status as Messiah. We should not confuse this with miracles or resurrections that may occur today. Jesus' resurrection stands alone as the ultimate sign and wonder.

## Our Response: Repentance and Faith

> When the people heard this, they were cut to the heart and said to Peter and the other apostles, "Brothers, what shall we do?" (Acts 2:37)

The proclamation of the gospel produces conviction. The people understood that they had participated in killing the Messiah, and they were desperate to know how to make it right.

> Peter replied, "Repent and be baptized, every one of you, in the name of Jesus Christ for the forgiveness of your sins. And you will receive the gift of the Holy Spirit. The promise is for you and your children and for all who are far off—for all whom the Lord our God will call." (Acts 2:38-39)

Peter's call is simple and clear: turn from your sins and be baptized in Jesus' name. This isn't about earning forgiveness through ritual—baptism is an outward expression of inward repentance. And the promise isn't limited to that day's audience; it extends to their descendants and to all whom God calls, regardless of distance or ethnic background.

> With many other words he warned them; and he pleaded with them, "Save yourselves from this corrupt generation." (Acts 2:40)

Peter urgently pressed them to separate themselves from the prevailing corruption of their society. This is not passive faith; it's an active turning away from worldly values.

> Those who accepted his message were baptized, and about three thousand were added to their number that day. (Acts 2:41)

Three thousand people responded in a single day. This wasn't a slow trickle of converts—it was a harvest. God was gathering his people.

## Life in the Early Church

> They devoted themselves to the apostles' teaching and to fellowship, to the breaking of bread and to prayer. Everyone was filled with awe at the many wonders and signs performed by the apostles. (Acts 2:42-43)

The new believers immediately committed to four priorities: learning God's Word through apostolic teaching, enjoying deep community with one another, sharing meals together, and praying. And God continued to validate his Word through signs and wonders.

> All the believers were together and had everything in common. They sold property and possessions to give to anyone who had need. (Acts 2:44-45)

This passage is often misread as communism or socialism. It's neither. The believers practiced generous, voluntary sharing because of their love for one another and their recognition that all things belong ultimately to God. This was not a permanent, compulsory system but rather an expression of radical generosity in response to genuine need.

**Important clarification:** Voluntary, loving generosity is not the same as state-mandated communism. The New Testament affirms private property throughout. Ananias and Sapphira's sin (Acts 5) was not that they kept back some of their property—Peter explicitly says it remained their own possession—but that they lied about giving it all while taking credit as if they had. The early church practiced *community of use*, not *community of possession*. They owned things privately but used them generously.

> Every day they continued to meet together in the temple courts. They broke bread in their homes and ate together with glad and sincere hearts, praising God and enjoying the favor of all the people. And the Lord added to their number daily those who were being saved. (Acts 2:46-47)

Daily worship, daily fellowship, daily meals together, and continuous growth. The church was alive, vibrant, and visible in its generosity and joy. This wasn't hidden away—the people around them noticed and respected them, and God himself was adding to their number.

---

## Summary

The church is built on God's plan accomplished through Christ's death and resurrection, responded to through repentance and faith, and lived out in joyful community, apostolic teaching, and generous love. This is the pattern for the church in every generation.
