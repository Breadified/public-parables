# Daniel 8:1-27

**Title:** How Long

## The Gift of Foresight

Most of us wish we had foresight—the ability to know which exam questions will be asked, which stocks will rise, what the future holds. Instead, we're left with hindsight, seeing our mistakes only after it's too late to change them.

Daniel, however, receives something remarkable: a vision of the distant future. Through this prophecy, God grants him insight into what will happen to His people over the next 500 years. This vision addresses two critical questions: **What will happen?** and **When will it end?**

---

## Part One: What to Expect—The Vision

### The Setting

> In the third year of King Belshazzar's reign, I, Daniel, had a vision, after the one that had already appeared to me. In my vision I saw myself in the citadel of Susa in the province of Elam; in the vision I was beside the Ulai Canal. (Daniel 8:1-2)

Daniel receives this vision during Babylon's reign, but it reveals what comes next: the rise and fall of empires that will affect God's people for centuries.

### The Ram: Medo-Persia

> I looked up, and there before me was a ram with two horns, standing beside the canal, and the horns were long. One of the horns was longer than the other but grew up later. I watched the ram as it charged toward the west and the north and the south. No animal could stand against it, and none could rescue from its power. It did as it pleased and became great. (Daniel 8:3-4)

The vision is interpreted for us directly:

> The two-horned ram that you saw represents the kings of Media and Persia. (Daniel 8:20)

The imbalanced horns represent the smaller Medes and the greater Persians. Though the Medes were initially stronger, Persia ultimately dominated under King Cyrus the Great. This combined kingdom would rule for approximately 200 years, far outlasting Babylon's 70-year reign. The ram's ability to charge in all directions—west, north, and south—reflects Persia's military dominance and unstoppable expansion.

### The Goat: Greece

> Suddenly a goat with a prominent horn between its eyes came from the west, crossing the whole earth without touching the ground. It came toward the two-horned ram I had seen standing beside the canal and charged at it in great rage. I saw it attack the ram furiously, striking the ram and shattering its two horns. The ram was powerless to stand against it; the goat knocked it to the ground and trampled on it, and none could rescue the ram from its power. (Daniel 8:5-7)

Scripture identifies this clearly:

> The shaggy goat is the king of Greece, and the large horn between its eyes is the first king. (Daniel 8:21)

The prominent horn represents Alexander the Great—a legendary figure who tamed a wild horse at age ten, studied under Aristotle, and became the most renowned military commander in history. The goat "crossing the whole earth without touching the ground" describes Alexander's extraordinary speed: he conquered the entire Persian Empire in just ten years (336-326 BC), extending his dominion all the way to the borders of India.

### The Four Horns: Divided Kingdoms

> The goat became very great, but at the height of its power the large horn was broken off, and in its place four prominent horns grew up toward the four winds of heaven. (Daniel 8:8)

Alexander's death in 323 BC was sudden and unexpected. After his demise, his kingdom fragmented through intrigue and warfare, eventually dividing among four of his generals:

- **Cassander** controlled Macedonia and Greece
- **Lysimachus** ruled Thrace and much of Asia Minor  
- **Ptolemy** claimed Egypt (ancestor of Cleopatra)
- **Seleucus** inherited the former Persian Empire

Scripture confirms this division:

> The four horns that replaced the one that was broken off represent four kingdoms that will emerge from his nation but will not have the same power. (Daniel 8:22)

### The Little Horn: Antiochus Epiphanes

From the Seleucid kingdom in the east comes a new power—a small horn that grows increasingly mighty:

> Out of one of them came another horn, which started small but grew in power to the south and to the east and toward the Beautiful Land. It grew until it reached the host of the heavens, and it threw some of the starry host down to the earth and trampled on them. (Daniel 8:9-10)

This "Beautiful Land" is Jerusalem and Judea. The prophecy continues:

> In the latter part of their reign, when rebels have become completely wicked, a fierce-looking king, a master of intrigue, will arise. He will become very strong, but not by his own power. He will cause astounding devastation and will succeed in whatever he does. He will destroy those who are mighty, the holy people. He will cause deceit to prosper, and he will consider himself superior. When they feel secure, he will destroy many and take his stand against the Prince of princes. (Daniel 8:23-25)

Historians identify this ruler as **Antiochus IV Epiphanes**, one of the last Seleucid kings. "Epiphanes" means "God manifest"—a title he gave himself, claiming to be a god incarnate. He was the first of the Greek kings to assume such a title.

### Antiochus's Persecution of the Jews

In 167 BC, returning enraged from a military defeat in Egypt, Antiochus attacked Jerusalem. The historical record is brutal:

> On the King's return from Egypt, he attacked Jerusalem and restored Menelaus. When these happenings were reported to the king, he thought that Judea was in revolt. Raging like a wild animal, he took Jerusalem by storm. He ordered his soldiers to cut down without mercy those whom they met and to slay those who took refuge in their houses. There was a massacre of young and old, a killing of women and children, a slaughter of virgins and infants. In the space of three days, eighty thousand were lost, forty thousand meeting a violent death, and the same number being sold into slavery. (2 Maccabees 5:11-14)

Determined to consolidate his empire, Antiochus outlawed Jewish religious practices and ordered the worship of Zeus as supreme. When observant Jews refused, he sent an army to enforce his decree. He desecrated the temple, placing an idol and sacrificing a pig at the altar—forcing Jews to eat the unclean meat.

The prophecy captures this outrage:

> It set itself up to be as great as the commander of the army of the Lord; it took away the daily sacrifice from the Lord, and his sanctuary was thrown down. Because of rebellion, the Lord's people and the daily sacrifice were given over to it. It prospered in everything it did, and truth was thrown to the ground. (Daniel 8:11-12)

## Part Two: When Will It End?—The Timeline

Faced with this devastating vision, Daniel hears the crucial question:

> How long will it take for the vision to be fulfilled—the vision concerning the daily sacrifice, the rebellion that causes desolation, the surrender of the sanctuary and the trampling underfoot of the Lord's people? (Daniel 8:13)

The answer comes with divine specificity:

> It will take 2,300 evenings and mornings; then the sanctuary will be reconsecrated. (Daniel 8:14)

"Evenings and mornings" refers to the daily temple sacrifices. According to historical records, Antiochus desecrated the temple on Kislev (December) 15, 167 BC, and it was rededicated on Kislev 25, 164 BC—spanning approximately three years and ten days. (The Jewish people commemorate this rededication in the festival of Hanukkah, the "Feast of Dedication.")

### Why This Matters

The precision of this prophecy is extraordinary. Consider: Daniel prophesies events that would occur some 500 years in his future—equivalent to someone in Shakespeare's time predicting that Washington DC would become the center of world power and that a specific 21st-century leader would implement particular trade policies. The specificity is staggering.

Some scholars doubt Daniel's authorship because the prophecy is so detailed. They argue it must have been written *after* these events occurred. But this misunderstands how biblical prophecy works. Deuteronomy establishes the test for a true prophet:

> If the prophet speaks in the Lord's name but his prediction does not happen or come true, you will know that the Lord did not give that message. (Deuteronomy 18:22)

Daniel's inclusion in the Jewish scriptures is not because it reads like history, but because it has stood the test of time as an accurate prophecy.

## A Message of Comfort to God's People

There's a shift in tone beginning with chapter 8. Chapters 1-7 address the whole world, explaining who the true King of Kings is. From chapter 8 onward, the focus narrows: *What will happen to God's people?* The language returns to Hebrew—a special word written to Daniel and the ancient Jews.

The core message is one of comfort:

> Yet he will be destroyed, but not by human power. (Daniel 8:25)

History confirms this. Antiochus's persecution sparked the **Maccabean Revolt**. Under Judas Maccabeus, the Jewish people rose up and defeated the Greeks. Antiochus himself died of a horrible disease, never subjugating Judea permanently. God kept His promise.

### God Explains What's Coming

Consider a doctor's explanation before surgery or childbirth. The doctor details each step: remove your shirt, wash the area, apply anesthetic (it might hurt briefly), then the procedure, then recovery. Why? Not to make you a surgeon, but to allay your fears. Understanding what's coming makes us less anxious.

Similarly, God gives Daniel this detailed timetable not to alarm but to inform:

> It's going to get rough for a little while, but I have it all under control, and I will protect you.

## The Broader Application: From Daniel to Jesus

Daniel's vision extends beyond Antiochus to encompass the "time of the end." In Mark 13, Jesus himself references the "abomination that causes desolation":

> When you see the abomination that causes desolation standing where it does not belong—let the reader understand—then let those who are in Judea flee to the mountains. (Mark 13:14)

Jesus uses Daniel's language to speak of future desolation. But what is this ultimate abomination?

In John 2, Jesus describes himself as the temple that will be torn down: He is God's dwelling place on earth. The greatest abomination, the ultimate desolation, is God crucified on the cross. When evil men destroyed Jesus's physical body, they committed the most horrific act imaginable—yet God used it for our redemption.

Jesus has already come. The Anointed One was crucified for us. The Son of Man has ascended into heaven. We now live in the closing credits of the movie—that strange time after the main story ends but before we leave the theater.

## Living with Foresight

What does all this mean for us today?

> Watch out that no one deceives you. Many will come in my name, claiming, "I am he," and will deceive many. When you hear of wars and rumors of wars, do not be alarmed. Such things must happen, but the end is still to come... You will be handed over to the local councils and flogged in the synagogues. Everyone will hate you because of me, but the one who stands firm to the end will be saved. (Mark 13:5-7, 9, 13)

Nothing should surprise us as we await Jesus's return. This doesn't mean becoming fatalistic, but rather: **We should not be shocked at the depravity of the world.** Governments will exalt themselves as God. Powers will contradict God's commands. Evil will persist.

Yet we have been given something precious: **the gift of foresight**. We know how the story ends. We know evil will be defeated. This knowledge should shape our response:

- **We grow the church.** We evangelize where we can. If public witness becomes difficult, we expand privately.
- **We revert to organic growth.** Through marriage and children, through faithful families multiplying disciples.
- **We move step by step,** trusting God's plan even as uncertainty surrounds us.

### The Gift of Hindsight

Perhaps more important than foresight is **hindsight**—looking back to the cross.

> And the God of all grace, who called you to his eternal glory in Christ, after you have suffered a little while, will himself restore you and make you strong, firm and steadfast. (1 Peter 5:10)

> For our light and temporary affliction is producing for us an eternal glory that far outweighs our troubles. (2 Corinthians 4:17)

No matter what the immediate future holds, we have assurance in Christ. The cross is finished. It's done. The victory is won.

When Daniel heard the angel's explanation, he didn't fully understand. But we do. We have the gift of hindsight. We can look back at the cross and know with absolute certainty: **God wins. Evil is defeated. And we are His.**
