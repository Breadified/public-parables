# 1 Peter 4:7-4:17

**Title:** Being Different
**Date:** 11 February 2007
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction: The Challenge of Uniqueness

We often think of ourselves as unique individuals, yet we find ourselves following trends. Young people especially operate within a herd mentality—girls follow fashion trends, guys pursue the same technologies, we all aim for the same universities and courses. Peer pressure keeps us conforming, even as we claim individuality. It's difficult to find joy in being different, particularly when we're young and our friendships depend on fitting in.

As we reach the end of our study of Peter's first letter, we discover an encouraging truth: we're called to be different despite suffering. This isn't about rebelliousness for its own sake, but about participating in something greater than ourselves.

## 1. True Joy is in Suffering for Christ

### Don't Be Surprised by Trials

> Dear friends, do not be surprised at the fiery ordeal that has come on you to test you, as though something strange were happening to you. (1 Peter 4:12)

Peter tells us not to be surprised when painful trials come—they should not seem strange or alien to us as Christians. But notice that Peter is talking about a specific kind of suffering.

> If you suffer, it should not be as a murderer or thief or any other kind of criminal, or even as a meddler. (1 Peter 4:15)

We're not talking about suffering brought on by our own foolishness or harmful choices. We're certainly not talking about self-harm. If you're struggling with that, please speak with a leader. The suffering Peter addresses is something entirely different—suffering *as a Christian*, for living out our faith.

### The Attitude of Rejoicing

> However, if you suffer as a Christian, do not be ashamed, but praise God that you bear that name. (1 Peter 4:16)

> But rejoice inasmuch as you participate in the sufferings of Christ, so that you may be overjoyed when his glory is revealed. (1 Peter 4:13)

> If you are insulted because of the name of Christ, you are blessed, for the Spirit of glory and of God rests on you. (1 Peter 4:14)

This seems like a strange attitude—to rejoice when suffering for Christ's name. But there are profound reasons for this joy.

### Three Grounds for Rejoicing

**a. Participation with Christ**

When we suffer as Christians, we participate in Christ's sufferings. The Greek word *koinonia* means fellowship or shared experience. We're joining Christ in his suffering, becoming one with him. This isn't merely theoretical—it's about genuine companionship.

Christ is no fair-weather friend. He befriended us when we were unpopular. He suffered for us while we were yet sinners. He stood by us even when we were the cause of his suffering. Can we now abandon him when he is the cause of ours?

Job's wife challenged his faith during his suffering, but Job responded with wisdom:

> His wife said to him, "Are you still holding on to your integrity? Curse God and die!" He replied, "You are talking like a foolish woman. Shall we accept good from God, and not trouble?" (Job 2:9-10)

**b. The Spirit of God Rests on Us**

We often associate God's presence with positive experiences—health, prosperity, blessing, and joyful encounters. But the Spirit of God is also present during our suffering as Christians. His presence in our pain is evidence that God is with us, not that he has abandoned us.

Many Christians believe in triumphalism—the idea that faith always produces glory and prosperity in this life. But Scripture reveals a different order: suffering comes first, then glory. This life is the season of trials; the next life is the season of complete vindication.

The song "Blessed Be Your Name" captures this beautifully:

> Blessed be Your name  
> When I'm found in the desert place  
> Though I walk through the wilderness  
> Blessed be Your name

This reflects Job's confession:

> At this, Job got up and tore his robe and shaved his head. Then he fell to the ground in worship and said: "Naked I came from my mother's womb, and naked I will depart. The LORD gave and the LORD has taken away; may the name of the LORD be praised." (Job 1:20-21)

Joy is not triumphalism in the present, but hope in the future. When we sing of God's blessings during hardship, we sing reflectively, acknowledging the sadness of loss while trusting God's faithfulness. We're experiencing the divine presence even in darkness.

**c. Judgment Has Already Begun**

> For it is time for judgment to begin with God's household. (1 Peter 4:17)

> And if it begins with us, what will the outcome be for those who do not obey the gospel of God? And, "If it is hard for the righteous to be saved, what will become of the ungodly and the sinner?" (1 Peter 4:17-18)

Judgment sounds ominous, but consider an analogy: the HSC (high school certificate exams) is a tough time—demanding and stressful. Yet for those who have studied and prepared, it's also a relief. It marks the end of thirteen years and the beginning of a new phase. The prepared student wants the exams to begin; further delays only increase anxiety.

Christians are the prepared ones. Not because we're smarter or cleverer, but because someone else has tutored us thoroughly. Jesus has prepared us so completely that we might say he's already taken the exams for us. Christ died for us, bearing the penalty of judgment. His resurrection proves there is life after judgment and the possibility of redemption.

Yes, judgment will be stressful even for the prepared. But we face it knowing we're not alone and that we have been justified in Christ.

### Don't Be Surprised—Suffering Comes to All Christians

Suffering comes to Christians worldwide. In some places, it's intense persecution. In our context, it may be subtler—Christian parents with non-Christian children, young people pressured to choose between church and social activities. Some youth in our church have been converted through high school and face constant friction at home.

The media and culture constantly bash Christian faith. When these messages are repeated often enough, they become internalized as truth, creating an inferiority complex. We start believing we're simpletons without evidence, that the world and culture offer more than Scripture.

Some compare our situation to Middle Eastern persecution and conclude it's mild, so we needn't worry. But this reasoning is flawed. It's like saying antisemitism is acceptable because it hasn't reached the scale of the Holocaust. Antisemitism leads to the Holocaust. Tolerance of small pressures leads to greater persecution.

We should see current pressures as preparation. If we cannot sustain the pressure now, how will we endure when real persecution comes?

Fear may tempt us to back off, to hide our faith. But Peter's response is clear: Don't retreat. Instead, recommit yourself to Christ and continue doing good.

## 2. Keep Doing Good

> So then, those who suffer according to God's will should commit themselves to their faithful Creator and continue to do good. (1 Peter 4:19)

This seems counterintuitive. When facing pressure and suffering, wouldn't it be wiser to keep our heads down? The answer lies in understanding spiritual growth. Like exposure therapy for phobias, we overcome fear by gentle, repeated exposure. Withdrawing from our faith commitment only strengthens the fear and weakens our resolve.

Peter elaborates on this commitment to do good:

> The end of all things is near. Therefore be alert and of sober mind so that you may pray. Above all, love each other deeply, because love covers over a multitude of sins. Offer hospitality to one another without grumbling. Each of you should use whatever gift you have received to serve others, as faithful stewards of God's grace in its various forms. If anyone speaks, they should do so as one who speaks the very words of God. If anyone serves, they should do so with the strength God provides, so that in all things God may be praised through Jesus Christ. To him be the glory and the power for ever and ever. Amen. (1 Peter 4:7-11)

### Good Works Often Conflict with Popular Opinion

Doing good isn't always what the world wants. The world may celebrate certain causes—refugees, poverty, environmental concerns, social justice. Yet history shows that the most important moral victories came from small groups acting against popular opinion.

The abolition of slavery is now celebrated, but in its time it was unpopular. It threatened a way of life, affected the wealthy and powerful, opposed celebrities and elites who shaped public opinion. Yet Christians led the charge for freedom.

Here's the reality: if doing good were popular, you wouldn't face persecution for it. Authentic Christian good works often challenge what culture celebrates. They're the things Hollywood won't promote, politicians won't touch, newspapers won't feature prominently.

There's profound significance in this. Your church has opportunities to do good in unpopular areas that the world ignores. Issues that matter deeply to God may not matter to culture. This is where your unique contribution becomes visible—like salt in a meal, adding flavor and preservation.

### Your Unique Purpose

You are truly unique. God has given you particular gifts, particular circumstances, particular opportunities. Perhaps you're excellent at something—piano, athletics, leadership. Perhaps you were born on February 29th, making you statistically rare. When you lean into what makes you different, there's genuine joy in it.

God has a unique plan for you to do good. Don't back away from it because it's unpopular. That uniqueness is your strength, your contribution to God's kingdom, and ultimately your joy.
