# Revelation 5:1-14

**Title:** The Lion, The Switch, and the Throne Room  
**Date:** 12 January 2002  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction: Vision and Purpose

What is our vision of what the earth should look like? In *The Lord of the Rings*, every character is driven by their vision—some seeking power for control, others for protection, and some simply keeping options open. Frodo envisions a return to normalcy before the ring's corruption. Their adventures revolve around fulfilling that vision.

This same question drives John's revelation. Through an open door into heaven, he glimpses God's vision for the world.

> After this I looked, and there before me was a door standing open in heaven. And the voice I had first heard speaking to me like a trumpet said, "Come up here, and I will show you what must take place after this." (Revelation 4:1)

## The Throne Room

John's vision opens with the throne room itself—a scene of majesty and order. In Chapter 4, we see a throne surrounded by twenty-four elders, four-faced beasts, and the appearance of God like precious gemstones. All honour, glory, and power centre on this throne.

But then John's vision focuses on something held in the right hand of the Enthroned One: a scroll.

> I saw in the right hand of him who sat on the throne a scroll with writing on both sides and sealed with seven seals. (Revelation 5:1)

This scroll is comprehensive—written on both sides, inside and out, nothing left out. It contains God's plans and blessings for the world. To open it is to implement God's vision.

Then comes the challenge:

> I saw a mighty angel proclaiming in a loud voice, "Who is worthy to break the seals and open the scroll?" (Revelation 5:2)

The question echoes across all creation: **Who is good enough to open the seals? Who can implement the vision of God?**

The answer is devastating:

> No one in heaven or on earth or under the earth could open the scroll or even look inside it. (Revelation 5:3)

Not anyone in all the universe is worthy enough to fulfil God's plans. John's response is anguish:

> I wept and wept because no one was found who was worthy to open the scroll or look inside. (Revelation 5:4)

The agony is real—the promise of good things unfulfilled, with no one capable of bringing them to pass.

## The Lion: Power and Hope

Then comes comfort from one of the victorious elders in white:

> "Do not weep! See the Lion of the tribe of Judah, the root of David has triumphed. He is able to open the scroll and its seven seals." (Revelation 5:5)

Hope arrives with the Lion. The Lion represents everything powerful—strength, authority, security, wisdom, and kingship. Even in lands where lions no longer roam, they appear on coats of arms as symbols of royal power.

Who is this Lion? Throughout Revelation, the author weaves Old Testament imagery into a new vision. The "Lion of the tribe of Judah" points to Jesus, a descendant of King David. Since David's reign, the Israelites had longed for a king as great as he was. Jesus is that king—the one who conquers foreign invaders, brings peace by crushing his enemies, and rules as the great, majestic Lion.

As God's chosen representative, the Lion is able to open the scroll and implement God's vision.

## Switch #1: The Lion Becomes a Lamb

John would have looked up with hope, eyes lighting up. But what he actually sees defies expectation:

> Then I saw a Lamb, looking as if it had been slain, standing in the centre of the throne, encircled by the four living creatures and the elders. (Revelation 5:6a)

The powerful Lion switches to a powerless Lamb. Not even a full-grown sheep, but a slain one.

**Why would the Lion choose to become a weak, powerless, slain sheep?**

Jesus could have amassed armies. He could have commanded legions of angels. At a stroke, the kingdoms of the world would be his. He could have come victoriously, but instead he chose to be slain. When you are dead, you cannot conquer or be victorious. 

Yet this is precisely God's vision: **the suffering Christ. The gospel.**

### The Gospel's Apparent Weakness

What is our vision of the gospel? Today, many portray it triumphally—solved problems, prosperity, aspirational success. But the gospel appears weak. It does not look like grand music or triumphalistic Christianity. It looks like:

- Small groups loving each other
- One-to-one relationships
- Welcoming the stranger
- Ordinary people serving quietly

We see gospel growth through worldly eyes rather than Jesus's eyes. We look at the pathetic Lamb and see no power. We would rather trust the world's standards of wealth, wisdom, and strength.

Consider how we judge power: A luxury car commands respect; a humble Corolla does not. We are impressed by brand names, prestigious schools, designer clothes, and status symbols. These "props" give us the impression that we are successful. We use them to feel secure and important.

Yet **the powerless Lamb cuts across these perceptions.** God's vision is a Lamb, and we must trust it.

This is hard for us. Most of us have not been tested. We live in comfort and security. Only when we are terribly sick, fail exams, lose our popularity, or face death do we discover whether we really trust God. When our earthly props give way, that is when we learn where our true trust lies.

Jesus is the Lion without the props—mane shaved, roar silenced, nails and teeth filed down, size shrunk, life taken. What remains looks very much like the slain Lamb. Jesus relying not on himself, but on God.

**Can we trust God? Yes.** And here is the proof that it is worth it.

## Switch #2: The Slain Lamb Becomes Powerful

In an instant, the slain Lamb switches to become powerful:

> He had seven horns and seven eyes, which are the seven spirits of God sent out into all the earth. (Revelation 5:6b)

This mortally wounded Lamb stands alive. He is in the place of greatest prominence—standing in the centre of the throne, encircled by the four living creatures and elders. Seven horns signify complete power. Seven eyes point to complete knowledge. He possesses the complete Spirit of God.

**The Lamb is at once crucified and powerful.** This is the absurdity that reveals the truth: weakness and power together.

It is this Lamb who takes the scroll:

> [The Lamb] came and took the scroll from the right hand of him who sat on the throne. (Revelation 5:7)

Though the Lion was said to be worthy to take the scroll, it is the Lamb—the Lion looking as if it had been slain—who actually takes it. The weak baby rescues the world.

All creation immediately responds in worship:

> The four living creatures and the twenty-four elders fell down before the Lamb. Each one had a harp and they were holding golden bowls full of incense, which are the prayers of the saints. (Revelation 5:8)

Then heaven itself erupts in song:

> Then I looked and heard the voice of many angels, numbering thousands upon thousands, and ten thousand times ten thousand. In a loud voice they sang: "Worthy is the Lamb, who was slain, to receive power and wealth and wisdom and strength and honour and glory and praise!" (Revelation 5:11-12)

Thousands upon thousands—imagine the sound in surround sound, a deafening chorus. And what are they singing? That the slain Lamb is worthy to receive sevenfold adoration: power, wealth, wisdom, strength, honour, glory, and praise.

Where there had been silence over whether anyone was worthy to open the scroll, now every creature in all existence sings:

> "To him who sits on the throne and to the Lamb be praise and honour and glory and power, for ever and ever!" (Revelation 5:13)

Previously, all glory belonged to God alone. Now that same glory is given also to the Lamb—because the Lamb is God and worthy of it.

**The Lamb is so powerful that heaven's theme song has changed.** Heaven now sings a new song:

> "You are worthy to take the scroll and to open its seals; because you were slain, and with your blood you purchased men for God from every tribe and language and people and nation. You have made them to be a kingdom and priests to serve our God and they will reign on the earth." (Revelation 5:9-10)

Not only is a "farm animal" now on God's favourite throne, but heaven's pre-programmed song has been completely changed. The Lamb has redefined what power looks like.

## Application: Is It Worth It?

**For us, is following the way of the Lamb worth it?** Is giving up my time, my money, my relationships for the sake of the kingdom worth it? Is being a loser in this world worth it? Is trusting God all the way worth it?

**Yes.** Jesus lived on the premise that it is far better to trust God, be a loser, die, and go to heaven. This is how the vision works—through trusting God. That fact must affect how we live in this world.

Why be a slain Lamb when you can be a Lion? Why give up two weeks of summer for a beach mission? Why skip Tuesday nights at Bible Fellowship to catch a cheap movie? Why give up your paycheck for gospel work? Don't you want to have fun?

But here is the promise: **There is real hope and confidence in our demotion.** Christians are not suffering for nothing. There is a real promise of real good things in the future, which sustains us in the present. If we see heaven like this—if we can see the Lamb glorified—it gives us reason to give up the things of this world.

**Power comes from being Lamb-like. Power comes from giving up power and trusting God.**

It is terrifying, but it is a step of faith. We are given a vision from heaven today about what it will be like. The exalted Lamb means that trusting and relying in God makes sense. **It is worth it.**

## Called to Serve

The Lamb has done this for all humanity:

> You were slain, and with your blood you purchased men for God from every tribe and language and people and nation. You have made them to be a kingdom and priests to serve our God and they will reign on the earth. (Revelation 5:9-10)

The Lamb was slain to purchase people enslaved to sin and Satan. God paid the ransom—the price of his own Son's blood—to buy back his people. We no longer belong to Satan.

Now we are the people of God. We have been given not the power to sin, but the power to serve. We are purchased not to do as we like, but to serve sacrificially as priests. Through Jesus, we see that **the way to Lion power is through the Lamb.** If you want to be Lion, be the Lamb first.

### Serving in Hidden Places

Why serve each other in unattractive, boring areas? Cleaning dishes, washing tea towels, wiping toilets, taking out garbage, talking to socially difficult people—this gets us nowhere. There is no popularity in it, no credit, no one sees it.

At church, there are countless ugly, behind-the-scenes, Lamb-like jobs: packing up, picking up rubbish, managing the bins, taking down signs.

Yet **the Lamb on the throne shows that the way of the Lamb is not only worth it—it is followable.** We have been purchased for that very reason. This is how we are to be a kingdom. This is how we are to reign over the earth—through sacrificial service.

### Church Vision

What is our vision for the church this year? The same as last year:

1. **To follow the Lamb**
2. **To serve each other**

In this way, we live out God's vision. In this way, we become truly powerful.
