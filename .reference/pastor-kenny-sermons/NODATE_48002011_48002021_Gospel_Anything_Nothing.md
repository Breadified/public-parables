# Galatians 2:11-21

**Title:** Gospel + Anything = Nothing

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Background

Throughout history, we've all made mistakes. But some mistakes carry greater consequences than others. Missing exams in high school was a significant error, but missing university exams would have been catastrophic. Yet even those pale in comparison to the spiritual mistakes we can make in our faith.

Over the past weeks, we've explored the early church's struggle with a fundamental question: What makes someone a Christian? Is faith in Jesus alone sufficient, or must Gentile believers also adopt Jewish practices like circumcision? This was no minor disagreement—it was one of the most pressing issues facing the young church.

As we study this passage, come with your Bibles open. If you don't have one, please find someone to share with. Better yet, bring your spare Bibles next week and keep them in your lockers. If we're serious about hearing God's Word, we must engage it directly from Scripture.

## Paul Identifies the Problem: The Place of the Law (11-13)

> When Peter came to Antioch, I opposed him to his face, because he was clearly in the wrong. (Verse 11)

What exactly was Peter's mistake? Verse 12 reveals it.

Peter had been eating with Gentile Christians. This might seem ordinary to us, but it was explosive in first-century Judaism. The Jewish food laws—the regulations distinguishing clean from unclean—were foundational to Jewish identity. Pigs and seafood were forbidden. This wasn't arbitrary; it marked the boundary of covenant membership.

Peter himself had struggled with this. He'd grown up under these regulations, but God had shown him in a vision (Acts 10) that the old distinctions were obsolete. The new age had arrived, and Gentiles were now included in God's kingdom without becoming Jewish first.

But when certain men from James arrived, Peter's resolve crumbled. He withdrew from eating with Gentiles and began following the Jewish food laws again. These "men from James" represented the circumcision party—those who insisted that Gentile Christians must be circumcised and follow Jewish practices to truly belong to God's people.

Peter's behavior sent a devastating message: you cannot be a true Christian without following the Jewish law. In doing so, he became a hypocrite and led others astray, including even Barnabas.

## Paul Stands for the Truth of the Gospel (14-21)

Paul confronted Peter publicly because his actions contradicted the very heart of the gospel—the truth that had already been established in Jerusalem (as Paul reminds us in verse 5: "We did not give in to them for a moment, so that the truth of the gospel might remain with you").

> Peter, though a Jew, was living like a Gentile, implying freedom from the law. Yet now he wanted to compel Gentiles to follow Jewish customs. (Verse 14)

The absurdity is striking. Peter had torn down the wall between Jew and Gentile, then tried to rebuild it. Why would he do this?

### The Four Truths of the Gospel

**First Truth: Christians are justified by faith in Christ alone**

> We who are Jews by birth and not sinful Gentiles know that a person is not justified by the works of the law, but by faith in Jesus Christ. (Verses 15-16)

To be "justified" means to be declared righteous by a judge. Notice Paul's stunning claim: even Jews—with all their privileges, their covenant heritage, their history of following God's law—cannot be justified by works of the law. If the most devoted followers of God's law cannot earn God's approval through law-keeping, how could Gentiles?

The only pathway to righteousness is faith in Jesus Christ.

**Second Truth: Christians are dead to the merit-earning power of the law**

> I do not set aside the grace of God, for if righteousness could be gained through the law, Christ died for nothing! (Verse 21)

When we died with Christ, we died to the law as a system for earning God's approval. This doesn't mean the law is sinful or that we're free to sin. Rather, we're no longer bound to keep the law as the means of gaining heaven or God's favor.

Some rules are indeed good to keep—Jesus and the apostles affirmed these. But they no longer function as the criteria for entering God's kingdom. We follow God's commands not to earn acceptance, but because we're already accepted.

**Third Truth: Christians now live for Christ through faith**

> I have been crucified with Christ and I no longer live, but Christ lives in me. The life I now live in the body, I live by faith in the Son of God, who loved me and gave himself for me. (Verse 19-20)

Our motivation for obedience transforms completely. Before Christ, we might follow rules to earn reward or avoid punishment. Now, we follow Christ because He loved us and gave Himself for us.

Think of it this way: if you join a chess team, your motivation for practicing changes. Before joining, you practiced to try to make the team. After joining, you practice because you're part of the team and want to improve it.

When we bring our Bibles to study, our motivation isn't to earn spiritual points with God. Our motivation is that we belong to Him and want to know His Word better.

**Fourth Truth: There is no alternative if Christ is not sufficient**

How does Christ's death prove that law-keeping alone cannot save us? Here's the logic: If righteousness could come through the law, then Christ's death was unnecessary. His sacrifice would be wasted. His work would be pointless.

But consider this: If the greatest mathematician in the world cannot solve a problem, what hope do the rest of us have? If the law—God's perfect standard—cannot justify us, and Christ Himself had to die to accomplish what the law could not, then nothing else can possibly save us.

## Application

**Are you right with God? On what basis?**

Don't mistake this. Your standing before God rests on Christ alone—not on your obedience, your prayers, your works, or your church attendance.

**How are we tempted to add to the gospel?**

We often fall into the trap of thinking God rewards us based on our performance. If I pray more, God will bless me more. If I'm good, God will give me what I want. We've all heard versions of this: "Pray the Prayer of Jabez and God will prosper you." No—God doesn't prosper you because you turned off the television and studied instead of watching The Simpsons. That's works-righteousness dressed in religious language.

The book of Malachi speaks of giving and receiving, and we've mistaken God for a cosmic Santa Claus, as if He's obligated to reward our behavior. We think baptism, good works, church attendance, or communion can earn us standing before God. These are all good things, but they cannot justify us.

**The Bottom Line**

Whatever mistakes we make in life—even missing our final exams—there is one mistake we must never make: we must never believe that anything can be added to the gospel. Christ + anything = nothing.

The gospel stands alone. Jesus Christ is sufficient. Stand firm in this truth. Proclaim it daily. Every Friday in this study, in every conversation about faith, cling to this: the gospel needs no additives. It is complete, sufficient, and eternally secure in Christ alone.

> Therefore do not let anyone judge you by what you eat or drink, or with regard to a religious festival, a New Moon celebration or a Sabbath day. These are a shadow of the things that were to come; the reality, however, is found in Christ. (Colossians 2:16-17)
