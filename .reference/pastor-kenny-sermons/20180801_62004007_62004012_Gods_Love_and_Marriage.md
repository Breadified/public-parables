# 1 John 4:7-12

**Title:** God's Love and Marriage  
**Date:** 25 August 2018  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Introduction: What Love Really Means

Today we celebrate a wedding—the culmination of months of planning, the coming together of two families, and most importantly, the commitment of Nicholas and Judy to one another. But as we gather to celebrate love, we must ask: what kind of love are we really celebrating?

Our culture offers us several definitions of love, each incomplete in its own way.

**The Evolutionary Definition:** According to evolutionary biology, love is fundamentally about physical attraction and reproduction. For men, a woman's body proportions signal fertility. For women, a man's strength suggests he can provide security and protection.

**The Chemical Definition:** Neuroscience tells us that love is a cascade of chemicals. Testosterone and estrogen get us "out looking." When we find "the one," our brains flood with dopamine (making us feel euphoric), adrenaline (our hearts race), and serotonin (temporarily making us irrational). Finally, oxytocin and vasopressin bond us to our beloved.

These definitions aren't wrong—they describe real phenomena. Perhaps this is what Nicholas and Judy experienced that first date, overlooking Sydney Harbour or working together.

But there is another definition: **the theological definition. God's definition of love.**

---

## 1. God Is Love

> **7 Dear friends, let us love one another, for love comes from God.**

God is the source of all love. Every act of genuine love traces back to Him.

> **8 Whoever does not love does not know God, because God is love.**

Notice this carefully: God doesn't merely *possess* love as an attribute He can remove at will. God *is* love. It's not a mask He wears; it's His very nature. Every fiber of His being is love. It's His desire, His motivation, His identity.

Think of it this way: if you took a chisel and carved off a piece of God, you wouldn't get marble—you'd get love. If you performed surgery and biopsied His deepest essence, you'd find love. If God were to sweat, He would sweat love. If you cut Him, He bleeds love.

This is the fundamental nature of God—pure, infinite, self-giving love.

---

## 2. Jesus Is Love Incarnate

> **9 This is how God showed his love among us: He sent his one and only Son into the world that we might live through him.**

Two thousand years ago, God entered human history. The infinite God became finite. Eternity entered time. And what we see in Jesus is what we see in God: love made visible.

> **10 This is love: not that we loved God, but that he loved us and sent his Son as an atoning sacrifice for our sins.**

Jesus didn't die because we deserved it. He died *despite* the fact that we didn't. He was crucified for crimes He did not commit—He sacrificed Himself for our sake. God bled. God was cut. All for us.

This is the radical difference between Jesus and the rest of humanity. While we are capable of love, it is not intrinsic to us. Beneath the surface, we find selfishness, hatred, and apathy. When pressure comes, we look out for number one.

But Jesus shows us something entirely different. In His atoning sacrifice, He demonstrates **other-centered love**—sacrificial, patient, kind. He shows us a God slow to anger and abounding in steadfast love. He shows us what true love actually looks like.

---

## 3. We Are Called to Love One Another

> **11 Dear friends, since God so loved us, we also ought to love one another.**

When we encounter God's love in Jesus, we are transformed. That love becomes infectious—it flows through us to others, just as Judy's love of cats spread to Nicholas, even to loving Judy's grumpy cat Venus.

God's love transforms us so that we cannot help but love others. And for Nicholas and Judy, this is where their marriage begins—not with chemistry or attraction alone, but with God's love flowing through them toward one another.

### The Promise: "I Will"

When Nicholas and Judy made their vows, they didn't say "I do"—they said something far more profound: **"I will."**

"I do" means "I love you today." And who wouldn't—look at this couple! But they promised "I *will*"—not just for today, but for tomorrow and all the tomorrows after that.

This is unconditional love. It's the commitment to love when the initial attraction fades, when those chemicals stop flooding your brain, when the body changes with age. It's the decision to love even when your spouse fails you—when he's a jerk, when she's nagging, when he's aloof, when she's messy.

This is the love first shown to us by God in Jesus—a love that doesn't depend on feelings but on commitment. A love that sacrifices. A love that perseveres.

### The Call

Dear friends, since God so loved us, we also ought to love one another. May Nicholas and Judy reflect Jesus' sacrificial, other-centered love to each other. May their marriage be sustained not by chemistry or attraction alone, but by the theological definition of love—God's love working through them.

> **12 No one has ever seen God; but if we love one another, God lives in us and his love is made complete in us.**

When we love as God loves, we become the visible evidence of God's love to a watching world. Through their faithful, sacrificial love for one another, Nicholas and Judy will testify to God's reality and character.
