**Title:** Alien Identity

## Christ's Death and Our New Status

### Our Identity: Brought to God (3:18)

> For Christ died for sins once for all, the righteous for the unrighteous, to bring you to God.

The foundation of the Christian identity is this: we have been brought to God. This is who we are now.

Christ's death accomplished what no one else could. He died for our sins—bearing the penalty we deserved—once and for all. The righteous died for the unrighteous. This substitutionary truth finds its roots in the prophecies of Isaiah, spoken centuries before Christ's birth:

> But he was pierced for our transgressions,
> he was crushed for our iniquities;
> the punishment that brought us peace was upon him,
> and by his wounds we are healed.
> We all, like sheep, have gone astray,
> each of us has turned to his own way;
> and the LORD has laid on him
> the iniquity of us all.
> — *Isaiah 53:5-6*

Through Christ's substitution, we are now seated in the heavenly realms. Our identity has fundamentally changed: we are no longer separated from God, but belong to Him.

### Christ's Work Extends to the Past (3:18b-20a)

Peter introduces a difficult passage that speaks to God's faithfulness across time:

> He was put to death in the body but made alive by the Spirit,
> through whom also he went and preached to the spirits in prison
> who disobeyed long ago when God waited patiently in the days of Noah while the ark was being built.

This is one of Scripture's most mysterious verses. What does it mean that Christ preached to "spirits in prison"?

Several interpretations have been proposed—some involving fallen angels, others suggesting purgatory. But as Protestant Christians, we reject purgatory. It contradicts the sufficiency of Christ's death ("once for all") and the promise that we have been brought to God. Purgatory suggests we still owe a debt, that Christ's sacrifice was incomplete.

The most compelling understanding comes from the Old Testament context: those who died in faith before Christ—the exiles and faithful of Israel—did not miss out. Christ, by His Spirit, proclaimed to them that their promised salvation had finally been fulfilled. Isaiah 61 describes this very proclamation:

> The Spirit of the Sovereign LORD is on me,
> because the LORD has anointed me
> to preach good news to the poor.
> He has sent me to bind up the brokenhearted,
> to proclaim freedom for the captives
> and release from darkness for the prisoners.
> — *Isaiah 61:1*

John the Baptist later directed his followers to Jesus, pointing to these very signs (Matthew 11:3-6). The time had come. The Old Testament believers were not left behind.

### The Pattern from Noah (3:20b-21)

Peter invokes Noah as the pattern of God's faithfulness. Even when God judged the world, He saved Noah's family and preserved His promises:

> In the days of Noah while the ark was being built. In it only a few people, eight in all, were saved through water,
> and this water symbolizes baptism that now saves you also—not the removal of dirt from the body but the pledge of a good conscience toward God.
> It saves you by the resurrection of Jesus Christ,
> who has gone into heaven and is at God's right hand.

The water that brought death to the world brought salvation to Noah's household. Isaiah reinforced this to the exiles—those who felt abandoned and judged:

> "For a brief moment I abandoned you,
> but with deep compassion I will bring you back.
> In a surge of anger I hid my face from you for a moment,
> but with everlasting kindness I will have compassion on you," says the LORD your Redeemer.
> "To me this is like the days of Noah,
> when I swore that the waters of Noah would never again cover the earth.
> So now I have sworn not to be angry with you,
> never to rebuke you again."
> — *Isaiah 54:7-9*

Baptism in Peter's teaching represents our identification with Christ's death and resurrection. It is not the water itself that saves, but our pledge to turn from sin and commit to Christ. We die with Him; we rise with Him.

## Living as Aliens in the World

### 1. Renounce the Desires of the Flesh (4:1-5)

> Therefore, since Christ suffered in his body, arm yourselves also with the same attitude, because he who has suffered in his body is done with sin.

If we identify with Christ, we must follow His pattern. His suffering in the body led to being done with sin. Our baptism—our death and resurrection with Christ—marks a symbolic farewell to the flesh and its desires.

> As a result, he does not live the rest of his earthly life for evil human desires, but rather for the will of God.
> For you have spent enough time in the past doing what pagans choose to do—living in debauchery, lust, drunkenness, orgies, carousing and detestable idolatry.
> They think it strange that you do not plunge with them into the same flood of dissipation, and they heap abuse on you.

The world around us lives in what Peter calls a "flood of dissipation"—pouring themselves into pleasure without regard for consequence. We are called to something different.

Consider alcohol. Many justify it, yet the statistics tell a sobering story: alcohol is the second leading preventable cause of death, linked to violence, brain damage, and cancer. Yet this is not Peter's primary concern about "suffering in the body." Rather, he means suffering the withdrawal from worldly desires—the discipline of saying no to what once seemed normal.

If we are aliens, we will hunger for different sustenance. Be discerning about what you consume, what you watch, and how you spend your time.

### 2. Live for Prayer, Love, and Service (4:7-11)

> The end of all things is near. Therefore be clear minded and self-controlled so that you can pray.

With Christ's return drawing near, clarity of mind matters. How do we maintain it? Through prayer. When tempted toward evil, prayer reminds us of our new identity and reconnects us to God.

> Above all, love each other deeply, because love covers over a multitude of sins.
> Offer hospitality to one another without grumbling.

Love and hospitality are not sentimental extras—they are spiritual disciplines. When you serve food to others, when you open your home, you practice the skills of the Kingdom.

> Each one should use whatever gift he has received to serve others, faithfully administering God's grace in its various forms.
> If anyone speaks, he should do it as one speaking the very words of God. If anyone serves, he should do it with the strength God provides, so that in all things God may be praised through Jesus Christ.

Whether you speak or serve, do so as one ministering God's grace. Ask for feedback. Be self-critical. Improve. Volunteer generously—not as a favor to others, but as a steward of God's gifts.

### 3. Rejoice in Your Alien Status (4:12-19)

> Dear friends, do not be surprised at the painful trials you are suffering, as though something strange were happening to you.
> But rejoice that you participate in the sufferings of Christ, so that you may be overjoyed when his glory is revealed.
> If you are insulted because of the name of Christ, you are blessed, for the Spirit of glory and of God rests on you.
> If you suffer, it should not be as a murderer or thief or any other kind of criminal, or even as a meddler. However, if you suffer as a Christian, do not be ashamed, but praise God that you bear that name.

Suffering as a Christian is not a sign something has gone wrong—it is a sign of belonging. You bear the name of Christ. The same Spirit that rested on Him rests on you.

> For it is time for judgment to begin with the family of God; and if it begins with us, what will the outcome be for those who do not obey the gospel of God?
> "If it is hard for the righteous to be saved, what will become of the ungodly and the sinner?"
> So then, those who suffer according to God's will should commit themselves to their faithful Creator and continue to do good.

This is our assurance: God has not abandoned you in difficulty. He is faithful. You have been brought to God through Christ. Rejoice in this identity—even when the world thinks you strange for living differently.

## The Assurance of Christ's Sufficiency

Christ's death is the game-changing event—one that secures both our future and transforms our present. His resurrection means we have no need to paralyze ourselves with doubt about what comes next. Yes, life holds uncertainties. We make commitments—to homes, to relationships, to callings—not because we can predict the future, but because we trust in God's faithfulness.

This is our strength: **we have been brought to God**. This status shapes everything else.
