# Romans 1:18–2:16

**Title:** The Wrath of God

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Who Is This Passage About?

Paul addresses three possible groups in Romans 1:18–32: the ignorant, Adam, and Israel. Understanding who is condemned helps us grasp the universal nature of human guilt before God.

### 1. The Ignorant (1:19–23)

One interpretation suggests that everyone will hear the gospel through creation itself—that natural revelation provides sufficient knowledge of God.

> For what can be known about God is plain to them, because God has shown it to them. For his invisible attributes, namely, his eternal power and divine nature, have been clearly perceived, ever since the creation of the world, in the things that have been made. So they are without excuse. (Romans 1:19–20)

**The argument seems reasonable:** People have access to God's knowledge through creation and therefore have no excuse.

**Three key points emerge:**

- **They possess knowledge of God.** Creation reveals God's eternal power and divine nature plainly to all.

- **But they suppress this knowledge.** Although they knew God, they did not honor Him or give thanks. Instead, their thinking became futile and their hearts were darkened.

- **They turn to idolatry.** Claiming to be wise, they became fools and exchanged the glory of the immortal God for images of mortal things.

**Natural revelation** refers to the discovery of God's attributes through observing creation—in contrast to Scripture's written revelation. Since creation bears God's fingerprints, we should be able to observe what God is like through natural theology (knowledge of God based on nature).

At first glance, this seems logical. A beautifully designed building reveals something about its designer—their skill, care, and character. Similarly, the universe's design should reveal God's nature. This logic forms the basis of arguments like the cosmological argument:

- We know the universe had a beginning
- We know cause precedes effect
- Therefore, there must be a first cause—God
- This first cause is independent of space (limitless), time (eternal), and matter (immaterial)

These are God's "invisible attributes" made visible through creation.

**However, natural revelation has serious limitations.** Consider the problems: How do you know the designer is good, or that there's only one designer rather than many? How do you distinguish between actual design and random generation? Without direct revelation, we're merely guessing. Logic is only as reliable as the information provided, and without God's direct Word, we have no reliable information about Him.

This was a key problem the Reformation addressed against medieval theology. Instead of trying to logically infer God's purposes, why not simply read Scripture and discover what God has actually revealed?

**Kenny's key insight:** Christianity is fundamentally a religion of truth—God's truth revealed directly to us through Scripture. The truth about God cannot be worked out from scratch; it can only come from God Himself.

This contrasts sharply with Eastern worldviews (Buddhism, Hinduism) where truth is subjective. There is no objective standard; each person meditates and creates their own vision of God. When there's no truth, there's no right or wrong—just personal preference. If that's the definition of religion, Kenny says, we want nothing to do with it.

**The critical question remains:** What about people in remote areas who have never heard the gospel? The answer lies in understanding that under God's law, ignorance is no excuse—just as it wouldn't be with a police officer.

### 2. Adam (1:19–23)

A second interpretation suggests this passage describes Adam's fall in Genesis 1–3, and the parallels are striking.

**The clues are compelling:**

- Romans 1:23 echoes Genesis 1:26: "Let us make man in our image, after our likeness"
- The reference to animals parallels Adam naming the creatures in Genesis 2
- Adam's literal presence in creation mirrors the direct knowledge described in Romans 1:19–20

**Adam's situation parallels Romans 1 perfectly:**

- Adam was in God's presence—God literally appeared to him and walked in the garden, so he knew God directly
- Adam was present at creation's beginning and witnessed God's work firsthand
- Yet although he knew God, he failed to honor Him as God
- His thinking became futile, his heart darkened (Romans 1:21)
- His fall resulted from pride—desiring to be as God, seeking knowledge of good and evil

**The results of Adam's failure:**

- He suppressed God's truth and instead obeyed the serpent, giving his allegiance to a creature rather than the Creator
- A reversal occurred: Man was made in God's image, but now humanity makes God in their image
- Humanity was to govern creation, but now creation rules—the snake commands, and Adam obeys

### 3. Israel

The final possibility is Israel—another group to whom God revealed Himself in physical, tangible ways.

Israel witnessed God's direct revelation on Mount Sinai. God appeared and spoke to Moses and the people. The parallels to Romans 1:23 are evident:

> They made a calf in Horeb and worshiped a metal image. They exchanged the glory of God for the image of an ox that eats grass. They forgot God, their Savior, who had done great things in Egypt. (Psalm 106:19–21)

Deuteronomy 4:16–18 also parallels Romans 1:23, warning Israel against making carved images:

> Beware lest you act corruptly by making a carved image for yourselves, in the form of any figure, the likeness of male or female, the likeness of any animal that is on the earth, the likeness of any winged bird that flies in the air, the likeness of anything that creeps on the ground, the likeness of any fish that is in the water under the earth. (Deuteronomy 4:16–18)

**The pattern is identical:** God warned Israel at Mount Sinai not to make idols. Yet when Moses returned with the tablets, they had constructed and were worshiping a golden calf. Israel received all the knowledge of God but chose to ignore, exchange, and condemn it. Therefore, Israel has no excuse.

### 4. The Answer: Adam and Israel

Kenny concludes the passage refers to **both Adam and Israel**—indeed, to all of humanity.

**The logic is elegant:**

- If you are Jewish, the condemnation applies to you as an Israelite: Israel was shown God's truth but rejected it. No excuse.
- If you are not Jewish, you are condemned because you are in Adam: Adam was shown God's truth but rejected it. No excuse.

**But what if you claim:** "I'm not Adam. I was never presented with this choice. I've never even read the Bible."

Kenny's response cuts to the heart of human solidarity: **We all inherit the wisdom and sins of our ancestors.** No one starts with a clean slate. This sounds unfair, but consider the evidence.

When Australian Prime Minister Kevin Rudd apologized to Aboriginal Australians for the "stolen generation" (children forcibly removed from their families), he made an apology on behalf of the entire nation—including those who never participated in that evil. Yet as citizens of Australia, we inherit both the good and the bad of our national heritage.

We benefit from things we never earned: a stable climate, a strong passport, accessible education, rule of law, democracy, minimum wages, first-world living standards. These are gifts from our ancestors' labor and decisions. We wouldn't dream of giving them up.

**But consistency demands:** If we accept the good legacies, we must accept the bad ones too. Just as we inherit Australia's benefits, so we inherit Adam's sin. We are in Adam, so we inherit both his humanity and his guilt. The conclusion is inescapable: **We all have no excuse.**

**But there is glorious good news—which Paul develops in chapter 5:**

> If we are in Adam and so sin together, the upside is that we can be in Christ and so rise with Him together also—based on nothing we have done. The righteousness of God has been revealed in Jesus. But in Jesus, the wrath of God is also revealed. On the cross, the punishment of God fell upon Jesus on behalf of those who follow Him. But for those who don't believe in Jesus, that same judgment is promised to you.

## The Pattern of God's Judgment (1:18, 23–32)

### The Wrath of God (1:18)

> For the wrath of God is revealed from heaven against all ungodliness and unrighteousness of men, who by their unrighteousness suppress the truth. (Romans 1:18)

God's wrath is not arbitrary cruelty but a just response to humanity's suppression of truth. Notice the pattern: if you give up the knowledge of God, God gives you up—a principle illustrated in Psalm 106:40–41.

### The Consequences Fit the Crime

Paul presents an escalation of sin with consequences that match each transgression:

#### Exchanging Glory (1:23–24)

> They exchanged the glory of the immortal God for images resembling mortal man and birds and animals and creeping things. Therefore God gave them up in the lusts of their hearts to impurity, to the dishonouring of their bodies among themselves. (Romans 1:23–24)

The consequence mirrors the crime: they suppressed God's glory, so God gave them over to the dishonoring of their own bodies.

#### Exchanging Truth (1:25–27)

> They exchanged the truth about God for a lie and worshiped and served the creature rather than the Creator. For this reason God gave them up to dishonorable passions. For their women exchanged natural relations for those that are contrary to nature; and the men likewise gave up natural relations with women and were consumed with passion for one another. (Romans 1:25–27)

The consequence is parallel: they exchanged God's truth for a lie, so they exchanged natural relations for unnatural ones.

#### Failing to Acknowledge God (1:28)

> Since they did not see fit to acknowledge God, He gave them up to a debased mind to do what ought not to be done. (Romans 1:28)

Their minds refused to acknowledge God, so their minds became morally degraded and incapable of discerning right from wrong.

## Idolatry: The Root of All Sin

Idolatry is where the descent into sin begins (1:23, 25, 28). When we suppress the truth about God, we create false gods from our imagination.

**We craft idols from whatever we see around us:**

- **From nature:** We observe a cow—such a useful animal—and think, "Perhaps God looks like a cow." Thus we worship creation rather than the Creator.

- **From admiration:** We see a strong or beautiful person and think, "If there's a god, he must look like that." So we worship celebrities and public figures, buying magazines about them, imitating their clothes and lifestyle.

- **From self:** We declare, "My needs are most important. I come first. I can rationalize anything." Thus we worship ourselves—our desires, our comfort, our image.

This is why natural revelation alone cannot guide us. Once we suppress God's truth, our imaginations run wild, and we inevitably create gods in our own image—whether animal, human, or self.

## Sexual Immorality: The Consequence of Idolatry

Sexual immorality follows idolatry (1:24–28). They are inseparable.

> Therefore God gave them up in the lusts of their hearts to impurity. For this reason God gave them up to dishonorable passions. For their women exchanged natural relations for those that are contrary to nature; and the men likewise gave up natural relations with women and were consumed with passion for one another, men committing shameless acts with men and receiving in themselves the due penalty for their error. (Romans 1:24, 26–27)

**On the cultural argument:** The world claims the Bible is culturally outdated and points to "nature" as justification for redefining sexuality. But Paul's argument is also from nature—he appeals to the natural, biological complementarity of male and female.

**The fundamental problem:** Natural revelation alone cannot establish moral truth. Nature is too easily manipulated by our desires. We need something outside this world to tell us what is right and wrong. That tie-breaker is God's revelation. And the biological nature of male and female won't change anytime soon.

Once idolatry takes root and we suppress truth, **no morality remains.** Right becomes whatever you think is right; wrong is whatever contradicts your desires.

## From Idolatry to Total Depravity

The descent continues in 1:29–32:

> They were filled with all manner of unrighteousness, evil, covetousness, malice. They are full of envy, murder, strife, deceit, maliciousness. They are gossips, slanderers, haters of God, insolent, haughty, boastful, inventors of evil, disobedient to parents, foolish, faithless, heartless, ruthless. Though they know God's righteous decree that those who practice such things deserve to die, they not only do them but give approval to those who practice them. (Romans 1:29–32)

**Notice the final step:** Not only do people practice these sins, but they give approval to others who practice them. This is where society becomes complicit in wickedness.

We see this happening around us: approval for polygamy, pederasty, pedophilia. We witness the normalization of late-term abortion even on healthy babies. **It's a wicked thing occurring in our world.**

And yet, Kenny observes with pastoral honesty: We are all complicit. We vote for governments out of self-interest, and they legislate wickedness. We buy cheap clothes knowing someone is being exploited. We cannot claim innocence.

**The reality is stark:** The wrath of God is being revealed in our culture through idolatry, sexual immorality, and every kind of wickedness.

## The Hope: Revelation Through Christ

### Why Natural Revelation Fails

The critical difference between the condemned and the forgiven is not that the righteous avoid all sin—they don't. **The difference is that we are forgiven, and we do not suppress the truth.**

We have specific revelation—God's Word made known through Jesus Christ and Scripture. This is the only reliable knowledge of God available to humanity.

### The Mandate to Proclaim

> What is the difference between the wicked and us? The difference is that we are forgiven. The difference is that we are not people who suppress the truth. Don't suppress it. Tell your friends—don't suppress the truth. Don't be ashamed. There is no other revelation for your friends. They will not work out Jesus by themselves—no amount of looking into the stars or studying will make them suddenly realize. They need you.

**There is no excuse not to evangelize.** Your friends cannot discover Jesus on their own. They need to hear the gospel from you.

### The Inheritance Principle Applied to Gospel Responsibility

Just as we inherit the good and bad of our ancestors' choices, so we inherit their knowledge and faith. Kenny illustrates this with his own journey: He didn't choose to live in Australia, benefit from its education system, or inherit the fruits of Confucian ethics. Yet he enjoys all these gifts.

Similarly, we are the beneficiaries of two thousand years of Christian witness. The gospel reached us because others before us didn't suppress the truth. **Now the responsibility falls to us:** to pass on this truth to those who haven't heard, to tell them Jesus is the only way.

---

## The Gospel: Power and Righteousness

The passage begins with wrath but climaxes with hope. God's righteousness has been revealed in Jesus Christ—the power of salvation for all who believe. There is no other way.

For the wicked who suppress truth, judgment comes. But for those who receive Christ, that judgment was already paid at the cross. Jesus bore God's wrath on our behalf so that we might be forgiven and restored.

**This is the gospel:** We are all in Adam, all under condemnation, but we can be in Christ—rising with Him, receiving His righteousness, inheriting His life.

The choice remains: Will we suppress the truth, or will we proclaim it?
