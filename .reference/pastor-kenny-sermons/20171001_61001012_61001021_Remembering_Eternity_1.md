# 2 Peter 1:12-21

**Title:** Remembering Eternity
**Date:** October 2017
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Introduction: The Power of Memory

Memory shapes our entire existence. Without it, we would have no accountability, no learning, no gratitude. We'd be trapped repeating each day endlessly, vulnerable to manipulation, unable to grow from our mistakes. A world without memory would be a world without history itself.

Consider the tragedy of someone losing their memory to dementia—it's a kind of living death, a severing of identity from lived experience. Memory is how we know who we are and where we've come from.

Peter understood this profound importance. At the end of his life, with his death imminent, his deepest concern is that his readers remember. This is the whole purpose of his letter.

---

## 1. The Importance of Memory (v. 12-15)

**Peter's earnest plea:**

> So I will always remind you of these things, even though you know them and are firmly established in the truth you now have. I think it is right to refresh your memory as long as I live in the tent of this body, because I know that I will soon put it aside, as our Lord Jesus Christ has made clear to me. And I will make every effort to see that after my departure you will always be able to remember these things.

Peter is an aging apostle facing imminent death. His final wish isn't for comfort or recognition—it's to ensure that future generations remember. The phrase "tent of this body" acknowledges the temporary nature of physical life. He has been given clarity about his coming departure, and he's determined to leave a legacy of remembrance.

This concern for memory threads throughout the entire letter:
- 3:1 - "This is now my second letter to you. I have written both of them as reminders to stimulate you to wholesome thinking."
- 3:2 - "I want you to recall the words spoken in the past by the holy prophets and the command given by our Lord and Savior through your apostles."
- 3:5, 8 - Repeated warnings: "do not forget"

**Our forgetfulness is profound.** We forget God's faithfulness within weeks. The Israelites witnessed the most extraordinary miracles—Egypt's devastation, the parting of the sea, God's provision in the wilderness—yet within months they were complaining:

> The rabble with them began to crave other food, and again the Israelites started wailing and said, "If only we had meat to eat! We remember the fish we ate in Egypt at no cost—also the cucumbers, melons, leeks, onions and garlic. But now we have lost our appetite; we never see anything but this manna!" (Numbers 11:4-5)

They wanted to return to slavery because they'd forgotten their deliverance. The Book of Numbers is essentially a record of their repeated grumbling and faithlessness despite witnessing God's power firsthand.

**The Hebrew writer applies this lesson to us:**

> See to it, brothers and sisters, that none of you has a sinful, unbelieving heart that turns away from the living God. But encourage one another daily, as long as it is called Today, so that none of you may be hardened by sin's deceitfulness. (Hebrews 12:12-13)

Like the Israelites, we are prone to spiritual amnesia. We take God's blessings for granted. We need constant reminding—not because the message changes, but because our hearts drift.

**The purpose of Christian community:**

When we gather as a church, we're fulfilling a sacred function: reminding each other of God's truth. Some might think regular Bible teaching is boring repetition—the same themes, week after week. But consider a family dinner: the same vegetables, meat, and rice each night. Mundane? Yes. But it nourishes. The daily repetition of God's Word functions the same way. Through consistent teaching and fellowship, bit by bit, week by week, we grow spiritually.

Remembering, by definition, means returning to the same truths. This repetition isn't a flaw—it's the mechanism of spiritual formation.

## 2. The Content of Memory (v. 1-2, 16-21)

But what exactly should we remember? Peter identifies two sources of reliable memory: **the apostles** and **the prophets**.

### The Apostles' Memory (v. 16-18)

> For we did not follow cleverly devised stories when we told you about the coming of our Lord Jesus Christ in power, but we were eyewitnesses of his majesty. He received honor and glory from God the Father when the voice came to him from the Majestic Glory, saying, "This is my Son, whom I love; with him I am well pleased." We ourselves heard this voice that came from heaven when we were with him on the sacred mountain.

Peter grounds his authority in eyewitness testimony. He was there. He saw Jesus transfigured in glory. He heard the Father's voice from heaven affirming Jesus as His beloved Son. This wasn't mythology or philosophical speculation—it was a witnessed historical event.

**Why this matters:** The Christian faith is rooted in memory, not in abstract ideals. It's historical, not mythological. The Transfiguration actually happened—Moses and Elijah appeared, giving the prophets' endorsement to Jesus. The message was clear: Jesus is the promised King (from Psalm 2) and the suffering Servant (from Isaiah 42). These weren't random symbols; they were specific theological affirmations.

**You likely wouldn't invent this narrative.** The disciples faced martyrdom for maintaining this account. False teachers might embellish to attract followers, but men willing to die for their testimony have little incentive to lie. The details are specific enough to be credible, yet coherent enough to be true. This is the hallmark of genuine eyewitness testimony.

Peter is essentially saying: **You can trust this because it's grounded in witnessed history, not human imagination.**

### The Prophets' Memory (v. 19-21)

> We also have the prophetic message as something completely reliable, and you will do well to pay attention to it, as to a light shining in a dark place, until the day dawns and the morning star rises in your hearts. Above all, you must understand that no prophecy of Scripture came about by the prophet's own interpretation of things. For prophecy never had its origin in the human will, but prophets, though human, spoke from God as they were carried along by the Holy Spirit.

If you're skeptical of Peter or Paul, Peter invites you to examine the Old Testament prophets. Their words point to the same truth—that God acts in history, that the Messiah will come, that there is judgment and redemption. The prophets weren't giving personal interpretations; they were vessels of the Holy Spirit, speaking God's Word.

The "morning star" is Jesus (Revelation 22:16). Until He returns and the new day dawns, the prophetic Word stands as our "light shining in a dark place." It illuminates our path through present darkness.

**The point is unmistakable: What we possess is not made up.** It's the collective memory of faithful witnesses—apostles and prophets—who were moved by the Holy Spirit to speak truth about God's redemptive work in history.

---

## 3. The Advantage of Written Memory

The apostles and prophets have left us written testimony. This has profound implications:

### Equal Standing with the First Generation

One advantage of written memory is that you have equal access to the source. Some people say, "If only I could have lived in the first century and seen Jesus personally." But through Scripture, you do something nearly as valuable—you encounter the apostles' direct testimony without the distortion of oral transmission.

This isn't like a game of telephone where the message degrades with each retelling. When you open your Bible and read Peter's words, you're reading Peter. Not Peter's interpretation filtered through a teacher, and not that teacher's interpretation filtered through another. You have direct access to apostolic memory. Your standing as a believer is equal to any Christian throughout history. There are no second-class Christians in terms of revelation. We all receive the Word equally.

### The Word Speaks to Us Now

Peter says: "After my departure, you will always be able to remember these things." He's providing for the present. **The Bible is not an ancient relic; it's a living Word that speaks to us now.**

This isn't magic or mysticism. It operates through speech-act theory—the reality that words do things to us. They inform, transform, and shape our reality. The Word of God, being God's Word, carries power into the present moment. It's not outmoded or irrelevant. It continues to change those who genuinely engage with it.

---

## 4. The Cost of Remembering

Yet there's a price to remembrance that modern culture increasingly refuses to pay.

**First, there's the cost of effort.** Remembering requires discipline. You must invest time learning Scripture, attending church, engaging in study and discussion. It's easier to follow cultural trends than to preserve what Christ has accomplished for us. But that preservation—for yourself and for the next generation—requires intentional work.

If Christianity is treated like just another subject to study casually, it will be lost. The gospel is not rediscoverable like scientific knowledge. Once it's gone from a culture's memory, it's gone. We must actively transmit it, proclaim it, teach it.

**Second, there's the cost of boredom.** Many complain that church is repetitive. The same themes, the same passages, the same messages. We live in a culture addicted to novelty. But sometimes boring repetition is exactly what we need. Learning your multiplication tables is tedious, but necessary. Recalling Scripture may feel rote, but it's the mechanism by which we hold fast to what Christ has done for us.

**Third, remembrance carries real cost in a forgetful culture.** Consider Anzac Day—nations invest billions annually to remember fallen soldiers. Why? Because forgetting would be catastrophic. Similarly, if we don't remember the resurrection of Christ, the reality of sin and judgment, the virgin birth, and Christ's return, we lose our moorings. Without remembrance, we fall away spiritually. As Peter warns later: "He is nearsighted and blind and has forgotten that he has been cleansed from his past sins" (2 Peter 2:9).

**A sacred responsibility awaits us:** to pass this memory forward. The written Bible is only effective if someone is there to explain it, to testify to it, to live it. Even if every physical copy disappeared, the collective memory of believers could recreate Scripture. But the reverse isn't true—if no one remembers to teach and proclaim, the Bible's presence becomes irrelevant.

---

## Conclusion: Will You Remember Eternity?

Peter's greatest concern is that we remember not just historical facts, but **eternity itself.** Many Christians live as if this world is all that matters. We chase cultural approval. We become ashamed of unpopular truths—the resurrection, the judgment to come, biblical sexuality, Christ's exclusivity.

But here's the sobering question: **One day, when you are old, will you remember Christ?** Or will you have spent your life forgetting the one great Reality that gives everything meaning?

Our responsibility is clear. We must remember. We must remind each other. We must pass the memory forward. Not because repetition is thrilling, but because the alternative—spiritual amnesia—is catastrophic.

The Word stands forever. Christ calls us to remember.
