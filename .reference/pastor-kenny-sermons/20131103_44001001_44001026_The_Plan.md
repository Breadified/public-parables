# Acts 1:1-26

**Title:** The Plan
**Date:** November 3, 2013
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Weight of Waiting

Many of us carry unfulfilled goals—dreams we expected to reach long ago that remain stuck in bureaucracy, procrastination, or circumstances beyond our control. We watch the clock, waiting for approval, waiting for answers, waiting for the next chapter to begin. The disciples in Acts knew this experience intimately. After centuries of promises about God's kingdom, they found themselves in the peculiar tension between hope and uncertainty—waiting for a kingdom that had arrived but was also somehow delayed.

## The Kingdom of God Is Here

### The Promise Fulfilled

The kingdom of God stands as perhaps the central theme of Scripture. To reverse the devastating effects of Adam's sin, God made a covenant with Abraham that would reshape human history:

> I will make you into a great nation, and I will bless you; I will make your name great, and you will be a blessing. All peoples on earth will be blessed through you. (Genesis 12:2-3)

A true kingdom requires three things: people, land, and prosperity. Abraham's descendants became a nation—Jacob, renamed Israel, whose twelve sons founded the twelve tribes. After four hundred years of slavery in Egypt, they numbered over a million. They were freed and given the promised land.

### The Kingdom's Zenith and Decline

Three hundred years later, the kingdom reached its pinnacle under David and Solomon. The queen of Sheba herself testified to Solomon's glory:

> The report I heard in my own country about your achievements and your wisdom is true. But I did not believe these things until I came and saw with my own eyes. Indeed, not even half was told me; in wisdom and wealth you have far exceeded the report I heard. (1 Kings 10:6-7)

Yet success bred arrogance. The people abandoned God for idols—Molech, the deity of child sacrifice; Ashtoreth, god of sex; and various gods of weather and fertility. God, to purify the land, allowed them to be conquered and exiled to Assyria and Babylon.

But even in exile, God did not forget His covenant. Through the prophets, He promised redemption for the repentant. A new kingdom would emerge, ruled by a coming king:

> In the days of those kings the God of heaven will set up a kingdom that shall never be destroyed, nor shall the kingdom be left to another people. (Daniel 2:44)

This king would be called:

> Wonderful Counselor, Mighty God, Everlasting Father, Prince of Peace. Of the greatness of his government and peace there will be no end. He will reign on David's throne and over his kingdom, establishing and upholding it with justice and righteousness from that time on and forever. (Isaiah 9:6-7)

But the prophets revealed something unexpected—this king would suffer:

> He was assigned a grave with the wicked, and with the rich in his death, though he had done no violence, nor was any deceit in his mouth. Yet it was the Lord's will to crush him and cause him to suffer, and though the Lord makes his life an offering for sin, he will see his offspring and prolong his days, and the will of the Lord will prosper in his hand. (Isaiah 53:9-10)

### The Kingdom Arrives

After four hundred years of waiting, we arrive at Acts. And there stands Jesus—risen from the dead, presenting Himself with convincing proofs of His resurrection:

> After his suffering, he presented himself to them and gave many convincing proofs that he was alive. He appeared to them over a period of forty days and spoke about the kingdom of God. (Acts 1:3)

The kingdom is here. Jesus Himself declared it:

> The Law and the Prophets were proclaimed until John. Since that time, the good news of the kingdom of God is being preached, and everyone is forcing their way into it. (Luke 16:16)

When asked when the kingdom would come, Jesus answered:

> The coming of the kingdom of God is not something that can be observed, nor will people say, "Here it is" or "There it is," because the kingdom of God is in your midst. (Luke 17:20-21)

The kingdom manifested itself through healing and deliverance:

> Heal the sick who are there and tell them, "The kingdom of God has come near to you." (Luke 10:9)

> But if I cast out demons by the finger of God, surely the kingdom of God has come upon you. (Luke 11:20)

When the King is present, so is His kingdom—a realm of justice, freedom from suffering and pain, absence of death, and true prosperity.

### Our Hope

For many of us, life carries profound hardship. Illness that never improves. The loss of loved ones we long to see again. Injustice that seems unresolved. Grief that pierces the soul. But we have hope—not wishful thinking, but the certain promise that Jesus will return in fullness:

> Then I saw a new heaven and a new earth, for the first heaven and the first earth had passed away. I saw the Holy City, the new Jerusalem, coming down out of heaven from God, prepared as a bride beautifully dressed for her husband. And I heard a loud voice from the throne saying, "Look! God's dwelling place is now among the people, and he will dwell with them. They will be his people, and God himself will be with them and be their God. He will wipe every tear from their eyes. There will be no more death or mourning or crying or pain, for the old order of things has passed away." (Revelation 21:1-4)

This is not empty sentiment. This is our inheritance. We hold to this hope. We encourage one another to persevere toward this promise.

## The Kingdom of God Is Not Yet Here

### Why the Delay?

Yet we face a paradox. The kingdom came through Jesus, yet Jesus left. He ascended into heaven, vanishing from sight with no revealed timetable for His return:

> It is not for you to know the times or dates the Father has set by his own authority. (Acts 1:7)

Why must we wait at all?

### He Has Not Abandoned Us

First, understand this: Jesus has not abandoned us. He ascended to accomplish three essential works on our behalf.

**He Prepares a Place**

> Do not let your hearts be troubled. You believe in God; believe also in me. My Father's house has many rooms; if that were not so, would I have told you that I am going there to prepare a place for you? And if I go and prepare a place for you, I will come back and take you to be with me that you also may be where I am. (John 14:1-3)

**He Intercedes for Us**

> He is able to save completely those who come to God through him, because he always lives to intercede for them. (Hebrews 7:25)

Our past sins are forgiven through His sacrifice. Now, seated at the Father's right hand, He intercedes for our present struggles and ongoing failures—speaking on our behalf even when we stumble.

**He Intercedes for the Lost**

> The Lord is not slow in keeping his promise, as some understand slowness. Instead he is patient with you, not wanting anyone to perish, but everyone to come to repentance. (2 Peter 3:9)

If the kingdom came in its fullness right now, no more opportunity for repentance would remain. Those who rejected Christ would be lost forever. Jesus, in His mercy, pleads with the Father: *"Keep the earth alive one more day. One more season. One more year. So that more can enter heaven."*

While Jesus waits, He is not idle. He is kept busy with intercession, with preparation, with redemption. And He invites us into that same purposeful activity.

## The Gift and Our Task

### The Holy Spirit

Jesus gave the disciples clear instruction:

> Do not leave Jerusalem, but wait for the gift my Father promised, which you have heard me speak about. For John baptized with water, but in a few days you will be baptized with the Holy Spirit. (Acts 1:4-5)

Different Christian traditions understand the baptism of the Holy Spirit differently. Some see it as conversion. Others emphasize supernatural empowerment with miraculous signs. But Luke's specific language—using "baptized with the Holy Spirit" only in Acts 2, 8, 10, and 19—suggests these were unique, time-bound events marking the gospel's progression through Jerusalem, Judea, Samaria, and finally to the Gentiles.

> You will receive power when the Holy Spirit comes on you; and you will be my witnesses in Jerusalem, and in all Judea and Samaria, and to the ends of the earth. (Acts 1:8)

Each stage was marked by miraculous signs—booster rockets, so to speak, signaling that the gospel was breaking new ground:

- Acts 2: The disciples in Jerusalem
- Acts 8: The Samaritans believed, then the Spirit fell
- Acts 10: The Spirit fell upon Gentiles at Cornelius's house
- Acts 19: The disciples at Ephesus received the Spirit

Once the gospel reached the Gentiles—once the barrier between Jew and non-Jew was shattered—the pattern was complete. There was no further boundary to cross. Every nation, kindred, and tongue could now respond to Christ.

### Our Inheritance

We are inheritors of that legacy, though not as eyewitnesses ourselves. We don't perform miracles or signs. Instead, we inherit the testimony of those who witnessed Christ's resurrection and the Spirit's outpouring. Their eyewitness accounts are preserved in Scripture:

> Many have undertaken to draw up an account of the things that have been fulfilled among us, just as they were handed down to us by those who from the first were eyewitnesses and servants of the word. With this in mind, since I myself have carefully investigated everything from the beginning, I too decided to write an orderly account for you, most excellent Theophilus, so that you may know the certainty of the things you have been taught. (Luke 1:1-4)

The good news of the kingdom is not confined to a small corner of the Middle East. It extends to all people:

> People will come from east and west and north and south, and will take their places at the feast in the kingdom of God. (Luke 13:29)

### Our Commission

Our task is to participate in the filling of God's kingdom—to "infill" while we wait for Jesus's return. We do this in countless ways:

- **Actively:** Proclaim God's Word to friends and strangers. Evangelize.
- **Passively:** Live righteous lives and give an answer for the hope within us when others ask.
- **Through relationships:** Disciple others as single persons; create, grow, and disciple children as married persons.
- **Within and beyond the church:** Serve in church ministries and in secular vocations.
- **Formally or informally:** Pastor a congregation or lead as a CEO, judge, politician, or other institutional leader.
- **Full-time or part-time:** Serve in vocational ministry or financially support God's work.

The essential call is to *go out*. Don't remain idle. Don't wait passively.

## Don't Stare at the Sky

### The Disciples' Mistake

When Jesus ascended, the disciples were caught off guard:

> After he said this, he was taken up before their very eyes, and a cloud hid him from their sight. They were looking intently up into the sky as he was going, when suddenly two men dressed in white stood beside them. "Men of Galilee," they said, "why do you stand here looking into the sky? This same Jesus, who has been taken from you into heaven, will come back in the same way you have seen him go into heaven." (Acts 1:9-11)

The disciples stared. They gawked. They wondered—Is He coming back in five minutes? Should we set up camp and wait here? The men in white essentially asked: *"Why are you wasting time staring at nothing searching for an answer you already know? No one knows when He will return."*

Get on with the task.

### Our Calling While We Wait

Friends, while we wait for heaven, let us not look longingly into the sky. We must not sit idle, twiddling our thumbs, paralyzed by uncertainty.

Life is happening right now. This moment is precious and limited. It will pass in a blink of an eye.

- If unemployed, don't sit paralyzed in front of your computer waiting for the email. Get trained. Volunteer. Move forward.
- Waiting to marry a godly person? Make yourself godly first. Don't wait for perfection in your circumstances.
- When not actively fighting, sharpen your sword. Develop yourself. Prepare.
- Don't expect a perfect world where everything aligns before you act. Multitask. Move forward even as you wait.
- Don't stare at the clock.

I waited three years for my house extension to be approved. While waiting, I cleaned out the garage, did other projects, secured financing. And then, suddenly, one of the major obstacles cleared. In a blink of an eye.

Most importantly, don't waste the time and ability you have to tell others about the gospel. Sometimes the waiting is painful. The uncertainty is real. But while you wait, tell others about Jesus. Because in a blink of an eye, that season will pass.

The kingdom is here. The kingdom is coming. And while we await its consummation, we have work to do.
