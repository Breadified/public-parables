# Acts 16:1-40

**Title:** The Household of God

**Date:** June 6, 2015

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Flexibility as Individuals

Paul and Barnabas decided to revisit the churches they had planted on their first missionary journey. However, their partnership fractured over a personal disagreement—a sharp conflict that ultimately led them to part ways.

> Some time later Paul said to Barnabas, "Let us go back and visit the believers in all the towns where we preached the word of the Lord and see how they are doing." (Acts 16:36)

### The Disagreement Over John Mark

Barnabas wanted to take John Mark along, but Paul objected, reasoning that Mark had deserted them during their first journey in Pamphylia and had not remained faithful to the work.

> Barnabas wanted to take John, also called Mark, with them, but Paul did not think it wise to take him, because he had deserted them in Pamphylia and had not continued with them in the work. (Acts 13:37-38)

The exact reason for Mark's departure remains unclear. Some scholars suggest he was bothered by Paul's leadership shift away from Barnabas, his cousin. Others propose he couldn't endure the hardships of missionary travel. Whatever the cause, he had abandoned the mission at a critical moment.

What's remarkable is that Mark eventually reconciled with Paul. Years later, Paul would request Mark's presence, describing him as "helpful to me in my ministry." Peter also referred to Mark affectionately as "my son." By God's grace, this young man's failure was not the end of his story.

**When individuals disagree, we face choices.** Sometimes it's appropriate to agree to disagree and pursue separate paths. In matters of indifference—things neither commanded nor forbidden by Scripture—we have freedom to choose differently. We're called to give way gracefully, not to insist on every right.

### The Circumcision of Timothy

Paul encountered Timothy at Lystra, a young believer whose mother was Jewish and a believer, but whose father was Greek. Paul wanted to take Timothy as a companion on his missionary journey.

> Paul came to Derbe and then to Lystra, where a disciple named Timothy lived, whose mother was Jewish and a believer but whose father was a Greek... Paul wanted to take him along on the journey, so he circumcised him because of the Jews who lived in that area, for they all knew that his father was a Greek. (Acts 16:1-3)

This is instructive. Just the previous week, the Jerusalem Council had affirmed that Gentiles were not required to become Jews to be Christians. By extension, Jews were not required to be circumcised for salvation. Timothy was already a believer before his circumcision.

Yet Paul circumcised him anyway—not out of legalistic necessity, but for strategic Gospel effectiveness. To the unconverted Jewish mind, an uncircumcised man of Jewish descent was unthinkable. Paul knew that if he appeared to sponsor a Jewish apostate, it would hinder his ability to reach Jewish audiences. Circumcision remained a significant part of Jewish covenant heritage, even though its deepest significance was fulfilled in Christ.

**Paul's principle was clear:** when adiaphora—matters of indifference—could be modified for Gospel advancement without compromising truth, he adapted. He became a Jew to the Jews, not by becoming a sinner, but by respecting cultural sensibilities that didn't violate Scripture.

### Divine Guidance and Flexibility

As they traveled through Phrygia and Galatia, they were forbidden by the Holy Spirit from preaching in the province of Asia. When they attempted to enter Bithynia, the Spirit of Jesus prevented them. Instead, Paul received a vision of a Macedonian man calling, "Come over to Macedonia and help us."

> Paul and his companions traveled throughout the region of Phrygia and Galatia, having been kept by the Holy Spirit from preaching the word in the province of Asia. When they came to the border of Mysia, they tried to enter Bithynia, but the Spirit of Jesus would not allow them. So they passed by Mysia and went down to Troas. During the night Paul had a vision of a man of Macedonia standing and begging him, "Come over to Macedonia and help us." After Paul had seen the vision, we got ready at once to leave for Macedonia, concluding that God had called us to preach the gospel to them. (Acts 16:6-10)

These were remarkable supernatural interventions. Yet Paul's response was not frustration at his blocked plans, but readiness to follow God's redirected course. **His flexibility revealed his true priority: advancing the Gospel mattered infinitely more than which region received it.**

The lesson for us: Make your plans with the big picture in mind. If circumstances barred one path, change course. Don't cling rigidly to how you thought things should unfold. Lots of things in life simply don't matter. Individuals must give and take. We shouldn't constantly assert rights or demand that everything go our way. We must trust God and follow where He leads.

**The core principle:** As individuals, be flexible. Don't be legalistic about matters of indifference. Many things don't require laws—laws everywhere make life brittle and joyless. The Ten Commandments established the principles we need; endless regulations suffocate freedom.

## Start with Households

Paul and his companions traveled to Philippi, a Roman colony and the leading city of that district in Macedonia. They stayed several days.

> From Troas we put out to sea and sailed straight for Samothrace, and the next day we went on to Neapolis. From there we traveled to Philippi, a Roman colony and the leading city of that district of Macedonia. And we stayed there several days. (Acts 16:11-12)

Since there was apparently no synagogue in Philippi, they sought out a place of prayer by the river, where they found a small group of women gathered.

### Lydia: The First European Convert

> On the Sabbath we went outside the city gate to the river, where we expected to find a place of prayer. We sat down and began to speak to the women who had gathered there. One of those listening was a woman from the city of Thyatira named Lydia, a dealer in purple cloth. She was a worshiper of God. The Lord opened her heart to respond to Paul's message. When she and the members of her household were baptized, she invited us to her home. (Acts 16:13-15)

Lydia was a wealthy merchant dealing in the luxury goods of her time. More importantly, she was a "worshiper of God"—someone drawn to the faith but not yet a full believer. When Paul proclaimed Jesus, the Lord opened her heart. She and her entire household believed and were baptized.

Notice the household dimension: evangelism in the New Testament typically flowed through family units. Lydia became the first documented Christian convert in Europe, and she immediately opened her home as a base for ministry.

### The Demon-Possessed Slave Girl

As they continued visiting the place of prayer, they encountered a young slave woman possessed by a spirit of divination—the spirit the Greeks associated with the oracle at Delphi. She made significant money for her owners through fortune-telling.

> Once when we were going to the place of prayer, we were met by a female slave who had a spirit of python by which she predicted the future. She earned a great deal of money for her owners by fortune-telling. She followed Paul and the rest of us, shouting, "These men are servants of the Most High God, who are telling you the way to be saved." She kept this up for many days. Finally Paul became so annoyed that he turned around and said to the spirit, "In the name of Jesus Christ I command you to come out of her!" At that moment the spirit left her. (Acts 16:16-18)

Ironically, the demonized girl spoke truth—they were indeed servants of God announcing the way of salvation. Yet Paul was troubled by her. Perhaps he recognized the contradiction: true salvation cannot come through demonic promotion, no matter how accurate the words. The demonic realm cannot authentically herald the Gospel.

Paul cast out the spirit in Jesus's name. The girl was delivered—freed from bondage.

### The Backlash and the Jailer's Household

But liberation brought economic disaster to her owners. Their lucrative business vanished.

> When her owners realized that their hope of making money was gone, they seized Paul and Silas and dragged them into the marketplace to face the authorities. They brought them before the magistrates and said, "These men are Jews, and are throwing our city into an uproar by advocating customs unlawful for us Romans to accept or practice." (Acts 16:19-21)

The owners incited a mob. Paul and Silas were stripped, beaten with rods, and thrown into the inner prison with their feet locked in stocks. Their jailer was commanded to guard them carefully.

At midnight, while other prisoners listened, Paul and Silas were praying and singing hymns to God. Suddenly, a violent earthquake shook the prison foundations, the doors flew open, and everyone's chains came loose.

The jailer awoke in terror. Assuming the prisoners had escaped, he drew his sword to kill himself—he would face execution for negligence.

> But Paul shouted, "Don't harm yourself! We are all here!" The jailer called for lights, rushed in and fell trembling before Paul and Silas. He then brought them out and asked, "Sirs, what must I do to be saved?" They replied, "Believe in the Lord Jesus, and you will be saved—you and your household." Then they spoke the word of the Lord to him and to all the others in his house. At that hour of the night the jailer took them and washed their wounds; then immediately he and all his household were baptized. The jailer brought them into his house and set a meal before them; he was filled with joy because he had come to believe in God—he and his whole household. (Acts 16:28-34)

This is crucial: the phrase "you and your household" should not be misunderstood. It doesn't mean that when the household head believes, salvation automatically extends to his family. Rather, the Gospel was offered to the jailer and his household. The message was proclaimed to them all. And when the jailer believed, his household believed with him.

**The power of household evangelism:** The Gospel flourished through family networks. People are naturally most receptive to those they love and trust. When one household member encounters Christ, others in that home face the Gospel in its most compelling form—not from a stranger, but from someone they know intimately.

Christianity is relational at its core. We understand God as Father. Jesus is our brother. Fellow believers are brothers and sisters. Husbands and wives represent Christ and the Church. These familial terms reveal that Gospel truth is inseparable from relational love—the kind we first learn in families.

**When families break down, the Gospel loses its natural context.** It's difficult to grasp God the Father's love when your earthly father is abusive. It's hard to understand unconditional love when your mother is self-centered. It's challenging to believe in covenant commitment when marriages dissolve. The decline in Christianity correlates with the decline in family stability.

**If you want to arrest the spread of irreligiousness, start with the family.** Many of you in our church are the first Christians in your generation, the first in your families. This is both sobering and exciting. You have the privilege—and responsibility—of establishing Christian families that model God's love and values for the next generation. You're not merely following Christ individually; you're pioneering a new legacy of faith in your household.

## Be Just as a State

### Paul Asserts His Rights

The next morning, the magistrates sent officers with orders to release Paul and Silas.

> When it was daylight, the magistrates sent their officers to the jailer with the order: "Release those men." The jailer told Paul, "The magistrates have ordered that you and Silas be released. Now you can leave. Go in peace." (Acts 16:35-36)

The jailer was relieved to see them go. But Paul refused to leave quietly.

> But Paul said to the officers: "They beat us publicly without a trial, even though we are Roman citizens, and threw us into prison. And now do they want to get rid of us quietly? No! Let them come themselves and escort us out." (Acts 16:37)

This is striking. Paul—who showed flexibility about Mark, about circumcision, about missionary routes—does not bend here. Why?

Roman citizenship was a precious status. Roman citizens could not be arrested without trial, subjected to torture, or flogged. The magistrates had violated fundamental Roman law in a flagrant act of injustice.

> The officers reported this to the magistrates, and when they heard that Paul and Silas were Roman citizens, they were alarmed. They came to appease them and escorted them from the prison, requesting them to leave the city. (Acts 16:38-39)

### Fighting for Justice, Not Just Rights

The magistrates' alarm reveals they understood the gravity: they had violated the rule of law. But why did Paul, who practiced flexibility so readily in personal matters, take such a hard stance here?

**The answer is profound:** Paul wasn't fighting merely for his own rights. He was fighting for the rule of law itself—which protects everyone.

When laws are broken with impunity, corruption spreads. The next person in the next town in the next generation faces a weakened justice system. If Paul had accepted this injustice quietly, he would have signaled that law-breaking has no consequences.

There's a fundamental difference between adiaphora—matters where we can graciously give way—and justice. In personal disputes, flexibility is virtuous. But in matters of law and justice, silence is complicity.

### The Christian Temptation to Passivity

Many Christians today fear imposing their views on secular societies. "We're a minority," we reason. "We shouldn't force our standards on others." But this confuses two entirely different things.

There's a difference between imposing Christian theology and enforcing law. When we advocate for laws reflecting biblical principles, we're not imposing our "views"—we're enforcing justice. And justice applies to everyone, believer and unbeliever alike.

Our historical Christian nation established laws against theft, murder, fraud, and child exploitation. These aren't "Christian values being forced on others"—they're the foundation of any civilized society. When we fall silent while those laws are broken or eroded, we're abdicating our responsibility.

### Examples of Injustice Ignored

Consider a concrete case: when an education department banned approved religious education textbooks in violation of state law, Christians made noise for a while. But when the ban was lifted, we fell silent. We congratulated ourselves on our "tolerance."

But injustice occurred. Who was disciplined? Who was held accountable? What mechanisms ensure it won't happen again? By accepting an apology without demanding justice, we essentially disarmed ourselves—we invited future violations.

It's like a bully beating a child, then stopping. Do we thank the bully? No. We demand an apology and restitution. We ensure accountability. That's justice.

When we fight for traditional marriage, we're not imposing Christian values. We're fighting for the rule of law and children's rights—the right to know and be raised by their biological parents when possible. These are rights that transcend religious belief.

### The Irish Referendum and Christian Passivity

The 2015 Irish referendum on same-sex marriage is instructive. Eighty-two percent of the population is nominally Catholic—a nation built on Christian heritage. Yet the referendum passed.

But look closer at the numbers: only 60% of eligible voters actually voted. Of those who voted, 62% voted yes. This means only 37% of all eligible Irish citizens voted in favor—hardly a decisive national mandate.

Why did the result seem so overwhelming? Because the pro-marriage equality side mobilized effectively. They used legal means—fundraising (over $11 million from U.S. sources), organizing, advertising, media campaigns. They got their people to vote.

Meanwhile, Christians largely abstained. We didn't fundraise. We didn't organize. We didn't vote. We preferred not to "impose our views."

**But this isn't humility—it's abdication.**

### A Call to Just Engagement

There's nothing inevitable about the decline of traditional values. It's only inevitable if we cede the field. If we refuse to use legal, democratic means available to us—voting, speaking, writing, petitioning, funding—then we guarantee the outcome we fear.

Paul used his Roman citizenship. He appealed to law. Not aggressively, not illegally, but strategically and courageously.

We should do likewise. Get online. Post thoughtfully. Write to your representatives. Vote. Support organizations defending justice. Make your voice heard—civilly, truthfully, persistently.

Yes, we'll make mistakes. Condensing complex truths into social media posts is hard. But imperfection is not an excuse for silence.

**The fight is to be had. And it must be had before it's too late.**

## The Gospel and Civilization

### How Christianity Transformed Europe

When Western Christianity was born, Europe was backward. While China invented paper, silk, the compass, gunpowder, and built massive fleets, Europe remained pagan and barbaric. Europeans wore animal skins, practiced superstition and slavery, sacrificed children, practiced polygamy and pillage.

Yet Christian Europe became the engine of Western civilization. What transformed it?

**The Gospel transformed Europe.** The preaching that Jesus rose from the dead meant all would face judgment. This belief demanded ethical living. It drove the creation of monasteries—centers of learning that preserved classical knowledge and spawned the scientific revolution. It gave birth to ideals of chivalry, civility, and dignity that shaped our entire culture.

The monks copied manuscripts. Monasteries became productive centers. Wastelands became hamlets, towns, cities, commonwealths. The savage barbarian warrior became the Christian knight. From pagan darkness emerged the flowering of Western civilization.

### The Cost of Abandoning Gospel Values

Today, Europe has abandoned its Christian faith. It keeps the buildings—cathedrals, universities, parliaments—but the soul is gone. And as Christianity retreats, so do all the blessings it provided: more debauchery, more crime, more violence against the vulnerable, more Sharia law creeping into parts of the continent.

Australia, moving slower than Europe but on the same trajectory, is retreating from its Christian heritage. As we do, the dysfunctions will return.

**But this is not inevitable.** It's only inevitable if we're lazy in enforcing the laws that preserve justice and the Gospel that transforms hearts.

Paul was an individual—flexible, gracious, willing to adapt. But he was also part of households, and he stood up for justice in the state. He understood that all three levels matter.

**Fight for it before it's too late. Fight for the good laws of this country. Fight for justice. Fight for the Gospel.**
