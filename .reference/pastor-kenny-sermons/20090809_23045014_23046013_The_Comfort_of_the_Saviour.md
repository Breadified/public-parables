# Isaiah 45:14–46:13

**Title:** The Comfort of the Saviour

**Date:** August 9, 2009

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Security: Where Do We Find It?

I once foolishly took a shortcut across the University of Sydney late at night on my way back from Bible college to Redfern station. The campus was eerily quiet and dark. I felt profoundly unsafe—no security guards, no lights, just the constant fear that someone could emerge from the shadows and no one would hear my cries. I walked as quickly as I could, grateful when the journey ended without incident.

This experience touches on something fundamental to human nature. The psychologist Abraham Maslow identified safety as the second most basic human need, after physiological survival. Once our physical needs are relatively satisfied, our need for security takes over and dominates our behavior. We yearn for a predictable, orderly world where injustice and inconsistency are minimized, where the familiar is frequent and the unfamiliar rare. When this need goes unmet, we feel anxious and tense, even without actual physical danger.

For many of us, the drive to seek security is one of life's primary motivators. Those from migrant or poor backgrounds know this well—we arrive with little and work hard to find security. Even when we reach our goals, that sense of security remains elusive, so we keep working. A worker wants job security, savings accounts, and insurance policies. Teenagers seek safety in numbers and feel insecure when out of sync with their peers. A Year 7 student entering high school or a freshman beginning university faces new rules, new freedoms, new decisions. A Year 12 student awaits their trials with anxiety about the future.

This fundamental human insecurity is only heightened when we consider what Isaiah tells us about God.

## 1. God Is Hidden (45:15)

> Truly you are a God who hides himself, O God and Savior of Israel. (Isaiah 45:15)

When Isaiah says God "hides himself," he doesn't mean God is playing hide and seek, or that He is shy, modest, or afraid. Rather, "hides" means God is invisible—you cannot find Him. He is out of reach. The theological term for this is *transcendent*: He lives beyond the ordinary range of perception, beyond the limits of human experience, and therefore unknowable to us in our natural state.

This transcendence stands in stark contrast to the visible idols of the nations around Israel:

> Some pour out gold from their bags and weigh out silver on the scales; they hire a goldsmith to make it into a god, and they bow down and worship it. They lift it to their shoulders and carry it; they set it up in its place, and there it stands. (Isaiah 46:6–7)

The idols of the nations can be seen, touched, and carried. God cannot. He is not part of this material world because He precedes it—He is the Creator, not the creation.

> For this is what the LORD says—he who created the heavens, he is God; he who fashioned and made the earth, he founded it; he did not create it to be empty, but formed it to be inhabited—he says: "I am the LORD, and there is no other." (Isaiah 45:18)

We often imagine God as an old man with a beard wearing white—or as popular culture depicts Him. But understanding God is like an ant trying to comprehend a textbook. Where would you even begin to list all the impossibilities? Or imagine a six-week-old infant trying to understand calculus. Her eyes aren't even working properly yet—everything is a blur.

This sense of God's transcendence is acknowledged throughout human history by the extraordinary steps people take to try to reach Him: climbing high mountains, visiting quiet places in hopes of catching God in a rare moment, seeking out spiritual gurus, meditating and chanting, discovering secret formulas or spells to summon Him, crafting golden and wooden objects as conduits between themselves and the divine.

### Application: The Nature of God's Transcendence

God is transcendent—completely separate from creation. This is why pantheism and panentheism are wrong. Hinduism teaches that God is in each of us. Matthew Fox wrote, "As the ocean is in the fish and the fish are in God, so God is in everything and everything is in God." No. God is not in everything. He is separate from His creation. Yes, this distance can feel alienating, but it is the reality of God's transcendence—as vast as the distance between a subject and a monarch.

We cannot know God by ourselves. We can only perceive the material world, and we cannot cross that gap to the immaterial without external help. This is why mysticism—the belief that we can zone into God through mountaintop experiences or special feelings—cannot work. The gap is too wide. We are finite and mortal; He is infinite and eternal. Without God Himself bridging that distance, we remain shut out.

## 2. God Is Not Hidden (45:19ff)

But here is where hope breaks through the darkness. God has not left His ways obscure or His will secret. Though God is transcendent, He is not hidden from us. Notice how many times Isaiah uses words of communication—speak, declare, utter:

> I have not spoken in secret, from somewhere in a land of darkness; I have not said to Jacob's descendants, "Seek me in vain." I, the LORD, speak the truth; I declare what is right. (Isaiah 45:19)

> Declare what is to be, present it—let them take counsel together. Who foretold this long ago, who declared it from the distant past? Was it not I, the LORD? (Isaiah 45:21)

> By myself I have sworn, my mouth has uttered in all integrity a word that will not be revoked: Before me every knee will bow; by me every tongue will swear. (Isaiah 45:23)

Just when we thought we were lost in a silent world, just when we believed that seeking God was futile, a voice cries out to us. **God speaks.**

Though it is impossible for the Israelites to bridge the transcendence and reach God, God is perfectly capable of bridging that gap to reach them. He comes to them. Across the void, God speaks.

### Knowing Jesus

And in these last days, God has done more than merely speak. Seven hundred years after Isaiah, God came down Himself in the form of a man—Jesus—to reveal God to us. The Word of God took on flesh, blood, and physical shape. Not an ethereal, transcendent being or ghost, but living bone and flesh. If you had been in Palestine during that time, you could have touched God, felt God, played with God. That is exactly what happened.

> That which was from the beginning, which we have heard, which we have seen with our eyes, which we have looked at and our hands have touched—this we proclaim concerning the Word of life. The life appeared; we have seen it and testify to it, and we proclaim to you the eternal life, which was with the Father and has appeared to us. We proclaim to you what we have seen and heard, so that you also may have fellowship with us. And our fellowship is with the Father and with his Son, Jesus Christ. We write this to make our joy complete. (1 John 1:1–4)

While God remains transcendent, the knowledge of God is no longer transcendent. The Jesus of history has appeared. We can know God if we know Jesus. We can know God because the records of what Jesus said and did have been preserved. You hold those records in your hands: the Bible.

### What the Word of God Is—and Isn't

**What it is: Words**

God's choice of revelation is words. He speaks in order to reveal insight into the mind of the transcendent God. There is no room for anti-intellectualism in Christianity. Since God's revelation comes through words, we must learn, study, and think. This is why preaching, Bible study groups, and personal reading are so essential—they are how God communicates His truth to us.

Remarkably, it is by knowing the truth that the Word of God changes us. By knowing the truth and being convicted of our sins, we repent and transform our actions.

**What it is not: Impressions and Feelings**

One crucial consequence of believing that God speaks through words is this: God does not primarily communicate through feelings and impressions. I am not saying God cannot communicate this way—He is all-powerful and can do as He pleases—but His chosen method of revelation is words.

Yet some Christians, when facing a difficult decision, keep saying, "I feel God is telling me..." But when God spoke in the Old Testament from the sky, His words were clear and unambiguous. He does not speak through vague feelings. He sent us Jesus and gave us the Bible.

There is nothing wrong with feelings—they are natural responses to the world around us. But if we mistake them for God's communication, we subscribe to a form of mysticism that keeps God as unknowable, unclear, and mysterious. We try to find answers He has already given or answer questions He is not interested in.

God has spoken. Judge your decisions by the written Word of God.

*Dating:* It feels good to spend time alone in each other's rooms? No, it is not right. Judge it by the Word of God.

*Lifestyle:* You want to sing and enjoy yourself but with disregard for your neighbor? Judge it by the Word of God.

*Career:* What course should you study? Judge it by the Word of God. Will it help others, or primarily serve yourself? Will it enable you to serve others, or encourage you to serve yourself?

## 3. The Message: Trust God or Trust Idols?

God has spoken, but what does He say? His message is clear.

### A. Trust God for True Security (46:3–13)

> Listen to me, O house of Jacob, all you who remain of the house of Israel, you whom I have upheld since you were conceived, and have carried since your birth. Even to your old age and gray hairs I am he, I am he who will sustain you. I have made you and I will carry you; I will sustain you and I will rescue you. (Isaiah 46:3–4)

God's promise to Israel is personal and relational. He has carried them from birth. He will sustain them through old age. He will rescue them.

> Remember this, fix it in mind, take it to heart, you rebels. Remember the former things, those of long ago; I am God, and there is no other; I am God, and there is none like me. I make known the end from the beginning, from ancient times, what is still to come. I say: My purpose will stand, and I will do all that I please. From the east I summon a bird of prey; from a far-off land, a man to fulfill my purpose. What I have said, that will I bring about; what I have planned, that will I do. (Isaiah 46:8–11)

God knows the future completely. He is fulfilling His purposes through history itself—summoning Cyrus from the east to liberate Israel from Babylon. His Word will not fail.

> Listen to me, you stubborn-hearted, you who are far from righteousness. I am bringing my righteousness near, it is not far away; and my salvation will not be delayed. I will grant salvation to Zion, my splendor to Israel. (Isaiah 46:12–13)

God extends His salvation not only to Israel but to all nations:

> Gather together and come; assemble, you fugitives from the nations. Ignorant are those who carry about idols of wood, who pray to gods that cannot save... There is no God apart from me, a righteous God and a Savior; there is none but me. Turn to me and be saved, all you ends of the earth; for I am God, and there is no other. (Isaiah 45:20–22)

> They will say of me, "In the LORD alone are righteousness and strength." All who have raged against him will come to him and be put to shame. But in the LORD all the descendants of Israel will be found righteous and will exult. (Isaiah 45:24–25)

### B. Do Not Trust Idols for Security (46:1–7)

> Bel bows down, Nebo stoops low; their idols are borne by beasts of burden. The images that are carried about are burdensome, a burden for the weary. They stoop and bow down together; unable to rescue the burden, they themselves go off into captivity. (Isaiah 46:1–2)

The irony is devastating: Babylon's idols, which were supposed to protect the nation, are themselves carried away as plunder by Cyrus. The gods cannot even save themselves.

> Some pour out gold from their bags and weigh out silver on the scales; they hire a goldsmith to make it into a god, and they bow down and worship it. They lift it to their shoulders and carry it; they set it up in its place, and there it stands. From that spot it cannot move. Though one cries out to it, it does not answer; it cannot save him from his troubles. (Isaiah 46:6–7)

Idols cannot deliver. Consider the modern parallels: celebrities and entertainment personalities who rise to heroic status, only to eventually fall from grace. We see the foolishness of the Australian Idol franchise, the sensationalism of shock radio personalities like Kyle Sandilands. These are the idols our culture manufactures, and they cannot save us.

The book of Jonah captures this truth beautifully. In his moment of clarity, having been rescued from the great fish, Jonah reflects:

> Those who cling to worthless idols forfeit the grace that could be theirs. For salvation comes from the Lord. (Jonah 2:8)

### Understanding Modern Idolatry

When we speak of idols today, we must distinguish between the traditional and the modern.

**Traditional idols** are physical objects—statues of Buddha, Jesus, or Mary—that we invest with spiritual power. As we mature in faith, we should move away from dependence on such objects, just as a three-year-old gradually becomes less dependent on a security blanket.

**Modern idols** are anything we trust for security instead of God. Consider the first major case of idol worship in Scripture—the golden calf (Exodus 31–32). When Moses stayed on Mount Sinai longer than expected, the people grew anxious and asked Aaron to make them gods to go before them:

> Aaron answered them, "Take off the gold earrings that your wives, your sons and your daughters are wearing, and bring them to me." So all the people took off their earrings and brought them to Aaron. He took what they handed him and made it into an idol cast in the shape of a calf... Then they said, "These are your gods, O Israel, who brought you up out of Egypt." (Exodus 32:2–4)

Why did they do this? They were worried. They wanted security. They wanted reassurance that they had a tangible god to guide them and protect them—something they could see and touch. When we feel anxious and insecure, we create material things to comfort us: rain idols for agriculture, sun idols for warmth, fertility idols for children.

The impulse is understandable, but it is ultimately futile.

### Where Do We Find Security Today?

**Materialism**

We think we are secure when our garages are full of useless trinkets and state-of-the-art appliances we never use. We feel secure holding a mobile phone in the middle of Sydney University at midnight. We are secure when surrounded by home comforts—a plasma television, air-conditioning, the latest fashion so no one will pick on us, the latest security system for our homes. But as Jesus said to the man who built larger and larger barns to store his possessions: "You fool! This very night your life will be demanded from you" (Luke 12:20). Material things cannot protect us. One family can be killed in a single night.

**Bank Balances**

We think our bank balances will protect us. Yet the Global Financial Crisis showed us how quickly superannuation and savings can evaporate. Money is simply the potential to buy material things, and material things cannot ultimately secure us.

**Career and Achievement**

We believe that getting a good job will protect us, that it will give us the financial buffer we need. We pursue high ATAR scores in school to access good universities, to access good jobs, to access material security. But a job can be lost. A company can fail. Circumstances change.

**Experience as Comfort**

Our modern, feeling-oriented society has invented a new idol: experience. "How did you feel?" we ask after someone accomplishes something. We take trips to Europe for the experience—once a lifetime event in retirement, then a gap year thing, then multiple times: before work, before marriage, before children, at great expense. But why are we living? For a photo album? For feelings?

Many Christians feel pressure to have sensory experiences of God. But God has chosen to reveal Himself through words, not feelings. Experiences are too subjective. And the danger is acute: when you no longer sense God, what then? Emotions and feelings are products of our brain chemistry, hormones, and circumstances. Depression can follow when those feelings fade.

**We must give these idols up.**

## Finding True Security

All of us face this question and must continue to face it. I faced it when I came to a major decision: whether to pursue full-time ministry.

I wanted to teach the Bible as much as I could. My mentors affirmed that I had the character and ability to do it. But one thing held me back: I wanted security.

I worried about job security—churches change their minds; they run out of money. I worried about income—ministry doesn't pay much; there is no financial buffer. I considered opportunity costs—six years of study and training sacrificed. I feared what would happen if I got sick, had an accident, or faced family crises. With less income, there would be less cushion for life's hardships.

But then I was challenged to ask myself: Where do I ultimately place my security?

I realized that I could be the richest man on my block, living comfortably, and yet my life could be taken from me in an instant. Better to die serving God than to die serving myself. Money and material things cannot provide the true security we need to do the right thing.

Jim Elliot, the missionary martyred in Ecuador, captured this truth perfectly: "He is no fool who gives what he cannot keep to gain what he cannot lose."

Do you really think you can keep what you're holding on to?

### Application: Where Is Your Security?

If God had remained hidden and silent, yes, we would be insecure. If we did not know God, we would be insecure, because the future would be truly unknowable and random. But God has not remained silent. He has revealed Himself in Jesus, and He continues to speak through the Bible.

Jesus promises that the future is secure for those who trust in Him. He calls on us to follow Him, despite the cost. He says that one day the whole world will be brought into judgment and account. What will our answer be then?

What are you holding on to that makes it hard for you to trust God? Is it money? Is it possessions? Is it a job that gives you so much security that you would rather do the wrong thing to keep it? Are you serving your idols instead of serving God?

Instead, be generous. Care for others. Use the time and energy freed from serving idols to serve God. Give those idols up. Trust God fully.

> But Israel will be saved by the LORD with an everlasting salvation; you will never be put to shame or disgraced, to ages everlasting. (Isaiah 45:17)
