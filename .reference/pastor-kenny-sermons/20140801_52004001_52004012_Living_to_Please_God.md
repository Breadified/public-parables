# 1 Thessalonians 4:1-12

**Title:** Living to Please God
**Date:** 30 August 2014
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction: The Challenge of "More and More"

We live in a culture obsessed with ambition—wanting more titles, more credentials, more money, more possessions. There's nothing inherently wrong with healthy ambition, but Paul challenges us with a crucial question: **What should we want more and more?**

God's answer comes through Paul's letter to the Thessalonians. He addresses two fundamental areas where believers must pursue growth: holiness and love. The framework for our teaching comes from Paul's reminder in 3:13, where he asks God to strengthen hearts so that believers will be "blameless and holy" when Jesus returns. This theme echoes through our passage, with Paul urging the Thessalonians to "do this more and more"—both in holiness (verses 1-8) and in love for one another (verses 9-12).

## Part 1: Be Holy (4:1-8)

### God's Clear Will

> It is God's will that you should be sanctified: that you should avoid sexual immorality. (4:3)

Paul begins by stating God's will plainly: **sanctification**. The word "sanctified" means "holy"—to be set apart, made different, sacred. It's not a mystical concept but a practical one.

Think of it this way: in a Chinese home, there are chopsticks designated only for serving shared dishes—set apart for that purpose alone. An indoor broom serves a different function than an outdoor broom. Similarly, as God's people, we are set apart for His purposes, separated from the world around us.

When God brought Israel out of Egypt, He placed them among pagan nations and called them to be different. The contrast was stark:
- Israel had one God; the nations had many gods
- Israel had laws; the nations were lawless
- Israel refused to sacrifice children; the nations practiced child sacrifice  
- Israel valued sexual faithfulness; the pagan nations celebrated sexual promiscuity through their fertility religions and temple prostitutes

Today, just as in Egypt, Jesus—our Passover Lamb—was sacrificed to deliver us from slavery to sin. We are now God's set-apart people, called to be different from our unbelieving neighbors.

### Holiness and Sexual Integrity

The first practical expression of this holiness is clear: **avoid sexual immorality** (porneia—sexual activity outside of marriage).

Many young people approach relationships with naive terminology. "Going out" seems harmless—movies, dinners, getting to know each other. But when "going out" becomes "making out," boundaries have been crossed. What began as friendship has entered territory reserved for marriage.

Similarly, the terms "boyfriend" and "girlfriend" sound innocent. You have a friend who's a boy; you have a friend who's a girl—what's wrong with that? But these terms have come to mean something entirely different: a pseudo-marriage, a counterfeit of the real thing. And that counterfeit carries real consequences.

Consider what happens in such relationships:
- The couple becomes inseparable at social gatherings
- They spend significant time together, going on holidays as a unit
- Their identities merge; friends begin to think of them as one person
- They know each other's habits, have rhythms to their arguments, might even adopt a dog together
- Physical and emotional boundaries blur into territory that belongs to marriage alone

This isn't friendship—this is playing house without commitment. And when such relationships end, the pain isn't the clean break of friendship; it's the devastating rupture of lovers, with permanent effects on future marriages.

Paul's instruction is radical by today's standards: **if you're attracted to someone, be friends. Go out for coffee, have lunch—with no strings attached.** Yes, it might be a special friendship, but keep it as friendship until you've made the commitment of marriage.

Brothers, remember: she is someone else's future wife. Sisters, remember: he is someone's future husband. What you do today shapes that future.

> No one should wrong or take advantage of a brother or sister in this matter. The Lord will punish all those who commit such sins, as we told you and warned you before. (4:6-7)

The world sees the opposite sex as prey, as a target, as a desire to be satisfied, as a conquest to be claimed. But we are not like that. We are set apart.

Good brothers protect their sisters from harm—protecting them from predators, refusing to initiate anything that leads across marriage's boundaries, never making implicit promises through their actions.

Good sisters protect their brothers—protecting them from visual temptation, from the struggle of exposure to inappropriate images, from flirting and innuendo that stokes temptation.

### What Makes Us Different

Here's what's remarkable: you can tell different religions apart by their external markers—the foods they eat or avoid, the clothes they wear, the language they speak. But Christianity isn't primarily about these externals.

A stranger shouldn't be able to identify us by our food, our clothing, or our language. They should only be able to tell us apart by our **ethics**—by what we do.

In Paul's day and in ours, sexual morality is that defining marker. No one cares if you eat different foods. No one cares if you dress differently. No one cares what language you speak. But stand up for sexual integrity? Control your body? Refuse to compromise on the value and exclusiveness of marriage? **That marks you out.** That's when the world notices something genuinely different.

In our age, we're engulfed in a culture obsessed with sexual immorality. Every magazine, every television show normalizes sleeping around. Popular sitcoms treat infidelity and casual sex as entertainment. Parents, don't put televisions in your children's rooms. Be vigilant about what shapes their understanding of sexuality.

True love—a love that says no to sexual immorality—is what separates us from the world. That is what marks us out as God's people.

## Part 2: Love One Another (4:9-12)

### Growing in Love

> Now about your love for one another we do not need to write to you, for you yourselves have been taught by God to love each other. And in fact, you do love all of God's family throughout Macedonia. Yet we urge you, brothers and sisters, to do so more and more. (4:9-10)

Paul affirms the Thessalonians: they're already doing well at loving one another. They've been taught by God Himself. But then—crucially—he urges them to do this **more and more**. Love isn't a destination; it's a direction. It's something we grow into continuously.

### Make It Your Ambition: A Quiet Life

> Make it your ambition to lead a quiet life: You should mind your own business and work with your hands, just as we told you. (4:11)

This might sound counterintuitive. "Quiet life" sounds passive, even lazy. But the context reveals its true meaning. Paul describes the opposite condition in 2 Thessalonians 3: idle people who are "not busy; they are busybodies"—people who talk much but do nothing, make excuses, blame everyone but themselves, never execute their plans.

A "quiet life" means something very different. It means:
- **Work steadily.** Get a job. Not every job is glamorous, but a job is a job, and it pays the bills
- **Don't be fussy.** There's no perfect job. Overcome your pride, laziness, procrastination, and the fantasy that something better will come along. Just work
- **Work without fanfare.** Do your thing diligently and faithfully, with a low profile
- **Keep your ego out of it.** Don't link your identity and worth to your career. You can serve others and contribute whether you wear a suit or overalls

Some in Thessalonica had stopped working, apparently because they'd become fatalistic about Jesus' return. We face different temptations. In our affluent society, we're prone to being too picky about work—rejecting jobs because they're dirty, boring, hard, don't pay enough, require travel, or fall "beneath our qualifications."

But here's the reality: **we all have to eat. We all have to pay rent and buy petrol. Who else is responsible for that?** Unless you're a child, you are responsible for yourself.

### The Purpose: Respect and Independence

> So that your daily life may win the respect of outsiders and so that you will not be dependent on anybody. (4:12)

When we work faithfully—even at unglamorous jobs, with humble attitudes—we gain something powerful: the respect of those watching. The world takes notice when Christians don't complain, don't make excuses, don't expect handouts. Our integrity speaks louder than our words.

Moreover, we become independent. We don't burden others. We can actually help others. We can support our families, contribute to our church community, and extend generosity to the wider world.

This is love in action. God isn't primarily concerned with your career title. He's concerned with whether you're loving one another. He's concerned with your holiness and your integrity as you live out your daily life.

## Conclusion: More and More

What are your ambitions? God's call is clear: pursue holiness and love more and more.

**On Holiness:**
- Put away sexual sin
- Remove those magazines, avoid those websites
- Be accountable to mature believers about your relationships
- If you're in a relationship that's crossed boundaries, consider whether it's time to step back
- Guard your integrity fiercely

**On Love and Work:**
- Be a quiet, steady worker—no fuss, no ego
- Don't put your worth into your job title
- Don't be a burden on others
- Help your family, your church family, the wider world
- Earn the respect of those watching by your consistent godliness

In a world that chases more and more of everything, let's redirect that ambition. Let's pursue more and more holiness. More and more love. More and more integrity.

Because **God's will is your holiness**. That's what we should be doing more and more.
