# Romans 10:1-21

**Title:** The Response of Man  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Challenge of Understanding God's Will and Human Responsibility

Paul opens this passage with a deep pastoral concern: "Brothers and sisters, my heart's desire and prayer to God for the Israelites is that they may be saved." This introduces a tension that runs through all of Scripture—the relationship between God's sovereign control and human responsibility. Last week we explored predestination; this week we examine how these two truths interact in the life of faith.

A Calvinist arrives at heaven's gates and sees two lines: one marked "predestined," the other "free will." He chooses the predestined line. An angel asks why, and he replies, "Because I chose it." The angel responds, "Then you should be in the free will line." Bewildered, he moves over, only to be asked again why he's there. This time he says, "Someone made me come here." The joke captures our confusion—but Scripture doesn't leave us in confusion. It calls us to understand both truths.

## Part One: God's Sovereign Work (Romans 10:1-13)

### The Problem: Self-Reliance Instead of God-Reliance

Paul could testify about the Israelites: "They are zealous for God, but their zeal is not based on knowledge. Since they did not know the righteousness of God and sought to establish their own, they did not submit to God's righteousness."

The Jews relied on their own effort. They thought salvation was *their* work to accomplish. They understood the law—Moses had given it to them—but they misunderstood its purpose. As Moses wrote, "The person who does these things will live by them." This sounds like good news until you realize the condition: if you choose to live by the law, you must follow it *perfectly*. Which is impossible.

We still fall into this same trap. We think we can climb our way to God through our own goodness—being a good parent, a good child, giving to the poor, paying our taxes to the dollar. We imagine that God will look at the little good we do and simply overlook our sins. But our sins are too great for Him to ignore. We cannot earn our way to heaven.

### The Solution: Christ's Complete Work

"Christ is the culmination of the law so that there may be righteousness for everyone who believes." He did it all on the cross. There is no excuse for the Israelites—or for us—not to follow Jesus.

Here Paul reapplies words from Deuteronomy about the accessibility of God's law: "Do not say in your heart, 'Who will ascend into heaven?' (that is, to bring Christ down) or 'Who will descend into the deep?' (that is, to bring Christ up from the dead)."

The point is profound: we don't need anybody to ascend to heaven to bring Christ down—He came willingly. We don't need anybody to descend into the deep to bring Christ up from the dead—He rose by His own power. God has made every effort to reveal Himself. Two thousand years ago, He appeared in the flesh. And if someone says, "I'd believe if God would just show Himself to me"—He did. He appeared. There are records for those who came after.

> "The Word became flesh and made his dwelling among us. We have seen his glory, the glory of the One and Only, who came from the Father, full of grace and truth." (John 1:14)

### The Response Required: Simple Faith

> "The word is near you; it is in your mouth and in your heart, that is, the message concerning faith that we proclaim: If you declare with your mouth, 'Jesus is Lord,' and believe in your heart that God raised him from the dead, you will be saved." (Romans 10:8-9)

God is revealed to us in His word. We do nothing except respond by confessing that God is sovereign over salvation—that He is Lord. 

Some people say, "I want the real thing. I want God to appear to me personally." But God is not a comfort toy we command to appear whenever we feel doubt. He is God of the universe. He has showed up. If you missed it, that's okay—you have the records, which are just as good and far cheaper than the original experience. Taylor Swift appeared at a concert; some saw her, some didn't have the money or time. She didn't fly across the world just for those who missed out. They have the CDs instead. It's not the same, but it's still her songs. God has made His word available to all. Don't complain—the word is near you.

> "Anyone who believes in him will never be put to shame. For there is no difference between Jew and Gentile—the same Lord is Lord of all and richly blesses all who call on him, for, 'Everyone who calls on the name of the Lord will be saved.'" (Romans 10:11-13)

The gospel is brought to you by Christ alone. He initiated His coming, His sacrifice, His resurrection. He was in control through all of it. Salvation is near, and everything in the gospel has been handled for us—even the translation and packaging of Scripture. Let us confess Christ as Lord and be thankful.

## Part Two: Human Responsibility (Romans 10:14-21)

### The Apparent Contradiction: Are We Really Responsible?

The concern about predestination is fatalism: the belief that since all events are predetermined, there's no point in making any decisions. If God has already decided who will believe, why evangelize? Why pray? Why do anything?

Yet the strange thing is that God does not expect us to be fatalistic. He expects action from us.

### Prayer as Human Responsibility

Paul begins this section by stating his own practice: "My heart's desire and prayer to God for the Israelites is that they may be saved." He prays. He cares. He intercedes.

### Evangelism as Human Responsibility

> "How, then, can they call on the one they have not believed in? And how can they believe in the one of whom they have not heard? And how can they hear without someone preaching to them? And how can anyone preach unless they are sent?" (Romans 10:14-15)

The logic is clear: you can only call on God if you believe in Him, you can only believe if you hear, you can only hear if someone preaches, and someone preaches only if they are sent.

Therefore, we are to send and preach! "How beautiful are the feet of those who bring good news!" It's striking that feet—something we don't normally admire about people—become an object of admiration here because these feet bring the good news of salvation.

We are responsible for evangelism. We are the beautiful feet that invite others to know Christ. We bring them to church, we baptize them, we go and speak to them in our schools, our workplaces, our neighborhoods.

### Our Responsibility for Our Own Salvation

But not all the Israelites accepted the good news. Isaiah cries out, "Lord, who has believed our message?" Yet faith comes from hearing, and the message is heard through the word about Christ. The Israelites had every opportunity to hear—"Their voice has gone out into all the earth, their words to the ends of the world." They understood the message, but they rejected it. They were disobedient and obstinate, stubborn in their refusal. And so God rightly judges them.

Our responsibility today is the same: hear and obey.

## Part Three: God's Sovereignty and Human Responsibility (The Integration)

### They Are Not Contradictory

In the Bible, God's predestining sovereignty is never pitted against human will. This is not a contradiction. God determines the future *through* our free-willing action. Our actions are compatible with God's actions.

It's not 50% God and 50% man. It's 100% God and 100% man.

God controls us through our free decisions. His control is so great that it encompasses the real and valid will of a person. His control is so powerful that we don't even feel it.

### The Pavlov Illustration

Consider Ivan Pavlov's famous experiment. He presented dogs with a ringing bell followed by food. The food naturally caused the dogs to salivate, and after repeated pairings, the bell alone caused salivation. Who was controlling the salivation—the scientist or the dog?

On one hand, the scientist was in control—he designed the experiment and determined when the bell rang and when the food came. On the other hand, the dog freely and willingly salivated. Both were in control. The scientist controlled the dog *through* the dog's free will. If we can control animals this way, surely God has a unique way whereby He controls through our free-making decisions.

### The Prime Example: Christ's Death

The perfect example is Christ's death. Peter preaches at Pentecost:

> "This man was handed over to you by God's set purpose and foreknowledge; and you, with the help of wicked men, put him to death by nailing him to the cross." (Acts 2:23)

Who is responsible for Christ's death? God was sovereign—"This man was handed over to you by God's set purpose and foreknowledge." Yet *you* put Him to death by nailing Him to the cross. Both are true. God is in control, using our free-will decisions.

### How This Works Practically

In normal life, we don't worry about God's sovereign will. We don't know everything about His plans. I don't know how long I will live. I don't know if it will rain next week. And it's not our job to figure this out.

What we do worry about are the things that have been revealed to us already—the things God has made clear in His word. This word tells us that He is Lord. This word tells us to repent. This word tells us how we should be making good decisions about how to live. We are told to live godly lives and to use our feet and mouths to tell the good news.

Under God's sovereignty, we have to make good decisions. Things might not turn out how we planned. But if we have made godly decisions and taken godly action, then we rest the results with God.

### Three Levels of God's Will

To understand this, imagine a three-tiered diagram:

1. **God's mysterious will** (upstairs): His pre-ordained plans and purposes that we don't know down to their last detail. This realm is unknown to us.

2. **God's will known in hindsight**: We understand God's purposes after the fact, like understanding the meaning of September 11 years later.

3. **God's revealed will**: What God has actually made known to us through Jesus Christ and His word—His character, His moral will, warnings of the end, and the knowledge that He came near and left His word for us.

Because the first two are either mysterious or known only in hindsight, for all practical purposes, we downstairs carry out our day-to-day activities without needing to reference those plans. We can't anticipate what we don't know. But we're not acting blindly. We're armed with the information we have—God's revealed will—so we continue to act wisely and faithfully.

### Freedom From Fatalism

There is no room for fatalism because we have to make real and valid decisions every day. The decision to be a Christian is a real one, made without access to detailed knowledge of whether you're among God's elect. You find that out in hindsight. If you want to know whether it's God's will for you to be a Christian, become one, and in hindsight you'll know that God indeed wanted you to be. Similarly, you discover it's God's will for you to marry someone *after* you get married.

### How Sovereignty Encourages Evangelism

Rather than discouraging evangelism, believing that God is truly sovereign is actually an encouragement to evangelism. Paul writes:

> "Therefore I endure everything for the sake of the elect, that they too may obtain the salvation that is in Christ Jesus, with eternal glory." (2 Timothy 2:10)

Election is Paul's guarantee that there will be some success in his evangelism. He knows that among the people he speaks to, some will be God's elect, and they will believe and be saved. It's like someone inviting you to go fishing and saying, "I guarantee you'll catch some fish—they're hungry and waiting."

When we believe that the response of those who hear is in God's hands, we're free to present the gospel clearly and faithfully. We're not tempted to manipulate or preach merely for a response. We're not tempted to think that special techniques or methods are the key to conversion. We don't know who out there God has elected. We simply operate based on God's revealed will—He wants people to be saved. He wants beautiful feet to go out.

Some of you are entering new seasons: new school, new university, new grade, new work, new friends. You have a hundred names of people to systematically get to know and to find opportunities to tell the good news. Out of all that, bring at least one friend to church this year. And pray for all hundred.

### Prayer Is Not Fatalistic

Someone might ask, "If God will accomplish His purpose, does it matter if I pray?" True, God will accomplish His purpose without your prayer. But how do you know He doesn't plan to accomplish His purpose *through* your prayer for it?

We don't know what we don't know. So let's instead listen to His instruction to pray for your friend. In the coming age, your friend who becomes a Christian will say, "Yes, God ordained it," but also "downstairs, you prayed for my salvation and God heard it." God will make a difference through you.

## Conclusion

It's incredibly comforting to know that God is in charge of people's salvation. If you're one of those who keeps telling people about Jesus, if you're one of those who keeps praying for people, and you see very little fruit—please keep it up. It's much better to put your hope in the predestining God than in the choices of man.

Your hardened parents, sibling, spouse, friends—you've had fights that ended in tears because they refuse. If it were all about them, they would never change. I wouldn't trust them to. But instead, pray to God who is able to change them. Pray that He will answer your prayers. Pray that His election would include these people you care about. Only God can change them.

How great is it when that person whom we'd almost dismissed as too hard, too cool, too bored—they visited youth group once and didn't enjoy it at all. We had no more hope. But some kept praying and trying. And miraculously, a year later, she returns, shows interest, gets her questions answered, and wants to live God's way. That's not human effort, friends. That's all God doing.
