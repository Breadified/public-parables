# Zephaniah 1:1-3:20

**Title:** Judgment Day

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Part 1: The Punishment for Idolatry (1:1-13)

### A Sweeping Judgment

God announces a judgment of cataclysmic proportions, recalling the time of creation's reversal—but worse than even the flood of Noah's day.

> "I will sweep away everything from the face of the earth," declares the LORD. "I will sweep away both men and animals; I will sweep away the birds of the air and the fish of the sea. The wicked will have only heaps of rubble when I cut off man from the face of the earth," declares the LORD. (Zephaniah 1:2-3)

This is a judgment on all of creation—the fifth and sixth days of creation undone. No escape, no refuge. Even the fish of the sea will not be spared.

### Jerusalem Under Judgment

The judgment focuses directly on Jerusalem and Judah. The northern kingdom is already gone; now the capital itself faces destruction. The invaders from the north—Babylon—will leave no place untouched.

> "On that day," declares the LORD, "a cry will go up from the Fish Gate, a wailing from the New Quarter, and a loud crash from the hills. Wail, you who live in the market district; all your merchants will be wiped out, all who trade with silver will be ruined. (Zephaniah 1:10-11)

This judgment is personal and invasive. God will search Jerusalem with lamps, finding and punishing the complacent—those who assume the Lord will do nothing, either good or bad.

> At that time I will search Jerusalem with lamps and punish those who are complacent, who are like wine left on its dregs, who think, "The LORD will do nothing, either good or bad." (Zephaniah 1:12)

Even the royal household will not escape:

> On the day of the LORD's sacrifice I will punish the princes and the king's sons and all those clad in foreign clothes. On that day I will punish all who avoid stepping on the threshold, who fill the temple of their gods with violence and deceit. (Zephaniah 1:8-9)

The consequences are utter devastation: wealth plundered, houses demolished, vineyards planted but their fruit never enjoyed—a curse of futility.

### The Root Sin: Idolatry

The reason for this sweeping judgment is clear: idolatry and practical atheism.

> "I will stretch out my hand against Judah and against all who live in Jerusalem. I will cut off from this place every remnant of Baal, the names of the pagan and the idolatrous priests—those who bow down on the roofs to worship the starry host, those who bow down and swear by the LORD and who also swear by Molech, those who turn back from following the LORD and neither seek the LORD nor inquire of him. (Zephaniah 1:4-6)

The people worship Baal and Molech, and worse, they swear by the LORD while simultaneously swearing by other gods. They have become practical atheists—people who claim faith but live as though God is irrelevant.

This speaks directly to our own time. We live as though judgment day is not coming. We prepare for so many other things—our studies, our careers, our futures—yet we neglect to prepare ourselves spiritually or to warn those we love. How foolish to assume we have unlimited time when Scripture clearly declares otherwise.

### A Call to Silent Expectation

By the time this word comes, there is no remedy left. All Judah can do is fall silent in dreadful expectation:

> Be silent before the Sovereign LORD, for the day of the LORD is near. The LORD has prepared a sacrifice; he has consecrated those he has invited. (Zephaniah 1:7)

This is God's word to Zephaniah during the reign of King Josiah—a good king who brought reforms. Yet even Josiah's revival could only postpone the judgment. The hope now lies not in the king, but in the remnant—those who will survive and be restored.

The world's days are numbered. This is not speculation or prophecy from false sources. It is here in Scripture, written long ago, waiting to be believed.

## Part 2: The Emotional Reality of Judgment (1:14-3:7)

### The Dread of That Day

Having revealed the sweeping scope of judgment, Scripture now turns to its emotional and spiritual reality. This is not merely physical destruction—it is the experience of those facing God's wrath.

> "The great day of the LORD is near—near and coming quickly. Listen! The cry on the day of the LORD will be bitter, the shouting of the warrior there. That day will be a day of wrath, a day of distress and anguish, a day of trouble and ruin, a day of darkness and gloom, a day of clouds and blackness, a day of trumpet and battle cry against the fortified cities and against the corner towers. I will bring distress on the people and they will walk like blind men, because they have sinned against the LORD. Their blood will be poured out like dust and their entrails like filth. Neither their silver nor their gold will be able to save them on the day of the LORD's wrath. In the fire of his jealousy the whole world will be consumed, for he will make a sudden end of all who live on the earth." (Zephaniah 1:14-18)

This paints a graphic picture of siege warfare—the ultimate helplessness. The imagery recalls the fall of Jerusalem to Babylon and similar catastrophes recorded in Jeremiah and Ezekiel. Material wealth offers no protection. There is only distress, despair, and the knowledge that one has sinned against the Lord.

Many of us have tasted small versions of this—the weight of regret, the despair that seems inescapable, the feeling of being trapped in a hole with no way out. But judgment day is the permanence of that darkness. Hell is not merely physical torment; it is the spiritual reality of loneliness, bitterness, regret, and separation from God's presence—forever.

### The Call to Seek Refuge

Yet before the judgment falls completely, there is a window of time—brief and urgent—for repentance and seeking the Lord:

> Gather together, gather together, O shameful nation, before the appointed time arrives and that day sweeps on like chaff, before the fierce anger of the LORD comes upon you, before the day of the LORD's wrath comes upon you. Seek the LORD, all you humble of the land, you who do what he commands. Seek righteousness, seek humility; perhaps you will be sheltered on the day of the LORD's anger. (Zephaniah 2:1-3)

This echoes Jesus's teaching in Matthew 5: seek first the kingdom of God. There is urgency here. The day comes swiftly, like chaff blown away. But the humble—those who do what the Lord commands and pursue righteousness—may find shelter in His anger.

### Nations Under Judgment

God's judgment extends beyond Judah to the nations surrounding her. Gaza, Ashkelon, Ashdod, Ekron—the Philistine cities—will be abandoned and destroyed. The lands will become pasture for shepherds and sheep, eventually belonging to the remnant of Judah.

> Gaza will be abandoned and Ashkelon left in ruins. At midday Ashdod will be emptied and Ekron uprooted... The land by the sea, where the Kerethites dwell, will be a place for shepherds and sheep pens. It will belong to the remnant of the house of Judah; there they will find pasture. In the evening they will lie down in the houses of Ashkelon. The LORD their God will care for them; he will restore their fortunes. (Zephaniah 2:4-7)

Moab and Ammon, who have insulted Israel and made threats against her, will become like Sodom and Gomorrah—places of desolation and wasteland forever. Yet the remnant of God's people will inherit what was lost.

> "I have heard the insults of Moab and the taunts of the Ammonites, who insulted my people and made threats against their land. Therefore, as surely as I live," declares the LORD Almighty, the God of Israel, "surely Moab will become like Sodom, the Ammonites like Gomorrah—a place of weeds and salt pits, a wasteland forever. The remnant of my people will plunder them; the survivors of my nation will inherit their land." (Zephaniah 2:8-9)

This is God's justice: those who mock His people and defy His name will face His wrath. When He destroys all the gods of the land, the nations will recognize His awesome power and worship Him, each in their own land.

> The LORD will be awesome to them when he destroys all the gods of the land. The nations on every shore will worship him, every one in its own land. (Zephaniah 2:11)

Even mighty Assyria, the great northern power, will not escape:

> He will stretch out his hand against the north and destroy Assyria, leaving Nineveh utterly desolate and dry as the desert. Flocks and herds will lie down there, creatures of every kind. The desert owl and the screech owl will roost on her columns. Their calls will echo through the windows, rubble will be in the doorways, the beams of cedar will be exposed. This is the carefree city that lived in safety. She said to herself, "I am, and there is none besides me." What a ruin she has become, a lair for wild beasts! All who pass by her scoff and shake their fists. (Zephaniah 2:13-15)

Once proud, self-sufficient Nineveh becomes a desolate place where only wild beasts dwell. Her arrogance—"I am, and there is none besides me"—is her undoing.

### The City of Oppressors

Jerusalem itself, despite her covenant status, is condemned as a city of oppression and defiance:

> Woe to the city of oppressors, rebellious and defiled! She obeys no one, she accepts no correction. She does not trust in the LORD, she does not draw near to her God. Her officials are roaring lions, her rulers are evening wolves, who leave nothing for the morning. Her prophets are arrogant; they are treacherous men. Her priests profane the sanctuary and do violence to the law. (Zephaniah 3:1-4)

Yet in the midst of this corruption, God Himself remains righteous:

> The LORD within her is righteous; he does no wrong. Morning by morning he dispenses his justice, and every new day he does not fail, yet the unrighteous know no shame. (Zephaniah 3:5)

God is faithful and just, day after day, yet the unrighteous remain shameless, hardened in their rebellion. He has even cut off nations and demolished their strongholds to warn Jerusalem, yet she refuses to learn:

> "I have cut off nations; their strongholds are demolished. I have left their streets deserted, with no one passing through. Their cities are destroyed; no one will be left—no one at all. I said to the city, 'Surely you will fear me and accept correction!' Then her dwelling would not be cut off, nor all my punishments come upon her. But they were still eager to act corruptly in all they did." (Zephaniah 3:6-7)

## Part 3: Salvation and Restoration (3:8-20)

### Final Judgment and Purification

After pronouncing judgment on the nations and the city, Scripture moves to the ultimate judgment and what lies beyond it:

> "Therefore wait for me," declares the LORD, "for the day I will stand up to testify. I have decided to assemble the nations, to gather the kingdoms and to pour out my wrath on them—all my fierce anger. The whole world will be consumed by the fire of my jealous anger." (Zephaniah 3:8)

But judgment is not the final word. In that same judgment, God will purify His people:

> "Then will I purify the lips of the peoples, that all of them may call on the name of the LORD and serve him shoulder to shoulder. From beyond the rivers of Cush my worshipers, my scattered people, will bring me offerings." (Zephaniah 3:9-10)

This is the reversal of Babel—when God confused human languages. Here, in the age to come, He purifies the lips of the peoples so they speak one language: the name of the LORD. Those scattered across the earth will return as His worshipers, bringing offerings to Him.

### The Remnant Restored

The judgment clears away the proud and corrupt, leaving only the humble and faithful:

> "On that day you will not be put to shame for all the wrongs you have done to me, because I will remove from this city those who rejoice in their pride. Never again will you be haughty on my holy hill. But I will leave within you the meek and humble, who trust in the name of the LORD. The remnant of Israel will do no wrong; they will speak no lies, nor will deceit be found in their mouths. They will eat and lie down and no one will make them afraid." (Zephaniah 3:11-13)

What a beautiful vision: a people free from the burden of shame, the shame of past wrongs now removed. Only the meek and humble remain—those who trust in the LORD's name. The remnant is transformed. They no longer do wrong, speak lies, or practice deceit. They live in security and peace, unafraid.

### The Song of Restoration

The final passage is a song of joy and restoration, beginning with Zion herself:

> "Sing, O Daughter of Zion; shout aloud, O Israel! Be glad and rejoice with all your heart, O Daughter of Jerusalem! The LORD has taken away your punishment, he has turned back your enemy. The LORD, the King of Israel, is with you; never again will you fear any harm. On that day they will say to Jerusalem, 'Do not fear, O Zion; do not let your hands hang limp. The LORD your God is with you, he is mighty to save. He will take great delight in you, he will quiet you with his love, he will rejoice over you with singing.'" (Zephaniah 3:14-17)

Here is the heart of Kenny's pastoral warmth: the LORD takes great delight in His people. He quiets them with His love. He rejoices over them with singing. This is not a distant, impersonal God—it is the God who sings over His beloved people.

The burdens are removed:

> "The sorrows for the appointed feasts I will remove from you; they are a burden and a reproach to you. At that time I will deal with all who oppressed you; I will rescue the lame and gather those who have been scattered. I will give them praise and honor in every land where they were put to shame." (Zephaniah 3:18-19)

And the promise culminates in gathering and restoration:

> "At that time I will gather you; at that time I will bring you home. I will give you honor and praise among all the peoples of the earth when I restore your fortunes before your very eyes," says the LORD. (Zephaniah 3:20)

## The Complete Pattern

The book of Zephaniah holds together the full theology of God's judgment day:

- **Judgment** on idolatry and rebellion (chapters 1-2)
- **The emotional reality** of that judgment—despair and darkness (1:14-3:7)
- **Salvation and restoration** through the purification of the remnant (3:8-20)
- **Future glory** when God gathers His people, restores their fortunes, and sings over them with delight

This is not speculation. It is the word of the LORD, written for our instruction and our hope.
