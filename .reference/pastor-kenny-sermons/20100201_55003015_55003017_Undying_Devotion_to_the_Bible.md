## The Authority Question

In 1910, a small organization at Cambridge called CICCU (Cambridge Inter-Collegiate Christian Union) made a bold decision. Just 22 student leaders voted, with 17 in favor and 5 opposed, to disaffiliate from the SCM (Student Christian Movement). It seemed insignificant—200 students standing against an organization representing 150,000 worldwide. The world assumed CICCU would fade away.

But they had taken a stand over something fundamental: the authority of the Bible.

The nineteenth century had shaken evangelical Christianity. Scientific advances following the Enlightenment—Darwin, Galileo, Copernicus—combined with higher biblical criticism meant people were raising serious questions about whether the Bible was truly authoritative and inspired. Some Christians remained confident that orthodoxy could meet these challenges. Others became defensive, fearing all innovation threatened the historic gospel. Still others embraced the innovations wholesale.

CICCU chose to hold the line on biblical authority. The SCM preferred a less dogmatic approach, willing to suggest parts of the Bible might not be entirely true.

Today we face our own challenges. Post-moderns dismiss absolute truth altogether. Others claim certain passages are wrong—the patriarchal sections, apparent contradictions. But let's hear what Scripture itself says about its own nature.

## The Bible is God-Breathed

> 14 But as for you, continue in what you have learned and have become convinced of, because you know those from whom you learned it, 15 and how from infancy you have known the holy Scriptures, which are able to make you wise for salvation through faith in Christ Jesus. 16 All Scripture is God-breathed.

"God-breathed"—literally, God expired His breath into His word. This is not poetic language; it describes the origin of Scripture.

**God's Spirit and His Word are Inseparable**

God's spirit dwells in His word. His word carries His spirit. They are not separate things—there is no word without His spirit, no spirit without His word. This is why some charismatic confusion arises when we treat them as disconnected. Years ago, an American preacher joked that Sydney's theology amounts to "God the Father, God the Son, and God the Holy Scriptures." His joke, though meant sarcastically, isn't far from the truth. The Scripture is indeed called the "sword of the Spirit."

**This Authority is Living and Active**

The Bible's authority is not dead history. It is living, current authority that acts upon us. The word is not static information—it does things. It changes you. The historical nature of Scripture doesn't nullify its authority; rather, our authority rests in Jesus Himself, and Scripture provides the reliable record of what happened.

## Which Authority Rules Your Life?

When faced with conflicting claims, which authority do you ultimately follow? Christians typically appeal to several sources:

- **The Bible**
- **Experience** (personal feelings, what I've experienced)
- **Institution** (what the church or my leader tells me)
- **Rationalism** (what science says, what makes logical sense)
- **Pragmatism** (what works, what makes us grow)

We naturally draw on all of these in daily decisions. The question is: when they conflict, which one is in charge?

Consider the extremes:

- **Experience-centered:** "I felt the Holy Spirit move, so this must be true. I experienced a miracle, so this interpretation is correct." (Pentecostal churches often operate here)
- **Institution-centered:** "The church leadership tells me this is doctrine." (Roman Catholicism historically, where the Pope calls the shots)
- **Rationalism-centered:** "People don't rise from the dead, therefore the resurrection is false. Society was patriarchal then; we're more enlightened now, so those teachings don't apply." Culture becomes the judge.
- **Pragmatism-centered:** "This program works and attracts people. Who cares if it's heretical? We're growing and people are happy."

But as evangelical Christians—people of the gospel, people of the Word, people of the Bible—we must subordinate all other authorities to Scripture. Truth matters more than comfort or success.

## Application: Where Do You Stand?

Ask yourself honestly: Where do I prefer my authority to come from? Many of us are experiential people in this feeling-driven generation. Others lean rationalist and skeptical of anything unscientific. Some seek a higher authority because they feel lost.

But as Christians, we must align ourselves under the Bible's authority—**even when we don't like what it says**.

The temptation is to dilute Scripture according to our preferences in two ways:

**First, selective reading:** Taking only parts of Scripture we like, nullifying inconvenient passages through the other authorities.

**Second, the "balanced approach":** Consider Jesus's command: "Give all you have to the poor and come follow me" (Matthew 19:21). We find a middle ground between literal poverty and wealth. Or His teaching about cutting off your hand if it causes you to sin (Matthew 5:29)—we recognize this isn't literally about self-mutilation because Scripture as a whole speaks against such extremes.

But here's the uncomfortable truth: we're often just being selective through bias. We dismiss hard passages as "unbalanced," yet when Scripture says "support your family" or "work to support society," suddenly we treat it literally as though nothing else matters.

The Bible isn't a textbook offering balanced answers. It's narrative and letter. It's not scientific fact but persuasion—and persuasion works over time. When the whole of Scripture is properly expounded, it is indeed balanced, but that doesn't mean every individual passage should be soft-pedaled.

**Sit under Scripture's authority.** Don't assume you know what a passage means before considering its weight. If Jesus says cutting off your hand illustrates the seriousness of sin, take it seriously. You may not cut off your hand, but perhaps you'll cut off your television or internet—something you'd never do if you'd simply dismissed the text as "unbalanced."

Let God's authority shape your life, not the other way around.

## The Bible is Useful

> And is useful for teaching, rebuking, correcting and training in righteousness, so that the man of God may be thoroughly equipped for every good work.

Notice the four functions Scripture performs: teaching, rebuking, correcting, and training. These are not the effects of a novel or poem—you don't read Harry Potter and expect to be taught, rebuked, corrected, and trained in righteousness.

Scripture is functional. It's not merely telling a nice story; it's explaining God's mind and His vision for how things should be and how we should live today.

The language is distinctly authoritative. "Rebuking" carries weight. This isn't gentle suggestion—it's discipleship. A disciple (like a Shaolin monk or a student in "The Karate Kid") is trained systematically in a discipline. Scripture trains us in godliness, preparing us thoroughly for every good work.

---

## Application: Be Trained by Scripture

**First, reading Scripture is training—treat it like any discipline.** Training requires habit. It takes years. Young believers (Year 7s, new Christians) typically need five to ten years of serious, consistent engagement with Scripture to mature spiritually. Basically all of us need more of this training.

But understand what this training means: it's not like a life coach giving you tips to reach a goal you've already chosen. God's training involves God Himself telling you where to go. You sit under Scripture and allow it to reshape your desires and direction. This requires submission to it.

As Rev. G.T. Manley observed, "The difference is one of attitude to the Bible. It is not so much a question of the verbal inspiration or inerrancy of the Bible but of the deep conviction that the Bible is the word of God, and therefore true. The IVF goes to the Bible to be taught by it; the typical SCM attitude is to discuss it."

**Second, all our Bible study groups should be training groups.** Whether a Year 6-7 Bible study or a mature Christian group, the Bible should be taught and absorbed, not merely discussed.

**Third, as you are trained and convinced by God's word, do what it says.** Bear fruit in good works—ministry at church and beyond. Serve as servants of the Word by proclaiming it:

> In the presence of God and of Christ Jesus, who will judge the living and the dead, and in view of his appearing and his kingdom, I give you this charge: Preach the Word; be prepared in season and out of season; correct, rebuke and encourage—with great patience and careful instruction. (2 Timothy 4:1-2)

Preaching doesn't mean standing in a pulpit or a park. It means speaking about Christ in conversations, at home, whenever opportunity presents itself—in season and out of season.

## The Bible Makes You Wise for Salvation

> And how from infancy you have known the holy Scriptures, which are able to make you wise for salvation through faith in Christ Jesus. (2 Timothy 3:15)

Scripture's final purpose is not merely to improve this life but to make us wise unto salvation—unto the life to come.

The Bible tells us this story: Jesus is now resurrected and Lord. One day He will return to judge. We are in trouble because of our sins, and judgment is coming. But Jesus has paid for our sins through His death. Yes, there are historical questions and criticisms we must address, but Scripture answers them adequately. History is not a scientific document (and science itself, as current debates on climate change reveal, is not the infallible authority some imagine).

Scripture alone tells us this saving story. As the historic statement affirms:

> Holy Scripture containeth all things necessary to salvation: so that whatsoever is not read therein, nor may be proved thereby, is not to be required of any man, that it should be believed as an article of the Faith, or be thought requisite or necessary to salvation.

**Application for the non-Christian:** Get this right. Recognize the Bible's authority over your life and its usefulness in training you. It will make you wise unto salvation.

## Standing Firm When Pressure Comes

Remember: "Man does not live on bread alone, but on every word that comes from the mouth of God" (Matthew 4:4).

It is critical that we get this right in our heads now, because pressures will come—sooner or later—to downgrade the Bible. They may come through:

- **Experience:** A best friend or spouse's spiritual experience contradicts Scripture
- **Institution:** A leader or church you've trusted asks you to compromise biblical teaching
- **Pragmatism:** A new program is heretical but it works and excites people
- **Rationalism:** A claim "just doesn't make sense" by contemporary standards
- **Persecution:** External pressure to deny or abandon Scripture

When these moments come, we must be ready to:

- Stand against our best friend
- Leave an institution if necessary
- Abandon apparent success
- Defer judgment and seek further evidence
- Endure persecution

As one theologian noted:

> We must not be surprised that the problem of authority still divides Christian people. The deepest cleavages in Christendom are doctrinal, and the deepest doctrinal cleavages are those which result from disagreement about authority.

## Handling Biblical Difficulties

Higher criticism raises real questions: How do we handle inconsistencies in Scripture? Several approaches exist:

- **Traditional:** Read the literature as a whole; apparent inconsistencies smooth out when understood in context
- **Source criticism:** Inconsistent sections were inserted later
- **Redaction criticism:** Inconsistent elements were added for a theological purpose
- **Literary criticism:** Rather than debate what counts as inconsistency, simply read the literature as an integrated whole

The path forward isn't to panic or dilute our confidence in Scripture. Be patient. Yes, problems exist, but don't sell out the Bible trying to solve them. Be confident that the word of God never fails. It is God-breathed.

## The CICCU Legacy

This is what CICCU did in 1910. They held the line on biblical authority despite pressure, despite being the smaller group, despite the world's opposition.

Their disaffiliation directly led to the formation of the Inter-Varsity Fellowship (with Oxford), which became the Universities and Colleges Christian Fellowship (UCCF). By 1947, UCCF became a founding member of IFES (International Fellowship of Evangelical Students), from which your AFES group descends.

The contrast between the two organizations remains instructive:

**UCCF (continuing CICCU's legacy):**
- Clear doctrinal statement about what it believes
- Focus: building individual faith, outreach, and evangelism

**SCM (continuing the other direction):**
- No doctrinal statement
- Focus: encouraging exploration of faith without definitive commitment

Put bluntly: IFES continues to proclaim God's word and call for repentance. SCM invites people to sit around a candle and chat about whether the Bible is true.

## A Call to the Next Generation

To everyone here, but especially university students: you inherit a legacy purchased at real cost. Previous generations stood against the watering down of Scripture's authority. They stood despite pressure, despite being outnumbered, despite the world's mockery.

So be confident in the Bible. And when it's your turn, stand up.

That is undying devotion to the Bible.
