# Mark 3:7-34

**Title:** Jesus—Fad, Mad, Bad, or Family?  
**Date:** 29 June 2003  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Why Do Crowds Gather?

Think about the different crowds we see in life—at sporting events, end-of-year sales, school assemblies, concerts. Each gathers for a different reason. I love sales because, like most Malaysians, I enjoy a good bargain (even if I end up buying things I don't need rather than what I actually need). At sales, you'll find people hunting for deals, others shopping for genuine needs, and some looking for opportunities to shoplift in the chaos.

In Mark 3, we see crowds gathering around Jesus too—but for vastly different reasons. As we'll discover, people approached Jesus thinking he was a Fad, or that he was Mad, or that he was Bad. But there's a fourth option: Jesus as Family.

## Jesus Is NOT a Fad (3:7-12)

> Jesus withdrew with his disciples to the lake, and a large crowd from Galilee followed. When they heard all he was doing, many people came to him from Judea, Jerusalem, Idumea, and the regions across the Jordan and around Tyre and Sidon. There were so many people that Jesus asked for a small boat to be ready for him, to keep people from crowding him. For he had healed many, so that those with diseases were pushing forward to touch him.

The crowds weren't gathering for a sale. They came because Jesus was performing miracles and healing diseases. They wanted the spectacular. They were there because Jesus was the latest fad.

Ironically, the only ones who truly recognized who Jesus was were the evil spirits. Whenever they saw him, they fell down before him and cried out: "You are the Son of God!" Yet the crowds only saw him as a miracle worker. They were like shoppers caught up in a sale, not knowing what's actually good but following the crowd because everyone else is.

### Understanding Fads

A fad is something that comes and goes. It's popular one day, a social outcast the next. People can't get enough of it until suddenly no one cares anymore. I remember when yoyos were all the rage in my youth. I saved my money, learned the tricks, and spent weeks trying to master "walking the dog." Finally, after all that work, I could do it—only to discover that by then, no one cared anymore. It was out of fashion.

The problem with following Jesus because of popularity is that it doesn't last. Later in Mark, we see a huge crowd welcoming Jesus into Jerusalem, thinking he's a super king. But when he starts talking about death, the crowds fade away. Once the spectacular is gone, so is their interest.

### The Danger for Us

Following Jesus because it's a fad won't last. Jesus demands loyalty—24/7, not just when it's popular or convenient. Like soldiers in an army, we don't get to change our minds when things get tough. The army has no use for fashion trends; they keep the same uniform because consistency matters.

Many so-called Christians follow the latest charismatic leader or attend churches where spectacular things happen—spectacular signs, spectacular music, spectacular shows. But they're following because of the spectacular, not because of Jesus. When the spectacular fades (and it will), they fall away.

I wonder if we at Fungus face a different danger. Being a Christian here is cool—we have games, songs, cool leaders, and all our friends are here. But will we still be Christians when we graduate from high school? When we change churches? When there are fewer games, fewer songs, fewer attractive people, fewer friends? What about places where it's hard to be a Christian—like school? Will we stay loyal to Jesus then?

**The call is clear: Stay loyal through the tough times, like a good friend would.**

## Jesus Is NOT Mad (3:20-21)

> Then Jesus entered a house and again a crowd gathered, so that he and his disciples were not even able to eat. When his family heard about this, they went to take charge of him, for they said, "He is out of his mind."

From the family's perspective, Jesus looked mad. He had gathered a posse roaming around Judea. There were rumors of a cult forming. People were coming from everywhere. The Herodians and Pharisees had even conspired together to kill him. How could their son—just a carpenter—cause such chaos? And he wasn't even eating properly! His family wanted him to stop this silliness, come home, and get his life back to normal.

It's like parents following a young athlete around. If he's being overworked, skipping meals, and facing media pressure, of course they'd want to take him home and get him back on track.

### The Real Misunderstanding

But here's the problem: Jesus's own parents and siblings misunderstood his task. Taking him home wasn't the solution. Jesus is the unexpected Son of God—not just a miracle worker or a good role model of love. He has announced that the Kingdom of God is near. The time has come. "Repent and believe the good news."

He hasn't come to physically heal everyone, start a political career, launch a clothes label, or run a fish-sticker factory. The cross is not a fashion accessory. He has come to die for sins. And we think that's mad?

People misunderstand the gospel today, thinking it's about morals—a list of dos and don'ts. No. The gospel is the good news that Jesus has paid for our sins so that God can forgive us.

## Jesus Is NOT Bad (3:22-30)

> The teachers of the law came down from Jerusalem said, "He is possessed by Beelzebub! By the prince of demons he is driving out demons."

Jesus isn't mad. He knows exactly what he's doing. In just five chapters, he'll begin his road to the cross. This is his time to preach and do it earnestly, driven by the task before him. But the teachers of the law—the supposed experts—come with a new accusation: Jesus is bad. He's evil. He's possessed by demons.

Why did they say this? They'd seen the healings. They couldn't deny that a real power was at work. But they knew something else: if they acknowledged that Jesus's power came from God, they'd have to acknowledge him as God's representative. So instead, they admitted he had power but claimed it came from the devil.

### Jesus's Response

It's like someone losing to us on an exam but refusing to admit it. Instead, they say, "You must have cheated. You must have paid the teacher." That's what the Pharisees did—they refused to acknowledge Jesus's power as coming from God.

Jesus's reply is devastating:

> How can Satan drive out Satan? If a kingdom is divided against itself, that kingdom cannot stand. If a house is divided against itself, that house cannot stand. And if Satan opposes himself and is divided, it cannot stand; his end has come.

When Baghdad was being bombed, no one thought Saddam was bombing his own city. Of course not—those were enemy planes! When Satan's kingdom is being bombed by demon expulsion, it's not Satan doing it; it's Satan's enemy—Jesus. This isn't a civil war; it's Jesus destroying Satan. Jesus is stronger than Satan himself.

The statues of Saddam were taken down, the trappings of power removed, the presidential palace taken over, the prisoners set free. Not only is Jesus not of Satan, but he brings about Satan's end.

The Pharisees were completely wrong about Jesus. They pointblank refused to recognize him for who he was—even when it was obvious. Driving demons out of people is God's work, not Satan's.

### The Unforgivable Sin

Jesus concludes with words of sobering seriousness:

> I tell you the truth. All the sins and blasphemies of men will be forgiven them. But whoever blasphemes against the Holy Spirit will never be forgiven; he is guilty of an eternal sin.

Blaspheming against the Holy Spirit isn't just swearing or thinking bad things about the Spirit. Jesus is saying that rejecting the Son of God is unforgivable. The Pharisees were guilty of this because they accused Jesus of having an evil spirit—the ultimate rejection of God's own power working through his Son.

This rejection is eternal and never forgiven. I know someone who became a Christian largely because of this verse. He was so worried about committing an eternal sin by rejecting the Son of God—rejecting the very means for forgiveness—that he came to church on a Monday afternoon (not his usual habit) to find out how to get into God's family.

**Therefore, do not reject him. Get right with God today.**

## Jesus IS Family (3:31-35)

> Then Jesus's mother and brothers arrived. Standing outside, they sent someone in to call him. A crowd was sitting around him, and they told him, "Your mother and brothers are outside looking for you." Who are my mother and my brothers? Then he looked at those seated in a circle around him and said, "Here are my mother and my brothers. Whoever does God's will is my brother and sister and mother."

It's the end of the day. A bunch of people are sitting around Jesus. His parents finally arrive and want to take him home. And in that moment, Jesus redefines what family means.

His true relatives are no longer bound by blood or cultural ties. The connection to Jesus is bigger than family. Those who are truly in his family are those who do his will—those who respond with repentance and obedience to his word.

### The Real Distinction

Crowds gather for all sorts of reasons. The people who followed for his healings, his parents who followed his progress wanting to take him home, the teachers of the law watching for him to stumble—all of these could call themselves followers of Jesus. But they were not true followers.

Jesus is not a fad. Your family doesn't come and go. They're there forever. We are called to be loyal to Jesus's family in good times and bad.

Jesus is not mad. Our brother Jesus gave his life for us—not on some crazy man's quest, but because it was the only way. We are to accept that his way is the only way to God.

Jesus is not bad. On the cross, Jesus brings us into fellowship with God. He continues to look out for our good. So do not reject him. If you're not in his family, you're in a desperate situation—so get into God's family! And if you are in God's family, the sign that you're really in it is if you obey his will.

In the end, Jesus's true family consists of those he has called, who come to him, listen to him, and do his will. They obey him.
