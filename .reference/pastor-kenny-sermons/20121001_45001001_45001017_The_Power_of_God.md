# Romans 1:1-17

**Title:** The Power of God

**Date:** 21 October 2012 (Reformation Sunday)

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## The Gospel's Power Unveiled

On Reformation Day—31 October 1517—a monk named Martin Luther nailed 95 theses to a church door in Wittenberg, Germany, protesting certain Catholic practices of his time. What might seem like a solitary act sparked what we know as the Protestant Reformation. Thanks to the printing press, those theses spread virally across Europe within weeks—a remarkable feat in an age without modern communication.

What drove Martin Luther to this bold action? He had been reading the Book of Romans.

During the Middle Ages, few people had direct access to Scripture. Only educated monastics like Luther—a trained lawyer—could study the Bible. When Luther encountered Romans, he was transformed by what he discovered. In his preface to his Romans commentary, he wrote:

> This letter is truly the most important piece in the New Testament. It is purest Gospel. We find in this letter the richest possible teaching about what a Christian should know: the meaning of law, Gospel, sin, punishment, grace, faith, justice, Christ, God, good works, love, hope and the cross.

Luther concluded that every Christian should make Romans "the habitual and constant object of his study." The verses that struck him most profoundly were Romans 1:16-17, which opened his eyes to the true nature of God's righteousness and sparked the reformation of Christianity itself.

## The Gospel Is Powerful (v. 16)

> For I am not ashamed of the gospel, for it is the power of God for salvation to everyone who believes, to the Jew first and also to the Greek.

The gospel simply means "good news"—from the Old English words "good spell." Paul uses it here to refer not to the four biographies of Jesus or to a musical style, but to the substance of the Good News itself.

### Jesus: The Powerful Descendant

What makes the gospel powerful? First, consider its subject—Jesus Christ. Paul tells us:

> Concerning his Son, who was descended from David according to the flesh.

Jesus possessed legitimate royal power through His bloodline. David, the great king of Israel about 1000 years before Jesus, was the subject of ancient prophecy:

> Then a throne will be established in steadfast love, and on it will sit in faithfulness in the tent of David one who judges and seeks justice and is swift to do righteousness. (Isaiah 9:7)

Through His mother Mary, Jesus inherited David's royalty via Nathan (Luke's genealogy). Legally and through inheritance, He came as the legitimate heir of David's power—not as an illegitimate pretender, but as the rightful king. This matters: real power comes from legitimate authority.

### Jesus: The Eternal King

But Jesus differs radically from all earthly kings: He rose from the dead.

> And was declared to be the Son of God in power according to the Spirit of holiness by his resurrection from the dead.

Here lies the revolutionary difference. Great leaders of the past—whether school principals, sports captains, or business titans—eventually die. Lee Kuan Yew transformed Singapore, yet his death raises questions about succession. Bill Gates built Microsoft. Steve Jobs created Apple. Michael Jordan dominated basketball. But all such greatness is temporary. The kingdoms they built falter when they depart.

Jesus is different. He alone is "the Son of God in power"—a king who will not die, who will not retire, who cannot be replaced. His power isn't borrowed or time-limited. His resurrection proves He is the eternal king whom the Old Testament prophets anticipated.

Real transformative power isn't merely being great; it's being great forever. Only the living Christ can offer this.

### Jesus: The Savior

Because Jesus is an eternal king, He alone can truly save. He will never grow tired or abandon His people. He will never retire or become mortal. His reliability and dependability are absolute.

Dead prophets and dead gods cannot help us. Only the living King—the one who conquered death itself—possesses genuine power to save us. The cross, as the symbol of Christ's sacrifice, points to His resurrection as the ultimate triumph. This is why the gospel has power: it rests on the only savior who can never fail.

## The Gospel Is Revealed (v. 17)

> For in it the righteousness of God is revealed from faith for faith, as it is written, The righteous shall live by faith.

What makes the gospel powerful is not only that Christ is powerful, but that His power fulfills God's ancient promises.

### God Keeps His Promises

Paul reminds us:

> Which he promised beforehand through his prophets in the holy Scriptures.

The gospel wasn't invented by Paul. It was promised 4,000 years ago. Isaiah spoke of a new David arising from Jesse's stump—a new king who would rescue Israel. Jeremiah foretold that this king would transform His people by giving them a new heart. When we read the Old Testament, we must continually ask: How does this passage point forward to Jesus? The entire Old Testament is good news because it all points to Christ.

Why does this matter? Because it demonstrates that God keeps His promises. He has the power to keep them, and He desires to keep them. If we can see God fulfilling ancient promises in history, we can trust Him with future promises.

This is why faith is possible. Faith isn't believing something untrue. Faith is believing in something proven true in the past so we can trust the future. Because God's past promises have been fulfilled, we can have confidence that His future promise—to save us when judgment comes—will also come true. We can trust God's Word. We can be confident in His love even through hardship and trouble.

The righteousness of God—His perfect standards and His faithful character—is revealed through the gospel. This discovery transformed Martin Luther and sparked the Reformation.

## Don't Be Ashamed (v. 16)

> For I am not ashamed of the gospel.

Paul calls Christians to radical confidence: don't be ashamed of the gospel.

Yet often we are. We may think:

- The gospel isn't powerful enough to change anything
- We lack Paul's calling or authority to evangelize
- The gospel sounds foolish or outdated
- People will reject us
- We might wreck someone's life
- We don't have the right to tell others
- We'll come across as judgmental

### Paul's Example: Unashamed Boldness

But look at Paul himself. He sees his calling clearly:

> Paul, a servant of Christ Jesus, called to be an apostle, set apart for the gospel of God. Through whom we have received grace and apostleship to bring about the obedience of faith for the sake of his name among all the nations.

At his conversion, the Lord told him:

> Go, for he is a chosen instrument of mine to carry my name before the Gentiles and kings and the children of Israel. For I will show him how much he must suffer for the sake of my name. (Acts 9:15-16)

Paul lived for this task. Notice how he approaches the Roman church—people he had never met:

> I have often intended to come to you (but thus far have been prevented), in order that I may reap some harvest among you as well as among the rest of the Gentiles. I am under obligation both to Greeks and to barbarians, both to the wise and to the foolish. So I am eager to preach the gospel to you also who are in Rome.

Paul is remarkably upfront. He doesn't pretend he's just "hanging out" with these Roman believers. He explicitly states his purpose: to encourage them spiritually, yes, but also to "reap a harvest"—to see people come to faith. Some might say he has "ulterior motives," yet he's completely unashamed about it. He's even coming to ask them for financial support for his mission to Spain. He could have hidden this agenda, but he doesn't. Paul is transparent about why he wants to build relationships with them.

### Rethinking Friendship

This raises an uncomfortable question: How do we reconcile Paul's transparency with the Christian virtue of genuine friendship?

Many of us worry that having an evangelistic motive somehow taints a friendship. We think we need to be "best friends" first—building a deep, purely relational bond—before we can ever mention Jesus. Only then would it feel authentic.

But consider this honestly: Where do most of our friendships come from? We have friends because we study the same course and help each other. We have friends because we enjoy the same food or music. We have friends because we work in the same place or worship in the same church. We bond over shared interests, shared circumstances, shared purposes.

This isn't wrong. It's simply how human relationships work. Most genuine friendships exist around some common agenda or shared context. This doesn't make them less real or less valuable than the imaginary friendship where two people sit in a room together with no purpose at all—a friendship that rarely exists except perhaps with parents or spouses.

Paul understands this. He's not pretending to be someone's best friend before mentioning Jesus. He's being transparent: "I care about your eternal destiny. That's why I want to know you. That's why I'm writing to you."

What Paul models is this: We can and should be upfront about our faith without pretending friendship exists where it doesn't. We love people not because we share a hobby, but because judgment day is coming and only Jesus can save them. We have the purest motivation of all—their eternal wellbeing. There is nothing shameful about this. This is not a hidden agenda; this is what we live for.

### Living Without Shame

So what does it look like to not be ashamed of the gospel?

- When you meet someone for the first time, seize the opportunity: "What a wonderful chance to meet you. I'd love to tell you about Jesus sometime."
- When you organize a gathering: "I've arranged this party because I want to share the gospel with you."
- When you're building a friendship: "I'm genuinely interested in knowing you so I can tell you about God."
- When years have passed: "I've known you a long time but never shared what I believe about life and death. Can you give me a few minutes?"

Yes, it's awkward. Yes, some relationships may become strained. But consider the cost: a little awkwardness—or even a lot—is a small price to pay for being used by God to witness to His power. If the rest of the party is uncomfortable, or if the bus ride becomes tense, it's worth it.

For students involved in campus Christian groups, think strategically about mission and O-week. Your Facebook friends are your campus. You are called to tell them the truth.

Telling others about Jesus isn't optional—it's integral to the Christian calling. Paul's apostolic role was unique, but we're called to emulate his unashamed boldness in some measure.

## The Source of Our Confidence

Why can Paul be so bold? He explains:

> For in it the righteousness of God is revealed from faith for faith, as it is written, The righteous shall live by faith.

Paul shows why the gospel is powerful:

- Jesus is the promised Christ, descended from David
- Jesus is the eternal Christ through His resurrection
- God has kept His promise and will not fail us in the future

We know the gospel works because it is God Himself acting in history to restore us to Him. God became human in Jesus to bear the punishment we deserve. The gospel is the story that God loves you.

Those transformed by it know it works. It changes people from hopelessness to hope, from directionlessness to purpose, from fear of the future to confidence. I remember when Romans challenged me—amid tears—to surrender my petty ambitions and work for the gospel instead. That same transforming power has affected believers across centuries and continents.

The gospel passed from Jesus to Paul, was obscured during the Middle Ages, was rearticulated during the Reformation, reached England and reformed the Church of England, came to Australia in 1788 through the Anglican church, and now—200 years later—it impacts you. This is powerful.

Confidence is essential. In economics, consumer confidence drives growth. In parenting, when children believe they'll fail, they stop trying. In the Christian life, we need confidence in the gospel's power.

We have been given that confidence. So it's time for others to know this gospel too.

## The Dual Nature of Jesus: Why He Can Save

For Jesus to save us, He must be both fully human and fully God.

**He is human** so that He can identify with us. We understand God when He speaks our language and takes our form. More importantly, because humans sinned, humans must bear the punishment. Jesus is the suitable replacement—only like can redeem like. As C.S. Lewis wrote: "The Son of God became a man that men might become sons of God."

**He is God** because only divinity has the power to save. Since sin is fundamentally against God, God Himself must bear the penalty of forgiveness. Any other arrangement would make an innocent third party the victim—which is precisely the problem with Jehovah's Witnesses theology.

Consider a judge whose own child has committed a crime:
- To dismiss the sentence would be unjust
- To carry out the sentence would destroy his child
- To punish an innocent third party would be unfair

There is a fourth option: The judge pronounces the sentence, steps down from the bench, and says: "Punish me instead."

This is what our sovereign God did. He stepped down from His throne, became the man Jesus, and bore the punishment we deserve. This is the gospel that saves.
