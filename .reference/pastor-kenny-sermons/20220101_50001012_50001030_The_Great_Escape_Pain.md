# Philippians 1:12-30

**Title:** The Great Escape: Pain
**Date:** 16 January 2022
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Understanding Our Relationship with Pain

We live in pain. All of us experience it—whether physical, emotional, or spiritual. The natural human response is to escape it, to find relief, to avoid suffering at all costs. But Paul's letter to the Philippian church offers us a radically different perspective on how to relate to pain.

Pain itself is a biological signal: our nervous system fires when tissue is damaged, sending messages through our nerves to alert us to danger. But pain is far more complex than mere biology. Modern science reveals that pain involves memory, emotion, expectation, environment, and social context. A man who stepped on a nail felt agony until it was revealed he hadn't actually been injured. Children who are told they have control over their pain feel less of it than those who are told they have none. Your friends' pain tolerance influences your own. Pain is deeply conditioned and socialized—it's not just physical sensation, but a perception shaped by everything around us.

### The Two Paths: Escape or Endure

**Escape from Pain (Philippians 1:12-13, 22-23)**

Paul writes from prison in Rome—a cold, dark, damp place where he suffered hunger, disease, and mistreatment. He faced multiple afflictions: arthritis, possibly epilepsy, vision problems so severe he wrote in large letters. His body bore the scars of stoning, shipwreck, and constant danger (2 Corinthians 11:23-28). The pain was the story of his life.

Yet instead of seeking escape, Paul faced an agonizing dilemma:

> "What shall I choose? I do not know! I am torn between the two: I desire to depart and be with Christ, which is better by far." (1:23)

Paul was tempted to escape through death—to confess falsely to the magistrate and receive swift Roman execution, ending his misery. We understand this impulse. We would prefer to escape pain. We seek therapeutic relief, drugs, psychological comfort—anything to avoid suffering. In our desperation, we've even reshaped our entire lives around pain avoidance: abandoning relationships to avoid heartbreak, refusing risk to avoid failure, isolating ourselves to avoid illness.

But Paul made a choice that seems foolish to our ears.

**Tolerating Pain for Greater Purpose (Philippians 1:24-26, 1:20-22)**

Instead of departing, Paul chose to remain:

> "But it is more necessary for you that I remain in the body. Convinced of this, I know that I will remain, and I will continue with all of you for your progress and joy in the faith, so that through my being with you again your boasting in Christ Jesus will abound on account of me." (1:24-26)

Paul chose to endure his suffering because others needed him. His presence mattered more than his comfort. This wasn't masochism—he didn't seek pain for its own sake. Rather, he recognized that serving others sometimes requires sacrifice. Sometimes the good we're called to do involves pain.

What's remarkable is Paul's theological perspective: from God's point of view, whether he lived or died, it made no difference. 

> "I eagerly expect and hope that I will in no way be ashamed, but will have sufficient courage so that now as always Christ will be exalted in my body, whether by life or by death." (1:20)

To live is to serve Christ. To die is to gain eternal rest. Both glorify God equally. But we often reverse this truth. We think: "Since I have my eternal insurance with God, let me gain everything now—wealth, security, influence, fame." We treat this life as our opportunity to maximize personal gain. How different this is from Christ's example, who saw this life not as a time to gain, but to give.

The call is to discern what is best for the gospel and the welfare of others. This requires that we care about people's future salvation, not just our present comfort.

## Finding Joy Through Pain

**The Advancement of the Gospel (Philippians 1:12-18)**

How did Paul manage to endure such suffering? He changed his mind about pain. He found joy through it.

> "Now I want you to know, brothers and sisters, that what has happened to me has actually served to advance the gospel. As a result, it has become clear throughout the whole palace guard and to everyone else that I am in chains for Christ." (1:12-13)

Paul's suffering drew attention. His chains became a testimony. People saw his sacrifice and were moved.

> "And because of my chains, most of the brothers and sisters have become confident in the Lord and dare all the more to proclaim the gospel without fear." (1:14)

Paul's faithfulness under suffering emboldened others. If he could maintain his faith in prison, surely they could find courage to speak the gospel without fear.

More remarkably, Paul even found joy in rivalry. Some preachers in Rome were proclaiming Christ out of envy and selfish ambition, wanting to cause him trouble while he languished in chains. Others preached out of genuine love and partnership. But Paul's response was extraordinary:

> "It is true that some preach Christ out of envy and rivalry, but others out of goodwill... But what does it matter? The important thing is that in every way, whether from false motives or true, Christ is preached. And because of this I rejoice." (1:15-18)

Instead of bitterness, Paul saw expansion of the gospel. Rather than seeing competitors as enemies, he recognized that the message was advancing regardless of their motives. He transformed his losses into advancement—physical limitation became spiritual victory, moral opposition became opportunity.

This reveals Paul's formula for joy despite setbacks: he looked beyond his immediate circumstances to see how God was working through them. Like playing a video game where obstacles and enemies are actually clues to discover, bosses to conquer. Pain is built into life. We learn to overcome through it, not by avoiding it.

### Paul's Motto: "To Live is Christ, to Die is Gain"

> "For to me, to live is Christ and to die is gain." (1:21)

This is Paul's fundamental perspective. To live means fruitful labor for Christ—proclaiming his word, encouraging the church, teaching the next generation, being an example. To die means final relief from pain and the joy of beholding Christ. Both outcomes serve God's purposes.

But we reverse this motto. We live as if "to die is Christ, to live is gain"—as though heaven is our insurance policy and earth is our opportunity to accumulate. This fundamentally misunderstands the Christian life.

**Finding Joy in Deliverance (Philippians 1:18b-20)**

Paul's deepest confidence came from looking beyond his present suffering:

> "Yes, and I will continue to rejoice, for I know that through your prayers and God's provision of the Spirit of Jesus Christ what has happened to me will turn out for my deliverance." (1:18b-19)

This echoes Job's confidence after losing everything: "This will turn out for my deliverance" (Job 13:16). Both men refused to curse God despite overwhelming suffering, and both were ultimately vindicated.

Paul's concern wasn't primarily for his own release. Rather, he worried about whether he had looked out for God's interests:

> "I eagerly expect and hope that I will in no way be ashamed, but will have sufficient courage so that now as always Christ will be exalted in my body, whether by life or by death." (1:20)

"Not be ashamed" meant he would not be a coward, would not abandon Christ or the church, would maintain his faith whatever came. God would ensure that everything turned out right—if not in this life, then in the next.

## Raising Our Pain Threshold

Modern science reveals something crucial: **pain tolerance can be increased.**

Research shows that athletes who regularly experience pain—contact sport players, for instance—have significantly higher pain tolerance than sedentary people. Regular exposure to manageable pain increases our capacity to bear it. Physical activity, especially aerobic exercise, raises pain tolerance and decreases pain perception.

Children taught they have control over their pain experience less of it. Those surrounded by anxious, fearful language experience more. A baby who hears the calm indifference of a parent to a minor bump continues playing; one who hears anxious concern cries in response to the parent's anxiety, not the pain itself.

Pain is socialized and conditioned. Our friends' pain tolerance influences our own. The language we use shapes our experience: calling something "degenerative" sounds catastrophic, but our bodies are always changing under various stresses. We live in an imperfect world where pain is inevitable.

Our current crisis has revealed how much our pain tolerance has been shaped by fear. During a pandemic, we became desperate to avoid all discomfort—lockdowns, isolation, abandoning community connection. But this created a vicious cycle: we became more anxious, which lowered our pain threshold, which made us more fearful.

## Living Worthy of the Gospel

Paul's ultimate call to the Philippians is this:

> "Whatever happens, conduct yourselves in a manner worthy of the gospel of Christ. Then, whether I come and see you or only hear about you in my absence, I will know that you stand firm in the one Spirit, striving together as one for the faith of the gospel without being frightened in any way by those who oppose you." (1:27-28)

And then, the remarkable privilege:

> "For it has been granted to you on behalf of Christ not only to believe in him, but also to suffer for him, since you are going through the same struggle you saw I had, and now hear that I still have." (1:29-30)

It is the Christian's privilege to suffer for Christ. Hard to believe in our safe spaces, but this is the reality for believers throughout history and across the world even today.

## The Call to Courage

You are stronger than you think. Daniel Scali, who suffers from complex regional pain syndrome—a condition where his brain sends wrong signals causing pain from any touch, movement, wind, or water—set a world record for planking, holding it for 9 hours and 30 minutes. He used his pain to defeat his pain.

This is our calling: not to escape pain, but to endure it with purpose. Not alone, but together. We need each other so that we can survive the pain. We encourage one another by reminding each other that we are stronger than we think—that we have the courage to face whatever comes.

The questions God will ask us are these: During these difficult years, what did you do for God's mission? How did you look after your church? Did you conquer your fears, or did you succumb to them?

Our answer should be: "I did all I could. I stood firm. I was not ashamed."
