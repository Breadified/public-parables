# Ecclesiastes 1:1-18; 12:8-14
**Title:** The Limits of Wisdom
**Date:** 2 November 2014
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Search for Meaning

We face an ambitious task—exploring the meaning of life itself. This question has haunted humanity since we became conscious of our mortality and gazed at the stars. We ask: What's the point of life? Where am I going? Is there a purpose to anything I do?

This is the search for meaning, and we need to find it or we cannot truly live. Viktor Frankl, a Jewish psychologist who survived the Nazi concentration camps, observed that those who survived best were those who found meaning. Even in the worst suffering imaginable, meaning enabled people to endure. Frankl believed that people are primarily driven by a "striving to find meaning in one's life," and this sense of meaning determines whether we survive or perish.

But Ecclesiastes has much to say about this search. The book opens and closes with the same refrain, and today we'll look at the beginning, middle, and end to get the full picture.

## Meaninglessness (Ecclesiastes 1:1-18)

Ecclesiastes is a difficult book. There are debates about when it was written, who wrote it, and its poetic style. Most challenging, it presents a deeply pessimistic view of life.

> "Meaningless! Meaningless!" says the Teacher. "Utterly meaningless! Everything is meaningless."
>
> What does man gain from all his labor at which he toils under the sun?

The key word is **meaninglessness** (Hebrew: *hevel*). It appears 38 times throughout Ecclesiastes. The term literally means ephemeral, temporary—like breath or mist that vanishes. In Isaiah, we see this same word connected to worthlessness and falsehood. But in the context of human toil, *hevel* means unsatisfying, futile, senseless, purposeless, unfulfilling—even absurd.

So the answer to the Teacher's question is stark: Man seems to gain nothing.

### Observation of Nature's Cycles

The meaninglessness is evident in the immutable character of the natural world. Look at what the Teacher observes:

> Generations come and generations go, but the earth remains forever.

People die. Individuals, families, tribes, civilizations—all come and go. Think of the great civilizations of South America—the Incas, the Aztecs. Their stone returns to dust. Jungles grow over their ruins. Rain erodes them back to the state they once occupied. Yet the earth remains.

> The sun rises and the sun sets, and hurries back to where it rises.
> The wind blows to the south and turns to the north; round and round it goes, ever returning on its course.
> All streams flow into the sea, yet the sea is never full. To the place the streams come from, there they return again.

Vast activity, yet essentially nothing changes. We are small, insignificant creatures who make no tangible difference. The world goes up and down without the slightest reference to us.

> All things are wearisome, more than one can say. The eye never has enough of seeing, nor the ear its fill of hearing.

As a result:

> What has been will be again, what has been done will be done again; there is nothing new under the sun.
>
> There is no remembrance of men of old, and even those who are yet to come will not be remembered by those who follow.

Despite tremendous activity in all aspects of life, nothing ultimately changes. There is no contribution that can be made to the world, nothing new that anyone can offer. Humanity as a whole cannot profit from the toil of the individual.

The old understand this better than the young. They have seen one generation grow up and another generation pass. They see the cycles repeat. They recognize the weary patterns—the same mistakes, the same struggles, the same outcomes—and it is wearisome. Like painting the Sydney Harbour Bridge: clean off the rust, repaint it, and once finished, start over again. Where is the satisfaction?

> What is twisted cannot be straightened; what is lacking cannot be counted.

Sin has bent the world out of shape. It cannot be fixed. A kink in a pipe, a fold in cardboard, a dent in a car—these still function, but imperfectly. This is what sin has done to the world. And this is true even for the wisest person alive.

> I thought to myself, "Look, I have grown and increased in wisdom more than anyone who has ruled over Jerusalem before me; I have experienced much of wisdom and knowledge." Then I applied myself to the understanding of wisdom, and also of madness and folly, but I learned that this, too, is a chasing after the wind. For with much wisdom comes much sorrow; the more knowledge, the more grief.

## Can Wisdom Help Us? (Ecclesiastes 7:11-29)

The Teacher turns to chapter 7 as his search for meaning continues. Solomon was king and he sought wisdom—the best way he knew how.

> Wisdom, like an inheritance, is a good thing and benefits those who see the sun.
> Wisdom is a shelter, as money is a shelter, but the advantage of knowledge is this: that wisdom preserves the life of its possessor.

**Wisdom is good.** It benefits. It protects. It preserves life. Wisdom leads to life; folly leads to death.

Yet Solomon also notices something troubling. Wisdom only applies to "those who see the sun"—only while we are living. In the short term, wisdom is superior to folly. But long term, there are no guarantees. There is no remembrance of old men (1:11).

> Consider what God has done: Who can straighten what he has made crooked?
> When times are good, be happy; but when times are bad, consider: God has made the one as well as the other. Therefore, a man cannot discover anything about his future.

Life seems random. A righteous man perishing in his righteousness, and a wicked man living long in his wickedness. What's the point of being wise if outcomes are so random?

> Do not be overrighteous, neither be overwise—why destroy yourself? Do not be overwicked, and do not be a fool—why die before your time? It is good to grasp the one and not let go of the other. The man who fears God will avoid all extremes.

Wisdom is not sufficient to solve the riddle. Solomon's conclusion is stark:

> All this I tested by wisdom and I said, "I am determined to be wise"—but this was beyond me.
> Whatever wisdom may be, it is far off and most profound—who can discover it?

The implication is clear: no one can find the ultimate wisdom about life.

Yet the only thing Solomon finds is sin:

> I find more bitter than death the woman who is a snare, whose heart is a trap and whose hands are chains. The man who pleases God will escape her, but the sinner she will ensnare.

In his systematic search, adding one thing to another to discover the scheme of things, Solomon finds that the truly righteous person is rarer than rare—one upright man among a thousand. But not one upright woman among them all. The point is not about gender—it is that he finds practically no one.

> This only have I found: God made mankind upright, but men have gone in search of many schemes.

**Sin.** Men have gone off in search of many schemes. We don't find wisdom. We find sin. And this sin has twisted the world so that it doesn't work like it's supposed to.

> For the creation was subjected to frustration, not by its own choice, but by the will of the one who subjected it, in hope that the creation itself will be liberated from its bondage to decay.

As a result, all human endeavor is subjected to the same air of futility:

> I have seen all the things that are done under the sun; all of them are meaningless, a chasing after the wind.

## The Beginning of Wisdom (Ecclesiastes 12:8-14)

Back to the third person. We return to where we started:

> "Meaningless! Meaningless!" says the Teacher. "Everything is meaningless!"

Yet something remarkable follows:

> Not only was the Teacher wise, but also he imparted knowledge to the people. He pondered and searched out and set in order many proverbs. The Teacher searched to find just the right words, and what he wrote was upright and true.
>
> The words of the wise are like goads, their collected sayings like firmly embedded nails—given by one Shepherd.

Notice something profound: the epilogist confirms that the Teacher was wise. This is striking because in chapter 7, the Teacher himself said wisdom was "beyond me" and "far off and most profound—who can discover it?"

What happened? I believe the Teacher, by accident, acknowledged what is truly wise. He recognized the truth:

> Fear God and keep his commandments, for this is the whole duty of man.

Fear God. It's the beginning of wisdom, but it is also the end of wisdom. Fear is not the answer to every question, but in the final analysis, we cannot find perfect answers to all our questions. Fear the Lord. This puts us in our place, and all other fears, hopes, and admirations in their place.

> For God will bring every deed into judgment, including every hidden thing, whether it is good or evil.

This is impossible to say from an "under the sun" perspective. Yet it is the root reason why it is wise and good for man to fear God and keep His commandments. There is an eternal accounting for everything we do. This means that everything has meaning and importance, both for the present and for eternity. If God cares as much as this, nothing can be pointless.

Amazingly, in these last days, God has revealed true wisdom to us:

> For the message of the cross is foolishness to those who are perishing, but to us who are being saved it is the power of God. For it is written: "I will destroy the wisdom of the wise; the intelligence of the intelligent I will frustrate."

By revelation, through Jesus, we learn that the world is not eternal. It will end. But those who believe in the message of the cross are saved by the power of God.

God came in the form of a man to redeem us through the cross. According to conventional wisdom, this is foolish. How can someone dying save anyone? Yet in God's wisdom—not man's—this is His wise plan. The righteous died for sins once for all, for the unrighteous, to bring us to God.

Jesus is perhaps the one out of a thousand—the truly wise one—for He feared God and obeyed His commands wholeheartedly.

This is the conclusion of wisdom: repent and believe in Jesus Christ. Fear God and keep His commandments. In doing so, we find not just meaning, but redemption.
