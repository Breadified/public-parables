# Acts 3-4:32

**Title:** The Gathering of All
**Date:** November 20, 2013
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Temple Gate Beautiful: A Healing and Its Meaning

### The Context of Neglect

Peter and John were going up to the temple at the time of prayer—the three o'clock hour. Outside the Beautiful Gate sat a man crippled from birth, carried daily to beg from those entering the temple courts. The irony is sharp: the temple, adorned with silver and gold, overlooks the suffering at its very gate.

This reflects a deeper tragedy. God had commanded in Deuteronomy that there should be no poor among His people, that the Israelites should be openhanded and generous. Yet the temple—rebuilt under Herod's massive expansion project, funded by heavy taxes on the local Jewish population—had become a monument to human pride rather than a house of mercy. The silver and gold that should have cared for the poor were instead poured into perpetuating Herod's name for eternity.

The man had been crippled for forty years—long enough that everyone in Jerusalem knew him. No one could claim this healing was a recent problem or a deception. When Peter and John approached, the beggar didn't even look up; his condition had made begging so routine that he expected nothing.

### The Healing

Peter commanded, "Look at us!" The man obeyed, expecting money. Instead, Peter said: "Silver or gold I do not have, but what I have I give you. In the name of Jesus Christ of Nazareth, walk." 

Immediately, the man's feet and ankles became strong. He jumped to his feet, walked, and went with them into the temple courts, walking and jumping and praising God. The people who saw him recognized him as the same beggar from the Beautiful Gate, and they were filled with wonder and amazement.

## Understanding Miracles: Signs, Not Mysteries

The biblical terms for miracles reveal something crucial. In Acts, we see "wonders and miraculous signs"—the word "miraculous" is added to the term "signs" because we have become wedded to the idea that miracles mean the breaking of natural laws to demonstrate God's existence.

But this misses the point entirely. God is not distant or absent, turning on "autopilot" while the world runs itself. Rather, He is actively sustaining all things: every star, every comet, every blood cell moving by His direction. Matthew 10:29-31 tells us that not one sparrow falls outside His care, and the very hairs of our head are numbered. If God is sovereign over such small things as hair growth and skin replication, then everything—healing from cancer or from a cold, even how bruises fade and eczema persists—all of this is under His providential care.

If this is true, then the word "miracle" as something special and exceptional becomes redundant. Everything is a miracle. The better term, as the Bible uses it, is *sign*—something that points to revelation and to God's unfolding purposes.

### Signs Point to Fulfillment

Signs are not meant to prove God's existence (everything does that). Rather, they point to a new revelation from God, marking something significant in salvation history. This healing of the lame man was such a sign, fulfilling the prophecy of Isaiah 35: "Then will the lame leap like a deer, and the mute tongue shout for joy." The healing declared that the last days had come—the time of restoration had arrived.

But once a sign has been given and its revelation received, it need not be repeated. We do not need another healing to tell us what the first already revealed. Christ has come. Salvation history is established. The revelation is complete. Whatever strange things occur in our world today—whether in quantum mechanics or unexpected recoveries—they are not signs pointing to new revelation. We know God exists through the historical reality of Jesus and His resurrection.

### Responding to Personal Experiences

When friends claim to have experienced healing or felt God's presence in unusual ways, what should we say? This is delicate ground. Personal experiences are deeply subjective and difficult to discuss. If someone believes they've been healed, it would be wrong and cruel to demand proof like some cynical skeptic.

The truth is, all healing comes from God—whether dramatic or ordinary. God heals us from colds just as He may bring remission from cancer. In that sense, it doesn't matter whether we can measure or verify it. We can rightly thank God for His providential care.

But when experiences begin to claim a significance beyond providence—when people insist this is a special sign, a new word from God, an indication of what God is doing today—we must gently ask: if all events reflect God's care, what makes this particular experience significant? A coincidence is not a sign simply because it seemed timely. A déjà vu, though psychologically interesting, is morally neutral.

The danger comes when we seek assurance through experiences rather than through God's word. We want to prove God's existence and reality by what happens to us. But that is backwards. We know God exists and is active, not through our healings, but through the historical reality of Jesus Christ. Signs and wonders have been accomplished by Jesus and confirmed by His disciples. The revelation is finished. There is no sequel to the gospel story except the final great day of judgment.

## Peter's Proclamation: The Sign Points to Jesus

The healing became an occasion for Peter to speak the words of God. Peter, "filled with the Holy Spirit," addressed the crowd: "Men of Israel, why does this surprise you? Why do you stare at us as if by our own power or godliness we had made this man walk?"

Peter's message was unmistakably about Jesus:

> The God of Abraham, Isaac and Jacob, the God of our fathers, has glorified his servant Jesus. You handed him over to be killed, and you disowned him before Pilate, though he had decided to let him go. You disowned the Holy and Righteous One and asked that a murderer be released to you. You killed the author of life, but God raised him from the dead.

Yet Peter held out a second chance to the Jewish people: "Repent, then, and turn to God, so that your sins may be wiped out, that times of refreshing may come from the Lord."

The sign of the crippled man's healing pointed to a new era of restoration. And just as the prophecy said the lame would walk, so now the gospel—no longer confined to the gate but moving through Jerusalem—would "walk" to Judea, Samaria, and the ends of the earth. It is no longer the gates that are beautiful; it is the feet of those who bring good news.

## The True Experiences of Christianity

What should Christians expect to experience? Not primarily miracles, but three things: persecution, fellowship, and generosity.

### Persecution

The priests, the temple guard, and the Sadducees came and seized Peter and John, disturbing because the apostles were proclaiming in Jesus the resurrection of the dead. They were imprisoned overnight. Yet even this became an opportunity to speak: "Judge for yourselves whether it is right in God's sight to obey you rather than God. For we cannot help speaking about what we have seen and heard."

Paul writes in 2 Timothy 3:12: "Everyone who wants to live a godly life in Christ Jesus will be persecuted." This is not a promise of comfort, but a clear-eyed reality. Those who oppose Jesus cannot touch Him directly, so they target His people. When believers face rage and ridicule—whether regarding sexuality, religious conviction, or the very existence of God—understand that this rage is ultimately against Christ, not against us. We should not be surprised.

Yet there is also courage. The Sanhedrin, looking at Peter and John, "realized that they were unschooled, ordinary men, and they took note that these men had been with Jesus." They could not deny the healing. They could not punish the apostles because all the people were praising God. And the apostles left, not discouraged, but emboldened to continue speaking what they had seen and heard.

### Fellowship

The believers "were one in heart and mind." They "devoted themselves to the apostles' teaching and to the fellowship, to the breaking of bread and to prayer." Daily they met in the temple courts, broke bread in their homes, and ate together "with glad and sincere hearts, praising God and enjoying the favor of all the people."

Though persecution comes, believers are not alone. There is a body of people watching our backs, encouraging us, praying with us. This is the comfort of Christian fellowship—intentional, regular, rooted in the apostles' teaching and prayer. In seasons of rest, this fellowship deepens. We are called to devote ourselves to it, to encourage one another, to be present in one another's lives.

### Generosity

The early church demonstrated radical generosity: "No one claimed that any of his possessions was his own, but they shared everything they had." Those who owned lands or houses sold them and brought the money to the apostles' feet to be distributed to anyone who had need. "There were no needy persons among them."

This is not advocating socialism, but rather a lightness with possessions. When we treat our things as gifts to be shared rather than treasures to be hoarded, we open ourselves to opportunities for kindness and gospel proclamation. A shared car, shared tools, helping a neighbor in need—these humble acts often create doorways to speak of Christ.

Yet generosity must be wise. Not all charity equally helps—some funds sit unused, some aid reaches those better off than we think. True generosity looks close to home: to schools that need support, to churches, to neighborhoods. It means asking where real need exists and meeting it personally, relationally, in ways that provide genuine help and create authentic opportunities to share the gospel.

At the end of Acts 4, we meet Joseph, a Levite from Cyprus called Barnabas—"Son of Encouragement." He sold a field he owned and brought the money to the apostles' feet. This is remarkable: a Levite, who was not supposed to own land, doing so—a picture of one repentant Jew reversing the waste of Herod's temple. His generosity embodied the teaching.

## The Gift of the Holy Spirit

The Spirit comes upon believers in distinct ways throughout Acts, and understanding these distinctions prevents confusion.

The baptism in the Spirit—the pouring out of the Spirit at Pentecost and subsequent outpourings—is an emphatic, visible demonstration that the eschatological gift of the Spirit has arrived. These are dramatic, public events marked by wind and fire.

But there is also the filling with the Spirit—a different word entirely, describing temporary bursts of inspired proclamation. Peter was "filled with the Spirit" when standing before the Sanhedrin. The church was "filled with the Spirit" when they prayed after Peter and John's release, and "they spoke the word of God boldly."

And there is the indwelling of the Spirit—the normal gift to believers at conversion, distinct from the dramatic baptism or outpouring. Believers receive the Spirit to dwell within them, transforming hearts and minds.

### Pentecost and the Giving of the Law

In the Old Testament, three great festivals required all Israelite males to travel to Jerusalem: Passover (remembering deliverance and the lamb), the Feast of Tabernacles (remembering wilderness provision), and Pentecost (commemorating the giving of the Law at Sinai).

The prophets spoke of the day when the law would go forth from Zion—not written on stone, but inscribed on hearts. Isaiah and Micah both prophesied this reversal. At Pentecost, God gave the Spirit—fulfilling Joel's prophecy of outpouring and the Baptist's prophecy of Spirit-baptism.

As Hebrews 10:15-17 says:

> The Holy Spirit testifies to us about this. First he says: "This is the covenant I will make with them after that time, says the Lord. I will put my laws in their hearts, and I will write them on their minds. Then he adds: Their sins and lawless acts I will remember no more."

The old covenant written on tablets of stone gave way to a new covenant written on hearts by the Spirit. The gospel alone generates the motivation necessary for obedience. Legalism never works—only the good news of Christ's death and resurrection can transform a heart to love God's law.

### The Gospel: Death and Resurrection

Peter proclaimed the gospel simply and clearly: God raised Jesus from the dead, "freeing him from the agony of death, because it was impossible for death to keep its hold on him." David prophesied this resurrection, yet David himself remains in the grave. But Jesus was raised and exalted to the right hand of God, where He received from the Father the promised Holy Spirit and poured it out on all who believe.

When the people heard this, they were "cut to the heart and said to Peter and the other apostles, 'Brothers, what shall we do?'" Peter replied: "Repent and be baptized, every one of you, in the name of Jesus Christ for the forgiveness of your sins. And you will receive the gift of the Holy Spirit."

About three thousand were baptized that day. They devoted themselves to the apostles' teaching, to fellowship, to the breaking of bread, to prayer. And the Lord added to their number daily those who were being saved.

## The Power of the Gospel

Signs validate the one who speaks for God. A prophet's claim to speak God's words must be tested, as Deuteronomy 18:15-20 commands. Miracles are the credential. When Elijah called down fire from heaven at Mount Carmel, his authority was vindicated.

But the greater authority is the gospel itself—the word of God concerning Christ's death and resurrection. This word, spoken boldly and faithfully, is the power of God for salvation. The Spirit fills believers with courage to proclaim it. And through this proclamation, God gathers His people—not through the wonders that authenticate the messenger, but through the message itself: that Jesus died for our sins and rose again, and all who turn to Him in repentance and faith receive forgiveness and the gift of the Spirit.

This is the gathering of all—Jew and Gentile, male and female, rich and poor—into the body of Christ. This is the true experience of Christianity: proclamation sustained by persecution, fellowship, and generosity, empowered by the indwelling and filling of the Holy Spirit, centered always on Jesus.
