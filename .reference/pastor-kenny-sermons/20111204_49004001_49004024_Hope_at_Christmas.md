# Ephesians 4:1-24

**Title:** Hope at Christmas
**Date:** December 4, 2011
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## 1. Called to One Hope

> There is one body and one Spirit—just as you were called to one hope when you were called—one Lord, one faith, one baptism; one God and Father of all, who is over all and through all and in all. (Ephesians 4:4-6)

The apostle Paul emphasizes the unity of believers through the repetition of "oneness" in these verses. The components of this one hope are:

- **One Lord**: Jesus Christ
- **One faith**: Faith in this Lord
- **One baptism**: The shared experience of repentance
- **One God**: The Father over all

Hope here means far more than wishing for something. It is a confident anticipation of the future—a future where Jesus Christ will reign as the sole ruler, where justice flows from one God. This one hope produces one body and one spirit among all who share it.

### The True Nature of Unity

Many human attempts at unity fail—the Olympics, the United Nations, the World Council of Churches, the European Union—because they lack a common, transcendent hope. Christians, by contrast, are united not through organizational structures but through the gospel itself.

**Unity is not expressed merely by gathering together.** Yes, we gather to read Scripture, know one another better, and encourage perseverance in Christ. But gathering alone does not create unity. If people in a room don't share the one Lord, faith, and hope, they remain disunited no matter how long they spend together. Conversely, Christians separated geographically but sharing the same Lord and resurrection hope remain genuinely united.

Our unity is grounded in the next life, in our shared purpose of seeing people saved, and in our alignment with God's purposes now. We're united because we share the same resurrection hope and the same call.

### Unity Requires Honest Conflict

True unity is not expressed through mere tokens of peace or by maintaining a facade of agreement. Cultures that value honor and shame often discourage open disagreement, creating "cold wars" where tensions simmer beneath polite surfaces. When conflict finally erupts, people say, "You should have known."

**True unity requires bringing issues into the light.** Following Matthew 18, if you believe you're right, speak directly to the person involved. If the matter is minor, forgive and let it go rather than spreading it around. If you're wrong, apologize and confess your sin. If you're mediating, work to bring reconciliation—don't remain passive.

This kind of honest engagement helps us sharpen one another so that we truly embrace the same faith, the same Lord, and the same repentance.

## 2. The Order of Salvation: Christ's Role and Jewish Mission

> To each one of us grace has been given as Christ apportioned it. This is why it says: "When he ascended on high, he led captives in his train and gave gifts to men." (Ephesians 4:7-8)

Paul draws on Psalm 68 to explain how the different races now share one hope. In that psalm, God comes to Jerusalem as a conqueror, receiving gifts from the nations and granting gifts so that the Gentiles might dwell with Him. The psalmist envisioned a day when distant kingdoms would bring tribute and all the earth would sing God's praise.

When Paul quotes this passage in Ephesians, he shifts the emphasis slightly. Instead of the nations *giving* gifts to God, Paul says Christ *gives* gifts to men—even to the rebellious. Christ ascended specifically to empower both Jews and Gentiles to participate in His kingdom.

### The Gifts Christ Gave

> He gave some to be apostles, some to be prophets, some to be evangelists, and some to be pastors and teachers, to equip his people for works of service, so that the body of Christ may be built up until we all reach unity in the faith and in the knowledge of the Son of God and become mature, attaining to the whole measure of the fullness of Christ. (Ephesians 4:11-13)

Traditional interpretation sees apostles and prophets as foundational offices of the early church, with evangelists and pastor-teachers continuing into the present age. But contextually, these leadership gifts serve a particular purpose: to prepare God's people—the Jewish Christians—to be beacons to the Gentile world.

### God's Strategy: From the One to the Many

God's historical plan reveals a deliberate narrowing before expansion:

- **Promise to Abraham**: Blessing to all nations flows through one man
- **Israel chosen**: Not for power, but precisely because they were small and weak, demonstrating God's strength
- **Israel's failure**: As a collective, Israel failed to declare God's praises to the nations
- **The One Jew**: Jesus emerged as the true Israelite, the fulfillment of Israel's calling

For 2,000 years, Scripture focuses intently on Israel, often passing over the mighty civilizations of the world—China's dynasties, Rome's power—to follow the Jewish story. Why? Because God's plan of redemption centered on this one nation and ultimately on one person: Jesus Christ.

Jesus is the ultimate:
- Apostle (the sent one)
- Prophet (God's spokesman)
- Evangelist (proclaimer of good news)
- Pastor and teacher (chief shepherd and rabbi)

Now, both Jews and Gentiles depend entirely on Him. Neither group possesses the advantages that made them "superior"; both are saved solely through Christ.

## 3. Therefore, Walk with Each Other

> As a prisoner for the Lord, I urge you to live a life worthy of the calling you have received. Be completely humble and gentle; be patient, bearing with one another in love. Make every effort to keep the unity of the Spirit through the bond of peace. (Ephesians 4:1-3)

Having established that Jews and Gentiles now share one hope, Paul urges the Gentile believers to *walk* (live out daily) a life worthy of their invitation to join God's people. This walking together requires:

- **Complete humility and gentleness**
- **Patience**
- **Bearing with one another in love**
- **Maintaining the unity of the Spirit through the bond of peace**

Both Jews and Gentiles, despite their histories and advantages, are worthless apart from Christ. Both are saved together under Jesus alone. There is no room for envy or a sense of superiority.

### Learning to Live Together

The world offers many examples of rival groups brought together—liberal versus labor politics, different ethnic communities, mainland China and Taiwan. The early church faced this on a spiritual level: Jews and Gentiles learning to share meals, navigate food laws, and accept one another as full members of God's family.

In our own contexts, this means not being overly competitive, looking out for the weakest members of our community, and recognizing that while we each have different gifts, there's no reason to boast. For those who tend toward criticism, we must practice patience. We must be willing to address issues openly rather than allowing tensions to fester. Above all, we must remain completely humble and gentle.

## 4. Therefore, Walk Like the True Jew

> I tell you this, and insist on it in the Lord: You must no longer live as the Gentiles do, in the futility of their thinking. They are darkened in their understanding and separated from the life of God because of the ignorance that is in them due to the hardening of their hearts. Having lost all sensitivity, they have given themselves over to sensuality so as to indulge in every kind of impurity, with a continual lust for more. (Ephesians 4:17-19)

Paul now contrasts the pagan Gentile lifestyle with the way of life to which these believers have been called. The pagan Gentile world is characterized by:

- Futility of thinking
- Darkened understanding
- Separation from God's life
- Ignorance and hardened hearts
- Loss of sensitivity
- Enslavement to sensuality and impurity
- Endless craving for more

### A New Way of Living

> You, however, did not come to know Christ that way. You heard of him and were taught in accordance with the truth that is in Jesus. You were taught to put off your old self, which is being corrupted by its deceitful desires, to be made new in the attitude of your minds, and to put on the new self, created to be like God in true righteousness and holiness. Therefore each of you must put off falsehood and speak truthfully to your neighbor, for we are all members of one body. (Ephesians 4:20-25)

For those who come to Christ from a non-Christian background—from drunkenness, lewd talk, sexual immorality, gambling, or deception—the call is clear: *You must leave that lifestyle.*

This isn't a call reserved for a special spiritual elite. Both church-raised believers (who may struggle with arrogance and spiritual forgetfulness) and those from pagan backgrounds (who must break patterns of sensuality and excess) are called to the same standard: walk like the true Jew—like Jesus, who perfectly embodied righteousness and holiness.

If drunkenness has been part of your life, you cannot live that way anymore. Perhaps one or two drinks to remain hospitable with non-Christian friends, but not to the point of intoxication. If your friends cannot respect your desire to leave that lifestyle behind, you may need new friends who support your commitment to Christ.

If you've lived a lifestyle involving sexual immorality and nightclubbing, recognize that you cannot continue down that path. It's a murky pool—easy to wade into but dangerous. Stay away entirely.

### Living Out the Truth

The call is not to perfection but to a radical reorientation. You've been invited into God's people. You share one hope with your Jewish brothers and sisters. You are one body, called to serve one another in love and truth.

Walk like the true Jew. Walk like Jesus.
