# Acts 14:1-28

**Title:** Speak Freely
**Date:** 25 May 2015
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Introduction: Free Speech and Speaking Up

When you speak something controversial or unpopular, how does that go for you? Years ago, I called into talkback radio about marriage—a controversial topic at the time. I said a few things and got called some names. Today, the controversial topics have shifted from marriage and climate change to the pandemic. I was nervous then. I'm nervous now as I wade into these issues.

The concept of free speech traces back to ancient Greek civilization. One pivotal moment came around 700 BC when Hesiod, a lay person, wrote *Theogony*—a theological work that declared the gods and universe were matters of private interest between himself and God. The priests could no longer control what he could think or say about God.

Today we see Paul, an early follower of Jesus living at the tail end of Greek civilization, exercising his free speech and traveling to various cities to share the good news. His experiences reveal three distinct responses to his speech—responses just as applicable to us today as we speak about Jesus.

---

## 1. The Idolization of Paul (14:8-18)

### A Miracle and Its Echo

In Lystra, Paul encounters a man lame from birth who had never walked. As Paul speaks, he looks directly at the man, sees his faith for healing, and calls out, "Stand up on your feet!" The man jumps up and begins to walk.

This account closely parallels an earlier miracle in Acts 3, when Peter and John heal a man lame from birth at the temple gate. The similarities are striking:

- Both men are **lame** from **birth**
- Both apostles **look straight** at them intently
- Both healed men **jump** and begin to **walk**

Luke appears intentionally showing that Paul possesses the same apostolic authority as Peter. Paul was a latecomer to the apostles—he came late to faith and initially persecuted Christians. Yet God would use him to bring the gospel to the Gentiles. It was crucial that Paul be recognized as an equal apostle with valid authority.

### The Danger of Overexuberant Response

But the crowd's response is overwhelmingly positive—dangerously so.

> When the crowd saw what Paul had done, they shouted in the Lycaonian language, "The gods have come down to us in human form!" Barnabas they called Zeus, and Paul they called Hermes because he was the chief speaker.

The priest of Zeus, whose temple stood just outside the city, brought bulls and wreaths to the gates, wanting to offer sacrifices to them.

Think about the temptation Paul and Barnabas faced. Here was a new city where they could start fresh—faster gospel growth, gain friends, no past to chase them down, no weaknesses exposed. They could be worshipped as gods. But to accept such worship would contradict everything Paul believed.

### Paul's Immediate Response

Paul and Barnabas tore their clothes—exposing themselves as ordinary humans—and rushed into the crowd, shouting:

> Friends, why are you doing this? We too are only human, like you. We are bringing you good news, telling you to turn from these worthless things to the **living God**.

This echoes Peter's response in Acts 3:12 when people marveled at Peter's power. Both apostles recognized that to claim divinity contradicts the very heart of their message.

### The True God

The people of Lystra didn't understand that God had already come down in human form—not in Paul or Barnabas, but in Jesus. God became flesh. If you met Jesus, you met God in the flesh. You could have mingled with him in the crowd.

And the sacrifice was already completed. God became flesh and that flesh died for our sakes. Jesus gave his life as a sacrifice for our sins—and was raised to life, still living. The living God, in contrast to the dead, immovable idols.

> He made the heavens and the earth and the sea and everything in them. In the past, he let all nations go their own way. Yet he has not left himself without testimony: He has shown kindness by giving you rain from heaven and crops in their seasons; he provides you with plenty of food and fills your hearts with joy.

This same God who provides rain and crops and joy also provided Jesus for our redemption.

### Modern Idolatry

God is in charge of all things—good and bad. In times of crisis like a pandemic, we desperately search for people to trust: politicians, leaders, celebrities, experts. We want someone cool to follow so we can stop thinking and critiquing, and instead blindly trust. They become our Australian Idols.

We revere politicians like they are gods. We put our hopes in doctors. But when they fail us—when a politician can't work 24/7 or a doctor says "there's nothing we can do"—we get angry at them the way we sometimes get angry at God. "Why didn't you make the perfect life for us?"

Morrison is not God. Doctors are not God. They are frail people just like us. Don't put ultimate hope in the celebrities of the world. Turn to the true and living God and stay away from these idols.

In our calamity, we must remember that God is in charge. When I share this message or post on Facebook, it is not God making a pronouncement as final truth. It's simply noticing something curious, trying to connect ideas, and questioning further. I'm not God, not a politician, not making policy. God is in charge of this crisis. The rest of us are side-line commenting on daily events.

This crisis challenges us: What do we idolize?

> Even with these words, they had difficulty keeping the crowd from sacrificing to them.

The people of Lystra didn't get it. But at least the mob was naive and did Paul and Barnabas no harm. This attitude soon turns to hatred.

## 2. The Hatred Toward Paul (14:1-7, 19-20)

### The Shift from Worship to Rejection

At Iconium, initially some believed. But soon it turned to hatred. A large crowd reacted negatively toward Paul and Barnabas.

> The Jews who refused to believe stirred up the other Gentiles and poisoned their minds against the brothers.

A plot quickly escalated:

> There was a plot afoot among both Gentiles and Jews, together with their leaders, to mistreat them and stone them. But they found out about it and fled to the Lycaonian cities of Lystra and Derbe and to the surrounding country, where they continued to preach the gospel.

Then, dramatically:

> Some Jews came from Antioch and Iconium and won the crowd over. They stoned Paul and dragged him outside the city, thinking he was dead.

### Why Such Violence?

Why would the people of Lystra stone Paul and Barnabas? They came peacefully. They spoke. They didn't threaten or coerce anyone. They presented a new idea—unconventional perhaps, but potentially useful for people.

This is why we value free speech. As long as speech isn't slander, our society believes people can speak their mind.

Free speech originated from religious liberty. When Christians gained political power, they didn't want what happened to Paul, Peter, and the early martyrs to repeat. So they protected freedom of speech—the principle that the state couldn't stop the expression of an idea.

This freedom extended even to untruths. Even if what was said was a lie, or promoted a different religion or false idols, western society protected it. False ideas would be defeated not by force but by better ideas. The word would be supreme.

And so western civilization flourished. The best ideas, tested over time and refined through mistakes, rose to the top when there was no hidden agenda.

But today, western civilization is rethinking these ideals. Politicians increasingly believe speech shouldn't be free if it might hurt someone's feelings. The fight over religious freedom is on hold. Just before the crisis, a bill before parliament addressed this—now everything is paused.

My concern: This crisis gives government unprecedented power over people. I understand it may be necessary, but it comes at a cost.

China and Hong Kong handled the pandemic well, but they are the same countries without as much liberty. Today, you might think health is the most important thing. But tomorrow, you might be stuck. Hong Kong didn't think being under China was a big deal until it was.

Power is addictive. Crises bring out our inner totalitarian impulses. Everything becomes justified because, after all, it's a crisis!

We see strange things now: officials insisting only three people in a room, police asking where you've been and demanding proof you were shopping, people fined for sitting in a car with their girlfriend, Facebook nagging you to do what you're already doing.

Speak while we can, before they completely shut us down.

## 3. They Believed Paul (14:21-28)

### Resilience and Return

> After the disciples had gathered around him, he got up and went back into the city. The next day he and Barnabas left for Derbe. They preached the gospel in that city and won a large number of disciples.

What a contrast. The same message. Different responses.

Just as viral videos don't go viral immediately—you keep doing them until one hits—the gospel work requires persistence. Paul didn't despair. He got up the next day. He didn't idolize his health. He idolized the will of God.

### Why We Speak Up

We speak not for popularity or to be unnecessarily provocative or offensive. We speak for that one in a million chance that the elect will hear the voice of the shepherd and follow him. We speak to call disciples for Jesus, to preach the gospel—that good news that the kingdom of God is here and we can enter it freely because Christ has paid for our sins.

We speak up in our families about sin. We speak up against unrighteousness in our workplaces and society. We speak up for religious freedoms and liberties. We speak up for truths.

And some will take it as offensive. We have to be ready for that.

> Then they returned to Lystra, Iconium and Antioch, strengthening the disciples and encouraging them to remain true to the faith. "We must go through many hardships to enter the kingdom of God," they said.

This task isn't just for Paul. It's for us.

> Paul and Barnabas appointed elders for them in each church and, with prayer and fasting, committed them to the Lord, in whom they had put their trust.

The authority passes to leaders and, to varying degrees, to all of us. We all have the responsibility to speak up. Remain true to the faith and go through hardships as we speak to others. Trials of the kingdom. Don't despair.

Staying silent doesn't make things better. The world won't progress to make it easier for Christians. We must speak now before it's too late.

### The Gospel for All

The talk of God is no longer restricted to some priestly caste. The gospel has been given to all of us. We are all worthy and equipped to tell the world how Christ has impacted our lives. You are experts.

So go—in your own way and timing—and tell your parents, siblings, and friends. Tell your neighbors, workmates, the student you tutor, your barber, shopkeeper, florist, and baker. Speak so that all might know the surpassing love of Christ for us all.

## Conclusion: Speak Freely, Speak Faithfully

Paul's first missionary journey ends as he and Barnabas return to Antioch:

> After going through Pisidia, they came into Pamphylia, and when they had preached the word in Perga, they went down to Attalia. From Attalia they sailed back to Antioch, where they had been committed to the grace of God for the work they had now completed.

His example teaches us: Don't despair. He was stoned and dragged outside the city, yet he got up the next day. He didn't feel sorry for himself because he had more important things to do.

Speak freely while you can. Speak truthfully about Jesus. Speak boldly for religious liberty. Speak up for the living God.
