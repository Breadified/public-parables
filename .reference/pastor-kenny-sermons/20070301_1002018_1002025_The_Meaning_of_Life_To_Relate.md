**Title:** The Meaning of Life: To Relate?

## We Are Not Created to Be Alone

> "It is not good that the man should be alone." — Genesis 2:18

At the pinnacle of creation stands mankind—blessed by God, sustained by His provision, and declared "very good" by the Creator. Yet in Genesis 2:18, God says something striking: it is not good for man to be alone.

When Scripture uses the word "alone" here, it speaks not of occasional solitude but of a mindset—**individualism**. We are created as individuals, intimately formed by a loving God, but we are not created to live as individualists.

### The Problem with Individualism

Our culture celebrates the isolated self. Songs proclaim personal freedom. Advertising declares "you are the most important person in the world." Economic systems are built on everyone looking out for number one. Even in Christian contexts, bookstore shelves overflow with self-help literature about unleashing your inner potential.

This worldview has devastated our relationships. If friendship no longer serves your needs, you change friends. If your marriage doesn't fit your current desires, you upgrade your spouse. When family becomes inconvenient, you move out. It's no wonder that marriages crumble and commitments dissolve—we've been taught that loyalty lasts only as long as personal benefit.

God says something radically different: it is not good to be alone. **Individualism and selfishness are not good.**

### A Cautionary Word

This truth demands our attention, especially for young adults. The "travel bug"—the desire to disappear for months or years to "see the world"—captures this spirit perfectly. The logic seems sound: why not go now, while you're young and free? Work abroad, gain experience, travel wherever you want.

But this reveals the deeper problem: living only for yourself, believing you need no one. Some begin this journey as committed Christians and return as spiritual drifters, having spent months floating in their own autonomous bubble. The teaching of Scripture is clear: **we are created for relationship, not isolation.**

## Created for Relationship

> "I will make him a helper suitable for him." — Genesis 2:18b

God's response to Adam's aloneness was relational creation. He brought the animals before Adam—a beautiful picture of dominion and naming—but none could be that suitable helper. Then God performed surgery. He caused Adam to fall into a deep sleep, took one of his ribs, and fashioned a woman.

When Adam awoke and saw her, he burst into poetry:

> "This is now bone of my bones and flesh of my flesh; she shall be called Woman, because she was taken out of Man." — Genesis 2:23

### The Foundation: Marriage and Family

The relationship God creates here is specifically marriage—the complementary union of man and woman. But Genesis points beyond the marital bond to something broader: **family itself**.

> "That is why a man leaves his father and mother and is united to his wife, and they become one flesh." — Genesis 2:24

Though Adam and Eve had no parents, this pattern is established for all generations to come. The man and woman leave their birth families to form a new family unit. This is no afterthought—it's the basic building block of society.

### Application One: Our Blood Relatives

How do we treat our own families? Do we arrive home from university or work and vanish into our rooms? Do we fail to communicate our schedules, our needs, our busy lives?

We are privileged to be in families, and privilege demands gratitude, not indifference. Our parents have labored for us—cooking meals, planning weekends, providing shelter. The least we can do is communicate honestly about our schedules, our activities, our plans. This isn't burden; it's love expressed through presence and honesty.

### Application Two: The Church as Family

What applies to blood relatives applies equally to the family of God—the church. Paul writes:

> "So then you are no longer strangers and aliens, but you are fellow citizens with the saints and members of the household of God." — Ephesians 2:19

And again:

> "Do not rebuke an older man but encourage him as you would a father. Treat younger men like brothers, older women like mothers, younger women like sisters, in all purity." — 1 Timothy 5:1-2

As Christians, we are intrinsically linked through Christ. We share one Father, one great Brother, and each other. Therefore: **"As we have opportunity, let us do good to everyone, and especially to those who are of the household of faith." (Galatians 6:10)**

The foundation of true relationship is **other-person-centeredness**—genuine interest in another's welfare and a commitment to their wellbeing through every appropriate means.

### Keeping Our Commitments

Our generation struggles with one particular area: **faithfulness to commitments**. Once we gain independence—a car, a mobile phone, a little money—we become remarkably flexible. We treat appointments as optional, as if they're merely requests rather than covenants.

But consider a volleyball team: everyone counts. When one person fails to show, the whole team suffers. The same is true in church. When we fail to keep our word—by being late, by not showing up, by not responding to invitations—we break the bond of trust. How can others trust you if you're unfaithful to your commitments?

At church, we operate on an unwritten but sacred premise: **those who have committed to being here have committed to turning up each Sunday, rain or shine.** Not merely to learn from the Bible, but to look out for one another, to be present unless genuinely ill or facing genuine emergency.

An RSVP is an investment in relationship. It means: *"I'm coming to your thing, and I'm choosing nothing else over that commitment."* When we must break an appointment, we're genuinely breaking something. We should feel the weight of it. We should apologize sincerely and seek forgiveness—not through a casual text, but face-to-face with real repentance.

This is the relational model—and it's the best way to live.

## Because We Are Created by a Relational God

Why does relationship matter so deeply? Because we bear the image of a relational God.

> "The Father loves the Son and has given all things into his hand." — John 3:35
>
> "I seek not my own will but the will of him who sent me." — John 5:30
>
> "I do as the Father has commanded me, so that the world may know that I love the Father." — John 14:31
>
> "When the Spirit of truth comes, he will guide you into all the truth, for he will not speak on his own authority, but whatever he hears he will speak, and he will declare to you the things that are to come. He will glorify me, for he will take what is mine and declare it to you." — John 16:13-14

### The Trinity: Eternal Other-Centeredness

In the Godhead, we witness complete mutual other-centeredness. The Father gives all things to the Son. The Son obeys the Father and glorifies Him. The Spirit does not seek His own glory but glorifies the Son, who glorifies the Father. There is no competition, no self-seeking, no autonomy—only eternal relationship characterized by perfect love and deference.

**In the Trinity, we see relationship in its purest form.** And because mankind is made in the image of God, relationship must be at the core of our being. We are fundamentally relational creatures because our Creator is fundamentally relational.

### The Unique God

This distinguishes our God from false conceptions. The Muslim god is impersonal and existed in no eternal relationship. Only our God—the God of the Bible—is able to sympathize with us, to understand love, to show mercy, because He has existed eternally in relationship. He knows intimacy. He knows communion. He knows what it means to be betrayed and abandoned.

## Getting Right with Our Relational God

Understanding that God is relational doesn't merely call us to reconcile with each other—it calls us first to reconcile with Him.

The fundamental barrier to all relationship is **sin**: self-centeredness, autonomy, the refusal to need another. Sin is the spirit that says, *"I don't need you."* This is what broke the world.

Jesus came to deal with this. He alone perfectly reflected the nature of God, living eternally in relationship with His Father. Yet He who was never alone—never abandoned, never cut off—became utterly alone for our sakes. He was abandoned by His Father, rejected by His people, betrayed by His friends.

Not because He deserved it. Because we did.

> "For he himself is our peace, who has made us both one and has broken down in his flesh the dividing wall of hostility by abolishing the law of commandments and ordinances, that he might create in himself one new man in place of the two, so making peace, and might reconcile us both to God in one body through the cross, thereby killing the hostility." — Ephesians 2:14-16

In supreme other-centeredness, Christ died to kill the hostilities between us and God. **Through His sacrifice, we are reconciled to God and to each other.**

---

## Living as Relational Beings

This is profound comfort: we are built for one another, destined to serve one another. When loneliness threatens, when abandonment looms, when we need help—we have each other. The beauty of family, in both its physical and spiritual forms, is that it demands love unconditionally. Even when we break important commitments, even when we let others down, we find forgiveness in Christ.

Before marriage, you may describe yourself as single. But you are never truly alone—you belong to a family. You belong to a God who is eternally relational and who has made you in His image.

**That is worth investing in.**

---

## Conclusion

In a world saturated with individualism, what does Genesis declare?

It is not good for man to be alone. The purpose of life is **genuine, committed relationship**—with one another and ultimately with God Himself.

On your deathbed, you will not wish you had studied harder or earned more money or stayed later at the office. You will wish you had spent more time with people you love. You will wish you had invested in genuine relationships.

We are created for relationship because our ultimate reality is a God in eternal relationship. He has made us in His image and likeness. This means man is fundamentally a relational being—designed for connection with the Creator and communion with one another.

There is no higher calling than this: to love God with all your heart, and to love your neighbor as yourself.
