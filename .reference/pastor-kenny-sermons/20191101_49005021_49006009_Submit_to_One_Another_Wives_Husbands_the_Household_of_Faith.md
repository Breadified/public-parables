# Ephesians 5:21-6:9

**Title:** Submit to One Another - Wives, Husbands & the Household of Faith
**Date:** 17 November 2019
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Opening

Welcome back Marcus and Cindy from your honeymoon. It wouldn't have been the same without you, and the timing is perfect—this sermon was written with you in mind.

This passage speaks directly to husbands and wives. For those of you not yet married, hear these principles as applying to your future roles. The application is for all of us.

One thing is certain: newly married couples quickly discover they won't always come to agreement. Life brings shocking differences. But willing submission—knowing the flaws and yet choosing to submit for the greater good—is what God calls us toward.

## Understanding Submission

The word "submit" means to be under the authority of someone else, to be a subject. This isn't a command about inferiority; it's about order. Just as we submit to parents and to God himself, submission is a structural principle for relationships.

> Wives, submit yourselves to your own husbands as you do to the Lord. For the husband is the head of the wife as Christ is the head of the church, his body, of which he is the Savior. Now as the church submits to Christ, so also wives should submit to their husbands in everything. (Ephesians 5:22-24)

### The Meaning of "Submit"

The same Greek word describes:
- Children being obedient to parents (Luke 2:51: Jesus was obedient to Mary and Joseph)
- Sarah obeying Abraham, calling him "lord" or "master" (1 Peter 3:5-6)

Submission is not slavery or doormat weakness. Rather, it's a conscious choice to be under authority—just as the church willingly follows Christ unconditionally.

### Critical Limits on Submission

God always claims first loyalty. If a husband commands sin—whether theft, abortion, or forbidding Christian teaching—a wife's obligation to God supersedes her husband's authority. In such scenarios, she must do what is right before God.

However, these exceptions represent perhaps 1% of married life. The other 99% concerns everyday decisions: what to eat, how to organize chores, which relatives to visit, where to live, budgeting disputes. In these matters, wives submit.

Even if the decision isn't ideal, if the husband insists, the wife yields. The decision might not be the best way, but when he leads, she follows.

### Why Submit "in Everything"?

This isn't about the husband being better, smarter, more godly, or more responsible. He might be none of these things. God chose the husband as the tie-breaker not because of his superiority, but because someone must lead when impasse arrives. This prevents marital dissolution over irreconcilable differences.

If God had chosen the wife instead, we'd have the same discussion with reversed terms. It might seem arbitrary, but order—not arbitrariness—serves families.

## The Husband's Call: Love as Death

The instruction for husbands is far more demanding than for wives. The passage devotes merely a few verses to wives but an entire paragraph to husbands.

> Husbands, love your wives, just as Christ loved the church and gave himself up for her to make her holy, cleansing her by the washing with water through the word, and to present her to himself as a radiant church, without stain or wrinkle or any other blemish, but holy and blameless. (Ephesians 5:25-27)

The word "love" here doesn't mean romantic infatuation or attraction. It means concrete caring for another's welfare. Christ's love is the model: He sacrificed His life on the cross to save the church, cleanse her, protect her, and present her holy before God.

**Husbands, this means: die for your wives.**

Not die *from* your wives, but die *for* them—in small ways, daily.

### The Shape of Dying for Your Wife

This death takes multiple forms:

**Emotionally:** When family breakdown occurs, the husband initiates reconciliation. He is first to apologize. He carries the emotional weight of repair.

**Spiritually:** It's his role to guide the family toward godliness, ensuring she becomes "holy and blameless" as Christ desires the church.

**Materially:** He provides food and shelter. If recession strikes and only humble work is available, he must die to his ego and pride to care for his family.

**Physically:** If danger comes—a burglar at the door, a threat to safety—he stands between the danger and his wife. In the most extreme scenario, if a bullet is coming, he takes it.

### Looking After Your Own Body

> In this same way, husbands ought to love their wives as their own bodies. He who loves his wife loves himself. After all, no one ever hated their own body, but they feed and care for their body, just as Christ does the church. (Ephesians 5:28-29)

We all know how to care for ourselves—we feed ourselves well, exercise, protect ourselves from stress. The husband must extend this same instinctive care to his wife. Transform the selfishness we naturally direct toward ourselves into selflessness directed toward her.

## The Symmetry of Sacrifice

Consider what's asked of each partner:

**Wives:** Submit (one verse)

**Husbands:** Die (one paragraph)

Women are not commanded to die for husbands. They're called to submit. Men are called to die—to sacrifice everything.

Ask yourselves: which is easier?

## The Foundation: Order, Not Domination

Two people cannot lead a team of two. A unit needs a leader. This is why the passage specifies hierarchy.

### Why Mutual Leadership Fails

If both partners attempt to lead, decisions become deadlocked. After discussion, consultation, and good-faith debate, someone must eventually decide. A democracy of two leaves you where you started.

This order exists for families that want to endure. If you believe marriage is temporary, you can simply divorce when irreconcilable differences appear. But if you believe marriage is for life—stronger than temporary disagreement—then submission becomes necessary to preserve unity.

God established this order in advance knowing that conflict comes. He doesn't want you split between Brisbane and Melbourne. He wants you together, as one family unit.

### The Role of Chivalry

In Christianized Western culture, we see men trained to die:
- Opening car doors for women
- Paying for dates
- Carrying bags
- Walking girls home at night
- Stacking chairs at church
- Taking initiative in spiritual leadership

These aren't sexist affronts to women's capability. Women can open their own doors and pay for themselves. Rather, these practices cultivate a mindset in young men: *You are responsible. You are called to serve. You are the one who will die for others.*

This training begins young. When boys learn not to hit their sisters, to hold their hand crossing the road, to offer to pay for meals—they're being shaped into men who will keep a vow to die for their wives.

So when a man stands at the altar and says "I will," you can believe him. His character has been formed by a lifetime of small deaths.

## Addressing Domestic Abuse

The bushfires this week have sparked discussion. Some claim that heroic masculine service encourages toxic masculinity and domestic violence. The images—subjugation, forced submission—are repugnant and un-Christian. No serious Christian endorses abuse.

Yet perhaps there's a grain of truth worth considering: After an intense day fighting fires, adrenaline high and exhaustion deep, if a wife nags about dirt tracked inside or complains about laundry, conflict can escalate into violence. That's no excuse—none whatsoever. But the scenario could unfold differently.

Imagine the wife as his number one fan, grateful he didn't fight the fire himself, offering him gratitude, a cold drink, care for his weary frame. She submits not resentfully but gratefully—grateful he's willing to risk his life, which means he'll die for her and their family.

This creates a completely different home.

## The Larger Mystery

> For this reason a man will leave his father and mother and be united to his wife, and the two will become one flesh. This is a profound mystery—but I am talking about Christ and the church. (Ephesians 5:31-32)

Marriage isn't ultimately about husbands and wives. It's a picture of Christ and the church. The submission and sacrificial love you practice in marriage are rehearsals for your submission to Christ and His love for you.

God asks each of us to submit to Christ as the church submits to a husband. This isn't submission to an authoritarian tyrant but to a God who proved His love by dying for us. If we let Him lead, we live healthier, more fulfilled lives now and forever.

## Practical Wisdom for the Unmarried

If this teaching about submission resonates with you, understand that these principles guide your choice of a future spouse.

**For women:** When checking out a potential husband, ask: Can I submit to this man's authority? If he's selfish or immature, why would you? If he's not Christian, how can you trust he'll present you "as a radiant church, without stain or wrinkle or any other blemish, but holy and blameless" before Jesus?

Even Christian men aren't automatically good husbands. If he doesn't read his Bible, shows no interest in God, lacks maturity, isn't working on godliness, or leaves his faith in constant doubt—why risk it? He may grow up someday, but until then, he should not be an option. Wait for a man whose character shows he will die for you.

**For men:** Consider what kind of bride you can present to Christ. Will she listen to you? Can you lead her? How can you lead her spotless before Jesus if she's not a Christian? Are you mature enough to shoulder this responsibility?

## A Final Word on Equality and Order

Our culture confuses equality with uniformity, demanding everyone be identical. But God calls us to be individually unique while functioning as unified households.

Husbands and wives aren't individuals anymore after marriage—they're one flesh, one family unit. A unit needs leadership. A waltz works only when someone leads and someone follows.

This is God's mercy toward married couples: structure that preserves love through conflict. Submit to appropriate authorities in their proper order. When you do, you participate in a mystery greater than yourselves—the love of Christ for His church.
