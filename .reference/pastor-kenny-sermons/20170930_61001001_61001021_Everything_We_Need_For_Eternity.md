## Introduction: Everything We Need

Have you ever watched MacGyver? The old TV show that ended in 1992 follows an FBI secret agent who refuses to carry a gun and instead solves problems using only his mind, a Swiss pocket knife, and duct tape—along with whatever he finds in his environment. In one episode he's trapped in a jail cell and makes a bomb from bullets. In another, he's stuck in a freezer and uses a light bulb to melt ice, channeling the water to a lock where it refreezes and expands to break him free.

Among older generations, "MacGyver" has become a verb: to solve an impossible problem using everyday objects. It describes the ability to find extraordinary solutions from ordinary resources.

This passage asks us a crucial question: **Do we, as Christians, have everything we need?** Many of us feel lacking, powerless, or as though something vital is missing. Peter's answer in this letter is clear: yes, we have everything we need—not just for today, but for eternity.

---

## 1. Everything We Need for Eternity (1:1-4)

### The Gospel Is All We Need

Peter addresses the church universally, reminding us that we possess everything necessary for godliness and eternal life. Notice how verses 2, 3, and 4 work together in parallel:

> **Verse 2:** Grace and peace be yours in abundance through the knowledge of God and of Jesus our Lord.
>
> **Verse 3:** His divine power has given us everything we need for life and godliness through our knowledge of him who called us by his own glory and goodness.
>
> **Verse 4:** Through these he has given us his very great and precious promises, so that through them you may participate in the divine nature and escape the corruption in the world caused by evil desires.

What exactly have we been given?

- **Verse 2:** Grace and peace in abundance
- **Verse 4:** His very great and precious promises

These gifts comprise the gospel—the good news of salvation. In verse 1, Peter refers to "a faith as precious as ours" received through the righteousness of Christ. The gospel is grace. The gospel is peace with God. We receive both abundantly.

The heart of this good news: **Jesus saves us from God's wrath through his death on the cross.** He took our place. We deserved the crucifix; he took it instead. Though we will die in this life, we will have eternal life.

### A Complete Provision

Think of the gospel like a Swiss pocket knife or a smartphone—it contains everything you need. It's how we reach heaven. It's how we live today. It provides comfort, learning, rebuke, wisdom, and guidance. The Bible—Scripture in its entirety—testifies to the gospel and covers every need.

By contrast, the world constantly tells us we lack something. Every magazine, billboard, and social media post is designed to generate discontent. "Buy this. Get that. Wear this. Experience that." Even our friends post pictures of experiences we don't have, subtly suggesting that without them, we're incomplete.

But this is a lie.

Consider what we think we desperately need: the latest iPhone, the newest computer game, more money. Where are last year's versions now? Sitting unused in drawers. We don't truly need them. Even in countries where Christians have no food, no clothes, and no peace, believers survive and even thrive—because they possess the gospel first and foremost.

**Nothing is crucial except the cross.**

## 2. We Have Everything We Need to Know (1:3)

### Knowledge Is Not Anti-Intellectual

How do we gain the gospel? Through knowledge. Peter emphasizes this throughout his letter:

> His divine power has given us everything we need for a godly life **through our knowledge of him** who called us by his own glory and goodness.

This is profoundly important: **the gospel comes to us through knowledge.** God has not left us in darkness. He takes the initiative, revealing himself so we can know him.

This means Christianity is not anti-intellectual—contrary to what critics claim. We pursue knowledge. We learn. Our understanding of Scripture deepens year by year. And this knowledge extends beyond Bible study to all of life. The first modern scientists were Christians, often pastors themselves. The first universities began as Christian seminaries. The first school in New South Wales was founded by a clergyman. **Knowledge and learning are part of the Christian's DNA.**

Therefore, it is entirely proper for our churches to have Bible studies, sermons, instruction, Sunday school, and teachers. Yet these are often the very things churches are pressured to dilute in their zeal to be "relevant" and "exciting." Have we forgotten how we actually come to know God?

The tool of the church is not primarily a guitar or a candle—though these have their place. The primary tool is pen, paper, and Scripture. Put what you learn to music with the guitar; use the candle to read the Bible.

Some argue that "God is bigger than knowledge" and therefore studying Scripture constantly is improper. While it's true that God transcends books, **he has chosen to be known through the Book.** As Hebrews reminds us:

> In the past God spoke to our ancestors through the prophets at many times and in various ways, but in these last days he has spoken to us by his Son, whom he appointed heir of all things, and through whom also he made the universe. The Son is the radiance of God's glory and the exact representation of his being, sustaining all things by his powerful word.

### Full Knowledge Available to All

The fact that the gospel can be known means you don't need a Ph.D. in theology to be a complete Christian. You don't need university education or even to have finished secondary school. A poor, uneducated laborer in rural China can know the gospel fully. That person possesses the same knowledge of salvation as a Nobel laureate. As Martin Luther said, "Even a farmboy can understand the gospel."

This is not secret knowledge for the elite—not Gnosticism. This is objective truth, available to all.

When Peter uses the word "knowledge" in verse 3, the Greek original means **full knowledge**—*epignōsis*. Christians possess everything we need to know. Not in the sense that we understand quantum physics or know what everyone is thinking, but in this crucial sense: **we have the tools to understand the world and interpret it rightly.** We have the glasses through which we can see reality in focus.

The world grows increasingly chaotic the further it distances itself from God. But we have been given the framework to reason through it all.

## 3. We Have Everything We Need to Do (1:5-11)

### Knowledge Must Bear Fruit

Yet this knowledge must not remain locked in ivory towers of academia. It must flow into ordinary life. And here's the vital connection: **knowledge exists for a purpose** (verse 3):

> His divine power has given us everything we need for a godly life.

Knowledge leads to godliness. When we asked earlier about "participating in the divine nature" (verse 4), the answer is this: **it means becoming godly**—being like God in holiness and character.

This is not claiming that Christians receive God's DNA or that the Holy Spirit gives us supernatural powers in a mystical sense. Rather, it means we have the capacity to live a godly life because we possess the knowledge of Christ and his salvation.

### The Call to Active Growth

Now look at verses 5-11:

> For this very reason, make every effort to add to your faith goodness; and to goodness, knowledge; and to knowledge, self-control; and to self-control, perseverance; and to perseverance, godliness; and to godliness, mutual affection; and to mutual affection, love. For if you possess these qualities in increasing measure, they will keep you from being ineffective and unproductive in your knowledge of our Lord Jesus Christ. But whoever does not have them is nearsighted and blind, forgetting that they have been cleansed from their past sins. Therefore, my brothers and sisters, make every effort to confirm your calling and election. For if you do these things, you will never stumble, and you will receive a rich welcome into the eternal kingdom of our Lord and Savior Jesus Christ.

The list isn't necessarily sequential, but the emphasis is unmistakable: **keep adding. Make every effort. Be eager.**

The qualities listed—goodness, knowledge, self-control, perseverance, godliness, brotherly kindness, love—all work together to make us **productive and effective** in our knowledge of Jesus Christ. This means: You already have the gospel. Now live it out. Use it. Be fruitful with it.

Think of productive land: it needs fertilizer, seed, and time to grow. Your knowledge of Christ is the same.

### A Practical Challenge

This week many of you have holidays coming, and summer approaches. You all make lists:

- Xbox for a day
- Movies to watch
- Get a job
- Get my license
- Visit places
- Schoolies week
- Time with friends

These are temporary things. Meanwhile, God has his list:

- Add goodness
- Add knowledge
- Add self-control
- Add perseverance
- Add godliness
- Add brotherly kindness
- Add love

Verse 10-11 reminds us of the stakes:

> Make every effort to confirm your calling and election. For if you do these things, you will never stumble, and you will receive a rich welcome into the eternal kingdom of our Lord and Savior Jesus Christ.

### Breaking Through Excuses

We often tell ourselves: "I'll be more godly if only..."

- If only I had more money
- If only I felt more secure
- If only I had more time
- If only my parents were different
- If only my church were better
- If only my friends were more supportive
- If only my life circumstances were different
- If only my job didn't interfere
- If only society made more sense

We're waiting for the perfect formula, the ideal circumstances. But the truth is: **we have everything we need right now.**

I think we should all be a little more like MacGyver. Less complaining. Get on with the job. Whatever situation you're stuck in, see the wonderful provision already given to you. **You can be godly because you've been given the knowledge of the gospel.**

The resources are there. The tools are in your hand. The only question is: will you use them?
