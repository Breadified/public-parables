# Hebrews 8:13—10:25

**Title:** The Better Sacrifice
**Date:** 8 October 2009
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Obsolete Systems

We live in a world of obsolete technology. Commodore 64s, Nokia phones, Angry Birds—they're all relics now. But there's something far more significant that became obsolete: the entire Levitical system of worship.

---

## Part 1: What Was Obsolete (Hebrews 8:13)

> By calling this covenant "new," he has made the first one obsolete; and what is obsolete and aging will soon disappear. (Hebrews 8:13)

The old covenant system had three essential components:

### The Temple

> Now the first covenant had regulations for worship and also an earthly sanctuary. A tabernacle was set up. In its first room were the lampstand, the table and the consecrated bread; this was called the Holy Place. Behind the second curtain was a room called the Most Holy Place, which had the golden altar of incense and the gold-covered ark of the covenant. This ark contained the gold jar of manna, Aaron's staff that had budded, and the stone tablets of the covenant. Above the ark were the cherubim of the Glory, overshadowing the atonement cover. (Hebrews 9:1–5)

The temple was designed like a series of progressively holier rooms. As worshipers moved deeper into the sanctuary, they entered more sacred space, much like moving through increasingly special rooms in a house. The Most Holy Place was where God's presence dwelt.

### The Priest

> When everything had been arranged like this, the priests entered regularly into the outer room to carry on their ministry. But only the high priest entered the inner room, and that only once a year, and never without blood, which he offered for himself and for the sins the people had committed in ignorance. (Hebrews 9:6–7)

Only the high priest could enter the Most Holy Place, and even he could do so just once a year. The priest served as the exclusive mediator between the people and God.

### The Offering

> But only the high priest entered the inner room, and that only once a year, and never without blood, which he offered for himself and for the sins the people had committed in ignorance. (Hebrews 9:7)

Animals were sacrificed. Their blood was spilled as a recognition that sin against God was serious—it carried violent consequences. This was God's justice in action.

Why couldn't God simply overlook sin? When we're deeply hurt by a close friend, we understand that forgiveness requires more than pretending the injury didn't happen. There must be some form of reconciliation. If we struggle with this, how much more must the infinite and perfectly just God, who never forgets, require justice? The sacrificial system allowed God's justice to be satisfied through the substitution of animals on behalf of the people.

Yet this entire system became obsolete.

---

## Part 2: Why It Was Obsolete (Hebrews 10:1–11)

### The Sacrifices Never Actually Worked

> The law is only a shadow of the good things that are coming—not the realities themselves. For this reason it can never, by the same sacrifices repeated endlessly year after year, make perfect those who draw near to worship. If it could, would they not have stopped being offered? For the worshipers would have been cleansed once for all, and would no longer have felt guilty for their sins. (Hebrews 10:1–2)

The simple logic is undeniable: if the sacrifices had actually worked, they wouldn't have needed to keep offering them. The fact that they were repeated year after year proves they were ineffective. It's like a government endlessly subsidizing a company that keeps asking for more money—the endless cycle itself proves the system isn't working.

> But those sacrifices are an annual reminder of sins, because it is impossible for the blood of bulls and goats to take away sins. (Hebrews 10:3–4)

The sacrifices served only as reminders of sin, not as solutions to it. They pointed to something else—a future reality that could actually take away sin.

### The Temple Was Never the Real Thing

> The Holy Spirit was showing by this that the way into the Most Holy Place had not yet been disclosed as long as the first tabernacle was still standing. (Hebrews 9:8)

The temporary, earthly temple was a shadow. The real Most Holy Place—direct access to God's presence—had not yet been revealed. The ancient Israelite hearing this would have been confused: "What do you mean it hasn't been disclosed? Here's the temple right here!" But the point is that the earthly temple was never the ultimate destination.

### The Priest Was Ineffective

> Day after day every priest stands and performs his religious duties; again and again he offers the same sacrifices, which can never take away sins. (Hebrews 10:11)

Day after day, year after year—the priest performed the same rituals that never actually accomplished their stated purpose. It was like the movie Groundhog Day: endless repetition without progress.

### A Profound Truth

The system wasn't broken because there was something wrong with it. Rather, **it was never designed to actually take away sins**. It was always meant to be a temporary illustration of something greater—a toy car, not a real vehicle.

A toy car looks remarkably real with its steering wheel, forward and reverse gears, and rubber wheels. But it will never drive on a real road. It will never be registered with the RTA. It exists to give children an idea of driving, a way of moving around besides walking.

There's nothing wrong with a toy car—it's excellent for its purpose. But give a toy car to an adult, especially one who just failed a driving test or had an accident, and it becomes an insult. A real car is needed.

> The law is only a shadow of the good things that are coming—not the realities themselves. (Hebrews 10:1)

## Part 3: The Better Sacrifice (Hebrews 9:11–15)

### Jesus as the Ultimate High Priest, Temple, and Sacrifice

> When Christ came as high priest of the good things that are already here, he went through the greater and more perfect tabernacle that is not man-made, that is to say, not a part of this creation. He did not enter by means of the blood of goats and calves; but he entered the Most Holy Place once for all by his own blood, having obtained eternal redemption. (Hebrews 9:11–12)

Jesus fulfilled all three roles:

- **The Priest**: Christ came as the ultimate High Priest
- **The Temple**: He entered not an earthly sanctuary but the true, heavenly Most Holy Place
- **The Sacrifice**: He offered not animal blood but his own blood

His blood was the price of forgiveness—paid once, for all time.

> For this reason Christ is the mediator of a new covenant, that those who are called may receive the promised eternal inheritance—now that he has died as a ransom to set them free from the sins committed under the first covenant. (Hebrews 9:15)

If the old system was a toy car, Jesus is a real Subaru WRX with a turbo. When you have the real thing, you don't need the toy anymore.

## Part 4: We Don't Need a System Anymore (Hebrews 10:19–25)

### Direct Access to God

> Therefore, brothers, since we have confidence to enter the Most Holy Place by the blood of Jesus, by a new and living way opened for us through the curtain, that is, his body, and since we have a great priest over the house of God... (Hebrews 10:19–21)

Through Jesus, we have immediate access to God. No priests. No temple. No offerings and sacrifices.

The men at the front of the church are teachers, not priests. Our buildings are shelters, not temples. And when we talk about "giving an offering," we should be clear about what we mean: voluntary financial support for the gospel ministry of the church. God doesn't need our money any more than he needed small oranges on altars or the blood of bulls and goats. He doesn't need our singing. He already has the ultimate sacrifice—Jesus—so there are no more offerings to make to God.

### What God Actually Wants

> First he said, "Sacrifices and offerings, burnt offerings and sin offerings you did not desire, nor were you pleased with them" (although the law required them to be made). Then he said, "Here I am, I have come to do your will." He sets aside the first to establish the second. And by that will, we have been made holy through the sacrifice of the body of Jesus Christ once for all. (Hebrews 10:8–10)

Rather than offerings and sacrifices, God asks for obedience—obedience to his word, godliness, fellowship with one another, and love for others. That is what he desires.

When we give money to the church, we're expressing love for others and supporting the church's effort to serve. That's what counts—not things offered to God directly, but things offered to one another.

### Two Calls to Action

Hebrews 10:19 onwards contains four "let us" statements, which divide into two pairs:

**First pair—toward God:**

> Let us draw near to God with a sincere heart in full assurance of faith, having our hearts sprinkled to cleanse us from a guilty conscience and having our bodies washed with pure water. Let us hold unswervingly to the hope we profess, for he who promised is faithful. (Hebrews 10:22–23)

Draw near to God. Not through mystical means, but through faith in Jesus. This is the call to become a Christian. If you are not yet a believer, you must recognize that you are a sinner deserving judgment, but Jesus, in his mercy, stands in your place. He is the perfect Lamb, slaughtered for you. You need to believe that the works-based religious systems are obsolete, that what you need is Jesus alone.

**Second pair—toward one another:**

> Let us consider how we may spur one another on toward love and good deeds. Let us not give up meeting together, as some are in the habit of doing, but let us encourage one another—and all the more as you see the Day approaching. (Hebrews 10:24–25)

When we gather together, our purpose is simple: to learn the apostles' teaching and to encourage one another to follow Jesus and love one another. Nothing mystical, nothing weird, nothing merely religious. The early church was sometimes thought to be atheistic because they had no temple, no idols, no offerings, no priests—only love for each other in action.

God wants to see you loving one another. That's the bottom line. When the New Testament uses temple language, it's often saying this: if you want to offer something to God, offer it to the poor. If you want to make a sacrifice, sacrifice for others. This is the call to abandon ceremonial religion and embrace genuine love.

## The Modern Drift: Recovering the Truth

Around 300 AD, Emperor Constantine became a Christian. Once Christianity became the official religion of the Roman Empire, something tragic happened: Christians began absorbing the symbols and practices of Judaism into their own faith. Partly to reclaim their inheritance, partly because superstition crept in and people forgot what Jesus had accomplished.

Over centuries, a shift occurred:
- Temples became "churches"
- Meal tables became "altars"
- The Lord's Supper became a "sacrifice"
- Bible teachers became "priests"
- Donations became "offerings"
- Singing became "sacrifices of praise offered to God"

The Reformation corrected much of the theology, but some of the practices lingered. We may no longer have incense, altars, or blood, but do we still think of church buildings as temples where we come to worship God and offer some kind of sacrifice—whether of our lips in song or our wallets in the offering? Do we wear special clothes for church, as if entering a sacred space? Do we prohibit eating in the church building?

Some modern worship songs, though beautiful, can reinforce an unhelpful understanding:

> *We enter your courts, Lord / With grace and thanksgiving / With hands lifted up / And hearts open wide* 
> 
> *Here I am to worship / Here I am to bow down / Here I am to say that You're my God*

This language echoes the Levitical system—bowing down, presenting sacrifices, offering something to God. While these songs are lovely to sing individually, they're not always helpful in a congregational setting where we're supposed to be teaching one another the truth.

The truth is: Jesus has made all of that obsolete. We don't need systems. We need faith in God and love for one another.

---

## Summary

The Levitical system—the temple, the priesthood, the sacrifices—was never designed to actually remove sin. It was a shadow, a toy car pointing to the real thing.

Jesus is the real thing. He is the perfect High Priest, the true Temple, and the ultimate Sacrifice. His blood purchased eternal redemption, making direct access to God possible for all who believe.

We don't need religious systems anymore. We need:
- **Faith in God** (Hebrews 10:22–23)
- **Love for one another** (Hebrews 10:24–25)
- **Encouragement and fellowship** (Hebrews 10:25)

This is what Jesus brings us together to experience: knowing God and knowing one another through genuine love and service.
