# Luke 16:1-16

**Title:** Hope in the World?
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Shrewd Manager (Luke 16:1-8)

Jesus tells his disciples about a rich man whose manager was accused of wasting his possessions. Facing dismissal, the manager had no good options. He was not strong enough to dig in the fields, and his pride prevented him from begging. But he had one advantage: time before his termination took effect.

> "The manager said to himself, 'What shall I do now? My master is taking away my job. I'm not strong enough to dig, and I'm ashamed to beg. I know what I'll do so that, when I lose my job here, people will welcome me into their houses.'" (Luke 16:3-4)

So he called in each of his master's debtors and reduced their debts—the first owed eight hundred gallons of olive oil, which he cut to four hundred; the second owed a thousand bushels of wheat, which he reduced to eight hundred. The manager was generating favours, making friends among those he had previously lorded over. His shrewd calculation was simple: secure allies who would welcome him when he lost his position.

> "The master commended the dishonest manager because he had acted shrewdly. For the people of this world are more shrewd in dealing with their own kind than are the people of the light." (Luke 16:8)

The master commended him—not for the discounts themselves, but for his shrewdness. This manager understood how the world works. Whether he was reducing inflated prices back to fair value or genuinely losing money to gain favour, the point was the same: he acted consistently with his priorities and got results.

## The Application: Wise Use of Possessions

Jesus is teaching something crucial about money and possessions. We often waste our resources thoughtlessly. Many of us have purchased items that sit unused—a scanner gathering dust for a year, eventually broken in frustration. Billions are spent on gifts that end up regifted or thrown away. Studies show that half of Christmas gifts are regifted, and people regularly receive items they don't want or need—popcorn makers, novelty soaps, gadgets that serve no purpose.

This is waste. And waste is a tragedy when we consider what our possessions could actually accomplish.

> "I tell you, use worldly wealth to gain friends for yourselves, so that when it is gone, you will be welcomed into eternal dwellings." (Luke 16:9)

Use what you have to make friends—friends among the poor, friends among those you might normally overlook. These are the people who will remember your kindness. These are the people who become your friends not just in this life, but in the life to come.

## The Pharisees and Money (Luke 16:9-15)

Jesus addresses this teaching directly to the Pharisees, who had been listening and sneering at his words.

> "Whoever can be trusted with very little can also be trusted with much, and whoever is dishonest with very little will also be dishonest with much. So if you have not been trustworthy in handling worldly wealth, who will trust you with true riches? And if you have not been trustworthy with someone else's property, who will give you property of your own?" (Luke 16:10-12)

The Pharisees loved money. They had missed the whole point. They should have looked out for sinners and tax collectors, helping them through generosity and kindness. Instead, they saw wealth as something to hoard and preserve. And in doing so, they isolated themselves.

> "No servant can serve two masters. Either he will hate the one and love the other, or he will be devoted to the one and despise the other. You cannot serve both God and money." (Luke 16:13)

This is the fundamental choice. You cannot love both. The Pharisees had made their choice, and it was the wrong one.

> "The Pharisees, who loved money, heard all this and were sneering at Jesus. He said to them, 'You are the ones who justify yourselves in the eyes of men, but God knows your hearts. What is highly valued among men is detestable in God's sight.'" (Luke 16:14-15)

They justified themselves before others, but God sees the heart. What they valued—wealth, status, self-righteousness—is detestable in God's eyes.

## The Consequence: Friendlessness in Eternity (Luke 16:19-31)

Later in this chapter, Jesus tells the story of the rich man and Lazarus. The rich man ignored the beggar at his gate, and in death, he found himself in Hades—alone, without friends, without comfort. The very people who would have helped him in life, had he shown them kindness, could not help him now.

This is the tragedy of hoarding wealth and ignoring the poor. You lose friendships that would have sustained you eternally.

## The Priority Shift (Luke 16:16)

> "The Law and the Prophets were proclaimed until John. Since that time, the good news of the kingdom of God is being preached, and everyone is forcing his way into it." (Luke 16:16)

Something has changed. The kingdom of God is not waiting for anyone. People are pressing into it. The old order is being displaced, and the Pharisees—who should have been leading the way—are being left behind.

Rather than condemning sinners, the Pharisees should have been welcoming them. The sinners and tax collectors are entering the kingdom. The Pharisees, with all their knowledge and advantages, are squandering their inheritance. They had the wealth of God's revelation, and they did nothing with it. Where there should have been godliness, there was nothing. Where there should have been repentance spread among the people, there was nothing. Where there should have been Gentiles coming to faith, there was nothing.

The Pharisees wasted their master's resources. And now they must choose: get on with the program, or be left out altogether.

> "You are the ones who justify yourselves in the eyes of men, but God knows your hearts." (Luke 16:15)

## Application: Don't Waste Your Inheritance

This teaching confronts us in our own time. Some of us carry the same pride as the Pharisees. We have our Christian friends, our church, our youth group. We are satisfied with our circle. We have retreated to safety.

But we have been given knowledge and resources that were not meant for us alone. When you are challenged to invite friends and share about Jesus, the common excuse is, "All my friends are Christian." If that is truly the case, then it is time to go further—to your neighbours, to your parents, to people you meet in tutoring, in school, in everyday life.

Don't waste the possessions God has given you. Don't waste your knowledge of him. The gospel is for everyone, and the time to act is now. Others are pressing into the kingdom—don't be left behind.

This Christmas, reconsider your giving. Instead of trinkets and gadgets that will be regifted or discarded, give generously to those in need. Support gospel work. Sponsor a child. Invite someone to lunch. Use your wealth—whether time, money, or knowledge—to gain friends. Make friends with God by caring for the poor and forgotten.

The shrewd manager knew which side his bread was buttered on. He made friends with his enemies. The Pharisees need the same wisdom. And so do we.
