# 1 Peter 1:1-12

**Title:** Born into a Living Hope
**Date:** October 16, 2011
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## The Privilege of Birthright

Being born in a certain land brings certain privileges. I was born in Malaysia, in a small mining town called Ipoh—not particularly distinguished except that the actress Michelle Yeoh comes from there. But the greatest privilege of birthright is citizenship itself. In Hong Kong, for example, anyone born in the territory automatically becomes a permanent resident, regardless of their parents' citizenship status. This was confirmed by a 2001 court case, which led to a remarkable surge in mainland Chinese mothers traveling to Hong Kong to give birth.

Peter writes to a scattered people—strangers and aliens without a homeland, facing persecution under successive Roman emperors. Yet he begins his letter with remarkable news: Christians have a birthright too. We are born into privileges that far exceed any earthly citizenship.

## Born into a Living Hope (v. 3)

> Praise be to the God and Father of our Lord Jesus Christ! In his great mercy he has given us new birth into a living hope through the resurrection of Jesus Christ from the dead.

Peter announces that we are born into an **inheritance**—a concept deeply rooted in Old Testament promise. Throughout Scripture, inheritance refers primarily to land:

- Genesis 31:14 records God's promise to Abraham that his descendants would inherit the land of Canaan
- Exodus 15:17 describes Moses leading the Israelite slaves into their promised inheritance
- Numbers 18:20 tells us the Levites received no land inheritance—not because they lacked God's blessing, but because they were set apart to serve the temple
- Genesis 23:17-19 shows Abraham's profound faith: though he never possessed Canaan, he purchased a burial site there for himself and Sarah
- Exodus 13:19 reveals Joseph's faith: he refused the honor of Egyptian burial and insisted his bones be taken with the exodus to the promised land

For the patriarchs and prophets understood that this promised land was where God's kingdom would be established and where the resurrection of the dead would occur.

Peter addresses a landless people—scattered across Pontus, Galatia, Cappadocia, Asia, and Bithynia, suffering trials and persecution. To them, he proclaims: you have an inheritance. You have a birthright.

## Our Inheritance Is Protected (v. 4-8)

While we await this inheritance, we are assured that it is completely protected:

> Into an inheritance that can never perish, spoil or fade—kept in heaven for you, who through faith are shielded by God's power until the coming of the salvation that is ready to be revealed in the last time.

This inheritance is:
- **Never perish** — no inflation or erosion of value
- **Never spoil** — no financial crisis can diminish it
- **Never fade** — no decay, no corruption, no theft can touch it
- **Kept in heaven** — safe from the effects of this world: no global warming, no natural disasters, no earthly decay
- **Shielded by God's power** — protected by divine authority that cannot be breached

Even the wealthiest—like Bill Gates with his fifty-eight billion dollars—cannot guarantee their inheritance will remain intact for their children. But our inheritance is protected by God Himself.

## Born into a Living Hope Amid Present Suffering (v. 6-9)

Yet notice Peter's paradox: Christians can rejoice greatly even while suffering.

> Though you have not seen him, you love him; and even though you do not see him now, you believe in him and are filled with an inexpressible and glorious joy, for you are receiving the goal of your faith, the salvation of your souls.

This is the normal Christian experience—to have not seen Jesus, yet to love Him; to not see Him now, yet to believe in Him. We are filled with an inexpressible and glorious joy, even in trials.

It is possible to be simultaneously sad and joyful—grieving present troubles while celebrating the salvation coming tomorrow. Those with doubts about God's presence, those who wish He would appear or perform miracles—this is normal. The absence of miraculous signs is, in fact, the common Christian experience. Our faith must rest not on feeling but on the word of God.

## Born of a Living Word (v. 23)

But how can we, born of earth, inherit a heavenly kingdom? How do those without earthly birthright gain access to heaven?

> For you have been born again, not of perishable seed, but of imperishable, through the living and enduring word of God.

We are born of the **living and enduring word of God**. Though we were not born in heaven, we are born of the Word from heaven. Just as individuals can gain citizenship through ancestral heritage—an Italian can claim citizenship through Italian ancestry without ever visiting Italy—so Christians gain the privileges of heaven by being born of the Word of God from heaven.

The word is imperishable, therefore its fruit—us—are also imperishable. Death no longer holds us. We are resurrected.

## The Word Spoke in the Past (v. 10-12)

How do we know this word is imperishable? Because it was alive throughout history.

> Concerning this salvation, the prophets, who spoke of the grace that was to come to you, searched intently and with the greatest care, trying to find out the time and circumstances to which the Spirit of Christ in them was pointing when he predicted the sufferings of Christ and the glories that would follow. It was revealed to them that they were not serving themselves but you, when they spoke of the things that have now been told you by those who have preached the gospel to you by the Holy Spirit sent from heaven.

The Old Testament prophets spoke the same eternal word of God. Their words were not temporary or seasonal—they were truth that stands forever:

- At creation, God spoke
- To Abraham, God spoke new promises
- Through Moses, God spoke liberation: "Let my people go"

These ancient prophets preached the gospel to us. They told us about Jesus. They predicted His sufferings and His blood on the cross. They predicted His resurrection. That same gospel that affected them affects us today—it is living and enduring.

Even the angels long to understand these things.

## The Word Will Stand Forever (v. 24-25)

And this word will continue to be alive in the future:

> All men are like grass, and all their glory is like the flowers of the field; the grass withers and the flowers fall, but the word of the Lord stands forever.

Our inheritance is possible, imperishable, because we are born of something imperishable—the eternal word of God.

## Two Modern Attacks on God's Word

We must ask: Do we believe the word of the Lord stands forever? Is the word of God from the past still relevant today?

**The first attack: Abandoning the Word through Cultural Pressure**

Culture tells us the Bible is old and outdated. We hear: "I can't follow this instruction in the Bible. Times have changed. A teacher somewhere says it means something else." So we abandon the traditional reading of Scripture. In extreme cases—like the contemporary gay marriage debate—some Christians have abandoned what the Bible clearly teaches because they believe it is too old to be relevant.

But if in doubt, we must stick to the conservative, historic reading of Scripture. Any legitimate change in interpretation must come through Scripture interpreting Scripture—not through cultural pressure or other authorities.

**The second attack: Adding to the Word through False Revelation**

While some deny what the Bible says, others add to it. The Bible does not tell us everything—should I move back to Hong Kong? Should I change jobs? Should I buy an iPad? We are tempted to fill these gaps with extra words from God: visions, dreams, voices. But how do we know these voices are truly from God and not our own desires or the influence of our peers?

The Bible is always sufficient and relevant. If it doesn't address a particular question, we may simply not be meant to know the answer—or we are meant to work it out ourselves. Perhaps we need to study Scripture more carefully, pray that we are reading it rightly, and approach it ready to listen to what it actually says rather than making it say what we want to hear.

Consider something practical: Bring your Bible to church. Open it during the sermon. Write notes. Keep a notebook if you don't want to write in the Bible itself. Keep Scripture close to you—physically, mentally, spiritually. When I travel to a foreign country, I carry my passport everywhere. The Bible is our precious passport. We are not in heaven; we are in a foreign land. Let us keep it near us always.

## Alien Born

Where are you born? What is your hope? Peter tells us: we are alien-born. We are not of this earth.

Some of you may have inadequate retirement savings that will erode over time. Some of you may struggle to enter the property market in Australia. Some of you may feel you are not leaving much inheritance for your children.

It is well. Thank God we are born of the Word of God into an inheritance that can never be destroyed.
