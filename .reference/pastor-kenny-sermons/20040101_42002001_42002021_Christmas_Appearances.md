# Luke 2:1-21

**Title:** Christmas Appearances

**Date:** 2 January 2004

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Appearances Can Be Deceiving

When we look at the Christmas story, we discover a profound truth: the world is not what it appears to be. Behind every surface lies a deeper reality about God's sovereignty and character.

## The Appearance of Augustus

### Caesar's Apparent Dominion

> In those days a decree went out from Caesar Augustus that all the world should be registered. (Luke 2:1)

Augustus stands as the most powerful man of his age. Adopted son of Julius Caesar, he consolidated Rome's power through military might and political acumen, establishing what became known as the Pax Romana—peace enforced by garrisons and the constant threat of military response to rebellion.

The empire recognized him as a god. An imperial cult formed, worshiping the emperor as divine. For over forty years, Caesar held the throne. To all appearances, the world belonged to him.

Look at the mechanism of his control in these opening verses:
- Augustus made a decree that all the world be registered
- His governors, including Quirinius, obeyed the command
- All the people went to comply

This was unprecedented—a single man controlling an entire civilization through his word alone. The registration itself demonstrates his reach: recording every person, their occupations, their property and wealth—a complete accounting of his domain, like counting shares in a portfolio to determine net worth.

### But God Truly Reigns

Yet eight centuries before Augustus was even born, the prophet Micah had written:

> But you, O Bethlehem Ephrathah, who are too little to be among the clans of Judah, from you shall come forth for me one who is to be ruler in Israel, whose origin is from of old, from ancient days. (Micah 5:2)

This is the heart of it: God ordained the birthplace of the Messiah centuries in advance. While Caesar appeared to direct events through his decree, he was unknowingly fulfilling God's eternal purpose. Joseph and Mary, responding to Caesar's command, traveled to Bethlehem—exactly where God had planned for Jesus to be born.

Who truly lasted? Caesar's name appears once a year in the calendar—August. But who celebrates Caesar's birth annually? The world remembers Jesus.

### Application: God Is in Control

This reality offers Christians tremendous comfort. Despite how it may appear—despite the secular worldview that dominates our media, the materialism that exploits the weak, the mockery of our beliefs—God remains in absolute control. He orchestrates history toward His purposes.

But it is also a warning: God's intention is that every person, every government, every knee will bow before Him. We may live as if we control our own destiny, spending freely, pursuing our desires without thought of God's will. Yet like Caesar, those who resist God's purposes will find themselves on the wrong side of history.

## The Appearance of the King

### Why Jesus Doesn't Look Like a King

Despite being the long-awaited Messiah, nothing about Jesus's birth inspires confidence. Four reasons explain this apparent defeat:

**1. Bethlehem—An Insignificant Town**

Micah describes it perfectly: "too little to be among the clans of Judah." This was a backwater village so insignificant that in times of war, it wouldn't even be summoned to fight. The name itself—Bethlehem, "house of bread"—sounds more like a bakery than the birthplace of a king.

**2. Born in a Manger**

> While they were there, the time came for her to give birth. She gave birth to her firstborn son and wrapped him in swaddling cloths and laid him in a manger, because there was no place for them in the inn. (Luke 2:6-7)

In Palestinian homes, everything existed in one room: sleeping quarters, animal stalls, and kitchen together. When the guestroom was full, Mary gave birth near the animals. The smell, the filth—hardly a place befitting royalty.

Compare this with the birth of Posh Spice's son, Romeo, born at an exclusive London hospital with a private suite, precious and pampered from birth. Now *that's* how you welcome a future king or superstar.

**3. Announced to Shepherds**

> In the same region there were shepherds out in the field, keeping watch over their flock by night. (Luke 2:8)

Shepherds occupied the lowest rung of society. They were peasants who couldn't find decent daytime work, forced to labor through the night. These are the people who receive the news of the King's birth.

Imagine if instead: when a Roman emperor was born, the world declared a holiday and celebrated with public fanfare. When Romeo was born, tabloids and television swarmed; celebrities like Elton John served as godparents. But for Jesus? A stranger finds a shepherd working the midnight shift at a petrol station. Absurd.

**4. Missing the Royal Trappings**

Unlike Matthew's account, Luke's gospel contains no star, no wise men bearing royal gifts. This is deliberate. Luke emphasizes that Christ's birth wasn't fancy. It was humble. It was poor. It was weak by earthly standards.

## Why This Humble Birth?

This poverty aligns perfectly with God's character and method. As Mary herself prophesied:

> He has brought down the mighty from their thrones and exalted those of humble estate; he has filled the hungry with good things, and the rich he has sent empty away. (Luke 1:52-53)

Paul echoes this truth:

> For you know the grace of our Lord Jesus Christ, that though he was rich, yet for your sake he became poor, so that you by his poverty might become rich. (2 Corinthians 8:9)

Not material riches—but spiritual riches. Christ's poverty became the means of our spiritual enrichment. He came not in splendor but in humility. For us living in affluent suburbs, surrounded by the relentless drive to accumulate and consume, this is difficult to grasp. Yet this is God's way: He exalts the weak and the lowly.

### Application: Rethinking Christmas

Christmas challenges our consumerism. Rather than buying another dress, another gadget we don't need, God calls us to give generously to the poor and to the gospel's work. We should be driven by Christ's example—becoming poorer ourselves so that others might be enriched.

Look for those who don't fit in. Notice those who are lonely. This is how we live out our religion: by honoring God's preference for the humble and the forgotten.

## The Appearance of the Angels

### What the Heavenly Host Sees

> The angel said to them, "Fear not, for behold, I bring you good news of a great joy that will be for all the people. For unto you is born this day in the city of David a Savior, who is Christ the Lord. And this will be a sign for you: you will find a baby wrapped in swaddling cloths and lying in a manger." Suddenly there was with the angel a multitude of the heavenly host praising God and saying, "Glory to God in the highest, and on earth peace among those with whom he is pleased!" (Luke 2:10-14)

The angels see past earthly appearances:

1. They call Bethlehem "the city of David"—a royal city, in line with God's prophecies, not a mere hole.

2. They see the manger not as derogatory but as a *sign* of God's work.

3. They recognize that despite Augustus's rule, Jesus is the true King, the Christ, the Lord.

4. They proclaim this as good news for all people on earth.

The heavenly host—God's army—stands behind this King. Thousands strong, incomparably mightier than any force Caesar can muster. And this King will establish real peace—not the Pax Romana, which merely suppresses rebellion through fear, but genuine peace between God and humanity, achieved through Christ's sacrifice.

Thirty years later, Jesus would make Himself poor in earthly life so that others might be eternally enriched. He gave His life as an atoning sacrifice for our sins. In heaven, the angels knew: this man is the Savior.

## Our Response to Christmas

### The Shepherds' Example

> The shepherds said to one another, "Let us go over to Bethlehem and see this thing that has happened, which the Lord has made known to us." They went with haste and found Mary and Joseph and the baby lying in a manger. When they saw it, they made known the saying that had been told them concerning the child. And all who heard it wondered. But Mary treasured up all these things, pondering them in her heart. The shepherds returned, glorifying and praising God for all they had heard and seen. (Luke 2:15-20)

Note their urgency: "Let us go!" They dropped everything and hurried to Bethlehem. They didn't delay; they didn't make excuses. They went at once.

### What Will You Do?

Every Christmas, the angels' message reaches us: "Fear not, for unto you is born this day in the city of David a Savior, who is Christ the Lord."

Will you go in haste to find this King?

If you are lowly, if you feel like you have nothing much going for you—excellent. You've come to the right place. This is good news for you. The powerful of the earth sleep soundly while the humble shepherds are awake to receive this word.

If Bible reading isn't part of your regular life, we invite you to investigate this Jesus—not merely as a baby in a manger, but as the risen King. Come and meet Him not just as an infant but as the man who gives His life for us.

This is a place for the lowly—where one peasant tells another peasant where to find the Messiah. We come as a gathering of humble seekers, and we are reminded that God's gospel is good news precisely for those the world overlooks.

The message of Christmas is this:

> He has brought down the mighty from their thrones and exalted those of humble estate; he has filled the hungry with good things, and the rich he has sent empty away.

Perhaps Bethlehem isn't such a loser town after all. It is, after all, the house of bread—and we come as hungry beggars searching for the bread of life.
