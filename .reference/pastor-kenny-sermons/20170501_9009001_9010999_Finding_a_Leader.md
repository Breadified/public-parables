## Introduction: The Character a Leader Reveals

This week brought news of Roger Moore's passing at 89—the actor famous for playing James Bond with humor and charm. A scriptwriter later shared a beautiful story about meeting Moore as a boy at Nice Airport. After receiving an autograph, the child noticed Moore had signed his own name rather than "James Bond." When the grandfather complained, Moore paused, glanced around mysteriously, and whispered: "I have to sign as Roger Moore because otherwise Blofeld might find out I was here." He asked the boy to keep his secret.

This spontaneous moment reveals Moore's true character—genuine warmth beneath the famous persona. As it is with Moore, so it is with leadership: our unrehearsed reactions show who we really are.

## Part 1: A Reluctant Leader (9:1-10:16)

### The Setup: Israel Demands a King

Last week we saw Israel's request: *"Grant their request and appoint a king to rule them."* (1 Samuel 8:22)

Now God raises a man who appears perfect for the role:

> There was a Benjamite, a man of standing, whose name was Kish son of Abiel, the son of Zeror, the son of Bekorath, the son of Aphiah of Benjamin. Kish had a son named Saul, as handsome a young man as could be found anywhere in Israel, and he was a head taller than anyone else. (1 Samuel 9:1-2)

Strong. Mighty. Tall. Is this the king Israel is looking for? The text will show us otherwise.

### Finding Donkeys: A Reluctant Search (9:3-5)

Saul's father sends him to find lost donkeys. But notice what happens:

- He passes through Ephraim—no donkeys found
- He searches Shalisha—nothing
- He searches Benjamin—still nothing
- Then, discouraged, he gives up

> When they reached the district of Zuph, Saul said to the servant who was with him, Come, let's go back, or my father will stop thinking about the donkeys and start worrying about us. (1 Samuel 9:5)

Here is our first glimpse of Saul's character: when the task becomes difficult, he wants to abandon it and go home. He doesn't persist. He doesn't press on.

But the servant shows more courage than the future king. Where Saul gives up, the servant takes initiative:

> The servant replied, Look, in this town there is a man of God; he is highly respected, and everything he says comes true. Let's go there now. Perhaps he will tell us what way to take. (1 Samuel 9:6)

### Finding a Coin: Another Excuse (9:7-10)

Saul's next objection is practical: *"If we go, what can we give the man? The food in our sacks is gone. We have no gift to take to the man of God."*

Again, the servant persists where Saul hesitates:

> The servant answered him again. Look, I have a quarter of a shekel of silver on me. I will give it to the man of God so that he will tell us what way to take. (1 Samuel 9:8-9)

The irony is sharp: Saul, who will be king, requires direction, advice, and even financial support from one of those he ought to lead.

### Finding the Prophet: Complete Ignorance (9:11-19)

As they climb toward town, they meet young women drawing water. These servants know far more than Saul—they can direct him to Samuel, describe his reputation, and even tell him Samuel has just arrived. They're nobody. They're maids. Yet they possess knowledge that the future king completely lacks.

When Saul finally meets Samuel face-to-face, he doesn't even recognize him:

> Saul approached Samuel in the gateway and asked, Would you please tell me where the seer's house is? I am the seer, Samuel replied. (1 Samuel 9:18-19)

How embarrassing. This is the prophet whose fame has spread throughout Israel—and Saul didn't know who he was.

### The Donkeys Finally Found (9:20-21)

Samuel tells Saul:

> As for the donkeys you lost three days ago, do not worry about them; they have been found. (1 Samuel 9:20)

Notice the pattern: throughout this opening, we keep hearing the same refrain—the donkeys have been found. Three times we're told this. Why? Because the text is drawing a parallel: Saul set out to find donkeys and failed. But he himself has been found by God. The narrative cleverly substitutes the quest for animals with the discovery of a man.

Saul's response is telling:

> But am I not a Benjamite, from the smallest tribe of Israel, and is not my clan the least of all the clans of the tribe of Benjamin? Why do you say such a thing to me? (1 Samuel 9:21)

Reluctance again. Humility, perhaps—or simply weakness and self-doubt.

### Anointed with Power (9:22-10:1)

Samuel seats Saul at the head of the feast and gives him the thigh—priestly food that symbolizes power and strength, as when Samson tore lions apart. Then Samuel anoints him with oil:

> Has not the Lord anointed you ruler over his inheritance? (1 Samuel 10:1)

This isn't a crown but a coronation nonetheless. Saul is consecrated for leadership.

### The Three Signs (10:2-8)

Samuel gives Saul three signs to expect:

**Sign 1:** He will meet two men near Rachel's tomb who will tell him the donkeys have been found.

**Sign 2:** He will meet three men at the great tree of Tabor, and they will offer him two loaves of bread.

**Sign 3:** He will come to Gibeah of God, where there is a Philistine outpost. He will meet a procession of prophets, and the Spirit of the Lord will come powerfully upon him, and he will prophesy with them.

Then comes a crucial instruction:

> Once these signs are fulfilled, do whatever your hand finds to do, for God is with you. Go down ahead of me to Gilgal. I will surely come down to you to sacrifice burnt offerings and fellowship offerings, but you must wait seven days until I come to you and tell you what you are to do. (1 Samuel 10:7-8)

This phrase—"do whatever your hand finds to do"—is language used for war. In Judges 9:33, it describes rushing upon a city in battle. God is enlisting Saul as a military leader against the Philistines who oppress Israel.

### What Saul Actually Did (10:9-16)

We're told:

> As Saul turned to leave Samuel, God changed Saul's heart, and all these signs were fulfilled that day. (1 Samuel 10:9)

And indeed, the last sign occurs:

> When he and his servant arrived at Gibeah, a procession of prophets met him; the Spirit of God came powerfully upon him, and he joined in their prophesying. When all those who had formerly known him saw him prophesying with the prophets, they asked each other, What is this that has happened to the son of Kish? Is Saul also among the prophets? (1 Samuel 10:10-11)

It becomes a national saying: *Is Saul also among the prophets?*

But here's what's troubling: what was Saul supposed to do after these signs? "Do whatever your hand finds to do"—declare war on the Philistines. That's what "your hand finds to do" means. That's what the Spirit of the Lord empowers judges to do throughout Scripture:

> The Spirit of the Lord came on him, so that he became Israel's judge and went to war. (Judges 3:10)

> Then the Spirit of the Lord came on Gideon, and he blew a trumpet. (Judges 6:34)

> The Spirit of the Lord came powerfully upon him so that he tore the lion apart with his bare hands. (Judges 14:6)

The Spirit gives strength for war. Saul was changed into another man in order to become God's military deliverer.

Instead of going to war, what did Saul do?

> After Saul stopped prophesying, he went to the high place. (1 Samuel 10:13)

And then we learn:

> Saul replied, He assured us that the donkeys had been found. But he did not tell his uncle what Samuel had said about the kingship. (1 Samuel 10:16)

He told no one. He went home. He did nothing. Three times in this section we're reminded that the donkeys were found—but the real task, the calling to war, remains untouched. Saul had the opportunity to prove himself as God's chosen leader through military victory, as the judges had been chosen before him. Instead, he retreated into silence.

Perhaps he was afraid. Perhaps he knew he had failed. Either way, his silence reveals his heart: reluctant, weak, fearful—hardly the profile of a true leader.

## Part 2: A Leader Cannot Be Found (10:17-27)

### The Selection by Lot

Samuel summons all Israel to Mizpah (the very place where God's watching presence was first proclaimed in Genesis 31:49). He reminds them:

> This is what the Lord, the God of Israel, says: I brought Israel up out of Egypt, and I delivered you from the power of Egypt and all the kingdoms that oppressed you. But you have now rejected your God, who saves you out of all your disasters and calamities. And you have said, No, appoint a king over us. (1 Samuel 10:18-19)

They wanted to reject God's direct leadership. Now they will have their wish—but God's hand will guide even this.

The selection proceeds by lot, tribe by tribe, clan by clan. The lot falls on Benjamin. Then on Matri's clan. Then on Saul son of Kish.

> But when they looked for him, he was not to be found. So they inquired further of the Lord, Has the man come here yet? And the Lord said, Yes, he has hidden himself among the supplies. (1 Samuel 10:21-22)

The future king of Israel—the tallest man in the nation—is hiding among the bags of supplies. When they bring him out, Samuel announces:

> Do you see the man the Lord has chosen? There is no one like him among all the people. (1 Samuel 10:24)

Is this sarcasm? The author certainly seems to suggest it. Here stands the man selected by God—hiding like a coward.

The people shout, *Long live the king!* But not everyone. Some call out:

> How can this fellow save us? They despised him and brought him no gifts. But Saul kept silent. (1 Samuel 10:27)

Again, Saul's silence. He speaks nothing. He judges nothing. He does nothing.

## Part 3: What Makes a True Leader?

### The Character of Leadership

Looking at Saul's portrait, we see what he is not. A true leader is not passive. A true leader does not ask permission to serve but sees a good task that needs doing and does it.

Listen to Paul in 1 Timothy:

> Whoever aspires to be an overseer desires a noble task. (1 Timothy 3:1)

The word "desires" is active—eager, reaching toward. Leadership is not something one stumbles into reluctantly. It's a calling you *want* to answer because you see good work that must be done.

> Leadership is seeing a good task that needs to be done and then doing it. It involves troubleshooting and fixing. A desire to serve—because you know it's good, not necessarily for yourself, but for everyone.

This is why consultants are not leaders. A consultant sees a problem, provides ten solutions, takes payment, and leaves. A leader actually does the work. Leadership requires getting your hands dirty, implementing those lofty ideals in the messy reality of people's actual lives. It's not authority to tell others to do work you've thought of while sitting comfortably apart—it's the willingness to serve yourself.

Jesus made this clear:

> Not so with you. Instead, whoever wants to become great among you must be your servant, and whoever wants to be first must be slave of all. (Mark 10:43-44)

### Leaders Must Be Willing

Throughout Scripture, God honors those who volunteer for His service. Even in the law, provision is made for willing service. Deuteronomy mandates that soldiers be allowed to opt out of war:

> The officers shall say to the army: Has anyone built a new house and not yet begun to live in it? Let him go home... Has anyone become pledged to a woman and not married her? Let him go home so that his fellow soldiers will not become disheartened. (Deuteronomy 20:5-7)

And crucially:

> Is anyone afraid or fainthearted? Let him go home so that his fellow soldiers will not become disheartened. (Deuteronomy 20:8)

God wants willing soldiers, not conscripts. The Psalms celebrate this:

> When the princes in Israel take the lead, when the people willingly offer themselves—praise the Lord! My heart is with Israel's princes, with the willing volunteers among the people. (Judges 5:2, 9)

### A Leader Must Speak

One final charge: a leader must not be silent. When confronted with injustice or error, a true leader speaks. Saul, selected by God, should have rebuked those who mocked him. Instead, he kept silent.

Consider Jesus finding the donkey—quite different from Saul's fruitless search:

> Go to the village ahead of you, and as you enter it, you will find a colt tied there, which no one has ever ridden. Untie it and bring it here. If anyone asks you, Why are you untying it? say, The Lord needs it. (Luke 19:28-31)

Jesus didn't hesitate. He didn't hide among the supplies. He spoke with authority, claiming what belonged to His purpose. When the crowds later hailed Him:

> Blessed is the king who comes in the name of the Lord! Peace in heaven and glory in the highest! (Luke 19:38)

He accepted their praise. He was the true servant-king, willing to speak truth and act decisively.

## Application: For Leaders in Our Congregation

We have many leaders at every level of responsibility and commitment. Thank you. You serve because you desire to do good work. That's noble.

If you aspire to leadership—write on a comment card. We need people willing to step into the work, not merely to consult about it. We need courage. We need initiative. We need willingness to serve.

But we also need to understand what we're being called to. A leader keeps watch, as Hebrews reminds us:

> Have confidence in your leaders and submit to their authority, because they keep watch over you as those who must give an account. Do this so that their work will be a joy, not a burden, for that would be of no benefit to you. (Hebrews 13:17)

Leaders must be equipped to rebuke and judge gently, to encourage daily:

> See to it, brothers and sisters, that none of you has a sinful, unbelieving heart that turns away from the living God. But encourage one another daily, as long as it is called Today, so that none of you may be hardened by sin's deceitfulness. (Hebrews 3:12-13)

And remember: God will equip you. The Spirit is given:

> Each of you should use whatever gift you have received to serve others, as faithful stewards of God's grace in its various forms. If anyone speaks, they should do so as one who speaks the very words of God. If anyone serves, they should do so with the strength God provides, so that in all things God may be praised through Jesus Christ. (1 Peter 4:10-11)

---

## Conclusion: What Israel Found Instead

The Israelites set out to find a king. What did they find instead? The text circles back repeatedly to one phrase: *the donkeys have been found*.

In the end, they found the donkeys. But they lost the opportunity for God's true king—because when Saul was empowered and called to lead, he hid among the supplies and kept silent.

May we not make his mistake. May we step boldly into the good work God calls us to. May we speak truth. May we serve with willing hearts. That is the mark of true leadership.
