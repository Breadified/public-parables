# Ruth 1:1-2:23

**Title:** Jesus's Loyalty and God's Undeserved Kindness
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Ruth 1: When Life Moves Us to Crisis

### The Journey into Hardship

Like many of us, the story of Ruth begins with movement. I've experienced this myself—moving countries, switching schools, relocating homes. Ruth's family faced the same: they moved from Bethlehem (literally "the house of bread") to Moab, a foreign land outside Israel's borders. It's like leaving Australia for New Zealand, stepping outside the place of blessing into uncertain territory.

But disaster struck. Death claimed Elimelech and his sons, leaving Naomi, Ruth, and Orpah in devastating poverty. In an agricultural society, the men were the breadwinners. Without them, these women had no income, no food, no security. They were bankrupt and alone.

### Two Ways to Face Suffering

When crisis came, Naomi and Ruth responded very differently.

**Naomi's Response:** Her name means "pleasant," and she had treated her daughters-in-law with genuine kindness, releasing them from their obligations to her. Yet her treatment of God was bitter. She became angry, blaming Him with a sarcastic tone: "The Lord's hand has gone out against me" (Ruth 1:13). She repeated this accusation multiple times, treating God as though He were deliberately trying to make her life miserable.

But here's the truth: Naomi's family had chosen to leave the promised land first. They departed from Bethlehem—the home of food—and moved to Moab, an enemy of Israel. Yes, they fled because of famine, but verse 6 shows that the famine was temporary. They moved away from God, then blamed God for their suffering.

**Ruth's Response:** Ruth faced identical suffering. She lost her husband. As a widow in that culture, she had lost her family and her place in society. As a Moabite, she would face discrimination if she returned to Judah. Yet even in this darkness, she made a stunning declaration: "Where you go I will go, and where you stay I will stay. Your people will be my people and your God my God" (Ruth 1:16-17).

Ruth stayed loyal to Naomi, yes—but more importantly, she stayed loyal to God. She clung to Him while Naomi complained against Him. She moved toward God, toward the promised land.

### Jesus: The Ultimate Ruth

Ruth's loyalty foreshadows how Jesus responded to suffering. He faced persecution, betrayal, abandonment, and finally death—suffering He didn't deserve, as He had never departed from God. Yet when tempted to cry out bitterly, He prayed, "Not my will, but yours" (Luke 22:42). He didn't curse God; He stayed loyal. He remained on the cross to bear the punishment for those who had moved from God—for Naomi, for you, for me.

When it was finished, He moved to the ultimate promised land: heaven, the home not just of bread, but of friends, family, comfort, security, and peace.

### How We Should Respond

When suffering comes—and it will—we must ask ourselves: Do we see trials as God embittering us? Or as God intending good, like a test designed to strengthen us?

Some of you are facing hard things right now: struggles at school, difficulty at home, fractured relationships, or the strangeness of moving to a new place or country. Others may face betrayal, disappointment, conflict, anxiety, or loss. But we must respond like Ruth: cling to God, knowing He loves you and has the best for you.

## Ruth 2: The Kindness of a Stranger

### Love at First Sight

Valentine's Day teaches us about love—that mysterious emotion where hearts flutter for no apparent reason. We see a hint of this in Ruth 2, as the friendship between Ruth and Boaz begins to grow.

### Gleaning and Generosity

Desperate and poor, Ruth went to glean in the fields—collecting grain left behind by the harvesters. This was God's law in Israel: don't harvest to the edges of your field or pick up what falls. Leave it for the poor and foreigners (Leviticus 19:9-10). 

But the field Ruth entered belonged to Boaz, a man of standing and reputation. When he arrived and saw her, something shifted: "Who is that young woman?" (Ruth 2:5).

### Undeserved Kindness

Boaz showed Ruth extraordinary kindness—not because she had earned it, but simply because he chose to:

- He told her to stay in his field where it was safe, not to wander where she might be harmed
- He instructed his men not to touch her
- He provided her water as though she were family
- At mealtime, he gave her bread, wine, and roasted grain—more than she needed
- He told his workers to leave extra grain among the sheaves, even to pull stalks and drop them for her to gather
- She collected an ephah—about 35 liters, as much as one person could carry—to bring home to Naomi

It was like someone buying you unlimited food and drink at the canteen, then giving you the rest of the canteen's supplies to share with your friends, all while you were a stranger.

### The Logic of Love

Why did Boaz do this? Ruth was a foreigner, a nobody. She had come seeking refuge, trusting in God. And yes—he loved her at first sight.

Love is illogical, isn't it? There's no reason for it, just a fondness that compels you to show kindness. When you love someone, you want to spend time with them, give them gifts, take care of them. That's not calculated; that's love.

This is exactly how God loves us.

We are foreigners in His eyes. We have sinned against Him; we have disobeyed Him. Yet He loves us. I don't know why. I can't explain it. But just as love is sometimes inexplicable, so His love for us transcends reason. He genuinely cares. He genuinely wants to share everything He has with us, offering us undeserved kindness.

He demonstrates this love most fully through Jesus:

> Remember that at that time you were separate from Christ, excluded from citizenship in Israel and foreigners to the covenants of the promise, without hope and without God in the world. But now in Christ Jesus you who once were far away have been brought near through the blood of Christ. (Ephesians 2:12-13)

Through Jesus, God hasn't given us mere gleanings—He's given us the entire harvest. He's given us eternal life.

### Living Out God's Generosity

If God shows us such love, how should we live?

Take refuge in the God of Israel, under whose wings you have come to take refuge (Ruth 2:12). And spare a thought for those in need around you. As God shows His love to us, let us show love to others.

Notice something in the practice of gleaning: it wasn't about being calculatingly efficient. The landowner deliberately left grain behind. Some of us could be more academically successful if we devoted every ounce of energy to school. But we keep Sundays free, Friday nights free, Friday lunchtimes free—not to maximize our achievement, but to care for friends in our church and youth group, to encourage each other, to look out for one another.

Let's keep doing that, knowing that God loved us first.

# Ruth 3

**Title:** Taking the Initiative in Faith
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## A Misunderstood Scene

When many people read Ruth 3, they see something suspicious:
- Clothes and perfume suggest seduction
- Nighttime secrecy implies shame
- The phrase "lie at his feet" sounds inappropriate
- Some even detect sexual innuendo

But this interpretation contradicts the entire point of Ruth's story. Boaz is characterized by kindness; Ruth is marked by noble character. They are righteous people. We need another explanation.

## The Reversal of Moab's History

To understand this chapter, we must remember that Ruth is a Moabite—and Moab's history is morally compromised.

In Genesis 19, Lot's daughters feared their family line would die out, so they got their father drunk and lay with him incestuously. Their son was named Moab. Because of this origin, Deuteronomy 23:3 declared, "No Moabite shall be admitted to the assembly of the Lord."

Ruth 3 contains striking parallels to this story: Naomi wants to preserve her family line, Boaz has been drinking, Ruth lies at the feet of her kinsman, and he gives her grain. But here's the reversal: Ruth is not sexually immoral. What's happening is that Ruth, a Moabite, is reversing her ancestral history. She's breaking the cycle of shame.

## The Righteous Moab

Read in this light, the chapter tells a very different story:

- Naomi tells Ruth to get out of her mourning clothes and put on perfume—the signs of returning to normal life
- The threshing floor is a public place of work, full of people
- Ruth uncovers Boaz's feet to wake him respectfully—how you wake an honored elder
- It works; he wakes and asks, "Who are you?"
- Ruth says something remarkable: "Spread the corner of your garment over me" (Ruth 3:9)

That word "corner" is the same Hebrew word for "wing"—the very word from chapter 2: "the God of Israel, under whose wings you have come to take refuge." Ruth is asking Boaz for refuge, for protection, for help.

## Ruth Takes the Initiative

What Ruth is doing is extraordinary for her time: she's proposing to Boaz. She takes the initiative because she's desperate for help. She acts decisively, asking the man who has already shown her undeserved generosity. Perhaps, she reasons, he will help her again.

And Boaz, humbled and honored, says yes—though a complication awaits, which we'll see next week.

## Active Faith vs. Passive Faith

Ruth and Boaz's story is analogous to our relationship with God. Like Ruth, we must take the initiative to seek out the God who has shown us His kindness.

Yet many of us live passively: "If God really wants me to believe in Him, He'll appear to me." "If God really wants me to follow Him, He'll make me feel motivated." This is passive faith. This is doing nothing.

But Ruth's story says: seek God. Go to Him. Just as Ruth said to Boaz, "Protect me," let us say to God: "Protect me. I know the world is not always safe, not always certain, not always predictable. I know I am too often selfish; I know I have failed morally. So please, protect me."

> Seek the Lord while he may be found! (Isaiah 55:6)

# Ruth 4

**Title:** The Ultimate Love Story—Jesus as Our Redeemer
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Final Complication

Every love story needs a complication. In Ruth's story, that complication arrives: another man also has the legal right to marry Ruth and redeem the land.

Boaz calls this man to the gate and proposes the redemption: "Will you redeem the land?"

## What Does Redemption Mean?

To redeem means to pay something in order to get something back—like redeeming a mortgage. Your land goes into the bank's hands, and you must pay to get it back.

Elimelech, Ruth's deceased husband, had owned land, but because he was poor during the famine, it was essentially held by others. Someone needed to pay the price to redeem it.

The other kinsman redeemer says yes: "I'll redeem the land!" Land is valuable; he wants it.

But there's a catch.

## The True Cost

"You'll also have to marry Ruth, the dead man's widow," Boaz explains (Ruth 4:5).

In Israel's practice, if a man died without children, his closest unmarried relative could marry his widow. The first son born to them would bear the dead man's name, preserving his family line. But this meant the redeemer's inheritance would be divided. If he had no sons of his own, everything might go to a son bearing a different name.

"I can't do it," the man says. "It might endanger my own estate" (Ruth 4:6).

He removes his sandal—a contract sealed—and walks away.

Boaz steps forward. He takes on the danger. He endangers his own estate to be with Ruth. He pays the price to redeem the land. He risks his name, his inheritance, his future.

## Jesus: The Kinsman Redeemer

Boaz points us to someone far greater. Look at verse 21: David. David's descendant. Jesus.

Jesus endangered His life to redeem His people.

From eternity, He stepped into time and became a man. From immortality, He became mortal. When the hurdle to loving us was death itself, when the price of our redemption was His sacrifice and His blood, He paid it.

> Greater love has no one than this: to lay down one's life for one's friends. (John 15:13)

I know many people have sacrificed much for us out of love. But only One has laid down His life for you and me. He loves us more deeply still.

God, in Jesus, loves you and me. This is the greatest love story ever told.

## The Story of God's Love for Us

But the Book of Ruth is an even better love story than any earthly romance—because it's not ultimately about Ruth. It's about God's love for us.

**God is always at work.** Even through suffering, He makes things better. Ruth and Naomi began chapter one poor, destitute, and empty. They end the book full.

**God shows undeserved generosity.** Like Boaz, He showers His people with mercy upon mercy.

**We must respond actively.** Like Ruth, we ought to seek Him out while He may be found. He is near. In His Bible, we have free access to everything we need to know about Him.

**No hurdle can stop His love.** Not distance, not our sin, not death itself. He loves us relentlessly.

We've journeyed through Ruth's story these past four weeks. I hope you've found it truly beautiful. 

Next week, we turn our attention to the Man Ruth's story pointed toward all along. We'll explore Jesus through the Gospel of Mark. Come ready to encounter the greatest love story ever told.

> Christ Jesus gave himself for us to redeem us from all wickedness and to make us his very own people, eager to do what is good. (Titus 2:14)
