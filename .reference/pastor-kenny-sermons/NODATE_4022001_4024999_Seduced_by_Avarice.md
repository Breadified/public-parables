# Numbers 22-24

**Title:** Seduced by Avarice
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction: The Lobbyist

Israel stands at the edge of the Promised Land after forty years in the wilderness. They have just defeated Og and Sihon—their first military victories in four hundred years. Now, camping on the plains of Moab along the Jordan River, they face opposition not from direct attack, but from an ancient form of influence-peddling: a professional lobbyist named Balaam.

This passage teaches us about the danger of greed and how it corrupts even those who claim to speak for God.

## 1. Balaam the False Prophet (22:1-7)

### The Setting

> Then the Israelites travelled to the plains of Moab and camped along the Jordan across from Jericho. (Numbers 22:1)

Balak, king of Moab, sees Israel's encampment and grows terrified, despite Israel having no intention to attack. The Moabites were distant cousins to Israel (descendants of Lot, Abraham's nephew) and could have welcomed them peacefully. Instead, Balak's insecurity leads him to hire a lobbyist.

### Who Was Balaam?

Balaam was a diviner who claimed access to God through sorcery, magic, and astrology. Balak believed that Balaam could manipulate divine blessing or curse through his supernatural abilities. Balak sent messengers with a fee for divination:

> A people has come out of Egypt; they cover the face of the land and have settled next to me. Now come and put a curse on these people, because they are too powerful for me. Perhaps then I will be able to defeat them and drive them out of the country. For I know that those you bless are blessed, and those you curse are cursed. (Numbers 22:5-6)

### The Character of False Teachers

The Old Testament and New Testament reveal Balaam's true nature. He is compared with false teachers throughout Scripture—those who claim to speak for God but actually serve their own greed.

> They will be paid back with harm for the harm they have done. Their idea of pleasure is to carouse in broad daylight. They are blots and blemishes, revelling in their pleasures while they feast with you. With eyes full of adultery, they never stop sinning; they seduce the unstable; they are experts in greed—an accursed brood! They have left the straight way and wandered off to follow the way of Balaam son of Beor, who loved the wages of wickedness. (2 Peter 2:13-15)

> Woe to them! They have taken the way of Cain; they have rushed for profit into Balaam's error; they have been destroyed in Korah's rebellion. (Jude 1:11)

Balaam represents the worst kind of influence-peddler: the greedy kind. He is motivated entirely by the fee:

> The elders of Moab and Midian left, taking with them the fee for divination. When they came to Balaam, they told him what Balak had said. (Numbers 22:7)

This pattern repeats throughout history. Modern false teachers operate the same way—televangelist "miracle crusades," prosperity gospel preachers promising wealth for donations, personalities selling influence. Their greed disguises itself as spirituality.

## 2. Israel Cannot Be Cursed (22:8-22)

### God's Covenant Cannot Be Broken

God's first response to Balaam is clear:

> But God said to Balaam, "Do not go with them. You must not put a curse on those people, because they are blessed." (Numbers 22:12)

This command rests on God's eternal covenant with Abraham:

> I will bless those who bless you, and whoever curses you I will curse; and all peoples on earth will be blessed through you. (Genesis 12:3)

God—not Balaam—determines blessing and curse. The promises made to Abraham are permanent. They are fulfilled through Christ, and through His blood, that blessing extends to all who walk by faith.

### The Heart of Disobedience

Balaam obediently refuses Balak's first offer and tells the messengers to return home. But Balak persists with more messengers, more prestige, and more money:

> Do not let anything keep you from coming to me, because I will reward you handsomely and do whatever you say. Come and put a curse on these people for me. (Numbers 22:16-17)

Balaam's response seems righteous:

> Even if Balak gave me his palace filled with silver and gold, I could not do anything great or small to go beyond the command of the LORD my God. (Numbers 22:18)

Yet something shifts in his heart. He says he will seek God's counsel again, as though the answer might change:

> Now stay here tonight as the others did, and I will find out what else the LORD will tell me. (Numbers 22:19)

God's second response reveals the problem:

> That night God came to Balaam and said, "Since these men have come to summon you, go with them, but do only what I tell you." (Numbers 22:20)

The word translated "since" can also mean "if." God's meaning: *If they ask again, then go with them.* But they don't ask again. Yet Balaam goes anyway:

> Balaam got up in the morning, saddled his donkey and went with the princes of Moab. But God was very angry when he went. (Numbers 22:21-22)

### Why God Was Angry

Balaam tried to manipulate God by asking an already-answered question. He pretended not to know God's will while secretly hoping for a different answer. He was behaving like Balak himself—trying to negotiate, to find a loophole, to lobby God into changing His mind.

This reflects a pattern in our own lives. We ask God for guidance while already knowing His answer:

- *Should I marry a non-Christian?* (We know from Scripture: no.)
- *Should I pursue this unhealthy habit?* (We know: no.)
- *Should I serve in ministry when I'm capable?* (We know: yes.)

We lobby God. We cajole Him with promises ("If You let me do this, I'll be faithful in that"). We negotiate. We say "maybe" when God has said "no."

> We are trying to lobby him. Cajole him with rewards and threats. Try to negotiate. But when we ignore God's will with feigned ignorance or wishful thinking—God is angry. For God is constant. He does not change his mind as easily as ours changes.

## 3. Balaam and the Donkey (22:22-38)

### A Stubborn Servant Rebuked

God sends an angel with a drawn sword to block Balaam's path. Three times, the donkey sees what Balaam cannot see and turns away to safety. Three times, Balaam beats the donkey, furious at her apparent stubbornness:

> When the donkey saw the angel of the LORD standing in the road with a drawn sword in his hand, she turned off the road into a field. Balaam beat her to get her back on the road. (Numbers 22:23-24)

This happens again in a narrow vineyard path, crushing Balaam's foot. And a third time in a place with no room to turn:

> When the donkey saw the angel of the LORD, she lay down under Balaam, and he was angry and beat her with his staff. (Numbers 22:27)

### The Donkey Speaks

Then comes the extraordinary moment:

> Then the LORD opened the donkey's mouth, and she said to Balaam, "What have I done to you to make you beat me these three times?" (Numbers 22:28)

Balaam's response reveals his character:

> You have made a fool of me! If I had a sword in my hand, I would kill you right now. (Numbers 22:29)

But the donkey responds with reason:

> Am I not your own donkey, which you have always ridden, to this day? Have I been in the habit of doing this to you? (Numbers 22:30)

Balaam finally admits the truth: "No."

### Eyes Opened

Only then does God open Balaam's eyes:

> Then the LORD opened Balaam's eyes, and he saw the angel of the LORD standing in the road with his sword drawn. So he bowed low and fell facedown. (Numbers 22:31)

The angel rebukes him:

> Why have you beaten your donkey these three times? I have come here to oppose you because your path is a reckless one before me. The donkey saw me and turned away from me these three times. If she had not turned away, I would certainly have killed you by now, but I would have spared her. (Numbers 22:32-33)

Balaam repents:

> I have sinned. I did not realize you were standing in the road to oppose me. Now if you are displeased, I will go back. (Numbers 22:34)

But the angel gives him a new command: Go with the men, but speak only what God tells you. Balaam's repentance comes too late to save him from what follows.

### The Irony

There is a pattern here that will repeat with Balak. Just as Balaam beat the donkey three times to force her onto his chosen path, Balak will try three times to force Balaam to curse Israel. And just as the donkey becomes God's instrument despite being beaten, Balaam becomes God's instrument despite his greed.

## 4. Balaam's Blessings (22:39-24:14)

### First Blessing: The People

After ritual sacrifice, God meets Balaam and puts a message in his mouth. Instead of a curse, Balaam speaks blessing:

> How can I curse those whom God has not cursed? How can I denounce those whom the LORD has not denounced? From the rocky peaks I see them, from the heights I view them. I see a people who live apart and do not consider themselves one of the nations. Who can count the dust of Jacob or number the fourth part of Israel? Let me die the death of the righteous, and may my end be like theirs! (Numbers 23:8-10)

Balak is furious:

> What have you done to me? I brought you to curse my enemies, but you have done nothing but bless them! (Numbers 23:11)

Balaam's response is simple:

> Must I not speak what the LORD puts in my mouth? (Numbers 23:12)

### Second Blessing: The Strength

Balak tries again from a different location. Once more, God meets Balaam and puts a message in his mouth. This time the blessing focuses on God's faithfulness:

> God is not a man, that he should lie, nor a son of man, that he should change his mind. Does he speak and then not act? Does he promise and not fulfill? I have received a command to bless; he has blessed, and I cannot change it. (Numbers 23:19-20)

> No misfortune is seen in Jacob, no misery observed in Israel. The LORD their God is with them; the shout of the King is among them. God brought them out of Egypt; they have the strength of a wild ox. There is no sorcery against Jacob, no divination against Israel. The people rise like a lioness; they rouse themselves like a lion that does not rest till he devours his prey. (Numbers 23:21-24)

Again, Balak protests:

> Neither curse them at all nor bless them at all! (Numbers 23:25)

And Balaam responds:

> Did I not tell you I must do whatever the LORD says? (Numbers 23:26)

### Third Blessing: The Place

Balak makes one final attempt. Balaam sees that it pleases the Lord to bless Israel, and he turns his face toward the desert. The Spirit of God comes upon him:

> The oracle of Balaam son of Beor, the oracle of one whose eye sees clearly, the oracle of one who hears the words of God, who sees a vision from the Almighty, who falls prostrate, and whose eyes are opened: How beautiful are your tents, O Jacob, your dwelling places, O Israel! Like valleys they spread out, like gardens beside a river, like aloes planted by the LORD, like cedars beside the waters. Water will flow from their buckets; their seed will have abundant water. Their king will be greater than Agag; their kingdom will be exalted. (Numbers 24:3-7)

Balak's anger burns:

> I summoned you to curse my enemies, but you have blessed them these three times. Now leave at once and go home! I said I would reward you handsomely, but the LORD has kept you from being rewarded. (Numbers 24:10-11)

### The Lesson of the Hired Voice

There is a profound irony in Balaam's position. A donkey speaks God's truth while Balaam, the professional speaker, is reduced to being a tool. Just as the donkey carried Balaam against his will, Balaam now carries God's word against his preference. He becomes what he despised—a voice without choice.

> Balaam is a voice for hire. He doesn't care what he speaks as long as he gets paid. Greed is his motivation, not his content. In this, he is no better than a donkey. A donkey is thoughtless and stupid. It doesn't discriminate what it carries on its back. Just as long as it gets its feed and carrots at the end of the day, it is happy. A person who simply does what he is told without discrimination, just for greed, is like a donkey.

This speaks directly to those who use their voices in the world. Lawyers, doctors, teachers, marketers, accountants—all are voices for hire in some sense. The question is: will you speak truth, or will you compromise for payment?

> If anyone speaks, he should do it as one speaking the very words of God. If anyone serves, he should do it with the strength God provides, so that in all things God may be praised through Jesus Christ. To him be the glory and the power for ever and ever. Amen. (1 Peter 4:11)

If we don't, we are nothing more than a mule for hire.

## 5. God Lobbies for Us (24:15-25)

### The Messianic Vision

Balaam's final oracle points beyond his own time to the coming Messiah:

> I see him, but not now; I behold him, but not near. A star will come out of Jacob; a scepter will rise out of Israel. He will crush the foreheads of Moab, the skulls of all the sons of Sheth. Edom will be conquered; Seir, his enemy, will be conquered, but Israel will grow strong. A ruler will come out of Jacob and destroy the survivors of the city. (Numbers 24:17-19)

Balaam's one moment of clarity reveals what God has always been doing. While human lobbyists scheme and negotiate, God is at work securing blessing for His people. Two thousand years later, that promised Messiah came. Magi—diviners and astrologers like Balaam—saw a star and followed it. But unlike Balaam, they chose to worship:

> The oracle of Balaam son of Beor speaks of a star rising out of Jacob. About two thousand years ago, the Messiah did come. Some people—magi, diviners, astrologers—did see that star. But they did something different from Balaam. They voluntarily followed it and came to worship Jesus.

### The Real Lobbyist

The great reversal of this passage is that we need not fear the lobbyists of this world. God Himself is lobbying for us:

> Don't worry. God cannot be thwarted. He will protect His people. Stay faithful to Him.

And Jesus continues to intercede on our behalf before the Father. Our integrity and beliefs cannot be bought by greed. The world's systems of influence will fail. God's purpose will stand.

## Conclusion

The story of Balaam is ultimately about God's unstoppable commitment to His people. Even when hired to curse, even when tempted by money, even when resisting through stubborn disobedience, God's word stands firm. Israel cannot be cursed because God has blessed them. And through Christ, that blessing extends to us.

The question for us is simple: Will we be voices for truth, or will we be mules for hire? Will we use whatever influence we have to point others to Christ, or will we compromise for payment? Will we trust that God lobbies for us, or will we try to lobby God into blessing our own agendas?

Let our answer be clear. Let our integrity be uncompromised. And let our voices declare the greatness of the God who fights for His people.
