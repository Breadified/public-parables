# Revelation 12:7-17

**Title:** War in Heaven  
**Date:** June 7, 2015  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## A War in Heaven

The vision John received speaks of a cosmic battle between spiritual forces. This is no earthly conflict but a war waged in the heavenly realms—a reality that shapes everything we see on earth.

### The Combatants

> Then war broke out in heaven. Michael and his angels fought against the dragon, and the dragon and his angels fought back.

**Michael** represents Jesus, whose name means "like God" (Micah = like Yahweh). While some view Michael merely as an angelic prince, Scripture describes him in terms that echo Jesus's role—Daniel 10:21 calls him "your prince," language reserved for the Messiah. Jesus is the non-angelic leader commanding an army of angels.

**The Dragon** embodies Satan—the ancient serpent who "leads the whole world astray" with lies and half-truths, just as he did in the Garden of Eden with his deceptive promise: "You shall not die."

### A Complete Victory

> But he was not strong enough, and they lost their place in heaven.

The outcome is decisive. Jesus prevails completely. The word "hurled" is repeated three times to emphasize Satan's total defeat—cast down from heaven, cast to the earth, his position utterly destroyed.

> Now have come the salvation and the power and the kingdom of our God, and the authority of his Messiah.

Jesus sits at the right hand of God with all authority and power. The world belongs to His kingdom. During His earthly ministry, He demonstrated this victory repeatedly: overcoming temptation in the desert, driving out demons, conquering the powers of sin. He won then, and He wins now.

### Our Victory in His Victory

This cosmic victory has profound implications for us:

> For the accuser of our brothers and sisters, who accuses them before our God day and night, has been hurled down.

Satan's role as "the accuser" is defeated. He brings charges against us—and he is right about our sins; we have all done things we regret and feel ashamed of. But Jesus absorbs those crushing accusations, taking them upon Himself at the cross, so we are free.

> They triumphed over him by the blood of the Lamb and by the word of their testimony; they did not love their lives so much as to shrink from death.

Our triumph rests on two pillars: Christ's sacrificial death and our faithful witness. When soldiers liberated a nation, civilians won freedom they did nothing to earn. Similarly, Jesus won the decisive battle, and we inherit His victory. Just as Anzac soldiers' triumph became our freedom, so Christ's triumph becomes ours.

> Therefore rejoice, you heavens and you who dwell in them!

There is cause for celebration. God has won. Good has prevailed over evil. Nothing can separate us from God's love—not because we are stronger than evil, but because Jesus is.

## War on Earth

Yet there remains a problem: the victory looks incomplete. Open the newspapers, check social media, and we see a different reality.

### The Continuing Conflict

> But woe to the earth and the sea, because the devil has gone down to you! He is filled with fury, because he knows that his time is short.

Around the world, Christians face unprecedented persecution. The media largely ignores it, but Christianity is the most persecuted religion globally. Eleven Christians are killed every hour—273 per day, over 100,000 yearly. In the Middle East, faith communities that have existed for 2,000 years are being eradicated by violent extremism. Even in the West, religious freedom erodes; Christians face exclusion from public discourse and legal action simply for adhering to biblical teaching.

Personally, we may face attacks from circumstances, society, even family. It can feel like Satan is winning. If God's victory is complete, why does the war continue?

### An Important Distinction

> When the dragon saw that he had been hurled to the earth, he pursued the woman who had given birth to the male child.

The "woman" represents God's people; the "male child" is Jesus. Satan now pursues not the risen Christ but His followers.

Consider the analogy of Japanese soldiers who, decades after World War II ended, continued fighting in remote islands because they didn't believe in surrender. Sixty years later, elderly men emerged with rusted guns and tattered uniforms. The war was won; they could still harm you with those corroded weapons, but they could not kill you. It was war with no ammunition.

Similarly, Satan is a defeated enemy with limited time and power. He can harm believers but cannot take us from God's grasp.

> The woman was given the two wings of a great eagle, so that she might fly to the place prepared for her in the wilderness, where she would be taken care of for a time, times and half a time, out of the serpent's reach.

God's people are protected and sustained. The timeframe—"a time, times and half a time" (essentially three and a half years, or 1,260 days)—signifies that Satan's remaining time is limited. In biblical symbolism, seven represents completion; half of seven means the worst is past. We are in the downhill stretch of history.

> Then the dragon was enraged at the woman and went off to wage war against the rest of her offspring—those who keep God's commands and hold fast their testimony about Jesus.

Satan wages his final assault on believers, but it is the thrashing of a defeated foe. His rage stems from knowing his time grows short.

## The Call to Faithful Witness

This vision carries a profound challenge for us today.

### The Present Reality

When we look at the world—wars, persecution, personal suffering—it may appear that evil is winning. But this is illusion. The real battle is already won. God has prevailed. Jesus has conquered evil. Nothing can take us from His love.

Yet we live in the tension between His completed victory and its final manifestation. We experience the last rage of an angry Satan, a defeated enemy inflicting final blows. This is why Christians suffer. This is why the faithful face persecution. The victory is complete, but its effects ripple through a still-fallen world.

### A Challenge to Our Commitment

Some of you will face profound testing—perhaps beyond what you think you can endure. Do not shrink back from persecution or suffering. Be brave.

Countless Christians are dying for their faith today. While we cannot solve their crisis, we can honor their courage and sacrifice by refusing to be lazy, selfish, apathetic, or cowardly. 

Consider what we demand of ourselves: We skip church because of rain. We won't endure minor inconveniences. Yet believers across the globe are marched into deserts and massacred for confessing Jesus. They teach their children Christian values knowing it could cost their lives, while we worry if our children receive enough tutoring. They choose death over denying Christ, while many of us abandon biblical teaching if it threatens our comfort or reputation.

Others have given up everything—freedom, security, even life itself—for the faith. And we cower at social disapproval, unwilling to declare Jesus on social media for fear of mean comments.

This isn't about judgment of individuals; it's about recognizing a spiritual reality. If we cannot commit to church in peace when others die to attend, how will we endure when persecution comes? If we collapse at first minor setback, what hope do we have?

### The Anzac Challenge

We honor Anzac soldiers for their bravery—young men no older than our Year 11 and 12 students who stood at Gallipoli as artillery fire cut them down. They didn't want to fight. They would have preferred to stay home, play cricket, marry, and raise families. But they identified with righteousness, gave up their desires, put aside their fears, and fought. They stood fast.

They fought so we could enjoy freedom. All they ask is that we preserve their legacy—that we don't squander the freedom they purchased with blood, that we're prepared to defend it again if threatened.

Jesus has purchased our freedom at far greater cost—His own blood. He asks the same: preserve His legacy. Live out His Word. Bear witness to His testimony. Be willing to sacrifice.

### The Ultimate Question

We must ask ourselves: Would we still profess faith if it cost us our lives? If Christianity faced the persecution other nations experience, would 80 percent of us still claim it? Or would it shrink to 20 percent, 5 percent, 1 percent?

Will you be among the faithful, however small the number? Do you believe in Christ deeply enough to give up everything? Do you believe enough to give up *anything*?

This is not a question I can answer for you. Each of us must settle it with God.

### Living in Light of the Victory

The victory in heaven is complete. Satan is defeated. Your eternal security rests not on your strength or courage but on Christ's triumph. Yet we live in a time when that victory is contested on earth. We are called to faithfulness, not because the outcome is uncertain—it isn't—but because our witness matters.

Hold fast to God's commands. Bear witness to Jesus. By the blood of the Lamb and the word of your testimony, triumph over fear. This is how we honor the martyrs, preserve Christ's legacy, and live as citizens of His already-won kingdom.

The battle rages. Satan knows his time is short and fights with fury. But the war is over.

Rejoice.
