# Luke 22:39-62

**Title:** The King Betrayed  
**Date:** 18 March 2012  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Understanding Betrayal

What makes betrayal so devastating? It's the breaking of trust—and betrayal can only be done by friends, never by enemies. Today we witness Jesus being betrayed by those closest to him during his darkest hour.

## The Faithless Disciples

### The Disciples Sleep While Jesus Prays (verses 39-46)

> He went out and made His way as usual to the Mount of Olives, and the disciples followed Him. When He reached the place, He told them, "Pray that you may not enter into temptation."

Jesus instructs his followers to pray against temptation—specifically, the temptation to betray him. Yet when he returns from prayer, he finds them asleep, exhausted from sorrow.

> Why are you sleeping? Get up and pray so that you will not fall into temptation.

### Betrayed by Judas (verses 47-48)

> While he was still speaking a crowd came up, and the man who was called Judas, one of the Twelve, was leading them. He approached Jesus to kiss him, but Jesus asked him, "Judas, are you betraying the Son of Man with a kiss?"

The weight of this moment cannot be overstated. **Judas was one of the Twelve**—not just a follower, but part of Jesus's innermost circle. These were twelve men like your closest friends, who spent three years with Jesus daily.

The **kiss** was an act of identification. In the chaos of Passover with crowds filling Jerusalem, mistaken identity was possible. But the kiss carries deeper significance—it's one of life's most intimate gestures, making Judas's betrayal all the more cruel. In a gesture of friendship, he stabs Jesus in the back.

That single act forever made the name Judas synonymous with betrayal—so much so that it became impossible to use the name without conjuring images of treachery.

### Betrayed by Peter (verses 54-62)

But the tragedy deepens. Jesus is betrayed not just by Judas, but by his dearest friend—Peter, the rock upon whom Jesus would build his church.

> Then seizing him, they led him away and took him into the house of the high priest. Peter followed at a distance. And when some there had kindled a fire in the middle of the courtyard and had sat down together, Peter sat down with them. A servant girl saw him seated there in the firelight. She looked closely at him and said, "This man was with him."
>
> But he denied it. "Woman, I don't know him," he said.
>
> A little later someone else saw him and said, "You also are one of them."
>
> "Man, I am not!" Peter replied.
>
> About an hour later another asserted, "Certainly this fellow was with him, for he is a Galilean."
>
> Peter replied, "Man, I don't know what you're talking about!"

Three times Peter denies Jesus. He denies knowing him. He denies being with the disciples. He denies his very identity as a Galilean—denying the history they shared, the moment he first met Jesus. He completely disowns Jesus. He gets cancelled.

But then came the rooster's crow:

> The Lord turned and looked straight at Peter. Then Peter remembered the word the Lord had spoken to him: "Before the rooster crows today, you will disown me three times." And he went outside and wept bitterly.

Peter had pledged:

> "Lord, I'm ready to go with You both to prison and to death!"

Jesus had responded:

> "I tell you, Peter, the rooster will not crow today until you deny three times that you know Me!"

What went so terribly wrong? Peter went from "I will die with you" to "I don't know him." His look—was it disappointment? Did Jesus's heart break as he watched his closest friend deny him?

## How Betrayal Happens Gradually

Here's what we must understand: most people don't decide one day that they're done being Christian. Betrayal happens in stages.

It often begins with subtle withdrawals. You attend church less frequently. Bible study becomes optional. Without fellowship, motivation to pray or read Scripture fades. Then life gets busy—legitimate reasons pile up, one activity after another, until there's no room left for Jesus. By this point, no one asks the hard questions anymore. You've simply drifted away, and one day Jesus becomes but a distant memory of childhood fantasy.

Consider the young person who put studies first. He stopped coming to church and youth group to focus on exams. He told himself he'd read the Bible alone, but away from his Christian friends, it was too easy to drift. He never returned.

Or the Christian who fell in love with someone who didn't share his faith. To accommodate her, he stopped attending church. He reasoned he needed to earn the opportunity to share the gospel with her. But there were always other things to do when together, and mentioning Jesus felt awkward. After a year, he realized she had no interest in following Christ—but by then he was in love. He made his choice: Jesus could wait in the background. He betrayed Jesus.

This is what we're witnessing with the disciples. Jesus warned them: "Pray that you may not enter into temptation." They failed to pray. They fell asleep. And when the test came, they failed.

## Jesus Remains Faithful

In striking contrast to his wavering disciples, Jesus demonstrates unwavering faithfulness to God's will.

### "Not My Will, But Yours Be Done" (verses 39-42)

> He went out as usual to the Mount of Olives, and his disciples followed him. On reaching the place, he said to them, "Pray that you will not fall into temptation." He withdrew about a stone's throw beyond them, knelt down and prayed, "Father, if you are willing, take this cup from me; yet not my will, but yours be done."

The cup is a biblical symbol of God's wrath. Isaiah speaks of it:

> Awake, awake! Rise up, Jerusalem, you who have drunk from the hand of the Lord the cup of his wrath, you who have drained to its dregs the goblet that makes people stagger.

Jesus shrinks from this cup—not from cowardice, but from recognition of its terrible reality. Separation from God is the worst thing in the world. We sometimes imagine hell as being left alone to do our own thing, but that's wishful thinking. Hell is separation from God and all the good things that flow from him—blessing, pleasure, family, justice, love. It's the complete absence of everything that makes life worth living.

Yet Jesus doesn't stop there. He prays for strength and resolution to drink it. As one commentator notes: "In that case the cup from which he shrank was something different. It symbolized neither the physical pain of being flogged and crucified, nor the mental distress of being despised and rejected even by his own people, but rather the spiritual agony of bearing the sins of the world—in other words, of enduring the divine judgment which those sins deserved."

Why does Jesus drink the cup? Because he chooses to be identified with sinners. He takes on the penalty that belongs to us.

> Therefore I will give him a portion among the great, and he will divide the spoils with the strong, because he poured out his life unto death, and was numbered with the transgressors. For he bore the sin of many, and made intercession for the transgressors. (Isaiah 53:12)

Jesus came to this very hour for this very purpose. He submits: "Not my will, but yours be done."

### Jesus Accepts the Inevitability of God's Plan (verses 49-53)

When his disciples attempt to fight back—one even drawing a sword and cutting off the servant's ear—Jesus responds:

> "Let it go. Let it proceed."

These words have been mistranslated in many modern versions as "No more of this!" But the Greek phrase (éate heos toutou) nowhere else in Luke means "stop." It means "let it be" or "let it go," as in "let it proceed." Jesus acknowledges the inevitability of what must happen. God has spoken. He must drink this cup. He must be betrayed and handed over to his enemies.

He addresses those who've come to arrest him:

> "Am I leading a rebellion, that you have come with swords and clubs? Every day I was with you in the temple courts, and you did not lay a hand on me. But this is your hour—when darkness reigns."

The irony is stark: Jesus, the truly innocent one, is identified as a criminal. Yet he goes willingly, faithful to his Father's plan.

### Jesus Remains Faithful to the Elect (verses 31-32)

But even in his suffering, Jesus thinks of his disciples. Before his arrest, he had told Peter:

> "Simon, Simon, look out! Satan has asked to sift you like wheat. But I have prayed for you that your faith may not fail. And you, when you have turned back, strengthen your brothers."

Satan wanted to destroy the disciples—to sift them like wheat. But Jesus intercedes for Peter. This temptation is not fatal because Jesus has prayed for him. Even though Peter will fail in this moment, he will turn back. He will repent. And when he does, he can strengthen his brothers.

Notice the contrast with Judas. Judas's fall seems permanent—his name forever stained by betrayal. But Peter represents hope: restoration is possible through repentance.

## A Pastoral Challenge

How are you walking with Jesus? I suspect no one here has fallen away completely—otherwise you wouldn't be here. But if you sense you're heading that direction, why not do something about it now? Talk to a trusted friend. Work out something that will help. Strengthen your feeble arms and stay loyal.

Don't wait for a moment of crisis. Start now by openly identifying with Christ. At school, tell others you're attending the Christian group. If you won't do it now in year seven, when you think popularity matters most, do you honestly think you'll stand when real persecution comes?

Consider joining us when we go flyering today. It's not just for the church or our neighbors—it's for you. As someone wisely put it in a comment last week: if you cannot do flyering out of fear and embarrassment, do you really think you'll stand when the stakes are higher?

## The Call to Faithfulness

> Kiss the Son, lest he be angry and you be destroyed in your way, for his wrath can flare up in a moment. Blessed are all who take refuge in him. (Psalm 2:12)

God has been loyal to us—all the way to the cross. Let's not view God with skepticism and suspicion. He did what he said he would do. He sent his Son to bear our sins. He made a way for us to be reconciled to him.

The question before us is simple: Will we be faithful to him?
