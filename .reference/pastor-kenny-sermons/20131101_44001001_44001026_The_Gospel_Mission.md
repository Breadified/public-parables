## Introduction: How Quickly Messages Spread

News travels fast. When Miranda Kerr and Orlando Bloom separated, everyone knew about their "Kerr-Boom" within days. An engagement ring appears, and suddenly it's all over town. A baby is born—congratulations spread through text messages before the parents finish calling their own families. Years ago, when we had a newborn, I casually texted one person that we might name him David. Within hours, people were texting congratulations on the name David. Even my future son was getting messages celebrating his name before it was officially decided.

This is how messages spread in our world. And remarkably, this is how the Christian message spread in the early centuries.

Within 300 years, Christianity exploded from a small group of 120 people in Jerusalem to three million believers—becoming the official religion of the Roman Empire. This movement shaped our language, calendar, legal systems, welfare structures, government, and education. Yet it happened with no institutional power, facing constant persecution, always as underdogs.

The book of Acts traces these early beginnings. As we study it, we're not just looking back at history—we're being reminded that this movement continues today. We are part of this history. This study calls us to reclaim the mission and purpose we've been given, to recover the joy and excitement of the early church, and to understand how influence spreads not through wealth or power, but through transformed lives and faithful witness.

## 1. Luke's Careful Account (1:1-2)

> In my former book, Theophilus, I wrote about all that Jesus began to do and to teach until the day he was taken up to heaven, after giving instructions through the Holy Spirit to the apostles he had chosen. (Acts 1:1-2)

Luke was not an uneducated writer. He was a physician (Colossians 4:14), a careful investigator of historical facts. When he writes about "my former book," he's referring to the Gospel of Luke. In ancient times, this would have been written on scrolls, not bound books. Later, when the codex format was invented—scrolls cut into pages and bound together—the gospels were collected and ordered: Matthew, Mark, Luke, and John.

One consequence of this reorganization is that Luke and Acts were separated. Ideally, they should be read together as a single narrative, because Luke's purpose in both volumes is consistent: to show that Jesus's mission continues through his witnesses.

In the Gospel of Luke, Luke writes:

> Many have undertaken to draw up an account of the things that have been fulfilled among us, just as they were handed down to us by those who from the first were eyewitnesses and servants of the word. Therefore, since I myself have carefully investigated everything from the beginning, it seemed good also to me to write an orderly account for you, most excellent Theophilus, so that you may know the certainty of the things you have been taught. (Luke 1:1-4)

### This is Real History, Not Mythology

Luke emphasizes that this is **real history**: real people (Jesus, the disciples), real places (Jerusalem, specific geographic references). This is not a fairy tale set in an enchanted forest somewhere. The Bible is a historical document that should be approached with the same historical rigor we apply to any ancient text.

Many dismiss the Bible as biased, but everyone writing history brings bias. What matters is transparency about purpose. Luke is explicit: he investigated carefully and organized his account so that Theophilus—and we—can know the certainty of these events. He invites anyone who disagrees to write their own account or correct his version. This is how history actually works.

Even secular historians acknowledge the core facts: Jesus was a real person who performed miracles, taught extensively, and attracted a large following. Where historians debate is interpretation, not whether these events occurred.

## 2. Jesus Teaches About His Mission (1:3-8)

### The Resurrected Jesus Appears

> After his suffering, he showed himself to these men and gave many convincing proofs that he was alive. He appeared to them over a period of forty days and spoke about the kingdom of God. (Acts 1:3)

Notice the emphasis: Jesus did not appear as a ghost or vision. He **showed himself**—they saw him physically. He gave **many convincing proofs** that he was alive. This wasn't hearsay or legend; it was repeated, personal encounter with the risen Jesus over forty days.

The number forty carries significance throughout Scripture. It rained for forty days during Noah's flood—enough rain. The twelve spies spent forty days surveying Canaan—enough time to scout. Israel wandered forty years in the wilderness—enough time for a generation to learn obedience. Jesus was tempted forty days in the wilderness—enough time to face every kind of trial. In each case, forty days or years means "sufficient time to accomplish the purpose." So forty days of appearances means Jesus had enough time to convince his disciples thoroughly and completely of his resurrection.

> On one occasion, while he was eating with them, he gave them this command: "Do not leave Jerusalem, but wait for the gift my Father promised, which you have heard me speak about. For John baptized with water, but in a few days you will be baptized with the Holy Spirit." (Acts 1:4-5)

Jesus eats with them—another proof that he's not a hologram or hallucination, because dreams don't taste. He tells them to stay in Jerusalem, the very place where his death occurred. If the resurrection were a hoax, they would spread rumors elsewhere, in a place where no one could verify the story. Yet they stayed right where the supposed hoax would be most easily exposed.

Jesus promises the "gift my Father promised"—the Holy Spirit. The disciples would not begin their mission immediately; they would wait for empowerment from God.

### The Question About the Kingdom

> So when they met together, they asked him, "Lord, are you at this time going to restore the kingdom to Israel?" (Acts 1:6)

The disciples still had political hopes. Israel had been divided for centuries; the twelve tribes were long scattered. Even under Roman occupation, they dreamed of national restoration. They ask: Is this the time when you will restore Israel's kingdom?

Jesus's answer redirects their vision:

> He said to them: "It is not for you to know the times or dates the Father has set by his own authority. But you will receive power when the Holy Spirit comes on you; and you will be my witnesses in Jerusalem, and in all Judea and Samaria, and to the ends of the earth." (Acts 1:7-8)

Jesus doesn't deny that restoration is coming—but it's God's timing, not theirs to know. More importantly, he expands their vision. The mission is not just restoration of Israel; it's witness to all people—Jerusalem, Judea, Samaria (the hated half-Jews), and the ends of the earth.

Isaiah had prophesied this broader mission:

> He says: "It is too small a thing for you to be my servant to restore the tribes of Jacob and bring back those of Israel I have kept. I will also make you a light for the Gentiles, that you may bring my salvation to the ends of the earth." (Isaiah 49:6)

In Jesus's time, different regions were thought to have different gods. Even today, we carry echoes of this thinking: China is "Buddhist," the Middle East is "Islamic," India is "Hindu." But the ascension declares something radical—there is one ruler over the entire world: Christ. There is no local deity, no regional god. His kingdom encompasses all peoples and nations.

## 3. The Ascension: Jesus Enthroned (1:9-11)

### Jesus Is Lifted Up and Exalted

> After he said this, he was taken up before their very eyes, and a cloud hid him from their sight. (Acts 1:9)

The language here is significant. The verb "taken up" appears four times in Acts 1:1-11, emphasizing the reality of the ascension. Jesus was "lifted up" (like a sail catching wind), received by a cloud (the divine presence), and taken into heaven.

Some might dismiss the ascension as unremarkable. After all, Enoch was taken to heaven, and Elijah was caught up in a chariot of fire. But Jesus's ascension is categorically different. As Jesus himself said:

> No one has ever gone into heaven except the one who came from heaven—the Son of Man. (John 3:13)

This ascension is unique because it means **Christ is exalted and enthroned as ruler**.

### The Ascension as Enthronement

When Jesus ascends through the clouds to the Ancient of Days (God the Father), he is being enthroned as ruler. This echoes the imagery in Daniel:

> In my vision at night I looked, and there before me was one like a son of man, coming with the clouds of heaven. He approached the Ancient of Days and was led into his presence. He was given authority, glory and sovereign power; all peoples, nations and men of every language worshiped him. His dominion is an everlasting dominion that will not pass away, and his kingdom is one that will never be destroyed. (Daniel 7:13-14)

The psalms celebrate this ascension and enthronement:

> Lift up your heads, O you gates; be lifted up, you ancient doors, that the King of glory may come in. (Psalm 24:7)

> God has ascended amid shouts of joy, the LORD amid the sounding of trumpets. Sing praises to God, sing praises; sing praises to our King, sing praises. For God is the King of all the earth; God reigns over the nations; God is seated on his holy throne. (Psalm 47:5-8)

Think of the Australian Prime Minister-Elect going to Yarralumla to be formally recognized by the Governor-General. The Prime Minister doesn't have authority until that moment. Ascending to Yarralumla is ascending to the highest authority in the land. Similarly, Jesus's ascension through the clouds is his going before the Father to be enthroned as ruler of all creation.

### Implications of Jesus's Ascension

Because of the bodily resurrection, Jesus is not a spirit diluted throughout creation. He is a human being with an exalted body, which means his location is fixed. He is somewhere—described in Scripture as "seated at the right hand of God" and "far above all the heavens" (Ephesians 4:10). We cannot locate him on a map or reach him by rocket ship, but his ascension to heaven doesn't diminish his authority. He rules from his exalted position and will return as King—not merely as Savior or spiritual presence, but as the one who will judge all things.

## 4. Jesus's Ongoing Work: Intercession and Advocacy (1:9-11)

### A Human in Heaven for the First Time

The incarnation declared: the Word becomes flesh. The ascension declares something equally radical: flesh enters heaven. For the first time in cosmic history, a human body dwells in the presence of God. This means Jesus can help us:

> Therefore he is able to save completely those who come to God through him, because he always lives to intercede for them. (Hebrews 7:25)

Jesus is our advocate—not just through his prayers for us, but through his very presence as a human in heaven. We have a friend on the inside who intercedes for us.

Think of it practically: having a lawyer in the family when you're in legal trouble. Having a friend who works at a company where you're applying for a job—they can recommend you. Having a connection at Coles for a staff discount. Jesus is our friend in heaven, and being in power, he helps us.

### His Presence Speaks for Us

Jesus doesn't merely pray for us; his very presence in heaven speaks to God on our behalf:

> Such a high priest meets our need—one who is holy, blameless, pure, set apart from sinners, exalted above the heavens. Unlike the other high priests, he does not need to offer sacrifices day after day, first for his own sins, and then for the sins of the people. He sacrificed for their sins once for all when he offered himself. (Hebrews 7:26-27)

This is radically different from other religions, which teach endless cycles of works, effort, and repeated sacrifices. Jesus's work is complete: "Done, done, done."

It is not merely what Jesus says, but his very presence—the presence of the one who paid the price for our sins—that speaks to God. The fact that he stands there means we need never fear punishment. He is our advocate because he is our propitiation. His presence alone declares to God: "The debt is paid."

This is the security of the Christian: when we sin, we don't look inward at our own righteousness (we have none to speak of). We look **up** to Jesus, enthroned in heaven. We remember that his work is finished. His very presence there speaks to God for us. While he sits, we are secure.

When we confess our sins, we're not doing something—we're admitting that nothing needs to be done because it's already been done. Our assurance doesn't rest on the passage of time since we last sinned or on our own moral improvement. It rests on the finished work of Christ.

## 5. The Apostles as Witnesses (1:12-26)

### The Problem: A Missing Apostle

> Then they returned to Jerusalem from the hill called the Mount of Olives, a Sabbath day's walk from the city. When they arrived, they went upstairs to the room where they were staying. Those present were Peter, John, James and Andrew; Philip and Thomas, Bartholomew and Matthew; James son of Alphaeus and Simon the Zealot, and Judas son of James. (Acts 1:12-13)

Count the names: eleven apostles. There should be twelve. There's an elephant in the room. One is missing.

> They all joined together constantly in prayer, along with the women and Mary the mother of Jesus, and with his brothers. In those days Peter stood up among the believers (a group numbering about a hundred and twenty) and said, "Brothers, the Scripture had to be fulfilled which the Holy Spirit spoke long ago through the mouth of David concerning Judas, who served as guide for those who arrested Jesus—he was one of our number and shared in this ministry." (Acts 1:14-17)

Judas betrayed Jesus. What happened to him?

> (With the reward he got for his wickedness, Judas bought a field; there he fell headlong, his body burst open and all his intestines spilled out. Everyone in Jerusalem heard about this, so they called that field in their language Akeldama, that is, Field of Blood.) (Acts 1:18-19)

Matthew's gospel records that Judas hung himself. Luke adds that after hanging, his body fell and burst open. These accounts aren't contradictory—the rope probably broke over time, causing the body to fall.

### Finding a Replacement

The apostles understood that their group of twelve was symbolically significant—representing the restoration of Israel's twelve tribes. They needed to find a replacement for Judas, but with specific criteria:

> Therefore it is necessary to choose one of the men who have been with us the whole time the Lord Jesus went in and out among us, beginning from John's baptism to the time when Jesus was taken up from us. For one of these must become a witness with us of his resurrection. (Acts 1:21-22)

A witness to the resurrection must have been present from the beginning—from John's baptism through the resurrection and ascension. They couldn't just choose anyone; the witness had to have direct knowledge of all these events.

Two men qualified: Joseph called Barsabbas (also known as Justus) and Matthias. They prayed and cast lots:

> Then they prayed, "Lord, you know everyone's heart. Show us which of these two you have chosen to take over this apostolic ministry, which Judas left to go where he belongs." Then they cast lots, and the lot fell to Matthias; so he was added to the eleven apostles. (Acts 1:24-26)

Casting lots is a valid way to make decisions—but only when all the important criteria have already been carefully considered. When choosing between ice cream flavors, you don't need to deliberate endlessly; vanilla or chocolate, just pick one. When choosing a spouse, you don't roll dice—you know the answer based on character. But when both candidates are qualified, godly, and faithful, and when all the essential criteria point equally to both, then drawing lots is a way to entrust the final decision to God.

In this case, both Joseph and Matthias were good. God's choice fell on Matthias—which worked out well, because "Barsabbas Media" wouldn't have had quite the same ring as "Matthias Media."

## 6. Our Mission Today

The apostles were set apart as witnesses to the gospel. They were enlisted into Jesus's mission. But this was a tough job—witnessing always happens in the context of conflict, when people have opposing views.

In a derivative sense, this task falls on us today. We are not apostles, but the words of the apostles continue to testify. Luke's gospel and the book of Acts preserve their witness for us. Through these accounts, we hear the apostles speak again, witnessing to the life, death, resurrection, and ascension of Jesus.

What's remarkable about their testimony is that they had nothing to gain from it. None of them became wealthy; all eventually died for their faith. Could they have been brainwashed? Perhaps, but who could have brainwashed them? Jesus? Could they have brainwashed others? Perhaps, but why would they die for a lie they themselves created, knowing it was false? It's one thing for the brainwashed to die for a delusion. It's another entirely for the originator of the story to die for it, knowing it's a lie. Their willingness to die is a powerful testimony to their sincerity.

### Our Role: Bearing Witness to the Apostles' Witness

Our mission is to expose others to the words of these apostles who witnessed to Jesus. We do this through the Bible, through talks and teaching, through various means of sharing their testimony.

I don't claim to be an authority. I claim the Bible to be the authority. That's the difference: I'm simply pointing you to the one authoritative witness—the Scripture itself. We don't need a pope or human intermediary. We need to read and study what the apostles wrote.

The Gospel Mission continues because the risen, ascended Jesus is alive, ruling from heaven, interceding for all who come to God through him. Our job is simply to bear witness to that reality.
