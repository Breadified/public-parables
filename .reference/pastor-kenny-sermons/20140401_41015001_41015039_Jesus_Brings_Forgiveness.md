# Mark 15:1-39

**Title:** Jesus Brings Forgiveness  
**Date:** 13 April 2014  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction: Jesus's Job Description

Everyone has a job description. For Jesus, it's simple: **to forgive**. Yet forgiveness is one of the hardest things to do.

In Mark 10:32-34, Jesus previews what's coming:

> "We are going up to Jerusalem," he said, "and the Son of Man will be betrayed to the chief priests and teachers of the law. They will condemn him to death and will hand him over to the Gentiles, who will mock him and spit on him, flog him and kill him. Three days later he will rise."

Jesus will be betrayed, condemned, mocked, killed, and raised. We'll focus on three aspects of his suffering: his condemnation, his mockery, and his death—and how each reveals his forgiveness.

## 1. Condemned (15:1-15)

### Everybody Loves Jesus—Except His Claims

The Jewish leadership rushed Jesus through a nighttime trial, then brought him before Pilate at dawn. Mark 15:1 tells us:

> Very early in the morning, the chief priests, with the elders, the teachers of the law and the whole Sanhedrin, reached a decision. They bound Jesus, led him away and handed him over to Pilate.

Only the Romans could execute someone, so the Jewish authorities needed Pilate's approval. But here's the remarkable part: Pilate quickly recognized Jesus's innocence.

> "Are you the king of the Jews?" asked Pilate. "Yes, it is as you say," Jesus replied. The chief priests accused him of many things. So again Pilate asked him, "Aren't you going to answer? See how many things they are accusing you of." But Jesus still made no reply, and Pilate was amazed.

Jesus remained silent because he had already answered the question. He is the king of the Jews. And Pilate knew it—he even asked, "What crime has he committed?"

### The Paradox: We Love Jesus, But Not as King

Here's the irony: today, Jesus is the most popular figure in history. In 2013, a UK survey ranked famous individuals by their projected popularity 200 years after their death. Jesus topped the list. Even atheists like Richard Dawkins acknowledge Jesus as "a great moral and intelligent teacher."

Who wouldn't love someone who taught us to love our neighbors, to live in service, to turn the other cheek, to care for the poor, and to challenge unjust authority?

**But the moment Jesus opens his mouth about his actual claims, people reject him.**

He claims to be King—not just of the Jews, but of the universe, of you and me. He says we must deny ourselves. He defines marriage. He demands that we submit to God. He tells us we're sinners in need of a savior.

This is why people say, "I love Jesus; I just hate the church." But churches aren't doing anything radical—they're simply opening their Bibles and repeating what Jesus said. What people truly dislike isn't the church's organization; it's that the church keeps talking about Jesus's actual words.

**We cannot separate Jesus from his teaching.** The same place we discover his mercy and compassion is the same place we hear about sin, judgment, and hell. His commands for justice, his vision for marriage, his call for self-denial—they all flow from the same source: his love for God and for us.

### The Choice Before Us

Imagine if you met someone and they chose to listen to only part of who you are. They'd miss the consistency of your character. Jesus is a package deal. You cannot take the Jesus you like and ignore the Jesus who makes demands on your life.

**Are you like Pilate?** Have you condemned Jesus based on incomplete evidence? Why not examine the full case?

All the positive things you know about Jesus—they're true. But go deeper. Move beyond the superficial. Hear the words of this man you respect, and decide whether you'll accept him as he truly is, not as you'd like him to be.

### Pilate's Escape and Our Substitute

Pilate tried to find a way out. He offered to release a prisoner as was the custom at Passover. A man named Barabbas was in prison—he had committed murder in an uprising. Surely the crowd would ask for Jesus's release?

> "Do you want me to release to you the king of the Jews?" asked Pilate, knowing it was out of envy that the chief priests had handed Jesus over to him. But the chief priests stirred up the crowd to have Pilate release Barabbas instead.

The crowd demanded the release of the guilty man and the execution of the innocent one.

> "What shall I do, then, with the one you call the king of the Jews?" Pilate asked. "Crucify him!" they shouted. "Why? What crime has he committed?" asked Pilate. But they shouted all the louder, "Crucify him!" Wanting to satisfy the crowd, Pilate released Barabbas to them. He had Jesus flogged, and handed him over to be crucified.

**Here we see the first picture of substitution.** The guilty man goes free. The innocent man takes his place. This is what Jesus came to do for all of us.

## 2. Mocked (15:16-24)

### Why Mock the Innocent?

After his condemnation, Jesus faced mockery:

> The soldiers led Jesus away into the palace and called together the whole company of soldiers. They put a purple robe on him, then twisted together a crown of thorns and set it on him. And they began to call out to him, "Hail, king of the Jews!" Again and again they struck him on the head with a staff and spit on him. Falling on their knees, they paid homage to him. And when they had mocked him, they took off the purple robe and put his own clothes on him. Then they led him out to crucify him. They brought Jesus to the place called Golgotha. Then they offered him wine mixed with myrrh, but he did not take it. And they crucified him.

The soldiers loaded this moment with bitter irony. Purple robe—the color of royalty. A crown—symbol of authority. "Hail, king of the Jews"—mockery of honor. They struck him, spat on him, bent the knee in false worship.

**But what had Jesus done to deserve this?** He hadn't organized violence. He hadn't corrupted anyone. He hadn't stolen. He was innocent—and they knew it.

The cruelty makes no sense. It's like someone shouting at the sky, "There is no God!" Why bother mocking what you don't believe exists?

### Why Do We Mock What Is Good?

There's something peculiar about mocking Christianity itself. Many define themselves by what they're *not*—"I'm an atheist," meaning "I'm a non-believer." But that's defining yourself by absence, not by presence. It's like someone answering "Who are you?" with "Well, I'm not John. I definitely don't live in Strathfield. I'm a non-exerciser, a non-African, not two meters tall." 

**But who *are* you?**

If you reject God's answers about morality and meaning, what's *your* explanation? Where do right and wrong come from? How did the universe begin?

Yet consider this: Christianity has been the greatest force for good in human history. The world's largest orphanage system was founded by Christian Thomas John Barnardo. The church is the single largest provider of healthcare worldwide. The church is the single largest provider of education worldwide. Ninety-three of the first 110 universities in the United States were church-founded—Yale, Princeton, Harvard. The University of Hong Kong traces its roots to the London Missionary Society.

**Is the world better with Christianity or without it?** The empirical answer is clear.

Now, Christians haven't always done good. There have been wars, scandals, failures. And yes, other belief systems do good things too. But no one does it better, more consistently, or to the extent that Christianity does—generation after generation.

**Why mock this heritage?** Why mock people for staying consistent with teachings they've held for thousands of years? Just because culture shifts doesn't mean the church should follow. You wouldn't want that, would you? You don't want a belief system that changes its mind based on popularity. That's like parents who simply follow their children's whims—worthless.

Christianity stands firm because it's anchored in eternal truth, not cultural fashion.

## 3. Killed (15:25-39)

### The Either/Or of Love

Jesus hung on the cross with two criminals, one on each side. The crowd continued mocking:

> Those who passed by hurled insults at him, shaking their heads and saying, "So! You who are going to destroy the temple and build it in three days, come down from the cross and save yourself!"

And again: "Let this Christ, this King of Israel, come down now from the cross, that we may see and believe."

The mockers interpreted Jesus's refusal to come down as weakness. *If you were truly powerful, you'd save yourself.*

But they missed something crucial. **Jesus *chose* not to come down.** The chief priests and teachers understood the stakes better than they realized:

> "He saved others," they said, "but he can't save himself!"

They were right—but not in the way they meant. This wasn't about inability; it was about choice. **Jesus faced an either/or scenario: either save himself or save others. He could not do both.**

### Substitution: The Heart of Forgiveness

There is only one reason the God of the universe would endure condemnation, mockery, and crucifixion: **there is no other way to save us.**

By suffering and dying, Jesus substitutes himself for us. He takes our place. We see this theme woven throughout the passage:

**Barabbas**: The murderer deserved death. Jesus should have gone free. Instead, Jesus is condemned and Barabbas is released. The guilty are freed; the innocent dies.

**Simon of Cyrene**: Soldiers forced Simon to carry Jesus's cross. In those days, a man carrying a cross was a condemned man. When Jesus was crucified, he symbolically took the cross from Simon. Jesus carried what should have been Simon's burden.

> A certain man from Cyrene, Simon, the father of Alexander and Rufus, was passing by on his way in from the country, and they forced him to carry the cross.

**The Pattern**: It is *we* who deserve condemnation, mockery, and death. We who condemn, mock, and kill others—and through our rejection and apathy toward God, we condemn, mock, and kill Jesus himself. **It should have been me.** But Jesus did not save himself. He did it for you. He did it for me.

### The Cost of Forgiveness

Forgiveness is not merely a change of feelings, as some define it. Rather, forgiveness is **absolving claims on a debt.** And debt forgiveness costs something real.

When you forgive a financial debt, you give up money you could have used for a car, a house, a holiday, food for your family, your children's education. That money was yours. To forgive is to sacrifice what was rightfully owed to you.

On a human level, when someone wrongs us—when they backstab, steal from, belittle, or bully us—vengeance feels justified. "An eye for an eye, tooth for a tooth." It feels *right*. To let go of that anger costs something. We lose the satisfaction of retribution.

But we humans are forgetful. We get distracted. Other people sin against us, and eventually, the sting fades. **When God tells us to forgive, it's easier for us because we forget.**

Not so with God. He has perfect knowledge. Perfect consistency. He remains in that first moment of righteous anger forever. **So when God forgives, it costs him infinitely more than it costs us.**

Jesus bore that infinite cost. He absorbed an unimaginable debt—the sum total of all our sins, all our rebellion, all our rejection of God. And the cost of that forgiveness was an unimaginable, infinite amount of pain.

He was condemned. He was mocked. He was killed. **This is how much forgiveness costs.**

### The Veil Is Torn

> At the sixth hour darkness came over the whole land until the ninth hour. And at the ninth hour Jesus cried out in a loud voice, "Eloi, Eloi, lama sabachthani?"—which means, "My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?"

Jesus quoted Psalm 22—not a cry of despair but a prayer of trust even in abandonment. God the Father did not forsake Jesus; rather, Jesus fully identified with our condition. He experienced what we deserve.

> With a loud cry, Jesus breathed his last. The curtain of the temple was torn in two from top to bottom.

That curtain was the symbol of division between God and us—the barrier created by our sin. Only the high priest could pass through it once a year, after payment for sin. **The curtain is torn.** The barrier is removed. We have full access to God.

The centurion, standing at the foot of the cross, witnessed all of this. He heard Jesus's cry. He saw how he died. And he understood:

> "Surely this man was the Son of God!"

## Application: Will You Accept His Forgiveness?

There are some here who have never accepted God's offer of forgiveness. Jesus has paid your debt. The condemnation you deserve, he bore. The mockery you deserve, he endured. The death you deserve, he died.

**Will you let him forgive you?**

A prayer for trusting Jesus:

> Dear Father, I'm sorry that I've been trying to live my life without you. Thank you for sending your Son Jesus to die the death I deserved to die. Thank you that he rose again and is alive today. Father, please forgive me and help me to follow you as my Lord and my God for the rest of my life. In Jesus's name I pray, amen.
