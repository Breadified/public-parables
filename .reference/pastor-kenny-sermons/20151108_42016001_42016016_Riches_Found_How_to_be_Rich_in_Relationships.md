# Luke 16:1-16

**Title:** Riches Found: How to be Rich in Relationships

**Date:** November 8, 2015

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Making Friends on Earth (vv. 1-9)

> A rich man had a manager accused of wasting his possessions. Called to account, the manager faced unemployment and all that came with it—loss of income, housing, food, and access to the household's resources. With his reputation ruined and his future bleak, he reflected on his situation: too weak for manual labor, too proud to beg. Time was running out.

The manager's desperation led to action. While still employed, he called in his master's debtors and offered them significant discounts—cutting 900 gallons of olive oil to 450, reducing 1,000 bushels of wheat to 800. He worked quickly, knowing his window of opportunity was closing.

> The master commended the dishonest manager because he had acted shrewdly. For the people of this world are more shrewd in dealing with their own kind than are the people of the light.

**Understanding the Manager's Character**

There's debate about whether the manager's action was dishonest, righteous, or something else. Some see it as pure deception—creative accounting consistent with worldly values. Others suggest he was correcting price gouging or collecting debts responsibly. What matters most is that the master recognized the manager's shrewd strategy: he was making friends while he still could.

> I know what I'll do so that, when I lose my job here, people will welcome me into their houses.

The manager understood a fundamental principle of human nature: relationships matter more than contracts. Success in the world depends not on credentials and written agreements, but on trust and loyalty—on having friends who will help you when you need it most.

### The Wisdom of Relationships Over Transactions

The world operates on a principle often stated as: "It's not what you know, it's who you know."

In business cultures, especially in the East, this takes the form of *guanxi* (关系)—personal relationships where trust and mutual obligation replace formal contracts. When doing business in China, you don't simply hire the lowest bidder; you cultivate relationships with people you can trust across many transactions and years.

In the West, we pride ourselves on contracts, credentials, courts, and price competition. We get quotes from three plumbers, choose the cheapest, and start fresh next time we have a problem. But the truly successful operate differently. They're not looking for the best deal on a single job—they're looking for a plumber they can be friends with, someone who will show up at midnight on a holiday weekend because you have a relationship, not because you're paying premium rates.

This principle extends everywhere. The successful person doesn't just attend the coffee shop for the cheapest coffee; they become a regular and build a friendship with the owner. When choosing who to fix their car, they're thinking about who they trust, not just the lowest price. Even a toilet leak becomes an opportunity to strengthen a friendship with a reliable tradesman.

The wealthy send their children to expensive private schools not primarily for HSC results—many public schools achieve better grades—but for the networks and connections. What price do you place on friendships with future leaders, princes, billionaires' children, politicians' families? The A student works for the C student because the C student had connections.

This is the essence of the manager's wisdom: **while there is still time, make friends.**

### A Sobering Application for Christians

As we become a minority in our culture, this parable speaks directly to us. The Jewish community has a saying: "When one Jew makes money, five Jews make money." An individualistic Westerner becomes wealthy alone—everyone celebrates his cleverness and hard work. But in Eastern cultures, when one person finds success, they pull their entire network up with them. Why? Because they had to rely on each other. They supported one another through struggles, shared business ideas, took risks together, failed together, and succeeded together.

Christians must learn this lesson. Our legal system won't protect us. Our culture increasingly won't help us. Relationships—deep, loyal friendships—will be our survival and strength. We must be intentional about making friends with each other and with those we hope to reach with the gospel.

The wise person doesn't use and discard people. They think of everyone as a potential friend. The fool uses someone and moves on, extracting value and discarding them. The wise sow first, asking not "What do I get today?" but "How can I help this person so that perhaps they might help me in the future?"

> Use worldly wealth to gain friends for yourselves, so that when it is gone, you will be welcomed into eternal dwellings.

## Making Friends in Heaven (vv. 8-16)

### The Heavenly Parallel

The parable shifts now from earthly success to eternal significance. Just as the manager made earthly friends through shrewd generosity, Jesus calls his followers to use their temporary wealth to make eternal friends—to invest in people's souls.

> The Law and the Prophets were proclaimed until John. Since that time, the good news of the kingdom of God is being preached, and everyone is forcing their way into it.

The timeline matters: there's a window of opportunity, just as there was for the manager. The manager had limited time before losing his job. We have limited time before Christ returns. Time is short. The "eternal dwellings" refers not to earthly houses but to heaven itself—the true tabernacle, the presence of God.

### God Welcomes Us

First, God himself welcomes us to heaven. This is possible only because Christ made us friends with God at great cost—he became poor so that we might become rich. Christ paid the price of friendship that we could never afford ourselves.

> No one can serve two masters. Either you will hate the one and love the other, or you will be devoted to the one and despise the other. You cannot serve both God and money.

Yet some reject this generous friendship with God and choose money instead.

### The Pharisees' Failure

> The Pharisees, who loved money, heard all this and were sneering at Jesus.

The Pharisees were like us—committed to God's Word, living godly lives, knowing God's grace. But they were supposed to be channels of that grace to the Gentiles, making friends for God's kingdom. Instead, they loved money and kept themselves separate.

> This man welcomes sinners and eats with them.

They condemned Jesus for spending time with outcasts—the very people they should have been befriending and bringing to God. And Jesus responded:

> You are the ones who justify yourselves in the eyes of others, but God knows your hearts. What people value highly is detestable in God's sight.

God would bypass them. The Gentiles, tax collectors, and sinners would force their way into the kingdom, directly encountering Jesus, no longer waiting for Jewish mediation. The Pharisees lost their opportunity. They didn't make friends for heaven, and heaven didn't welcome them.

### The Call to Us

This warning now comes to us, the new people of light. Will we use our worldly wealth—our time, our resources, our money—to gain friends for eternal dwellings? Will we be rich in relationships?

Consider the cost of friendship:
- It costs money to have coffee together, to share lunch
- It costs money to attend church, to sacrifice work and income for worship
- It costs church leaders and volunteers their own resources to serve
- Parents give up careers to raise their children in faith
- Youth leaders pay for petrol and snacks from their own pockets, not for profit but to make friends—to seek and save the lost

All of this is giving up worldly wealth to make friends for heaven.

### The Invitation

As we head into the coming days, recognize the opportunity before you. Stay for lunch. Have coffee with someone. Invite a friend to church. Visit a neighbor. Build the relationships that will matter when worldly wealth is gone and only friends remain.

The manager's example teaches us this: while there is time, be shrewd about investing in people. Make friends on earth, yes—that's wisdom the world understands. But more importantly, use everything you have to make friends for heaven, where you will be welcomed into eternal dwellings when this temporary life is finished.
