# Job 1:1-2:13

**Title:** Where is God?

**Date:** 7 July 2013 (SMAC) / 14 September 2025 (ARISE)

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Introduction: The Question That Haunts Us

We gather today to address one of life's most pressing questions: Where is God in the midst of suffering? This question becomes especially urgent when tragedy strikes—when innocent people suffer without apparent reason.

Recently, we mourned the loss of a young Christian man whose life was cut short by violence. His death left a grieving family and a watching world asking: How can such things happen? Where is God?

This question isn't merely academic. It's deeply personal. Many of us are wrestling with our own losses—the death of loved ones, devastating diagnoses, financial ruin, broken relationships. And in those dark moments, we ask: How will we overcome this grief? How will our faith survive?

The problem of evil and suffering stands as the greatest intellectual objection to belief in God. As the ancient Greek philosopher Epicurus posed it: "Is God willing to prevent evil, but not able? Then he is impotent. Is he able, but not willing? Then he is malevolent."

From this tension arise the questions that haunt us:
- Why do good people suffer?
- Is there any reason for this pain?
- How is any of this fair?
- How long must this suffering last?

The Book of Job grapples with these questions head-on. Over the coming weeks, we'll explore it together, seeking answers from Scripture itself.

---

## 1. A Great Man (Job 1:1-5)

> In the land of Uz there lived a man whose name was Job. This man was blameless and upright; he feared God and shunned evil. He had seven sons and three daughters, and he owned seven thousand sheep, three thousand camels, five hundred yoke of oxen and five hundred donkeys, and had a large number of servants. He was the greatest man among all the people of the East.

Job is not an ordinary man. He is a great man in every sense.

**His Wealth:** Seven thousand sheep, three thousand camels, five hundred oxen, five hundred donkeys, and numerous servants. In today's terms, he possessed vast real estate, multiple businesses, scores of employees. He was extraordinarily wealthy.

**His Family:** Ten children—seven sons and three daughters. In the ancient world, children were understood as blessings and assets, not liabilities. The symbolic completeness of his family (seven sons representing fullness, plus daughters) meant he had achieved what every person of his culture would consider the ideal.

**His Character:** Yet it's not merely his numbers that define him. His character is equally impressive:

- **Blameless:** Not sinless, but possessing personal integrity—his life is of one piece, marked by sincerity
- **Upright:** Living with ethical consistency in his dealings
- **Feared God:** He recognized God alone as Creator and Lord, to whom he and his entire world owed their existence
- **Shunned Evil:** As a result of his reverence, he actively turned from wrongdoing

**His Spiritual Leadership:** Most remarkably, Job's righteousness extended beyond himself to his children:

> When a period of feasting had run its course, Job would send and have them purified. Early in the morning he would sacrifice a burnt offering for each of them, thinking, "Perhaps my children have sinned and cursed God in their hearts." This was Job's regular custom.

Job didn't merely sacrifice; he sacrificed habitually and carefully. He was so concerned for his children's spiritual welfare that he offered sacrifices "just in case" they had sinned. His devotion was not lazy or sporadic, but regular and intentional.

**His Family Harmony:** The book notes that his sons held feasts together and invited their sisters—women who had married outside the family. This beautiful picture shows not envious competition, but genuine harmony. Brothers working together, sisters welcomed despite being technically outside the family structure. There was no fighting for inheritance or status, only cooperation and generosity.

Job is someone we want to "win." He embodies what we aspire to become: a man of character, wealth, family blessing, and spiritual faithfulness.

## 2. A Great Test (Job 1:6-12, 2:1-6)

But then comes the shock. We shift from earth to heaven, and Scripture gives us a glimpse behind the curtain.

> One day the angels came to present themselves before the LORD, and Satan also came with them. The LORD said to Satan, "Where have you come from?" Satan answered the LORD, "From roaming through the earth and going back and forth in it."

The scene unfolds like a royal court. God sits as King. The angels present themselves before Him in sequence. And Satan—"the Accuser"—is called out for special attention.

God asks: "Where have you been?" And Satan responds that he's been roaming the earth, observing humanity.

Then God says to Satan:

> "Have you considered my servant Job? There is no one on earth like him; he is blameless and upright, a man who fears God and shuns evil."

God Himself vouches for Job's character. This is significant. God is not blind to Job's righteousness.

Satan's response reveals his accusation:

> "Does Job fear God for nothing? Have you not put a hedge around him and his household and everything he has? You have blessed the work of his hands, so that his flocks and herds are spread throughout the land. But stretch out your hand and strike everything he has, and he will surely curse you to your face."

Satan's argument is simple and devastating: Job loves God only because God has blessed him. Remove the blessings, and Job will curse God. Satan says, essentially, that God is nothing but a cosmic Santa Claus, and Job's faith is merely transactional.

God permits the test but sets boundaries:

> "Very well, then, everything he has is in your hands, but on the man himself do not lay a finger."

Satan may destroy Job's possessions and family, but he cannot touch Job's body—not yet.

In a second encounter (Job 2:1-6), after Job has endured the first test with integrity, Satan returns with a harder challenge:

> "Skin for skin! A man will give all he has for his own life. But stretch out your hand and strike his flesh and bones, and he will surely curse you to your face."

This time God permits Satan to afflict Job's body—but not his life:

> "Very well, then, he is in your hands; but you must spare his life."

**The Purpose of Testing:** What Satan means for destruction, God uses for refinement. Tests reveal what we truly value. Exams distinguish genuine knowledge from pretense. Friendships tested by time and circumstance show their true nature. When we're "hedged" by blessings, it's easy to profess faith. But what happens when the hedge is removed?

The Christian faith is sometimes subjected to the same accusation: Christians believe in God only when life is good. Take away comfort and security, and watch faith crumble. But is this true? When times are hard, do we discover what we truly value?

> **James 1:12** "Blessed is the man who perseveres under trial, because when he has stood the test, he will receive the crown of life that God has promised to those who love him."

## 3. The Greatest Response (Job 1:13-22, 2:7-10)

Then comes the catastrophe. Four successive calamities strike in a single day:

> When Job's sons and daughters were feasting and drinking wine at the oldest brother's house, a messenger came and said, "The Sabeans attacked and carried off your oxen and donkeys, and put the servants to the sword. I alone escaped to tell you!"
>
> While he was still speaking, another came and said, "Fire fell from heaven and burned up your sheep and servants. I alone escaped to tell you!"
>
> While he was still speaking, another came and said, "The Chaldeans formed three raiding parties and swept down on your camels and carried them off. They put the servants to the sword. I alone escaped to tell you!"
>
> While he was still speaking, yet another came and said, "Your sons and daughters were feasting at their brother's house when suddenly a mighty wind swept in from the desert and struck the four corners of the house. It collapsed on them and they are dead. I alone escaped to tell you!"

The reversal is total and devastating. In a single afternoon, Job goes from being the greatest man in the East to having nothing—no wealth, no children, no servants, nothing but his own life and his wife.

Later, the affliction extends to his own body:

> So Satan went out from the presence of the LORD and afflicted Job with painful sores from the soles of his feet to the top of his head. Then Job took a piece of broken pottery and scraped himself with it as he sat among the ashes.

Job experiences the full spectrum of human suffering: natural disasters, violence and injustice, loss of loved ones, loss of property, bankruptcy, disease, and pain. All at once.

To illustrate the magnitude of this reversal, consider the collapse of Lehman Brothers in 2008. Richard Fuld had worked his way up from the bottom over forty years, becoming the longest-tenured CEO in the financial industry. In 2006, he was voted No. 1 CEO. He received over $40 million in compensation. But when the collapse came, he was voted worst American CEO of all time. His beloved firm was bankrupt. He was bankrupted. He lost his financial license and became unemployed. He was even assaulted by a disgruntled investor. From hero to zero in weeks.

Imagine losing everything—every relationship, every possession, every comfort. For students here, imagine losing your laptop, phone, backup accounts, all your photos, your entire digital life. Everything. Gone.

**And we ask: Where is God?**

But notice something crucial: Job's suffering is not the result of his sin. This is the heart of the problem. Job is innocent. He is righteous. The suffering is undeserved. This is what makes the question so agonizing: Why do the innocent suffer?

We don't ask why murderers and tyrants suffer gruesome deaths. We might even feel they deserve it. But Job? Job is pious. This is unfair.

Yet in this darkness, Job responds in a way that astonishes us:

> At this, Job got up and tore his robe and shaved his head. Then he fell to the ground in worship and said:
>
> "Naked I came from my mother's womb,
> and naked I will depart.
> The LORD gave and the LORD has taken away;
> may the name of the LORD be praised."

Job's response reveals something profound about God's sovereignty. Notice what Job says: "The LORD gave and the LORD has taken away."

We often think in dualistic terms—God gives good things, and Satan gives bad things. There are two powers fighting for control, and we're caught in the middle. When the good God is winning, we're blessed. When the evil god is winning, we suffer. And God is doing His best, but He's not quite in control.

But Job penetrates beyond this dualism to a deeper truth: **All things come from God.** The Lord sent the Sabeans. The Lord sent the fire from heaven. The Lord blew the wind that collapsed the house.

This doesn't mean God is evil. It means God is sovereign. God is in absolute control—not weakly trying, not frustrated by Satan's interference, but deliberately permitting and orchestrating all things according to His purposes.

Later, when Job's wife urges him to curse God and die, Job responds:

> "You are talking like a foolish woman. Shall we accept good from God, and not trouble?"

The word "trouble" literally means "evil." Job acknowledges that evil comes from God's hand. Satan may be the immediate cause, but God is the ultimate source. And Job does not sin by acknowledging this.

> In all this, Job did not sin in what he said.

What is remarkable is Job's refusal to curse God. Satan's goal—repeated twice—was to get Job to curse the Lord. And Job's wife echoes Satan's temptation: "Curse God and die!"

But Job maintains his integrity. He will not charge God with wrongdoing. Instead, he submits to God's sovereignty:

> "The LORD gave and the LORD has taken away; may the name of the LORD be praised."

**Why We Hesitate to Say This:** Many of us resist attributing suffering to God because we fear it makes God evil. Sickness is evil; if God sends it, God must be evil. But this reasoning is flawed. God is good. God is also sovereign. These are not contradictory. God can send suffering for righteous purposes—as a test, as discipline, as refinement—without being malevolent.

The real sin is the opposite: to suggest that God is not quite in control. That Satan can upset His plans. That God is weak and hoping things work out. This diminishes God's sovereignty and blasphemes His character.

God's sovereignty is so vast that even Satan's work ultimately serves God's purposes. Satan may intend evil, but God uses it for good.

> **Psalm 115:3** "Our God is in heaven; he does whatever pleases him."

## 4. The Sovereignty of God in Suffering

This revelation transforms how we understand suffering. The world is not random. It is not controlled by competing gods fighting for dominance. There is one God, and He is sovereign over all things—even evil, even Satan.

God is in control, and He sets the rules:

- Satan must present himself before God
- Satan cannot touch Job without God's permission
- Satan cannot touch Job's body without specific authorization
- Satan cannot kill Job even when given permission to afflict him
- Satan cannot touch Job's spirit or his eternal life

God's sovereignty means there is always a reason for suffering, even when we don't see it. We may not understand God's purposes. We may never fully comprehend why He permits what He permits. But this is not because God is ignorant or weak—it's because we are finite and He is infinite.

As the apostle Paul reminds us in Romans 9, we are like clay in the potter's hands. The clay doesn't get to question the potter's purposes. We may not understand why God has fashioned us for one purpose and another for another, but we can trust that He knows what He's doing.

**The Test Reveals Our Hearts:** Suffering acts as a revealer. In comfort, it's easy to claim we love God. But when comfort is stripped away, what remains? Do we love God for His gifts, or do we love God for Himself?

This is Satan's accusation: that our faith is merely transactional. And it's an accusation we must take seriously, because it often contains a grain of truth. How many of us would follow Jesus if it guaranteed comfort and prosperity? How many would follow if it guaranteed suffering?

Jesus Himself faced this test in the garden of Gethsemane. He agonized over the coming suffering, yet He ultimately submitted: "Not my will, but yours be done." And later, when He hung on the cross in agony, He did not curse God. He entrusted Himself to the Father who judges justly.

> **1 Peter 2:22-24** "He committed no sin, and no deceit was found in his mouth. When they heaped abuse on him, he did not retaliate; when he suffered, he made no threats, but entrusted himself to him who judges justly. He himself bore our sins in his body on the tree, so that we might die to sin and live to righteousness."

**The Question for Us:** What is the price of your righteousness? What comfort would Satan need to offer you—what suffering would need to be removed—for you to deny God?

Is your price material comfort? Social status? Freedom from pain? What would it take for you to curse God?

The answer, for those who follow Christ, must be: **Nothing.** We cannot be bought. We were already bought—with the blood of Jesus.

> We can't be bought by the momentary comforts Satan offers.
> We can't even be bought by material blessings that God provides.
> We were already purchased by the blood of Christ.

## 5. The Challenge to Our Faith

We live in a world that asks difficult questions. Not everyone will understand. Not everyone will agree. Some will call you names. Some will oppose you. Some will make your life harder.

One young Christian man faced exactly this choice. He could have remained silent about his faith. He could have confined his convictions to private moments. But instead, he lived openly as a Christian, bringing Jesus into his cultural commentary, preaching that following Jesus was the most important thing in life.

When he was assassinated this week, his death proclaimed a message: Some things are worth dying for. Some convictions run deeper than comfort. Some commitments transcend the fear of suffering.

In light of his witness, people around the world are returning to church, asking about Jesus, reconsidering their faith. His life—and his death—became a testimony that God is real and worthy of allegiance, even unto death.

**We need more men and women like this.** We lost a righteous voice this week. But I see in this room—in all of you—the potential to collectively fill that void. We have the capacity to be righteous in hard times, to stand for truth when it's costly, to follow Jesus even when the culture opposes.

What will your answer be when tested? Will you maintain your integrity like Job? Will you refuse to curse God even when everything is stripped away? Will you be faithful in good times and bad?

---

## 6. God Is Here

If you are not yet a Christian, don't let suffering make you conclude that God doesn't exist. God is here.

Two thousand years ago, He made a personal appearance in Jesus Christ. Jesus was like Job in this respect—He was blameless and upright, He feared God and shunned evil. Out of love for His brothers and sisters, He offered Himself in sacrifice.

But His sacrifice was not animals. He sacrificed His own blameless life. Jesus is the greatest man—greater even than Job. And the cross was lifted high so that all could see God's presence and reach out to Him for forgiveness, mercy, and hope.

**In a world that often seems random and meaningless, God is here.** He has a plan. He knows what He is doing. Nothing can affect His sovereignty. God is in control.

We may not see everything yet. There are still mysteries. Things remain hidden behind the veil between heaven and earth. But today, through Job's story, we've been given a glimpse into heaven itself. We see the absolute sovereignty of God.

To ignore God is to ignore the only true solution to suffering and evil. God has put us into difficult situations—with His own good reasons—BUT remember: He's also the only one who can get us out.

**May we have the courage to be righteous in hard times.** May we refuse to curse God even when tested. May we trust His sovereignty even when we cannot see His purposes. And may we, like Job and like Jesus, maintain our integrity and faith through whatever suffering comes our way.

In Jesus' name, Amen.
