# Hebrews 11:1-7

**Title:** Faith and the Good Life
**Date:** 1 July 2018
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Understanding Faith

> Now faith is confidence in what we hope for and assurance about what we do not see.

Faith contains a paradox that blends two seemingly opposite meanings. In our modern, empirical age—where we're taught that reality is what we can see, touch, and measure—faith operates in the opposite direction. We live in an age of materialism and scientism, where "seeing is believing." Yet biblical faith moves from the *unseen to the seen*, not the other way around.

Consider the 17th-century Dutch tulip mania: speculators bought and sold tulip bulbs for extraordinary prices, not because the flowers were inherently valuable, but because they believed prices would keep rising. This is faith of a kind—confidence without evidence, speculation without intrinsic value. The difference with Christian faith is crucial: our faith rests not on market sentiment or wishful thinking, but on the voice and command of God.

### Faith Moves from Unseen to Seen

> By faith we understand that the universe was formed at God's command, so that what is seen was not made out of what was visible.

In Genesis, there was an empty, formless void—nothing visible. Then, at God's command, the moon and stars appeared, land and water emerged, animals and humanity came into being. God saw what was unseen and spoke it into reality. That is faith in its purest form.

Our modern mindset travels from seen to unseen—we observe the present and speculate about the future. But God's order works differently: faith perceives the invisible foundation and trusts it into visibility. This is not blind faith or superstition; it's faith grounded in God's word.

> And without faith it is impossible to please God, because anyone who comes to him must believe that he exists and that he rewards those who earnestly seek him.

## Abel: Persevering Against the Devil

> By faith Abel brought God a better offering than Cain did. By faith he was commended as righteous, when God spoke well of his offerings. And through his faith Abel still speaks, even though he is dead.

We don't know all the details of why God accepted Abel's animal offering but rejected Cain's fruit offering. Perhaps there was an unwritten sacrificial system already in place—a way for Adam and Eve's descendants to approach God at the gates of Eden, where the cherubim stood. What matters is that Abel trusted God's way, while Cain invented his own path to God.

Cain represents the first false religion: human invention replacing divine instruction. When God warned Cain that sin was crouching at his door—"it desires to have you, but you must rule over it"—Cain chose differently. He killed his brother in jealous rage, becoming the first martyr's murderer.

Yet Abel's faith speaks beyond death. Through his blood, his witness endures. Jesus said:

> Because you have seen me, you have believed; blessed are those who have not seen and yet have believed.

**Application:** We face opposition from the devil, from false religion, from the pressure to invent our own way to God. Like Abel, we're called to persevere in faith despite unfairness, despite not fully understanding God's choices. God sees injustice and will judge it. Our task is to remain faithful.

## Enoch: Persevering Against the Flesh

> By faith Enoch was taken from this life, so that he did not experience death: "He could not be found, because God had taken him away." For before he was taken, he was commended as one who pleased God.

Enoch lived 365 years. That's not just time—that's a lifetime of temptation, of accumulated habits, of a body growing weaker with age. In our fallen state, our bodies are like aging devices accumulating glitches. Yet Enoch "walked with God" throughout his long life, maintaining communion with the Almighty day after day, year after year.

This teaches us something crucial: faith isn't only necessary when facing martyrdom. It's essential in the ordinary struggles of everyday life, in resisting the creeping compromises that erode our character.

> And without faith it is impossible to please God, because anyone who comes to him must believe that he exists and that he rewards those who earnestly seek him.

**Application:** We live in comfort and relative prosperity, which brings its own temptations. Materialism can make us forget that all we have flows from God—we're channels of blessing, not the source. The cares of this world, the deceitfulness of riches, and the lusts of other things choke the word and make it unfruitful.

We construct our own prisons: taking longer holidays, increasing our distance from accountability, slowly drifting from God while telling ourselves we're fine. We want rewards without seeking God. But rewards come only to those who earnestly pursue him.

We must persevere against the flesh: the comfort, the busyness, the distraction. Be disciplined. Shape your desires, passions, and habits into godly form.

## Noah: Persevering Against the World

> By faith Noah, when warned about things not yet seen, in holy fear built an ark to save his family. By his faith he condemned the world and became heir of the righteousness that is in keeping with faith.

Noah faced a world utterly opposed to him. He was warned of a flood—something the earth had never experienced. In Genesis, we learn that before the flood, it had never rained on the earth; water came up from underground springs. Noah was told to build an ark for an event that had no precedent, no visible warning signs.

Yet Noah acted in holy fear—a reverent, wholesome fear that gripped only him while the world mocked. He built the ark, not knowing he was saving human history itself. By his faith, he "condemned the world"—his obedience was a silent judgment on the world's refusal to listen.

**Application:** Today, we live in a world that mocks Christ. There's a double standard: you can say anything about Jesus with impunity, swear at him, mock him. Yet the same culture protects other beliefs fiercely. You can persecute his followers, ridicule them, cost them jobs, ostracize them—and nothing happens.

The world appears to be winning. The establishment, media, celebrities, politicians, and big businesses have power, money, and influence. If you operate by sight, you follow the visible winners. If you operate by faith, you follow Jesus—knowing another judgment is coming, not by water this time, but by fire.

We cannot see where this will end. But faith means trusting in what is unseen—in Christ's promises, in his ultimate return, in the reality that he will have the final word.

## Faith is Not Blind—It's Based on God's Word

Faith isn't wishful thinking or superstition. It's grounded in something solid: God's voice. Abel heard God's requirement. Enoch walked and talked with God. Noah received direct instruction to build the ark. Their faith wasn't baseless speculation—it was response to God's word.

> By faith we understand that the universe was formed at God's command.

The word of God was spoken. Creation itself testifies to the power of divine speech. We have this same foundation: God's verbal communication to us, preserved in Scripture. The word written for us today, meant to be heard and proclaimed, not merely read.

Earlier in Hebrews, we learned that Christ is superior to the angels, the prophets, Moses, Aaron, Joshua, the priesthood, and the old covenant itself. This word of God—testifying to Christ's supremacy—is our assurance. It calls us to go where no one has gone before, into the unseen future, trusting not because we're foolish, but because we've heard from the God who speaks worlds into existence.

---

## The End of Faith: Seeing God

And this faith leads somewhere. It doesn't end in uncertainty—it ends in sight.

We will see God's face in Jesus. We will hear him say, "Well done, good and faithful servant. You persevered against the devil, the flesh, and the world. Come into my kingdom and enjoy the banquet."

This is where faith leads: from trusting in Christ's completed work in history, through the unseen present, to the day we see him face to face.

> Be thou my vision, O Lord of my heart.
