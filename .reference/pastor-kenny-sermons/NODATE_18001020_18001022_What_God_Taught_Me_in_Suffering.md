# Job 1:20-22

**Title:** What God Taught Me in Suffering  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Weight of Suffering

Life itself carries a certain disability—we all carry something wrong with us. We live in a world where tragedy strikes without warning. Recently, the assassination of Charlie Kirk, a young Christian voice and debater, left his wife and two children without a husband and father. At just 31 years old, he's gone. His family now faces the raw, overwhelming grief of loss. And we understand this deeply because we each carry our own sorrows—the sting of tragedies, the finality of death, the weight of misfortune. How do we overcome such grief?

## Job's Example of Righteousness in Tragedy

As we study Job at church, we encounter a man who endured extreme tragedy and catastrophic loss. He lost everything: his children, his home, his wealth, his health. Yet through it all, he maintained his righteousness. This echoes Jesus on the cross—enduring injustice without cursing God.

What was it about Job's thinking that we can learn?

> **Naked I came from my mother's womb, and naked I will depart. The Lord gave and the Lord has taken away; may the name of the Lord be praised.** (Job 1:21)

> In all this, Job did not sin by charging God with wrongdoing. (Job 1:22)

## The Foundation: God's Sovereignty

How did Job endure? His perspective was rooted in a simple truth: we arrive with nothing and we leave with nothing. God, as Creator, determines the distribution of blessings. He decides when we live and when we die. In many trials, we don't understand the reason. In Job's case, unknown to him, behind the curtain, this was a test—a call to persevere and to praise the Lord's name.

This applies to our modern struggles too. High school exams. Unemployment. The death of loved ones. God has His own purposes, though they remain hidden from us. Yet we are called to remain righteous through it all.

## A Prayer for Our Leaders

We pray for our elected officials to be honorable and righteous. We thank You, Lord, for the blessings You provide. With these gifts, may our leaders—and all of us—do what is right in the eyes of both our communities and the Almighty.
