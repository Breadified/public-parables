# Hebrews 13:1-6

**Title:** Persevering in the Church II
**Date:** September 9, 2018
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Foundation: Brotherly Love

> Keep on loving one another as brothers and sisters. (Hebrews 13:1)

The Greek word *Philadelphia* (philo = love; adelphos = brethren) means "love of the brethren." This isn't merely sentiment—it's a deliberate, active commitment to embrace one another as family within the church community.

This command reaches beyond those we naturally prefer. It extends to strangers, to outsiders, to those we might ordinarily overlook. The early church understood this radical inclusion as central to their witness.

## Welcoming Strangers with Hospitality

> Do not forget to show hospitality to strangers, for by so doing some people have shown hospitality to angels without knowing it. (Hebrews 13:2)

In the first century, hospitality wasn't a luxury—it was a lifeline. With no safe inns available and travelers vulnerable, homes became the refuge for pilgrims and sojourners. To welcome a stranger was a genuinely costly act.

The word "stranger" (*xenos* in Greek, from Latin *extraneous*) describes someone foreign, unknown, potentially dangerous. Yet Scripture repeatedly commands us to welcome them as we would welcome Christ himself.

### The Connection to Matthew 25

Jesus teaches that caring for the vulnerable—the hungry, thirsty, stranger, naked, sick, and imprisoned—is ultimately caring for Him:

> Whatever you did for one of the least of these brothers and sisters of mine, you did for me. (Matthew 25:40)

This isn't about sentiment; it's about recognizing Christ in the marginalized and responding with concrete love.

### Hospitality Rooted in Memory

> When a foreigner resides among you in your land, do not mistreat them. The foreigner residing among you must be treated as your native-born. Love them as yourself, for you were foreigners in Egypt. (Leviticus 19:33-34)

The Israelites were commanded to remember their own displacement, their own vulnerability. That memory was meant to cultivate compassion. We, too, are strangers in this world, pilgrims awaiting the city to come. This identity should shape how we treat others.

### The Practice at Local Level

While Western, Christian-influenced nations have become remarkably multicultural on a macro level, the real test of our hospitality is at home—in our churches and neighborhoods. Do we welcome the stranger at our door? Do we invite the outsider into our community?

## Remembering Those Who Suffer

> Continue to remember those in prison as if you were together with them in prison, and those who are mistreated as if you yourselves were suffering. (Hebrews 13:3)

Paul writes to a community facing persecution. "Prison" here represents not just literal confinement but suffering for faith. The church is called to empathetic solidarity—not distant pity, but identification.

Christianity remains the most persecuted religion globally. Millions face violence, imprisonment, and death for refusing to renounce their faith. Some face injustice undeservedly. Our response should be more than awareness; it should be prayerful advocacy and willingness to bear their burden as our own.

This requires *discipline*—the willingness to prioritize others' suffering over our comfort, to maintain faith when surrounded by difficulty.

## Honoring Marriage

> Marriage should be honored by all, and the marriage bed kept pure, for God will judge the adulterers and all the sexually immoral. (Hebrews 13:4)

The command is both corporate ("honored by all") and intimate ("kept pure"). The Greek word *amianto* means "undefiled" or "set apart"—sacred, reserved, sanctified.

### What Purity Means

The sanctity of the marriage bed isn't about prudishness. It's about reserving sexual intimacy for the covenant relationship alone. This excludes:

- Pornography (even if both spouses "agree")
- Third parties
- Entertainment that undermines the marriage (like reality TV framed around infidelity)
- The assumption that sexuality can be shared casually before marriage

Couples honor the marriage by protecting it—by not inviting the world into their intimate life through media, gossip, or compromise.

### The Broader Principle

Even before marriage, sexual purity matters. "Quasi-marriages"—cohabitation, extended vacations together that simulate marriage—blur sacred boundaries. The discipline required is not restrictive; it's protective.

## Freedom from the Love of Money

> Keep your lives free from the love of money and be content with what you have, because God has said, "Never will I leave you; never will I forsake you." (Hebrews 13:5)

The love of money—not money itself, but the grip it holds—is corrosive to love. It creates an inverse correlation: the more we grasp for wealth, the less hospitable we become. A small house squeezed with guests shows more generosity than a mansion guarded jealously.

### Contentment as Trust

True contentment isn't apathy or indifference. It's the fruit of trust—believing God's promise: "Never will I leave you; never will I forsake you." When we believe He is our provider, we can release our grip on accumulation and open our hands to generosity.

Contentment means we're not discontent about everything except one thing: the kingdom, obedience, faithfulness, and loyalty to Christ. These are the only pursuits worthy of our striving.

## Confidence in God's Protection

> So we say with confidence, "The Lord is my helper; I will not be afraid. What can mere mortals do to me?" (Hebrews 13:6)

In the face of persecution and hardship, this confession anchors faith. God is our helper. We are not abandoned to the whims of human power.

## The Theme: Perseverance Through Discipline

Chapter 13 presents a series of commands that all require one thing: **discipline**. Not the harsh rigidity of legalism, but the steady faithfulness of a disciple.

- Loving when it's inconvenient
- Welcoming when it's costly
- Remembering the suffering when it's uncomfortable
- Honoring marriage when the world encourages compromise
- Choosing contentment when consumerism demands more

Our generation struggles with this. We've become skilled at quitting—jobs, relationships, commitments. We expect satisfaction immediately and abandon difficulty quickly. We post our absences on social media instead of honoring our word. Half of our marriages end in divorce. We exhibit little of the grit of previous generations.

Yet this is precisely what discipleship demands: the will to persevere despite hardship, to remain faithful when faithfulness costs something.

### The Story of Perseverance

Consider Abebe Bikila competing in the 1968 Olympic marathon. After a terrible fall 19 kilometers into the race, with a bleeding leg and what appeared to be a dislocated knee, medical staff urged him to withdraw. But he finished—last place, over an hour after the winner—because, as he later said:

> My country did not send me 5,000 miles just to start the race; they sent me to finish the race.

No one remembers the gold medalist's name. But the world honors Bikila because he finished despite impossible circumstances.

We are called to finish the race. Not perfectly, but faithfully. This is the character of the Christian: perseverance rooted in love for God and His people.
