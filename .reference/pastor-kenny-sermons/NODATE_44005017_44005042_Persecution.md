# Acts 5:17-42

**Title:** Persecution

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## A Brief History of Persecution

Christianity's story is fundamentally a story of persecution. Throughout history and into the present day, Christians have faced opposition for their faith. Today, a Christian is being killed every eleven minutes across more than 130 countries worldwide. Yet this opposition is not new—it stretches back two thousand years to the very beginning of the church.

The persecution of Christians has intensified in regions where democratization has occurred. The Arab Spring, while bringing political change, has paradoxically accelerated the exodus of Christians from the Middle East. A century ago, Christians comprised roughly 20% of the Middle East's population; today that figure has dropped to approximately 5%. Without intervention, within fifty years there may be virtually no Christians remaining in lands where Christianity has thrived for two millennia.

In Egypt, the Coptic Christian community has been devastated. Hundreds of thousands have fled, with some churches collapsing from thousands of members to mere handfuls. Following the removal of the Morsi government, churches and Christian facilities were attacked across the nation. A Christian businessman was brutally murdered near Luxor, and entire villages of Christian homes were burned. As a result, approximately 100,000 Christians left Egypt in 2011 alone.

Iraq has experienced similar devastation. Since the fall of Saddam Hussein in 2003, the Christian population may have declined by as much as 50% in just a decade.

In Palestine, the transformation has been equally profound. Christians represented 15% of the Arab population in 1950; today they comprise just 2%. Both Bethlehem and Nazareth, once overwhelmingly Christian cities, now have strong Muslim majorities. Jerusalem's Christian population has shrunk to 2.5%, despite nearly 600 historic churches standing in the city. At the present rate of decline, within a generation there may be no Christians living in Bethlehem, the birthplace of Jesus, or in Nazareth, where he grew up.

## The Apostles Filled with Jealousy (5:17-26)

### The Cause: Success Brings Opposition

> Then the high priest and all his associates, who were members of the party of the Sadducees, were filled with jealousy. They arrested the apostles and put them in the public jail. (Acts 5:17-18)

The apostles' arrest stems directly from jealousy. What provoked this jealousy? The explosive growth of the church. Consider the progression:

- At Pentecost, three thousand believed (Acts 2:41)
- Later, about five thousand men believed (Acts 4:4)
- Now, more and more men and women were being added to their number daily (Acts 5:14)

The crowds wanted to follow the apostles' teaching. This same dynamic appears elsewhere in Scripture:

> When the Jews saw the crowds, they were filled with jealousy and talked abusively against what Paul was saying. (Acts 14:45)

### The Response: Mobilizing Authority

The religious establishment took the threat seriously. They called together the entire Sanhedrin—the full assembly of the elders of Israel—to deal with this problem. This was no minor matter; the highest religious court in Jerusalem was convened.

But then comes an almost comical turn:

> On arriving at the jail, the officers did not find them there. So they went back and reported, "We found the jail securely locked, with the guards standing at the doors; but when we opened them, we found no one inside." On hearing this report, the captain of the temple guard and the chief priests were puzzled, wondering what would come of this. (Acts 5:22-24)

The guards faced execution for losing their prisoners, yet the apostles were not hiding or fleeing the country. Instead:

> Then someone came and said, "Look! The men you put in jail are standing in the temple courts teaching the people." (Acts 5:25)

### Divine Deliverance and Obedience

The explanation for this miraculous escape is given simply:

> During the night an angel of the Lord opened the doors of the jail and brought them out. "Go," said the angel, "stand in the temple courts and tell the people the full message of this new life." (Acts 5:19-20)

The apostles received a clear command: proclaim the full message of this new life. And that is exactly what they did.

> At daybreak they entered the temple courts, as they had been told, and began to teach the people. (Acts 5:21)

There was no hesitation, no reconsidering after their harrowing imprisonment. At the earliest opportunity, they resumed their testimony in the very place where they had been arrested. In contrast to the Sanhedrin's attempt to suppress them with their full assembly, the apostles were called to tell the people the complete message of eternal life.

## The Apostles Filled with Teaching (5:27-42)

### The Accusation and Response

> Having brought the apostles, they made them appear before the Sanhedrin to be questioned by the high priest. "We gave you strict orders not to teach in this name," he said. "Yet you have filled Jerusalem with your teaching and are determined to make us guilty of this man's blood." (Acts 5:27-28)

The high priest captures the heart of the conflict: the apostles have "filled Jerusalem" with their teaching. The religious leaders demanded obedience to their prohibition; the apostles instead filled an entire city with the good news.

Peter's response cuts to the core principle:

> Peter and the other apostles replied: "We must obey God rather than men!" (Acts 5:29)

### The Message They Proclaimed

What was this teaching that posed such a threat? Peter explains:

> The God of our fathers raised Jesus from the dead—whom you had killed by hanging him on a tree. God exalted him to his own right hand as Prince and Savior that he might give repentance and forgiveness of sins to Israel. We are witnesses of these things, and so is the Holy Spirit, whom God has given to those who obey him. (Acts 5:30-32)

The core of the apostles' message was fundamentally about Jesus:
- He was killed by the Jewish leaders
- God raised him from the dead
- He is exalted at God's right hand
- He offers repentance and forgiveness to Israel
- The Holy Spirit testifies to these truths

Peter did not speak out of anti-Semitic hatred; he himself was a Jew telling the truth to his own people. The leaders had killed Jesus, and now they needed to seek repentance. But Peter also offered something more: a solution. The very one they had rejected could still grant them forgiveness.

### Personal Application of the Message

This message applies to all of us. In a real sense, we have all participated in killing Jesus. Not directly, but indirectly—through sin. Each of us, as part of humanity, bears responsibility for driving him to the cross. Our sins hammered another nail into his hands and feet. He came to earth because of us; we were the reason for his sacrifice.

As one contemporary song expresses it:

> We are the reason that He gave His life
> We were the reason that He suffered and died
> To a world that was lost He gave all He could give
> To show us the reason to live

This awareness of Christ's sacrifice for us is what compels Christians to give themselves to others, to sacrifice for one another, to love without reserve—because Christ loved us first.

### The Apostles' Joyful Perseverance

> The apostles left the Sanhedrin, rejoicing because they had been counted worthy of suffering disgrace for the Name. Day after day, in the temple courts and from house to house, they never stopped teaching and proclaiming the good news that Jesus is the Christ. (Acts 5:41-42)

Here is the remarkable response to persecution: joy. They considered it an honor to suffer for the name of Jesus. And rather than being intimidated into silence, they intensified their witness. Day after day, publicly and house to house, they proclaimed that Jesus is the Christ.

## A Wise Voice: Gamaliel's Counsel

Not all the Sanhedrin wanted to execute the apostles. One respected teacher spoke up:

> A Pharisee named Gamaliel, a teacher of the law, who was honored by all the people, stood up in the Sanhedrin and ordered that the men be put outside for a little while. (Acts 5:34)

Gamaliel then offered two arguments for restraint:

First, he cited historical precedent. Theudas had claimed to be someone important, gathering four hundred followers, but when he was killed, his movement collapsed and scattered. Similarly, Judas the Galilean led a rebellion but was killed, and his followers were dispersed. Gamaliel's point: movements rarely survive the death of their founder. Unless this movement had something genuine at its core, it too would fail on its own.

Second, Gamaliel appealed to the sovereignty of God:

> Leave these men alone! Let them go! For if their purpose or activity is of human origin, it will fail. But if it is from God, you will not be able to stop these men; you will only find yourselves fighting against God. (Acts 5:38-39)

Why persecute people for preaching and acts of kindness? Why not simply let time reveal whether this movement is genuine? If it is merely human, it will collapse. If it is truly from God, no amount of opposition will stop it—and opposition would only put the Sanhedrin in the position of fighting against God himself.

Gamaliel's wisdom prevailed—partially. The apostles were released, but not before receiving a severe flogging and being ordered not to speak in Jesus' name.

## Why the Hostility?

### Persecution in the Modern West

In our contemporary context, persecution takes different forms. Rather than arrest and execution, Western Christians face a clamping down of their voice in the public square.

The media has been remarkably effective at ridiculing Christian positions. To express anything distinctly Christian—whether about the sanctity of life, the nature of marriage, or the moral foundations of society—is met with derision. In classrooms and tutorials, many Christian students fear speaking honestly about their convictions. There is a stark asymmetry: while Christians are increasingly silenced, advocates for alternative worldviews are given considerable platform and freedom to speak.

This censorship often hides behind the language of tolerance. But true tolerance is not the absence of offense; it is the ability to live in community with those we disagree with, to raise our children according to our own values, and to speak freely without fear of violence or suppression. Real tolerance means we can offend each other through disagreement and yet maintain civil society and mutual respect.

Yet increasingly, the standard being applied is: if something offends me, it should be silenced. This is a fundamental misunderstanding of tolerance. If offense were sufficient grounds for censorship, countless ideas would be outlawed—but we allow that because free speech is foundational to a free society.

One particularly telling case involved Pastor Daniel Nallilah in Victoria, Australia, who spoke against Islam at a conference. Three Muslim attendees brought legal action against him under anti-vilification laws. Though initially found guilty, the Victorian Supreme Court ultimately acquitted him. The pastor had been clear in his message: love and witness to Muslims, not hatred. Speaking against an idea is not dangerous; it is the substance of free discourse in a tolerant society.

### The Real Fear Beneath Persecution

What lies beneath this hostility toward Christianity? What prompts both ancient Sanhedrin and modern secular societies to attempt silencing Christian voices?

Perhaps the answer lies in what Christians have actually accomplished when left to pursue their convictions freely. History shows that wherever Christianity has taken root, societies have experienced profound transformation:

**Against infanticide and oppression:** The early Church Fathers advocated fiercely against practices that degraded human life—infanticide, polygamy, child abuse, and slavery. The Romans discarded unwanted babies on trash heaps or sold them into slavery and prostitution. It was distinctly Christian conviction about the sanctity of human life that brought these horrors to an end.

**Against brutality:** The Roman games—where beasts fought men, and vast crowds gathered hoping to witness death—reveal the lust for blood that characterized pagan Rome. Christianity redirected this society toward compassion.

**Strengthening marriage:** Church teaching profoundly shaped the legal and social understanding of marriage as a sacred covenant, not a mere economic transaction. During the Gregorian Reform, the Church developed a theology of marriage as a sacrament that continues to influence Western law and custom.

**Civilizing the barbarian world:** Following the collapse of Charlemagne's empire, when forces of disintegration and decay threatened to destroy Western civilization, it was the Latin Christian Church that "patiently and persistently labored to combat the forces of disintegration and decay," restraining violence and restoring order, justice, and decency.

**Healthcare:** Christians played a pivotal role in establishing hospitals. The Council of Nicea in 325 A.D. decreed that hospitals should be established wherever the Church was established. St. Basil of Caesarea built a hospital in 370 A.D. that even treated lepers, who had previously been isolated from society entirely. The Church remains the single largest provider of healthcare in the world.

**Education:** Nearly all schools before the state assumed responsibility for education were founded by the Church. In the United States, 100 of the first 110 universities were church-founded, including Yale, Princeton, and Harvard. The University of Hong Kong traces its origins to the London Missionary Society.

**Abolishing slavery:** In England, it was Christian conviction that powered the movement to abolish the slave trade. William Wilberforce, driven by his Christian faith, led the charge against one of humanity's greatest evils.

**Ending widow burning:** In India, Christian missionaries and advocates fought against the practice of widow burning, saving countless women from this barbaric custom.

**Caring for the vulnerable:** Thomas John Barnardo founded what became the world's largest orphanage system. Camillus de Lellis founded the original Red Cross. The Salvation Army has served the destitute and broken for generations.

**Social stability during upheaval:** The teaching of John and Charles Wesley was largely responsible for preventing the social unrest and violence that might otherwise have accompanied the Industrial Revolution in England.

### Christianity's Global Influence

I am not claiming that Christians have always done good—there have been wars and failures aplenty. Nor am I claiming that other belief systems never do good; I am certain there are humanist hospitals, Buddhist charitable works, and Muslim orphanages. But the sheer consistency, scale, and generative power of Christian charity and social transformation is unparalleled. No other religion or belief system has the capacity for self-correction and renewal that Christianity possesses.

The attractiveness of Western civilization—whose laws, ethos, and culture are built on Judeo-Christian principles—can be seen in the way people worldwide clamor to immigrate to Western societies. Whether through dangerous journeys across deserts or treacherous boat crossings, people still seek to reach the shores of America, Europe, and Australia. They recognize, perhaps instinctively, that where Christian values have flourished, human dignity and opportunity flourish as well.

**South Korea:** The story of Korea's transformation is striking. In the 1960s, the nation lay devastated by war. Today, over the past forty years, Korea has experienced an economic miracle—the very phones we carry (Samsung, LG) testify to this transformation. But the spiritual transformation is equally remarkable: approximately one-third of South Koreans are now Christian. Seoul boasts ten of the eleven largest Christian congregations in the world. South Korea sends more missionaries abroad than any nation except the United States. In 1950, only 2.4% of Koreans were Protestant; now they comprise most of the 30% Christian population.

**South Africa:** Nelson Mandela, after spending 27 years in prison for the cause of freedom, emerged with his Christian convictions intact. In his autobiography, *The Long Walk to Freedom*, he reflected on his early experiences with Christianity: "The Church was as concerned with this world as the next: I saw that virtually all of the achievements of Africans seemed to have come about through the missionary work of the Church." Mandela understood that Christian faith had been the engine of liberation and human dignity in his nation.

**China's Perspective:** Perhaps most striking is the testimony of scholars from within China. David Aikman, a journalist formerly with *Time* magazine, documented an interview with a scholar from the Chinese Academy of Social Sciences in Beijing. In 2002, this premier research institute was tasked with understanding what accounted for the success and preeminence of the West globally. The scholar recounted:

> We studied everything we could from the historical, political, economic and cultural perspective. At first, we thought it was because you had more powerful guns than we had. Then we thought it was because you had the best political system. Then we focused on your economic system. But in the past twenty years, we have realised that the heart of your culture is your religion: Christianity. That is why the West has been so powerful. The Christian moral foundation of social and cultural life was what made possible the emergence of capitalism and the successful transition to democratic politics.

So profound was this recognition that in 2007, President Hu Jintao added the word "religion" to the Communist Party constitution, challenging the party to view religion as a source of economic and social stability.

## The Application: Our Task to Proclaim

The apostles' example is clear. Their job was to preach, and that responsibility is not limited to church pastors—it belongs to every Christian.

> Day after day, in the temple courts and from house to house, they never stopped teaching and proclaiming the good news that Jesus is the Christ. (Acts 5:42)

Notice the characteristics of their witness:
- **Daily persistence:** Not occasional, but day after day
- **Comprehensive scope:** Both publicly and house to house
- **Unwavering commitment:** They never stopped
- **Clear message:** Jesus is the Christ

We are called to the same task. We need not fear speaking. We do not live in a totalitarian society. Speaking about our convictions is natural—it is part of being a person who stands for something.

Consider Dr. Shinichi Suzuki, a Japanese music educator. After World War II devastated Japan, Suzuki envisioned teaching children music as naturally as they acquire language. He believed that exposure to fine music from birth, combined with instruction, would develop sensitivity, discipline, and endurance—creating children with beautiful and noble hearts. He went about telling everyone of his method. Today, there are over 8,000 Suzuki teachers worldwide, and more than 250,000 children learn by the Suzuki Method across five continents.

This is what naturally happens when someone discovers something genuinely good—they tell others. In the Christian case, it is not arrogance but, as one has said, one beggar telling another beggar where to find bread.

## The Question We Must Face

Yet we must ask: Why this hostility toward Jesus? By all historical accounts, he was a noble and sacrificial man. Why the aggression toward his disciples, who by all accounts have contributed immeasurably to human flourishing? Why the desire to kill them after killing their Master?

The answer may be found in a question posed by James Kennedy and Jerry Newcombe in their book *What If Jesus Had Never Been Born?* They argue that Christianity is often derided as oppressive and restrictive, presented as chains that bind human potential. But this analysis misses the truth. The chains on a swing are not restrictions preventing flight—they are what enables us to soar to great heights. To cut those chains is to condemn oneself to a painful fall.

The very success of Christianity is no proof by itself that Jesus is genuine. It could theoretically be merely a clever social and economic theory. But consider: when Christians were simply left alone to pursue their convictions, they did immeasurable good in the world. This historical reality invites us to listen to the words of Jesus himself. Perhaps we can remove the barriers—the jealousy, the fear, the hostility—that prevent us from hearing his message clearly and responding to it with our whole hearts.

The apostles left the Sanhedrin rejoicing, counted worthy of suffering for the name of Jesus. They continued proclaiming the good news without ceasing. Their joy was not despite persecution but because they had been entrusted with the greatest message the world could hear. May we discover that same joy, that same courage, that same unwavering commitment to proclaiming that Jesus is the Christ.
