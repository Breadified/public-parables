# 1 John 5:12-11

**Title:** Gifts in the Church: Finding Your Place in God's Work

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Understanding the Nature of Gifts

### The Word: *Charisma*

The Greek word for "gift" is *charisma*, derived from *charis* (grace). Literally, it means "a grace thing"—something undeserved and unattainable by our own effort.

At its heart, a gift reflects the nature of grace itself. Just as we cannot earn salvation, we cannot earn spiritual gifts through our efforts. We see this most clearly in Romans 6:23: "The wages of sin is death, but the gift of God is eternal life in Christ Jesus." Grace is doing something kind for someone who doesn't deserve it.

Interestingly, in Ephesians 4:7, Paul doesn't even use the word "gift." Instead, he says: "But to each one of us, *grace* has been given as Christ apportioned it." The word "grace" can itself mean "gift."

### A Critical Distinction: Natural Ability vs. Gift

There's a significant problem in how we use the word "gift" in English. The word carries two meanings: a thing given (a present) and a natural ability. When we call someone a "gifted" musician, we're describing their innate musical talent, not something given as a present.

This confusion infiltrates our thinking about spiritual gifts. A "gifted" preacher becomes someone with superior public speaking technique, which has little to do with the biblical concept of a gift as "a thing given."

The word "talent" compounds this problem. In Matthew 25, Jesus speaks of talents as ancient currency, but we've reinterpreted the parable to mean he's distributing natural abilities—something Jesus never intended.

## Theological Foundations: God's Sovereignty

### God Cannot Be Surprised

God is sovereign—in control of everything, dependent on nothing. We cannot surprise God, nor is he dependent on us for anything. The complete reverse is true.

Grace is always surprising. It is the very nature of a gift that we cannot know what it is until it is given. Therefore, we cannot always predict what a person's gift might be in any given situation.

### We Are Dependent on God

We are completely dependent on God for everything. We cannot even breathe apart from his sovereign care. This has profound implications for how we think about gifts: we must never compromise God's sovereignty.

When we think of gifts primarily in terms of natural abilities, God's work in the church becomes dependent on us—on what we can or cannot do. This is fundamentally unbiblical.

### Two Kinds of Gifts

God's natural gifts come to all people: the ability to breathe, walk, eat, and develop skills. But when the Bible speaks of gifts in the church, it refers to something different—our special contribution to building up the body.

Our natural abilities can certainly be used by God to build up the church, and he most often does just that. But it is not necessary that he do so—he is sovereign.

**The crucial distinction:** A gift to the church is not defined by what it looks like, but by how it functions. *Gifts are given by God for the building up of the body.* A natural ability that is not used to build up the body is not a gift to the church.

This answers our earlier question directly: Yes, every Christian does have a gift, but that gift is always and only connected with building up the body. If a particular Christian ministry does not build up the body, then no gift is being exercised, no matter how naturally talented the person is.

A musician, preacher, or evangelist who only builds up their own ego—or worse, damages the church—has no gift at all, regardless of their natural abilities. When God does not use our activity to bring blessing to the church, we have no gift at all.

### The Problem of "Static Gifting"

Much current thinking assumes that every Christian receives one, two, or three specific abilities at conversion—a fixed set that represents the sum total of their usefulness to the church. Some even claim to know the exact date they received a specific gift.

This directly contradicts God's sovereignty in two ways:

**First,** the Bible never suggests that every Christian has only one gift, or that gifts remain fixed for life. God is far more powerful than that. He can give us many different gifts at different times, and he can take them away. A gift is not simply a natural ability—it always builds up the church. God can build his church in any way, through any person he chooses. He is not bound by a gift he gave years ago. New situations bring new possibilities for gifting.

**Second,** static gifting makes God a hostage to our abilities and circumstances. To say "I have the gift of music" claims that you can only be used through music. If no opportunity for music arises, you become useless to the body. Further, churches can become dangerously unbalanced. Imagine a church where 80 percent of members are accomplished musicians—80 percent would compete for opportunities to perform while teaching the Word remains neglected.

This challenge to God's sovereignty is also a challenge to Christ's centrality in the church.

## Ecclesiology: The Doctrine of the Church

### What Is the Church?

The church is the body of Christ—the eternal gathering of believers around Christ in heaven. At the moment of belief, we are united to Christ our Lord through his death and resurrection, and therefore united to each other in him (Ephesians 2:1-6).

This heavenly church is expressed in the here and now through local gatherings of believers around Christ in his word. Each local church is a full and complete expression of the true church—not merely a part of it, but a true church in itself.

Christ is the head of the church and its goal. Paul uses a striking metaphor in Ephesians 4:15: "Speaking the truth in love, we will in all things grow up into him who is the Head, that is, Christ." Our goal is to grow in likeness to Christ.

### Four Implications for Understanding Gifts

**First,** Christ is the center and head. Nothing in the church is about me. The church is about Christ—it is *his* body. The church is not an opportunity for self-promotion, but a platform for drawing attention to Christ as the head.

**Second,** because the church is Christ's *body*—people irrevocably related in love to one another through Christ—not just a collection of individuals, my existence in the church must always benefit the body, not just myself. Our goal must be to build up the body, both in its heavenly and local expressions.

**Third,** we are members of Christ's body, not independent contractors. God has designed the church so that every member always needs the other members. No one is independent. Ephesians 4:16 tells us: "From Christ the whole body, joined and held together by every supporting ligament, grows and builds itself up in love, as each part does its work."

It is only as each part does its work that the body functions. I cannot grow up into Christ without your ministry, and you cannot without mine.

**Fourth,** the body is God's ordained means for fulfilling his plan in the world (Ephesians 3:10-11). As each local church fully expresses the true church which gathers eternally around Christ in heaven, it would be unbiblical to suggest that any church lacks the gifts needed to fulfill its calling under God. God always fulfills his purposes. He is able to provide what every local church needs to be built up into Christ, and he always does.

### Christ-Centered vs. Self-Centered Approaches

Static gifting theology is unavoidably human-centered. It focuses on "What can *I* do? What is *my* gift?" But as members of Christ's body, everything we do must first be Christ-centered, then church-centered.

Static gifting forces us inward first—into ourselves. Biblical Christianity moves outward first, toward Christ, the church, and the world. The fundamental questions are:

- How can Christ be most glorified?
- How can the church be best built up into Christ?
- How can Christ be most faithfully and effectively proclaimed to the world?

Only from this outward-focused position can we begin to discover our place in God's work.

Furthermore, static gifting is list-focused. It seeks to label and categorize specific gifts, then apply those labels to believers. It assumes that the three major gifts passages contain most of the gifts we can hope to find in any church—implying that no church is ever complete.

But a proper understanding of the church leads us away from rigid classification. What matters most is that the church fulfills its function under God—to be built up into Christ. Any ministry that contributes to this aim is a gift to the church, whether it can be labeled or not. Since Christ is central, we should focus on context-specific needs and opportunities for growth, not on general lists of vaguely-defined offices.

## Discovering Your Gift: A Practical Approach

### Five Practical Steps

**First,** begin with the church, not with yourself. The question to ask is not "What do I feel my gifts are?" but rather "What needs can I see in the church?" Start by looking for how Christ can be best glorified and how the body can be best built up.

**Second,** pray. Ask God to raise up and equip people to fulfill these needs.

**Third,** give it a go. Do something—anything—that helps meet this need, while praying for wisdom to know the best path forward.

**Fourth,** if it becomes clear that God has not equipped you for a particular task, step back and try something else. If God blesses your ministry, give thanks for the privilege of serving Christ.

**Fifth,** if you see several equally valuable needs but cannot do them all, choose the one you feel most naturally suited to.

### The Critical Qualification: Fellowship, Not Individualism

These five steps can easily be read individualistically, but that would be exactly wrong. **Every single point must be decided and taken in fellowship.**

It is not you alone, in your individual wisdom, who discerns your church's needs—it is *we*, the church, who think together. It is not you alone who prays for God's equipping, but the church as a body. You don't act in complete independence from the church, but in harmony with it, receiving their encouragement for the task.

You don't decide alone whether a task is right for you—the church encourages you into or away from particular ministries. Sometimes, even if a role suits you perfectly and blesses God's people, if the ministry as a whole fails to fulfill its task, the church may decide to close it down. You must submit to this.

And it is never you personally and privately who weigh which ministry best suits you. This is always done in conjunction with the fellowship. Sometimes the church will see you very differently from how you see yourself, and you need humbly to submit to their leading, even if you're convinced another way is better.

## Gifts in the New Testament: The Real Problem

### Why Paul Teaches About Gifts in Some Letters, Not Others

It's striking that only Romans, 1 Corinthians, and Ephesians contain major teaching about gifts. In other letters, Paul addresses every-member ministry without mentioning gifts at all. The Galatians are told to "carry each other's burdens" and "do good to all people, especially to those of the household of faith" (Galatians 6:2, 10). The Philippians are instructed to "look not only to your own interests, but also to the interests of others" (Philippians 2:4). The Colossians are to "walk worthily of the Lord, pleasing him in every way, bearing fruit in every good work" (Colossians 1:10). All of these are exhortations to exercise God's gifts—yet the word "gift" never appears.

This reveals something important: New Testament letters were not abstract theological treatises. Paul wrote in response to specific problems in specific churches. When a letter devotes significant space to a topic, that topic was addressing a real problem.

### The Common Problem: Exclusivism

All three churches with major gifts teaching faced the same fundamental problem: **exclusivism**—the sinful temptation to treat some members as more valuable, more spiritual, or closer to God than others.

**In Ephesus,** there was division between Jews and Gentiles, with Jewish Christians claiming superiority. Paul devotes two entire chapters to this (Ephesians 2–3), then stresses in chapter 4 that "there is one body and one Spirit" (Ephesians 4:4).

**In Rome,** there was likely similar division along ethnic lines, with some looking down on others for having scruples about eating certain foods (Romans 14:3). But here, each person—regardless of race—was convinced their own attitude made them superior.

**In Corinth,** there was no significant Jew-Gentile tension, but pride was rampant. Some boasted in their allegiance to certain leaders ("I follow Paul," "I follow Apollos," 1 Corinthians 3:4). Others created a hierarchy of spiritual value based on different states or expressions of Christian life—marriage versus singleness, tongues versus prophecy.

In each church, this pride focused on externals—on what people did or which party they belonged to.

### The Corrective: Equal Worth Before God

Paul's teaching about gifts serves as a corrective to exclusivism. It demonstrates that there is no inherent superiority of any one believer over another before God. As Paul writes in Galatians 3:28, "You are all one in Christ Jesus." The different roles each believer takes in the church are completely unrelated to a person's status before God.

Where there is no apparent problem with this kind of exclusivism, Paul achieves the same result of encouraging every-member ministry by focusing instead on the gospel as the foundation and motivation for all Christian service.

## Examining the Major Gifts Passages

### Romans 12:1-8

The context is crucial. Paul begins: "Therefore, in view of God's mercy..." Chapters 9–11 have been about God's mercy—that God chooses whom he chooses by his mercy alone, and Gentiles can be grafted into God's people only by undeserved grace.

Everything we do flows from awareness that God has already done everything. He didn't do it because we deserved it, but because he wanted to.

**Humility** is the first implication (verse 3). You are who you are only by God's mercy and kindness. Even Paul's gift of apostleship to the Gentiles is referred to as "the grace given me" (1:5).

Notice that "gift" and "function" are parallel expressions. The focus is on how people operate, not on what they possess.

Also notice the priority of the body: "Each member belongs to all the others" (12:5). If humility focuses us on what God does, this verse focuses us on the church and its needs.

The list of gifts that follows is remarkably non-specific. Paul doesn't specify that anyone *in particular* has these gifts. In fact, the original Greek has no clear subject. Literally, verses 5–8 read:

> "...in this way we, the many, are one body in Christ, and each one is a member of the others, having diverse gifts according to the grace given to us: if prophecy, according to the proportion of faith; if service, with service; if the one teaching, with teaching; if the one exhorting, with exhortation; the one giving, with generosity; the one leading, with zeal; the one showing mercy, with cheerfulness."

These are not gifts tied to particular people—they are opportunities for ministry in the church. The list almost seems random. Where are the apostles and evangelists of Ephesians 4? Where are the tongues, knowledge, and healing of 1 Corinthians 12? What matters is the functioning of the body, not the label you give to a particular person.

Strikingly, some gifts mentioned here are actually commanded of all Christians in the next passage. The leader's zeal (verse 8) is commanded of everyone (verse 11). Everyone is to be the generous giver and the merciful one, not just specially "gifted" members (verse 13).

The statement that immediately follows is striking: "Love must be without hypocrisy" (12:9). This serves as a hinge between the "specific" gifts (verses 6–8) and the general exhortations that follow (verses 9+). It describes how all gifts function. Everything is to be a sincere expression of love. Love is what makes gifts actually gifts, because love will turn the "functioning" of members into God's gift to the church.

### 1 Corinthians 12–14: The Problem of Pride

Paul begins with "spiritual things," not "spiritual gifts"—a significant distinction.

The letter can be divided into two sections: chapters 1–6 (Paul's teaching) and chapters 7–16 (answers to their questions). In the first section, Paul repeatedly addresses "spiritual things" and "fleshly things," indicating that this is one of the Corinthians' biggest problems.

The specific problem underlying their thinking about spiritual things is **pride**. Throughout chapters 1–4, Paul addresses their pride:

- In chapters 1–2, they're proud of their desire for a "spiritual" message that appears wise to the world, dressed in impressive oratory like their pagan philosophers. Paul counters: "I did not consider knowing anything except Jesus Christ and him crucified" (2:2).

- In chapter 3, they're proud of their allegiance to particular leaders. Paul challenges this: "When someone says, 'I am Paul's,' and another, 'I am of Apollos,' aren't you merely humans?" (3:4). He reminds them that leaders are "servants through whom you believed—and to each as the Lord has given" (3:5).

- In chapter 4, Paul shows the absurdity of their spiritual pride: "Already you have been filled; already you are rich; you have become kings without us! We are fools for Christ's sake, but you are sensible in Christ; we are weak, but you are strong" (4:8-10).

This pride centered on "spiritual things" continues into chapters 12–14. Paul identifies three expressions of it:

**First,** they use "gifts" to grade each other spiritually—to see who is closest to God. Paul counters: "There are different kinds of gifts, but the same Spirit" (12:4). One Spirit—they can't differentiate themselves by gifts.

**Second,** they're self-centered: "Now to each one the manifestation of the Spirit is given for the common good" (12:7). They were only interested in their own good.

**Third,** they desire independence: "The eye cannot say to the hand, 'I don't need you!' And the head cannot say to the feet, 'I don't need you!'" (12:21). If one part suffers, all suffer; if one is honored, all rejoice (12:26). Pride says "I'm fine by myself." Paul says "You can't survive without each other."

The flip-side of independence is chaos. Chapter 14 addresses this: "When you come together, everyone has a hymn, or a word of instruction, a revelation, a tongue or an interpretation. All of these must be done for the strengthening of the church...God is not a God of disorder, but of peace" (14:26, 33).

Paul even takes up their own language in 12:31 and 14:1: "Eagerly desire the greater gifts." It's highly unlikely Paul invented this concept—the Corinthians were already pursuing the "greater gifts."

### The Solution: Love in 1 Corinthians 13

There's a distinct change of mood from chapter 12 to chapter 13. Paul's closing words of chapter 12 signal this shift: "But I will show you the way that is beyond comparison."

He moves from discussing concrete manifestations of the Spirit—tongues, healing, prophecy—to talking about a "way." He moves from things that can be compared with each other to the very foundation of Christian life itself. This is not a manifestation of the Spirit like other gifts; it is the context in which all Christian life operates.

**The argument is simple: love is what makes a gift a gift. Without love, there is no gift.**

What is love? The famous description in verses 4–7 shows that love is centered outward, on the other person. It is concerned above all for what is best for others. It is the foundation, motivation, and means of Christian service.

So gifts (tongues, prophecy, generosity, etc.) are only gifts when used to serve others. As chapter 12 puts it: "To each one the manifestation of the Spirit is given for the common good" (12:7). Without this love as foundation, motivation, and means, they are nothing.

Note that the "gifts" Paul mentions in 13:1–3 are deliberately exaggerated for effect. If I speak in the tongues of men—even of angels—but have not love... (13:1). Even if I have faith that moves mountains, why would I want to? What value is burning yourself alive? Paul is piling one "amazing" gift upon another to create an impossible, even ludicrous picture. These verses are grotesque parodies meant to stress the point about the incomparability of love.

### Returning to Gifts in 1 Corinthians 14

Now that he has laid the foundation of love, Paul returns to the issue of gifts. Notice a small but significant shift from 12:31: "Pursue love, and eagerly desire spiritual things, the greatest being prophecy" (14:1).

**First,** love now takes first place. They must think about spiritual things from the foundation and viewpoint of love. What is truly spiritual is not what makes them look the best, but what comes from love.

**Second,** given the framework of love, Paul can now make a practical statement about which gift is greatest. In this context, it's prophecy.

**Third,** from the rest of chapter 14, the Corinthians' contest was really between only two gifts: prophecy and tongues. In other contexts with other churches, Paul might suggest something different (as in Ephesians 4). The principles of decision, however, remain the same.

The principle is love—seeking what is best for others. As Paul writes: "He who speaks in a tongue edifies himself, but he who prophesies edifies the church...Since you are eager for spiritual things, seek to overflow for the building up of the church" (14:4, 12).

**In other words, gifts are truly gifts when they spring from love and actively build up the church.**

### Ephesians 4:7-16

Notably, Ephesians 4:7 uses "grace" rather than "gift": "But to each one of us, grace has been given as Christ apportioned it." This connects to Romans 12:3 and 12:6, where both terms appear in parallel.

There's another interesting detail: it is **Christ**, not the Spirit, who gives these graces. Taking Romans, 1 Corinthians, and Ephesians together, the Bible presents a trinity of donation. In Romans, the Father gives gifts (12:3); in Ephesians, Christ gives (4:7); in 1 Corinthians, the Spirit gives (12:11). So gifts are gifts of God—Father, Son, and Spirit together. Gifts are not simply "gifts of the Spirit," and we cannot conclude from Scripture that we're in a special age of the Spirit.

In contrast to Romans, Paul here provides a very specific list: apostles, prophets, evangelists, and pastor-teachers. Notice that all are speaking gifts—they are foundational because they prepare the whole body to function properly (4:12). But this list is not complete or exhaustive. It's only the beginning—the tip of the iceberg. That Paul lists only these foundational gifts and no others suggests that the rest depends entirely on each church's situation. Faithful proclamation and teaching of the gospel will give people all they need to find ways of serving each other.

The goal of these word ministries is ultimately the building up of the body (verses 12, 16). While 1 Corinthians emphasizes "love," Ephesians carries the same meaning through the outward focus of "working for the good of the body to the glory of Christ." There is no concept of gifts as the personal possession of the fortunate individual. Gifts are only gifts as they build up the body.

Significantly, while Romans and 1 Corinthians describe gifting as each person using their gifts, Ephesians expresses the exact same concept differently: the word ministries function "to prepare God's people for works of service" (4:12). This is a very active view of gifts. There is no concept of a gift as something stored away to be used when you feel like it. **Gifts are only gifts when they are used.**

Finally, notice what Jesus has given his church: not gifts as things, but gifts as people. "It was he who gave some as..." (4:11). Nowhere in the New Testament is this clearer than here: **a gift is the gift of a person to the church in loving service for the purpose of building her up in Christ.**

## Practical Wisdom for Your Service

### A Better Way of Thinking About Gifts

The model we've explored makes the word "gift" almost obsolete. It's quite possible—even desirable—to speak about our contributions to God's work without mentioning "gift" at all. We could simply talk about "needs," "opportunities for building up the body," and "works of service."

This is exactly what we find throughout the rest of the New Testament. Paul told the Galatians to "carry each other's burdens" and "do good to all people, especially to those of the household of faith." The Philippians heard to "look not only to your own interests, but also to the interests of others." The Colossians were to "walk worthily of the Lord, pleasing him in every way, bearing fruit in every good work," and the Thessalonians were urged to "love one another more and more." All of these are exhortations to exercise God's gifts—without the word "gift" ever appearing.

### The Truth About Status and Service

Whatever formal or informal role you take in your church, it has nothing to do with your spiritual status before God. One of Paul's deepest convictions, most clearly expressed in Galatians 3:28, is that "you are all one in Christ Jesus."

This truth liberates us from the false constraints of so-called "giftedness" in particular areas. Once you understand the gospel—the new reality God has brought through his Son—you will actively seek whatever ways you can cooperate with God's great work of uniting all things under Christ. You will do so with confidence and joy, knowing that by God's sovereign and gracious might, you will be used as an effective and significant contributor to this end.

You will be responsive to the situations you find yourself in. You will discover countless and infinitely varied opportunities to serve Christ and his church. The gospel is not just the entry point to the Christian life; it is the foundation and motivation for all Christian service.
