# John 1:1-18

**Title:** God Revealed Fully  
**Date:** August 21, 2005  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## How Does God Reveal Himself?

Imagine there's someone you'd like to get to know. How would you introduce yourself to them? You might try clever pickup lines, write a letter, send an email, or invite them for coffee. But ultimately, the deepest form of introduction is to show up in person and meet them face-to-face.

This raises a profound question: How does God reveal himself to us? And if He wants our attention and belief, why doesn't He simply appear to us today? Many people would believe if they could see Him with their own eyes.

Before we can answer how God reveals Himself, we must understand what kind of God we're dealing with.

---

## 1. God Reveals Himself Through His Word

> In the beginning was the Word, and the Word was with God, and the Word was God. He was with God in the beginning. (John 1:1-2)

The opening verses of John tell us something crucial: God is a God who reveals Himself through His Word. He is someone who communicates and deals with creation by speaking.

For us in the Western tradition, a "word" is simply marks on a page or sounds we make. But for the Jewish people, the Word of God held profound meaning. God's Word is not passive—it is active and dynamic. It is God's very self-expression. Throughout the Old Testament, whenever we encounter "the Word of the Lord," we see it as the method by which God rules, communicates, and makes Himself known. God's Word carries divine authority and demands obedience from men and angels alike.

Notice how John echoes the opening of Genesis:

> In the beginning God created the heavens and the earth. Now the earth was formless and empty, darkness was over the surface of the deep, and the Spirit of God was hovering over the waters. And God said, "Let there be light," and there was light. (Genesis 1:1-3)

At the very start of creation, God speaks. By His Word, He brings light into being, separates it from darkness, and continues creating. This Word is not merely descriptive—it is active and creative.

John reinforces this: 

> Through him all things were made; without him nothing was made that has been made. (John 1:3)

All creation came into being through this Word. Literally, "all things became through him." Without the Word, there would be no sun, no trees, nothing at all. He alone is the sole agent of creation, needing no help. And He has always existed—He was present at the beginning, before anything was created. We see the contrast clearly: the Word existed eternally, while all creation came into being through His speaking.

Beyond creation, this Word gives life to people:

> In him was life, and that life was the light of all mankind. (John 1:4)

The Word is the sustainer of life itself. Through Him, you and I have breath and existence. He is the one who breathes into our nostrils the breath of life—the very thing God did in Genesis when He created humanity. This is what the Word does, and it is exactly what God does, because the Word *is* God.

For both Jews and Greeks, this understanding of "Word" as dynamic and active needed no explanation. It encompassed the full range of God's activity: creation, causation, transformation, and the giving of life. When God speaks His Word, He expresses Himself, and change happens. And this Word is God Himself.

### Application: Words Matter for Knowing God

This teaches us something vital: words are essential to knowing someone. Some people suggest that we don't need God's words—that we can sit in nature or listen to inspiring music and simply "feel" God's presence, and that His feelings will speak to us. But this misses the point. We need words to understand.

Consider how God has always worked: He didn't "feel" His way through creation—He spoke. And He speaks again for our salvation, calling us from death into eternal life. Christianity is not a religion of pictures, statues, and stained glass windows. We are a people of the Word. We believe in what God has spoken, and we proclaim that same Word to others.

This is why we emphasize reading and studying the Bible. Understanding God's words is understanding God Himself.

## 2. God Is Consistent With His Word

If God introduces Himself through His Word, then we must ask: How reliable is His introduction? After all, it's not the same as meeting face-to-face.

But look at the character of the Father revealed in these verses:

> We have seen his glory, the glory of the One and Only, who came from the Father, full of grace and truth. (John 1:14)

In Jesus, we see that God is full of grace and truth. Truth means He cannot deceive. What God says is who He is. He does not trick us. He cannot lie. There is no hint of falsehood in Him. Everything He says is true—and this includes His promises about the future.

We humans make promises that sometimes come true and sometimes don't. But with God, because everything He speaks is truth, His Word becomes reality. When He says, "Let there be light," it happens—because it is true. When He makes a promise, we can have absolute confidence it will come true. His Word is a perfect reflection of His character. It is never false, never wasted breath.

This might seem obvious, but consider how different we are. We reveal ourselves through our words, don't we? When I tell you I like dim sum, you know that about me because I told you. But here's the problem with human speech: I can lie. I could say I like chicken feet when I actually find them repulsive. My words don't always match the truth. What I say doesn't always represent who I really am. I can deceive, and I often do—sometimes without even realizing it.

We also ask people, "How are you?" without genuinely wanting to know. We respond, "I'm fine," when we're not. Our words frequently don't correspond to reality.

Moreover, human words lack power. I can say, "Let there be light," and nothing happens. I can wish to fly, and I remain grounded. My words don't change reality.

But God's Word is God's very essence—His self-expression. The Word cannot be separated from God. It's like breath and a person: when you speak and breathe, you can't separate the two. They are inseparable. Because God and His Word cannot be separated, His words truly reveal Him. They are never untrue. The Word *is* God.

### Application: Trust God's Words—Don't Separate From Them

We can trust God's words absolutely. The two cannot be separated: God and His Word are one.

Sometimes, when we're unhappy with what God says, we try to separate ourselves from His words. We might think, "This isn't really true," or "Didn't someone alter this version?" or "This is just one version among many." But if we want to be friends with God, we must be friends with His words too. We cannot have one without the other.

Just as God cannot be separated from His Word, we shouldn't be separated from our own words either. Our character should match what we say. Let our "yes" be yes, and our "no" be no. We should put off falsehood and be truthful in all our dealings.

Sometimes we promise friends to meet them at a certain place and time, or we promise our parents we'll do our chores, but then we don't. Instead of showing up where we said we'd be, we call them on our mobiles asking, "Where are you?" This is not honoring our word.

If you say you'll do the dishes, do them. Don't watch TV, procrastinate, and then leave them undone. I know we'll always fall short—we're still affected by sin—but let's try our best to be faithful to our words, just as God is faithful to His.

## 3. God's Revelation Is Complete in Jesus

> The Word became flesh and made his dwelling among us. We have seen his glory, the glory of the One and Only, who came from the Father, full of grace and truth. (John 1:14)

> John testifies concerning him. He cries out, saying, "This was he of whom I said, 'He who comes after me has surpassed me because he was before me.'" From the fullness of his grace we have all received one blessing after another. For the law was given through Moses; grace and truth came through Jesus Christ. No one has ever seen God, but God the One and Only, who is at the Father's side, has made him known. (John 1:15-18)

We've established that God reveals Himself through His Word, and that He is faithful to His Word. Now we learn something astounding: the Word became flesh.

The Word, who is God Himself, who was never flesh before, now took on flesh and "dwelt among us"—literally, pitched a tent in human form. In this way, God reveals Himself fully. And that person is Jesus.

This idea would have been shocking to many. The Greeks believed the material body was the worst state of existence. Buddhists today still aim to escape the body. Islam teaches that God's transcendence would be compromised by becoming human. After all, God is abstract, transcendent, and seems too far removed to be personal with us.

Yet here He is—God limited Himself to humanity. Instead of being free to go anywhere and do anything, He restricted Himself to one place at one time in first-century Palestine. He hungered. He got cold. He cried. He had a fully human body.

Which other god has ever done this? Which god desires to reveal Himself so fully to His people? Which god is willing to humble Himself and suffer on a cross to make Himself known?

Previously, God's self-expression came through multiple channels: creation, the law, the prophets. But none of these was a full revelation. Now, the God who stands at the Father's side has made Himself fully known. Instead of a letter, an email, or a blog post arriving, a real, living person—God Himself—has come.

Through Jesus, grace and truth are now visible to all. God has revealed Himself fully in the flesh. The glory of God is grace and truth—His generosity and His truthfulness. God has given grace upon grace, revealing Himself first through the law and prophets, and now in these last days, revealing Himself completely through Christ. God's self-expression has taken on human form.

Imagine if royalty visited your neighborhood. You'd feel special just knowing they were nearby. If you actually met them face-to-face and they greeted you, it would be extraordinary. But how much more extraordinary when the King of the universe, the Creator of all things, comes to earth and makes Himself known to us!

### Application: Why Doesn't God Show Himself Today?

A common objection people raise is: "Why doesn't God just show Himself now? If He showed up, I would believe." But God has come to earth. How can anyone say, "Why doesn't God show Himself?" He did.

Why not today? God is not our personal toy that pops into existence whenever and wherever we desire. Part of His earthly limitation was that He existed in one place at one particular time. How could we expect Him to appear repeatedly throughout history? He came once for all of mankind, once for all time. Indeed, He died once for all—a complete, final sacrifice.

Furthermore, we too are limited by time and space. We cannot physically be part of the events that happened in Israel. We can never witness them directly.

But here's the key: just because we weren't there doesn't disqualify us from believing. We rely on eyewitness testimony for everything—legal proceedings, accident investigations, historical understanding. John, the author of this Gospel, writes about these events and wants us to know them so that we can believe. As the theme of this Gospel states: "that you may believe."

God has finally revealed Himself fully in Jesus Christ. Jesus is the Word of God who creates all things. He is the Word that is utterly reliable and trustworthy. If you want to know Him more deeply, read the records left by His disciples. That is His invitation to you.
