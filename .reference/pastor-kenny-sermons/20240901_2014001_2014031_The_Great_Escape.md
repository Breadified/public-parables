# Exodus 14:1-31

**Title:** The Great Escape

**Date:** 15 September 2024

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Turning Back: The Cost of Forgetting

After ten devastating plagues, Pharaoh finally released the Israelites from slavery. But almost immediately, he regretted his decision. When he was told the people had fled, Pharaoh and his officials "changed their minds" about them and said:

> "What have we done? We have let the Israelites go and have lost their services!"

It's a dumb question when you think about it. How could he forget? His firstborn son had died. The plagues had devastated the land. Yet within moments, he forgot why he'd let them go.

This pattern is as old as human nature itself. We forget God's blessings. We forget the lessons He teaches us. In a crisis, we cry out to Him. But once the crisis passes, we forget why we needed Him in the first place. A student studies desperately before exams, but once the exams are over, the books gather dust. It's the definition of foolishness—as Proverbs says:

> As a dog returns to its vomit, so fools repeat their folly. (Proverbs 26:11)

We see this even in Scripture. The Pharisees witnessed Jesus healing a demon-possessed man who was blind and mute, restoring his ability to see and speak. The crowds were astonished, asking, "Could this be the Son of David?" But the Pharisees immediately attributed it to demonic power. Despite the miracle before their eyes, they explained it away.

Even believers fall into this trap. Peter wrote about those who escape corruption through Christ but then become entangled in sin again:

> If they have escaped the corruption of the world by knowing our Lord and Savior Jesus Christ and are again entangled in it and are overcome, they are worse off at the end than they were at the beginning. (2 Peter 2:20-22)

## The Temptation in the Wilderness

Pharaoh assembled his entire military might—six hundred of the best chariots, the most advanced military technology of Egypt, along with all his other forces. He pursued the Israelites and overtook them at the sea:

> The Egyptians—all Pharaoh's horses and chariots, horsemen and troops—pursued the Israelites and overtook them as they camped by the sea near Pi Hahiroth, opposite Baal Zephon. (Exodus 14:9)

When the Israelites saw the Egyptian army approaching, they were terrified. They cried out to the Lord, and then they turned on Moses with bitter complaints:

> Was it because there were no graves in Egypt that you brought us to the desert to die? What have you done to us by bringing us out of Egypt? Didn't we say to you in Egypt, "Leave us alone; let us serve the Egyptians"? It would have been better for us to serve the Egyptians than to die in the desert! (Exodus 14:11-12)

Their argument is absurd. Egypt is filled with graves—elaborate tombs, pyramids built as monuments to the dead. Yet they'd rather go back to slavery than trust God in the wilderness.

This reveals the profound temptation of the wilderness experience. When you leave Egypt—when you leave sin, guilt, shame, and the world's ways behind—you face a choice: Will you press forward into what God is calling you to, or will you long for the familiar bondage you've escaped?

The Israelites did indeed cry out to God, and He heard them. He knew their slavery, their oppression. He'd already proven His power through the plagues. Yet now, facing the wilderness, they wanted to go back.

This is the definition of whinging—forgetting how good you have it now and forgetting how bad it was then. We complain about our iPhones while ignoring that we're wealthier than our parents were at our age, wealthier than previous generations, wealthier than we ourselves were just years ago. Yet like the Israelites, we sometimes look back at our old life and feel nostalgia for its false comforts:

> The rabble with them began to crave other food, and again the Israelites started wailing and said, "If only we had meat to eat! We remember the fish we ate in Egypt at no cost—also the cucumbers, melons, leeks, onions and garlic." (Numbers 11:4-5)

The wilderness is what you must pass through. It's not your destination; it's the proving ground where you learn to trust God completely.

## God's Promise: Stand Still and Watch

Moses spoke to the people with words meant to strengthen their faith:

> Do not be afraid. Stand firm and you will see the deliverance the Lord will bring you today. The Egyptians you see today you will never see again. The Lord will fight for you; you need only to be still. (Exodus 14:13-14)

Then the Lord gave Moses a strange command:

> Why are you crying out to me? Tell the Israelites to move on. Raise your staff and stretch out your hand over the sea to divide the water so that the Israelites can go through the sea on dry ground. (Exodus 14:15-16)

God explained His purpose in what was about to happen:

> I will harden the hearts of the Egyptians so that they will go in after them. And I will gain glory through Pharaoh and all his army, through his chariots and his horsemen. The Egyptians will know that I am the Lord when I gain glory through Pharaoh, his chariots and his horsemen. (Exodus 14:17-18)

When God speaks of gaining glory, it might sound vain to our modern ears. But for God, this is acceptable and right. It's not about vanity—it's about honoring His own promises. Throughout Scripture, God's glory is connected to His covenant with Israel. The glory cloud that protects Israel, the glory of the Torah given at Sinai, the glory of the Lord resting on the Tabernacle—all these speak of God's special relationship with His people. God's glory is established through keeping His word and protecting His covenant nation.

## God Protects His People

Before the Egyptians could close in, God moved:

> Then the angel of God, who had been traveling in front of Israel's army, withdrew and went behind them. The pillar of cloud also moved from in front and stood behind them, coming between the armies of Egypt and Israel. Throughout the night the cloud brought darkness to the one side and light to the other side; so neither went near the other all night long. (Exodus 14:19-20)

God Himself stood as a barrier between the pursuing army and His people. Then came the great miracle:

> Then Moses stretched out his hand over the sea, and all that night the Lord drove the sea back with a strong east wind and turned it into dry land. The waters were divided, and the Israelites went through the sea on dry ground, with a wall of water on their right and on their left. (Exodus 14:21-22)

The Hebrew calls this the "Red Sea" (yam suph), though scholars debate the exact location. Some argue it should be the "Reed Sea" based on the etymology of the word. Yet every other certain reference to yam suph in Scripture points to the Red Sea or its northern extensions. The Septuagint translated it as "Red Sea," a translation the New Testament affirms in Acts and Hebrews. King Solomon built ships at Ezion Geber on the shore of the Red Sea, which would make little sense if it were a shallow reedy lake. The debate continues among scholars, but the miraculous crossing itself is what matters—whether it occurred in a deep sea or in the marshes of the Nile Delta, God parted the waters and provided a way through.

The Egyptians, hardened by God, pursued the Israelites into the sea:

> The Egyptians pursued them, and all Pharaoh's horses and chariots and horsemen followed them into the sea. During the last watch of the night the Lord looked down from the pillar of fire and cloud at the Egyptian army and threw it into confusion. He jammed the wheels of their chariots so that they had difficulty driving. And the Egyptians said, "Let's get away from the Israelites! The Lord is fighting for them against Egypt." (Exodus 14:23-25)

There's poetic justice here. Pharaoh had enslaved the Israelites, forcing them to make bricks by hardening clay. Now, on the sea bed, God reversed the process—making the clay soft so that the chariot wheels got stuck. The oppressor's own tools of control became his undoing.

Then God gave the final command:

> Stretch out your hand over the sea so that the waters may flow back over the Egyptians and their chariots and horsemen. (Exodus 14:26)

> Moses stretched out his hand over the sea, and at daybreak the sea went back to its place. The Egyptians were fleeing toward it, and the Lord swept them into the sea. The water flowed back and covered the chariots and horsemen—the entire army of Pharaoh that had followed the Israelites into the sea. Not one of them survived. (Exodus 14:27-28)

## No Turning Back

Before Pharaoh even caught up with the Israelites, God had orchestrated an escape route that would make retreat impossible:

> Tell the Israelites to turn back and encamp near Pi Hahiroth, between Migdol and the sea. They are to encamp by the sea, directly opposite Baal Zephon. Pharaoh will think, "The Israelites are wandering around the land in confusion, hemmed in by the desert." And I will harden Pharaoh's heart, and he will pursue them. (Exodus 14:2-4)

God's purpose wasn't just to rescue Israel—it was to prevent them from turning back. The Israelites were positioned between the sea and the desert, hemmed in. They couldn't return to Egypt even if they wanted to.

Earlier, God had revealed this same protective strategy:

> God did not lead them on the road through the Philistine country, though that was shorter. For God said, "If they face war, they might change their minds and return to Egypt." So God led the people around by the desert road toward the Red Sea. (Exodus 13:17-18)

God took the longer route because the shorter one would have been too tempting. When faith is weak and circumstances are hard, people look for escape routes. God removed the option.

This echoes Jesus' teaching to those considering discipleship:

> No one who puts a hand to the plow and looks back is fit for service in the kingdom of God. (Luke 9:62)

Alexander the Great understood this principle when he burned his fleet upon arriving in Persia. His vastly outnumbered army faced the greatest navy in the known world. To prevent his men from losing heart and seeking to flee, he burned the ships—the only means of escape or retreat. With no way home except through victory, his men fought with desperate determination.

God did something similar for Israel. He removed the option of turning back. The plagues had freed them. The sea crossing had sealed their fate. There was no going back to Egypt—not physically, and more importantly, not spiritually.

The result was transformation:

> But the Israelites went through the sea on dry ground, with a wall of water on their right and on their left. That day the Lord saved Israel from the hands of the Egyptians, and Israel saw the Egyptians lying dead on the shore. And when the Israelites saw the mighty hand of the Lord displayed against the Egyptians, the people feared the Lord and put their trust in him and in Moses his servant. (Exodus 14:29-31)

Witnessing God's power, they moved from terror to trust. They had no choice but to believe.

## Application: Commit Without Reserve

When God calls you out of bondage, He's serious. The wilderness may seem uncertain and frightening. You may face temptations to return to your old life. But God has already removed the bridges behind you. Your chains are broken. The only way forward is faith.

This is true whether it's a marriage commitment, a career calling, a church community, or spiritual growth. Half-heartedness won't work. You can't keep one foot in your old life while stepping into God's new calling.

Like Alexander's soldiers, we need to burn our boats—commit fully without a backup plan. Not rashly, but having counted the cost and decided that following God is worth everything.

The wilderness is what you pass through to reach the Promised Land. God stands between you and your enemies. He makes a way where there is no way. But you must commit to moving forward, trusting that His hand will protect you and His promise will sustain you.
