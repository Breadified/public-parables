# Romans 9:1-33

**Title:** The Choice of God  
**Date:** 3 February 2013  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## God Has Not Failed

Paul opens with profound emotion, revealing the deep pastoral heart beneath his theological teaching:

> I speak the truth in Christ—I am not lying, my conscience confirms it in the Holy Spirit—I have great sorrow and unceasing anguish in my heart. For I could wish that I myself were cursed and cut off from Christ for the sake of my brothers, those of my own race, the people of Israel. Theirs is the adoption as sons; theirs the divine glory, the covenants, the receiving of the law, the temple worship and the promises. Theirs are the patriarchs, and from them is traced the human ancestry of Christ, who is God over all, forever praised! Amen.

The first objection Paul addresses is devastating: Has God failed? The Jews—God's chosen people with every spiritual advantage—are rejecting Jesus en masse. Despite two thousand years of promises that Israel would be God's people forever (Exodus 6:6-8; 2 Samuel 7:22-24; Jeremiah 31:36-37), they are now refusing to follow their Messiah. How can God's word stand if His own chosen people are rejecting it?

Yet Paul's answer, surprisingly, is this:

> It is not as though God's word had failed.

The resolution lies in understanding what "all Israel" actually means. God has never promised to save every individual descended from Abraham. Even from the beginning, God made distinctions.

### The Definition of "All"

> Not all who are descended from Israel are Israel. Nor because they are his descendants are they all Abraham's children.

God's promises were never about blood relations alone—they were about the line of promise. Abraham had many descendants, but God's covenant people were defined by promise, not genealogy.

### The Example of Isaac

Consider Abraham's two sons. Abraham fathered Ishmael through Hagar, his servant, when he grew impatient waiting for God's promise. But God said to Abraham:

> It is through Isaac that your offspring will be reckoned.

Sarah later bore Isaac—the son of promise. This was never about who was born first or the legitimacy of descent. It was always about God's elective purpose. Even at the very beginning, there was a split between those included in God's purpose and those who were not.

> In other words, it is not the natural children who are God's children, but it is the children of the promise who are regarded as Abraham's offspring.

God's promise was stated before the twins were born. The covenant was always about promise, never about mere relation.

### The Example of Jacob

This pattern continued with Isaac's sons, Jacob and Esau:

> Rebekah's children had one and the same father, our father Isaac. Yet, before the twins were born or had done anything good or bad—in order that God's purpose in election might stand: not by works but by him who calls—she was told, "The older will serve the younger." Just as it is written: "Jacob I loved, but Esau I hated."

Notice what Paul emphasizes: they had the same father, they were twins (emphasizing sameness), they were chosen before they were born, and before they had done anything good or bad. This establishes that God's choice was based on His sovereign purpose, not on human merit or effort.

God has the right to choose. Throughout Scripture, this pattern repeats:

- Abraham was a selection
- Isaac was a selection  
- Jacob was a selection

So why would we expect anything different when it comes to Israel as a whole? God distinguishes individuals then, and He distinguishes now.

## God Is Just

The second objection emerges: This sounds profoundly unfair. We recoil from discrimination. Don't people have a right to be saved? How can God show partiality without being unjust?

> What then shall we say? Is God unjust? Not at all!

Paul's answer is radical. God is under no obligation to save anyone:

> I will have mercy on whom I have mercy, and I will have compassion on whom I have compassion. It does not, therefore, depend on man's desire or effort, but on God's mercy.

Salvation is not a right to be claimed—it is mercy to be received. God is no one's debtor. Because all of us deserve condemnation, any salvation at all is pure grace. God retains the absolute right to show mercy to whom He chooses.

### The Example of Pharaoh

This same principle applies to judgment. God raised up Pharaoh for a specific purpose:

> I raised you up for this very purpose, that I might display my power in you and that my name might be proclaimed in all the earth.

In the Exodus story, Pharaoh refused repeatedly to release Israel. Yet throughout this, God was working out His purposes—not just for Pharaoh's judgment, but for the deliverance of His people and the revelation of His power to all the earth. Pharaoh made his own choices, and he is justly condemned for them. But behind those choices stood God's sovereign plan.

God's justice operates on a different plane than our human sense of fairness. What we must understand is this:

**God is fair because:**

- Man's unwillingness to believe or obey is always blamed on that man, not on God. From our perspective, we are making the decision. From God's perspective, He is in control.

- God retains the right to show mercy on whom He will, in order to bring about His good purposes. It is perfectly fair for God not to save anyone. Salvation is never owed—it is always grace.

- God is deeply sorrowful at the condemnation of sinners. God does desire all to be saved, but He is committed to something even more valuable: the revelation of the full range of His glory—both in wrath and in mercy (Romans 9:22-23)—and the humbling of mankind so that we give all credit to God for our salvation.

There is something greater at stake than our individual choices: God's sovereignty and glory.

### Why This Is for Our Good

We often think freedom from God's control would be preferable. But consider what that world would look like. If God were not predestining all things, if He were not in control, we would face sheer terror. The world is already a place of randomness and disaster. Without God's sovereign hand, there would be no assurance, no purpose threading through suffering.

But we rest in this hope: Romans 8:28 promises that God works all things together for the good of those who love Him. These apparent randomness and sufferings are not actually random—in the life to come, they will all make sense and reveal their purpose. This only comes with a God who has a plan and controls that plan.

Remove God's control, and prayer becomes meaningless. God's reply would be, "I'll try, but I'm not sure if I can." Your future becomes genuinely uncertain. The devil could still win. You could be eternally lost.

A predestining God is not a tyrant—it is your security. Yes, there is the reality of His absolute authority, but consider the alternative: anarchy. At least under a sovereign God, there is law and order. What matters is being on the good side of this God.

Friends, this is the choice before us: a God who predetermines all things with all the assurance that brings, or a weakened God who cannot guarantee anything—leaving us at the mercy of chaos. A sovereign, predestining God is infinitely preferable. What's important is to get on His good side.

## "Why Blame Me?"

The third objection: If God is behind everything, why does He still hold us responsible? If God is sovereign, how can He justly blame us for sin?

> Then why does God still blame us? For who resists his will?

Paul's answer comes not with philosophical argumentation, but with a question that cuts to the heart of our presumption:

> But who are you, O man, to talk back to God? Shall what is formed say to him who formed it, "Why did you make me like this?" Does not the potter have the right to make out of the same lump of clay some pottery for noble purposes and some for common use?

God is the Potter. We are the clay. This is the fundamental reality we must accept: God is God, and you are not.

A potter shapes clay according to his design. Some vessels are made for honor, others for common use. God has made some people as objects of His mercy and others to display His justice. And He has done this for the benefit of those whom He has chosen for mercy:

> What if God, choosing to show his wrath and make his power known, bore with great patience the objects of his wrath—prepared for destruction? What if he did this to make the riches of his glory known to the objects of his mercy, whom he prepared in advance for glory—even us, whom he also called, not only from the Jews but also from the Gentiles?

The specific example is the hardening of Israel's heart—which ultimately opened the door for the Gentiles to enter God's family. God's election of some and rejection of others serves a cosmic purpose far larger than any individual's complaint about fairness.

> I will call them "my people" who are not my people; and I will call her "my loved one" who is not my loved one. It will happen that in the very place where it was said to them, "You are not my people," they will be called "sons of the living God."

This is God's poetry spanning thousands of generations. The arc of salvation history is a great love story, and predestination is woven through it.

### The Great Benefit of Predestination

Understanding predestination brings us immense comfort:

- **Predestination reveals the extent of God's love.** Before the foundation of the world, God chose us to be His. This is not a last-minute rescue but the eternal expression of His love (Ephesians 1:4-5).

- **God has always acted for our good.** Even in the midst of suffering, those whom God has called can trust that He works all things together for good (Romans 8:28). Nothing escapes His notice or care.

- **We can pray with confidence.** We know that God is fully able to accomplish His will. Prayer is not a desperate attempt to convince an indifferent deity—it is communion with a God who has already purposed our good.

### Freedom and Love, Not Robotics

Some object: "But this makes us robots, stripped of genuine freedom." The concern is understandable but misplaced.

Both humans and robots are creatures subject to their Creator. Both are the work of the Creator who determines what He will do with them. If humans can create AI robots that learn and make decisions through complex logic, why cannot God create beings who are so perfectly crafted that they feel completely free in their choices—because their desires align with His will—yet are also completely subject to His sovereignty?

The real issue is not that we are created, but that we want to be free *from* the Creator. We demand autonomy even though we accept our limitations everywhere else: we cannot fly unaided, we cannot think cancer away, we cannot change our genes. Yet we insist we must be free from God. The inconsistency reveals that our objection is not intellectual—it is moral. We want to be God. We want moral relativism. We want to answer to no one but ourselves.

But for our daily purposes, in the realm where we live, we do not experience being controlled or manipulated. Our decisions feel real. God calls us to make the right decisions based on His revealed will. That call is genuine. Your choices are real. Your accountability is real. From your perspective, you are freely choosing.

The question God asks is simply this: Will you choose to be on His side?
