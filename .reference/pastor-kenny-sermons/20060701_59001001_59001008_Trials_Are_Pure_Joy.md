# James 1:1-8

**Title:** Trials Are Pure Joy

**Date:** 2 July 2006

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## How We View Trials

When James calls us to consider trials "pure joy," it sounds shocking—even cruel, as if God were a distant figure callously playing with our lives. But James himself clarifies who God is just a few verses later:

> Every good and perfect gift is from above, coming down from the Father of the heavenly lights, who does not change like shifting shadows. (James 1:17)

God is our Father who gives good gifts. He is faithful and unchanging—completely reliable.

This raises the ancient question: How can a good God and human suffering coexist? Some argue this contradiction proves God doesn't exist, or that He must be weak or indifferent. Yet the logic fails. A loving parent disciplines and allows hardship for their child's good. Similarly, God may permit trials for a greater purpose—our ultimate good.

Trials are not a cruel test designed to make us fail. Rather, they're like an exam that measures what we *do* know rather than what we don't. They consolidate our faith. Through trials, we experience where we truly stand with God and remind ourselves: *God loves us. We can depend on Him because of Jesus. A better life awaits us.*

So when James says "rejoice," he doesn't mean we should pretend suffering doesn't hurt. We must be honest and call suffering what it is. Rather, we rejoice knowing these trials are reminders of God's faithfulness and prompts to look forward to our hope in Him.

## Trials Produce Perseverance

> Consider it pure joy, my brothers, whenever you face trials of many kinds, because you know that the testing of your faith develops perseverance. (James 1:2-3)

What is this perseverance James speaks of? There are two types. The first is athletic—building endurance through training, getting stronger and more powerful. The second is patience—doing the same thing faithfully, day after day, without necessarily getting visibly stronger.

James is describing the second kind. It's like walking up an escalator that's moving downward. You never really arrive; you never get medals for the effort. But you keep going anyway—one foot in front of the other, day in and day out.

This is perseverance in Scripture: long-term, often invisible, ordinary. Consider Job, or the farmers waiting for autumn and spring rains (James 5:7-8). Consider the prophets who suffered but remained faithful (James 5:10). Consider our own waiting for heaven (James 1:12).

God is quietly changing you, keeping you steady on that descending escalator. You may not see dramatic progress now. As Martin Luther observed, the world seems to push us back constantly so we make no visible headway—except we're not actually going backward. This counteracts false expectations. Not every prayer is answered with immediate success. Not everything can be "named and claimed." But we can wait without panicking or becoming too afraid. We trust that good is coming.

**Waiting means not sinning.** The danger when trials come is that we give in to despair, fear, or bitterness. But all we're called to do is wait—and that's not much. A mosquito itch will fade. Food poisoning will pass. A teenager's problems will change once they leave school. A fever will break. Sometimes we don't have to change; the circumstances do.

**For longer trials, take steps to alleviate them if you can.** But some things we cannot fix. And that's okay. We have one great goal that transcends all earthly concerns: heaven. In light of that eternal hope, we can endure present struggles.

## Completeness Is the Goal

> Perseverance must finish its work so that you may be mature and complete, not lacking anything. (James 1:4)

The aim of trials is completeness—maturity, wholeness, wisdom, godliness. It means lacking nothing. It looks like Jesus. What God begins, He finishes.

Now we are incomplete, immature, struggling with the waiting. Then, we will be complete, with minds thoroughly obedient to God. Trials and testing ultimately bring us to this completeness.

If you lack wisdom, ask God—He gives generously without finding fault (James 1:5). But understand what you're asking for. If you want maturity, you cannot simultaneously ask God to make you humble and then choose pride. You cannot ask for godliness while living immaturely. The process cannot be stopped midway.

> When he asks, he must believe and not doubt, because he who doubts is like a wave of the sea, blown and tossed by the wind. That man should not think he will receive anything from the Lord; he is a double-minded man, unstable in all he does. (James 1:6-8)

## The Motivation: Life Beyond Trials

The real question is not about trials themselves, but what lies on the other side of them. We live in light of the end.

There are many aims in life that seem important now. But consider: the best musician in this world may play something entirely different in heaven. The greatest artist's masterpiece will burn up. What will matter is whether we were faithful, whether we waited, whether we trusted our loving Father.

**Our response to trials reveals how much we live for today versus tomorrow.** It's an opportunity to repent, to turn again toward the living God.

So let us wait patiently. Let us pray, asking for wisdom. Let us expect suffering—but remember we have a Father who loves us. Rejoice, for by these trials He is making us complete.

And let us encourage one another in this journey. If you're struggling and feel alone, please know that we are here for you. That's why we gather together—to share our burdens, to wait together, to walk the escalator together.
