# 1 Corinthians 11:1-34

**Title:** Head Coverings and Order in Male-Female Relationships
**Speaker:** Claire Smith
**Date:** December 1, 2003

## Introduction

Head coverings have occupied an unusual place in recent news—from the visibility of Muslim headscarves following September 11 to the fashion dilemma at the Queen Mother's funeral, where the palace felt compelled to announce that women no longer *had* to wear hats to Royal church services. Yet for most of us, these cultural practices seem foreign and distant.

So what does 1 Corinthians 11 mean for modern Christians? Does it hold any relevance for us today? This passage deserves careful study, not dismissal.

## The Passage

> Now I commend you because you remember me in everything and maintain the traditions even as I delivered them to you. But I want you to understand that the head of every man is Christ, the head of a wife is her husband, and the head of Christ is God. Every man who prays or prophesies with his head covered dishonours his head, but every wife who prays or prophesies with her head uncovered dishonours her head—it is the same as if her head were shaven. For if a wife will not cover her head, then she should cut her hair short. But since it is disgraceful for a wife to cut off her hair or shave her head, let her cover her head. For a man ought not to cover his head, since he is the image and glory of God, but woman is the glory of man. For man was not made from woman, but woman from man. Neither was man created for woman, but woman for man. That is why a wife ought to have a symbol of authority on her head, because of the angels. Nevertheless, in the Lord woman is not independent of man nor man of woman; for as woman was made from man, so man is now born of woman. And all things are from God. Judge for yourselves: is it proper for a wife to pray to God with her head uncovered? Does not nature itself teach you that if a man wears long hair it is a disgrace for him, but if a woman has long hair, it is her glory? For her hair is given to her for a covering. If anyone is inclined to be contentious, we have no such practice, nor do the churches of God. (1 Corinthians 11:2-16, ESV)

## Context: Comparing 1 Corinthians 11 with 1 Timothy 2

This passage is one of six New Testament texts addressing male-female relationships in family and church (others include 1 Timothy 2, 1 Corinthians 14, Ephesians 5, 1 Peter 3, Colossians 3, and Titus 2). In our post-feminist age, these passages strike many as confronting and counter-cultural, yet they deserve careful study and application.

### Why 1 Corinthians 11 Is More Difficult

This passage is more than twice as long as 1 Timothy 2:9-15, with a more complex argument. Additionally, the cultural context is far removed from our own—nearly twenty centuries separate us, and practices like prophecy and head coverings are unfamiliar to modern experience.

### Key Similarities with 1 Timothy 2

- Paul authored both
- Both address what women wear and their hair
- Both reference childbirth and procreation
- Both emphasize decency and propriety
- Both expect women to participate actively in church gatherings
- Both teach that gender partly determines participation in church
- Both reference Adam and Eve and the creation order (man first, woman second)

### The Key Difference

The most significant difference: 1 Timothy 2 commands women's silence, while 1 Corinthians 11 *expects* women to pray and prophesy in church. However, closer reading reveals these aren't contradictory. 1 Timothy restricts women from teaching or exercising authority over men—it doesn't forbid prayer or prophecy. Each passage addresses different situations within the broader principle.

## The Crucial Interpretive Challenge: Verse 3

Verse 3 is Paul's starting point for the entire passage. Understanding this verse unlocks the rest.

> "The head of every man is Christ, the head of a wife is her husband, and the head of Christ is God."

The meaning of three words is contested: What does "head" mean? Should we read "man/woman" or "husband/wife"? What are the implications?

### Question 1: What Does "Head" Mean?

**The Modern Argument:** Some scholars argue "head" means "source" rather than "authority over," using the analogy of a river's source. Under this reading, the passages say nothing more significant than "woman came from man's rib"—merely about origins, not authority or order.

**Why This Interpretation Fails:**

*First*, extensive study of the word "head" (*kephalē*) in ancient Greek literature shows it typically means "authority over," not "source."

*Second*, Paul himself uses "head" consistently with the sense of authority (Ephesians 5:23; 1:22; 4:15; Colossians 1:18; 2:19), even when origins are tangentially involved.

*Third*, substituting "source" for "head" creates theological chaos. Verse 3 would then read: "The source of every man is Christ, the source of woman is man, and the source of Christ is God." This implies Christ was created (not eternally begotten), man was made from Christ (not dust), and leads directly to Arianism—the heresy that Christ was created rather than pre-existent. These conclusions are wildly unbiblical.

**Conclusion:** Paul uses "head" to denote order and authority in relationships.

### The Trinitarian Implications

This verse reveals something profound about God himself: there is order within the Godhead. While the Father is fully God, the Son is fully God, and the Spirit is fully God, this equality of being doesn't preclude differentiation and hierarchy in relationship.

Modern feminism often assumes equality requires identical roles and functions. But this is false. You can have differentiation and hierarchy in relationships without any inferiority of dignity, worth, or value. All three persons of the Trinity share fully in the divine nature, yet there exists an asymmetry in their relationships. Jesus, fully God, willingly submits to the Father's headship in his incarnate work and returns glory to the Father in all he does. This same principle—sameness with hierarchy—governs human male-female relationships.

### Question 2: "Man/Woman" or "Husband/Wife"?

Here lies another interpretive puzzle. Different Bible translations vary:
- Some render it: "the head of every man is Christ... the head of the *woman* is *man*"
- Others: "the head of the *wife* is the *husband*"

This matters because it determines scope: Is Paul addressing married couples specifically, or men and women generally?

**Clues from Context:**

*First*, Paul wouldn't suddenly shift the meaning of the same Greek word within the space of three phrases (man → husband → man again). The inconsistency would be jarring and unexplained.

*Second*, verses 7-9 (about human origins) and verses 14-15 (about hairstyles) only make sense if applied to all men and women, not merely husbands and wives.

**Conclusion:** Paul is addressing the male-female relationship generally, not just marriage.

### What "The Head of Woman Is Man" Means Today

This verse does *not* say all women must submit to all men. It addresses "headship" as a relational principle.

It does *not* diminish women's dignity, intelligence, worth, or purpose. Just as Jesus Christ isn't diminished in divinity and glory because his head is God, neither are women diminished by male headship.

But it does affirm that male-female relationships operate within an order. If verse 3 applies universally (not just to married couples), then the head of every woman is man. But which man? A plain reading suggests every man in relation to women—a principle that becomes *actual headship* in specific contexts (church leadership, eldership, marriage).

**The practical scope of headship in Scripture:**
- In church gatherings (1 Corinthians 11): different expressions in prayer/prophecy
- In evaluating prophecy (1 Corinthians 14): women don't evaluate
- In teaching (1 Timothy 2): women don't teach or exercise authority over men
- In marriage (Ephesians 5): wives submit; husbands love

Beyond these specific contexts, the New Testament is largely silent. We must work with what Scripture explicitly addresses, not speculate about implications it doesn't state.

## Understanding the Cultural Elements

Before applying verse 3 to the head covering issue, we must understand what first-century prophecy and head coverings meant.

### Prophecy in the New Testament

Confusion about prophecy rivals debate about "head." Even the first century couldn't fully agree on its definition. Here's a broad working definition:

**Authority Differences from the Old Testament:**
- Old Testament: Disobeying a prophet equaled disobeying God (severe consequences)
- New Testament: Prophecies were *weighed* (1 Corinthians 14; 1 Thessalonians 5); rejection didn't endanger the prophet

This diminished authority explains why women prophesied in 1 Corinthians 11 but couldn't weigh prophecies (1 Corinthians 14) or teach with authority (1 Timothy 2).

**Defining Characteristics:**
- Public and verbal
- Encouraging and pointed toward gospel truth (1 Corinthians 14:24)
- Available to both men and women (unlike teaching roles)
- Under the speaker's control (not ecstatic or involuntary)

**Does It Exist Today?**

Yes. Consider a missionary doctor speaking to her congregation about her orphanage work in Eastern Europe—ten minutes of public testimony about God's faithfulness and suffering for the gospel. Though not traditionally labeled "prophecy," it fits this broad definition perfectly. Such contributions, without replacing the sermon, edify the church significantly.

### Head Coverings: Meaning Over Form

The real issue isn't *what* the head covering was, but *why* Paul wanted women to wear it and men not to.

**Possibilities for the covering:**
1. A garment (veil, shawl, or cloak)
2. An attitude of mind
3. A particular hairstyle

Option 1 (a garment) makes most sense. An "attitude" doesn't fit Paul's emphasis on something visibly recognizable. A hairstyle doesn't explain verses 5-6, which suggest a woman could have her head uncovered yet still have her hair intact.

**What did it signify?**

For Paul and the Corinthians, the head covering was a meaningful expression of ordered relationships and authority. It functioned like the nursing caps and veils of the author's training—each stripe, each level of lace indicated one's place in the hierarchy. It was clothing with recognized meaning, communicating something about who wore it and their role.

## The Reasons: Why Paul Required This Distinction

### 1. The Godhead Reflects Order

The fundamental reason: the head covering represented the ordered male-female relationships that reflect the Trinity itself. Paul wanted men and women to *look* different because in appearing different, they visibly demonstrated the relational order God created—an order that mirrors God's own nature.

When men covered their heads during prayer and prophecy, they denied their male responsibility by dressing as women, thus dishonoring their head (both literal and Christ himself). When women failed to cover their heads—perhaps overextending their newfound freedom in Christ—they shamed both their literal head and their figurative head, the man. The comparison is stark: her shame equaled appearing as if her head were shaved.

### 2. Creation Order (Genesis 1-2)

Verses 7-9 ground the teaching in creation itself:

> "For a man ought not to cover his head, since he is the image and glory of God; but woman is the glory of man. For man was not made from woman, but woman from man; neither was man created for woman, but woman was created for man."

**A crucial clarification:** Does Paul say woman isn't made in God's image? *No.* If he meant that, he would have written "man is the image and glory of God; woman is *the* image and glory of man"—but he didn't. By omitting "image" in the second phrase, Paul focuses on "glory," not image-bearing. He assumes the obvious answer (that woman is made in God's image) and emphasizes instead woman's relationship to man.

Woman is man's glory because:
- She was taken from him (her origin)
- She was made for him (her purpose)

Man was firstborn with corresponding responsibilities. Woman was made from his bone and flesh—a closer relationship than creation from dust—and existed for his sake, to help him in his task.

**Important context:** Paul draws this from Genesis before the Fall. The order in male-female relationships reflects God's *original* creation design, not a consequence of sin (Genesis 3). Therefore, it's not a first-century cultural oddity to discard but an enduring created reality.

**The balance:** Verses 11-12 immediately follow to prevent inflated male pride. Men and women need each other. Woman comes from man, but man is born of woman. Woman was created for man's sake, but without her he cannot fulfill creation's mandate. Both are gifts from God to each other—a reciprocal dependence rooted in mutual need.

### 3. Authority and Dignity (Angels)

Verse 10 is notoriously difficult, but the meaning likely this:

The head covering functioned as a *sign of authority* on the woman's head—not her own authority, but the man's authority she accepts. Yet Paul adds a surprising dimension: angels.

In 1 Corinthians 6, Paul reminded the Corinthians they'll judge angels one day. In 1 Corinthians 4, he indicates angels witness earthly events. By accepting her place within God's order through the head covering, the woman demonstrated her own dignity and authority as one made in God's image who will, alongside man, judge the angels.

The head covering thus represents *both* the man's authority *and* the woman's dignity—bridging verses 7-9 (male-female order) and verses 11-12 (reciprocal dependence).

### 4. Nature Itself

Verse 14 appeals to nature as a teacher:

> "Does not nature itself teach you that if a man wears long hair it is a disgrace for him, but if a woman has long hair, it is her glory?"

Paul isn't merely appealing to social custom ("that's just how we do things here"). Elsewhere, his use of "nature" (Romans 1:26; 2:14) refers to innate, God-given moral intuition—not culture. His point: people instinctively recognize male-female differences and their created foundation. God's providential design of our inner sense of right and wrong aligns with his intentions for us.

### 5. Apostolic Tradition

Verse 2 commends the Corinthians for maintaining "traditions" Paul delivered. In New Testament usage, "tradition" (*paradosis*) is nearly technical terminology for the body of Christian teaching—the apostolic gospel itself, not cultural customs like Easter eggs or Mother's Day.

These traditions were commands to obey, defining genuine Christianity. Paul isn't making a casual suggestion; he's invoking apostolic authority.

### 6. Universal Church Practice

Verse 16 insists on uniformity:

> "If anyone is inclined to be contentious, we have no such practice, nor do the churches of God."

Paul isn't asking Corinth to do something unique. He's demanding they remain in fellowship with all God's churches by maintaining visible distinctions between men and women. The stakes are high: conformity to universal church practice matters deeply.

## Application for Today

### The Challenge of Cultural Equivalence

The author has visited only one church practicing what Paul describes—a gathering in southern England where all women wore headscarves. It was a memorable, joyful experience. Were these women right? Does the text require literal head coverings?

**If your conscience leads you to cover your head, do so.** You won't disobey God or sin against conscience by covering.

**However, literal replication is difficult:**
- We don't know the original covering's exact form
- Modern head coverings carry different meanings: sun-smartness, fashion, propriety—not acceptance of God's relational order
- Muslim headscarves, while similar in appearance, often communicate subservience and inequality rather than equality-with-difference

### A Principle Rather Than a Practice

**The principle:** Women should dress in ways that are culturally and distinctively feminine, visibly accepting that God made them women and ordaining their place in relational order.

This requires becoming students of our culture. What does our society recognize as distinctively female? Dresses? Makeup? Jewelry? Styled, long hair? Our culture is eclectic—no single garment conveys it all. Instead, a *range* of feminine clothing, hairstyles, and demeanor is widely recognized as belonging to women.

**Practically:**
- Dress distinctively as a woman
- No unisex clothing
- No butch haircuts
- No "one of the boys" demeanor

When we gather to praise God, we accept his design and express it visibly, especially in prayer and prophecy. We demonstrate submission to God's order through appearance and bearing, just as men do through their clothing and relational expression.

**The tension is real.** Women educated to reject the notion that men and women differ will find this teaching difficult. Yet God's Word declares these distinctions matter to him. The twenty-first century differs from the first, but human nature doesn't. God himself doesn't change. This teaching remains relevant and applicable today.
