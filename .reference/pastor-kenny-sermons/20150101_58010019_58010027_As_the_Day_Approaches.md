## Heaven in Australia

On Australia Day 1788, the first fleet arrived at Sydney Cove. A week later, on Sunday, 3 February, the colony's chaplain, Reverend Richard Johnson, preached the first sermon on Australian soil beneath a great tree near the corner of Bligh and Hunter Streets.

We don't know what he said, but we know the reading. From Psalm 116:12-13:

> What shall I render unto the Lord for all his benefits towards me? I will lift up the cup of salvation and call on the name of the Lord.

This passage was likely chosen from the standardized lectionary used throughout England. But it's profoundly meaningful: God had brought the first fleet on an eight-month journey, and here they stood—grateful and resolved to serve Him in this new land, to call upon His name in this new place.

Fast forward 228 years, and Australia has become a land of wealth, health, and prosperity. We enjoy beautiful weather, pristine beaches, spectacular mountains, and abundant food. As migrants and as Australians, we benefit from this heaven on earth.

Yet the Letter to Hebrews reveals something far greater: a hint of what true heaven—the Day—will be like, and how that future transforms our present behavior.

## Two Truths About Heaven

The text presents two central realities about heaven:

1. Heaven is friendship with God
2. Heaven is friendship with each other

### 1. Heaven is Friendship with God

**Access Through Christ**

> Therefore, brothers and sisters, since we have confidence to enter the Most Holy Place by the blood of Jesus, by a new and living way opened for us through the curtain, that is, his body, and since we have a great priest over the house of God... (Hebrews 10:19-21)

The Most Holy Place was the innermost sanctuary of the Jewish temple—a space where only the high priest could enter once a year. It was separated by a thick curtain from the outer courts. God's presence dwelled there, making it the holiest space on earth.

To understand its significance, imagine your house with different levels of access: the downstairs open to everyone, upstairs reserved for family and close friends, but your parents' room—the master bedroom with passports and valuables—accessible only to them. That sacred space held the tablets of the Ten Commandments, the relics of Israel's history, and the Ark of the Covenant, where God was said to dwell.

When Solomon's temple was first dedicated:

> When the priests withdrew from the Holy Place, the cloud filled the temple of the Lord. And the priests could not perform their service because of the cloud, for the glory of the Lord filled his temple. (1 Kings 8:10-11)

**The Problem of Sin**

Throughout history, God walked with His people: in the Garden with Adam and Eve, in the tabernacle during Moses' time, in Solomon's temple. But sin separated them. Before the Babylonian destruction of the temple, God withdrew. Yet in Jesus, God appeared in flesh again. Now, though Jesus has ascended to heaven, He promises we will be together once more.

There's one problem: sin. Just as the Most Holy Place was restricted, heaven is restricted. Sin separates us from God's holiness, and there's no way to divide the sin from the sinner. It's like trying to cut the burned carbon from barbecued meat—you end up with nothing left.

**The Solution: Jesus' Sacrifice**

Jesus provides the solution through His blood. He dies so we don't have to face eternal death. His blood is shed, His body crucified, so the sinner can be saved. He takes the penalty of sin upon Himself. It's as if we keep the damaged goods, but someone else bears the consequences.

This is how heaven is secured for us.

**What Heaven Will Be Like**

> Then I saw a new heaven and a new earth... I saw the Holy City, the new Jerusalem, coming down out of heaven from God, prepared as a bride beautifully dressed for her husband. And I heard a loud voice from the throne saying, "Look! God's dwelling place is now among the people, and he will dwell with them. They will be his people, and God himself will be with them and be their God. He will wipe every tear from their eyes. There will be no more death or mourning or crying or pain, for the old order of things has passed away." (Revelation 21:1-4)

Heaven won't be a distant corridor to God. It will be an entire city, greater than anything we know. We will be in the same room as God—able to talk with Him, be with Him, see Him face to face. All our unanswered questions—why suffering, why did this happen—will finally be answered. It will be safe, without death, crying, or pain.

**Heaven as Hope in Suffering**

Karl Marx claimed religion was invented to oppress the poor, promising "pie in the sky" to keep them compliant. But faith in heaven is not delusion—it's real hope, because the God who promised it is faithful and certain.

Critics argue heaven is merely a crutch for those who can't handle life. Yet the data tells a different story. Nations with high atheism show dramatically higher suicide rates than religious nations. When people lose hope for eternity, they lose hope for life itself.

The American Journal of Psychiatry reported that religiously unaffiliated people had more lifetime suicide attempts, fewer reasons for living, less moral objection to suicide, and more impulsivity and aggression. If atheism is true—that we're alone in the universe, our prayers go unanswered, humanity is random, we have no dignity, death is final, and our suffering is pointless—that is utterly tragic news.

But Christians have a real crutch! There truly is a heaven promised to us. Our hope is certain and sure because God who promised it is certain and sure. This makes us more optimistic, more resilient in suffering, more able to endure. When depressed or suicidal, it's not the end—there's another world coming. Life is tolerable as we wait patiently. We have friends.

**Live Accordingly: Don't Sin**

> If we deliberately keep on sinning after we have received the knowledge of the truth, no sacrifice for sins is left, but only a fearful expectation of judgment and of raging fire that will consume the enemies of God. (Hebrews 10:26-27)

In light of who we'll be with, don't sin. God is holy. Heaven is holy. Sinning makes no sense when you understand what's coming.

Think of it this way: you're about to enter a seafood buffet, but you spend the hour before carbo-loading with junk food. You're about to ski in the Alps, but you purposely break your legs beforehand. You're about to watch the film of the year, but you read spoilers online. That's the absurdity of sinning when you know heaven awaits.

Interestingly, our visions of heaven shape our behavior. The 9/11 terrorists believed in a heaven with 72 virgins, yet in the months before their attack, they drank, watched pornography, and visited strip clubs. If their heaven was filled with what they desired, why not wait for it? Because we don't do what contradicts our vision of heaven—we do what conforms to it.

Different visions of heaven create different kinds of people:
- If heaven doesn't exist, we become apathetic
- If heaven is about sensuality, we become sexualized and exploitative
- If heaven is holy, we pursue holiness

Jesus makes clear: there will be no marriage—no sex—in heaven. But something better awaits: deep, personal friendship with God.

If your vision is a holy place with no sin, then live that way now. Yes, it's difficult—the constant tension between being godly, missing out on experiences, vigilance against temptation, the feeling that you always have to be the sensible one, the loss of certain freedoms. It would be easier to give up. But it's only a little while longer.

There is a holy entrance, and we will enter it. God will be there. Sin will be gone. We'll have new bodies that won't be weak. While destruction falls on the evil world, we'll be safe. But we need each other to persevere.

### 2. Heaven is Friendship with Each Other

> And let us consider how we may spur one another on toward love and good deeds, not giving up meeting together, as some are in the habit of doing, but encouraging one another—and all the more as you see the Day approaching. (Hebrews 10:24-25)

Notice the repetition: one another, one another, one another. Heaven is not just a place to meet God—it's a place to meet each other.

In Revelation's vision, heaven is described as a grand city where God dwells among His people. Imagine walking through a corridor and emerging into a massive stadium filled with all your friends and loved ones. It's like coming home from overseas, walking through the airport corridor into a sea of smiling faces—your friends, your relatives. It's like a surprise party where your favorite people are all there.

Summer gives us a taste: we gather with friends for movies, beaches, rock climbing, holidays, sightseeing. We enjoy good, clean fun together. But summer ends. School starts, work resumes, friends move to different cities or overseas.

In heaven, friendship is eternal.

**Why Not Give Up Meeting?**

Because we need each other now, as we prepare. The mark of God's people is love and good deeds. The early church demonstrated this beautifully: they loved one another, pooled their resources, cared for widows and orphans, and met regularly. How can we keep each other accountable to sin if we never see one another? How can we encourage if we never speak?

Church is what distinguishes Christians from other religions. We don't merely attend a building as individuals. We sing, pray, eat, and play together as spiritual brothers and sisters, stressing our togetherness as a family. Visitors from other faiths are often surprised—they expected a temple or mosque, not a community.

**The Challenge for This Year**

> Do not give up meeting together, as some are in the habit of doing, but encouraging one another—and all the more as you see the Day approaching. (Hebrews 10:25)

As we look ahead, life will bring assignments, birthday parties, exams, holidays, emergencies, and important things. Some obstacles can't be avoided. But be careful of habits. Did skipping church for assignments become a habit last year? Did birthday parties? Did watching movies late into Saturday night?

Use this new year to break bad habits and pursue a different one: the habit of meeting together.

Christianity is a team sport. The more we're accountable to each other, the more transparent we allow ourselves to be, the better we'll all live the holy life. Welcome and encourage one another. See these as priorities.

Will you set a resolution for this year? Commit to church and to serving each other and your wider community?

## Australia in Heaven

Our vision of the future shapes our present.

Reverend Richard Johnson wasn't sent to Australia by the government. He was sent by the Eclectic Society—a group of evangelical clergy and laymen dedicated to missions and prison reform, including ex-slave trader John Newton (who wrote "Amazing Grace") and abolitionist politician William Wilberforce. Through their friendship with the Prime Minister, they intentionally placed an evangelical Christian as chaplain to the colony.

These men had a vision for Christianity that extended beyond London, beyond England itself. They saw heaven as a gathering that included convicts, the forgotten, the overlooked.

Before Johnson sailed, John Newton wrote a hymn for him:

> The Lord who sends thee hence...
> Go bear the Saviour's Name to lands unknown.

Johnson, only thirty years old, accepted the task. He immediately found a wife, Mary, and a month after marriage, they sailed to Australia with gospel tracts and Bibles, seeing heaven as a gathering place for this new colony.

**Johnson's Task Continues**

Johnson's vision remains unfinished, but ours is even greater. We don't just envision heaven in Australia—we envision Australia in heaven. We have our own "Operation Boomerang": bringing all Australians—whether Asian, white, or black—to a saving knowledge of Jesus Christ.

This land was proclaimed for Christ before it was claimed for the King of England.

As we remember this Australia Day, let Psalm 116:12 be our prayer:

> What shall I render unto the Lord for all his benefits towards me? I will lift up the cup of salvation and call on the name of the Lord.

## The Power of Future Hope

Our thinking today is profoundly shaped by what we believe about tomorrow. Share prices move trillions daily based on anticipated futures. Nations plan their economies on projected oil reserves. We book trips overseas, exchange currency, and change our lives based on a hypothetical future—an uncertain hope—and yet it powerfully shapes our present reality.

How much more should the certain hope of heaven reshape how we live today?

If the future is annihilation, there is no reason for significant hope. We're destined to live briefly like grass and perish, with all our memories, hopes, and labor ending in meaninglessness.

But we believe differently. The future affects us:

- **Optimism**: We invest in friendships and relationships that matter eternally
- **Resilience**: We endure suffering, knowing it's temporary and purposeful
- **Purpose**: We live with direction, knowing our choices have eternal weight

This is why we gather. This is why we persevere. This is why we don't give up meeting together.
