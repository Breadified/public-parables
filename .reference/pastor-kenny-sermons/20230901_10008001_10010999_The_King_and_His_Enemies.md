# 2 Samuel 8-10

**Title:** The King and His Enemies  
**Date:** 17 September 2023  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## 1. Destroying His Enemies (2 Samuel 8)

David has reached the height of his power and authority. As the text reveals, the Lord gives David victory everywhere he goes—a promise being fulfilled from God's covenant with Abraham to expand Israel's borders from Egypt to the Euphrates.

### The Scope of David's Conquest

David conquers enemies in all directions:

- **West (Philistines):** He defeats them and takes control of Metheg Ammah
- **South (Moab and Edom):** He defeats the Moabites and eighteen thousand Edomites in the Valley of Salt
- **North (Aram/Syria):** He defeats Hadadezer and the Arameans of Damascus at the Euphrates River

David's victories are comprehensive and decisive. He establishes garrisons throughout these territories, and all these nations become subject to him, bringing tribute. His cabinet is established with capable leaders managing military, administrative, and religious affairs.

### A Difficult Reality: God's Judgment Against Enemies

As we read through David's conquests, we might be surprised at the ruthlessness of his victories. When David defeats the Moabites, he measures them with a cord and executes two-thirds of them, sparing only one-third. Jewish tradition suggests the king of Moab had killed David's parents, but more fundamentally, this reveals how judgment has always worked: enemies are treated violently in warfare.

This applies spiritually as well. We in Australia, removed from war by both time and distance, can forget that the world is violent and hostile. We sit comfortably and moralize about geopolitics from our armchairs, yet we forget a critical truth: **we are God's enemies.**

### Understanding Our Position as God's Enemies

God is the Lord of Hosts—the Lord of armies. Our sin is equivalent to a coup against His government. Our sin killed His Son. His judgment against us would be harsh, involving pain and torture. Scripture speaks clearly of this fate:

> The angels will come and separate the wicked from the righteous and throw them into the blazing furnace, where there will be weeping and gnashing of teeth. (Matthew 13:49-50)

This is the gravity of our situation before God. We sometimes forget this reality because we live in comfortable circumstances.

### Why Church Matters

As we celebrate our church anniversary, we might think of many good reasons to gather: community, friendship, wisdom for living, mutual support during life's challenges. These are all true and valuable. But we can forget the most fundamental reason: **church exists for those who have been saved to find a solution to judgment.**

Understanding judgment is the first derivative—the foundational truth—that determines everything else in the gospel. When someone asks whether one can be a Christian without believing the "supernatural stuff," they've missed the whole point. It's like wanting to eat only the sprinkles from ice cream and leaving the dessert itself. The central reality of Christianity is dealing with God's judgment and finding salvation through Christ.

## 2. Kindness to an Enemy (2 Samuel 9)

### The Hebrew Word: Hesed

It's only when we understand judgment that we can truly grasp God's kindness toward His enemies.

In the midst of David's military victories and judgment against his foes, we see something remarkable: David shows kindness to one enemy he will not destroy. The Hebrew word used is **hesed**—often translated as lovingkindness, steadfast love, mercy, or grace. It means far more than mere niceness.

> The Lord, the Lord, the compassionate and gracious God, slow to anger, abounding in love (hesed) and faithfulness. (Exodus 34:6)

Hesed is an act of compassion bestowed by someone in authority on a person or group they wish to encourage and care for. Interestingly, the word "kind" itself comes from "kin"—family. So **kindness means treating a stranger or enemy like a relative, making them part of your family.**

### David's Act of Kindness

David asks if there is anyone left from Saul's house to whom he can show kindness—specifically for Jonathan's sake. He learns that Mephibosheth, Jonathan's son, still lives. Mephibosheth is lame in both feet, crippled and vulnerable.

David brings him from hiding and makes a stunning declaration:

> "Don't be afraid. I will surely show you kindness for the sake of your father Jonathan. I will restore to you all the land that belonged to your grandfather Saul, and you will always eat at my table." (2 Samuel 9:7)

This is adoption. Mephibosheth, from the enemy family, becomes one of David's sons. He eats at the king's table like one of David's own children. He is given back the family wealth and has servants assigned to maintain it.

### Why This Kindness?

Mephibosheth himself asks the question:

> "What is your servant, that you should notice a dead dog like me?" (2 Samuel 9:8)

A "dead dog" was a person condemned, a threat to be eliminated. Why would David show kindness? **Because David had made a promise.**

Years earlier, when Saul pursued David, David had the opportunity to kill Saul but refused. Saul acknowledged David's righteousness and asked David to swear an oath:

> "Swear to me by the Lord that you will not kill off my descendants or wipe out my name from my father's family." And David gave his oath to Saul. (1 Samuel 24:20-22)

David also made a covenant with Jonathan himself:

> "Show me unfailing kindness like the Lord's kindness as long as I live, so that I may not be killed, and do not ever cut off your kindness from my family—not even when the Lord has cut off every one of David's enemies from the face of the earth." (1 Samuel 20:14-15)

David keeps his word. He remembers his covenant and honors it, even when the natural impulse would be to eliminate the family of a rival king.

### Application: Adoption by God

This story points to our own adoption into God's family:

> For all who are led by the Spirit of God are sons of God. For you did not receive the spirit of slavery to fall back into fear, but you have received the Spirit of adoption as sons, by whom we cry, "Abba!" (Romans 8:14-15)

Like Mephibosheth, we were enemies of God. We deserved judgment. Yet God has shown us hesed—covenant kindness. He has taken away our condemnation and adopted us as His children. We are no longer enemies but family members sitting at His table.

### The Church as a Community of the Adopted

In our church, we are a fellowship of those who have been forgiven and adopted. When we gather—whether for formal meetings or shared meals—we remember that we were once considered "dead dogs" before God, yet He showed us mercy. We are brothers and sisters not because of shared interests or mutual benefit, but because we share a common Father.

Christian reconciliation is different from worldly reconciliation. In the world, peace is based on mutual agreements and self-interest. Christian reconciliation is based on a common covenant with one Father. We must get along with each other because we are family—not because we scratch each other's backs, but because we share one Lord.

> Be kind and compassionate to one another, forgiving each other, just as in Christ God forgave you. (Ephesians 4:32)

## 3. Retraction of Kindness from the Ungrateful (2 Samuel 10)

### Don't Take God's Kindness for Granted

The third movement of this passage shows us the other side of kindness: what happens when it is rejected and rebuffed.

When the king of the Ammonites dies and his son Hanun takes the throne, David extends kindness—intending to show honor to the new king as he had been honored by Hanun's father Nahash. David sends envoys to express his sympathy.

But the Ammonite commanders convince Hanun that David's gesture is merely a pretense—that the envoys are actually spies. In a shocking display of contempt, Hanun seizes David's men, shaves off half of each man's beard, cuts off their garments at the buttocks, and sends them away utterly humiliated.

### The Response to Rejected Kindness

David's response is swift and severe. He gathers all Israel and defeats the Ammonites and their Aramean allies in battle. The Arameans flee, and the Ammonites retreat into their city. Later, as recorded in 2 Samuel 12, David defeats them completely and enslaves them.

The principle is stark: when kindness is rejected and turned into an opportunity for mockery, judgment follows. This carries a sobering implication for us.

### Our Responsibility

Jesus taught this lesson directly:

> "Therefore, the kingdom of heaven is like a king who wanted to settle accounts with his servants. A man who owed ten thousand bags of gold was brought to him. Since he was not able to pay, the master ordered that he, his wife, his children, and all that he had be sold to repay the debt. The servant fell on his knees and begged, 'Be patient with me, and I will pay back everything.' The servant's master took pity on him, canceled the debt and let him go.

> "But when that servant went out, he found one of his fellow servants who owed him a hundred silver coins. He grabbed him and began to choke him. 'Pay back what you owe me!' he demanded. His fellow servant fell to his knees and begged him, 'Be patient with me, and I will pay it back.' But he refused. Instead, he went off and had the man thrown into prison.

> "When the other servants saw what had happened, they were outraged and went and told their master everything. Then the master called the servant in. 'You wicked servant,' he said, 'I canceled all that debt of yours because you begged me to. Shouldn't you have had mercy on your fellow servant just as I had on you?' In anger his master handed him over to the jailers to be tortured, until he should pay back all he owed.

> "This is how my heavenly Father will treat each of you unless you forgive your brother or sister from your heart." (Matthew 18:21-35)

### Living in Gratitude

We must not take God's kindness for granted. We must:

- Treat each other with the same kindness God has shown us
- Not hold grudges against one another
- Remember that no person is more important than another
- Live in gratitude, recognizing each day as a gift
- Actively serve and call others to faith in Jesus

The church exists for the saved—to encourage one another to remain in Jesus and to be active in extending His kingdom. We are a fellowship of those who have been shown covenant kindness, and we must extend that same kindness to one another and to those who have not yet experienced God's mercy.
