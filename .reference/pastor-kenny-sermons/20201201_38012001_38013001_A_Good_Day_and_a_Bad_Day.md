# Zechariah 12:1—13:1

**Title:** A Good Day and a Bad Day  
**Date:** 20 December 2020  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## A Special Day

What is the most special day in your year? A day you've been looking forward to—like when Year 12 graduates finally reach graduation, or when you celebrate a birthday or anniversary?

We look forward to special days on our calendar: Christmas Day and New Year's Day. But over the next two weeks, as we finish the Book of Zechariah, we'll discover another day that God looks forward to—one that is bittersweet. It's both a good day and a bad day.

## A Good Day

**The Shield and the Strength (12:8–9)**

> On that day the Lord will shield those who live in Jerusalem, so that the feeblest among them will be like David, and the house of David will be like God, like the angel of the Lord going before them. On that day I will set out to destroy all the nations that attack Jerusalem.

God promises a complete transformation. The feeblest will become like David, the mighty warrior king. The house of David will be elevated to divine status, with supernatural power like the angel of the Lord. This is necessary because Zechariah has been prophesying about a coming siege upon Jerusalem. Yet God will use this attack to defeat the enemies, and Jerusalem will remain safe.

**Jerusalem as a Cup of Poison (12:2–3)**

> I am going to make Jerusalem a cup that sends all the surrounding peoples reeling...

The enemy sees Jerusalem as a cup—a walled city containing riches and occupants ripe for conquest. They expect to drink sweet nectar. But what flows out is poison. The invaders realize halfway through that they've been deceived. The word "reeling" captures their stumbling, their hands grabbing their throats, trying to vomit out what they've consumed.

I remember a time when I caught up with a friend at a pub. He asked for ginger beer, and I got one from the bar. After paying $10 a glass, I realized it was alcoholic. I started feeling giddy, my body itched, I got groggy with a headache. What I thought was a normal ginger beer became poison. What I expected was completely different from reality.

Jerusalem is like an immovable rock (12:3). When enemies try to kick it aside, they don't realize it's part of a massive foundation stone hidden beneath the surface. They injure themselves in the attempt.

Notice the irony: God uses the enemies' own weapons against them. The siege they wage becomes their destruction. The rock they kick kicks back harder.

**Blinding the Horses (12:4–5)**

> On that day I will strike every horse with panic and its rider with madness. I will keep a watchful eye over Judah, but I will blind all the horses of the nations.

Horses were the war machines of the ancient world, particularly the Egyptians. Interestingly, horses have exceptional vision—a 350-degree field of view and the largest eyeball of any land mammal, with an oversized retina that magnifies everything they see. They're alert to predators and can sense danger instantly.

But God will blind these horses. In an instant, their greatest strength becomes their greatest weakness. Blinded, they panic and kick their riders off in confusion. They run into each other, neighing and bucking wildly. What was a military advantage becomes a liability.

This echoes Egypt's defeat in Exodus: "Sing to the Lord, for he is highly exalted. Both horse and driver he has hurled into the sea" (Exodus 15:21).

By contrast, God is not blind. God's watchful eye is always on His people, looking after them.

**The Clans of Judah as Fire (12:5–6)**

> Then the clans of Judah will say in their hearts, 'The people of Jerusalem are strong, because the Lord Almighty is their God.' On that day I will make the clans of Judah like a firepot in a woodpile, like a flaming torch among sheaves.

Imagine sheaves—bundles of dried grain stalks. A flaming torch among them. During the siege, fire will be hurled across the walls at Jerusalem. But whatever fire the enemies use will be turned back on them. Jerusalem might burn, but it will burn like a flaming torch among the enemies, ultimately destroying the invaders instead.

This is a pattern we see in movies: the villain's own weapons turned against them. The bad guy invents a death gun—only to be killed by it. The missile aimed at San Francisco ends up destroying the villain instead. The villain tries to drown the hero and ends up drowned. Darth Maul wields his double-bladed saber only to be cut in half by it. Every villain creates the instrument of their own defeat.

God has a sense of irony.

**The New Jerusalem (12:6–10a)**

> Jerusalem will remain intact in her place. The Lord will save the dwellings of Judah first, so that the honor of the house of David and of Jerusalem's inhabitants may not be greater than that of Judah. On that day the Lord will shield those who live in Jerusalem... And I will pour out on the house of David and the inhabitants of Jerusalem a spirit of grace and supplication.

This is a day of safety and protection. But we're looking forward to more than physical protection—we're looking forward to the elimination of wrongdoing, the elimination of death and disease. We're hoping for a world without hit-and-run drivers, without COVID, without viruses.

We cannot find total safety in vaccines or our own efforts. But we are looking forward to that Day of the Lord when enemies won't be watching to cancel us, when there are no bushfires, no threats, no insecurities, no disappointments. 

But this good day comes at a cost.

## A Bad Day

**The Mourning Over the Pierced One (12:10b–11)**

> They will look on me, the one they have pierced, and they will mourn for him as one mourns for an only child, and grieve bitterly for him as one grieves for a firstborn son. On that day the weeping in Jerusalem will be as great as the weeping of Hadad Rimmon in the plain of Megiddo.

The mourning here is profound—like mourning an only son. When the plane MH370 disappeared, the nation grieved. This describes that depth of sorrow.

There's a discrepancy about who is pierced: the text shifts between "me" and "him." Some think this refers to the prophet Zechariah himself, who was killed at the temple. Others suggest it's purposely ambiguous—someone is pierced.

The reference to Megiddo connects to King Josiah, the last good king of Judah. He was young, tried to reform the temple, removed idols, and fought for the kingdom. But while he was king, Pharaoh Necho of Egypt went up to the Euphrates River, and "King Josiah marched out to meet him in battle, but Necho faced him and killed him at Megiddo" (2 Kings 23:29).

Josiah was pierced by archers: "Archers shot King Josiah, and he told his officers, 'Take me away; I am badly wounded'" (2 Chronicles 35:23–24).

And the people mourned deeply: "All Judah and Jerusalem mourned for him. Jeremiah composed laments for Josiah, and to this day all the male and female singers commemorate Josiah in the laments" (2 Chronicles 35:24–25).

But Zechariah's prophecy is future. It will be like Josiah's mourning. As we look back at history, we find one man who perfectly fits this description—someone who came after Zechariah's prophecy.

He was young, in his 30s. He avoided horses and came into Jerusalem on a donkey. He was righteous—reforming the temple and driving out the traders. He was God's only begotten Son. But for that effort, he was rewarded with death.

They pierced him. They nailed him. And more specifically, he was pierced on his side.

> When he had received the drink, Jesus said, "It is finished." With that, he bowed his head and gave up his spirit. But when they came to Jesus and found that he was already dead, they did not break his legs. Instead, one of the soldiers pierced Jesus' side with a spear, bringing a sudden flow of blood and water. These things happened so that the scripture would be fulfilled: "Not one of his bones will be broken," and, as another scripture says, "They will look on the one they have pierced." (John 19:30, 33–37)

Jesus is the one whom they pierced—for our sakes. And the blood and water that flowed?

> On that day a fountain will be opened to the house of David and the inhabitants of Jerusalem, to cleanse them from sin and impurity. (Zechariah 13:1)

The fountain comes from Jesus' side. It cleanses us.

The centurion saw what happened and recognized, "Surely this was a righteous man" (Luke 23:47). When all the people witnessed it, "they beat their breasts" (Luke 23:48)—not just in sadness but in repentance. A large number of people followed him, "including women who mourned and wailed for him" (Luke 23:27).

Here is the one who gave up his spirit so that we might receive the Spirit. Pierced so that we don't have to be pierced. Mourning turned to joy.

This passage is perhaps more appropriate for Easter, but it also has something to say for Christmas.

## The Families of Repentance

> The land will mourn, each clan by itself, with their wives by themselves: the clan of the house of David and their wives, the clan of the house of Nathan and their wives, the clan of the house of Levi and their wives, the clan of Shimei and their wives, and all the rest of the clans and their wives. (12:12–14)

Mourning and repentance will spread across all leadership—the kingly families and the priestly families. David represents the kingly line. Nathan was David's son, linking the royal succession. Levi represents the priesthood, with Shimei as a priestly descendant.

These names may also represent those in David's court, the key figures who made decisions about justice and faith.

But more profoundly, these names trace the genealogy that leads to Jesus. Nathan appears in Luke's genealogy as the son of David through whom Jesus descended. The house of David mourns because one of their own was killed—Jesus, like a son.

When Simeon blessed Mary and the infant Jesus at the temple, he said: "Behold, this Child is appointed to cause the rise and fall of many in Israel, and to be a sign that will be spoken against... and a sword will pierce your soul as well" (Luke 2:34–35). Mary herself would experience the piercing.

## The Cost of Christmas

Christmas still points to what Jesus has done for us. Even in the manger, we see hints of what was to come.

Christmas didn't come cheap—not because we have to buy presents, but because Jesus came to be pierced for us. He came to shed blood and water for our cleansing. He came knowing that the good day of safety and joy would only come through the bad day of His suffering and death.

This is the heart of Christmas: God's redemptive love made incarnate, entering the world to pay a price we could never pay, so that we might know the fountain of grace that flows from His pierced side.
