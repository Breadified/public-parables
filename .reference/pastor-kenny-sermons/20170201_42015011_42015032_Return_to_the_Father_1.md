# Luke 15:11-32

**Title:** Return to the Father
**Date:** 26 February 2017
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Introduction: Two Sons

Jesus introduces us to a story about everyday people—a father with two sons. The characters mirror people we know in our own lives and communities.

The older son is reliable and obedient, the kind of person who volunteers without complaining. The younger son is reckless and impulsive, prone to trouble. But today we hear about the story of two sons—the older and the younger—neither of whom should be confused with any of my actual sons.

> "There was a man who had two sons." (Luke 15:11)

---

## 1. We Hurt God

### The Younger Son's Demand

The younger son approaches his father with a shocking request:

> "Father, give me my share of the estate." (Luke 15:12)

What lies beneath this demand? Somewhere, an argument has escalated into something bigger. But the real meaning of his words cuts deeply: *"I wish you were dead. Give me my inheritance now."*

In Jewish law, a father could designate which assets went to which sons, but the sons could only take possession upon the father's death. By demanding his share immediately, the younger son is essentially saying, *"You are as good as dead to me."*

### The Father's Shocking Response

What any normal father might do:
- Curse the son
- Stone him (as Deuteronomy 21:18-21 permitted for stubborn, rebellious sons)

Instead, he divides his property. The younger son receives one-third of the estate (the firstborn receiving two-thirds by law), and the father liquidates his life's work in a fire sale—fifty years of blood, sweat, and labor gone. One-third of his business model, his means of support, his ability to care for his family—vanished.

But the deepest wound is this: the son who is his life, whom he gave life to, whom he invested in and loved, is torn away from the family.

### The Cost of Rebellion

The Greek word for "divided his property" literally means "divided his life." When we demand our inheritance from God—when we say, "I'm sick of Your laws and Your ways, I want nothing to do with You"—we tear apart the very fabric of God's heart.

That's what we do to God. We are unhappy about the lives He's given us. We are tired of His commands. We are sick of His guilt. We tell Him to go to hell. We proclaim, with the Enlightenment philosophers, that "God is dead"—that we no longer need Him as the source of morality, value, or order.

But this doesn't only hurt God. **It hurts us.**

## 2. We Hurt Ourselves

### The Descent into Chaos

> "Not long after that, the younger son got together all he had, set off for a distant country and there squandered his wealth in wild living." (Luke 15:13)

The word "distant" is significant. The boy doesn't want anything to do with his father, his family, his village, or his past. He goes where no one knows him—where he can start fresh with a new identity, unrestrained by his history, free from the memory of his father.

*I can do what I want now. Nothing will keep me accountable.*

But this physical distance creates an even greater emotional and spiritual distance between father and son—a distance that will nearly destroy the son.

### Wild Living

The word "prodigal" means wasteful, reckless, imprudent, excessive, self-indulgent. Once the inheritance runs out, reality hits. He has spent everything—on fast cars, parties, girls, alcohol, gambling, drugs, prostitution. The chasing of worldliness with no inhibitions. After all, if God is dead, the only restraint is whether you get caught.

The severity strikes when both the money and the country are exhausted:

> "After he had spent everything, there was a severe famine in that whole country, and he began to be in need." (Luke 15:14)

Distance cannot save you. When desperate, when all friends have left, when all property is dispossessed, he hires himself out—into slavery—to feed pigs:

> "So he went and hired himself out to a citizen of that country, who sent him to his fields to feed pigs. He longed to fill his stomach with the pods that the pigs were eating, but no one gave him anything." (Luke 15:15-16)

The severity is compounded: pigs were unclean animals to a Jew. He was so hungry he wanted the worst job in the world for the worst food in the world—and he was still starving because no one gave him anything.

**Riches to rags. Wealth to filth. Wine to swine. Penthouse to outhouse. Everything to nothing.**

### The Pattern of Collapse

A researcher named Joseph Unwin studied 86 different societies through 5,000 years of history. In *Sex and Culture* (1934), he examined 80 primitive tribes and 6 known civilizations. Unwin had no Christian convictions and applied no moral judgment, yet he concluded:

> "Increased sexual constraints, either pre or post-nuptial, always led to increased flourishing of a culture. Conversely, increased sexual freedom always led to the collapse of a culture three generations later. Any human society is free to choose either to display great energy or to enjoy sexual freedom; the evidence is that it cannot do both for more than one generation." (Unwin, J. D., *Sex and Culture*, 1934)

Already we see the warning signs in our own time:
- We used to learn Greek in high school; now universities offer remedial English.
- We can no longer distinguish between male and female.
- Suicide rates are rising.
- Mental health crises are multiplying.

And this is happening despite our being the wealthiest, most educated, most resourced society in human history.

## 3. Come Back

### Coming to His Senses

Something uncommon happens. The rebellious son comes to his senses:

> "When he came to his senses, he said, 'How many of my father's hired servants have food to spare, and here I am starving to death!'" (Luke 15:17)

He rehearses his speech: *"I will set out and go back to my father and say to him: Father, I have sinned against heaven and against you. I am no longer worthy to be called your son; make me like one of your hired servants."* (Luke 15:18-19)

A hired servant was a day laborer, not even living on the property—far below even an indentured slave. Yet even this is better than where he is. As the psalmist says:

> "I would rather be a doorkeeper in the house of my God than dwell in the tents of the wicked." (Psalm 84:10)

Better to be poor in the richest country than rich in the poorest country. Better to suffer under God than to have all the success of the world outside of Him.

### True Repentance

He makes the journey. And when he arrives:

> "But while he was still a long way off, his father saw him and was filled with compassion for him; he ran to his son, threw his arms around him and kissed him." (Luke 15:20)

The son doesn't even get to finish his rehearsed speech. The father sees him—has he been waiting every day, scanning the horizon? He is filled with compassion, and he runs—culturally inappropriate for a dignified man. He kisses his son strongly, repeatedly.

Then he interrupts the boy's prepared words with restoration:

> "But the father said to his servants, 'Quick! Bring the best robe and put it on him. Put a ring on his finger and sandals on his feet. Bring the fattened calf and kill it. Let's have a feast and celebrate.'" (Luke 15:22-23)

Each item is a sign of sonship:
- **The best robe:** The father's own garment, restoring dignity
- **The ring:** The family signet ring, symbol of reinstatement and authority
- **Sandals:** Slaves went barefoot; sandals mark him as a son
- **The fattened calf:** The whole village will know—a public celebration

The father could have stoned him. Instead, he gives a second chance.

### The Heart of God

> "For this son of mine was dead and is alive again; he was lost and is found." (Luke 15:24)

God the Father is always willing to forgive. The problem is not God. The problem is us. We call Him dead, then we're too embarrassed to admit our wrongdoing. We're too proud to seek forgiveness.

But here's the radical truth: the same Jesus who tells you this parable is the son who took the punishment for the prodigal son. The judgment didn't go away. What the son did was criminal. By Jewish law, he would have been stoned. But Jesus takes the stoning upon Himself.

**Maybe you've been stupid. There is a way back to God.**

Churches are full of people who've fouled up, looking for a second chance. Go to Him. Rehearse your speech if you must. And see if He doesn't just run and hug you instead.

## 4. The Good Son

### The Older Son's Anger

Meanwhile, the older son hears the party:

> "The older son was in the field. When he came near the house, he heard music and dancing." (Luke 15:25)

He's been working hard while his brother was away. Must have been quite a party—loud music, cars filling the street. He calls a servant and asks what's going on.

> "Your brother has come," he replied, "and your father has killed the fattened calf because he has him back safe and sound." (Luke 15:27)

The older brother is not impressed:

> "The older brother became angry and refused to go in. So his father went out and pleaded with him." (Luke 15:28)

### The Mask of Righteousness

Now the older son reveals what's in his heart:

> "Look! All these years I've been slaving for you and never disobeyed your orders. Yet you never gave me even a young goat so I could celebrate with my friends. But when this son of yours who has squandered your property with prostitutes comes home, you kill the fattened calf for him!" (Luke 15:29-30)

The language is telling:
- **"Look"** — speaking disrespectfully to his father
- **"Slaving for you"** — he speaks as though he is not a son but a hired servant, as though his father is merely an employer
- **"Never gave me even a young goat"** — accusing the father of stinginess

This son speaks as though his father is not a father at all. But the father responds:

> "My son, you are always with me, and everything I have is yours." (Luke 15:31)

The older son has two-thirds of the estate. In fact, he has everything—he already possessed it. Yet he thought that by keeping all the work, by never disobeying orders, he would earn his father's love. **He already had it.**

### The Trap of Works Righteousness

The older son believes that if he does A, B, and C, then the father must do X, Y, and Z. He's trying to put God into his debt: *"You owe me."*

But Paul writes:

> "Now to the one who works, wages are not credited as a gift but as an obligation. However, to the one who does not work but trusts God who justifies the ungodly, their faith is credited as righteousness." (Romans 4:4-5)

The father's inheritance is a gift, not wages earned. God's salvation is mercy, not a transaction.

This week, many believe: *"God should let me into heaven because..."* But that's not how it works. We have a $50 million debt, and God doesn't accept partial payments.

### The Truth About Both Sons

For the bad son and the good son alike, **we all fall short of the glory of God.** All of God's acts are undeserved and a show of His mercy.

The older son has missed the point entirely. He doesn't see his father as a father—just as a means to an end. The father is a money provider, a security provider. He uses the relationship.

But the father pleads with him:

> "But we had to celebrate and be glad, because this brother of yours was dead and is alive again; he was lost and is found." (Luke 15:32)

---

## Conclusion

This parable isn't about who's worse—the reckless rebel or the self-righteous rule-follower. Both sons are lost. Both need to come home.

The invitation is open to anyone who will turn around and come back to the Father's house.
