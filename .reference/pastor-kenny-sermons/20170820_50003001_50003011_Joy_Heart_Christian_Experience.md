# Philippians 3:1-11

**Title:** Joy: Heart Christian Experience
**Date:** August 20, 2017
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Profit and Loss

Before coming into ministry, I worked as an accountant. One of the fundamental lessons I learned in that work was understanding profit and loss—what adds value and what takes it away. Paul's letter to the Philippians presents us with the most important profit and loss calculation any of us will ever make: the surpassing worth of knowing Christ compared to everything else we might rely upon.

## No Confidence in the Flesh

> Further, my brothers and sisters, rejoice in the Lord! It is no trouble for me to write the same things to you again, and it is a safeguard for you. Watch out for those dogs, those evildoers, those mutilators of the flesh. For it is we who are the circumcision, we who serve God by his Spirit, who boast in Christ Jesus, and who put no confidence in the flesh. (Philippians 3:1-3)

The Greek word *latreuō* (serve) carries deep significance here. It means far more than mere duty or obligation—it speaks of worship that involves a complete commitment of the heart, a life-perspective that characterizes our entire existence. This echoes Jesus' teaching to the Samaritan woman at the well: true worshipers worship "in spirit and truth" (John 4:23-24). Our service to God is fundamentally spiritual, not fleshly.

When Paul speaks of the "flesh" (*sarx*), he's referring to human achievement and accomplishment—even religious accomplishment. This includes the things we might naturally boast about: our heritage, our status, our credentials, our moral performance. For Paul, the flesh represents confidence in human effort, in what we can achieve through our own strength and merit.

## Paul's Remarkable Credentials

Paul understands this better than most. Listen to what he could claim:

> Though I myself have reasons for such confidence. If someone else thinks they have reasons to put confidence in the flesh, I have more: circumcised on the eighth day, of the people of Israel, of the tribe of Benjamin, a Hebrew of Hebrews; in regard to the law, a Pharisee; as for zeal, persecuting the church; as for righteousness based on the law, faultless. (Philippians 3:4-6)

These were not trivial accomplishments. Paul had impeccable religious credentials. He was zealous—so zealous that he persecuted the church. By the standards of religious achievement, he was faultless. By any human measure of success, Paul had it all.

## The Great Exchange

But then something happened that changed everything. Paul encountered Christ, and when he did, his entire accounting changed:

> But whatever were gains to me I now consider loss for the sake of Christ. What is more, I consider everything a loss because of the surpassing worth of knowing Christ Jesus my Lord, for whose sake I have lost all things. I consider them garbage, that I may gain Christ and be found in him, not having a righteousness of my own that comes from the law, but that which is through faith in Christ—the righteousness that comes from God on the basis of faith. (Philippians 3:7-9)

Notice Paul's language. He doesn't call his former accomplishments merely "less important." He calls them *loss*. He calls them *garbage*. This is not the balanced perspective of someone weighing two good things. This is the radical reorientation of someone who has seen what truly matters.

The critical shift is from a righteousness that comes from law—from our own effort and achievement—to a righteousness that comes *through faith*, a righteousness that is *from God*. This is not about earning God's favor. This is about receiving what God offers freely through faith in Christ.

## The Heart of Christian Desire

Paul goes on to express what now drives his deepest longings:

> I want to know Christ—yes, to know the power of his resurrection and participation in his sufferings, becoming like him in his death, and so, somehow, attaining to the resurrection from the dead. (Philippians 3:10-11)

This is the heart of authentic Christian experience. It's not about accumulating more credentials or achievements. It's about intimate knowledge of Christ—knowing the power of his resurrection and even sharing in the fellowship of his sufferings. True Christian life means becoming like him, even in his death, so that we might share in his resurrection.

This is the profit and loss that Paul urges us to understand. Everything we trust in ourselves—our accomplishments, our status, our moral performance—these must be surrendered in light of the surpassing worth of knowing Jesus Christ our Lord. That surrender is not loss; it is the greatest gain imaginable.
