# Ezekiel 1, 36-37

**Title:** Understanding God's Nature Through Ezekiel's Vision and Restoration Promises

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Ezekiel 1:1-21 – The Throne-Chariot Vision

### The Creature's Description

The vision in Ezekiel 1:4-21 reveals a remarkable creature with two striking characteristics:

- **Verses 4-13:** The creature is all-seeing, with eyes covering every part of its form
- **Verses 14-21:** The creature can move anywhere with swiftness, unrestricted by direction or limitation

### Historical Context

Ezekiel was a priest exiled to Babylon (along the Kebar river) as part of the first wave of Jerusalem's captivity. While many Israelites remained in Jerusalem, those in exile felt profoundly abandoned by God. Yet God's throne-chariot appears to Ezekiel to demonstrate His continued presence and power.

### Simultaneous Events in Jerusalem

Meanwhile, in Jerusalem, the temple leaders were defiling the sacred sanctuary. They were sacrificing to idols in darkness (Ezekiel 8:12), foolishly believing God could not see their actions. Their rebellion against the Lord continued unabated.

### God's Response

God's answer came in judgment: He departed from the temple and Jerusalem (Ezekiel 10:4, 9-18, 19; 11:22). The very source of Jerusalem's pride and security—God's dwelling place—left them. Yet God did not abandon His covenant people; He moved toward the exiles in Babylon to comfort them.

### The Central Misunderstanding

Both the Israelites in Jerusalem and the exiles misunderstood God's nature. They treated Him like the idols of surrounding nations—unable to see (blind) and unable to move (immobile). They forgot that their God is omniscient and omnipresent. The exiles in particular thought God was powerless to reach them in Babylon.

### Implications for Us Today

We face two similar temptations to misunderstand God:

1. **God's Omniscience:** We sometimes live as though God cannot see our sin, that He is unaware of our motivations and hidden actions. We sin in darkness, hoping to escape His notice. Yet God sees all and knows all. We must live with the shame of our wrongdoings and repent.

2. **God's Power to Forgive:** We sometimes believe God is powerless to forgive us, that our sin is too great or our nature too broken. Yet God comes to us through Jesus Christ with power to bring forgiveness despite our sins.

## Ezekiel 36:22-38 – The Promise of Restoration

### Historical Arc

Ezekiel served as a prophet to warn Judah that continued rebellion would bring God's judgment (chapters 4-24). He also prophesied against surrounding nations (chapters 25-32). True to His word, God's judgment fell: Jerusalem was destroyed in 586 BC (Ezekiel 33:21).

But God's judgment is not His final word. He is merciful and compassionate. In chapter 36, God reveals His intention to restore His people.

### God's Promises of Restoration (36:22-38)

**Vindication of God's Holy Name (verses 22-23)**
- God will vindicate His own name
- God will vindicate His holiness
- The nations will come to know the Lord through this restoration

**Transformation of the People (verses 24-27)**
- God will gather the exiles from all nations
- He will sprinkle clean water upon them (symbolic cleansing)
- He will give them a new heart and new spirit
- He will place His Spirit within them
- They will walk in God's statutes and obey Him

**Return to the Land (verses 28-32)**
- The people will dwell in their ancestral land
- The land will experience abundance and flourishing

**Summary of Restoration (verse 33)**
> "I will cleanse you from all your iniquities, and I will cause the cities to be inhabited."

God promises that a renewed and multiplied people will return to a restored and abundant land.

### Why Does God Do This?

The primary motivation is not merely Israel's need for forgiveness (though they certainly need it) or even the purification of the land (though that matters). Rather, God's central concern is His own name and glory. He will restore Israel so that the nations will recognize that the Lord is faithful: "Then the nations will know that I am the Lord, I have spoken, and I will do it" (Ezekiel 36:26).

### The New Heart and New Spirit

What does it mean for God to give an undivided heart and a new spirit?

- **An undivided heart** that will not waver from God (Ezekiel 11:19)
- **Removal of sin and offense** (Ezekiel 18:31)
- **A fundamental change in attitude**: from a desire to rebel against God to a desire to obey Him

This is not mystical or spooky; it is a real transformation of the human will from rebellion to willing obedience.

### Application: The New Birth in Christ

In John 3:5-16, Jesus teaches that this spiritual renewal comes through being born again:

- To see the kingdom of God
- To believe in the Son
- To have eternal life

**The critical question for believers:** Do you have God's new heart and spirit? Are you changed from wanting to rebel against God to wanting to obey Him?

## Ezekiel 37:1-14 – The Valley of Dry Bones

### The Symbolism of Death

Ezekiel's vision opens with a valley completely filled with bones—and these are not fresh bones, but dry bones that have been dead for a long time. The bones are described repeatedly:

- A valley full of them (verse 1)
- Very, very dry bones (verse 2)
- A great multitude of bones (verse 2)
- Dead bones (verse 3)
- Dry bones (verse 4)
- Dried up (verse 11)
- Without hope (verse 11)

This entire vision symbolizes Israel: the whole house of Israel is truly dead and decayed. All hope of their own resurrection appears gone.

### God's Power to Resurrect

Yet what is impossible for humans becomes possible for God. God commands Ezekiel to prophesy over the bones, and God Himself brings them back to life by breathing His breath into them and giving them spirit. God alone has the power to raise the dead through His words.

### Why Only God Can Resurrect

The Israelites cannot resurrect themselves. They are spiritually dead and helpless—as dry and lifeless as those ancient bones. A dead thing cannot raise itself; it requires the power of life from outside itself.

### Application: Spiritual Death and Resurrection in Christ

Paul describes our spiritual condition in Ephesians 2:1-9: we are dead through our disobedience and sin. Yet just as God resurrected Israel through His power, Jesus Christ resurrects us from spiritual death.

### The Question of Human Choice

Some argue that non-Christians retain some ability to choose God and become Christians. Yet Scripture reveals a deeper truth: although we have valid choices and are not robots, we do not inherently possess the ability to make good spiritual choices. Because of our sin, we cannot choose God through our own power. God must change us and resurrect us by His word and Spirit before we are able to obey Him.

Our freedom is not the ability to save ourselves, but the freedom we receive when God's Spirit transforms our dead hearts and enables us to willingly follow Him.
