# Revelation 1:1-20

**Title:** The Church in the World
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Understanding Revelation's Purpose

Revelation means to unveil or make known. The central question of this book is: *Who is being revealed?* Through John's vision, God pulls back the curtain to show us the reality of Christ's authority and the church's position in the world.

John serves as a messenger—a channel through whom God communicates to the seven churches. His role echoes that of the Old Testament prophets like Daniel, who received visions about God's kingdom (Daniel 2:25-30, 44). When Daniel speaks of the kingdom that "will never be destroyed" (v. 44), he's describing something cosmic and eternal. Yet notice the tension: in his own time, has this kingdom already started? Yes—it has begun, though its full consummation remains future. The promises are not merely about a distant tomorrow, but about a kingdom that invades history now.

## Who Is This Letter For?

This letter addresses seven specific churches in Asia Minor, churches facing real persecution. To claim the name "Christian" in that context meant facing possible imprisonment, torture, or death. But why does John write to these churches? And why should we read this letter today?

Because the church's struggle then mirrors the church's struggle now. We may not face physical persecution in the same way, but the core challenge remains: How do we live faithfully as God's people in a world that opposes His kingdom? This book is intensely practical—it's written to encourage believers caught between two worlds.

## The Throne Room Vision (1:4-6)

God is described here not in abstract theological language, but through a portrait drawn from Israel's worship. The greetings mention "the seven spirits before his throne" and Jesus Christ. This language, strange to modern ears, connects directly to the Jewish Tabernacle—that portable tent where God's presence dwelled among His people.

> "The seven spirits before his throne" and "Jesus Christ, the faithful witness"

Notice how the vision echoes Hebrews 8:1-5, where Jesus is described as the high priest seated at God's right hand, serving in "the true tabernacle." What John sees is the heavenly reality of which the earthly Tabernacle was merely a shadow. God hasn't abandoned His people to the empire's power—He rules from heaven.

## Jesus: The Risen, Exalted King

How does John describe Jesus in verses 5-8? Pay close attention to the tenses. He is:

- **The faithful witness** (past tense—He testified to truth even unto death)
- **The firstborn of the dead** (already risen, not merely promised resurrection)
- **The ruler of the kings of the earth** (present tense—He rules now)

This echoes Psalm 89:27 and Daniel 7:13. Jesus isn't a victim of Rome's power—He is the ultimate authority. He has conquered death itself. The present tense matters: this isn't a future hope alone, but a present reality that John's persecuted readers desperately needed to grasp.

## The Cosmic Portrait (1:12-16)

John sees "one like the Son of Man" walking among seven golden lampstands, and his appearance is overwhelming. Every detail is packed with Old Testament meaning:

- **Like a son of man** (Daniel 7:13—the divine figure)
- **Feet like bronze glowing in a furnace** (Daniel 10:6, Ezekiel 1:7)
- **A sharp, double-edged sword coming from his mouth** (Isaiah 49:2—the word of God)
- **Face like the sun shining in all its brilliance** (Matthew 17:2—divine glory)
- **Seven stars in his right hand** (representing the seven churches)

This isn't decoration—each image anchors Christ's identity in Scripture's deepest promises about God's power and presence.

## John's Response and Ours

When John sees this vision, he falls at Jesus's feet "as though dead" (v. 17). Is this the right response? Look at Daniel 8:17-18 and Luke 5:8—when sinful humans encounter the holy God, terror and worship are the natural response. Peter, seeing the miraculous catch of fish, cries out, "Go away from me, Lord; I am a sinner!" This isn't pathology; it's theology. We are undone in the presence of holiness.

But Jesus says to John, "Do not be afraid." The same Jesus who conquered death offers His reassurance.

## The Symbols Explained

The vision's key symbols:
- **Seven lampstands** = seven churches
- **Seven stars** = seven angels of the seven churches
- **Jesus holding the stars** = Jesus holds the church's destiny

What unites the church? What is at the center? *Jesus Christ.* He doesn't observe the churches from distant heaven—He walks among them, holding them in His hand. The image is of the churches as lampstands shining in darkness (Philippians 2:14-16). They don't control the world's systems, but they shine with the light of Christ.

## The Church's Mission in a Hostile World

Do Christians rule the world? No—not yet. The kingdoms of this world still belong to earthly powers. Yet here's the pattern that structures everything:

**In this world we must** *[live faithfully, proclaim Christ, bear witness]*
**+** **What Christ has done** *[conquered death, purchased the church, revealed God's glory]*
**=** **Our response** *[faithful witness, holy living, patient endurance]*

Are you doing this? This is the core of Revelation's message—not predictions about the future alone, but a call to present obedience.

## A Practical Book for Practical Christians

Notice the closing blessing:

> "Blessed is the one who reads aloud the words of this prophecy, and blessed are those who hear, and who keep what is written in it, for the time is near." (Revelation 1:3)

Revelation is not a mysterious puzzle reserved for scholars. It's a *practical book for practical Christians*—written to people facing real suffering, needing real encouragement. Its purpose is to shape how we live now, not to satisfy our curiosity about the future.

As you move into chapters 2 and 3, look closely for patterns and repetitions. What does Jesus say to each church? What warnings repeat? What promises does He offer? The pattern itself is the message.
