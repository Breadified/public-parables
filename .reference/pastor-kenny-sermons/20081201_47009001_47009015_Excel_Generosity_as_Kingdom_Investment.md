# 2 Corinthians 9:1-15

**Title:** Excel: Generosity as Kingdom Investment

**Date:** 12 December 2008

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Context: Paul's Collection for the Jerusalem Church

Paul is collecting funds from the Greek churches—specifically from Corinth, Achaia, and Macedonia—to support poor Jewish Christians in Jerusalem. This is described as "service to the saints," referring to both the material aid for those in need and the gospel ministry to the poor.

The situation reveals Paul's broader missionary strategy: gather support from Greece, travel to Jerusalem, then continue to Rome and ultimately Spain. Throughout his letters (1 Corinthians, 2 Corinthians, Philippians, and Romans), Paul returns to this theme because **money matters in ministry**.

## Why We Should Talk About Money

Christians often shy away from discussing finances—some have been burned by con-artists, others fear that talking about money suggests God-reliance, and still others consider it too personal. Yet Paul doesn't think this way. Even though he worked as a tentmaker, he still needed financial help from others. Money is essential both for sustaining our own lives and for supporting ministry.

Consider how we approach other resources: if someone spent all their time in the gym or studying rather than serving the church, leaders would challenge them to realign their priorities. Yet when it comes to money—often our most significant resource in a modern economy—we remain silent.

**The real issue:** In our specialized, diversified economy, we've become time-poor but money-rich, working 45+ hours per week to earn. How we spend our money reveals what we truly value. In previous generations, ministry relied on volunteer labor—people cleaned church buildings, served as honorary treasurers, offered their time freely. Today, we hire professional cleaners, accountants, and trained pastors. Whether we like it or not, **modern ministry survives on money**. Money is the lifeblood.

This doesn't mean money is our ultimate motivation—we reach out to youth, plant churches, and serve because we love Jesus. But money cannot be last in our practical considerations; it must be somewhere in the equation. So Paul's challenge remains: **put your wallet where your values are.**

## Three Encouragements for Generous Giving

### 1. Your Reward: The Harvest of Righteousness (9:6-11)

> **Remember this: Whoever sows sparingly will also reap sparingly, and whoever sows generously will also reap generously.** (2 Corinthians 9:6)

Paul uses the image of a farmer sowing seed. There's effort involved—sowing in the heat of the day, watering, tending the crop. But harvest comes in due time, bringing reward for that investment.

Paul supports this principle with Psalm 112:9:

> **He has scattered abroad his gifts to the poor; his righteousness endures forever.**

The blessed person is the one who is generous. Notice what Paul calls the fruit of this giving:

> **Now he who supplies seed to the sower and bread for food will also supply and increase your store of seed and will enlarge the harvest of your righteousness.** (2 Corinthians 9:10)

The harvest is not material wealth—it's *righteousness*. This echoes throughout Scripture:

- **Hebrews 12:11**: Discipline "produces a harvest of righteousness and peace for those who have been trained by it."
- **James 3:18**: "Peacemakers who sow in peace raise a harvest of righteousness."

The reward for generosity is spiritual blessing: God's affirmation—"Well done, good and faithful servant"—and the gratitude of those you've helped.

**What does this harvest mean for us?**

First, **you will be thanked.** The fruits of the ministry you fund—the lives touched, the gospel preached, the poor cared for—will result in thanksgiving rising to God on your behalf. As a pastor, when people thank me for my work, I must be honest: I'm doing what I'm paid to do. The true generosity comes from those who give financially, freeing me from secular employment so I can serve the church, visit schools, meet with people, and prepare teachings. In heaven, you will have people thanking you for your generosity. You will reap generously.

Second, **generosity frees us from materialism.** We live in an incredibly materialistic society where advertising, economics, and peer pressure trap us in endless competition for more stuff. Our government even labels us as "consumers" when planning policy. The second most important cabinet position is now the Treasurer—we've become economic units. But we are more than the sum of our wallets. We are made for relationship, for caring for one another, for generosity. When you sow your money freely, you break free from the tyranny of materialism. You liberate yourself from the harassment of advertisers and the pressure of competition. Generosity is freedom.

### 2. Your Freedom: Give from Your Heart (9:7)

> **Each man should give what he has decided in his heart to give, not reluctantly or under compulsion, for God loves a cheerful giver.** (2 Corinthians 9:7)

Paul emphasizes three critical points:
- Give **what you have decided in your heart**—intentionally, thoughtfully
- Don't give **reluctantly or under compulsion**
- Understand that **God loves a cheerful giver**

You might read Paul's letters and think he's putting pressure on the Corinthians. But context matters: they had already committed to give. Paul is simply calling them to follow through on their own promise. His expectation is reasonable—their previous commitment had already inspired the Macedonians and Achains to give generously.

The principle underlying all of this is **grace**. Recall from 2 Corinthians 8: Christ became poor so that we might be rich. God's nature is grace and generosity; therefore, our giving should reflect that same character—freely, joyfully, without compulsion.

This is why legalistic tithing doesn't work. The principle isn't a percentage—it's not that everyone must give 10%. Rather, the principle is generosity according to ability: it might be 5% for one person, 10% for another, 20% for someone else. The key is being **intentional and ready**.

**How to be ready:**

- **Direct debit** is the modern equivalent of being prepared. Most of us don't carry cash anymore. Set up automatic giving from your bank account.
- **If you prefer cash**, set it aside on Saturday night when you receive your paycheck or pocket money. Be ready.
- **Regarding coins**: We want to move away from viewing the church as a charity box for loose change. The church isn't a repository for spare 5-cent and 20-cent pieces. We're grateful for any gift, but we need steady, committed cash flow to fund the gospel work in our suburbs. For those who are genuinely poor, save your coins and give a lump sum when you can afford it—God sees your heart. But to those who are richer among us: you could easily solve this problem. Give thoughtfully; give from intention.

### 3. Your Ability: God Will Provide (9:8)

> **And God is able to make all grace abound to you, so that in all things at all times, having all that you need, you will abound in every good work.** (2 Corinthians 9:8)

Paul's final assurance: **God will look after you.**

Now, we must be careful here. Some read verses 10-11—"You will be made rich in every way so that you can be generous on every occasion"—and assume Paul is promising material prosperity. This is the danger of prosperity doctrine: give more, get more in return. But that's not what Scripture teaches. If you give generously, you will be materially poorer. There's no guarantee of earthly material blessing in return. The harvest of righteousness is reaped at the end-time, in eternity.

I believe Paul's language here is **hyperbolic**—meant to reassure and encourage, not to promise earthly wealth. He's saying: "God will look after all your needs so that you can do all the good you're called to do."

Consider how many of us worry: *We can't survive without those clothes, that dinner out, those savings, that portfolio, that tutoring for our kids.* But we can. We can survive these things. Look at our situation: most of us have jobs, own homes in nice suburbs, eat out at least once a week, have full fridges, and live in one of the richest countries in the world. **We are okay.**

Yes, there are seasons when generosity might be harder—times of genuine unemployment, depression, or war. But that's not our reality. Things are actually looking up for most of us, even as economies fluctuate. We're unlikely to experience the poverty and famine our grandparents endured in war-torn China.

**Don't let unreasonable fear stop you from harvesting a rich harvest.**

## The Fruit of Generosity: Thanksgiving Overflows (9:12-15)

> **This service that you perform is not only supplying the needs of God's people but is also overflowing in many expressions of thanks to God. Because of the service by which you have proved yourselves, men will praise God for the obedience that accompanies your confession of the gospel of Christ, and for your generosity in sharing with them and with everyone else. And in their prayers for you their hearts will go out to you, because of the surpassing grace God has given you.** (2 Corinthians 9:12-14)

When you give, the results ripple far beyond the immediate gift. The poor are provided for, yes. But more than that, thanksgiving erupts. Men and women praise God. Prayers ascend on your behalf. Your generosity becomes a testimony to the grace of Christ.

> **Thanks be to God for his indescribable gift!** (2 Corinthians 9:15)

In the end, all our giving is a response to God's indescribable gift to us—Christ himself. Everything flows from that grace.
