## The Glorious Son

### The Transfiguration (Matthew 17:1-9)

> After six days Jesus took with him Peter, James and John the brother of James, and led them up a high mountain by themselves. There he was transfigured before them. His face shone like the sun, and his clothes became as white as the light. Just then there appeared before them Moses and Elijah, talking with Jesus.

The transfiguration is Jesus "morphing"—transforming into His glorious state. Yet what follows is even stranger: Moses and Elijah appear, speaking with Him.

Peter's instinct is to build shelters (tents), echoing the Old Testament pattern where God dwells in tabernacles. But the Father's response reorients everything:

> "This is my Son, whom I love; with him I am well pleased. Listen to him!"

When the disciples hear this voice from the cloud, they fall facedown in terror. But Jesus touches them and says, "Get up. Don't be afraid." When they look up, they see only Jesus.

### Jesus as the New Moses

To understand the transfiguration's significance, we need to see its connection to Exodus 24:9-25:9, where Moses ascends the mountain with companions, sees God's glory in a cloud that covers the mountain for six days, and receives the law written on stone tablets.

Matthew is showing us that Jesus fulfills this pattern—but more gloriously.

**Key connections:**
- Three companions accompany Him on the mountain
- A cloud covers the mountain and a voice speaks from heaven
- The "six days" mentioned in both passages (Matthew 17:1; Exodus 24:15-16)
- The building of shelters, echoing the tabernacle

But Jesus surpasses Moses in every way. His face shines with divine glory. His relationship to God is more intimate: "This is my Son." When Peter suggests building tents for Jesus, Moses, and Elijah as equals, the Father's voice cuts through: there is no need for tents. Jesus is God in the flesh.

### Listen to Him

Embedded in this scene is an earlier promise in Exodus 23:20-22, where God promises to send "an angel" (messenger/prophet) ahead to guard His people:

> "Pay attention to him and listen to what he says. Do not rebel against him; for my Name is in him."

This messianic promise finds its fulfillment in Jesus. The disciples are called to listen to Him—not to the law of Moses, but to the voice of the Son who embodies all of God's will.

The most striking moment comes when the disciples look up: "they saw no one except Jesus." Moses and Elijah fade away. Only Jesus remains—the ultimate authority, the living Word of God.

## The New Law: Servants of Mercy

### Be Little (Matthew 18:1-4)

The disciples, obsessed with power and status, ask Jesus: "Who is the greatest in the kingdom of heaven?"

Jesus's answer shatters their ambition:

> He called a little child and had him stand among them. And he said: "I tell you the truth, unless you change and become like little children, you will never enter the kingdom of heaven. Therefore, whoever humbles himself like this child is the greatest in the kingdom of heaven."

A "little one" in Jesus's teaching is not merely a small person—it represents someone who:
- **Listens** to God with simple faith
- **Believes** without qualification (as opposed to the disciples' constant doubt)
- **Serves** without seeking recognition or power
- **Depends** utterly on their Father

This is the radical inversion at the heart of the kingdom: greatness comes through humility and service, not ambition.

### Welcome the Little (Matthew 18:5-20)

> "And whoever welcomes a little child like this in my name welcomes me."

To welcome the little ones is to accept them, to clear the way for them, to serve them. The inverse is equally stark:

> "But if anyone causes one of these little ones who believe in me to sin, it would be better for him to have a large millstone hung around his neck and to be drowned in the depths of the sea."

The Greek word here—*skandalizo* (scandal/stumbling block)—appears throughout this section. To scandalize or offend means to place obstacles in someone's path to God. The disciples must not become barriers to the kingdom.

This applies even to those with authority and gifts. A leader who blocks others through ambition, arrogance, or the careless exercise of power closes the door of the kingdom.

### The Healing of the Epileptic Boy (Matthew 17:14-20)

When a desperate father brings his demon-possessed son to Jesus's disciples, they cannot heal him. Jesus rebukes them:

> "O unbelieving and perverse generation, how long shall I stay with you? How long shall I put up with you? Bring the boy here to me."

Jesus casts out the demon immediately. When the disciples later ask privately why they failed, His answer cuts to the heart:

> "Because you have so little faith. I tell you the truth, if you have faith as small as a mustard seed, you can say to this mountain, 'Move from here to there' and it will move. Nothing will be impossible for you."

The disciples lacked faith—that childlike trust and belief. They were seeking to perform miracles as a display of power rather than as an act of service and compassion. In their ambition, they blocked a suffering child from healing.

### The Temple Tax (Matthew 17:24-27)

When tax collectors ask whether Jesus pays the temple tax, He responds with a surprising lesson in servanthood:

> "What do you think, Simon? From whom do the kings of the earth collect duty and taxes—from their own sons or from others?"
>
> "From others," Peter answered.
>
> "Then the sons are exempt," Jesus said to him. "But so that we may not offend them, go to the lake and throw out your line. Take the first fish you catch; open its mouth and you will find a four-drachma coin. Take it and give it to them for my tax and yours."

The point is profound: Jesus, the Son of God, is exempt from the tax—yet He pays it. He humbles Himself to avoid offending others. He does not insist on His rights; instead, He serves by meeting the expectations of those around Him.

This is what it means to deny self and carry your cross.

### The Lost Sheep (Matthew 18:12-20)

Jesus teaches that God's heart is to seek the one who wanders:

> "If a man owns a hundred sheep, and one of them wanders away, will he not leave the ninety-nine on the hills and go to look for the one that wandered off? In the same way your Father in heaven is not willing that any of these little ones should be lost."

What follows is a process of reconciliation—going to a brother who sins, bringing witnesses, involving the church—all designed to restore the lost. The disciples are given authority to "bind and loose" on earth, reflecting God's own authority to declare His will. But this authority exists for restoration and mercy, not for condemnation.

## A Call to Listen and Serve

### Why Don't We Listen?

Jesus, the new Moses, gives His disciples a new law: become servants of mercy. Yet they—and we—often resist. When Scripture confronts us with difficult teachings, we tend to:

1. **Skim** past troubling passages, mumbling excuses about complexity
2. **Appeal to tradition** or our theological systems, dismissing what we don't like
3. **Appeal to culture**, treating the Bible as culturally conditioned rather than authoritative
4. **Unduly qualify** the text until it means nothing
5. **Excise** what we disagree with, treating the Bible as flawed
6. **Dismiss** whole sections as outdated
7. **Balance** passages until they lose their force and impact

Take Matthew 18:8: "If your hand or your foot causes you to sin, cut it off and throw it away." Our first instinct is to dismiss this as hyperbole and move on. But perhaps we should pause and sit under the weight of Jesus's words.

Jesus is not literally calling for self-mutilation. However, He *is* communicating the absolute seriousness of sin and the lengths to which we must go to overcome it. The hyperbolic language forces us to ask: What would it cost to cut off my hand? I would hurt. I would miss it. I would be disabled. If that would be necessary to stop this sin, how seriously do I take it?

The point is not the surgical removal but the radical willingness to sacrifice comfort and convenience for holiness. Maybe you won't cut off your arm—but perhaps you'll cancel the internet subscription that feeds your temptation. Maybe you'll stop using that hand to steal or strike. Maybe you won't gouge out your eye—but you'll turn off that TV program or discard that magazine.

Only by taking the text seriously, by sitting under its authority rather than dismissing it, do we allow God's Word to reshape our values and choices.

### Lowering Yourself for Others

The overarching theme threads through every episode in Matthew 16-20: **Deny yourself. Take up your cross. Follow Jesus. Serve the little ones. Welcome the excluded. Pursue the lost. Forgive without limit.**

This is what it means to be a disciple. It is what it means to truly listen to Jesus.

**Are you willing to welcome and serve?**

Consider the practical reality: Who is the host at a party? The person who arrives early and stays late, attending to guests and cleanup. Not the one who shows up late, flutters around socially, and leaves before the work is done.

If you want to welcome others and serve them in church, in Bible study, in campus ministry—you have to be there. Early. Late. Present. Committed.

**Are you willing to be little for others?** This is precisely what Jesus did. He emptied Himself, took on human flesh, served His disciples by washing their feet, and ultimately gave His life.

**Do we close off the gospel for others?** We can stumble those around us through:
- Stupid behavior that brings shame to the faith
- Arrogance that says "who is the greatest?" instead of "how can I serve?"
- The careless or unrestrained exercise of our gifts and knowledge

**Do you forgive?** Peter asks if he should forgive his brother seven times. Jesus responds: "Not seven times, but seventy-seven times"—meaning without limit.

The parable of the unforgiving servant illustrates this starkly. A servant, forgiven an enormous debt by his master, refuses to forgive a fellow servant a tiny debt. The master's response is severe: mercy withheld will be mercy withheld from you.

God's forgiveness toward us is infinite. Our forgiveness toward others must reflect that same grace.

## Morphin Time

Jesus, the glorious Son of God, came not to be served but to serve, and to give His life as a ransom for many. The call to discipleship is a call to follow Him in this radical servanthood.

**The question for you is simple: Will you listen to Him?**

Will you lower yourself for others? Will you welcome the little ones—the overlooked, the struggling, the lost? Will you refuse to block anyone's path to God through your pride or carelessness? Will you forgive as you have been forgiven?

This is not sentimental Christianity. It is costly. It demands everything. But it is the way of the kingdom, and it is the way of life eternal.
