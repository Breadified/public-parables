# 1 Peter 2:12-25

**Title:** Alien Strategy
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Context: Persecution and the Alien's Dilemma

When Peter wrote this letter around 64 AD, Rome was engulfed in flames. Emperor Nero, seizing the opportunity to consolidate power, blamed the Christians. What followed was systematic torture and execution so severe that believers wondered if Nero himself was the Antichrist. Historical accounts describe Christians being impaled on stakes throughout his gardens and set alight as torches for nighttime entertainment.

This pattern repeated through history. During China's Cultural Revolution (1966-1976), religious expression was effectively outlawed. Believers were imprisoned, tortured, and humiliated. Bibles were destroyed, and churches were ransacked. The question becomes urgent: **What should Christians do when facing such opposition?**

## The Fundamental Problem

Peter reminds us that we are "a chosen people, a royal priesthood, a holy nation, a people belonging to God." Our purpose is clear: to declare the praises of him who called us out of darkness into his wonderful light.

Yet what happens when circumstances prevent us from speaking? When government persecution silences us? When we're the only Christian in our family and our faith is forbidden? When our workplace or school restricts what we can say?

The core challenge: **We are a minority, often powerless. How can we be heard?**

## 1. Creating Opportunities Through Good Lives

Peter's solution is surprisingly counterintuitive. He writes:

> Live such good lives among the pagans that, though they accuse you of doing wrong, by seeing your good deeds they may glorify God on the day he visits us. (1 Peter 2:12)

### The Foundation: Moral Living

The good life means living with genuine moral integrity. Before verse 12, Peter urges us:

> Dear friends, I urge you, as aliens and strangers in the world, to abstain from sinful desires, which war against your soul. (1 Peter 2:11)

This is the crux: Christians must be *morally different* from the world around them. Look at any news cycle, any newspaper, any social media feed. The world is not a moral place. Abortion, prostitution, exploitation—these are normalized. When we refuse to join this "flood of dissipation," people notice and often respond with hostility:

> They think it strange that you do not plunge with them into the same flood of dissipation, and they heap abuse on you. (1 Peter 4:4)

### How This Works: Silencing Slander

Peter explains the mechanism in chapter 3:

> For it is God's will that by doing good you should silence the ignorant talk of foolish men. (1 Peter 2:15)

Good works have power. They confound skeptics and render their slander ineffective. Over time, accusations lose their force against a life that consistently demonstrates integrity.

### The Real Goal: Creating Space to Speak

But silence isn't the end goal—it's the platform. As people notice your moral distinctiveness, they'll ask. Peter says:

> Always be prepared to give an answer to everyone who asks you to give the reason for the hope that you have. But do this with gentleness and respect. (1 Peter 3:15)

**Here is Peter's strategy:** Live morally. Some will accuse you, but their voices will be muzzled. Others will become curious. They'll ask why you're different. In that moment, you speak.

### The Role of Words

Never misunderstand Peter's point. Salvation comes through words—the gospel message, the great news that salvation is *done*, not *do*. Good works alone cannot save anyone. A person cannot look at your charity and think, "That's why God loves him—I should be more charitable so God will love me too." That's a false gospel of works, not a gospel of grace.

The saying "always preach the gospel, and if necessary use words" is backward. Instead: **Always preach the gospel, and if you can't speak, live good lives to create the opportunity to.**

### Practical Examples

Consider these real situations:

- A student cleans up someone's vomit in class. That's noticed.
- You find a mobile phone and return it. When the grateful owner offers payment, you decline: "I'm a Christian." The conversation opens.
- A mechanic asks if you'd like an off-the-books receipt. You refuse: "I'm a Christian."
- At a party, you decline alcohol. You're underage and want to stay in control. People notice.
- A young woman travels to Hong Kong for only a weekend wedding. Colleagues are puzzled—why such a short trip? She explains her priorities: family, obligations at home, her values. She's not driven by FOMO because of hope in the resurrection. A conversation about what matters unfolds.
- Having five children in a culture obsessed with materialism and personal fulfillment creates inevitable questions. Yes, some will judge. But others will be intrigued by your different moral framework.

### Our Problem: We've Lost Our Alien Status

Here's the uncomfortable truth: **Most of us have abandoned our distinctiveness.** Our definition of the good life mirrors the world's:

- The Australian Dream: good food, good entertainment, good friends.
- The Asian Dream: high ATAR scores, professional status, a house, a car, the latest technology.

We think we can witness by being successful doctors or lawyers, imagining people will see our earthly achievements and glorify God. They won't. Why would they? You want exactly what they want. You're doing the very same things they're doing.

If our lifestyle is identical—the same Friday nights, the same clothes, the same spending habits, the same ambitions, the same vacation destinations—then what's different about us that would provoke a question? We've surrendered our alien status. We've become just like them.

Why would they ask about your hope? They already know it. It's the same hope as theirs: hope in the world itself.

Even our good deeds look worldly. We talk about environmentalism and poverty relief—good things, but indistinguishable from what the world already does.

### Imagine Another Future

What if you didn't pursue medicine when everyone expected it? What if you became a teacher instead? That raises questions.

Think about attending a Christian group every Friday lunch. You walk away from your friends, from handball games, from food, from comfort. Your actions announce: *I value something greater than all these things.* That's witnessed.

## 2. Submission to Kings (1 Peter 2:13-17)

Peter now shifts to a specific way Christians create opportunities: through submission to governing authority, even when that authority opposes them.

> Submit yourselves for the Lord's sake to every authority instituted among men: whether to the king, as the supreme authority, or to governors, who are sent by him to punish those who do wrong and to commend those who do right. (1 Peter 2:13-14)

This echoes Paul's instruction in Romans:

> Everyone must submit himself to the governing authorities, for there is no authority except that which God has established. The authorities that exist have been established by God. (Romans 13:1-2)

Government authority comes from God. Authorities are appointed to maintain order—to punish wrongdoing and commend right action.

### The Tension: Freedom in Christ

The danger is assuming that Christian freedom means exemption from earthly law. After all, a pagan government opposes us. We're aliens under God's law. Why submit to their law?

Paul addresses this tension:

> It is for freedom that Christ has set us free. Stand firm, then, and do not let yourselves be burdened again by a yoke of slavery. (Galatians 5:1)

True. We are free in Christ. But Martin Luther captured the paradox beautifully:

> A Christian is perfectly free lord of all, subject to none. A Christian is a perfectly dutiful servant of all, subject to all.

Our freedom is not a license for rebellion. Peter warns:

> Live as free men, but do not use your freedom as a cover-up for evil; live as servants of God. (1 Peter 2:16)

### Practical Obedience

This means:

- Year 12 students: don't speed, don't use mobile phones while driving.
- Don't pirate media, even if pricing is inflated or international releases are delayed.
- Don't skip official school events. The Department of Education has authority. Beyond rules, these events offer broader education: sport, friendship, seeing teachers as humans.

### The Hard Question: What About Unjust Laws?

Immediately, we think of exceptions. What about Nazi Germany?

A few principles:

**First:** Even imperfect authorities are preferable to anarchy. Saddam Hussein's Iraq, once he fell, descended into chaos. Imperfect order beats lawlessness.

There's a romantic notion about resistance—smuggling Bibles into China, organizing underground churches. But **ends don't justify means.** Consider alternatives within legal frameworks:

- Family gatherings (governments typically don't forbid family worship)
- One-on-one conversations
- Scripture memorization
- Underground churches lack accountability and transparency—they're not inherently good

China's social contract requires some surrender of individuality for national harmony. If you continuously break laws, you lose your audience entirely. How will anyone listen to you?

**Second:** If a law genuinely opposes God's command, submit to the authority by refusing to obey—and accept the consequences. That's how laws work. There's always an opt-out clause, with consequences.

Daniel refused to stop praying despite the king's decree. He knew the cost and paid it willingly. But notice: he didn't organize rebellion. He simply obeyed God and faced the penalty.

When you skip a school sports carnival for study, don't lie about being sick. State your real reason. Integrity earns respect in a way dishonesty never will.

In extreme cases—like Nazi persecution of Jews—the emergency is real, but it doesn't establish good case law. Other responses exist: silence, offering yourself, recognizing that government cannot legislate hearts and minds.

### The Real Goal

Submission to government is a strategy to gain an audience. Peter writes:

> For it is commendable if a man bears up under the pain of unjust suffering because he is conscious of God. But if you suffer for doing good and you endure it, this is commendable before God. (1 Peter 2:19-20)

This is the opposite of the world's approach. The world qualifies submission:

- Submit until they tell you something you don't want to hear—then stop.
- Submit until they insult you—then stop.
- Submit until it hurts—then stop.

But the key is developing a *heart of submission*, not finding exceptions. This is the way of Jesus.

## 3. Submission to Masters (1 Peter 2:18-25)

Peter applies the same principle to another oppressive structure: slavery. We must understand the biblical context first.

### Biblical Slavery vs. Slave Trading

We rightly abhor slavery. The 18th and 19th centuries saw unspeakable horrors—families torn apart, people treated as commodities, captains drowning enslaved people for insurance claims.

But biblical slavery was fundamentally different.

The Bible does not forbid slavery but explicitly forbids slave trading. Paul lists slave traders in the same category as murderers:

> The law is made not for the righteous but for lawbreakers and rebels, the ungodly and sinful, the unholy and irreligious; for those who kill their fathers or mothers, for murderers, for adulterers and perverts, for slave traders and liars and perjurers. (1 Timothy 1:9-10)

Slave trading—the kidnapping, buying, and selling of people—was punishable by death under Old Testament law (Exodus 21:16; Deuteronomy 24:7).

### Biblical Slaves Had Rights

Unlike Roman slavery or the transatlantic slave trade, biblical slavery operated under strict protections:

- Slaves could not be abused without consequence:

> If a man beats his male or female slave with a rod and the slave dies as a direct result, he must be punished. (Exodus 21:20)

- All creatures, including slaves, were granted rest:

> Six days do your work, but on the seventh day do not work, so that your ox and your donkey may rest and the slave born in your household, and the alien as well, may be refreshed. (Exodus 23:12)

- Hebrew slaves could not be enslaved permanently. The year of Jubilee freed them after seven years maximum.

Biblical slaves acquired property rights, conducted business on behalf of their masters, and functioned more like the faithful servant in Matthew 24:14-15 (given ten talents) or like Joseph in Egypt and Daniel in Babylon.

### Why Slavery Existed

Slavery in the ancient world served several functions:

- **Prisoners of war:** Without modern prisons, nations faced a choice: execute captives or enslave them. Lifelong bondage was arguably more merciful than execution.

- **Debt resolution:** Those who couldn't repay debts or who committed crimes had no bankruptcy protection. Bondage was a fair alternative to debtors' prisons or execution.

- **Voluntary poverty escape:** Some people sold themselves into slavery to escape poverty. Parents, in desperation, sold children—a cruel-sounding act today, but an act of mercy in contexts where they couldn't feed them.

### Bondage Today

We might not use the word "slavery," but bondage exists:

- Year 12 students signing scholarship commitments. Some accept cadetships, bonded for years to serve in specified roles.
- My cousin came from Malaysia to study medicine at Newcastle University. The Malaysian government funded her education in exchange for ten years of service upon graduation.
- HECS debt must be repaid.
- Phone contracts bind you for 24 months. The debt doesn't pay itself.

Teachers with mortgages and families can't simply walk away from uncertainty. Some work in fields where jobs are identical everywhere—changing jobs doesn't change the bondage.

**There's nothing inherently wrong with being in bondage.** It's a structure that exists for practical reasons.

### Submission Even When It's Hard

Peter's point is radical: we submit even when submission is difficult.

> Slaves, submit yourselves to your masters with all respect, not only to those who are good and considerate, but also to those who are harsh. (1 Peter 2:18)

We submit to the office, not to the person. We submit to a principal because of the office of Principal, not because they're nice or competent. We submit to governance structures because they exist, not because we approve of every decision.

This might seem weak, but it's actually powerful:

> For what is there to boast about if you receive a beating for doing wrong and endure it? But if you suffer for doing good and you endure it, this is commendable before God. (1 Peter 2:20)

Anyone can submit to good authorities. Pagans do it. Rebels do it when it suits them. But submission to harsh authority while maintaining integrity—that's distinctive. That's witnessed.

The office also removes the personal. You're not submitting because someone is a better person than you. This prevents bitterness from taking root.

### The Jesus Example

Peter points us to Christ:

> To this you were called, because Christ suffered for you, leaving you an example, that you should follow in his steps. He committed no sin, and no deceit was found in his mouth. When they hurled their insults at him, he did not retaliate; when he suffered, he made no threats. Instead, he entrusted himself to him who judges justly. He himself bore our sins in his body on the tree, so that we might die to sins and live for righteousness; by his wounds you have been healed. (1 Peter 2:21-24)

Jesus submitted to evil authorities without retaliation. He refused to do wrong and allowed them to deal with him as they saw fit. There are no qualifications in his example—no asterisks, no exceptions.

### Modern Application

Some of you have part-time jobs. If your employer is harsh, you might want to quit. But not everyone has that freedom. A worker with a mortgage and family can't simply change jobs at a whim.

The temptation is to make up for mistreatment through small rebellions:

- Stealing stationery to compensate for unpaid overtime.
- Malice in the workplace.
- Time wasted on social media (British employment research found workers spending over an hour daily on social platforms—more than an eighth of their working day).

Instead, respond with diligent hard work despite persecution. Be radical through honesty. Work over-time without complaint. Don't gossip about others. By doing so, you create opportunities to declare God's praise in your workplace.

### For Those Without Adult Freedom

If you live with parents who restrict your faith:

- You can't always attend church. Find other ways—ISCF meetings, for example.
- Submit in other ways: not just through study grades, but through character. That speaks louder than marks.
- Show generosity to siblings, respect to parents, good manners, a pleasant personality, ethical decisions, sensibility.
- Things will change. You'll become an adult and make your own decisions.

## The Alien Strategy: A Summary

When silenced by circumstance, the Christian's tool is **action**. Live good lives. Be morally upright. Trust that your deeds will silence accusers and create openings for conversation. When people ask about the hope you have, speak.

This isn't triumphalism. It's humility and submission. It's faith that God's purposes work through small, faithful lives lived with integrity in hostile environments.

**Be different by being morally upright. And when they do ask—speak.**
