# Ephesians 2:1-10

**Title:** The Problem of Entitlement
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction: First World Problems

A few years ago, a young woman at Connect created an artwork titled "First World Problems"—a perfect illustration of how we in wealthy nations take blessings for granted and feel entitled to what is actually luxury.

First world problems are those that only wealthy societies experience. We complain about technology inconveniences (forgotten earphones, slow laptops, broken treadmills), wealth-related frustrations (our gardener mowing too early, not enough dip for our chips), and luxury dilemmas (too many seeds in watermelon, not enough decaf soy lattes). We've even elevated this to life decisions: "Should I study Commerce/Law or Medicine?"

These are problems born from entitlement—the assumption that we deserve what much of the world considers extraordinary privilege.

Today's passage speaks directly to this problem of entitlement and calls us to a radically different way of living.

## Part One: My Work—The Problem of Deadness (verses 1-3)

> As for you, you were dead in your transgressions and sins, in which you used to live when you followed the ways of this world and of the ruler of the kingdom of the air, the spirit who is now at work in those who are disobedient. All of us also lived among them at one time, gratifying the cravings of our sinful nature and following its desires and thoughts. Like the rest, we were by nature objects of wrath.

Paul reminds us of our spiritual condition before Christ. We were dead—not merely sick or wounded, but completely dead. And this death was active; it shaped everything we did.

**Why is God angry?** Because we:
- **Broke His laws** (transgressions and sins)
- **Made our own laws** (followed the ways of this world)
- **Followed the ruler of darkness** (the prince of the power of the air)

Satan is real—a personified enemy who, though defeated in heaven's battle, was given temporary rule over the earth (Revelation 12:3-5). The "air" refers to the unseen realm between earth and heaven, the domain from which evil currently operates. The world systems we live in are under his influence.

**The result?** Left to our own devices, we naturally produce bad works. We don't do good naturally. Even when we intend to do good, we often do evil instead. We give out of guilt, offer token charity, pressure others into service, and rarely stop to consider whether our "good works" are actually good at all.

## Part Two: God's Work—The Reality of Grace (verses 4-8)

> But because of his great love for us, God, who is rich in mercy, made us alive with Christ even when we were dead in transgressions—it is by grace you have been saved. And God raised us up with Christ and seated us with him in the heavenly realms in Christ Jesus, in order that in the coming ages he might show the incomparable riches of his grace, expressed in his kindness to us in Christ Jesus. For it is by grace you have been saved, through faith—and this not from yourselves, it is the gift of God.

Here is the turning point. Despite our deadness, God acted. He didn't wait for us to get our act together. He demonstrated His great love and mercy by making us alive in Christ.

**The proper response to grace is thankfulness.** When we truly grasp what God has done—raising us from spiritual death and seating us with Christ in heavenly places—gratitude should overflow from us. We give, serve, and love not out of obligation but out of overflow.

But here's the danger: **we lose this motivation when we feel entitled.** The language of social justice, while sometimes helpful, can transform grace into obligation. It can make us feel we *owe* generosity rather than that we've been *given* grace to share. The bigger the gap we recognize between where we were (dead in sin) and where we are now (alive in Christ, seated in heavenly places), the more we appreciate what we've received—and the more naturally we respond with gratitude-driven service.

## Part Three: God's Workmanship—The Purpose of Good Works (verses 9-10)

> For we are God's workmanship, created in Christ Jesus to do good works, which God prepared in advance for us to do.

Paul uses the imagery of a craftsman. We are God's masterpiece—created with intention and skill, engineered for a specific purpose: to do good works.

**This calling is comprehensive.** It's not limited to certain spiritual activities or charitable moments. Good works encompass everything—parenting, household chores, employment, tutoring, giving, activism, befriending the lonely, carrying others' burdens. It includes both the dramatic (fighting real injustices like abortion, working for economic freedom) and the mundane (helping a neighbor, picking someone up for church, training in faithfulness).

The tragedy is that we often create a **hierarchy of generosity**—grand gestures once a year while ignoring daily faithfulness. We dream of building toilets in Africa while never caring enough to pick up a neighbor in the suburbs. We organize large food collections when distributing help locally would be simpler and more sustainable.

**Can we start at home?** Real generosity is trained through faithfulness in small things. Who cannot be generous for one day? That day becomes a pattern. That pattern becomes a lifetime.

## Living This Out

This passage sits within a series on social justice. Last week we studied Zaccheus—the call to identify with others and empathize. The Samaritan teaches us to love our neighbor. But knowing about justice and having empathy must lead somewhere: to actual good works.

We are created for this. God has prepared good works in advance for us to do. Not out of guilt. Not out of social pressure. Not with the entitlement that assumes the world owes us justice. But out of grateful hearts that remember: we were dead, and God made us alive. Everything we have and everything we do should flow from that grace.
