# Luke 15:11-32

**Title:** The Prodigal Son: Two Lost Brothers
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction

Jesus told a parable about two sons who became lost. Unlike those who survive through their own resourcefulness, these brothers face a different kind of danger. In this story about lostness and restoration, Jesus reveals something profound about what it means to be separated from our heavenly Father.

## 1. The Younger Brother

> "There was a man who had two sons. The younger one said to his father, 'Father, give me my share of the estate.' So he divided his property between them." — Luke 15:11-12

### Lost His Sonship

The younger son's request seems straightforward on the surface—children borrow from parents all the time. But look closely at what he's asking for: his inheritance. Inheritance comes when parents die, yet his father is still alive. By asking for his share now, the younger son is effectively telling his father to die so he can have his money. More profoundly, he's rejecting his identity as a son.

When the father liquidates his assets to give his son the wealth, something irreversible has happened. The son has chosen to step outside the family relationship.

### Lost His Wealth

> "Not long after that, the younger son got together all he had, set off for a distant country and there squandered his wealth in wild living." — Luke 15:13

The son doesn't simply leave home—he travels to a distant country, as far from his father as possible. Then he squanders everything through wild living. This wasn't careful stewardship or investment. This was reckless abandonment of his father's years of hard work. It's like watching a professional athlete or lottery winner waste millions through carelessness, unable to imagine that the money could run out.

By running away and spending without restraint, the younger son demonstrates his true feelings: his father means nothing to him.

### Lost in the Mud

> "After he had spent everything, there was a severe famine in that whole country, and he began to be in need." — Luke 15:14

The timing couldn't be worse. A famine strikes—the kind of economic catastrophe where money becomes impossibly valuable, inflation skyrockets, and jobs vanish. The younger son, who squandered his wealth, now has nothing when everything becomes expensive and scarce.

Worse still, he has no father to return to. He's lost his sonship, lost his wealth, and lost any safety net. Desperate and hungry, he hires himself out to feed pigs.

> "So he went and hired himself out to a citizen of that country, who sent him to his fields to feed pigs. He longed to fill his stomach with the pods that the pigs were eating, but no one gave him anything." — Luke 15:15-16

For any person this would be degrading work—mud, filth, and poverty. But for a Jewish son, it's ceremonially unclean. The younger son has descended so far that even the pigs are better fed than he is. Yet his suffering brings no relief. His work earns him nothing.

### Loses His Pride and Returns

At rock bottom, the younger son finally sees his situation clearly. He has sinned against his father. Repentance begins—the decision to admit wrong and change course.

He plans to return home, not as a son but as a hired servant. He's already lost his sonship; becoming a hired hand would at least provide food and shelter. But what happens when he arrives home shatters all his expectations.

Instead of anger or rejection, his father runs to him. The father embraces him, kisses him, and restores him completely—not to servanthood, but to sonship. He receives new clothes, a ring, and celebration. The father's compassion overflows before the son even finishes his prepared speech.

## Interlude: The Application to Us

This parable speaks to anyone who has run from God. Some of us have acted like the younger son: we've demanded God give us what we want and then lived selfishly, chasing money, fame, sex, or other desires. We've squandered our lives in wild living.

The call here is to repentance. Like the younger son, we need to recognize that all we have comes from our Father, and that running from Him leads to destruction. The shame we feel about returning—the fear of "I told you so"—is real. But Jesus is telling us that God's response is not what we expect. He runs toward us. He embraces us. He restores us completely.

## 2. The Older Brother

> "Meanwhile, the older son was in the field. When he came near the house, he heard music and dancing. So he called one of the servants and asked him what was going on. 'Your brother has come,' he replied, 'and your father has killed the fattened calf because he has him back safe and sound.'" — Luke 15:25-27

### Lost Sonship (Through Pride)

On the surface, the older brother is the "good son." He stayed home. He worked. He obeyed. Yet when he learns of the celebration for his wayward brother, he becomes angry and refuses to enter.

The father must leave the feast to plead with him. But notice the older son's response:

> "Look! All these years I've been slaving for you and never disobeyed your orders. Yet you never gave me even a young goat so I could celebrate with my friends. But when this son of yours who has squandered your property with prostitutes comes home, you kill the fattened calf for him!" — Luke 15:29-30

His tone is as disrespectful as his younger brother's was. He addresses his father not with "Father" but with "Look!"—aggressive and arrogant. He claims to be a slave rather than a son. He demands reward for obedience.

But here's the tragedy: the older brother is losing his sonship in the very act of trying to preserve it. He treats obedience as a transaction—labor exchanged for payment—rather than as the natural expression of a loving relationship with his father.

### Lost in Desire for Reward

The older son has misunderstood what it means to be a son. He obeys not out of love or respect, but to earn a reward. When his father doesn't pay him for his obedience, he feels cheated.

But consider what he actually possesses: he lives with his father. Everything the father owns will one day be his. As a son, he could have asked for anything—the fattened calf, a feast with friends, anything—and his father would have given it gladly. The father's wealth is already his inheritance.

The older son's error is fundamental: he's treating his relationship with his father as a job, when it's actually the greatest privilege imaginable. He's downgrading from sonship to hired servanthood—the same mistake his younger brother made, but approached from the opposite direction.

### Still Lost: The Open Ending

The parable ends without resolution. The older son faces a choice: he can enter the feast, accept his father's love, and celebrate his brother's return. Or he can remain outside in the darkness, bitter and alone.

Jesus leaves the ending open because He's offering His listeners—including us—the same choice.

## 3. The Father

The father in this parable reveals the character of God.

### Slow to Anger

When the younger son demands his inheritance—effectively telling his father to die—the father doesn't scold or punish. He gives the son what he asks for.

When the older son speaks arrogantly about being a slave and demands reward, the father again refuses to become angry. Instead, he gently tries to help his son understand why he had such compassion for the younger brother.

This father absorbs disrespect from both sons without retaliation. He is slow to anger—which is exactly what Psalm 103:8 tells us about God.

### Abounding in Love

The father's love is extravagant and immediate. When the younger son returns—dirty, humiliated, smelling of the pig sty—the father doesn't wait for explanations or apologies. He runs to his son, embraces him, kisses him, and immediately restores him to full sonship.

To the older brother, though he has failed in his heart to honor his father, the father still desires to leave him his entire inheritance. He still pleads with him to enter the celebration.

The father's love doesn't depend on earning it through obedience. It precedes everything. It's the foundation of everything.

## 4. Us: The Choice Before Us

This parable engages us directly. Jesus ends it without resolution because we are the ones who must decide.

### Which Brother Are You?

Some of us are like the younger son: we've run from God to live selfishly and wildly. We've squandered our lives on things that don't satisfy. We're broken and lost.

Others of us are like the older son: we've outwardly lived respectable lives, done good things, and followed the rules. But inwardly, we're bitter. We resent God. We think He owes us for our suffering or our goodness. We believe relationship is transactional.

Regardless of which we are, we are all sinners. Our relationship with God is broken—either by our deeds or by the condition of our hearts.

### God's Judgment

If we remain as we are, we face judgment. One day God will judge the living and the dead. Those who have not restored their relationship with Him will have nowhere to hide from His judgment.

### Restoration Through Jesus

There is only one way to restore our broken relationship with God: through Jesus Christ.

Jesus is the one who came looking for us—unlike the older brother in the parable who never searched for his lost sibling. Through His death on the cross, Jesus paid the price for all our sins. He restored our sonship to God.

All we must do is repent—to turn from our sin and return to God—and trust in Jesus. When God judges the world, those who have placed their faith in Jesus will be clothed from His judgment. They will be counted as God's children. They will inherit eternal life and celebrate at the heavenly feast.

### The Decision

Like the two sons in the parable, we face a choice today. Will you repent of living without God? Will you trust Jesus as your Lord and Savior? Will you return to your heavenly Father?

Jesus continues to wait for the lost to return. But one day He will stop waiting. He will return to His house, to the party for those who have come home. That celebration will begin whether or not everyone has returned.

The time to repent is now. The final famine is coming.
