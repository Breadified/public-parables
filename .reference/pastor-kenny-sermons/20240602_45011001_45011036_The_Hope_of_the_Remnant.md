# Romans 11:1-36

**Title:** The Hope of the Remnant
**Date:** June 2, 2024 (ARISE); Original: February 17, 2013 (SMAC)
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## The Central Question

Paul opens with an urgent question that resonates through the centuries: Has God rejected His people Israel? The context makes this question sting. In the preceding chapters, Paul has described Israel with devastating language—hardened hearts, spiritual blindness, broken branches cut from the tree of promise. After such a grim portrait, one might conclude that God's covenant with Israel has ended in permanent rejection.

But Paul's answer is decisive and clear: "By no means!"

## Part One: God Has Not Rejected All His People (11:1-11a)

### Paul Himself: A Living Witness

Paul's first argument is personal and powerful. He is a Jew—an Israelite, a descendant of Abraham, from the tribe of Benjamin. If God had completely rejected Israel, how could Paul himself exist as a believer? His very conversion proves that God has not abandoned His ancient people.

Paul was no ordinary opponent. He had been a fierce persecutor of the church:

> "For you have heard of my previous way of life in Judaism, how intensely I persecuted the church of God and tried to destroy it. I was advancing in Judaism beyond many of my own age among my people and was extremely zealous for the traditions of my fathers." (Galatians 1:13-14)

He was a Pharisee among Pharisees, a zealot who actively hunted Christians. Yet God showed him mercy. If God could save Paul—the chief of sinners against the gospel—then surely there is hope for all Israel.

### The Remnant: God's Faithful Few

Paul reaches back to the prophet Elijah for a second witness. Elijah lived in the darkest days of Israel's history, under the corrupt reign of King Ahab and Queen Jezebel. The nation had turned wholly to idolatry. The prophets of God had been hunted down and killed. The altars of the Lord were torn down. The false prophets of Baal and Asherah had become the official religion, receiving the king's patronage and protection.

Elijah stood alone. He felt utterly isolated—like a solitary man facing down tanks. In his despair, he cried out to God: "Lord, they have killed your prophets and torn down your altars. I am the only one left, and they are trying to kill me too" (Romans 11:3, quoting 1 Kings 19:10).

But God's response pierced through the darkness: "I have reserved for myself seven thousand who have not bowed the knee to Baal" (Romans 11:4).

In Israel's bleakest hour, when it seemed all was lost, God had preserved a faithful remnant. This is the critical word: *remnant* (Greek: *leimma*). It speaks of survivors, of a faithful core preserved by God's grace through the darkest times.

> "In that day the remnant of Israel, the survivors of Jacob, will no longer rely on him who struck them down but will truly rely on the Lord, the Holy One of Israel." (Isaiah 10:20-21)

Paul applies this ancient pattern to his own time. Though many Jews rejected Jesus, though the nation as a whole had hardened their hearts, God had not completely abandoned Israel. Just as in Elijah's day, God was preserving a remnant—Jewish believers who trusted in Jesus.

### Grace, Not Works

The existence of this remnant reveals the fundamental truth about God's character and saving work:

> "So too, at the present time there is a remnant chosen by grace. And if by grace, then it cannot be based on works; if it were, grace would no longer be grace." (Romans 11:5-6)

This is the beating heart of the gospel. Election is not based on human merit or achievement. It flows entirely from God's gracious choice. God doesn't choose the righteous; He chooses according to His own mysterious will. He may choose the good or the wicked, Jews or Gentiles. His selection is sovereign and free.

This is actually good news for us. If salvation depended on how good we are, we would all be hopeless. But since it depends entirely on God's mercy and grace, there is hope even for the worst of sinners.

### Application: Hope for the Hardened

This doctrine has a pastoral urgency. Many of us know people whose hearts seem permanently closed to the gospel—the stubborn atheist, the hostile skeptic, the relative who refuses to listen. We pray for them. We sometimes hint at spiritual truths, trying not to nag. We work and hope, caught between Calvinist theology and Arminian practicality.

But Paul offers us hope: As long as someone is alive, the story is not over. God has not given up on those with hard hearts. The example of Elijah shows that even in the worst circumstances, God preserves a faithful few. The example of Paul shows that even the fiercest enemies of Christ can become His apostles.

Consider how you came to faith. Who brought you? Often it was the most unlikely person—someone you didn't expect, someone you might have overlooked. Meanwhile, someone obvious and apparently well-suited did nothing.

## Part Two: God Is Still Working (11b-24)

### The Mystery of Apparent Tragedy

What troubles the apostle Paul deeply is this: How could the Jews—God's chosen people, the bearers of His covenant, the recipients of His promises—reject their Messiah? How could the very people who should have recognized Jesus ended up opposing Him most fiercely?

Paul himself experienced this tragedy personally. His own people rejected both him and his message. Time after time, he was thrown out of synagogues. His family likely rejected him. His fellow Jews became his primary antagonists. In Chapter 10, Paul expressed his anguish so intensely that he wished himself "cursed and cut off from Christ for the sake of my people, Israel" (Romans 9:3).

The situation felt hopeless. But Paul reveals that God was not trapped by this catastrophe. Even in the midst of apparent tragedy, God was executing His purposes.

### Rejection Brings Blessing to the Gentiles

> "Again I ask: Did they stumble so as to fall beyond recovery? Not at all! Rather, because of their transgression, salvation has come to the Gentiles to make Israel envious." (Romans 11:11)

This is the remarkable reversal: Because the Jews rejected the gospel, the Gentiles received it. Their loss became the world's gain.

> "But if their transgression means riches for the world, and their loss means riches for the Gentiles, how much greater riches will their fullness bring!" (Romans 11:12)

Paul himself became a living witness to this principle. After being driven out of Jewish synagogues, where could he go? To the Gentiles! He traveled throughout Asia Minor and into Europe, proclaiming Jesus to the very people who had no covenant history with God, no Torah, no temple worship. God hardened the hearts of the Jews not to destroy them, but to open the door for the Gentiles.

This reminds us of how God used Pharaoh in Egypt. Though Pharaoh was hardened in his rebellion, God used that very hardness to display His power and to liberate the Hebrew slaves. The instrument of judgment became the means of salvation for others.

### The Olive Tree Metaphor

Paul uses a striking image to explain the relationship between Jews and Gentiles in God's plan:

> "If some of the branches have been broken off, and you, though a wild olive shoot, have been grafted in among the others and now share in the nourishing sap from the olive root, do not boast over those branches." (Romans 11:17-18)

The natural branches—Israel—were broken off because of unbelief. The wild branches—the Gentiles—were grafted into the cultivated tree through faith. This is a remarkable reversal of nature. Normally, you graft cultivated wood into a wild tree, not the other way around. But God does what seems impossible.

Yet Paul issues a stern warning to the Gentile believers:

> "Do not be arrogant, but be afraid. For if God did not spare the natural branches, he will not spare you either." (Romans 11:20-21)

You stand by faith, not by nature. Your position is as precarious as the Jews'. If you fall into pride and unbelief, you too can be cut off. The only ground of security is humble faith in God's grace.

> "Consider therefore the kindness and sternness of God: sternness to those who fell, but kindness to you, provided that you continue in his kindness. Otherwise, you also will be cut off." (Romans 11:22)

God's severity is real. His grace is real. The Gentiles have received mercy because the Jews stumbled, but this mercy is not unconditional. It demands continued faith and humility.

### The Chef's Masterpiece

There's a profound theological principle here that applies far beyond this passage: God is sovereign over all circumstances, even negative ones. He uses punishment, suffering, and seeming disaster as ingredients in His grand design. Nothing is wasted. Even our falls and failures can serve His purposes.

It's like a master chef using every ingredient—even the bitter and sour—to create a magnificent dish. God has incorporated even the hardness of Jewish hearts and the stumbling of the nation into His plan to bring salvation to all peoples.

## Part Three: God Works Toward Jewish Salvation (23-27)

### God Has Not Forgotten Israel

Yet here is the surprise that validates Paul's original thesis: Even though Israel stumbled, even though most rejected Jesus, God has not forgotten them. His plan includes their salvation too.

> "And if they do not persist in unbelief, they will be grafted in, for God is able to graft them back in." (Romans 11:23)

This is the turning point. What seems like abandonment is actually a temporary hardening. The branches that were broken off can be grafted back in. Their rejection is not permanent.

### The Mystery of "All Israel"

Paul culminates his argument with one of Scripture's most debated verses:

> "And in this way all Israel will be saved, as it is written: 'The deliverer will come from Zion; he will turn godlessness away from Jacob. And this is my covenant with them when I take away their sins.'" (Romans 11:26-27)

What does "all Israel" mean? Interpreters offer various options:

**Option 1: Every Jewish person who ever lived** would suggest a mass conversion of the entire nation at a future time. But this contradicts Paul's earlier teaching that "not all who are descended from Israel are Israel" (Romans 9:6). If individual unbelief excludes some from true Israel, then not every ethnic Jew will necessarily be saved.

**Option 2: All the elect—both Jewish and Gentile believers** constitute the true Israel. This reading treats "Israel" in verse 26 as spiritual Israel (the church), just as Paul uses it in Galatians 6:16. The promise is that all God's chosen people, Jewish and Gentile alike, will be saved.

**Option 3: All elect Jews throughout history** will be saved. The word "all" refers not to a moment in time but to the complete, final gathering of all Jewish believers across the ages. The "and so" (kai houtos) indicates the manner and means—through jealousy, through the success of Gentile salvation, through eventually seeing God's work among the nations.

Whatever the precise interpretation, the fundamental point is clear: Jewish salvation is part of God's unfolding plan. The pattern Paul describes is: Jewish rejection → Gentile acceptance → Gentile success provokes Jewish jealousy → Jewish acceptance. The narrative arc bends ultimately toward inclusion, not exclusion.

### The Immutability of God's Gifts

Paul concludes this section with a statement of profound comfort:

> "As far as the gospel is concerned, they are enemies on your account; but as far as election is concerned, they are loved on account of the patriarchs, for God's gifts and his call are irrevocable." (Romans 11:28-29)

This is one of the most important verses in Scripture. God's promises to Israel were made to Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob. Those promises cannot be revoked. God does not take back His gifts. His calling is irrevocable.

The Jews may currently be enemies of the gospel. But they remain the beloved of God because of the patriarchs. God's covenantal faithfulness does not depend on current behavior. It depends on His immutable character.

## The Conclusion: Predestination Without Despair (11:28-36)

### A Paradox Resolved

We arrive now at the deep mystery that has troubled many: If God has predetermined all things, including who will believe and who will be hardened, doesn't this eliminate human responsibility? Doesn't this lead to despair?

Paul addresses this head-on, not by solving the paradox but by embracing it:

> "For God has bound everyone over to disobedience so that he may have mercy on them all." (Romans 11:32)

Even through disobedience, God's mercy flows. Even through rebellion, His purposes advance. This isn't a problem to be solved but a mystery to be marveled at.

Consider how this actually worked out. Throughout history, we see God's purposes unfolding in surprising ways:

In 1917, amid World War I, the British declared their support for a Jewish homeland in Palestine. The Balfour Declaration emerged partly from Christian Zionist theology—the belief that God had promised the land to the Jews and that Christians should facilitate their return. This was driven by interpretations of Romans 11 and other passages suggesting a future, grand restoration of Israel.

Whether one agrees with Christian Zionism or not, the point is this: People who read Romans 11 saw in it the promise of Jewish restoration and acted accordingly. God's stated purposes for Israel produced real historical effects.

But we must be humble about predicting God's future. We see His wisdom only in hindsight. What seemed like complete rejection of Israel actually served the purpose of bringing salvation to the Gentiles. What seemed like disaster was preparation for restoration.

### The Ultimate Mystery

Paul concludes with a doxology that acknowledges the limits of human understanding:

> "Oh, the depth of the riches of the wisdom and knowledge of God! How unsearchable his judgments, and his paths beyond tracing out! Who has known the mind of the Lord? Or who has been his counselor? Who has ever given to God, that God should repay them? For from him and through him and to him are all things. To him be the glory forever! Amen." (Romans 11:33-36)

This is the appropriate posture before God's sovereignty. Not despair. Not passive resignation. But wonder, humility, and worship.

God's wisdom is fathomless. His judgments are unsearchable. His ways are untraceable. No one can instruct God. No one can put Him in debt. Everything—absolutely everything—comes from Him, through Him, and for Him.

## Final Application: Living as God's Witnesses

For those of us who believe, this chapter teaches us something vital about our role in God's plan. We are not accidents. We are not random believers. We stand where we stand by God's gracious choice.

Gentiles: You were once far from the covenants, without the law, without the temple, without the promises. Yet God grafted you in. This is astonishing mercy. Live accordingly. Live with humility, not arrogance. Remember where you came from. Your faith is the means by which Jewish people will see God's grace and be provoked to jealousy.

Don't assume that hardened hearts are hopeless. God used the hardness of Jewish hearts to open doors for you. He can use your faithful witness to open hearts again. The story isn't over. As long as people live, there is hope. God has not abandoned anyone He loves.

Live in such a way that those around you—especially those who seem most resistant to the gospel—will see something beautiful in Christ. Your joy, your love, your integrity, your hope in the face of suffering—these things become invitations. Not arguments, but invitations.

And remember: predestination is not an excuse for passivity. The fact that God has chosen and called means we should work and pray with all urgency. God's sovereignty and human responsibility are not opposites. They are two sides of the same reality. God's predestination secures the outcome while our faithful effort becomes the very means by which He accomplishes His will.
