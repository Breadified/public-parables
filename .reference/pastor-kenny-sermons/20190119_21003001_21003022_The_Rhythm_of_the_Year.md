# Ecclesiastes 3:1-22

**Title:** The Rhythm of the Year  
**Date:** January 19, 2019  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Timing Is Everything

The difference between success and failure often comes down to one thing: doing the right thing at the right time.

Consider Bill Gates. He's a billionaire not merely because he's brilliant or ambitious—plenty of people share those qualities. Malcolm Gladwell's research in *Outliers* reveals that Gates likely wouldn't have founded Microsoft if he hadn't been born in 1955. That timing proved critical: he was old enough to recognize the opportunities emerging with the personal computer revolution, yet young enough to take the leap. This pattern repeats across Silicon Valley's pioneers—Steve Jobs (1955), Paul Allen (1953), Bill Joy and Scott McNealy (1954)—all positioned perfectly at the intersection of talent and opportunity.

This principle extends everywhere: knowing when to buy or sell determines business success; arriving at the right moment makes the difference between passing and failing an exam; shopping at the right time means catching sushi at discount prices.

The Teacher in Ecclesiastes grasps this deeply. Solomon wants to tell us about doing the right things at the right time. But his message goes far deeper than mere productivity hacks.

## Part One: Time Under the Sun Is Cyclical (3:1-13)

> There is a time for everything,
> and a season for every activity
> under the heavens.

Solomon then paints life with alternating seasons:

> A time to be born and a time to die,
> a time to plant and a time to uproot,
> a time to kill and a time to heal,
> a time to tear down and a time to build,
> a time to weep and a time to laugh,
> a time to mourn and a time to dance,
> a time to scatter stones and a time to gather them,
> a time to embrace and a time to refrain from embracing,
> a time to search and a time to give up,
> a time to keep and a time to throw away,
> a time to tear and a time to mend,
> a time to be silent and a time to speak,
> a time to love and a time to hate,
> a time for war and a time for peace.

This reflects an agricultural society where rhythm and repetition govern life. Plant in spring, harvest in fall. Birth comes, then death. Joy follows sorrow. Each season has its proper activity, and life feels cyclical—wheels turning, seasons returning, patterns repeating endlessly.

We recognize this rhythm intuitively. Certain times of year trigger familiar activities: back-to-school season means shopping for uniforms; Easter brings special services and holidays; term after term after term rolls past. Drought gives way to rain. Autumn becomes winter. The pattern is predictable, inevitable, and—as the Teacher suggests—burdensome.

The Teacher's assessment is stark:

> What do workers gain from their toil?
> I have seen the burden God has laid on the human race.

Life's endless cycles feel like a burden. For those under 35, this may seem exciting—new courses, new jobs, new relationships, new homes. But the Teacher warns: it levels out. The cycles that once felt like building eventually become cycles of decline. The house ages. The marriage loses its spark. Work drains energy. Medical appointments accumulate.

Consider what awaits:

- **Students**: Another term, another grind, another lunch packed, another piece of homework, thirteen years of it.
- **University attendees**: Another class, another group assignment with unmotivated teammates, another mid-semester rush, another set of exams. Six to ten semesters. Sixty thousand dollars in debt.
- **Workers**: Fifty years of waking up, grabbing coffee, catching the train. The same cycle, daily.
- **Parents**: Another nappy, another feeding, another doctor's appointment, another argument. Food, nappy, food, nappy, food, nappy. Even holidays—you do the same thing in a different place.
- **Retirees**: Swimming, breakfast, durian shopping, nap, cleaning, dinner, a movie, sleep. Repeat.

The cycle of franchises—another Marvel movie, another *Star Wars* film. Are you bored yet?

Life seems no better than that of animals:

> As for humans, God tests them so that they may see that they are like the animals.

Consider the chicken you ate last night. Or the bacon this morning. Raised to be used and disposed of. Born from dust, returning to dust. Restart.

> Surely the fate of human beings is like that of the animals; the same fate awaits them both: As one dies, so dies the other. All have the same breath; humans have no advantage over animals. Everything is meaningless. All go to the same place; all come from dust, and to dust all return.

No wonder some religions see escape as the only hope. Buddhism and Hinduism recognize these cyclical patterns and conclude we're trapped in endless reincarnation—the same lives repeating over and over, like the film *Groundhog Day*. Their answer: Nirvana, literally "blown out," extinguished—escape through non-existence. Better to not exist than to endure the cycle.

Yet the Teacher isn't fatalistic:

> So I saw that there is nothing better for a person than to enjoy their work, because that is their lot. For who can bring them to see what will happen after them?

How do we hold these two truths together—identifying with animals and futility on one hand, yet enjoining enjoyment on the other?

### Three Ways People Have Read Ecclesiastes

**First interpretation: Thoroughly negative.** Life is profitless and absurd. Enjoy it while you can because old age comes quickly and everything is meaningless. This reading dismisses positive passages as later additions. The conclusion: eat, drink, and be merry, for tomorrow you die. But this sounds dangerously like hedonism—the lifestyle of those who reject resurrection and God's judgment.

**Second interpretation: Find meaning in small things.** Yes, life is cyclical and temporary, but find satisfaction in the little joys: praise from your boss, a pay raise, beautiful sunsets, good coffee, holidays. Make your own meaning. But this is logically weak. How can temporary, fleeting things provide real meaning? A job becomes meaningless the day your position is made redundant. This approach makes meaning subjective and fragile.

**Third interpretation: Accept that life under the sun offers no permanent satisfaction.** The Teacher's conclusion becomes clear:

> Meaningless! Meaningless! says the Teacher.
> Everything is meaningless!
> 
> Now all has been heard;
> here is the conclusion of the matter:
> Fear God and keep his commandments,
> for this is the duty of all mankind.

Life isn't about finding meaning, satisfaction, or the perfect fitting-together of your life story. Rather, in light of our mortality and the nature of the world—in light of everything being temporary—we should worry less about meaning and more about obedience.

If you could choose one epitaph: "I found satisfaction" or "I obeyed God"—the Teacher urges you to pick the second. The first is an illusion we chase endlessly, always escalating, always seeking something more.

## Part Two: Time Under the Sun Is Linear (3:14-22)

But there's something else the Teacher wants us to see:

> He has made everything beautiful in its time. He has also set eternity in the hearts of men; yet no one can fathom what God has done from beginning to end.

Time is linear, not circular. A line has a beginning and an end. A circle does not. This fundamentally changes everything.

God is not Buddhist. Life is not an endless cycle of rebirth and death with no purpose. There is an end—a final accounting:

> I know that everything God does will endure forever; nothing can be added to it and nothing taken from it. God does it so that people will fear him.

And at that end comes judgment:

> I said to myself,
> God will bring into judgment
> both the righteous and the wicked,
> for there will be a time for every activity,
> a time to judge every deed.

The fact that judgment day exists makes time purposeful and linear. It drives toward an end. Like the HSC exam—you think it's terrible while you're in it, but it's merciful because it ends. There's finality. An end point.

This linear view isn't nihilistic. Rather, it's merciful in the deepest sense: something awaits after judgment. There's a future, even if we can't predict it.

As Peter reminds us:

> With the Lord a day is like a thousand years, and a thousand years are like a day. The Lord is not slow in keeping his promise, as some understand slowness. He is patient with you, not wanting anyone to perish, but everyone to come to repentance. But the day of the Lord will come like a thief. The heavens will disappear with a roar; the elements will be destroyed by fire, and the earth and everything in it will be laid bare.

The slowness of God doesn't mean life is cyclical and meaningless. It means God is patient—with us, with all His elect. He's giving time for repentance.

## Part Three: Living According to the Times

So how should we live?

First, recognize that **everything is beautiful in its time**, even the mundane:

> I know that there is nothing better for people than to be happy and to do good while they live.

Not because each moment contains inherent meaning. The monotony of another hour, another nappy, another meal cleanup—these aren't meaningful in themselves. But they're *beautiful* because they're the right action at the right time.

We're not animals. We have something they lack:

> He has also set eternity in the human hearts.

We sense time. We know there's a future, even if we can't fathom it completely. That changes everything.

Our error is thinking the cyclical is wrong or should be avoided. We become discontent with our season:

- When we're students, we want to be adults. When we're adults, we want to be children again. Young people delay leaving home; governments celebrate extending benefits to age 26.
- When we're young, we want to marry. Once married, we want to be single. Once children arrive, we want our freedom back.
- We want careers when it's time for children; children when careers are done and it's too late. (The proper order, if you want both, is children first.)
- We want to hang with kids when working; want to sleep when with kids; want to work when sleeping.

> They are like children sitting in the marketplace and calling out to each other: "We played the pipe for you, and you did not dance; we sang a dirge, and you did not cry."

We're chronically dissatisfied with our season. The grass always looks greener on the other side.

### A Personal Illustration

A few months ago, one of my children got sick with vomiting. I'd just gone to sleep after a late night of work, hoping for deep rest. I'd reached that sweet spot of happy tiredness. Then: a cough, cries. I tried ignoring it, but the cries grew louder. I dragged myself up into the cold darkness, stepped on the vomit he'd coughed up. I felt anger rise as I turned on the light—it was everywhere. The cot, the floor, his clothes.

I knew the drill: collect the pieces, remove soiled sheets and clothes, put them in the wash, wipe everything down, shower him, fresh clothes, remake the cot, get him back to sleep. An hour of my carefully planned evening—gone. How would I get that hour back? How would I fall asleep again? I don't need this. Why am I doing this?

But then I remembered Ecclesiastes:

> I know that there is nothing better for people than to be happy and to do good while they live. That each of them may eat and drink, and find satisfaction in all their toil—this is the gift of God.

It wasn't about finding meaning in vomit. Vomit is vomit. But here was a time to obey God—a father caring for his child, loving her, cleaning up after her, serving her, ensuring she wasn't distressed. I sat with her a while longer. She was wide awake now, needing comfort. We talked. This was a good time. This was the right action at the right time.

### Specific Seasons and Their Callings

**If you're a student:** Study hard—not for ultimate meaning, but because this is the time to learn. Every person must learn how to live in society, whether through formal schooling, trade work, or reading. Children learn from adults and become the next productive, godly generation. Do good in this season.

**If you're without work:** Your work is finding work. Treat it like a 9-to-5 job. Start at 9 AM in the library with newspapers and job sites. Tailor your resumes. Spend the afternoon applying and visiting workplaces. If ideal jobs don't want you, don't panic—build experience. Work your way up. God wants us to carry our own burden and help others. You can't do that while dependent.

**If you're a parent:** Parenting is mundane. Changing nappies, cooking meals, managing craft projects that accumulate endlessly. You keep a few drawings and chuck the rest. It's poo and wee and petty daily concerns. But this is the time to love your child, to serve her, to build her into a godly person. Do good in this season.

## Part Four: The Deeper Logic

The wisdom of Ecclesiastes associates the value of human life not with permanent accomplishment or material gain, but with **life as a gift from God**—irrespective of what we accomplish:

- Work itself isn't the source of meaning; God is
- Our task isn't satisfaction but obedience
- We can't see the entire picture, so we trust and follow

> To the man who pleases God, he gives wisdom, knowledge and happiness, but to the sinner he gives the task of gathering and storing up wealth to hand it over to the one who pleases God.

There's no way to determine from material success who God finds pleasing. Solomon himself—given wisdom, knowledge, and happiness—ultimately lost it all to his successors. Even success doesn't guarantee meaning or permanence.

---

## Closing

Today, whatever season you're in, do good and see good.

**Fear God and obey His commandments.** Follow His timing.

When life feels cyclical and monotonous, remember: time is linear. Judgment comes. An end approaches. This present world drives toward a finish line.

The slowness you perceive? That's God's patience, giving time for repentance.

Your calling now? **Do good.** Serve your season. Love your family. Work diligently. Encourage your brothers and sisters in Christ. Be willing to share your struggles and pray for one another.

Write the script of your life in pencil for the future—but what happens today is written in pen. Make sure it records that you are doing good and seeing good.
