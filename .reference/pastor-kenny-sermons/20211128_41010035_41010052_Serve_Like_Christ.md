# Mark 10:35-52

**Title:** Serve Like Christ  
**Date:** November 28, 2021  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Opening: The Human Desire for Glory

We have much to celebrate this morning—people stepping into new seasons. Some of you have found new jobs this past week. Others are starting families. Congratulations on these steps forward.

But our passage today addresses something deeply human: the desire for glory and recognition. We see it everywhere in our culture—YouTube stars, Instagram influencers, the endless pursuit of likes and acknowledgment. We all carry an innate need to feel honored, to know that we have something valuable to offer the world. This is beautifully normal. Even presentation nights and graduation ceremonies reflect this legitimate human need to be recognized for our work.

James and John, the sons of Zebedee, share this need. Their story becomes a window into how Jesus reshapes our understanding of what true greatness looks like.

---

## 1. Right and Left: The Misplaced Request (10:35-41)

> 35 Then James and John, the sons of Zebedee, came to him. "Teacher," they said, "we want you to do for us whatever we ask."
>
> 36 "What do you want me to do for you?" he asked.
>
> 37 They replied, "Let one of us sit at your right and the other at your left in your glory."

To understand this request, we need context. Throughout Mark 9-11, Jesus has been answering a central question: Who is Jesus, and what has he come to do? James and John's plea reveals three interwoven longings.

**Perhaps recognition.** The word "glory" here is the Greek *doxa*, but it echoes the Hebrew *kavod*—heaviness, weight, like medals worn on a soldier's chest. To sit at Jesus's right and left is to be honored, to be seen as important. When Jesus speaks elsewhere of glory—"the Son of Man will come in his Father's glory with the holy angels" (Mark 8:38)—glory and honor are inseparable. James and John want prestige.

**Perhaps power.** The right and left positions are seats of influence. In ancient courts, sitting to the king's right or left meant having his ear, wielding influence. Think of a vice president or deputy prime minister—positions that confer authority and access. They want power.

**Perhaps comfort.** Notice the word "sit." After three years of following Jesus through the desert, walking dusty roads, they're tired. They want to ascend the hierarchy, to be more comfortable. They're thinking about retirement planning—a well-earned rest at the top.

Likely, all three desires intertwine. And so they make their request, jockeying for position before the race is even won.

But Jesus responds with a sobering question:

> 38 "You don't know what you are asking," Jesus said. "Can you drink the cup I drink or be baptized with the baptism I am baptized with?"
>
> 39 "We can," they answered confidently.
>
> Jesus said to them, "You will drink the cup I drink and be baptized with the baptism I am baptized with,"

**Here is their critical mistake: they are counting too soon.** They want glory without grasping that glory comes *after* suffering.

The "cup" Jesus speaks of is the cup of God's wrath—the cup he would drink in Gethsemane, crying out to the Father: "Take this cup from me. Yet not what I will, but what you will" (Mark 14:36). To drink his cup means identification with his suffering. The "baptism" is not water but judgment—a sign that Jesus is walking into death itself, bearing the sins of the world, becoming the suffering servant.

This is what they would be called to share. Not a coronation, but a crucifixion. Not comfort, but identifying with Christ's sacrifice.

Their error is timing—like making retirement plans before you've worked, or booking overseas trips as yet another COVID variant emerges. The restaurant doesn't let you pay at the beginning of the meal; you serve first, then receive your reward. This is the order of all kingdom work: persevere first, then receive your reward. It's too soon to count.

When the other ten disciples hear about James and John's request, indignation spreads through their ranks. Everyone begins jockeying for position.

But Jesus clarifies something crucial in verse 40:

> 40 but to sit at my right or left is not for me to grant. These places belong to those for whom they have been prepared.

This seems puzzling at first. How can Jesus, the Son of God, not grant positions in his kingdom? Doesn't the Father give him authority? Yet throughout Scripture, Jesus chooses—he appoints the Twelve, he selects his leaders. The tension resolves when we read the Greek carefully. The phrase includes "except" (often dropped in translation): "It is not for me to give, *except* to those for whom it has been prepared."

In other words: I will give these positions—but only to those for whom they have been prepared. Jesus is not abdicating his authority. He is saying he grants glory only to those whose lives demonstrate they understand what following him truly costs.

**Here is the great comfort:** Jesus remembers. He's not playing politics or forgetting those who serve quietly. His eyes are attentive; his ears are open. The problem won't be with God. The problem will be with us—whether we're willing to serve first and trust that he hasn't forgotten to reward faithfulness.

## 2. Top and Bottom: Christian Glory Inverts the World's (10:42-45; 10:32-34)

> 42 Jesus called them together and said, "You know that those who are regarded as rulers of the Gentiles lord it over them, and their high officials exercise authority over them. 43 Not so with you. Instead, whoever wants to become great among you must be your servant, 44 and whoever wants to be first must be slave of all.

Here, Jesus inverts the world's entire value system. In the world's kingdom, greatness flows from power—from lording it over others, from making decisions that others must obey. But in Christ's kingdom, greatness is redefined entirely. To be great is to be a servant. To be first is to be a slave of all.

This teaching has echoed through history in unexpected ways. In modern management theory, there is something called "servant leadership"—a philosophy where the leader exists to serve the needs of those they lead, sharing power and helping people flourish. Robert Greenleaf formally proposed this concept in 1970. But here's what fascinates me: servant leadership wasn't invented in 1970.

Look at verse 45:

> 45 For even the Son of Man did not come to be served, but to serve, and to give his life as a ransom for many.

Jesus established this pattern twenty centuries before Greenleaf. In fact, when you trace servant leadership backward, you discover that Greenleaf was influenced by Hermann Hesse's novel on servanthood. And Hesse? He was shaped by his parents, who were Christian missionaries in India. And they learned it from the very Jesus we're studying today.

The world has unknowingly borrowed Christ's kingdom upside down. Where the world says "lord it over," Christ says "serve." Where the world seeks titles and honor, Christ's followers take responsibility for the welfare of others. The world still carries an authoritarian streak—those at the top command, those at the bottom obey. But in Christ, the first serves the last.

To understand why Jesus does this, we must see the broader context. Just before James and John's request, Jesus has told his disciples plainly what lies ahead:

> 32 They were on their way up to Jerusalem, with Jesus leading the way, and the disciples were astonished, while those who followed were afraid. Again he took the Twelve aside and told them what was going to happen to him.
>
> 33 "We are going up to Jerusalem," he said, "and the Son of Man will be delivered over to the chief priests and the teachers of the law. They will condemn him to death and will hand him over to the Gentiles, 34 who will mock him and spit on him, flog him and kill him. Three days later he will rise."

Jesus is walking toward his own death—preparing the way alone, so that God might forgive mankind and open a way for sinners to come home. This is the preparation. This is why he redefines greatness: he is about to demonstrate it through suffering.

As we look at our own churches and communities, let us ask: How are we serving? Leadership doesn't mean grabbing the highest seat. It means asking: "What needs to be done? How can I help?" Even in our voluntary organizations, every role matters. If you don't yet have an answer to how you're serving, why not write it on a comment card? We have big tasks and small ones. If you're willing to help, we need you.

## 3. The Last Will Be First (10:46-52; see Mark 8:22-27)

The section of Mark spanning chapters 9-11 opens with one healing and closes with another—two blind men restored to sight. The first appears unnamed:

> 8:22 They came to Bethsaida, and some people brought a blind man and begged Jesus to touch him. 23 He took the blind man by the hand and led him outside the village. When he had spit on the man's eyes and put his hands on him, Jesus asked, "Do you see anything?" 24 He looked up and said, "I see people; they look like trees walking around."
>
> 25 Once more Jesus put his hands on the man's eyes. Then his eyes were opened, his sight was restored, and he saw everything clearly.
>
> 27 Jesus and his disciples went on to the villages around Caesarea Philippi. On the way he asked them, "Who do people say I am?"

Notice what follows: the disciples' confession that Jesus is the Messiah. But they don't yet grasp what it means. Following Christ in glory is one thing; following him in suffering is altogether harder.

Now, at the close of this section, we meet Bartimaeus:

> 46 Then they came to Jericho. As Jesus and his disciples, together with a large crowd, were leaving the city, a blind man, Bartimaeus (which means "son of Timaeus"), was sitting by the roadside begging. 47 When he heard that it was Jesus of Nazareth, he began to shout, "Jesus, Son of David, have mercy on me!"

Jericho is the last city before the promised land—the threshold where Joshua once led God's people across into their inheritance. Now Jesus, whose name in Greek is the same as the Hebrew Joshua, is leading his disciples toward Jerusalem, toward his own death and resurrection.

And here sits Bartimaeus—whose name means "son of honor"—begging on the roadside. But notice what he understands: he calls out "Son of David!" This is a messianic title. According to Mark, Bartimaeus offers the first public acknowledgment of Jesus as Christ, following only Peter's private confession in chapter 8.

But immediately:

> 48 Many rebuked him and told him to be quiet,

The crowd tells him to silence himself. You are last. You are nothing. Everyone wants a piece of Jesus—brands seeking his endorsement, people hoping he'll make them influencers. There's no room for a blind beggar in this procession. But Bartimaeus persists:

> but he shouted all the more, "Son of David, have mercy on me!"
>
> 49 Jesus stopped and said, "Call him."

Jesus hears his voice over the din. Bartimaeus called out to Jesus, and now Jesus calls him. This is the pattern of grace: we cry out, and he responds.

> 49b So they called to the blind man, "Cheer up! On your feet! He's calling you." 50 Throwing his cloak aside, he jumped to his feet and came to Jesus.

That cloak is significant—it's his begging mat, his collection cloth, all he owns. He throws it aside without hesitation to reach Jesus. He follows the voice he cannot see with his eyes.

> 51 "What do you want me to do for you?" Jesus asked him.
>
> The blind man said, "Rabbi, I want to see."
>
> 52 "Go," said Jesus, "your faith has healed you." Immediately he received his sight and followed Jesus along the road.

Here is the stunning contrast: James and John ask Jesus what they want *before* they're willing to follow. They negotiate terms—position, power, comfort. But Bartimaeus, the blind beggar, asks only to see. And when sight is given, he doesn't ask about salary or title or where he'll sit. He simply follows Jesus along the road.

What road? The road to Jerusalem. The road to suffering. The road to death. The road where one identifies with the suffering servant, drinking the cup, bearing the baptism of judgment.

**The blind man sees what the Twelve do not.** He understands that following Jesus is its own reward. The last, the outcast, the one pushed to the margins—he becomes the model of true faith. He walks with Jesus as surely as if he sat at his right hand.

And so we are invited to walk this same road. Not to seek titles or positions. Not to negotiate before we follow. But to trust that Jesus has not forgotten us, that he sees us, that he is leading us somewhere worthy of all we will sacrifice along the way.

---

## Conclusion

It's not wrong to desire glory. We will receive it—when we rest in eternity, when we sit at the left and right in his presence. But the path there runs through service, not through seeking.

**The last will be first, but for now the first must be last.**

We stand where Bartimaeus stood—at the threshold, at Jericho, the place between judgment and promise. Jesus is on the cusp of his suffering, preparing to enter Jerusalem knowing what awaits him there. He is leading his disciples into certain death.

And he invites you in.

Will you join him?
