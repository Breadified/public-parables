# Luke 18:1-17

**Title:** How to Adult: Persevere in Seeking Justice
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction: Justice in a Messy World

We live in complicated times. Politics is messy because life is messy. People are not black and white—not all good, not all evil. The world isn't as rosy as we once thought it was. Yet we deeply desire things to be black and white, which is why we seek justice.

Jesus speaks to this longing through two parables about people seeking something from authority figures. In doing so, he teaches us how to navigate adulthood with both persistence and humility.

## 1. Asking for Justice (Luke 18:1-8)

### The Widow's Persistence

> And there was a widow in that town who kept coming to him with the plea, "Grant me justice against my adversary." (v. 3)

A widow in Bible times was perhaps the most vulnerable person in society. Without a husband for protection and support, she was powerless and likely poor. Yet her vulnerability doesn't prevent her from seeking justice. She "kept coming" to the judge, pleading persistently for her case. She was, as Kenny puts it, "praysistent"—persistent in her prayer.

We don't know what injustice she suffered. Perhaps someone killed her husband, robbed her, or exploited her vulnerability. But her relentless pursuit of justice is clear.

### The Unjust Judge

> In a certain town there was a judge who neither feared God nor cared about men. (v. 2)

If the widow represents one end of the power spectrum, the judge represents the other. In ancient times, a judge wasn't simply a legal official in robes—he was the ruler of that place, the government itself. Judges were meant to use their authority for the good of citizens.

But this judge was corrupt in the most fundamental way: he did not fear God. This is significant. A judge who fears God answers to a higher authority and knows he will be judged for his decisions. But this judge feared no one. He cared nothing for people's suffering. His only concern was avoiding trouble for himself.

> Consider carefully what you do, because you are not judging for man but for the LORD, who is with you whenever you give a verdict. Now let the fear of the LORD be upon you. Judge carefully, for with the LORD our God there is no injustice or partiality or bribery. (2 Chronicles 19:6-7)

This is why those in positions of authority—judges, officials, leaders—must fear God. Without accountability to God, they become accountable to no one. And when rulers answer to no higher power, corruption follows.

### The Judge's Surprising Response

> For some time he refused. But finally he said to himself, "Even though I don't fear God or care about men, yet because this widow keeps bothering me, I will see that she gets justice, so that she won't eventually wear me out with her coming!" (v. 4-5)

The widow's persistence pays off. Though the judge cares nothing for her or her plight, he grows weary of her constant pleas. Perhaps there's something to learn here: sometimes persistence in seeking justice from earthly authorities requires making ourselves "heard," even through discomfort.

But this parable is about more than lobbying tactics. It reveals the relationship between God, justice, and us.

### Keep Seeking Justice

> And the Lord said, "Listen to what the unjust judge says. And will not God bring about justice for his chosen ones, who cry out to him day and night? Will he keep putting them off? I tell you, he will see that they get justice, and quickly." (v. 6-8)

This is a "how much more" argument. If an unjust judge who cares nothing for the widow will grant her justice, how much more will God—who is perfectly just and loves his chosen ones—bring about justice?

When we encounter injustice, we must respond actively and passively:

**Actively:** Keep seeking justice. Plead with authorities. Call out wrongs. Cry out to God.

**Passively:** Be patient. Trust that God is a God of justice and that he will act.

> Yet the LORD longs to be gracious to you; therefore he will rise up to show you compassion. For the LORD is a God of justice. Blessed are all who wait for him! (Isaiah 30:18)

### Understanding True Justice

As we mature into adulthood, we must distinguish between real justice and the symptoms of a broken world.

True justice punishes evil and rewards righteousness:

> For the one in authority is God's servant for your good. But if you do wrong, be afraid, for rulers do not bear the sword for no reason. They are God's servants, agents of wrath to bring punishment on the wrongdoer. (Romans 13:4)

This is retributive justice: evil is punished according to God's law.

However, modern discourse has introduced what some call "distributive justice" or "social justice"—the idea that inequality in outcomes proves injustice in causes. We see a rich person and a poor person and conclude: this is unfair; the rich person must have stolen from the poor. We ignore context and history, seeing only disparity, and declare this systemic injustice.

But this conflates injustice with the inherent inequality of a fallen world.

Consider: the ice cream vendor doesn't measure each scoop precisely. Parents buy different gifts for different children. Some people are born geniuses without effort. A basketball player earns $45 million while a nurse earns $45,000. Is this unfair? Perhaps—but it's not necessarily unjust.

When outcomes differ, it doesn't mean someone was wronged. A person's success doesn't diminish another's opportunity. Wishing Steve Curry well doesn't require breaking his ankle. Wanting him to be all he can be doesn't require making him less so we can be equal.

**As adults, we must learn to distinguish what is a real justice issue from what is simply the symptom of a broken world.**

### Positions of Authority and the Fear of God

As you grow older, you will gain influence. You may vote, become an employer, rise in your career, or even serve in government. In those positions, you will be like the judge.

Such positions of power need people who fear God—people who will exercise good judgment because they answer to someone greater than themselves.

## 2. The Mercy of God (Luke 18:9-14)

### The Pharisee and the Tax Collector

> To some who were confident of their own righteousness and looked down on everybody else, Jesus told this parable: Two men went up to the temple to pray, one a Pharisee and the other a tax collector. (v. 9-10)

Again, Jesus presents two contrasting figures.

**The Pharisee** was the righteous one—a keeper of the law who provided moral leadership. He was equivalent to the most upright person in town. In modern terms, he was triple-vaccinated, popular, a philanthropist. He fasted twice a week (a sign of piety), gave a tenth of his income (extraordinary generosity), and walked the walk. He ran charities, volunteered, gave blood.

> The Pharisee stood up and prayed about himself: "God, I thank you that I am not like other men—robbers, evildoers, adulterers—or even like this tax collector. I fast twice a week and give a tenth of all I get." (v. 11-12)

**The Tax Collector** occupied the opposite end of the spectrum. In Bible times, tax collectors were hated alongside robbers, evildoers, and adulterers. They were Jews who collected taxes for the Romans—traitors in Jewish eyes. They were also widely known for skimming extra money. Everyone despised them: the Jewish people who paid the taxes, and even the Romans they worked for. A tax collector was society's greatest villain.

### The Tax Collector's Prayer

> But the tax collector stood at a distance. He would not even look up to heaven, but beat his breast and said, "God, have mercy on me, a sinner." (v. 13)

Notice the contrast in their prayers. The Pharisee prays for justice: "God, punish this tax collector. Reward me for my righteousness."

The tax collector cannot pray for justice. He knows he is guilty. He has cheated people, perhaps causing suffering or even death—maybe someone's widow lost her last earnings to him. He knows he deserves punishment. So he does not pray for justice.

He prays for mercy.

He stands at a distance, ashamed to approach. He cannot even look to heaven. He beats his breast in contrition, as if physical pain could ease his inner guilt. His prayer is simple: "God, have mercy on me, a sinner."

### God's Surprising Verdict

> I tell you that this man, rather than the other, went home justified before God. For everyone who exalts himself will be humbled, and he who humbles himself will be exalted. (v. 14-15)

The tax collector is justified—declared righteous before God. He doesn't deserve it. Justice would demand his punishment. But God gives him mercy instead.

This mercy is possible because justice has fallen on Jesus. God can extend mercy to sinners without compromising his justice because Christ bore the punishment we deserve.

But we cannot demand mercy. Mercy is a gift, not a right.

### Justice and Mercy in Our Lives

When it's our fault, when it's nobody's fault, or when history is so convoluted we don't know who bears responsibility—we don't claim justice. We ask for mercy. We say "Help."

Yet our pride resists this. We prefer to claim our rights and entitlements rather than ask for help.

## 3. Becoming an Adult (Luke 18:15-17)

> People were also bringing babies to Jesus for him to place his hands on them. When the disciples saw this, they rebuked them. But Jesus called the children to him and said, "Let the little children come to me, and do not hinder them, for the kingdom of God belongs to such as these. Truly I tell you, anyone who will not receive the kingdom of God like a little child will never enter it." (v. 15-17)

Perhaps this is what it means to be an adult: to stop being entitled, to stop demanding justice, to start asking for mercy.

To be an adult means being realistic about the world. To accept that not everything is black and white, that we cannot easily find who caused suffering, that outcomes are complicated because the world is complicated and people are different.

To be an adult means being modest in our goals. Not giving up, but starting with ourselves, our families, our church, our community before trying to change the world. To do all this in dependence on God.

But to enter God's kingdom, we must become like children again—humble, dependent, asking for help rather than demanding our due.

## Conclusion: Praying for Those in Power

The widow's persistent prayers for justice were answered. Despite the silence of an unjust judge, God brought justice.

When Gladys Berejiklian legalized abortion in NSW and even attempted to criminalize prayer outside abortion clinics, patient saints prayed persistently for justice. Without political intrigue, without violence—justice caught up with her.

**We pray for all those in power: in all governments, in all positions of authority.**

**Give them a fear of God.**

**Help them to be humble.**

**And we pray for justice for all.**
