# 1 Samuel 18:1-30

**Title:** The Crown Prince  
**Date:** 21 August 2022 (ARISE); 25 June 2017 (SMAC)  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Introduction: The Appointment of a New King

God's Spirit has left Saul and gone to David, signifying the appointment of a new king. David will be king, but not yet. What's remarkable is that everyone loves this new crown prince—the women, all Israel and Judah, Michael, and Jonathan. This stands in sharp contrast to Saul's desperate attempts to cling to power.

## 1. All of Israel and Judah Loved David (vv. 6-16)

### The People's Celebration

When David killed Goliath, the women came out from all the towns of Israel to meet King Saul with singing, dancing, and music:

> "Saul has slain his thousands, and David his tens of thousands."

It was meant to be a celebration for Saul, but the song told a different story. Upon hearing the real news of David's victory, the women changed their tune. The contrast was unmistakable.

### Saul's Envy Awakens

Saul's response reveals a dangerous shift from love to hatred:

> "Saul was very angry; this refrain displeased him greatly. 'They have credited David with tens of thousands,' he thought, 'but me with only thousands. What more can he get but the kingdom?' And from that time on Saul kept a close eye on David."

This is envy in its purest form—the sin of desiring what your neighbor has in an unhealthy way. Unlike ambition (wanting to be strong like someone, or as wise as another), envy is fundamentally different. Envy says: *Your gain is my loss.*

### Understanding Covetousness

Covetousness is the root sin behind Saul's spiral. It operates from a "zero-sum" worldview where one person's blessing automatically diminishes another's. The person who wants not just a car like yours but your car. The person who wants not just a spouse but your spouse. The person who wants not just money but your money.

Paul calls covetousness idolatry (Colossians 3:5). Here's why: covetousness is desiring something so much that you lose your contentment in God. It's replacing the satisfaction you should get from Him with satisfaction from something else. Remarkably, the Ten Commandments begin and end with virtually the same command: "You shall have no other gods before me" and "You shall not covet." Coveting is desiring anything other than God in a way that betrays a loss of contentment in Him—a heart divided between two gods.

> "Godliness with contentment is great gain, for we brought nothing into the world, and we cannot take anything out of the world. But if we have food and clothing, with these we will be content." (1 Timothy 6:6-8)

### Saul's Descent Into Fear

Envy quickly transforms into murderous intent. As an evil spirit comes upon Saul while David plays the lyre, Saul attempts to pin David to the wall with his spear—not once, but multiple times. What's tragic is the irony: Saul wouldn't fight Goliath himself, yet he willingly attempts murder against an innocent young man who serves him faithfully.

When Saul realizes the Lord is with David and that he has lost God's favor, fear compounds his envy. He sends David away to command a thousand men in battle, hoping the Philistines will kill him. But this backfires. David has great success, and the people love him even more.

### The Root of the Problem

Saul's actions reveal a fundamental misunderstanding. Both Saul's kingship and David's anointing came from God. Either way, it was always God's choice. Saul's fear—that David would take the kingdom—missed the point: God had already appointed David. There was no throne to steal because it wasn't Saul's to protect.

When you understand that all good things come from God's hand alone, envy becomes senseless. Whether someone else receives a blessing is entirely God's business. Your contentment shouldn't depend on whether your neighbor gets something too.

## 2. Michal Loved David (vv. 17-30)

### Saul's Manipulation Through Marriage

Saul devises a new strategy. He offers David his older daughter, Merab, in marriage, but with a hidden agenda: send him into battle where the Philistines might kill him. David humbly accepts: "Who am I, and what is my family or my clan in Israel, that I should become the king's son-in-law?"

However, when it comes time for the wedding, Saul gives Merab to another man. His manipulation has failed.

### Michal's Love and Loyalty

Then something unexpected happens: Saul's younger daughter Michal loves David. The text emphasizes this clearly—not that David loves her, but that she loves him. Unlike the Jacob and Rachel narrative where mutual love is obvious, here the narrator tells us only of Michal's devotion.

Saul sees an opportunity. He tells David he can marry Michal, but the bride price is unusual: a hundred Philistine foreskins. Again, Saul's plan is for David to die in battle. But David doesn't die. He brings back two hundred foreskins and becomes the king's son-in-law. Through this marriage, David becomes a legitimate heir to the throne—a crown prince after Jonathan.

### The Point of Saul's Failure

What's most important isn't being loved by people; it's being loved by God. When the Lord is with you, trials and temptations aren't endpoints but means to a greater end. This gives us hope. Even when circumstances seem designed to destroy us, God's love transforms those circumstances into instruments of His purpose.

Saul's envy and fear have rendered him unable to govern: unable to govern his own emotions, unable to govern David, unable to control his people, unable to govern even his own children. Everything spins out of control because he refuses to accept what God has already decided.

## 3. Jonathan Loved David (18:1-5; 20:16-17)

### An Unexpected Loyalty

After David finished speaking with Saul, something remarkable occurred:

> "Jonathan's soul became one with David's soul, and he loved him as himself."

Jonathan made a covenant with David and gave him his royal robe, tunic, sword, bow, and belt. He voluntarily surrendered the symbols of his authority to the man who would become king instead of him.

### Clarifying "Love"

In our culture, we tend to sexualize love. Some interpret Jonathan's devotion as homoerotic. But this misses the full spectrum of what love means. Love can be sexual (as in marriage), but love can also exist without sex—in deep friendship, family bonds, sibling affection, and supremely in the love of God. In heaven, there will be no more marriage and therefore no more sex, yet there will be abundant love.

Here, Jonathan demonstrates brotherly love—unconditional devotion and sacrifice. When Jonathan strips off his royal garments and weapons, he's doing something profound: he's acknowledging that David, not he, is God's chosen king. He voluntarily abdicates his throne for David's sake.

### The Contrast With Saul

This is the right response to the anointed one. Compare it to Saul's refusal to submit to God's will. Saul clung to power through manipulation, attempted murder, and obsessive control. Jonathan, despite being the natural heir, chose surrender and friendship instead.

Every attempt Saul made to stop David only made God's choice more obvious. By chapter's end, Saul is revealed as a man unable to govern—not because David is stronger, but because envy and fear have destroyed his ability to lead.

## 4. The Attempted Murder and Escape (19:1-24)

### Jonathan's Deception

Jonathan intercepts Saul's murderous intention and warns David to go into hiding. He pleads with his father:

> "Let not the king do wrong to his servant David; he has not wronged you, and what he has done has benefited you greatly... Why would you do wrong to an innocent man like David by killing him for no reason?"

For a moment, Saul listens. He swears by the Lord that David will not be put to death. But when war breaks out again and David defeats the Philistines, Saul's evil spirit returns. He hurls his spear at David as he plays the lyre. David escapes, and Saul's daughter Michal helps him flee through a window that night, placing a household idol in his bed as a decoy.

### God's Protection Over Saul's Plans

David flees to Samuel at Ramah. Saul pursues, sending men to capture him, but when they encounter Samuel and the prophets of God, the Spirit of God comes upon them and they too begin prophesying. This happens a second time, then a third. Finally, Saul himself goes to Ramah, and the Spirit of God comes upon him as well. He walks along prophesying until he reaches the prophets' camp, where he strips off his garments and lies naked all day and night.

The irony is profound: Saul, the king who rejected God's Word through Samuel, is now forced into ecstatic prophecy in the very presence of the prophet who anointed him. God literally removes his ability to pursue David. None of Saul's plans work.

### David's Reverence for God's Anointed

Even when David has the chance to kill Saul in a cave at En Gedi—when Saul is vulnerable and his own men urge him to do it—David refuses:

> "The Lord forbid that I should do such a thing to my master, the Lord's anointed, or lay my hand on him; for he is the anointed of the Lord."

David cuts off only a piece of Saul's robe as proof. Afterward, he's conscience-stricken even for that small act. He bows before Saul and says:

> "See, my father, look at this piece of your robe in my hand! I cut off the corner of your robe but did not kill you... I have not wronged you, but you are hunting me down to take my life."

Saul, momentarily seeing David's heart, weeps and acknowledges: "You are more righteous than I. You have treated me well, but I have treated you badly... I know that you will surely be king and that the kingdom of Israel will be established in your hands."

## The Core Lesson: Submit to God's Anointed

Throughout this narrative, we see two responses to God's anointing of David:

**Saul's Response:** Envy, fear, manipulation, murder, control. A refusal to accept what God has already decided. This leads to complete loss of authority—over himself, over others, over events.

**Jonathan's Response:** Love, submission, sacrifice, friendship. An acknowledgment that God's choice is right, even when it costs him the throne he naturally expected to inherit.

**David's Response:** Trust in God's protection, reverence for authority even when abused, faith that God's purposes cannot be thwarted by human schemes.

## Application: Your Response to Jesus

The same question applies to us today: What is your response to Jesus, God's anointed King?

Are you still at war with Him? Do you still hold onto your armor and sword, still thinking you're the crown prince of your own life, that the world revolves around you? Or will you lay down your weapons and submit?

Will you respond like Saul—grasping, envious, afraid, trying to maintain control? Or like Jonathan—humbly accepting God's choice, surrendering your rights, and delighting in the anointed one's reign?

If you're in rebellion against God and want to follow Jesus now, do that today. Lay your robe at His feet. Acknowledge that He is the Lord of your life, not you. Trade the empty promise of self-rule for the joy of serving the King of kings.
