# Micah 7:1-20

**Title:** God's Saviour

**Date:** 22 December 2024

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## The Surprising Truth About Christmas

We often associate Christmas with goodness and celebration. But the book of Micah reveals a startling reality: that first Christmas should have been catastrophically worse than anything we could imagine.

## 1. A Miserable Christmas: The Day of Judgment (Verses 1-6, 11-13)

### The Spiritual Crisis

Micah opens with profound lament:

> What misery is mine! I am like one who gathers summer fruit at the gleaning of the vineyard; there is no cluster of grapes to eat, none of the early figs that I crave.

Using the imagery of Israel as God's vineyard, Micah describes a people bearing no spiritual fruit. The grapes and figs represent the blessings that come from living as God's people, yet they are entirely absent. It's like going to the refrigerator on Christmas morning expecting abundant leftovers, only to find it completely empty.

### A Society Collapsed Into Evil

The prophet then reveals the moral bankruptcy of his nation:

> The faithful have been swept from the land; not one upright person remains. Everyone lies in wait to shed blood; they hunt each other with nets.

The people have abandoned righteousness entirely. Not only the common folk, but the rulers and judges have become corrupt:

> Both hands are skilled in doing evil; the ruler demands gifts, the judge accepts bribes, the powerful dictate what they desire—and they all conspire together.

What should be a system of checks and balances has become a unified conspiracy of evil. Even when opposition parties exist, they function only as long as they oppose—but when they collude, all hope for justice vanishes.

The situation deteriorates further:

> The best of them is like a brier, the most upright worse than a thorn hedge.

Even the best of society has become corrupted. It's like running from criminals only to discover that the police are working with the gangsters.

### The Deepest Betrayal

The deterioration reaches its darkest point—the breakdown of family bonds:

> Do not trust a neighbor; put no confidence in a friend. Even with the woman who lies in your embrace guard the words of your lips. For a son dishonors his father, a daughter rises up against her mother, a daughter-in-law against her mother-in-law—a man's enemies are the members of his own household.

When trust collapses at every level of society—with neighbors, friends, spouses, and family—there is nowhere left to turn. This pattern is tragically visible in our modern world: the hesitation to help others for fear of legal liability, families divided by political disagreements, even parents and children becoming estranged over ideology.

### God's Day of Visitation

Yet buried within this darkness lies something astonishing. The prophecy reveals that God would come in judgment:

> The day God visits you has come, the day your watchmen sound the alarm. Now is the time of your confusion.

Notice the imagery: watchmen sounding alarms, not welcoming gates. An alarm signals approaching danger, not celebration. In ancient times, when watchmen saw enemy armies approaching, they sounded the alarm, the gates closed, and the city prepared for siege.

The prophecy continues:

> The day for building your walls will come, the day for extending your boundaries. In that day people will come to you from Assyria and the cities of Egypt, even from Egypt to the Euphrates and from sea to sea and from mountain to mountain. The earth will become desolate because of its inhabitants, as the result of their deeds.

**That first Christmas was meant to be a day of judgment and destruction.** On that day, God would come like an invading army with fire and judgment to destroy all who had sinned. Since the text declares that not one upright person remains, that meant everyone—including us—would be destroyed.

The picture is grim but necessary to understand what actually happened at Christmas. This is why we don't call it "Christ-Mass" but rather should recognize it as the day God came with fire, not to celebrate, but to judge. Instead of a Christmas tree, a battering ram. Instead of candy canes, a caning. Instead of mistletoe, thorns. Instead of presents, judgment. Instead of Santa Claus, the European figure of Krampus—the dark judge who visits houses to punish the naughty with no presents and no mercy.

## 2. A Merry Christmas: Unexpected Mercy (Verses 7-10, 14-20)

### Micah's Hope Against the Darkness

Yet even as Micah describes this coming judgment, he expresses something remarkable:

> But as for me, I watch in hope for the Lord, I wait for God my Savior; my God will hear me.

This is not a contradiction. Micah knows judgment is coming, yet he holds onto hope that somehow, in the midst of confusion and alarm, God might show mercy. He recognizes that God is both a God of justice and a God of grace.

> Do not gloat over me, my enemy! Though I have fallen, I will rise. Though I sit in darkness, the Lord will be my light. Because I have sinned against him, I will bear the Lord's wrath, until he pleads my case and upholds my cause. He will bring me out into the light; I will see his righteousness.

### The Character of God

The climax of Micah's prophecy reveals the heart of God:

> Who is a God like you, who pardons sin and forgives the transgression of the remnant of his inheritance? You do not stay angry forever but delight to show mercy. You will again have compassion on us; you will tread our sins underfoot and hurl all our iniquities into the depths of the sea. You will be faithful to Jacob, and show love to Abraham, as you pledged on oath to our ancestors in days long ago.

God is a God of grace. And here is what makes Christmas truly merry: God decided to show that grace—but it didn't come cheap.

### The Postponement of Judgment

When John the Baptist appeared, he preached judgment was imminent:

> The ax is already at the root of the trees, and every tree that does not produce good fruit will be cut down and thrown into the fire. I baptize you with water for repentance. But after me comes one who is more powerful than I, whose sandals I am not worthy to carry. He will baptize you with the Holy Spirit and fire. His winnowing fork is in his hand, and he will clear his threshing floor, gathering his wheat into the barn and burning up the chaff with unquenchable fire.

We often misread "baptize with fire" as something positive—passion for God, the gift of the Holy Spirit. But in this context, looking back at Micah, "fire" means the fires of judgment and hell. John was warning: repent before judgment comes!

Yet something remarkable happens:

> Then Jesus came from Galilee to the Jordan to be baptized by John.

Jesus appears not to bring immediate judgment but to be baptized—symbolically positioning himself to bear the judgment that should have fallen on us. God postpones the day of reckoning because:

> The Lord is not slow in keeping his promise, as some understand slowness. Instead he is patient with you, not wanting anyone to perish, but everyone to come to repentance.

God gave us a second chance. Stores stay open late so you can get your presents—they don't have to, but they do out of grace. Similarly, God extended time because He wanted us to have the opportunity to repent.

### What Jesus Bears

Jesus takes upon himself everything that should have been ours:

- The thorns instead of decorations
- The judgment instead of presents  
- The fire of God's wrath instead of celebration
- The booby trap instead of gifts
- The caning instead of kindness

All upon himself, so that God could show grace to us.

Many of us who hear this message regularly fail to appreciate how extraordinary this is. We haven't escaped destruction by our own merit—we've had a narrow escape. Jesus bore the judgment we deserved.

### Our Response: Gratitude and Mercy

Because God showed us mercy, we must show mercy to others. This is why Christmas traditions of reconciliation matter—families gathering to renew bonds, Boxing Day gifts for the poor. God has given us a second chance; we should give others second chances too.

But this second chance is the last chance. Before Jesus came, people could claim ignorance or appeal to lowly prophets. But now we have knowledge. God gave us His Son. Everything that needed to be said has been said. If that doesn't convince us, nothing will.

---

## Conclusion: We Got What We Needed, Not What We Deserved

> We did not get the Christmas we deserved, we got the Christmas that we needed.

God has shown us grace by sending His Son Jesus into the world—not to judge, but to save. This should move us to repentance and gratitude.

Even when Christmas is difficult, even in disaster and suffering, there is hope. Because we trust in Jesus, we can be merry. We can persevere. Even in what seems like a "Die Hard Christmas," we have a foundation that cannot be shaken.

As we move into a new year, remember: each Christmas is a gift—an extra year God has given us for repentance, for service, for calling others to turn to Him. Use it wisely. Love and serve Him more fully.

**Merry Christmas.**
