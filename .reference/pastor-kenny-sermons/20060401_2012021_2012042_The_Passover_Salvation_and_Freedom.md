# Exodus 12:21-42

**Title:** The Passover: Salvation and Freedom

**Date:** April 2006

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## The Passover Event: God's Final Judgment

We've been reading through Exodus and tracing God's people in their slavery in Egypt. For 400 years, they cried out to God in their bondage. Finally, God heard their prayers and sent Moses to demand Pharaoh's compliance. When Pharaoh refused again and again, God performed signs and wonders—blood in the Nile, frogs, gnats, flies, livestock disease, and boils. But each time, Pharaoh hardened his heart and refused to let God's people go.

Now comes the final plague, and it will be far more severe than anything before. God will not merely ruin rivers or darken the sun. This time, it will involve people themselves. Judgment has arrived.

> At midnight the Lord struck down all the firstborn in Egypt, from the firstborn of Pharaoh, who sat on the throne, to the firstborn of the prisoner, who was in the dungeon, and the firstborn of all the livestock as well. Pharaoh and all his officials and all the Egyptians got up during the night, and there was loud wailing in Egypt, for there was not a house without someone dead. (Exodus 12:29-30)

### Understanding "Midnight"

The phrase "at midnight" carries deep significance throughout Scripture—appearing 14 times, each marking a turning point, a moment of change, a quiet but decisive intervention. It reminds us of Christmas, but instead of Santa Clause bringing gifts, imagine a reverse Santa Clause carrying poison instead of presents. A sobering image.

When God "struck down all the firstborn," this was indiscriminate and thorough. Every family had a firstborn. Every one lay dead. This judgment showed no favoritism—from Pharaoh's palace to the dungeon, from the wealthiest to the poorest. The cry of anguish that rose from Egypt that night was the sound of absolute catastrophe.

### Why This Judgment?

This sounds harsh to us, and it should. We need to remember that:

- God is just, though we don't always see the full picture behind suffering (as the book of Job teaches us)
- God is sovereign over all human lives
- As Job said: "Naked came I out of my mother's womb, and naked shall I return thither: the Lord gave, and the Lord hath taken away; blessed be the name of the Lord."

Human lives are precious—this is an instruction for us as humans, for we are not the creators of life. We are stewards for a brief time. But God is the Creator of all life. Like a potter with clay, He has rights that we do not possess.

Consider this too: Pharaoh himself had ordered the indiscriminate killing of all Hebrew baby boys two years old and under. The judgment that fell on Egypt was, in a sense, a mirror of Pharaoh's own cruelty. The punishment fit the crime.

This is what judgment looks like.

## The Way of Escape: The Passover Lamb

### One Group Was Spared

But there was a group that didn't face judgment—the Israelites who obeyed God's instructions:

> Then Moses summoned all the elders of Israel and said to them, "Go at once and select the animals for your families and slaughter the Passover lamb. Take a bunch of hyssop, dip it into the blood in the basin and put some of the blood on the top and on both sides of the doorframe. None of you shall go out of the door of your house until morning. When the Lord goes through the land to strike down the Egyptians, he will see the blood on the top and sides of the doorframe and will pass over that doorway, and he will not permit the destroyer to enter your houses and strike you down. (Exodus 12:21-23)

Think about what this meant. If your family took a bunch of hyssop, dipped it into the blood of the lamb, and put some of that blood on the top and sides of your doorframes, Yahweh would see that blood and literally *pass over* you. The destroyer would not harm your family. No reverse Santa Clause for those houses.

### The Symbolism of the Lamb's Blood

This ritual anticipates the sacrifices of atonement that will be offered in the temple. The blood of a scapegoat—violence redirected onto the lamb instead of your firstborn—makes you exempt from judgment. The lamb dies so that your firstborn does not. There is a transfer of death: judgment was pronounced on all firstborns, but salvation from death is found in the blood and death of a lamb.

The Israelites were not spared because of moral superiority, strength, or good works. Their safety came solely from their faith in God's provision—the blood of the lamb. They had to trust God's promise and act on it.

### Jesus, the Lamb of God

This image of the lamb's blood is rich in theological significance. It points directly to Jesus Christ. As John the Baptist declared when he saw Jesus:

> "Look, the Lamb of God, who takes away the sin of the world!" (John 1:29)

Just as the blood of the lamb protected the Israelites from judgment, the blood of Christ shields us from the ultimate judgment of sin. Jesus is the Passover lamb—the one who was slaughtered. What happened to the lamb in Egypt foreshadows what will happen to Jesus.

### Application: Hide Under His Blood

So what does this mean for us today? Jesus is the lamb who takes away our sins. His blood represents his death on our behalf—he is the lamb that was killed on behalf of us, we who are under judgment. And this judgment is not just for firstborns; it is a judgment on all of us. All are destined to die once and face judgment.

Just as the Israelites hid under the blood of the lamb, we are to trust in Jesus' blood and death for our salvation. There is no literal blood today, of course. Instead, we trust in Jesus—we trust that God keeps His promises, just as the Israelites did when they put blood on their doorframes and stayed inside their homes that night.

But some Israelites, I'm sure, didn't trust. They thought, "This is too messy, it'll ruin my house's resale value. Or I'll just step outside for a moment before the shops close—surely the destroyer isn't coming yet." And they would have been caught outside the protection of the lamb. That was foolish.

Don't be like them. Trust and continue to trust that Jesus' blood and death will save you. If you haven't trusted God before, well, today is a good time to start. Trust that you have been saved from judgment through Jesus.

### The Freedom This Brings

And here's great news: If your life is about being saved by the lamb, it won't be about a lot of other things. It won't be about how well you do in school, how rich you are, how fashionable you dress, or how popular you are. It won't be about whether you have a girlfriend or boyfriend, or whether you hold a position of leadership. For Pharaoh had a title and power—yet it was the slaves who trusted the lamb who were saved. The titles and positions of this world cannot protect you from judgment; only the blood of the Lamb can.

## Remember the Passover: Freed to Serve God

### The Exodus and New Beginnings

> So the people took their dough before the yeast was added, and carried it on their shoulders in kneading troughs wrapped in clothing. The Israelites did as Moses instructed and asked the Egyptians for articles of silver and gold and for clothing. The Lord had made the Egyptians favorably disposed toward the people, and they gave them what they asked for; so they plundered the Egyptians. (Exodus 12:34-36)

Notice the emphasis on dough without yeast—unleavened bread. This wasn't incidental. The Israelites were driven out of Egypt in such haste that they didn't have time to let their dough rise. But this detail carries deeper meaning.

The symbolism of unleavened bread represents a new beginning, a break from the past. In Scripture, leaven often symbolizes sin or corruption. By removing it, the Israelites were leaving behind the old life of Egypt and starting anew. As Paul would later write:

> Get rid of the old yeast, so that you may be a new unleavened batch—as you really are. For Christ, our Passover lamb, has been sacrificed. Therefore let us keep the Festival, not with the old bread leavened with malice and wickedness, but with the unleavened bread of sincerity and truth. (1 Corinthians 5:7-8)

### The Nature of Freedom

Now here's a critical point: The Israelites were freed, but not freed to do whatever they wanted. That's a Western individualistic misconception of freedom. Look at what Pharaoh said to Moses:

> "Up! Leave my people, you and the Israelites! Go, worship the Lord as you have requested." (Exodus 12:31)

The word translated "worship" here doesn't mean singing or traditional religious observance—it means *work*, or service. The Israelites had been serving the Egyptians with hard labor for 400 years. They were enslaved to backbreaking work. But now they were being freed to serve someone else—Yahweh.

They weren't freed to chill out in the desert, or to wander wherever they pleased. They were freed to go east to the promised land. They were freed to serve God. Not freed from something only, but freed *for* something. Freed to be slaves of God, though His yoke is light and His burden easy.

### Application: We Are Also Called to Remember

The Passover was to be remembered perpetually as an ordinance:

> "Obey these instructions as a lasting ordinance for you and your descendants. When you enter the land that the Lord will give you as he promised, observe this ceremony. And when your children ask you, 'What does this ceremony mean to you?' then tell them, 'It is the Passover sacrifice to the Lord, who passed over the houses of the Israelites in Egypt and spared our homes when he struck down the Egyptians.'" (Exodus 12:24-27)

This command reminds us why we need regular remembrance. Today, we have Easter and Holy Communion to remind us. But I think every meal can be a reminder—many of you have the good habit of praying before you eat at each meal. This is a practice worth maintaining.

### Our Own Passover: From Slavery to Service

Jesus' death not only saves us from God's judgment but also rescues us from slavery—slavery to sin. We have served sin all our lives. It has been our master for many years, and its effects still linger. But you have been freed!

However, this freedom is not freedom to sin. We are now free to be free from sin and to serve God instead. Our previous lives of indulgence and sin have been forfeited. Our lives are no longer our own.

Our limbs are not ours. Our minds are not ours. Our money is not ours. Our jobs are not ours. Our weekends are not ours. Our children are not ours. They all belong to Yahweh, who calls each of these things into His service.

## Conclusion

> You have come to God, the Judge of all, to the spirits of the righteous made perfect, to the church of the firstborn, whose names are written in heaven. (Hebrews 12:23)

We are the firstborns bought at a price. That means we are no longer ours. Our previous lives have been forfeited. We are now free to serve.

If you haven't yet placed your trust in Jesus, the Lamb of God, this is the time. Be saved from judgment. Be freed from slavery. Become a servant of the Most High God—whose yoke is light, whose burden is easy, and whose love is everlasting.
