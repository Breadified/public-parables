**Title:** The Meaning of Life: To Relate

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## Relationships: A Fundamental Human Need

Relationships are undeniably important to us. From the headlines about royal families to the storylines of television dramas, we are captivated by human connection—families, friendships, and romantic bonds. Yet the same media that celebrates relationships also exposes their brokenness: divorces, bitterness, and betrayal.

This paradox reveals something crucial about the human condition. We desperately seek meaningful relationships, yet we struggle to maintain them. Why? The answer begins in Genesis.

## 1. We Are Not Created to Be Alone

> Then the Lord God said, "It is not good that the man should be alone." (Genesis 2:18)

After describing creation as "good" throughout Genesis 1, God makes a striking statement: something is "not good." That something is solitude.

This is not about literal isolation—sleeping alone or needing quiet moments. Rather, God addresses the mindset of **individualism**: the belief that we are complete in ourselves and need no one else.

### The Problem of Individualism

Our culture saturates us with messages of self-sufficiency:

- **In our music:** "I am free to do what I want"
- **In advertising:** "The most important person in the world is you"
- **In our economics:** Every person looking out for number one
- **In our spirituality:** Self-help gurus promising that unleashing your potential is what matters most

When individualism becomes our operating principle, relationships inevitably fracture. If a friendship no longer serves your needs, you discard it. If a marriage becomes inconvenient, you upgrade. If family feels burdensome, you move out. No wonder marriage and friendship commitments are failing at unprecedented rates.

God's diagnosis is clear: **individualism and selfishness are not good.**

The antidote is not isolation but the opposite—genuine, self-sacrificing relationship.

## 2. We Are Created for Relationship

> So the Lord God said, "I will make him a helper fit for him." (Genesis 2:18b)

God's solution to aloneness is companionship. After forming every creature, God recognizes that none of them adequately meet man's relational need. So God creates woman—bone of his bones, flesh of his flesh—as his perfect complement.

> "This at last is bone of my bones and flesh of my flesh; she shall be called Woman, because she was taken out of Man." (Genesis 2:23)

The text then establishes the pattern for all subsequent generations:

> "Therefore a man shall leave his father and his mother and hold fast to his wife, and they shall become one flesh. And the man and his wife were both naked and were not ashamed." (Genesis 2:24-25)

### The Foundation: Family

Marriage is the beginning, but the principle extends beyond it. God establishes **family** as the basic building block of human society and wellbeing.

#### With Our Blood Relatives

We must guard against taking family for granted. When you return home from university or work, are you truly present with them? Or do you retreat to your room, change, and immediately leave again? Your family has invested in you—your mother plans meals, your parents make sacrifices. They deserve your presence and your transparency about what's happening in your life.

#### Within the Church Family

The same relational commitment applies to the body of Christ. Scripture describes believers not as strangers or individuals but as family:

> "You are no longer strangers and aliens, but you are fellow citizens with the saints and members of the household of God." (Ephesians 2:19)

> "Do not rebuke an older man but encourage him as you would a father. Treat younger men like brothers, older women like mothers, younger women like sisters, in all purity." (1 Timothy 5:1-2)

### The Heart of Relationship: Other-Person-Centeredness

Genuine relationship requires one essential quality: **genuine interest in the other person's welfare**. It means investing your energy in serving others, not in extracting what you need from them.

This has a practical implication for church commitments. When you RSVP to a gathering, you're not simply saying you'll show up—you're making a covenant to be present, come rain or shine. You're declaring that this relationship matters more than whatever alternative might arise.

Breaking appointments is breaking trust. When you're consistently late, repeatedly absent, or dismissive in your cancellations, you communicate: *I don't value you enough to honor my word.* True community requires faithfulness, even when it's inconvenient.

## 3. We Are Created by a Relational God

The deepest reason we are created for relationship is that **God Himself is relational.**

Throughout Scripture, we see the Trinity in perfect relationship:

> "The Father loves the Son and has given all things into his hand." (John 3:35)

> "I seek not my own will but the will of him who sent me." (John 5:30)

> "When the Spirit of truth comes, he will glorify me, for he will take what is mine and declare it to you." (John 16:13-14)

Father glorifies Son. Son obeys Father. Spirit glorifies Son. In the eternal nature of God, there exists **complete mutual other-person-centeredness**. God's very essence is relational love.

Since we bear God's image, we too are fundamentally relational beings. We are wired for connection because we are made in the image of a connected God.

### The Gospel Healing Relational Brokenness

The fundamental barrier to relationship is **sin**—the self-centeredness that says, *I don't need you; I need only myself.* This is what breaks our connection with God and with one another.

Jesus came to heal this brokenness. Though He alone experienced perfect relationship with the Father from eternity, He willingly became alone—abandoned by His Father, rejected by people, forsaken by friends—to reconcile us to God.

> "He himself is our peace, who has made us both one and has broken down in his flesh the dividing wall of hostility... and might reconcile us both to God in one body through the cross, thereby killing the hostility." (Ephesians 2:14-16)

Through Christ's death, the barriers that divide us from God and from each other are demolished. We are restored to relationship with our Creator and with one another.

## The Comfort of Family

What a comfort this is. In a world that pressures us to be self-sufficient, we have been given family—both biological and spiritual. Even when we fail, when we break important commitments, when we let others down, we find forgiveness in Christ. Even if you're single and unmarried, you are not alone. You belong to the household of God.

That is something worth investing in.

## Conclusion

In a world of individualism, Genesis speaks a different word: **It is not good for man to be alone. The meaning of life is relationship.**

Genuine, committed relationships with family. Devoted connection within God's household. And ultimately, a restored relationship with the God who created us and loves us.

No one on their deathbed wishes they had worked harder or earned more money. They wish they had spent more time with the people they love and with God. We are fundamentally beings created for relationship because our ultimate reality is a God who exists in eternal relationship.

He has made us in His image—and His image is relational.
