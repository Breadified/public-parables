# Daniel 9:1-27

**Title:** How Long - Waiting for Forgiveness  
**Date:** August 25, 2019  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## God Punishes Sin

The dilemma of fatherhood mirrors a deeper question about God's relationship with His people: how long should we wait before forgiving? Daniel faces this question as he studies the scriptures and realizes that Israel's exile—punishment for sin—was always meant to last seventy years.

> In the first year of Darius son of Xerxes (a Mede by descent), who was made ruler over the Babylonian kingdom... in the first year of his reign, I, Daniel, understood from the Scriptures, according to the word of the Lord given to Jeremiah the prophet, that the desolation of Jerusalem would last seventy years. (Daniel 9:1-2)

Jeremiah had warned Israel for twenty-three years before the exile came. The price of their disobedience was clear: God would not forgive for seventy years—one generation, one lifetime. This was similar to God's judgment in the wilderness when the Israelites refused to enter the Promised Land and were condemned to wander for a generation until the faithless generation had passed away.

This principle shouldn't surprise us. God's discipline is part of normal life. We learned it from our earthly fathers:

> Moreover, we have all had human fathers who disciplined us and we respected them for it. How much more should we submit to the Father of spirits and live! They disciplined us for a little while as they thought best; but God disciplines us for our good, in order that we may share in his holiness. (Hebrews 12:9-10)

Earthly discipline produces a harvest of righteousness and peace for those trained by it. God's discipline is no different—except His discipline is without error. It springs from His love for us:

> Because the Lord disciplines the one he loves, and he chastens everyone he accepts as his son. Endure hardship as discipline; God is treating you as his children. (Hebrews 12:6-7)

## Genuine Repentance Requires Outward Signs

When the Babylonian Empire fell and Darius took the throne around 539 BC, Daniel realized the seventy-year punishment was nearly complete. But forgiveness doesn't come automatically—it requires genuine repentance. Daniel turned to God with the full acts of penitence:

> So I turned to the Lord God and pleaded with him in prayer and petition, in fasting, and in sackcloth and ashes. (Daniel 9:3)

These were not empty gestures. Prayer, petition, fasting, sackcloth, and ashes were the outward expressions of an inward turning. They mattered because we cannot read hearts—we need to see that repentance is real. As Daniel confessed on behalf of Israel, he admitted to their sin:

> Lord, the great and awesome God, who keeps his covenant of love with those who love him and keep his commandments, we have sinned and done wrong. We have been wicked and have rebelled; we have turned away from your commands and laws. We have not listened to your servants the prophets, who spoke in your name to our kings, our princes and our ancestors, and to all the people of the land. (Daniel 9:4-6)

True repentance includes three elements:

**It admits to sin.** Not the hollow modern apology—"I'm sorry you feel that way"—but genuine acknowledgment: we have done wrong, we have been wicked, we have rebelled, we have turned away, we have not listened.

**It brings shame.** Daniel acknowledged the weight of what Israel had done:

> Lord, you are righteous, but this day we are covered with shame—the people of Judah and the inhabitants of Jerusalem and all Israel, both near and far. (Daniel 9:7)

In our modern age, we have lost the sense of shame. Society parades immorality and resists any hint of disapproval. Yet deep down, if our conscience hasn't been seared, we know when we're doing wrong. Shame—the grief of disappointing God and those we love—should drive us to repentance, not distraction or blame-shifting.

**It seeks God's favor.** Repentance without seeking restoration is incomplete:

> The Lord is not slow in keeping his promise, as some understand slowness. Instead he is patient with you, not wanting anyone to perish, but everyone to come to repentance. (2 Peter 3:9)

Forgiveness requires repentance from the one who has sinned:

> So watch yourselves. If your brother or sister sins against you, rebuke them; and if they repent, forgive them. (Luke 17:3)

Reconciliation is a two-party process. One party repents; the other forgives. Then harmony is restored.

## God Forgives for His Own Sake, Not Because We Deserve It

As Daniel prays, he makes a critical shift. He stops asking God to forgive because Israel deserves it and instead appeals to God's character and His promises:

> Now, Lord our God, who brought your people out of Egypt with a mighty hand and who made for yourself a name that endures to this day, we have sinned, we have done wrong. Lord, in keeping with all your righteous acts, turn away your anger and your wrath from Jerusalem, your city, your holy hill. (Daniel 9:15-16)

And more directly:

> We do not make requests of you because we are righteous, but because of your great mercy. Lord, listen! Lord, forgive! Lord, hear and act! For your sake, my God, do not delay, because your city and your people bear your Name. (Daniel 9:18-19)

God's willingness to forgive rests entirely on His character, not on our worthiness. Forgiveness is costly—it means the one wronged bears the hurt themselves and chooses restoration despite the pain. When we forgive, we absorb the deserved consequences on behalf of the offender.

For Christians, this forgiveness comes fully through Christ:

> If we confess our sins, he is faithful and just and will forgive us our sins and purify us from all unrighteousness. (1 John 1:9)

## Forgiveness May Take Longer Than We Think

Daniel's prayer is answered immediately, but the answer contains unexpected news. The angel Gabriel appears while Daniel is still praying and reveals God's timeline:

> Seventy sevens are decreed for your people and your holy city to finish transgression, to put an end to sin, to atone for wickedness, to bring in everlasting righteousness, to seal up vision and prophecy and to anoint the Most Holy Place. (Daniel 9:24)

Not seventy years—but seventy sevens. This likely means seventy weeks of years, or 490 years in total. The fulfillment spans from Nehemiah's reconstruction of Jerusalem to the coming of the Messiah, with the final "seven" referring to Christ's atoning work:

> Know and understand this: From the time the word goes out to restore and rebuild Jerusalem until the Anointed One, the ruler, comes, there will be seven sevens, and sixty-two sevens. It will be rebuilt with streets and a trench, but in times of trouble. After the sixty-two sevens, the Anointed One will be put to death and will have nothing. (Daniel 9:25-26)

The message is both hopeful and sobering: God will partially fulfill His promise. King Cyrus will decree the Jews' return, and by 537 BC construction of the second temple begins. They come home. Yet it is never the same. The glory of Solomon's temple never returns. The Jews face subjugation by Persians, Greeks, and Romans. By AD 70, the temple is destroyed. Even in their own land, they remain exiled from God in a deeper sense.

## We Are Still Waiting

For believers today, this truth carries weight. We have repented and been forgiven through Christ's blood. Yet we remain in exile:

> Peter, an apostle of Jesus Christ, To God's elect, exiles scattered throughout the provinces of Pontus, Galatia, Cappadocia, Asia and Bithynia, who have been chosen according to the foreknowledge of God the Father, through the sanctifying work of the Spirit, to be obedient to Jesus Christ and sprinkled with his blood. (1 Peter 1:1-2)

Why are we still in exile if God has fully forgiven us? Not because our sins remain unpaid—that was accomplished completely when we repented sincerely. We wait to bear the burden of others' sin, to intercede for those who have not yet repented:

> With the Lord a day is like a thousand years, and a thousand years are like a day. The Lord is not slow in keeping his promise, as some understand slowness. Instead he is patient with you, not wanting anyone to perish, but everyone to come to repentance. (2 Peter 3:8-9)

God waits for those who have not yet turned to Him. We are part of that waiting. God wants to forgive anyone who sincerely repents. Have you?

## Therefore, Forgive as God Forgives

Because God is a forgiving God, we must be a forgiving people. When someone wrongs us and repents, we must forgive them—not once, but continually:

> Even if they sin against you seven times in a day and seven times come back to you saying "I repent," you must forgive them. (Luke 17:4)

Jesus elevated this to an even higher standard:

> Peter came to Jesus and asked, "Lord, how many times shall I forgive my brother or sister who sins against me? Up to seven times?" Jesus answered, "I tell you, not seven times, but seventy-seven times." (Matthew 18:21-22)

Seventy-seven times—the same language of "seventy sevens" used for the completion of God's redemptive work. This is how our Father in heaven forgives us:

> Forgive us our debts, as we also have forgiven our debtors. (Matthew 6:12)

The pattern is eternal and unending:

**Correction. Repentance. Forgiveness.**

This cycle continues as long as we walk this earth. Whether as fathers correcting children, as members of God's family correcting one another, or as a forgiven people extending forgiveness—we follow the pattern God has established for us.

For in forgiving others as God forgives us, we become like our Father and reflect His kingdom to a world still waiting for redemption.
