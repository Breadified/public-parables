# Genesis 2:18-25

**Title:** The Meaning of Life: To Relate?  
**Date:** March 11, 2007  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## We Are Not Created to Be Alone

> Then the Lord God said, "It is not good that the man should be alone." — Genesis 2:18

The culmination of all creation is humanity. We are not an afterthought, fulfilling the whims of the gods as the ancient world believed, but the pinnacle of creation—blessed by God, provided for by God. Throughout creation, God saw that it was good, and after creating mankind, He declared creation "very good" (Genesis 1:31). Yet here in Genesis 2:18, we hear God say something unexpected: "It is not good."

When God declares it is not good for man to be alone, He is not speaking merely of literal solitude, but of a deeper isolation—the mindset of individualism. We are created as individuals, each intimately fashioned by a loving God, yet we are not created to be individualistic.

### The Problem of Individualism

Our culture constantly reinforces self-centeredness:

- **In popular music:** "I am free to do what I want in any old time. Cause I'm free."
- **In advertising:** Magazine covers proclaim "It's all about You"; banks assure us "The most important person in the world is you."
- **In economics:** Financial models assume each person looks out for themselves first.
- **In self-help culture:** Gurus urge us to focus inward to "unleash the power within."
- **Even in Christian spaces:** Bookstores overflow with self-help books and songs celebrating "me" and "I."

These messages explain why friendships and marriages fail. If a relationship no longer meets our needs, we simply change friends or upgrade our spouse. If family becomes inconvenient, we move out. One-third to one-half of marriages end in divorce, not counting the countless unmarried relationships that dissolve silently. Despite our culture's constant celebration of relationships, people are less committed to each other than ever.

God's counter-cultural declaration stands firm: it is not good for humanity to be alone. Individualism and selfishness are not good.

### Application: Guard Against Isolation

Be intentional about the influences you absorb. Reject the lie that you are the only important person in your life.

For young adults especially, one danger is the travel mindset—not the occasional holiday, but disappearing for months or years under the banner of "seeing the world while we can." The logic seems sound: travel is expensive, so we might as well stay longer and work to fund more travel. But this reflects a dangerous principle: living only for yourself, believing you need no one. I know people who left as Christians, backpacked through Europe for a year, and returned unchanged—still alone, still adrift.

Resist this. We are created for relationship.

## We Are Created for Relationship

> I will make him a helper fit for him... So the Lord God caused a deep sleep to fall upon the man, and while he slept took one of his ribs and closed up its place with flesh. And the rib that the Lord God had taken from the man he made into a woman and brought her to the man. Then the man said, "This at last is bone of my bones and flesh of my flesh; she shall be called Woman, because she was taken out of Man." Therefore a man shall leave his father and his mother and hold fast to his wife, and they shall become one flesh. And the man and his wife were both naked and were not ashamed. — Genesis 2:18-25

God creates man as incomplete—a being who needs help. The earth is fruitful but untended; man is called to work it, to tend it, to bring water and cultivation. Man alone cannot fulfill this calling. He needs a helper—not a servant, but one who completes him.

The woman is not created because man is lonely (as we often interpret it), but because man is incomplete. He needs a genuine companion—a co-laborer, a counterpart. This is why a pet cannot satisfy, why a second male would not solve the problem. Companionship is not merely about romantic love. First John 4:7-21 speaks of love without mentioning sex. First Thessalonians 2:6-8 describes the apostle's love for the church—sharing life itself. First Corinthians 13, the great hymn of love, is not about marriage. Jesus called His followers not "lovers" but "friends" (John 15:15). Marriage is not the only answer to human need; it is one expression of the deeper truth that we are relational beings.

### Family: The Foundation

The pattern established here extends beyond marriage to encompass family itself.

> Therefore a man shall leave his father and his mother and hold fast to his wife, and they shall become one flesh.

Though Adam and Eve have no parents, the principle is set for all generations: a man and woman leave to establish a new family unit. Family is the foundational building block of society.

#### With Blood Relatives

How do you relate to your own family? When you return home from university or work, do you retreat to your room, change clothes, and leave again? That is not relational living.

We are privileged to belong to families. Do not take this for granted. Make effort to share what is happening in your busy lives. If your mother has cooked or planned a weekend, let her know in advance. The same applies to church activities, lectures, or your need for the car tonight. Relationships require communication and consideration.

#### In the Church Family

> So then you are no longer strangers and aliens, but you are fellow citizens with the saints and members of the household of God. — Ephesians 2:19

We relate to one another in the church as family:

> Do not rebuke an older man but encourage him as you would a father. Treat younger men like brothers, older women like mothers, younger women like sisters, in all purity. — 1 Timothy 5:1-2

As Christians, we are intrinsically linked through Christ. We share one Father, one Lord, one Spirit. The basic requirement for genuine personal relationship is other-person-centeredness—genuine interest in another person's welfare and active investment in their wellbeing.

> As we have opportunity, let us do good to everyone, and especially to those who are of the household of faith. — Galatians 6:10

### Application: Commitment to Community

Our church demonstrates this well. We see people serving one another; trust is growing as we engage in small groups. Yet our generation must guard against one tendency: failing to keep our commitments.

Once we have a car, a mobile phone, and some money, we become remarkably flexible. We begin treating commitments casually. But consider a volleyball team—everyone matters, and missing practice has consequences. You cannot build trust with someone who is unreliable.

When you make an appointment—whether Sunday service or a small group—you commit not merely to receive teaching but to look out for one another. Absence is reserved for illness or genuine emergencies. Though unwritten and unenforced, there is a family covenant: we gather together on Sunday at 5 p.m., rain or shine. Your friends notice when you don't show; they miss you.

Breaking appointments—arriving late, not showing up, failing to RSVP—breaks relationships. How can others trust you if you are unfaithful to your commitments? Nor is a casual text apology sufficient. If you break an appointment, truly repent. Seek forgiveness face to face.

Other-person-centeredness means asking not "What's in it for me?" but "How do I invest in this relationship?" An RSVP commits you to that person's event above others. Of course, emergencies arise and occasionally we must break commitments—but then realize what we have done. We have broken trust. We owe genuine repentance and reconciliation.

The relational model is not only what the Bible prescribes and what works practically; it is how we are made to live.

## We Are Created by a Relational God

> The Father loves the Son and has given all things into his hand. — John 3:35

> I seek not my own will but the will of him who sent me. — John 5:30

> I do as the Father has commanded me, so that the world may know that I love the Father. — John 14:31

> When the Spirit of truth comes, he will guide you into all the truth, for he will not speak on his own authority, but whatever he hears he will speak, and he will declare to you the things that are to come. He will glorify me, for he will take what is mine and declare it to you. — John 16:13-14

The Father gives all things to the Son. The Son obeys the Father and glorifies Him. The Spirit does not glorify Himself but glorifies the Son, just as the Son does not seek His own glory but the Father's.

In the Trinity, there exists perfect, eternal mutual other-person-centeredness. Each Person of the Godhead exists in complete devotion to the others.

Mankind bears the image of God, and this image must incorporate relationship—for God Himself is eternally relational. We are stamped with His nature.

### The God Who Understands

This reveals the inadequacy of impersonal theological systems. Other gods do not exist from eternity in relationship; they cannot sympathize with humanity because they are fundamentally alone. Only our God, being relational from eternity, can truly sympathize with us, know love, and show mercy. Our God is personal. Our God understands.

### Application: Get Right With God

Getting right with each other must be preceded by getting right with God. The fundamental thing that breaks all relationships is sin—self-centeredness, autonomy, the assertion that "I don't need you." This is what Jesus came to address.

Jesus perfectly reflects the nature of God because He exists in the eternal relationship of Son to Father; He is God's image made visible. Yet here is the mystery: He who was never alone became alone for our sake. He was abandoned by His Father, forsaken by humanity, betrayed by His friend.

Not because He deserved it, but because we were guilty.

> For he himself is our peace, who has made us both one and has broken down in his flesh the dividing wall of hostility by abolishing the law of commandments and ordinances, that he might create in himself one new man in place of the two, so making peace, and might reconcile us both to God in one body through the cross, thereby killing the hostility. — Ephesians 2:14-16

In His extraordinary other-centeredness, Jesus died on the cross to destroy the hostility between us and God, to reconcile us to Him.

What comfort this is! We can relate to God. We can relate to one another.

We are built for each other, built to serve one another. If you feel alone or abandoned or in need, you have one another. The beauty of family—in both senses—is that they must love you regardless. Even when you break important appointments, even when you disappoint others, you have forgiveness found in Christ. Before marriage, you may call yourself single, but you are never alone. You have family.

That is something worth investing in.

## Conclusion

In a world of relentless individualism, what does Genesis tell us? It declares that it is not good for man to be alone. The purpose of life is relationship—genuine family and committed relationships with one another and, ultimately, with the one Creator God of the universe.

No one on their deathbed wishes they had studied harder or worked longer or earned more money. They wish they had spent more time with the people they love and with God.

We are created to relate because our ultimate reality is a God in eternal relationship. He has created us in His image and likeness. This means humanity is fundamentally a being meant for personal relationship—with God and with one another.
