# 2 Timothy 3:1–4:8

**Title:** Defend the Gospel

**Date:** 27 February 2011

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Understanding the Challenge: Truth Under Assault

We live in a time when truth itself is under attack. It's not simply difficult to find truth—it's become fashionable to question whether truth exists at all and whether we can know it.

Three cultural shifts have created this crisis:

**Pluralism.** We've become aware of many cultures and religions, which has bred sensitivity but also confusion. The thinking goes: surely all these people can't be wrong, so there must be many truths and many ways to God. Truth becomes plural rather than singular.

**Postmodernism.** Alongside pluralism comes a deep skepticism of traditional authorities. History, we're told, is written by victors and manipulated by those in power. If authorities can't be trusted, how can we trust anything they teach?

**Doubt in ourselves.** The third shift is perhaps most insidious—doubt in our own ability to know truth. We feel the weight of the previous two shifts. We're confused, uncertain, perhaps even powerless. How do I know I know?

These same questions appear in Christian circles. From recent discussions, I've heard versions of all three:

- *Others are legitimate.* Every authority claims to be the authority. How do I know which is true? Shouldn't I listen to alternate truths—even those claiming to be teachers of false doctrine?
- *Closer to home.* How can I trust the authority at church? How do I know I haven't stepped into a cult? How do I know Kenny isn't a false teacher? How can I trust Paul? How can I trust Jesus?
- *Maybe I've been brainwashed.* What if I'm just a product of my upbringing—my parents' faith, Sunday school, youth group—inherited uncritically?

Fortunately, Paul anticipated these questions and provides answers through three pieces of circumstantial evidence about why you can be confident the gospel is true.

A word on method: Paul offers circumstantial evidence—not definitive proof, but enough individual pieces that together point toward truth with high probability. Like a detective finding a suspect with a bloodied knife, running away with a wallet—no one saw the actual crime, but the evidence suggests robbery.

Paul isn't here offering resurrection proofs (though he does that elsewhere in 1 Corinthians 15) or written historical evidence. Rather, he's answering the deeper question: who can you trust?

## 1. You Know Them: Recognizing False Teachers (3:1–9)

Paul begins with a clear directive: do not trust false teachers. You can know what they are like.

> There will be terrible times in the last days. People will be lovers of themselves, lovers of money, boastful, proud, abusive, disobedient to their parents, ungrateful, unholy, without love, unforgiving, slanderous, without self-control, brutal, not lovers of the good, treacherous, rash, conceited, lovers of pleasure rather than lovers of God. (2 Timothy 3:1–4)

It's an R-rated list—a catalog of spiritual corruption. False teachers are characterized not by isolated errors but by systematic opposition to godliness.

There's a second list in verse 6:

> They are the kind who worm their way into homes and gain control over weak-willed women, who are loaded down with sins and are swayed by all kinds of evil desires, always learning but never able to acknowledge the truth. (2 Timothy 3:6–7)

Notice their method: they infiltrate homes, prey on vulnerable people, and promise endless learning without ever arriving at truth.

**Their defining contradiction:** They have a form of godliness but deny its power.

They look godly. The word "form" (Greek *morphe*) suggests outward appearance—like a costume or mask. But there's no transformative reality behind it. They've denied the power of genuine godliness, just as they've denied God Himself (as mentioned in 2:12–13).

Think of Mystique from X-Men—the villain who morphs to look like others, pretending to be someone she isn't. False teachers are spiritual impostors. They appear righteous but lack any real transformation.

Verse 13 makes this explicit: *While evil men and impostors will go from bad to worse, deceiving and being deceived.* They are lovers of themselves, lovers of money, lovers of pleasure—sex, money, power. They've rejected the transformative power of the gospel.

**The fatal inconsistency:** Do they live what they teach?

If they preach materialism while loving money themselves, there's no integrity. If they preach sacrifice while refusing it, it's hollow. If they preach forgiveness while remaining unforgiving, if they preach loving others while loving only themselves, if they preach humility while being boastful and proud—the contradiction reveals them.

History provides examples. Roy Clements, a respected UK preacher, spent seven years on his website pillorying evangelical churches for hypocrisy. Then he came out as gay and abandoned his wife and children. A form of godliness with no power—invalidated by his inability to keep his promises.

Jimmy Swaggart, the American televangelist, spoke with flowery language about God and the Holy Spirit. He preached passionately against sexual sin. When he discovered another pastor in moral failure, he was quick to condemn. But then investigators found Swaggart himself with a prostitute. He apologized in tears, was reinstated, caught again years later, and eventually told people his private life was none of their business. This is a false teacher—appearance without reality.

You can see the difference. Real godliness transforms. Fake godliness is mere religion.

Paul's instruction is simple: Have nothing to do with them.

**Yet sometimes their folly isn't immediately apparent.** Verse 9 promises: *Their folly will be clear to everyone.* 

Truth operates like a web. When one part is skewed, it affects all the others. False beliefs inevitably corrupt godliness—sooner or later it shows. You can't pretend forever. Eventually, the inconsistency becomes undeniable.

Liberal theology demonstrates this pattern. It began by asking, "Let's find out what the Bible really has to say." Then: "Let's look for the real Jesus." Then: "People added words to the text—let's remove them." Then: "Maybe Paul didn't write these letters. Maybe 1 Timothy 2 is from a pagan temple cult, not Paul. Maybe it's cultural."

After fifty years of this incrementalism, where has it led? Churches now promoting the homosexual lifestyle, euthanasia, same-sex adoption. Churches declaring all religions equivalent. The agenda was hidden, but it couldn't stay hidden forever.

Benny Hinn, promising miracles while flying private jets—the greed eventually becomes apparent. Reformed charismatics fusing traditional teaching with charismatic emotionalism, prioritizing hype and numbers and attractiveness over God's Word—their fruit will eventually reveal their folly.

The principle stands: false teachers cannot hide their nature forever.

## 2. You Know Me: Paul's Personal Appeal (3:10–13)

Paul now calls Timothy to trust him through a personal appeal—unusual for Paul, who typically grounds his authority in his apostolic office or his encounter with Jesus on Damascus Road.

> You, however, know all about my teaching, my way of life, my purpose, faith, patience, love, endurance, persecutions and sufferings—what kinds of things happened to me in Antioch, Iconium and Lystra, the persecutions I endured. Yet the Lord rescued me from all of them. (2 Timothy 3:10–11)

Paul is essentially saying: Timothy, look at my life. Look at my teaching. Am I really talking rubbish? Have I been a hypocrite? Is it really consistent that I'm now a false teacher? Compare me to the other false teachers.

They've traveled extensively together, eaten together, escaped persecution together. Timothy has seen Paul's bank account—he knows Paul isn't feathering his nest like greedy false teachers do. Paul appeals not to credentials but to relationship.

> In fact, everyone who wants to live a godly life in Christ Jesus will be persecuted, while evil men and impostors will go from bad to worse, deceiving and being deceived. (2 Timothy 3:12–13)

Paul has suffered for the truth. He's endured to live consistently as a Christian. He's sacrificed to stay true to Christian belief. The persecution itself is evidence of authenticity.

**An application to leaders:** This is why godliness in leaders matters so deeply. The gospel often comes through relationship, not mere information transfer. Someone tells someone. Life communicates alongside words. If a leader's life is disreputable, it brings disrepute to the gospel—because objective truth comes through proclamation by imperfect people.

To those who asked, "Kenny, what if you're a false teacher?"—perhaps. I could raise defenses: Bible degree from a reputable college, denominational approval, references from previous churches. But papers and authorities may not convince those skeptical of all authority. 

Alternatively, I appeal to you: look at my life. Does that title fit what you know about me? You know my family. You've been to my house—floor full of toys, dishes uncleaned, just like yours. You know the university I attended, the suburb I live in, the schools I've been to—the same ones as you.

Compare my teaching with others you trust. Compare my gospel with what you've heard from reputable leaders elsewhere. Is it very different? Are they consistent?

**There is a possibility I'm a false teacher. But is it probable?**

If you genuinely believe so, we should talk.

## 3. You Know You: Standing Firm in Conviction (3:14–4:5)

Paul's third appeal addresses those who doubt themselves.

> But as for you, continue in what you have learned and have become convinced of, because you know those from whom you learned it, and how from infancy you have known the holy Scriptures, which are able to make you wise for salvation through faith in Christ Jesus. (2 Timothy 3:14–15)

**For those who've always been Christian:** You learned these truths from trustworthy people. From infancy you've known Scripture. You have it available to check for yourself. Don't abandon certainty without cause.

**For those who converted later:** You were in your right mind when you made that decision. You chose Christianity deliberately. What evidence has changed your mind? None—yet suddenly you want to start from scratch.

**The problem of unreasonable skepticism.**

Doubt is normal and healthy. But postmodernism practices skepticism without cause—doubting everything for the sake of doubting. It means meeting everyone with suspicion, forcing them to prove themselves first. This is unreasonable.

Unreasonable skepticism demands we constantly clear the slate and start from first principles, seeking some mythical "neutral" position untainted by upbringing or culture. But this is impossible and illogical.

I encountered someone who'd grown up in youth group and church for six years, then decided to abandon Christianity. When asked why, they raised questions about the Canaanite conquest in Joshua, Leviticus commands, the existence of other religions, the Crusades—standard skeptical objections. After an hour, I realized: you're trying to restart Christianity from zero. You want me to prove it all again from neutral ground.

But that's unreasonable. First, it's interminable. Second, why should we start from neutral? What evidence convinced you to change your mind? Nothing. You simply want to doubt everything.

This reveals something deeper: unreasonable skepticism is often a cover for sin.

We want to do something evil—steal, pursue greed, engage in unlawful sexual relationships, follow our lusts. So we manufacture doubt. For Christians, we reinterpret Scripture to fit what we want to do. We cast doubt on our conversion and long relationship with God as "brainwashing." We question whether Jesus actually existed. We raise clever questions—and suddenly it looks reasonable that Christianity is just an old, backward religion we don't have to follow. Or we can pick and choose. How convenient.

**Several marks of unreasonable skepticism:**

*It's a facade of reason without sincerity.* Questions sound interested and thoughtful, but there's no genuine seeking. It's performance—"I tried to find answers"—when really you just raised common objections with no interest in resolution. If you don't like my answer, what's yours?

*It's self-defeating.* The postmodern claim—"there is no truth"—is itself a truth claim. It collapses under its own logic.

*It's selective.* If you're truly skeptical of everything, why only Christianity? What about the TV you watch, the school you attend, secular culture consuming your time? You spend more hours on Facebook than in church, more time at school than with parents. You're not equally skeptical of all influences. Why single out Christianity?

*It masks deeper motives.* Real investigation asks: "Is this true? How does this affect me? How does this integrate with what I believe?" Unreasonable skepticism asks none of this.

You know yourself. You know when you're genuinely doubting (which can be addressed) versus doubting because you want to sin. You know when you're asking because you want to obey the answers versus asking to hide behind a rational facade.

So let us leave these unreasonable doubts behind.

## 4. Be Confident: You Have the Truth (4:1–8)

Paul now charges Timothy with confidence:

> In the presence of God and of Christ Jesus, who will judge the living and the dead, and in view of his appearing and his kingdom, I give you this charge: Preach the Word; be prepared in season and out of season; correct, rebuke and encourage—with great patience and careful instruction. For the time will come when men will not put up with sound doctrine. Instead, to suit their own desires, they will gather around them a great number of teachers to say what their itching ears want to hear. They will turn their ears away from the truth and turn aside to myths. But you, keep your head in all situations, endure hardship, do the work of an evangelist, discharge all the duties of your ministry. (2 Timothy 4:1–5)

This is challenging work. Defending the gospel is never popular because defense means saying no. You're constantly refuting, always opposing.

If we could predict heresies, we could take the offensive. But the attacks come from countless directions, and we can only respond. It takes patience. Scholarship catches up slowly—working through source criticism, redaction criticism, canonical criticism, literary criticism. We must wait for good answers.

Paul closes with his own testimony:

> For I am already being poured out like a drink offering, and the time has come for my departure. I have fought the good fight, I have finished the race, I have kept the faith. Now there is in store for me the crown of righteousness, which the Lord, the righteous Judge, will award to me on that day—and not only to me, but also to all who have longed for his appearing. (2 Timothy 4:6–8)

Paul speaks with conviction about his life's work. He has fought, finished, kept the faith. And he rests in the Lord's judgment—not human opinion.

**A final thought on doubt and knowledge:**

We often think knowledge is like a foundation—if one proof crumbles, everything collapses. But that's not how truth works. Probability rules decisions, not bare possibility. A thousand possible alternate scenarios doesn't mean one probable scenario is false.

Don't let possibilities rule your life. Let probability guide you. Compare with what you already believe. Make a stand.

The gospel is true. You know them—false teachers contradict themselves. You know me—authentic teachers live what they teach. You know you—you have learned from trustworthy sources and Scripture confirms it.

Be confident. Defend the gospel. The truth will vindicate itself.
