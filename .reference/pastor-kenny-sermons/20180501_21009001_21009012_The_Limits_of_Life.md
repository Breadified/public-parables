# Ecclesiastes 9:1-12

**Title:** The Limits of Life
**Date:** 6 May 2018
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Opening: A Difficult Moment

Dear brothers and sisters, I have some very sad news. Our dear sister in Christ, Chloe, passed away on Wednesday night in tragic circumstances. She took her own life.

She had just celebrated her 20th birthday and had struggled for a long time with depression. She felt she wasn't good enough and believed she was a burden to others. She was frustrated that she couldn't be as mentally fit as others seemed to be. I can understand that perspective—if we're all going to die anyway, what difference does timing make?

This ancient question echoes through the Book of Ecclesiastes, written 1000 years before Jesus. Today's passage addresses exactly this dilemma.

---

## Part 1: All Die—Five Categories of People (9:1-3, 11-12)

### The Universal Reality of Death

> This is the evil in everything that happens under the sun: **The same destiny overtakes all.**

Ecclesiastes describes five types of people, all equally destined to die:

- **The righteous and the wicked** — whether you pay your taxes, follow the rules, or live recklessly
- **The good and the clean, unclean** — whether you obey religious laws or ignore them
- **Those who offer sacrifices and those who do not** — whether you're pious or neglect religious observance
- **The good and the sinful** — whether you speak graciously or harshly
- **Those who take oaths and those who fear to take them** — whether you're foolishly confident or cautiously honourable

Whatever your character—whether you're Hitler or Mother Teresa, a psychopath or an agreeable person—all eventually die. The average age of death in Australia is 82. We all have a use-by date. A gangster might live to 100, while someone who never smoked, exercises regularly, and avoids risk could suddenly be hit by a car. Life is short, and death is unpredictable.

### Life Doesn't Work Out As We Imagine (9:11-12)

But there's more. Not only are our deaths unexpected—our lives often don't unfold the way we imagine either.

> The race is not to the swift or the battle to the strong, nor does food come to the wise or wealth to the brilliant or favor to the learned; but time and chance happen to them all.

You can do everything right and still lose. You might not be as brilliant as you thought. Your hopes and dreams get dashed. Your ambitions are thwarted. You might lose your job, face bankruptcy after a lifetime of building wealth, or become ill with something no drug or therapy can fix—and you cry each day without even knowing why.

You're supposed to have a certain lifespan, a certain amount of God-given wit and health to pursue your potential—then the rules change without notice, and you cannot achieve your goals. Life goes pear-shaped. You face the daily tiredness and difficulty of it all.

> Moreover, no one knows when their hour will come: As fish are caught in a cruel net, or birds are taken in a snare, so people are trapped by evil times that fall unexpectedly upon them.

Life is not just short but also cruel and brutish. And so it's tempting to escape.

### The Escape Temptation

Consider Job, a man whose life went completely wrong. He lost all his wealth, all his children, all his health—everything that made him who he was.

> "May the day of my birth perish, and the night that said, 'A boy is conceived!' May that day turn to darkness... Why did I not perish at birth, and die as I came from the womb?"

Job's despair captures the pain of the terminally ill, those with dementia who feel useless to the world, those who simply want the suffering to end. Euthanasia. Assisted suicide. Suicide itself.

> Enjoy life with your wife, whom you love, all the days of this meaningless life that God has given you under the sun---**all your meaningless days.**

If life is truly meaningless, isn't death preferable?

## Part 2: Five Reasons to Hope Even in Despair (9:4-6)

Yet in Solomon's despair, we are not to give up but to hope.

> **Anyone who is among the living has hope**—even a live dog is better off than a dead lion!

### Reason 1: The Living Know

> For the living know that they will die, but the dead know nothing.

You might know you will die. You might be depressed, your emotions in turmoil. But at least you know. Your brain is still functioning. Be glad, because the dead know nothing.

### Reason 2: The Living Still Have Reward

> They have no further reward.

No more productivity on this earth. The dead cannot contribute anymore.

### Reason 3: The Dead Are Forgotten

> And even their name is forgotten.

Names fade from tombstones. With cremation, there's not even a place to remember. You vanish like dust.

### Reason 4: The Dead Have Lost Love

> Their love, their hate and their jealousy have long since vanished.

We don't miss hate and jealousy, but their love has vanished. Relationships are severed forever.

### Reason 5: The Dead Cannot Participate

> Never again will they have a part in anything that happens under the sun.

You are no longer here to contribute. You miss birthdays, Christmas, weddings, steaks with friends, evening walks, mah-jong nights. You miss out on the living.

### The Dog vs. the Lion

But I don't want to be a dog. Yet better to be a living dog than a dead lion. And who says being a dog is bad anyway?

We might not all be mighty lions, but dogs can still contribute. Just by persevering, you help an entire generation—you encourage others to persevere too. Everybody contributes in this world. Everyone, as the body of Christ, contributes to the church.

> As it is, there are many parts, but one body... those parts of the body that seem to be weaker are indispensable... If one part suffers, every part suffers with it; if one part is honored, every part rejoices with it.

However low you feel about yourself, you are not worthless. You are made in the image of God. There's no need to compare yourself to others, because all die anyway.

You ought not to see yourself as a burden, but as a recipient and giver of gifts to and from others. We must not judge ourselves against those seemingly more successful than we are and get depressed over our alleged failures. Perhaps we are not lions, but being a dog is fine too—because you are **alive**.

Those of us attached to Christ should not see the future as bleak, but with hope. Even though this life brings suffering, we grieve our circumstances—but with hope.

> The thief comes only to steal and kill and destroy; I have come that they may have life, and have it to the full. (John 10:10)

Not just the next life, but this life also. We can live with our circumstances today. We can live with meaninglessness. We can live with tragedy.

## Part 3: Five Things to Do When You're Feeling Down (9:7-10)

When you're struggling, Solomon tells us five things to do:

### 1. Eat with Gladness

> Go, eat your food with gladness, and drink your wine with a joyful heart, for God has already approved what you do.

Even if food tastes like ash and you have no appetite, eat and keep going. Get your energy.

### 2. Clothe Yourself and Get Out

> Always be clothed in white, and always anoint your head with oil.

You don't want to get out of your pyjamas, but get out of the house and into the sunshine. Vitamin D will kick in the feel-good hormones. Don't want to shower or brush your teeth? Please do it anyway.

### 3. Enjoy Life with Those You Love

> Enjoy life with your wife, whom you love, all the days of this meaningless life that God has given you under the sun.

Enjoy with family, with friends. Enjoy the little blessings you do have. Count your blessings.

### 4. Do Whatever Your Hands Find to Do

> Whatever your hand finds to do, do it with all your might, for in the realm of the dead, where you are going, there is neither working nor planning nor knowledge nor wisdom.

This isn't a call to do whatever—it's a call to do what matters. What is that thing? The climax of Ecclesiastes spells it out:

> Now all has been heard; here is the conclusion of the matter: **Fear God and keep his commandments, for this is the whole duty of man.**

Life is not about finding meaning as though we little, insignificant people who live perhaps 82 years in one city can somehow decipher the meaning of God's actions across the universe with billions of people over millions of years. It's like reading one word from a Harry Potter novel and expecting to understand the entire book, then getting upset when you don't understand it.

> For none of us lives for ourselves alone, and none of us dies for ourselves alone. If we live, we live for the Lord; and if we die, we die for the Lord. So, whether we live or die, we belong to the Lord. (Romans 14:7-8)

Life is not about getting meaning. Life is about fearing God and keeping His commandment and using our hands to do that in whatever circumstances we face. We've been given a purpose. We have our marching orders. It remains for us to carry out our duty.

A premature death prevents us from doing that.

### 5. Do Good to the Living

**Don't encourage others to die.** Euthanasia is a terrible thing. Victoria has legalized it; NSW defeated a similar bill by only one vote.

**Don't abandon them.** They need support.

## Chloe: A Community's Faithful Response

When it comes to Chloe, you did good. So many of you tried to help her. Even though unsuccessful, you were righteous and wise.

Yes, I felt guilt. The last time we met, we ate some bargain $9 pho together, and she told me she loved Jesus. I was satisfied with that, but perhaps I should have asked more questions. Given her mental history, I should have cared more. I should have warned her about suicide.

But then I learned that Elaine had warned her just two weeks before. Elaine perhaps regrets not suggesting she see a psychologist. Mandy had been encouraging her to keep seeing doctors. Alice met up with her. Grace did. Jules did. And so many others.

You took her to movies, to sports, to church. You had coffee, went to concerts together. You read the Bible with her. Prayed with her. You organized a birthday party for her. And that's not even counting all the things her sister, mum, dad, relatives, psychologists, doctors, and so many others tried over many years.

No one person here could counsel her perfectly, but together, she was looked after. Yet for some reason, she made a strange and foolish resolution. In heaven in the future, I will be telling her off about what a foolish thing she did and how we are all hurting—before I give her a big hug.

Friends, please don't feel guilt. Collectively as a body, you covered all the major exit points. She was just too smart for us.

## A Word to Those Wanting to Die

Perhaps some of you are struggling right now and think death is the answer.

**Friends, please stay. Stay, not for your sake, but for ours.**

As you look around this room, we cannot do without you. You mean too much to us. You are not a burden. You are part of the body, and you make us whole. We get to obey God and fulfill our purposes in caring for you.

Please stay and take your part in the purposes of God. Please do good but persevere with the rest of us who also find life unbearable sometimes.

**Please talk to someone.** It might be good to have someone in mind right now. Talk to each other, a leader, or me. There are independent psychologists available. Your GP provides 10 free consultations. Resources are available at the back table.

## Closing: Grief with Hope

This week has been an emotional roller coaster for all of us—the joy of a wedding mixed with this grief. Life is a bit like that—a mix of happiness and sadness. Our brains are developed enough to cope with both concurrently, though I must admit this is rather extreme.

We grieve with hope, praying that God will squeeze good out of this tragedy and that she will be reunited with us on that final glorious day of Jesus' coming.

Pray for the family, for Phoebe, and for each other.

I love you all very much.
