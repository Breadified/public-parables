# 2 Corinthians 3:7–4:6

**Title:** The Glorious Ministry  
**Date:** 22 July 2009  
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Glory: What We Truly Seek

We all want significance. We want to climb the ranks of leadership, to be somebody. A youth leader once told me, "I'm just helping with the camera at Rice this year, but next year I'll be in charge. Then I'll be somebody." We naturally gravitate toward the glorious ministries—the ones with large crowds, dynamic things happening, impressive structures. It's hard not to think that glory motivates our ministry pursuits.

But Paul addresses this tension in 2 Corinthians, and his comparison between New Testament and Old Testament ministry offers us crucial perspective. Paul writes from a particular context: he hadn't made the planned trip to Corinth due to serious problems, not from unfaithfulness. Instead, he went to Macedonia. Now he needs to explain why the New Testament ministry surpasses the Old Testament without diminishing the law or disrespecting Judaism. How does he do it? Through a stunning reframing of what glory actually means.

---

## 1. The Christian Ministry Is More Glorious Than Judaism (3:7–11)

Paul begins with Exodus 34, the account of Moses coming down from Mount Sinai the second time he received the law.

> Now if the ministry that brought death, which was engraved in letters on stone, came with glory, so that the Israelites could not look steadily at the face of Moses because of its glory, fading though it was, will not the ministry of the Spirit be even more glorious? (2 Corinthians 3:7–8)

The law itself was glorious. When Moses descended from God's presence, his face shone so brightly that the Israelites couldn't bear to look at it. The very letters on stone—the law delivered by a prophet whose face radiated God's presence—carried unmistakable glory. This wasn't insignificant.

Paul himself testified to this glory in Romans 9:

> Theirs is the adoption as sons; theirs the divine glory, the covenants, the receiving of the law, the temple worship and the promises. Theirs are the patriarchs, and from them is traced the human ancestry of Christ, who is God over all, forever praised! Amen. (Romans 9:4–5)

Yet Paul makes a striking argument: if the ministry of condemnation was glorious, how much more glorious is the ministry of righteousness? What was glorious has no glory when compared with the surpassing glory of Christ. What was fading away came with glory, but how much greater is the glory of that which lasts?

---

## 2. The Unveiled Ministry (3:14–18)

Here's the crucial distinction: the Old Testament ministry was veiled, but the New Testament ministry is unveiled.

When Moses came down from Sinai, his face shone so brilliantly that he had to veil it. He only removed the veil when he entered God's presence or when he spoke the law to the people. The veil served a purpose—it blocked the light while the radiance was fading. But Paul reframes this physically literal veil as something deeper:

> But their minds were made dull, for to this day the same veil remains when the old covenant is read. It has not been removed, because only in Christ is it taken away. Even to this day when Moses is read, a veil covers their hearts. (2 Corinthians 3:14–15)

The veil is spiritual blindness. Without Christ, the law remains obscured in its true meaning. The Israelites, refusing to acknowledge Jesus as Messiah, remain spiritually veiled. The law becomes a message of death rather than pointing toward life in Christ.

But listen to what happens in Christ:

> But whenever anyone turns to the Lord, the veil is taken away. (2 Corinthians 3:16)

When you turn to the Lord, the veil is removed. No more hidden meanings. You read the law as fulfilled in Christ. You understand justification not through the sacrificial system but through His blood. The entire Hebrew Scripture becomes luminous—you see how it points to Jesus.

This unveiling transforms us:

> Now the Lord is the Spirit, and where the Spirit of the Lord is, there is freedom. And we, who with unveiled faces all reflect the Lord's glory, are being transformed into his likeness with ever-increasing glory, which comes from the Lord, who is the Spirit. (2 Corinthians 3:17–18)

We are those with unveiled faces. In your persona, in your actions, you reveal Christ's glory without a veil. You have personal knowledge of God, unencumbered and direct—like Moses in God's presence, but available to all believers through the Spirit. We see God's glory and reflect that glory. This is the unveiling: the knowledge of God. Glory is not heaviness or appearance or fame—it is God's revealed presence and truth made known.

---

## 3. Therefore, Proclaim Boldly (3:12)

> Therefore, since we have such a hope, we are very bold. (2 Corinthians 3:12)

Bold means we proclaim, not hide. We are like Moses unveiled to the world. We reflect the Lord's glory with unveiled faces, being transformed into His likeness with ever-increasing glory.

Notice what this means practically: glory is not found in climbing the ranks, in mastering the camera or the drums or the position. These things, pursued for self-advancement, are not glorious at all. They may even be negated by self-centered tendencies. Real glory lies in proclaiming the knowledge of Christ boldly. That is our ministry—not the external trappings of leadership, but the unveiling of Christ to a world that needs Him.

---

## 4. Therefore, Do Not Lose Heart (4:1–6)

> Therefore, since through God's mercy we have this ministry, we do not lose heart. Rather, we have renounced secret and shameful ways; we do not use deception, nor do we distort the word of God. On the contrary, by setting forth the truth plainly we commend ourselves to every man's conscience in the sight of God. (2 Corinthians 4:1–2)

Notice Paul's emphasis: we don't use deception, and we don't distort the word. We set forth the truth plainly.

The temptation is always present. Maybe we soften the message to make it more palatable. We host fun social events and slip in a Christian talk. We remove what people don't want to hear. We dilute the truth to make it more acceptable. But this is deception—distorting the gospel to serve our purposes.

Paul's logic is liberating: the truth is light. I don't have to improve it or polish it. It's like releasing a lion from a cage—I don't need to touch the lion or gloss it up. The light is sufficient. And if people remain blind, that's not because of our manipulation or obscurity. The god of this age has blinded the minds of unbelievers, not our lack of cleverness.

> And even if our gospel is veiled, it is veiled to those who are perishing. The god of this age has blinded the minds of unbelievers, so that they cannot see the light of the gospel of the glory of Christ, who is the image of God. (2 Corinthians 4:3–4)

Our task is singular:

> For we do not preach ourselves, but Jesus Christ as Lord, and ourselves as your servants for Jesus' sake. (2 Corinthians 4:5)

We preach Christ, not ourselves. We don't seek the glory. We serve.

And here is the promise:

> For God, who said, "Let light shine out of darkness," made his light shine in our hearts to give us the light of the knowledge of the glory of God in the face of Christ. (2 Corinthians 4:6)

The same God who spoke light into being at creation now speaks light into our hearts—the knowledge of God's glory revealed in Jesus Christ. This is what makes ministry glorious: not our position or our success, but the radiance of Christ shining through us, transforming us and those we serve.

---

## What Does a Glorious Ministry Look Like Today?

A glorious ministry is one that knows God and makes Him known. It's unswerving commitment to truth, even when truth is hard. It's refusing the shortcuts of deception, the compromises of distortion. It's boldly proclaiming Christ as Lord, serving others in His name, and allowing the light of God's glory to shine through our unveiled faces.

This is glory: not the applause of crowds or the prestige of position, but the manifest presence and knowledge of God transforming us from glory to glory—ever-increasing likeness to Christ, radiating His light to a world in darkness.
