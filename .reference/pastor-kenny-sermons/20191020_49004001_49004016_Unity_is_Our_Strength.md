# Ephesians 4:1-16

**Title:** Unity is Our Strength

**Date:** October 20, 2019

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

---

## Introduction: The Cultural Obsession with Diversity

Our world increasingly celebrates diversity as strength. It's become the rallying cry of schools, corporate boards, and social movements. Yet when we turn to Scripture, particularly Paul's letter to the Ephesians, we discover something unexpected: God's concern is not primarily about diversity, but about unity.

The contrast became particularly clear during the Anglican Synod in October 2019, when the church faced divisions over doctrine. Some accused church leadership of being unloving and divisive for maintaining traditional teaching. Yet the question Paul addresses here is more fundamental: How do we maintain unity without compromising truth?

---

## 1. Maintain Unity Through the Bond of Peace

### The Call to Vigilant Unity

> Make every effort to keep the unity of the Spirit through the bond of peace. (Ephesians 4:3)

The Greek words here are striking. "Make every effort" means to be eager, and "keep" means to guard—suggesting this unity requires active vigilance. We are called to be sentries, protecting something precious.

Notice the phrase "bond of peace." These terms seem paradoxical. The word "bond" (Greek: *desmos*) means ligament or something that constrains—like the prisoner's chains Paul mentions in verse 1. Peace, on the other hand, typically suggests freedom and comfort. Yet Paul binds them together.

This is the peace that comes from resolving conflict—not the comfortable emotional peace of feeling good, but the hard-won peace between formerly warring factions. It's like Siamese twins or a marriage: a forced unity born of necessity and commitment.

### Why Unity Matters: We Already Have Peace with God

Because we have been reconciled to God through Christ, we should experience peace with one another. Yet history shows a troubling pattern: after nations win wars together, their own factions often turn against each other. The American Civil War followed British defeat. China's civil war followed Japanese defeat. The "big peace" should lead to the "little peace."

But this is precisely where the church differs. We are called to let our peace with God translate into peace with each other—because we are now one.

> There is one body and one Spirit, just as you were called to one hope when you were called; one Lord, one faith, one baptism; one God and Father of all, who is over all and through all and in all. (Ephesians 4:4-6)

Paul reinforces the unity of the Spirit by repeating "one" seven times: one body, one Spirit, one hope, one Lord, one faith, one baptism, one God and Father. This is not abstract poetry—it is the foundation for peace.

### How Are We United?

**By destination:** We are called to one hope—not merely wishful thinking, but as Paul explains in Ephesians 1:18, the assured inheritance of God's glory in His people.

**By the way we enter that hope:** Through confession that Jesus is our one Lord, faith in the one Lord who sacrificed His life for us, and baptism—the act of repentance, the turning from sin.

**By the gospel itself:** It is the one gospel that both calls us and unites us, planned by the one Father and God of all.

### The Application: Humble Love in Community

> Be completely humble and gentle; be patient, bearing with one another in love. (Ephesians 4:2)

Maintaining unity means loving one another. Humility leaves no room for superiority. Gentleness and patience follow naturally from recognizing we are all equally loved, equally saved, equally valued by our Father.

The call is to work as a family, as a team. This requires sacrifice—not of core convictions, but of personal preferences. It might be:

- Music preferences
- Food preferences  
- Meeting times and locations
- Cultural customs

These things matter far less than the unity we're called to preserve. Yet we must be clear: we're not sacrificing truth for the sake of superficial harmony.

## 2. Attain Unity Through Theological Truth

### The Unity of Presence vs. The Unity of Essence

Many assume that unity is primarily about physical presence—Jews and Gentiles worshiping together. But that alone is insufficient. The early church had already achieved unity of presence; what they lacked was unity of essence.

True unity is theological unity. It is rooted in shared understanding of who God is and what He requires of us.

### Christ Gave Gifts to Build Theological Unity

> But to each one of us grace has been given as Christ apportioned it. (Ephesians 4:7)

The grace mentioned here is not the varied gifts discussed elsewhere (gifts of healing, administration, etc.). This is the singular grace of salvation given to all—the foundation upon which our unity rests.

> This is why it says: "When he ascended on high, he took many captives and gave gifts to his people." (Ephesians 4:8, quoting Psalm 68:18)

As the conquering King, Christ ascended into heaven after His descent (whether we interpret this as His incarnation, death, and resurrection, or His descent to Hades and ascent to heaven). As the victor, He distributed gifts to His people.

> So Christ himself gave the apostles, the prophets, the evangelists, the pastors and teachers, to equip his people for works of service, so that the body of Christ may be built up until we all reach unity in the faith and in the knowledge of the Son of God and become mature, attaining to the whole measure of the fullness of Christ. (Ephesians 4:11-13)

These gifted teachers—apostles, prophets, evangelists, pastors, and teachers—represent the historical teachers of God's people. Jesus Himself embodies all these roles: He is the apostle (the sent one), the prophet, the evangelist, and the chief shepherd and rabbi. His followers inherited this tradition and passed it down through the generations, ultimately resulting in Scripture itself.

The purpose is clear: **unity in the faith and in the knowledge of the Son of God**. We are not united by feelings, by music, by lights on a stage, or by a church name. These are superficial. True unity is theological unity—shared understanding of Christ and His ways.

### Maturity Means Discernment, Not Naivety

> Then we will no longer be infants, tossed back and forth by the waves, and blown here and there by every wind of teaching and by the cunning and craftiness of people in their deceitful scheming. (Ephesians 4:14)

Spiritual maturity means stability in conviction. It means not being swayed by every new idea that blows through our culture. The teachings of the church have stood for 2,000 years. The Old Testament wisdom spans another 2,000 years before that. Why should we overturn centuries of tested wisdom for ideas that have emerged in the last thirty years? Ten years? Last year?

This doesn't mean rejecting all innovation, but it does mean asking: How old is this idea? How long has it been tested? 

More troublingly, Paul warns us that some of these ideas are not innocent. There are people engaged in "cunning and craftiness" and "deceitful scheming." These are what Scripture calls false teachers—those actively eroding the teaching of Christ and destroying our unity.

We must respond with discernment:

- **Confront false teachers** (Titus 1:9): We must hold firmly to sound doctrine and refute those who oppose it.

- **Refuse support for false teachers** (2 John 10-11): We should not give them a platform or participate in their work.

- **Discipline false teachers** (1 Timothy 1:19-20): When teachers reject the faith and blaspheme, the church must address it.

This is not cruelty; it is care. As Paul writes in 2 Timothy 2:25-26, we instruct opponents gently in hope that God will grant them repentance, so they might escape the devil's trap.

### The Church as a Place of Honest Debate

The church must be a place where we can debate ideas without fear—where we test our faith against Scripture, not protect it from scrutiny. It is precisely because we believe in the sufficiency of God's Word that we are willing to discuss hard topics openly.

This is why the recent teachings have been intentionally challenging. Not to offend for its own sake, but to provoke thought. Our children are being formed daily by the media, by schools, by cultural voices speaking one perspective. To remain silent is to leave them defenseless.

True discernment requires naming both what is right and what is wrong. We must identify false teaching, not merely speak in generalities. We approve of faithful teachers and rebuke the teachings of those who deceive.

## 3. Speaking Truth in Love: How Unity Is Strengthened

> Instead, speaking the truth in love, we will grow to become in every respect the mature body of him who is the head, that is, Christ. From him the whole body, joined and held together by every supporting ligament, grows and builds itself up in love, as each part does its work. (Ephesians 4:15-16)

Notice Paul's return to the language of "ligament"—the same word used earlier for the "bond" of peace. Each sincere theological debate, each honest discussion, each moment of discomfort when we grapple with disagreement—these add another ligament to the body. We strengthen another connection.

When many ligaments and muscles are developed through such engagement, we have a strong body. We become held together at the core. The body works as one, not through superficial unity, but through the deep integration that comes from shared conviction and mature faith.

### Application: Sacrifice for the Sake of Unity

For those who gather regularly at this congregation: we are organized in teams, in community. This requires sacrifice:

- Come to church even when one or two weeks might not seem to matter much—your presence builds the body.

- Attend community meals even if the food isn't your preference. Some preferences must be surrendered for the sake of maintaining unity and allowing leaders to properly plan.

- Travel from afar without insisting on your own rights.

- Let go of cultural preferences that might interfere with our shared mission.

Sacrifice autonomy for the sake of achieving something greater together. Don't sweat the small stuff.

But we must also be clear about the big stuff. We maintain unity not through avoiding hard conversations, but through having them. We remain united not because we've compromised our convictions, but because we've clarified and defended them together.

True unity is found not in the same building, with the same music and lights, wearing the same name. It is found in the one Lord, one faith, one baptism, one hope. Those who share these and leave this room remain more united than those who sit beside us but do not share these convictions.

This clarity is unpopular. But it is biblical. And it alone makes us truly one.
