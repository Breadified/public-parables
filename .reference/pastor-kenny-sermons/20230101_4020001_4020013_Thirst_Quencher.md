# Numbers 20:1-13, 21:4-9

**Title:** Thirst Quencher

**Date:** 15 January 2023

**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## An Old Thirst (20:1-11)

In the fortieth year of Israel's wilderness journey, the people arrive at Kadesh facing a familiar crisis—they are thirsty and have no water. This moment echoes a pattern that runs throughout Numbers: the people grumble against their hardships, they are condemned to death, Moses intercedes, and they survive, though punished. But at Chapter 20, we're at the end of that forty-year cycle. Miriam dies at the beginning of this chapter, and Aaron will die before it ends. The old generation that rebelled is being replaced.

Yet the new generation, who should have learned from their parents' mistakes, begins exactly the same way—grumbling about water on the first day of their new beginning.

> Now there was no water for the community, and the people gathered in opposition to Moses and Aaron. They quarreled with Moses and said, "If only we had died when our brothers fell dead before the LORD! Why did you bring the LORD's community into this desert, that we and our livestock should die here? Why did you bring us up out of Egypt to this terrible place? It has no grain or figs, grapevines or pomegranates. And there is no water to drink!"

When Moses and Aaron approach the Tent of Meeting expecting judgment—as the pattern would suggest—they receive something different:

> The LORD said to Moses, "Take the staff, and you and your brother Aaron gather the assembly together. Speak to that rock before their eyes and it will pour out its water. You will bring water out of the rock for the community so they and their livestock can drink."

The command is clear: *speak* to the rock. But notice what Moses does instead:

> He and Aaron gathered the assembly together in front of the rock and Moses said to them, "Listen, you rebels, must we bring you water out of this rock?" Then Moses raised his arm and struck the rock twice with his staff. Water gushed out, and the community and their livestock drank.

## A New Beginning (20:12-13)

Water comes out. Everyone is satisfied. But God is not pleased.

> But the LORD said to Moses and Aaron, "Because you did not trust in me enough to honor me as holy in the sight of the Israelites, you will not bring this community into the land I give them."

Moses has committed two grave errors, each revealing a fundamental misunderstanding of God's character toward the new generation.

### A New Treatment

God commanded Moses to *speak* to the rock, but Moses addressed the people instead—and he addressed them harshly. He called them "rebels," echoing the language used for their fathers, not for this new generation. He asked, "Must *we* bring you water?" as though the task required his own effort, when God had already promised to provide for them.

This was not a minor deviation. God had determined that this new generation would not be known as rebels. Moses' harsh judgment violated God's decision to extend mercy to them. By treating them as the previous generation deserved to be treated, Moses failed to honor God's new approach to His people.

The Psalmist captures this clearly:

> By the waters of Meribah they angered the LORD, and trouble came to Moses because of them; for they rebelled against the Spirit of God, and rash words came from Moses' lips.

### A New Leader Needed

Moses' second error was more subtle but equally serious. When he "raised his arm" to strike the rock, he demonstrated rebellion against God Himself. Throughout Scripture, "raising the hand" carries a consistently negative connotation—it signifies defiance and insubordination. In Exodus, where a similar water miracle occurs, Moses is simply told to "strike the rock" with no mention of raising his arm. Here, the detail is significant: Moses raised his arm not merely to strike, but in an act of defiance against the God who had given him a different instruction.

The rock itself symbolizes God—the solid foundation, the source of life. When Moses struck it with a raised arm, he was acting rebelliously toward God. He did not trust God's method. He did not believe that simply speaking to the rock would produce water.

Because of this, Moses forfeits his entry into the Promised Land. A new leader must arise—one who will trust God completely and treat God's people with the mercy God Himself extends.

This becomes evident in what happens next.

## A New Forgiveness (21:4-9)

The people grumble again, and God sends venomous snakes among them. Many die. But when the people repent and ask Moses to intercede, God provides an unexpected solution:

> The LORD said to Moses, "Make a snake and put it up on a pole; anyone who is bitten can look at it and live." So Moses made a bronze snake and put it up on a pole. Then when anyone was bitten by a snake and looked at the bronze snake, he lived.

This symbol is remarkable. The snake on the pole looks forward to a leader who will come fifteen hundred years later and offer complete intercession for God's people. In the New Testament, Jesus Himself makes this connection explicit:

> Just as Moses lifted up the snake in the desert, so the Son of Man must be lifted up, that everyone who believes in him may have eternal life.

The bronze snake represents a pivotal shift. It is a dead snake—sin has been conquered. When people look to it, they are healed. Similarly, when we look to Jesus lifted up on the cross, we find salvation. The ancient symbol points to the one who would take upon Himself the poison of our sin, dying so that we might live.

But notice the profound change: the people no longer need Moses as their intercessor. The snake on the pole serves that function. God is preparing them for a time when Moses—who has been barred from entering the land—will no longer be present to plead for them.

## A New Victory (21:1-3)

The pattern of blessing continues as the new generation experiences military victories that their fathers knew only in defeat. Where the previous generation was beaten down by the Canaanites at Hormah, this new generation defeats them. The cycle of sin and punishment has been broken. God is treating this generation differently.

## The Real Game Changer

Just as the invention of Gatorade changed everything for athletes suffering from dehydration—addressing not just the lack of water but the loss of essential electrolytes and blood sugar—God's new approach to His people changes everything. 

The old system of reward and punishment, of judgment meted out according to works, is being replaced. God is moving toward a grace-based covenant where salvation depends not on perfect obedience but on faith in what God will provide. The scales of justice are being destroyed, not balanced more carefully.

For us who follow Jesus, this means we can draw near to God with confidence:

> Since we have confidence to enter the Most Holy Place by the blood of Jesus, by a new and living way opened for us through the curtain, that is, his body, and since we have a great priest over the house of God, let us draw near to God with a sincere heart in full assurance of faith.

## Application

Do not stumble your brothers and sisters in Christ by treating them as though God has not forgiven them. Those whom God has saved are forgiven people. We may judge behaviors, but we are not to judge hearts or declare someone outside God's family.

The new generation was the same as their parents—weak, grumbling, prone to complaint—yet God decided to treat them differently. That radical shift in God's approach culminates in Jesus, who takes upon Himself the judgment we deserve so that we might receive the mercy we do not.

If you have not yet believed in Him, look to the cross as the Israelites looked to the bronze serpent. If you are already a believer, remember: you are no longer under the law of works but under the grace of Christ. Live in that freedom, and extend that same grace to others.
