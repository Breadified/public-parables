## Introduction: The Question of Success

Over a hundred years ago, Francis Galton studied eminent figures across politics, sport, art, music, and science to understand why some achieve their goals while others fail. He concluded that high achievement stems from three factors: ability combined with zeal and the capacity for hard labour. When Galton shared this with his cousin Charles Darwin, Darwin replied with striking simplicity: "Excepting fools, men did not differ much in intellect, only in zeal and hard work."

Intelligence alone doesn't determine success. The difference lies in hard work and perseverance—what psychologist Dr. Angela Lee Duckworth calls **grit**.

Duckworth, drawing on her experience as a teacher and Harvard researcher, developed the Grit Scale to measure perseverance and passion for long-term goals. Testing it on Ivy League undergraduates, West Point cadets, and Scripps National Spelling Bee finalists, she found the same pattern: grit was the key predictor of success across every group. Grittier students achieved higher GPAs. Grittier cadets were more likely to complete their training. Grittier spellers outranked less tenacious competitors.

Her conclusion: "Perseverance may be as, if not more, important as intelligence in determining a person's success."

This principle echoes in the words of actor Will Smith, who describes himself as "slightly above average in talent." What sets him apart is his work ethic: "While the other guy's sleeping, I'm working. While the other guy's eating, I'm working. I am not afraid to die on a treadmill."

Hard labour, grit, perseverance—they are all another name for **discipline**. And this is what Paul addresses throughout Hebrews.

## Part 1: God Disciplines His Children (12:4–11)

### The Struggle Against Sin

Paul has been exhorting the Hebrews throughout this letter: persevere, do not give up on God. Look at their struggle as described in verse 4:

> In your struggle against sin...

The context suggests daily persecution and temptation. In this struggle, Paul rebukes them for two things:

**First:** "You have not yet resisted to the point of shedding your blood." This rebuke asks: Why haven't you endured even to the point of sacrifice? Why give up so easily when others are suffering persecution? Why avoid the cost of faithfulness?

**Second, and more importantly:** Paul reminds them they have forgotten the encouragement and exhortation found in Scripture, which addresses them as a father addresses his son.

### Discipline as Love

> My son, do not make light of the Lord's discipline, and do not lose heart when he rebukes you, because the Lord disciplines the one he loves, and he chastens everyone he accepts as his son. (Proverbs 3, quoted in Hebrews 12:5–6)

The fundamental truth is this: **life is one long training lesson by God the Father, done out of love.**

We understand this from our own experience with earthly fathers. From our youngest years, we are trained through discipline:

- We learn to use utensils, eat vegetables, wake on time
- We are taught mathematics and required to exercise
- We attend school and do chores
- Sometimes this involves pain: correction, scolding, deprivation

None of this feels pleasant at the time, yet our parents do it out of love. Would you want a child who never learned to care for themselves, who couldn't count, who had no self-control? Of course not. **Discipline is what every loving parent does. Not disciplining is what bad parents do.**

### The Legitimacy Test

> If you are not disciplined—and everyone undergoes discipline—then you are not legitimate, not true sons and daughters at all. (12:8)

The illegitimate child is the neglected child, abandoned without a father's training. This absence of discipline ruins them. The correlation is sobering: child poverty in wealthy nations stems not from inequality or overpopulation, but from the absence of stable, married parents—particularly the presence of a father. When 50–75% of children in some communities are born to single mothers, the cycle of undiscipline, unemployment, and despair perpetuates itself.

The child without discipline becomes the spoiled child—ruined, good for nothing.

### A Personal Illustration

My own father was not demonstrative in his affection, yet I respect him deeply for the discipline he instilled in me. I remember when school carnivals were mandatory, my father never wrote an excuse note like other parents did. He didn't believe it was right to claim illness when there was none. He had traveled 7,000 kilometres to Australia for our education, changed his job, left his relatives—and he would not allow me to skip my commitments. That discipline taught me honesty and reliability. I learned to show up to school, to church, to work, whether I felt like it or not. This training set me up well for everything that followed.

For those still at home, you may not appreciate your parents' discipline now. But in time, as Scripture says, you will respect them for it. Their discipline is part of their love.

### The Heavenly Father's Superior Discipline

If we respect our earthly fathers for disciplining us, how much more should we submit to God the Father?

> We have all had human fathers who disciplined us and we respected them for it. How much more should we submit to the Father of spirits and live! (12:9)

The contrast is profound:

> They disciplined us for a little while as they thought best; but God disciplines us for our good, in order that we may share in his holiness. (12:10)

Our earthly fathers act as they think best, with limited knowledge. God, with infinite wisdom, disciplines us for our eternal profit. And though discipline is never pleasant—it seems painful and sorrowful in the moment—it produces a harvest:

> Later on, however, it produces a harvest of righteousness and peace for those who have been trained by it. (12:11)

The contrast is between the temporary (the pain that "seems" and "a little while") and the eternal (sharing in holiness, righteousness, peace). If we understand this, how can we not submit to God's discipline?

### Resetting Our Expectations of God

We must learn not to be bitter under discipline but teachable. Two applications emerge:

**On Suffering:** Many grow jaded with God over suffering. One answer is that suffering results from sin—both personal (the direct consequences of wrongdoing) and global (we suffer the effects of a fallen world's sin). But there is hope. God uses present suffering as training for Christians:

> I consider that our present sufferings are not worth comparing with the glory that will be revealed in us... And we know that in all things God works for the good of those who love him, who have been called according to his purpose. (Romans 8:18, 28)

> No temptation has overtaken you except what is common to mankind. And God is faithful; he will not let you be tempted beyond what you can bear. But when you are tempted, he will also provide a way out so that you can endure it. (1 Corinthians 10:13)

Rather than view challenges negatively, remember that God is Father, acting in love. Under heaven's safety net, all hardships become opportunities to grow in holiness, to deepen understanding of Christ's love, to serve others better.

**Example: John Piper and Cancer**

John Piper, a renowned American pastor, was diagnosed with cancer. Rather than despair, he wrote "Don't Waste Your Cancer." Controversially, he called it a gift. The disease trained him to trust God more deeply, to read Scripture more carefully, to take sin seriously, to cherish Christ, to witness to others. He saw his illness not as disability but as training.

**Example: Nick Vujicic**

Nick Vujicic was born in Australia with no arms or legs—a rare condition called tetra-amelia syndrome. At eight years old, he struggled with God: "Why did you take my arms and legs? Why not give me what everybody has?"

But instead of remaining bitter, Nick reconciled with God and became thankful. Rather than ask God for a miracle, he became a miracle to others. With a cheerful disposition, he has impacted 200,000 souls worldwide. His own perspective: "What would I rather? No arms and legs on earth temporarily, or reach souls for Christ and spend eternity with them?"

This hardship made him who he is. God trained him through suffering for extraordinary service.

## Part 2: Therefore, Be Disciplined (12:12–13:6)

### Strengthen Yourself Spiritually

> Therefore, strengthen your feeble arms and weak knees. (12:12)

This is a spiritual metaphor. Stand firm. Raise your hands in worship. Brace yourself for God.

> Make level paths for your feet, so that the lame may not be disabled, but rather healed. (12:13)

Don't turn aside from truth to false teaching. Don't fall away. The metaphor warns against spiritual deviation.

### Cultivate Discipline Through Habit

Exercise daily with God's Word. Cultivate habits—Bible reading, prayer, church attendance, thinking on God's things. As surely as abdominal crunches build core muscles, these spiritual disciplines strengthen you. Good habits, formed now, will keep your paths straight.

### Pursue Peace and Holiness

> Make every effort to live in peace with everyone and to be holy; without holiness no one will see the Lord. (12:14)

> See to it that no one falls short of the grace of God and that no bitter root grows up to cause trouble and defile many. (12:15)

### Don't Forfeit Your Inheritance

> See that no one is sexually immoral, or is godless like Esau, who for a single meal sold his inheritance rights as the oldest son. Afterward, when he wanted to inherit this blessing, he was rejected. Even though he sought the blessing with tears, he could not change what he had done. (12:16–17)

Esau's story is a tragedy of shortsightedness. As the firstborn, his birthright was guaranteed—immense wealth and spiritual privilege. Yet, tired and hungry, he traded it to Jacob for a bowl of lentil soup. When he later desired his blessing, it was too late. He gave away everything for nothing and lived with permanent regret.

A modern parallel: In 2010, a programmer mined 10,000 Bitcoins and traded them for two pizzas—worth approximately $7 million at today's prices. A million dollars per slice. The regret must be agonizing.

Heaven is infinitely more valuable than any earthly inheritance. Never sell what God has given you.

### The Old Covenant and the New

The passage contrasts the terrifying experience of Mount Sinai—where God's law was given amid fire, darkness, and thunder—with the superior reality believers now possess in Christ:

> You have not come to a mountain that can be touched and that is burning with fire; to darkness, gloom and storm... (12:18–21)

That was 1,500 years before Jesus. It was real, but earthly.

But now:

> You have come to Mount Zion, to the city of the living God, the heavenly Jerusalem. You have come to thousands upon thousands of angels in joyful assembly, to the church of the firstborn, whose names are written in heaven. You have come to God, the Judge of all, to the spirits of the righteous made perfect, to Jesus the mediator of a new covenant, and to the sprinkled blood that speaks a better word than the blood of Abel. (12:22–24)

Abel's blood cried out for justice (Genesis 4). Jesus's blood speaks a better word—redemption, forgiveness, reconciliation. You have come to the heavenly reality, to salvation itself, to Christ.

### A Better Word

God once spoke through prophets at many times and in various ways. But:

> In these last days he has spoken to us by his Son, whom he appointed heir of all things, and through whom also he made the universe. (Hebrews 1:1–2)

This Word is alive and active:

> The word of God is alive and active. Sharper than any double-edged sword, it penetrates even to dividing soul and spirit, joints and marrow; it judges the thoughts and attitudes of the heart. Nothing in all creation is hidden from God's sight. Everything is uncovered and laid bare before the eyes of him to whom we must give account. (Hebrews 4:12–13)

The Word is no longer external—it cuts to the marrow, changing us from within.

### Don't Refuse Him Who Speaks

> See to it that you do not refuse him who speaks. If they did not escape when they refused him who warned them on earth, how much less will we, if we turn away from him who warns us from heaven? (12:25)

The revelation in Christ is infinitely greater. The consequences of turning away are infinitely more serious. The Father gives us all; the spoiled child who throws it back in His face faces judgment.

**We are not to be spoilt brats.** We are to persevere despite hardship, listen to God's instruction, and pursue righteousness and holiness through discipline.

## Part 3: Discipline in Practice (Chapter 13)

The character of a Christian is marked by faithfulness, commitment, and grit. To be a disciple is to have discipline. Righteousness, holiness, self-control, generosity, compassion, and service all require tremendous will power when confronted by the world's distractions.

Yet chapter 13 outlines practices requiring discipline:

> Keep on loving one another as brothers and sisters. (13:1)

> Do not forget to show hospitality to strangers, for by so doing some people have shown hospitality to angels without knowing it. (13:2)

> Continue to remember those in prison as if you were together with them in prison, and those who are mistreated as if you yourselves were suffering. (13:3)

> Marriage should be honored by all, and the marriage bed kept pure, for God will judge the adulterer and all the sexually immoral. (13:4)

> Keep your lives free from the love of money and be content with what you have, because God has said, "Never will I leave you; never will I forsake you." (13:5)

All of these require discipline. Yet our generation struggles deeply.

### A Crisis of Discipline

Generation Y (born 1982–1999, now ages 15–35) faces a crisis of perseverance. Youth unemployment approaches 40% because employers report that we cannot stick with jobs. We expect work to provide constant satisfaction. We show up late or not at all. We don't attend interviews. We post our absence on Facebook rather than calling our boss. We resign via SMS.

We don't even master basic prerequisites—half our university students don't finish the courses they start. We can't cook or iron a shirt. We exhibit little of the grit previous generations possessed. Gen Y is becoming "Generation Y bother to show up."

This flows into all of life:

- **Relationships:** How is continually breaking promises to show up or be on time loving to others? Half our marriages end in divorce.
- **Faith:** God becomes just another optional button on the Facebook of life.

### Why Our Church Emphasizes Discipline

If you've been here regularly or joined a serving group, you'll notice we emphasize certain habits: attending church consistently, serving diligently, notifying leaders if you won't be here, apologizing with good reason. From the outside, it can look legalistic and painful.

But what we're actually doing is **training in discipline**—learning to keep the promises we've made, to turn up to things we've committed to. Your leaders act out of love.

Church is voluntary. We don't need to attend to be saved by grace. But attendance certainly helps. To new visitors: you're welcome, please come back when you can. We're not weird.

But for those committed to church, think of discipline like the State Emergency Service (SES)—firefighters who volunteer their time. They're not paid. They have regular jobs. But when they sign up, they commit. They attend regular training. They respond to emergencies within their communities. They even drop their regular work for major fires. There's discipline, sacrifice, and commitment—not casual convenience.

We do the same here. Instead of fighting bush fires, we warn others of the fires of hell. If middle-aged volunteers can set time aside to fight bushfires, surely those convinced of Christ's need can be equally committed to His work.

As Woody Allen said: "80% of success in life is showing up."

### Encouragement and Challenge

We're seeing improvements:

- Many of you are committing to regular attendance and service, giving precious time to serve each other and the community
- Young people are cooking lunch for parents—I know few churches where youth serve their elders
- Some are pursuing driving licences; others are finding jobs
- Work hard.

We do this not just for those who organize these things, not just for those relying on us, and not just for ourselves (though discipline does make us more reliable and employable). We do this for our spiritual life:

- If we can't prioritize God's things as students, how will we when working, with a boss chasing us and pressure mounting?
- If we can't prioritize God's things while working, how will we when supporting a family, with spouse, sick children, and mortgage demands?
- If we can't do it as a student, young worker, or parent, why would you think you'll change when kids leave or you retire? You won't suddenly spend time on God—you'll spend it on golf.

**Friends: NOW IS THE TIME TO GET DISCIPLINE.**

To HSC students: prioritize God's things. To year 11 students: train yourselves now. Good habits formed in formative years last a lifetime. Young families: these are formative years for your children. What values and habits will you instill? Will your home be just a family, or a Christian family?

This is not the time to be complacent or spoilt. This is the time to be trained for righteousness and holiness. This is the time for self-control and perseverance.

## Conclusion: Finish the Race

As Angela Lee Duckworth concludes: "If it's important for you to become one of the best people in your field, you are going to have to stick with it when it's hard."

Or in Will Smith's words: "I am not afraid to die on that treadmill."

You are in the race of your life. Finish it.

The 1968 Olympic Marathon tells a story worth remembering. Runner Abebe Akhwari was competing in Mexico City when, at 19 km into the 42 km race, he fell badly. His knee was wounded, his shoulder bloodied against the pavement. Medical staff urged him to withdraw.

But Akhwari continued—walking, limping, running slowly. He finished last among the 57 competitors who completed the race. While the winner had finished long before (in 2:20:26), Akhwali crossed the line over an hour later (3:25:27). The sun had set. Only a few thousand remained in the stadium. But as he finished, a cheer erupted.

When asked why he ignored medical advice to withdraw, Akhwali said simply: "My country did not send me 5,000 miles just to start the race; they sent me to finish the race."

Of 75 who started that marathon, 18 others pulled out. No one remembers the gold medal winner's name. But we remember Akhwali because he finished the race in the most difficult circumstances.

**The important thing is to finish the race. Persevere.**
