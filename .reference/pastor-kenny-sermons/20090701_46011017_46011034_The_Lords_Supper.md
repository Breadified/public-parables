# 1 Corinthians 11:17-34

**Title:** The Lord's Supper
**Date:** 5 July 2009
**Speaker:** Kenny Liew

## What Is the Lord's Supper?

The Lord's Supper has been understood in different ways throughout Christian history. Some call it Holy Communion or the Eucharist (meaning "Giving Thanks"). Traditionally, it involves eating bread and drinking wine or juice to remember Jesus's death. Christian traditions vary widely—some insist on unleavened bread, others on wine specifically. The ritual is typically done monthly or weekly, often led only by ordained clergy, and is understood as solemn and reverent.

The church has historically held three main theological positions: **Transubstantiation** (the bread and wine literally become Christ's body and blood), **Real Presence** (Christ is truly present in the sacrament through the event itself, not through transformation of elements), and **Memorial View** (it is primarily a remembrance, not compulsory).

But before we ask *how* to observe this ritual, we must ask the more fundamental question: *Should* we be doing this at all? Is it compulsory? Paul's letter to the Corinthian church gives us surprising answers—and much more besides.

## Division Born from Self-Centeredness (1 Corinthians 11:17-22)

> In the following directives I have no praise for you, for your meetings do more harm than good. In the first place, I hear that when you come together as a church, there are divisions among you, and to some extent I believe it. No doubt there have to be differences among you to show which of you have God's approval. When you come together, it is not the Lord's Supper you eat, for as you eat, each of you goes ahead without waiting for anybody else. One remains hungry, another gets drunk. Don't you have homes to eat and drink in? Or do you despise the church of God and humiliate those who have nothing?

Paul's tone is remarkably blunt. He offers no praise—only criticism. The Corinthian gatherings "do more harm than good." Why? Because divisions plague their fellowship.

The problem becomes clear in how they eat together. When they gather, it is not truly the "Lord's Supper"—the meal of the Lord, eaten together as a community. Instead, each person brings their own food and eats selfishly, without waiting for others. Some gorge themselves and become drunk while others go hungry. This isn't a meal; it's barely civilized.

Paul cuts through their excuses: if you want to eat that way, why come to church? Stay home. But when you gather as God's people, things must be different. You must be other-centered. To eat selfishly in the presence of those who have nothing is to despise the church of God itself.

### What Meals Reveal

Meals are windows into the soul. They show us what people truly care about. Do they serve others or themselves? Do they eat the best portions while others go without? Do they help with preparation and cleanup? Meals expose whether we are self-centered or other-centered.

Consider families. How a family eats together—whether they wait for one another, whether they talk and encourage, whether they share—reveals what kind of family they are. The same is true for the church. Our eating practices reveal who we really are.

Many of us have been trained to think only of ourselves. Our mothers served us. Our teachers provided what we needed. Our church leaders arranged everything. We were never asked to think of others, to serve, to sacrifice. We became accustomed to thinking, "It's all about me." But this mindset has no place in the body of Christ.

### The Call to Share

When you gather as Christians, share your food. If you have plenty and others are hungry, that's not your food alone—it's for the community. There is no "I" in church, only "us."

When we gather for meals together, we have the opportunity to serve one another. Use a common spoon so no one passes germs. Pass the sauces and pour drinks for each other. Afterwards, clean up together.

When approaching a buffet, don't rush to grab the most food. Let the elderly and those who move slowly go first. Help those who cannot feed themselves. If you're young and able, go first—but go to get food for those who are immobile. Take only a small portion to ensure there's enough for everyone. Come back for seconds after you've made sure no one is left hungry. This is how you share.

Even walking down the street, share your food. If you see someone homeless and hungry, buy them something to eat. The basic essentials matter.

The next time you eat—and that will be today—notice how you eat. What does your eating reveal about you? If it shows selfishness, that's a problem. For how we eat shows who we are. And selfishness affects your family, your relationships, your church, and your standing before God.

## What Jesus Passed On (1 Corinthians 11:23-26)

> For I received from the Lord what I also passed on to you: The Lord Jesus, on the night he was betrayed, took bread, and when he had given thanks, he broke it and said, "This is my body, which is for you; do this in remembrance of me." In the same way, after supper he took the cup, saying, "This cup is the new covenant in my blood; do this, whenever you drink it, in remembrance of me." For whenever you eat this bread and drink this cup, you proclaim the Lord's death until he comes.

What did Jesus do at his last meal? He took bread, broke it, and gave it to others. He took the cup and gave it to others. In short, he served them.

Think about that. A person condemned to death faces their final request. They choose the most extravagant meal—caviar, champagne—something to indulge themselves. It's their last chance to be selfish. Jesus faced this same choice. He knew the agony and suffering ahead. No one would have blamed him for wanting one final night of comfort with his closest friends. But Jesus rejected that temptation. Instead, he served.

This meal is far more than a meal. It's symbolic. The broken bread represents his body, which would be broken for us. The cup represents his blood, poured out for us. And Jesus's service at the table points forward to his ultimate act of service: his death on the cross, taking our sins upon himself. Real sacrifice. Real blood. Real love.

### Other-Centeredness, Not Ritual

What did Jesus pass down to Paul, and Paul to the Corinthians? I don't believe it was a special ritual. Paul never even ate with Jesus. What Jesus passed down was not a ceremonial formula but the spirit of other-centeredness that flows from the cross. This is why Christ died—to serve us by bearing the punishment our sins deserve.

What is passed on is not pieces of bread (leavened or unleavened) or wine versus juice, not whether we gather daily or monthly, not who is authorized to lead. All the things churches divide over are ultimately beside the point. What's passed on is the proclamation of Christ's sacrificial, serving, other-centered love—even unto death.

The Corinthian church had completely missed this. While claiming to remember Jesus, they were doing the very opposite of what he taught and died for. Their selfish eating was disgusting to Paul because it contradicted the very heart of the gospel.

### Christian Meals as Spiritual Practice

For Christians, meals are not merely about fuel—that's a pagan approach. Christian meals are opportunities to wait for one another, to pray together, to practice grace. Grace itself is about receiving God's unmerited favor, and mealtime prayers help us remember this.

But more than that, meals are times to:
- Serve one another
- Pray for one another
- Talk meaningfully with one another
- Encourage one another

Yet our conversations often devolve into endless chatter about trivial matters. We trade witty observations but rarely dig deeper. Someone mentions they saw a movie; someone else comments on an actor; someone brings up a celebrity connection. But there's no real follow-up. No one asks, "What did you actually learn from your Bible study? Why do you admire that person? How has God been working in your life?"

Real conversation requires listening and genuine interest in understanding others. It's not about being funny; it's about being present. When we speak, we should actually go somewhere with our words—toward understanding each other more deeply and seeing how God affects our lives.

Think about the difference. Instead of:
- "How's your Bible study?" "Good, I saw a movie the other night..."

We should ask:
- "How's your Bible study? What have you been learning? How is God challenging you through Scripture?"

This is what it means to be other-centered. Jesus didn't look out for his own interests at his last meal; he looked out for others. Neither should we.

## Eating Worthily (1 Corinthians 11:27-34)

> Therefore, whoever eats the bread or drinks the cup of the Lord in an unworthy manner will be guilty of sinning against the body and blood of the Lord. A man ought to examine himself before he eats of the bread and drinks of the cup. For anyone who eats and drinks without recognizing the body of the Lord eats and drinks judgment on himself. That is why many among you are weak and sick, and a number of you have fallen asleep. But if we judged ourselves, we would not come under judgment. When we are judged by the Lord, we are being disciplined so that we will not be condemned with the world. So then, my brothers, when you come together to eat, wait for each other. If anyone is hungry, he should eat at home, so that when you meet together it may not result in judgment.

### What Is "Unworthy Manner"?

Many have misunderstood this passage. Some think it means a non-Christian shouldn't partake. Others believe those with hidden sins shouldn't participate. But in context, Paul is addressing something far more specific: **eating selfishly without thought for others**.

This is the unworthy manner. When you sin against the body and blood of the Lord, you're not sinning against the bread and wine. You're sinning against others—the very people for whom Christ's body was broken and blood was poured out. Your selfishness diminishes them. You treat as unimportant the very people Christ deemed worthy of his ultimate sacrifice.

### Examine Yourself

Paul calls us to examine ourselves before we eat. Ask yourself: Do I recognize the body of the Lord—that is, do I recognize and honor the other people here for whom Christ died? Or am I oblivious to their needs? Am I so focused on my own satisfaction that I fail to see their hunger?

If you eat selfishly, you eat and drink judgment on yourself. God is not pleased with selfishness in his church.

### The Cost of Selfishness

Paul then makes a sobering statement: "That is why many among you are weak and sick, and a number of you have fallen asleep" (meaning died). Some interpret this as God's supernatural punishment for improper ritual. But consider the simpler explanation: in an ancient, subsistence-level economy, if the poor and weak go hungry while the rich feast, the natural consequence is that some will become malnourished, fall ill, and die. Your brother or sister in Christ dies from hunger because you wouldn't share. You deserve condemnation.

### The Way Forward

The solution is straightforward: When you come together to eat, wait for each other. Don't rush ahead. Make sure everyone is fed before anyone feasts. And if someone is truly hungry, let them eat at home first so the communal meal isn't marred by desperation and division.

Above all, judge yourselves. Examine your hearts for selfishness. If we do this honest self-examination, we won't come under God's judgment. When God judges us, it's discipline—designed not to condemn us but to correct us so we won't be condemned along with the world.

## Is the Lord's Supper Compulsory?

The practice of Communion as a ritual has been part of Christian tradition for two thousand years. But is it obligatory? I don't believe so.

Like many traditions, it can be helpful—think of how reading Scripture at church each week nourishes faith. But traditions can also become empty, even harmful. A tiny piece of bread and a sip of juice can feel strange and disconnected from the gospel's heart. It can become superstitious rather than meaningful.

What matters is not the ceremony but the reality it points to: **fellowship**. Real sharing. Real community. Real other-centeredness.

The question for our churches should be: Do our current practices cultivate genuine fellowship, or have they become empty rituals? If a token meal doesn't help us practice real community, perhaps we should consider how to create genuine opportunities for shared meals where we actually serve one another, wait for each other, and strengthen our bonds in Christ.

Whatever form it takes, the Lord's Supper—in its truest sense—calls us back to the heart of the gospel: laying down our lives for one another, just as Christ laid down his life for us.
